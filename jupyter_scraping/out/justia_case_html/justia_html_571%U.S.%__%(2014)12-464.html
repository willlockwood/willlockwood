KALEY et vir v. UNITED STATES

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

No. 12\xe2\x80\x93464.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 16, 2013\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \xe2\x80\x94Decided February 25, 2014

\n

Title\n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7853(e)(1) empowers courts to enter pre-trial restraining orders to \xe2\x80\x9cpreserve the availability of [forfeitable] property\xe2\x80\x9d while criminal proceedings are pending. Such pre-trial asset restraints are constitutionally permissible whenever probable cause exists to think that a defendant has committed an offense permitting forfeiture and that the assets in dispute are traceable or otherwise sufficiently related to the crime charged. United States v. Monsanto,\n\n491 U.\xc2\xa0S. 600\n.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n After a grand jury indicted petitioners, Kerri and Brian Kaley, for reselling stolen medical devices and laundering the proceeds, the Government obtained a \xc2\xa7853(e)(1) restraining order against their assets. The Kaleys moved to vacate the order, intending to use a portion of the disputed assets for their legal fees. The District Court allowed them to challenge the assets\xe2\x80\x99 traceability to the offenses in question but not the facts supporting the underlying indictment. The Eleventh Circuit affirmed.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0When challenging the legality of a \xc2\xa7853(e)(1) pre-trial asset seizure, a criminal defendant who has been indicted is not constitutionally entitled to contest a grand jury\xe2\x80\x99s determination of probable cause to believe the defendant committed the crimes charged. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9321.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (a)\xc2\xa0In Monsanto, this Court held that the Government may seize assets before trial that a defendant intends to use to pay an attorney, so long as probable cause exists \xe2\x80\x9cto believe that the property will ultimately be proved forfeitable.\xe2\x80\x9d 491 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 615. The question whether indicted defendants like the Kaleys are constitutionally entitled to a judicial re-determination of the grand jury\xe2\x80\x99s probable cause conclusion in a hearing to lift an asset restraint has a ready answer in the fundamental and historic commitment of the criminal justice system to entrust probable cause findings to a grand jury. A probable cause finding sufficient to initiate a prosecution for a serious crime is \xe2\x80\x9cconclusive[e],\xe2\x80\x9d Gerstein v. Pugh,\n\n420 U.\xc2\xa0S. 103\n, and, as a general matter, \xe2\x80\x9ca challenge to the reliability or competence of the evidence\xe2\x80\x9d supporting that finding \xe2\x80\x9cwill not be heard,\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Williams,\n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 36\n. A grand jury\xe2\x80\x99s probable cause finding may, on its own, effect a pre-trial restraint on a person\xe2\x80\x99s liberty. Gerstein, 420 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 117, n.\xc2\xa019. The same result follows when it works to restrain a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s property.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Kaleys\xe2\x80\x99 alternative rule would have strange and destructive consequences. Allowing a judge to decide anew what the grand jury has already determined could result in two inconsistent findings governing different aspects of one criminal proceeding, with the same judge who found probable cause lacking presiding over a trial premised on its existence. That legal dissonance could not but undermine the criminal justice system\xe2\x80\x99s integrity, especially the grand jury\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional role. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (b)\xc2\xa0The balancing test of Mathews v. Eldridge,\n\n424 U.\xc2\xa0S. 319\n\xe2\x80\x94which requires a court to weigh (1) the burdens that a requested procedure would impose on the government against (2) the private interest at stake, as viewed alongside (3) \xe2\x80\x9cthe risk of an erroneous deprivation\xe2\x80\x9d of that interest without the procedure and \xe2\x80\x9cthe probable value, if any, of [the] additional .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. procedural safeguar[d],\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 335\xe2\x80\x94if applicable here, tips against the Kaleys. Because the Government\xe2\x80\x99s interest in freezing potentially forfeitable assets without an adversarial hearing about the probable cause underlying criminal charges and the Kaleys\xe2\x80\x99 interest in retaining counsel of their own choosing are both substantial, the test\xe2\x80\x99s third prong is critical. It boils down to the \xe2\x80\x9cprobable value, if any,\xe2\x80\x9d of a judicial hearing in uncovering mistaken grand jury probable cause findings. But when the legal standard is merely probable cause and the grand jury has already made that finding, a full-dress hearing will provide little benefit. See Florida v. Harris, 568 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. A finding of probable cause to think that a person committed a crime \xe2\x80\x9ccan be [made] reliably without an adversary hearing,\xe2\x80\x9d Gerstein, 420 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 120, and the value of requiring additional \xe2\x80\x9cformalities and safeguards\xe2\x80\x9d would \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n most cases .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. be too slight,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 121\xe2\x80\x93122. The experience of several Circuits corroborates this view. Neither the Kaleys nor their amici point to a single case in two decades where courts, holding hearings of the kind they seek, have found the absence of probable cause to believe that an indicted defendant committed the crime charged. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9320.

\n

677 F. 3d 1316, affirmed and remanded.

KERRI L. KALEY, et vir, PETITIONERS v. UNITED STATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

[February 25, 2014]

\n

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Justice Kagan delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n A federal statute,\n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7853(e), authorizes a court to freeze an indicted defendant\xe2\x80\x99s assets prior to trial if they would be subject to forfeiture upon conviction. In United States v. Monsanto,\n\n491 U. S. 600,\n615 (1989)\n, we approved the constitutionality of such an order so long as it is \xe2\x80\x9cbased on a finding of probable cause to believe that the property will ultimately be proved forfeitable.\xe2\x80\x9d And we held that standard to apply even when a defendant seeks to use the disputed property to pay for a lawyer.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In this case, two indicted defendants wishing to hire an attorney challenged a pre-trial restraint on their property. The trial court convened a hearing to consider the seizure\xe2\x80\x99s legality under Monsanto. The question presented is whether criminal defendants are constitutionally entitled at such a hearing to contest a grand jury\xe2\x80\x99s prior determination of probable cause to believe they committed the crimes charged. We hold that they have no right to relitigate that finding.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Criminal forfeitures are imposed upon conviction to confiscate assets used in or gained from certain serious crimes. See\n\n21 U. S. C. \xc2\xa7853(a). Forfeitures help to ensure that crime does not pay: They at once punish wrongdoing, deter future illegality, and \xe2\x80\x9clessen the economic power\xe2\x80\x9d of criminal enterprises. Caplin & Drysdale, Chartered v. United States,\n\n491 U.\xc2\xa0S. 617,\n630 (1989)\n; see id., at 634 (\xe2\x80\x9cForfeiture provisions are powerful weapons in the war on crime\xe2\x80\x9d). The Government also uses forfeited property to recompense victims of crime, improve conditions in crime-damaged communities, and support law enforcement activities like police training. See id., at 629\xe2\x80\x93630.[1] Accordingly, \xe2\x80\x9cthere is a strong governmental interest in obtaining full recovery of all forfeitable assets.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 631.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In line with that interest, \xc2\xa7853(e)(1) empowers courts to enter pre-trial restraining orders or injunctions to \xe2\x80\x9cpreserve the availability of [forfeitable] property\xe2\x80\x9d while criminal proceedings are pending. Such an order, issued \xe2\x80\x9c[u]pon application of the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d prevents a defendant from spending or transferring specified property, including to pay an attorney for legal services. Ibid. In Monsanto, our principal case involving this procedure, we held a pre-trial asset restraint constitutionally permissible whenever there is probable cause to believe that the property is forfeitable. See 491 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 615. That determinationhas two parts, reflecting the requirements for forfeit-ure under federal law: There must be probable cause to think (1) that the defendant has committed an offense permitting forfeiture, and (2) that the property at issue has the requisite connection to that crime. See \xc2\xa7853(a). The Monsanto Court, however, declined to consider \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the Due Process Clause requires a hearing\xe2\x80\x9d to establish either or both of those aspects of forfeitability. Id., at 615, n.\xc2\xa010.[2]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Since Monsanto, the lower courts have generally pro-vided a hearing to any indicted defendant seeking to lift an asset restraint to pay for a lawyer. In that hearing, they have uniformly allowed the defendant to litigate the second issue stated above: whether probable cause exists to believe that the assets in dispute are traceable or otherwise sufficiently related to the crime charged in the indictment.[3] But the courts have divided over extending the hearing to the first issue. Some have considered, while others have barred, a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s attempt to challenge the probable cause underlying a criminal charge.[4] This case raises the question whether an indicted defendant has a constitutional right to contest the grand jury\xe2\x80\x99s prior determination of that matter.

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The grand jury\xe2\x80\x99s indictment in this case charges a scheme to steal prescription medical devices and resell them for profit. The indictment accused petitioner Kerri Kaley, a sales representative for a subsidiary of Johnson & Johnson, and petitioner Brian Kaley, her husband, with transporting stolen medical devices across state lines and laundering the proceeds of that activity.[5] The Kaleys have contested those allegations throughout this litigation, arguing that the medical devices at issue were unwanted, excess hospital inventory, which they could lawfully take and market to others.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Immediately after obtaining the indictment, the Government sought a restraining order under \xc2\xa7853(e)(1) to prevent the Kaleys from transferring any assets traceable to or involved in the alleged offenses. Included among those assets is a $500,000 certificate of deposit that the Kaleys intended to use for legal fees. The District Court entered the requested order. Later, in response to the Kaleys\xe2\x80\x99 motion to vacate the asset restraint, the court denied a request for an evidentiary hearing and confirmed the order, except as to $63,000 that it found (based on the parties\xe2\x80\x99 written submissions) was not connected to the alleged offenses.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n On interlocutory appeal, the Eleventh Circuit reversed and remanded for further consideration of whether some kind of evidentiary hearing was warranted. See 579 F. 3d 1246 (2009). The District Court then concluded that it should hold a hearing, but only as to \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the restrained assets are traceable to or involved in the alleged criminal conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 43, n.\xc2\xa05. The Kaleys informed the court that they no longer disputed that issue; they wished to show only that the \xe2\x80\x9ccase against them is \xe2\x80\x98baseless.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 39; see App. 107 (\xe2\x80\x9cWe are not contesting that the assets restrained were .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. traceable to the conduct. Our quarrel is whether that conduct constitutes a crime\xe2\x80\x9d). Accordingly, the District Court affirmed the restraining order, and the Kaleys took another appeal. The Eleventh Circuit this time affirmed, holding that the Kaleys were not entitled at a hearing on the asset freeze \xe2\x80\x9cto challenge the factual foundation supporting the grand jury\xe2\x80\x99s probable cause determination[ ]\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94that is, \xe2\x80\x9cthe very validity of the underlying indictment.\xe2\x80\x9d 677 F. 3d 1316, 1317 (2012).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We granted certiorari in light of the Circuit split on the question presented, 568 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2013), and we now affirm the Eleventh Circuit.

\n

II

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This Court has twice considered claims, similar to the Kaleys\xe2\x80\x99, that the\n \nFifth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s right to due process and the\n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s right to counsel constrain the way the federal forfeiture statute applies to assets needed to retain an attorney. See Caplin & Drysdale,\n\n491 U.\xc2\xa0S. 617\n; Monsanto,\n\n491 U.\xc2\xa0S. 600\n. We begin with those rulings not as mere background, but as something much more. On the single day the Court decided both those cases, it cast the die on this one too.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In Caplin & Drysdale, we considered whether the Fifth and\n \nSixth Amendments exempt from forfeiture money that a convicted defendant has agreed to pay his attorney. See 491 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 623\xe2\x80\x93635. We conceded a factual premise of the constitutional claim made in the case: Sometimes \xe2\x80\x9ca defendant will be unable to retain the attorney of his choice,\xe2\x80\x9d if he cannot use forfeitable assets. Id., at 625. Still, we held, the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s claim was \xe2\x80\x9cuntenable.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 626. \xe2\x80\x9cA defendant has no\n \nSixth Amendment right to spend another person\xe2\x80\x99s money\xe2\x80\x9d for legal fees\xe2\x80\x94even if that is the only way to hire a preferred lawyer. Ibid. Consider, we submitted, the example of a \xe2\x80\x9crobbery suspect\xe2\x80\x9d who wishes to \xe2\x80\x9cuse funds he has stolen from a bank to retain an attorney to defend him if he is apprehended.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. That money is \xe2\x80\x9cnot rightfully his.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Accordingly, we concluded, the Government does not violate the Constitution if, pursuant to the forfeiture statute, \xe2\x80\x9cit seizes the robbery proceeds and refuses to permit the defendant to use them\xe2\x80\x9d to pay for his lawyer. Ibid.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n And then, we confirmed in Monsanto what our \xe2\x80\x9crobbery suspect\xe2\x80\x9d hypothetical indicated: Even prior to conviction (or trial)\xe2\x80\x94when the presumption of innocence still applies\xe2\x80\x94the Government could constitutionally use \xc2\xa7853(e) to freeze assets of an indicted defendant \xe2\x80\x9cbased on a find-ing of probable cause to believe that the property will ultimately be proved forfeitable.\xe2\x80\x9d 491 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 615. In Monsanto, too, the defendant wanted to use the property at issue to pay a lawyer, and maintained that the Fifth and\n \nSixth Amendments entitled him to do so. We dis-agreed. We first noted that the Government may sometimes \xe2\x80\x9crestrain persons where there is a finding of probable cause to believe that the accused has committed a serious offense.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 615\xe2\x80\x93616. Given that power, we could find \xe2\x80\x9cno constitutional infirmity in \xc2\xa7853(e)\xe2\x80\x99s authorization of a similar restraint on [the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s] property\xe2\x80\x9d in order to protect \xe2\x80\x9cthe community\xe2\x80\x99s interest\xe2\x80\x9d in recovering \xe2\x80\x9cill-gotten gains.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 616. Nor did the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s interest in retaining a lawyer with the disputed assets change the equation. Relying on Caplin & Drysdale, we reasoned: \xe2\x80\x9c[I]f the Government may, post-trial, forbid the use of forfeited assets to pay an attorney, then surely no constitutional violation occurs when, after probable cause is adequately established, the Government obtains an order barring a defendant from frustrating that end by dissipating his assets prior to trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. So again: With probable cause, a freeze is valid.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Kaleys little dispute that proposition; their argument is instead about who should have the last word as to probable cause. A grand jury has already found probable cause to think that the Kaleys committed the offenses charged; that is why an indictment issued. No one doubts that those crimes are serious enough to trigger forfeiture. Similarly, no one contests that the assets in question derive from, or were used in committing, the offenses. See supra, at 5. The only question is whether the Kaleys are constitutionally entitled to a judicial re-determination of the conclusion the grand jury already reached: that probable cause supports this criminal prosecution (or alternatively put, that the prosecution is not \xe2\x80\x9cbaseless,\xe2\x80\x9d as the Kaleys believe, supra, at 5). And that question, we think, has a ready answer, because a fundamental and historic commitment of our criminal justice system is to entrust those probable cause findings to grand juries.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This Court has often recognized the grand jury\xe2\x80\x99s singular role in finding the probable cause necessary to initiate a prosecution for a serious crime. See, e.g., Costello v. United States,\n\n350 U.\xc2\xa0S. 359,\n362 (1956)\n. \xe2\x80\x9c[A]n indictment \xe2\x80\x98fair upon its face,\xe2\x80\x99 and returned by a \xe2\x80\x98properly constituted grand jury,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d we have explained, \xe2\x80\x9cconclusively determines the existence of probable cause\xe2\x80\x9d to believe the defendant perpetrated the offense alleged. Gerstein v. Pugh,\n\n420 U.\xc2\xa0S. 103\n, n.\xc2\xa019 (1975) (quoting Ex parte United States,\n\n287 U.\xc2\xa0S. 241,\n250 (1932)\n). And \xe2\x80\x9cconclusively\xe2\x80\x9d has meant, case in and case out, just that. We have found no \xe2\x80\x9cauthority for looking into and revising the judgment of the grand jury upon the evidence, for the purpose of determining whether or not the finding was founded upon sufficient proof.\xe2\x80\x9d Costello, 350 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 362\xe2\x80\x93363 (quoting United States v. Reed, 27 F.\xc2\xa0Cas. 727, 738 (No. 16,134) (CC NDNY 1852) (Nelson, J.)). To the contrary, \xe2\x80\x9cthe whole history of the grand jury institution\xe2\x80\x9d demonstrates that \xe2\x80\x9ca challenge to the reliability or competence of the evidence\xe2\x80\x9d supporting a grand jury\xe2\x80\x99s finding of probable cause \xe2\x80\x9cwill not be heard.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Williams,\n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 36,\n54 (1992)\n (quoting Costello, 350 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 364, and Bank of Nova Scotia v. United States,\n\n487 U.\xc2\xa0S. 250,\n261 (1988)\n). The grand jury gets to say\xe2\x80\x94without any review, oversight, or second-guessing\xe2\x80\x94whether probable cause exists to think that a person committed a crime.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n And that inviolable grand jury finding, we have decided, may do more than commence a criminal proceeding (with all the economic, reputational, and personal harm that entails); the determination may also serve the purpose of immediately depriving the accused of her freedom. If the person charged is not yet in custody, an indictment triggers \xe2\x80\x9cissuance of an arrest warrant without further inquiry\xe2\x80\x9d into the case\xe2\x80\x99s strength. Gerstein, 420 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 117, n.\xc2\xa019; see Kalina v. Fletcher,\n\n522 U.\xc2\xa0S. 118,\n129 (1997)\n. Alternatively, if the person was arrested without a warrant, an indictment eliminates her\n \nFourth Amendment right to a prompt judicial assessment of probable cause to support any detention. See Gerstein, 420 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 114, 117, n.\xc2\xa019. In either situation, this Court\xe2\x80\x94relying on the grand jury\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9chistorical role of protecting individuals from unjust persecution\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94has \xe2\x80\x9clet [that body\xe2\x80\x99s] judgment substitute for that of a neutral and detached magistrate.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The grand jury, all on its own, may effect a pre-trial restraint on a person\xe2\x80\x99s liberty by finding probable cause to support a criminal charge.[6]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The same result follows when, as here, an infringement on the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s property depends on a showing of probable cause that she committed a crime. If judicial review of the grand jury\xe2\x80\x99s probable cause determination is not warranted (as we have so often held) to put a defendant on trial or place her in custody, then neither is it needed to freeze her property. The grand jury that is good enough\xe2\x80\x94reliable enough, protective enough\xe2\x80\x94to inflict those other grave consequences through its probable cause findings must needs be adequate to impose this one too. Indeed, Monsanto already noted the absence of any reason to hold property seizures to different rules: As described earlier, the Court partly based its adoption of the probable cause standard on the incongruity of subjecting an asset freeze to any stricter requirements than apply to an arrest or ensuing detention. See supra, at 6; 491 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 615 (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t would be odd to conclude that the Government may not restrain property\xe2\x80\x9d on the showing often sufficient to \xe2\x80\x9crestrain persons\xe2\x80\x9d). By similar token, the probable cause standard, once selected, should work no differently for the single purpose of freezing assets than for all others.[7] So the longstanding, unvarying rule of criminal procedure we have just described applies here as well: The grand jury\xe2\x80\x99s determination is conclusive.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n And indeed, the alternative rule the Kaleys seek would have strange and destructive consequences. The Kaleys here demand a do-over, except with a different referee. They wish a judge to decide anew the exact question the grand jury has already answered\xe2\x80\x94whether there is probable cause to think the Kaleys committed the crimes charged. But suppose the judge performed that task and came to the opposite conclusion. Two inconsistent findings would then govern different aspects of one criminal proceeding: Probable cause would exist to bring the Kaleys to trial (and, if otherwise appropriate, hold them in prison), but not to restrain their property. And assuming the prosecutor continued to press the charges,[8] the same judge who found probable cause lacking would preside over a trial premised on its presence. That legal dissonance, if sustainable at all, could not but undermine the criminal justice system\xe2\x80\x99s integrity\xe2\x80\x94and especially the grand jury\xe2\x80\x99s integral, constitutionally prescribed role. For in this new world, every prosecution involving a pre-trial asset freeze would potentially pit the judge against the grand jury as to the case\xe2\x80\x99s foundational issue.[9]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Kaleys counter (as does the dissent, post, at 7) that apparently inconsistent findings are not really so, because the prosecutor could have presented scantier evidence to the judge than he previously offered the grand jury. Suppose, for example, that at the judicial hearing the prosecutor put on only \xe2\x80\x9cone witness instead of all five\xe2\x80\x9d; then, the Kaleys maintain, the judge\xe2\x80\x99s decision of no probable cause would mean only that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Government did not satisfy its burden[ ] on that one day in time.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 12, 18; see Reply Brief 11\xe2\x80\x9312. But we do not think that hypothetical solves the problem. As an initial matter, it does not foreclose a different fact pattern: A judge could hear the exact same evidence as the grand jury, yet respond to it differently, thus rendering what even the Kaleys must concede is a contradictory finding. And when the Kaleys\xe2\x80\x99 hypothetical is true, just what does it show? Consider that the prosecutor in their example has left home some of the witnesses he took to the grand jury\xe2\x80\x94presumably because, as we later discuss, he does not yet wish to reveal their identities or likely testimony. See infra, at 14\xe2\x80\x9315. The judge\xe2\x80\x99s ruling of no probable cause therefore would not mean that the grand jury was wrong: As the Kaleys concede, the grand jury could have heard more than enough evidence to find probable cause that they committed the crimes charged. The Kaleys would win at the later hearing despite, not because of, the case\xe2\x80\x99s true merits. And we would then see still less reason for a judge to topple the grand jury\xe2\x80\x99s (better supported) finding of probable cause.[10]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Our reasoning so far is straightforward. We held in Monsanto that the probable cause standard governs the pre-trial seizure of forfeitable assets, even when they are needed to hire a lawyer. And we have repeatedly affirmed a corollary of that standard: A defendant has no right to judicial review of a grand jury\'s determination of probable cause to think a defendant committed a crime. In combination, those settled propositions signal defeat for the Kaleys because, in contesting the seizure of their property, they seek only to relitigate such a grand jury finding.

\n

III

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Kaleys would have us undertake a different analysis, which they contend would lead to a different conclusion. They urge us to apply the balancing test of Mathews v. Eldridge,\n\n424 U. S. 319 (1976)\n, to assess whether they have received a constitutionally sufficient opportunity to challenge the seizure of their assets. See Brief for Petitioners 32\xe2\x80\x9364. Under that three-pronged test (reordered here for expositional purposes), a court must weigh (1) the burdens that a requested procedure would impose on the Government against (2) the private interest at stake, as viewed alongside (3) \xe2\x80\x9cthe risk of an erroneous deprivation\xe2\x80\x9d of that interest without the procedure and \xe2\x80\x9cthe probable value, if any, of [the] additional .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. procedural safeguard[\xc2\xa0].\xe2\x80\x9d Mathews, 424 U. S., at 335. Stressing the importance of their interest in retaining chosen counsel, the Kaleys argue that the Mathews balance tilts hardin their favor. It thus overrides\xe2\x80\x94or so the Kaleys claim\xe2\x80\x94all we have previously held about the finality of grand jury findings, entitling them to an evidentiary hearing be-fore a judge to contest the probable cause underlying the indictment.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Government battles with the Kaleys over whether Mathews has any application to this case. This Court devised the test, the Government notes, in an administrative setting\xe2\x80\x94to decide whether a Social Security recipient was entitled to a hearing before her benefits were terminated. And although the Court has since employed the approach in other contexts, the Government reads Medina v. California,\n\n505 U. S. 437 (1992)\n, as foreclosing its use here. In that case, we held that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Mathews balancing test does not provide the appropriate framework for assessing the validity of state procedural rules which .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. are part of the criminal process,\xe2\x80\x9d reasoning that because the \xe2\x80\x9cBill of Rights speaks in explicit terms to many aspects of criminal procedure,\xe2\x80\x9d the Due Process Clause \xe2\x80\x9chas limited operation\xe2\x80\x9d in the field. Id., at 443. That settles that, asserts the Government. See Brief for United States 18. But the Kaleys argue that Medina addressed a State\xe2\x80\x99s procedural rule and relied on federalism principles not implicated here. Further, they claim that Medina concerned a criminal proceeding proper, not a collateral action seizing property. See Reply Brief 1\xe2\x80\x935. As to that sort of action, the Kaleys contend, Mathews should govern.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We decline to address those arguments, or to define the respective reach of Mathews and Medina, because we need not do so. Even if Mathews applied here\xe2\x80\x94even if, that is, its balancing inquiry were capable of trumping this Court\xe2\x80\x99s repeated admonitions that the grand jury\xe2\x80\x99s word is conclusive\xe2\x80\x94the Kaleys still would not be entitled to the hearing they seek. That is because the Mathews test tips against them, and so only reinforces what we have already said. As we will explain, the problem for the Kaleys comes from Mathews\xe2\x80\x99 prescribed inquiry into the requested procedure\xe2\x80\x99s usefulness in correcting erroneous deprivations of their private interest. In light of Monsanto\xe2\x80\x99s holding that a seizure of the Kaleys\xe2\x80\x99 property is erroneous only if unsupported by probable cause, the added procedure demanded here is not sufficiently likely to make any difference.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n To begin the Mathews analysis, the Government has a substantial interest in freezing potentially forfeitable assets without an evidentiary hearing about the probable cause underlying criminal charges. At the least, such an adversarial proceeding\xe2\x80\x94think of it as a pre-trial mini-trial (or maybe a pre-trial not-so-mini-trial)\xe2\x80\x94could consume significant prosecutorial time and resources. The hearing presumably would rehearse the case\xe2\x80\x99s merits, including the Government\xe2\x80\x99s theory and supporting evidence. And the Government also might have to litigate a range of ancillary questions relating to the conduct of the hearing itself (for example, could the Kaleys subpoena witnesses or exclude certain evidence?).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Still more seriously, requiring a proceeding of that kind could undermine the Government\xe2\x80\x99s ability either to obtain a conviction or to preserve forfeitable property. To ensure a favorable result at the hearing, the Government could choose to disclose all its witnesses and other evidence. But that would give the defendant knowledge of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s case and strategy well before the rules of criminal procedure\xe2\x80\x94or principles of due process, see, e.g., Brady v. Maryland,\n\n373 U.\xc2\xa0S. 83 (1963)\n\xe2\x80\x94would otherwise require. See Fed. Rules Crim. Proc.\xc2\xa026.2(a), 16(a)(2); Weatherford v. Bursey,\n\n429 U.\xc2\xa0S. 545\n\xe2\x80\x93561 (1977) (\xe2\x80\x9cThere is no general constitutional right to discovery in a criminal case\xe2\x80\x9d). And sometimes (particularly in organized crime and drug trafficking prosecutions, in which forfeit-ure questions often arise), that sneak preview might not just aid the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s preparations but also facilitate witness tampering or jeopardize witness safety. Alternatively, to ensure the success of its prosecution, the Government could hold back some of its evidence at the hearing or give up on the pre-trial seizure entirely. But if the Government took that tack, it would diminish the likelihood of ultimately recovering stolen assets to which the public is entitled.[11] So any defense counsel worth his salt\xe2\x80\x94whatever the merits of his case\xe2\x80\x94would put the prosecutor to a choice: \xe2\x80\x9cProtect your forfeiture by providing discovery\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cprotect your conviction by surrendering the assets.\xe2\x80\x9d[12] It is small wonder that the Government wants to avoid that lose-lose dilemma.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n For their part, however, defendants like the Kaleys have a vital interest at stake: the constitutional right to retain counsel of their own choosing. See Wheat v. United States,\n\n486 U.\xc2\xa0S. 153,\n159 (1988)\n (describing the scope of, and various limits on, that right). This Court has recently described that right, separate and apart from the guarantee to effective representation, as \xe2\x80\x9cthe root meaning\xe2\x80\x9d of the\n \nSixth Amendment. United States v. Gonzalez-Lopez,\n\n548 U.\xc2\xa0S. 140\n\xe2\x80\x93148 (2006); cf. Powell v. Alabama,\n\n287 U.\xc2\xa0S. 45,\n53 (1932)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is hardly necessary to say that, the right to counsel being conceded, a defendant should be afforded a fair opportunity to secure counsel of his own choice\xe2\x80\x9d).[13] Indeed, we have held that the wrongful deprivation of choice of counsel is \xe2\x80\x9cstructural error,\xe2\x80\x9d immune from review for harmlessness, because it \xe2\x80\x9cpervades the entire trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Gonzalez-Lopez, 548 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 150. Different lawyers do all kinds of things differently, sometimes \xe2\x80\x9caffect[ing] whether and on what terms the defendant .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. plea bargains, or decides instead to go to trial\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94and if the latter, possibly affecting whether she gets convicted or what sentence she receives. Ibid. So for defendants like the Kaleys, having the ability to retain the \xe2\x80\x9ccounsel [they] believe[ ] to be best\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94and who might in fact be superior to any existing alternatives\xe2\x80\x94matters profoundly. Id., at 146.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n And yet Monsanto held, crucially for the last part of our Mathews analysis, that an asset freeze depriving a defend-ant of that interest is erroneous only when unsupportedby a finding of probable cause. Recall that Monsanto considered a case just like this one, where the defendant wanted to use his property to pay his preferred lawyer. He urged the Court to hold that the Government could seize assets needed for that purpose only after conviction. But we instead decided that the Government could act \xe2\x80\x9cafter probable cause [that the assets are forfeitable] is adequately established.\xe2\x80\x9d 491 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 616. And that means in a case like this one\xe2\x80\x94where the assets\xe2\x80\x99 connection to the allegedly illegal conduct is not in dispute, see supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x94that a pre-trial seizure is wrongful only when there is no probable cause to believe the defendants committed the crimes charged. Or to put the same point differently, such a freeze is erroneous\xe2\x80\x94notwithstanding the weighty burden it imposes on the defendants\xe2\x80\x99 ability to hire a chosen lawyer\xe2\x80\x94only when the grand jury should never have issued the indictment.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Mathews test\xe2\x80\x99s remaining prong\xe2\x80\x94critical when the governmental and private interests both have weight\xe2\x80\x94thus boils down to the \xe2\x80\x9cprobable value, if any,\xe2\x80\x9d of a judicial hearing in uncovering mistaken grand jury findings of probable cause. 424 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 335. The Kaleys (and the dissent) contend that such proceedings will serve an important remedial function because grand juries hear only a \xe2\x80\x9cone-sided presentation[ ]\xe2\x80\x9d of evidence. Brief for Petitioners 57; see post, at 16. And that argument rests on a generally sound premise: that the adversarial process leads to better, more accurate decision-making. But in this context\xe2\x80\x94when the legal standard is merely probable cause and the grand jury has already made that finding\xe2\x80\x94both our precedents and other courts\xe2\x80\x99 experience indicate that a full-dress hearing will provide little benefit.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This Court has repeatedly declined to require the use of adversarial procedures to make probable cause determinations. Probable cause, we have often told litigants, is not a high bar: It requires only the \xe2\x80\x9ckind of \xe2\x80\x98fair probability\xe2\x80\x99 on which \xe2\x80\x98reasonable and prudent [people,] not legal technicians, act.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Florida v. Harris, 568 U.\xc2\xa0S. __, __ (2013) (slip op., at 5) (quoting Illinois v. Gates,\n\n462 U.\xc2\xa0S. 213,\n231,\n238 (1983)\n); see Gerstein, 420 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 121 (contrasting probable cause to reasonable-doubt and preponderance standards). That is why a grand jury\xe2\x80\x99s finding of probable cause to think that a person committed a crime \xe2\x80\x9ccan be [made] reliably without an adversary hearing,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 120; it is and \xe2\x80\x9chas always been thought sufficient to hear only the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s side,\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Williams,\n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 36,\n51 (1992)\n. So, for example, we have held the \xe2\x80\x9cconfrontation and cross-examination\xe2\x80\x9d of witnesses unnecessary in a grand jury proceeding. Gerstein, 420 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 121\xe2\x80\x93122. Similarly, we have declined to require the presentation of exculpatory evidence, see Williams, 504 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 51, and we have allowed the introduction of hearsay alone, see Costello, 350 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 362\xe2\x80\x93364. On each occasion, we relied on the same reasoning, stemming from our recognition that probable cause served only a gateway function: Given the relatively undemanding \xe2\x80\x9cnature of the determination,\xe2\x80\x9d the value of requiring any additional \xe2\x80\x9cformalities and safeguards\xe2\x80\x9d would \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n most cases .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. be too slight.\xe2\x80\x9d Gerstein, 420 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 121\xe2\x80\x93122.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We can come out no differently here. The probable cause determinations the Kaleys contest are simply those underlying the charges in the indictment. No doubt the Kaleys could seek to poke holes in the evidence the Government offered the grand jury to support those allegations. No doubt, too, the Kaleys could present evidence of their own, which might cast the Government\xe2\x80\x99s in a different light. (Presumably, the Kaleys would try in those two ways to show that they did not steal, but instead lawfully obtained the medical devices they later resold. See supra, at 4.) Our criminal justice system of course relies on such contestation at trial when the question becomes whether a defendant is guilty beyond peradventure. But as we have held before, an adversarial process is far less useful to the threshold finding of probable cause, which determines only whether adequate grounds exist to proceed to trial and reach that question. The probable cause decision, by its nature, is hard to undermine, and still harder to reverse. So the likelihood that a judge holding an evidentiary hearing will repudiate the grand jury\xe2\x80\x99s decision strikes us, once more, as \xe2\x80\x9ctoo slight\xe2\x80\x9d to support a constitutional requirement. Gerstein, 420 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 122.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The evidence from other courts corroborates that view, over and over and over again. In the past two decades, the courts in several Circuits have routinely held the kind of hearing the Kaleys seek. See supra, at 3, and n.\xc2\xa04. Yet neither the Kaleys nor their amici (mostly lawyers\xe2\x80\x99 associations) have found a single case in which a judge found an absence of probable cause to believe that an indicted defendant committed the crime charged. One amicus cites 25 reported cases involving pre-trial hearings on asset freezes. See Brief for New York Council of Defense Lawyers 4, n.\xc2\xa02. In 24 of those, the defendant lost outright. The last involved a not-yet-indicted defendant (so no grand jury finding); there, the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling for him was reversed on appeal. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 15, 36. To be sure, a kind of selection bias might affect those statistics: Perhaps a prosecutor with a very weak case would choose to abandon an asset freeze rather than face a difficult hearing. See id., at 16, 37. But the Kaleys and their amici have also failed to offer any anecdotes of that kind; and we suspect that the far more common reason a prosecutor relinquishes a freeze is just to avoid premature discovery. See supra, at 14\xe2\x80\x9315. So experience, as far as anyone has discerned it, cuts against the Kaleys: It confirms that even under Mathews, they have no right to revisit the grand jury\xe2\x80\x99s finding.[14]

\n

IV

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n When we decided Monsanto, we effectively resolved this case too. If the question in a pre-trial forfeiture case is whether there is probable cause to think the defendant committed the crime alleged, then the answer is: whatever the grand jury decides. And even if we test that proposition by applying Mathews, we arrive at the same place: In considering such findings of probable cause, we have never thought the value of enhanced evidentiary procedures worth their costs. Congress of course may strike its own balance and give defendants like the Kaleys the kind of hearing they want. Indeed, Congress could disapprove of Monsanto itself and hold pre-trial seizures of property to a higher standard than probable cause. But the Due Process Clause, even when combined with a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s\n \nSixth Amendment interests, does not command those results. Accordingly, the Kaleys cannot challenge the grand jury\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that probable cause supports the charges against them. The grand jury gets the final word.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We therefore affirm the judgment of the Eleventh Circuit and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

It is so ordered.

But the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s argument about what is at stake for the Kaleys says nothing about the crucial, last prong of , which asks whether and to what extent the adversarial procedures they request will in fact correct any grand jury errors. That part of the analysis is what requires our decision, and the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s view that the Government overreached in this particular case cannot overcome it.\n\t\n

KERRI L. KALEY, et vir, PETITIONERS v. UNITEDSTATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

[February 25, 2014]

\n

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Chief Justice Roberts, with whom Justice Breyer and Justice Sotomayor join, dissenting.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n An individual facing serious criminal charges brought by the United States has little but the Constitution and his attorney standing between him and prison. He might readily give all he owns to defend himself.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We have held, however, that the Government may effectively remove a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s primary weapon of defense\xe2\x80\x94the attorney he selects and trusts\xe2\x80\x94by freezing assets he needs to pay his lawyer. That ruling is not at issue. But today the Court goes further, holding that a defendant may be hobbled in this way without an opportunity to challenge the Government\xe2\x80\x99s decision to freeze those needed assets. I cannot subscribe to that holding and respectfully dissent.

\n

I

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The facts of this case are important. They highlight the significance to a defendant of being able to hire his counsel of choice, and the potential for unfairness inherent in giving the prosecutor the discretion to take that right away. Kerri Kaley worked as a sales representative for a Johnson & Johnson subsidiary, selling prescription medical devices. Kaley and other sales representatives occasionally obtained outmoded or surplus devices from staff members at the medical facilities they served, when, for example, those devices were no longer needed because they had been superseded by newer models. Kaley sold the unwanted devices to a Florida company, dividing the proceeds among the sales representatives.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Kaley learned in January 2005 that a federal grand jury was investigating those activities as a conspiracy to sell stolen prescription medical devices. Kaley and her husband (who allegedly helped ship the products to Florida) retained counsel, who immediately set to work preparing their defense against any impending charges. Counsel regularly discussed the investigation with the Kaleys, helped review documents demanded by the grand jury, and met with prosecutors in an attempt to ward off an indictment. Nonetheless preparing for the worst, the Kaleys applied for a $500,000 equity line of credit on their home to pay estimated legal fees associated with a trial. They used that money to purchase a $500,000 certificate of deposit, which they set aside until it would be needed to pay their attorneys for the trial.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In February 2007, the grand jury returned a seven-count indictment charging the Kaleys and another sales representative, Jennifer Gruenstrass, with violations of federal law. The indictment alleged that a \xe2\x80\x9cmoney judgment\xe2\x80\x9d of over $2 million and the $500,000 certificate of deposit were subject to forfeiture under\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7981(a)(1)(C) because those assets constituted \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d of the alleged crimes. Armed with this indictment, the prosecution obtained an ex\xc2\xa0parte order pursuant to\n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7853(e), thereby freezing all of the Kaleys\xe2\x80\x99 assets listed in the indictment, including the certificate of deposit set aside for legal fees. The Government did not seek to freeze any of Gruenstrass\xe2\x80\x99s assets.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Kaleys moved to vacate the order, requesting a hearing at which they could argue that there was no probable cause to believe their assets were forfeitable, because their alleged conduct was not criminal. They argued they were entitled to such a hearing because the restraining order targeted funds they needed and had set aside to retain for trial the same counsel who had been preparing their defense for two years. And they contended that the prosecution was baseless because the Government could not identify anyone who claimed ownership of the medical devices alleged to have been \xe2\x80\x9cstolen.\xe2\x80\x9d During a telephone conference with a Magistrate Judge on the motion, the prosecution conceded that it had been able to trace only $140,000 in allegedly criminal proceeds to the Kaleys, which led the Magistrate Judge to question the lawfulness of restraining the listed assets.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Just two business days after that conference, the Government obtained a superseding indictment that added a count of conspiracy to commit money laundering under\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71956(h). Adding that charge enabled the Government to proceed under a much broader forfeiture provision than the one in the original indictment. While the civil forfeiture provision in \xc2\xa7981(a)(1)(C) authorized forfeit- ure of property that \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutes or is derived from proceeds traceable to\xe2\x80\x9d a qualifying criminal violation, the criminal forfeiture provision now invoked by the Government\xe2\x80\x94\xc2\xa7982(a)(1)\xe2\x80\x94authorizes forfeiture of property \xe2\x80\x9cinvolved in\xe2\x80\x9d a qualifying offense, or \xe2\x80\x9cany property traceable to such property.\xe2\x80\x9d The superseding indictment alleged that a sum of more than $2 million, the certificate of deposit reserved to pay legal expenses, and now the Kaleys\xe2\x80\x99 home were subject to forfeiture. And again, the Government sought an order freezing substantially all those assets.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Kaleys objected, repeating the arguments they had previously raised, and also contending that the prosecutors were being vindictive in adding the money laundering charge and seeking broader forfeiture. The District Court nonetheless entered the broader order requested by the Government, and the restraint on the Kaleys\xe2\x80\x99 assets remains in place.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n While the Kaleys\xe2\x80\x99 appeal from that denial was pending, the Government proceeded to trial separately against their codefendant Gruenstrass. As the Government had not sought to freeze Gruenstrass\xe2\x80\x99s assets, she was represented by her chosen counsel. Her counsel argued that the Government was pitching a fraud without a victim, because no Government witness took the stand to claim ownership of the allegedly stolen devices. The jury acquitted Gruenstrass on all charges in less than three hours\xe2\x80\x94a good omen for the Kaleys and their counsel as they prepared for their own trial.

\n

II

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The issues at stake here implicate fundamental constitutional principles. The\n \nSixth Amendment provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n all criminal prosecutions, the accused shall enjoy the right .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to have the Assistance of Counsel for his defence.\xe2\x80\x9d In many ways, this is the most precious right a defendant has, because it is his attorney who will fight for the other rights the defendant enjoys. United States v. Cronic,\n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 648\n\xe2\x80\x93654 (1984). And more than 80 years ago, we found it \xe2\x80\x9chardly necessary to say that, the right to counsel being conceded, a defendant should be afforded a fair opportunity to secure counsel of his own choice.\xe2\x80\x9d Powell v. Alabama,\n\n287 U.\xc2\xa0S. 45,\n53 (1932)\n.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Indeed, we recently called the \xe2\x80\x9cright to select counsel of one\xe2\x80\x99s choice . . . . the root meaning of the constitutional guarantee\xe2\x80\x9d of the\n \nSixth Amendment. United States v. Gonzalez-Lopez,\n\n548 U.\xc2\xa0S. 140\n\xe2\x80\x93148 (2006). The Amendment requires \xe2\x80\x9cthat a particular guarantee of fair-ness be provided\xe2\x80\x94to wit, that the accused be defended by the counsel he believes to be best.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 146. An individual\xe2\x80\x99s right to counsel of choice is violated \xe2\x80\x9cwhenever the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s choice is wrongfully denied,\xe2\x80\x9d and such error \xe2\x80\x9cpervades the entire trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 150. A violation of this right is therefore a \xe2\x80\x9cstructural error,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid.; that is, one of the very few kinds of errors that \xe2\x80\x9cundermine the fairness of a criminal proceeding as a whole.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Davila, 569 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2013) (slip op., at 12).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n It is of course true that the right to counsel of choice is (like most rights) not absolute. A defendant has no right to choose counsel he cannot afford, counsel who is not a member of the bar, or counsel with an impermissible conflict of interest. Wheat v. United States,\n\n486 U.\xc2\xa0S. 153,\n159 (1988)\n. And a district court need not always shuffle its calendar to accommodate a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s preferred counsel if it has legitimate reasons not to do so. Morris v. Slappy,\n\n461 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n\xe2\x80\x9312 (1983). But none of those limitations is imposed at the unreviewable discretion of a prosecutor\xe2\x80\x94the party who wants the defendant to lose at trial.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This Court has held that the prosecution may freeze assets a defendant needs to retain his counsel of choice upon \xe2\x80\x9ca finding of probable cause to believe that the assets are forfeitable.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Monsanto,\n\n491 U.\xc2\xa0S. 600,\n615 (1989)\n. The Kaleys do not challenge that holding here. But the Court in Monsanto acknowledged and reserved the crucial question whether a defendant had the right to be heard before the Government could take such action. Id., at 615, n. 10.[1]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n There was good reason for that caution. The possibility that a prosecutor could elect to hamstring his target by preventing him from paying his counsel of choice raises substantial concerns about the fairness of the entire proceeding. \xe2\x80\x9cA fair trial in a fair tribunal is a basic requirement of due process.\xe2\x80\x9d In re Murchison,\n\n349 U.\xc2\xa0S. 133,\n136 (1955)\n. Issues concerning the denial of counsel of choice implicate the overall fairness of the trial because they \xe2\x80\x9cbear[\xc2\xa0] directly on the \xe2\x80\x98framework within which the trial proceeds.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Gonzalez-Lopez, supra, at 150 (quoting Arizona v. Fulminante,\n\n499 U.\xc2\xa0S. 279,\n310 (1991)\n).

\n

III

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Notwithstanding the substantial constitutional issues at stake, the majority believes that syllogistic-type reasoning effectively resolves this case. Ante, at 12. The majority\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning goes like this: First, to freeze assets prior to trial, the Government must show probable cause to believe that a defendant has committed an offense giving rise to forfeiture. Second, grand jury determinations of probable cause are nonreviewable. Therefore, the Kaleys cannot \xe2\x80\x9crelitigate [the] grand jury finding\xe2\x80\x9d of probable cause to avoid a pretrial restraint of assets they need to retain their counsel of choice. Ibid. I do not view the matter as nearly so \xe2\x80\x9cstraightforward,\xe2\x80\x9d and neither did the multiple Courts of Appeals since Monsanto that have granted defendants the type of hearing the Kaleys request. See ante, at 3, n.\xc2\xa04.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n To begin with, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion is wrong on its own terms. To freeze assets prior to trial, the Government must show probable cause to believe both that (1) a defendant has committed an offense giving rise to forfeiture and (2) the targeted assets have the requisite connection to the alleged criminal conduct.\n\n21 U. S. C. \xc2\xa7853(e)(1)(A). The Solicitor General concedes\xe2\x80\x94and all Courts of Appeals to have considered the issue have held\xe2\x80\x94that \xe2\x80\x9cdefendants are entitled to show that the assets that are restrained are not actually the proceeds of the charged criminal offense,\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 45; that is, that the second prong of the required showing is not satisfied. But by listing prop-erty in the indictment and alleging that it is subject to forfeiture\xe2\x80\x94as required to restrain assets before trial under \xc2\xa7853(e)(1)(A)\xe2\x80\x94the grand jury found probable cause to believe those assets were linked to the charged offenses, just as it found probable cause to believe the Kaleys committed the underlying crimes. App. 60\xe2\x80\x9361 (separate indictment section alleging criminal forfeiture, including of the certificate of deposit); see United States v. Jones, 160 F.\xc2\xa03d 641, 645 (CA10 1998); United States v. Monsanto, 924 F.\xc2\xa02d 1186, 1197 (CA2 1991) (en banc); Dept. of Justice, Asset Forfeiture Policy Manual 128 (2013) (\xe2\x80\x9cThat the indictment alleges that property is subject to forfeiture indicates that the grand jury has made a probable cause determination.\xe2\x80\x9d). Neither the Government nor the major-ity gives any reason why the District Court may reconsider the grand jury\xe2\x80\x99s probable cause finding as to traceability\xe2\x80\x94and in fact constitutionally must, if asked\xe2\x80\x94but may not do so as to the underlying charged offenses.[2]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In any event, the hearing the Kaleys seek would not be mere relitigation of the grand jury proceedings. At that hearing, the District Court would consider the merits of the prosecution to determine whether there is probable cause to believe the Kaleys\xe2\x80\x99 assets are forfeitable, not to determine whether the Kaleys may be tried at all. If the judge agrees with the Kaleys, he will merely hold that the Government has not met its burden at that hearing to justify freezing the assets the Kaleys need to pay their attorneys. The Government may proceed with the prosecution, but the Kaleys will have their chosen counsel at their side.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Even though the probable cause standard applies at both the indictment stage and the pretrial asset restraint hearing, the judge\xe2\x80\x99s determination will be based on different evidence than that previously presented to the grand jury. For its part, the Government may choose to put on more or less evidence at the hearing than it did before the grand jury. And of course the Kaleys would have the opportunity to tell their side of the story\xe2\x80\x94something the grand jury never hears. See United States v. Williams,\n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 36\n\xe2\x80\x9352 (1992). Here, much of what the Kaleys want to present comes from Gruenstrass\xe2\x80\x99s trial\xe2\x80\x94evidence that the grand jury obviously could not have considered. So even if the judge determined that probable cause to justify the pretrial asset restraint had not been adequately established, that determination would not in any way amount to \xe2\x80\x9clooking into and revising the judgment of the grand jury upon the evidence, for the purpose of determining whether or not the finding was founded upon sufficient proof.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 7 (quoting Costello v. United States,\n\n350 U.\xc2\xa0S. 359\n\xe2\x80\x93363 (1956) (internal quotation marks omitted)). The judge\xe2\x80\x99s decision based on the evidence presented at the hearing would have no necessary legal or logical consequence for the underlying prosecution because it would be based on different evidence and used for a different purpose.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The majority warns that allowing a judge to consider the underlying merits of the prosecution for purposes of determining whether a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s assets may be restrained pretrial could create \xe2\x80\x9clegal dissonance\xe2\x80\x9d with the grand jury\xe2\x80\x99s indictment, which \xe2\x80\x9ccould not but undermine the criminal justice system\xe2\x80\x99s integrity.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311. But as explained, such a judicial finding based on different evidence with both sides present would not contradict the grand jury\xe2\x80\x99s probable cause finding based on what was before it. That finding would still suffice to accomplish its purpose\xe2\x80\x94to call for a trial on the merits of the charges. Rather than creating \xe2\x80\x9cdissonance,\xe2\x80\x9d the traditional roles of the principal actors in our justice system would remain respected: The grand jury decides whether a defendant should be required to stand trial, the judge decides pre-trial matters and how the trial should proceed, and the jury decides whether the defendant is guilty of the crime.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Indeed, in the bail context\xe2\x80\x94the pretrial determination that is perhaps the closest analogue to the pretrial restraint of assets at issue here\xe2\x80\x94we allow judicial inquiries into the underlying merits of the indicted charges, without concern about intruding into the province of the grand jury. An indictment charging sufficiently serious crimes gives rise to a rebuttable presumption that a defend-ant is not eligible for pretrial release. See 18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73142(e)(3) and (f). Such a defendant is nonetheless entitled to an evidentiary hearing at which he may contest (among other things) \xe2\x80\x9cthe weight of the evidence against\xe2\x80\x9d him, \xc2\xa73142(g)(2). Yet no one would say that the district court encroached on the grand jury\xe2\x80\x99s role if the court de-termined that it would not authorize pretrial detention because of the weakness of the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s case. See, e.g., United States v. Hurtado, 779 F. 2d 1467, 1479\xe2\x80\x931480 (CA11 1985) (recognizing that in considering the \xe2\x80\x9cweight of the evidence\xe2\x80\x9d to decide whether the presumption is rebutted, \xe2\x80\x9cit may well be necessary to open up the issue of probable cause since that too is a question of evidentiary weight\xe2\x80\x9d). That makes sense, because the district court has considered the underlying merits of the charges based on different information and for a different purpose than the grand jury did. Such a defendant would be granted pretrial release, but would still have to show up for trial.[3]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In any event, few things could do more to \xe2\x80\x9cundermine the criminal justice system\xe2\x80\x99s integrity,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 11, than to allow the Government to initiate a prosecution and then, at its option, disarm its presumptively innocent opponent by depriving him of his counsel of choice\xe2\x80\x94without even an opportunity to be heard. That is the result of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in this case, and it is fundamentally at odds with our constitutional tradition and basic notions of fair play.

\n

IV

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The majority is no more persuasive in applying the due process balancing test set forth in Mathews v. Eldridge,\n\n424 U.\xc2\xa0S. 319 (1976)\n.[4] As an initial matter, the majority gives short shrift to the Kaleys\xe2\x80\x99 interests at stake. \xe2\x80\x9cThe presumption of innocence, although not articulated in the Constitution, is a basic component of a fair trial under our system of criminal justice.\xe2\x80\x9d Estelle v. Williams,\n\n425 U.\xc2\xa0S. 501,\n503 (1976)\n. Whatever serious crimes the grand jury alleges the Kaleys committed, they are presumptively innocent of those charges until final judgment. Their right to vindicate that presumption by choosing the advocate they believe will best defend them is, as explained, at the very core of the\n \nSixth Amendment.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n I suspect that, for the Kaleys, that right could hardly be more precious than it is now. In addition to potentially losing the property the Government has already frozen\xe2\x80\x94including their home\xe2\x80\x94the Kaleys face maximum prison terms of five years (\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7371), ten years (\xc2\xa72314), and 20 years (\xc2\xa71956(h)) for the charges in the superseding indictment. The indictment means they must stand trial on those charges. But the Kaleys plainly have an urgent interest in having their chosen counsel\xe2\x80\x94who has worked with them since the grand jury\xe2\x80\x99s investigation began, two years before the indictment\xe2\x80\x94mount their best possible defense at trial.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The majority alludes to our cases recognizing that indictments may result in the temporary deprivation of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s liberty without judicial review, and suggests that indictments therefore must also be \xe2\x80\x9cgood enough\xe2\x80\x9d to deprive a defendant of property without judicial review. Ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310. Even if this greater-includes-the-lesser reasoning might be valid in other contexts, it is not when the property at issue is needed to hire chosen counsel. In the context of a prosecution for serious crimes, it is far from clear which interest is greater\xe2\x80\x94the interest in temporary liberty pending trial, or the interest in using one\xe2\x80\x99s available means to avoid imprisonment for many years after trial. Retaining one\xe2\x80\x99s counsel of choice ensures the fundamental fairness of the actual trial, and thus may be far more valuable to a criminal defendant than pretrial release.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n As for the Government\xe2\x80\x99s side, the Court echoes the Government\xe2\x80\x99s concerns that a hearing would place demands on its resources and interfere with its desire to keep its trial strategy close to the vest. These concerns are somewhat curious in light of the majority\xe2\x80\x99s emphasis on how easy it is to make a probable cause showing. And they are even more surprising in light of the extensive discovery obligations already imposed on the Government by Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 16 and Brady v. Maryland,\n\n373 U.\xc2\xa0S. 83 (1963)\n. The emphasis the Government places on pretrial secrecy evokes an outdated conception of the criminal trial as \xe2\x80\x9ca poker game in which players enjoy an absolute right always to conceal their cards until played.\xe2\x80\x9d Williams v. Florida,\n\n399 U.\xc2\xa0S. 78,\n82 (1970)\n.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Moreover, recall that the Government concedes that due process guarantees defendants a hearing to contest the traceability of the restrained assets to the charged conduct. If a defendant requests such a hearing, the Government will likely be required to reveal something about its case to demonstrate that the assets have the requisite connection to the charged offenses.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In any event, these concerns are exaggerated. What the Government would be required to show in a pretrial restraint hearing is similar to pretrial showings prosecutors make in other contexts on a daily basis. As mentioned above, when the Government seeks an order detaining a defendant pending trial, it routinely makes an extensive evidentiary showing\xe2\x80\x94voluntarily disclosing much of its evidence and trial strategy\xe2\x80\x94in support of that relief. See Brief for California Attorneys for Criminal Justice as Amicus Curiae 11\xe2\x80\x9318. The Government makes similar showings in the context of other pretrial motions, such as motions to admit hearsay evidence under the co-conspirator exception, or to discover attorney-client communications made in furtherance of a future crime. Id., at 19\xe2\x80\x9328.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In those contexts, as in this one, the decision how much to \xe2\x80\x9cshow its hand\xe2\x80\x9d rests fully within the Government\xe2\x80\x99s discretion. If it has a strong case and believes that pretrial restraint is necessary to preserve the assets for forfeit-ure, the Government may choose to make a strong evi-dentiary showing and have little concern about doing so. In a closer case, where the Government is more concerned about tipping its hand, it may elect to forgo a pretrial restraint of those assets the defendant needs to pay his counsel. I see no great burden on the Government in allowing it to strike this balance as it sees fit when considering a pretrial asset restraint that would deprive a defendant of his right to counsel of choice. In the end, it is a bit much to argue that the Government has discretion to deprive a defendant\xe2\x80\x94without a hearing\xe2\x80\x94of the counsel he has chosen to present his defense, simply to avoid the mere possibility of a premature peek at some aspect of what the Government intends to do at trial.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The majority also significantly underestimates the amount of control judges can exercise in these types of hearings. The Circuits that allow such hearings have afforded judges a great deal of flexibility in structuring them. Judges need not apply the Federal Rules of Evidence during the hearings, and they can take many steps, including in camera proceedings, to ensure that witness safety and grand jury secrecy are fully preserved. See Monsanto, 924 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 1198; United States v. E-Gold, Ltd., 521 F.\xc2\xa03d 411, 418\xe2\x80\x93419 (CADC 2008).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Moreover, experience in the Second Circuit, where defendants have for more than 20 years been afforded the type of hearing the Kaleys seek, indicates that such hearings do not occur so often as to raise substantial concerns about taxing the resources of the Government and lower courts. See Brief for New York Council of Defense Lawyers as Amicus Curiae 4\xe2\x80\x939. As the majority notes, only 25 reported cases appear to have addressed such hearings. Id., at 4. This relative rarity is unsurprising. To even be entitled to the hearing, defendants must first show a genuine need to use the assets to retain counsel of choice. See United States v. Bonventre, 720 F.\xc2\xa03d 126, 131 (CA2 2013). And defendants too have an incentive not to tip their hands as to trial strategy\xe2\x80\x94perhaps to an even greater extent than the Government, given that defendants bear comparatively few discovery obligations at a criminal trial. In light of the low bar of the probable cause standard, many defendants likely conclude that the possible benefits of the hearing are not worth the candle.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n For those hearings that do occur, they are by all appearances ably controlled by district judges to keep them manageable and to limit the potential for excess or abuse. See Brief for New York Council of Defense Lawyers as Amicus Curiae 6\xe2\x80\x938. In addition, where such hearings are allowed, prosecutors and defense counsel often reach agreements concerning the scope and conditions of any protective order that accommodate the interests of both sides. See id., at 8\xe2\x80\x939. When the right at stake is as fundamental as hiring one\xe2\x80\x99s counsel of choice\xe2\x80\x94the \xe2\x80\x9croot meaning\xe2\x80\x9d of the\n \nSixth Amendment, Gonzalez-Lopez, 548 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 147\xe2\x80\x93148\xe2\x80\x94the Government\xe2\x80\x99s interest in saving the time and expense of a limited number of such proceedings is not particularly compelling.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Government does have legitimate interests that are served by forfeiture of allegedly tainted assets. Caplin & Drysdale, Chartered v. United States,\n\n491 U.\xc2\xa0S. 617,\n629 (1989)\n. And imposing a pretrial restraint on such assets does increase the likelihood that they will be available if the defendant is convicted.[5] But that interest is protected in other ways that mitigate the concern that defendants will successfully divert forfeitable assets from the Government\xe2\x80\x99s reach if afforded a hearing. The relation-back provision in\n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7853(c) provides that title to forfeitable assets, once adjudged forfeitable, vests in the Government as of the time the offense was committed. Section 853(c) then provides that the Government may seek a \xe2\x80\x9cspecial verdict of forfeiture\xe2\x80\x9d as to any forfeited property that was subsequently transferred to a third party. The Government protests that recovery of such assets will often be complicated and subject to the vagaries of state law. Tr. of Oral Arg. 49\xe2\x80\x9350. But such complaints of administrative inconvenience carry little weight in this particular context, when the Government knows exactly where the money has gone: to an attorney who is, after all, an officer of the court, and on notice that the Government claims title to the assets.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n And we are not talking about all of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s assets that are subject to forfeiture\xe2\x80\x94only those that the defendant can show are necessary to secure his counsel of choice. Here, for example, the Kaleys have identified as needed to pay counsel only a discrete portion of the assets the Government seeks. The statistics cited by the Court on the total amount of assets recovered by the Government and provided as restitution for victims, ante, at 2, n.\xc2\xa01, are completely beside the point.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The majority ultimately concludes that a pretrial hearing of the sort the Kaleys seek would be a waste of time. Ante, at 17\xe2\x80\x9320. No. It takes little imagination to see that seizures based entirely on ex\xc2\xa0parte proceedings create a heightened risk of error. Common sense tells us that secret decisions based on only one side of the story will prove inaccurate more often than those made after hearing from both sides. We have thus consistently recognized that the \xe2\x80\x9cfundamental instrument for judicial judgment\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9can adversary proceeding in which both parties may participate.\xe2\x80\x9d Carroll v. President and Comm\xe2\x80\x99rs of Princess Anne,\n\n393 U. S. 175,\n183 (1968)\n. In the present context, some defendants (like the Kaleys) may be able to show that the theory of prosecution is legally defective through an argument that almost certainly was not presented to the grand jury. And as discussed above, supra, at 13\xe2\x80\x9315, prosecutors in some cases elect not to freeze needed assets, or they negotiate tailored protective orders to serve the interests of both sides\xe2\x80\x94something they would be unlikely to do if the hearings were rote exercises.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Given the risk of an erroneous restraint of assets needed to retain chosen counsel, the \xe2\x80\x9cprobable value\xe2\x80\x9d of the \xe2\x80\x9cadditional safeguard\xe2\x80\x9d a pretrial hearing would provide is significant. That is because the right to counsel of choice is inherently transient, and the deprivation of that right effectively permanent. In our cases suggesting that little would be gained by requiring an adversary hearing on probable cause or imposing stricter evidentiary requirements in grand jury proceedings, we have noted that the grand jury is not where the ultimate question of \xe2\x80\x9cthe guilt or innocence of the accused is adjudicated.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Calandra,\n\n414 U.\xc2\xa0S. 338,\n343 (1974)\n; see United States v. Williams,\n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 36,\n51 (1992)\n (explaining that the grand jury hears only from the prosecutor because \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the finding of an indictment is only in the nature of an enquiry or accusation, which is afterwards to be tried and determined\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting 4 W. Blackstone, Commentaries 300 (1769)). If the grand jury considers incomplete or incompetent evidence in deciding to return an indictment, the defendant still has the full trial on the merits, with all its \xe2\x80\x9cformalities and safeguards,\xe2\x80\x9d Gerstein v. Pugh,\n\n420 U.\xc2\xa0S. 103,\n122 (1975)\n, to prove his innocence.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Here, by contrast, the Government seeks to use the grand jury\xe2\x80\x99s probable cause determination to strip the Kaleys of their counsel of choice. The Kaleys can take no comfort that they will be able to vindicate that right in a future adversarial proceeding. Once trial begins with someone other than chosen counsel, the right is lost, and it cannot be restored based on what happens at trial. \xe2\x80\x9cThe fundamental requirement of due process is the opportunity to be heard \xe2\x80\x98at a meaningful time and in a meaningful manner.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Mathews, 424 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 333 (quoting Armstrong v. Manzo,\n\n380 U.\xc2\xa0S. 545,\n552 (1965)\n). If the Kaleys are to have any opportunity to meaningfully challenge that deprivation, they must have it before the trial begins.

\n

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The issues presented here implicate some of the most fundamental precepts underlying the American criminal justice system. A person accused by the United States of committing a crime is presumed innocent until proven guilty beyond a reasonable doubt. But he faces a foe of powerful might and vast resources, intent on seeing him behind bars. That individual has the right to choose the advocate he believes will most ably defend his liberty at trial.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The trial is governed by rules designed to ensure that, whatever the ultimate verdict, we can be confident to the extent possible that justice was done, within the bounds of the Constitution. That confidence is grounded in our belief in the adversary system. \xe2\x80\x9cThe very premise of our adversary system of criminal justice is that partisan advocacy on both sides of a case will best promote the ultimate objective that the guilty be convicted and the innocent go free.\xe2\x80\x9d Herring v. New York,\n\n422 U.\xc2\xa0S. 853,\n862 (1975)\n. Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision erodes that confidence by permitting the Government to deprive a criminal defendant of his right to counsel of choice, without so much as a chance to be heard on why such a significant pretrial deprivation is unwarranted.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The majority wraps up its analysis by blandly noting that Congress is of course free to extend broader protection to criminal defendants. Ante, at 20. Not very likely. In this area it is to the courts that those charged with crime must turn.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Federal prosecutors, when they rise in court, represent the people of the United States. But so do defense lawyers\xe2\x80\x94one at a time. In my view, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion pays insufficient respect to the importance of an independent bar as a check on prosecutorial abuse and government overreaching. Granting the Government the power to take away a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s chosen advocate strikes at the heart of that significant role. I would not do it, and so respectfully dissent.
