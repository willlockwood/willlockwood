\n

SIMMONS et\xc2\xa0al. v. HIMMELREICH

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x93109.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 22, 2016\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 6, 2016

\n

This case began with two suits filed by respondent Walter Himmelreich, a federal prisoner. He first filed suit against the United States, alleging that a severe beating he received from a fellow inmate was the result of negligence by prison officials. The Government treated the suit as a claim under the Federal Tort Claims Act (FTCA), which allows plaintiffs to seek damages from the United States for certain torts committed by federal employees,\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71346(b), \xe2\x80\x9c[s]ubject to the provisions of chapter 171\xe2\x80\x9d of Title 28. But an \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d section of the FTCA dictates that \xe2\x80\x9cthe provisions of [Chapter 171] and section 1346(b) of this title .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall not apply\xe2\x80\x9d to certain categories of claims. The Government moved to dismiss the action on the ground that the claim fell into the exception for \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny claim based upon .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the exercise or performance .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [of] a discretionary function,\xe2\x80\x9d namely, deciding where to house inmates, \xc2\xa72680(a). While the motion was pending, Himmelreich filed a second suit: a constitutional tort suit against individual Bureau of Prison employees, again alleging that his beating was the result of prison officials\xe2\x80\x99 negligence. Ordinarily, the FTCA would have no bearing on that claim. But after the dismissal of Himmelreich\xe2\x80\x99s first suit, the individual employee defendants argued that Himmelreich\xe2\x80\x99s second suit was foreclosed by the FTCA\xe2\x80\x99s judgment bar provision, according to which a judgment in an FTCA suit forecloses any future suit against individual employees. Agreeing, the District Court granted summary judgment in favor of the individual prison employees. The Sixth Circuit reversed, however, holding that the judgment bar provision did not apply to Himmelreich\xe2\x80\x99s suit.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The judgment bar provision does not apply to the claims dismissed for falling within the \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d section of the FTCA. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0The FTCA explicitly excepts from its coverage certain categories of claims, including the one into which Himmelreich\xe2\x80\x99s first suit fell. If, as the Government maintains, Chapter 171\xe2\x80\x99s judgment bar provision applies to claims in that \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d category, it applied to Himmelreich\xe2\x80\x99s first suit and would preclude any future actions, including his second suit. On Himmelreich\xe2\x80\x99s reading, however, the provision does not apply and he may proceed with his second suit. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0Himmelreich is correct. The FTCA\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d section reads: \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he provisions of this chapter\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94Chapter 171\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cshall not apply to .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [a]ny claim based upon .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the exercise or performance .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [of] a discretionary function or duty.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72680(a). The judgment bar is a provision of Chapter 171. The \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d section\xe2\x80\x99s plain text thus dictates that the judgment bar does \xe2\x80\x9cnot apply\xe2\x80\x9d to cases that, like Himmelreich\xe2\x80\x99s first suit, are based on the performance of a discretionary function. Because the judgment bar provision does not apply to Himmelreich\xe2\x80\x99s first suit, his second suit\xe2\x80\x94against individual prison employees\xe2\x80\x94should be permitted to go forward. Nothing about the \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d section or the judgment bar provision gives this Court any reason to disregard the plain text of the statute. P.\xc2\xa05.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0United States v. Smith,\n\n499 U.\xc2\xa0S. 160\n, does not require a different result. There, the Court found that the exclusive remedies provision of Chapter 171\xe2\x80\x94which prevents a plaintiff from suing an employee where the FTCA would allow him to sue the United States instead, see \xc2\xa72679(b)(1)\xe2\x80\x94applied to a claim for injuries sustained at a hospital in Italy, even though that claim fell within the category of \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny claim arising in a foreign country,\xe2\x80\x9d one of the \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d to which \xe2\x80\x9cthe provisions of [Chapter 171] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall not apply,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72680(k). Smith\xe2\x80\x99s outcome, the Government argues, forecloses a literal reading of the \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d provision, but Smith does not control here. First, Smith does not even mention the \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d section\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cshall not apply\xe2\x80\x9d language. Second, the exclusive remedies provision at issue there was enacted as part of the Federal Employee Liability Reform and Tort Compensation Act of 1988, which also contained a mechanism to convert tort suits against Government employees into FTCA suits \xe2\x80\x9csubject to the limitations and exceptions applicable to those actions.\xe2\x80\x9d 499 U. S., at 166 (quoting \xc2\xa72679(d)(4); emphasis in Smith). By taking note of those \xe2\x80\x9climitations and exceptions,\xe2\x80\x9d the Smith Court reasoned, the Liability Reform Act was intended to apply to the \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d categories of claims. Nothing in the text of the judgment bar provision compels the same result here. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

(d)\xc2\xa0The Government\xe2\x80\x99s remaining counterargument is a parade of horribles that it believes will come to pass if every provision of Chapter 171 \xe2\x80\x9cshall not apply\xe2\x80\x9d to the \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d categories of claims, but it raises few concerns about the judgment bar provision itself. If the Government is right about Chapter 171\xe2\x80\x99s other provisions, the Court may hold so in the appropriate case, see Smith, 499 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 175, but the reading adopted here yields utterly sensible results. Had the District Court in this case issued a judgment dismissing Himmelreich\xe2\x80\x99s first suit because, e.g., the prison employees were not negligent, it would make sense that the judgment bar provision would prevent a second suit against the employees. But where an FTCA claim is dismissed because it falls within one of the \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions,\xe2\x80\x9d the dismissal signals merely that the United States cannot be held liable for a particular claim; it has no logical bearing on whether an employee can be liable instead. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

766 F.\xc2\xa03d 576, affirmed and remanded.

\n

JERMAINE SIMMONS, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. WALTER J. HIMMELREICH

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

[June 6, 2016]

\n

\n

Justice Sotomayor delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

The Federal Tort Claims Act (FTCA) allows plaintiffs to seek damages from the United States for certain torts committed by federal employees. 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71346(b), 2674. Many of the FTCA\xe2\x80\x99s procedural provisions are contained in a single chapter of the United States Code, Chapter 171. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72671\xe2\x80\x932680. But an \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d section of the FTCA dictates that \xe2\x80\x9cthe provisions of [Chapter 171] . . . shall not apply\xe2\x80\x9d to certain categories of claims. At issue in this case is whether one of the \xe2\x80\x9cprovisions of [Chapter 171]\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94the so-called judgment bar provision, \xc2\xa72676\xe2\x80\x94might nonetheless apply to one of the excepted claims. We conclude it does not.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

This case began with two suits filed by Walter Himmelreich. In each, Himmelreich alleged that he had been severely beaten by a fellow inmate in federal prison and that the beating was the result of prison officials\xe2\x80\x99 negligence. At the time of the beating, Himmelreich was incarcerated for producing child pornography. His assailant had warned prison officials that he would \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98smash\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d a pedophile if given the opportunity but was nonetheless released into the general prison population, where he assaulted Himmelreich. App. 46.

\n

Himmelreich filed a first suit against the United States. The Government treated this first suit as a claim under the FTCA and moved to dismiss the action, arguing that the claim fell into one of the \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d to the FTCA for \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny claim based upon .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the exercise or performance .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [of] a discretionary function,\xe2\x80\x9d namely, deciding where to house inmates. \xc2\xa72680(a). The District Court granted the Government\xe2\x80\x99s motion to dismiss. (Neither party here challenges the outcome of that first suit.)

\n

But before the District Court dismissed that first suit, Himmelreich filed a second suit, this one a constitutional tort suit against individual Bureau of Prison employees rather than against the United States. Ordinarily, the FTCA would have nothing to say about such claims. But after the dismissal of Himmelreich\xe2\x80\x99s first suit, the individual employee defendants argued that Himmelreich\xe2\x80\x99s second suit was foreclosed by the FTCA\xe2\x80\x99s judgment bar provision, according to which a judgment in an FTCA suit forecloses any future suit against individual employees. See \xc2\xa72676. As relevant here, the District Court agreed and granted summary judgment in favor of the individual prison employees.

\n

Himmelreich appealed that ruling. The Sixth Circuit reversed, holding that the judgment bar provision did not apply to Himmelreich\xe2\x80\x99s suit. Himmelreich v. Federal Bureau of Prisons et al., 766 F.\xc2\xa03d 576 (2014) (per curiam).

\n

We granted certiorari to resolve a Circuit split on whether the judgment bar provision applies to suits that, like Himmelreich\xe2\x80\x99s, are dismissed as falling within an \xe2\x80\x9cExceptio[n]\xe2\x80\x9d to the FTCA.[1] 577 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015).

\n

B

\n

The FTCA\xe2\x80\x99s provisions are contained in two areas of the United States Code. One,\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71346(b), gives federal district courts exclusive jurisdiction over tort claims against the United States for the acts of its employees \xe2\x80\x9c[s]ubject to the provisions of chapter 171\xe2\x80\x9d of Title 28.[2] Chapter 171, in turn, is labeled \xe2\x80\x9cTort Claims Procedure\xe2\x80\x9d and comprises the remaining provisions of the FTCA. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72671\xe2\x80\x932680.

\n

Chapter 171 contains an array of provisions. Some provisions govern how FTCA claims are to be adjudicated. See, e.g., \xc2\xa72674 (specifying scope of United States\xe2\x80\x99 liability); \xc2\xa72675(a) (exhaustion requirement); \xc2\xa72678 (restricting attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees). Other provisions limit plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 remedies outside the FTCA. See, e.g., \xc2\xa72679(a) (cannot sue agency for claims within scope of FTCA); \xc2\xa72679(d)(1) (suit against federal employee acting within scope of employment automatically converted to FTCA action).

\n

The District Court in this case relied on one suchremedies-limiting provision of Chapter 171, the judgment bar provision.[3] See \xc2\xa72676. Under the judgment bar provision, once a plaintiff receives a judgment (favorable or not) in an FTCA suit, he generally cannot proceed with a suit against an individual employee based on the same underlying facts. The District Court below held that Himmelreich had received a judgment in the first suit (the FTCA suit against the United States) and so could not proceed with the second suit (the individual employee suit based on the same underlying facts).

\n

The FTCA explicitly excepts from its coverage certain categories of claims, including the one into which Himmelreich\xe2\x80\x99s first suit fell:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cExceptions

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe provisions of this chapter and section 1346(b) of this title shall not apply to\xe2\x80\x94

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(a) Any claim based upon .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the exercise or performance or the failure to exercise or perform a discretionary function or duty .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. whether or not the discretion involved be abused.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72680.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe provisions of this chapter\xe2\x80\x9d referenced in the first line are the provisions of Chapter 171. \xe2\x80\x9c[S]ection 1346(b) of this title\xe2\x80\x9d is the provision giving district courts FTCA jurisdiction. And the \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d to which those portions of the FTCA \xe2\x80\x9cshall not apply\xe2\x80\x9d are 13 categories of claims, such as any claim that\xe2\x80\x94like Himmelreich\xe2\x80\x99s first suit\xe2\x80\x94arises from the performance of a \xe2\x80\x9cdiscretionary function,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72680(a); \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny claim arising in a foreign country,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72680(k); and \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny claim arising from the activities of the Tennessee Valley Authority,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72680(l).

\n

Both parties agree that district courts do not have jurisdiction over claims that fall into one of the 13 categories of \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9csection 1346(b) of this title\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94the provision conferring jurisdiction on district courts\xe2\x80\x94does \xe2\x80\x9cnot apply\xe2\x80\x9d to such claims. Both parties also agree that at least one of \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he provisions of [Chapter 171]\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94the provision delimiting the United States\xe2\x80\x99 liability, \xc2\xa72674\xe2\x80\x94need \xe2\x80\x9cnot apply\xe2\x80\x9d to claims in the \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d categories because no court will have jurisdiction to hold the United States liable on such claims in any event.

\n

The parties disagree, however, about whether the judgment bar provision of Chapter 171 \xe2\x80\x9cshall not apply\xe2\x80\x9d to claims in one of the \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d categories. The Government maintains that the judgment bar provision does apply to such claims. In that case, it applied to Himmelreich\xe2\x80\x99s first suit and would preclude any future actions, including his second suit. Himmelreich urges that it does not apply. On that reading, there is no reason he cannot proceed with his second suit.

\n

II

\n

Himmelreich is correct. The \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d section of the FTCA reads: \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he provisions of this chapter\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94Chapter 171\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cshall not apply to .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [a]ny claim based upon .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the exercise or performance .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [of] a discretionary function or duty.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72680(a). The judgment bar is a provision of Chapter 171; the plain text of the \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d section therefore dictates that it does \xe2\x80\x9cnot apply\xe2\x80\x9d to cases that, like Himmelreich\xe2\x80\x99s first suit, are based on the performance of a discretionary function. Because the judgment bar provision does not apply to Himmelreich\xe2\x80\x99s first suit, Himmelreich\xe2\x80\x99s second suit\xe2\x80\x94the one against individual prison employees\xe2\x80\x94should be permitted to go forward.

\n

Absent persuasive indications to the contrary, we presume Congress says what it means and means what it says. Nothing about the \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d section or the judgment bar provision gives us any reason to doubt the plain-text result in this case.

\n

III

\n

A

\n

Given the clarity of the \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d section\xe2\x80\x99s command, a reader might be forgiven for wondering how there could be any confusion about the statute\xe2\x80\x99s operation. The main source of uncertainty on this score, the Government submits, is United States v. Smith,\n\n499 U. S. 160 (1991)\n. In Smith, we considered another provision of Chapter 171, the exclusive remedies provision. Id., at 162. Under the exclusive remedies provision, a plaintiff generally cannot sue an employee where the FTCA would allow him to sue the United States instead. See \xc2\xa72679(b)(1).[4]

\n

The Smith Court held that this exclusive remedies provision applied to a claim for injuries sustained at an Army hospital in Italy, even though that claim fell within the category of \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny claim arising in a foreign country,\xe2\x80\x9d one of the \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d to which \xe2\x80\x9cthe provisions of [Chapter 171] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall not apply.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72680(k). The Government argues that our literal reading of the \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d provision would foreclose Smith\xe2\x80\x99s outcome because the Smith Court applied a provision of Chapter 171 (the exclusive remedies provision) to a claim falling within one of the \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d categories (a claim arising in a foreign country). Smith, the Government argues, thus establishes that we cannot read the command of the \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d section literally and that the judgment bar provision therefore should apply to Himmelreich\xe2\x80\x99s discretionary function claim.

\n

The Government\xe2\x80\x99s position has some force. Nonetheless, Smith does not control this case. First, Smith does not even cite, let alone discuss, the \xe2\x80\x9cshall not apply\xe2\x80\x9d language \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d provision. Second, the exclusive remedies provision at issue in Smith was enacted as part of the Federal Employee Liability Reform and Tort Compensation Act of 1988, which contained a mechanism to reduce the number of tort suits against Government employees. As the Smith Court explained, if \xe2\x80\x9cthe Attorney General . . . certif[ies] that a Government employee named as defendant was acting within the scope of his employment when he committed the alleged tort,\xe2\x80\x9d the Liability Reform Act dictates that the United States be substituted as the sole defendant, and that the action \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98shall proceed in the same manner\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d as an FTCA action \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98and shall be subject to the limitations and exceptions applicable to those actions.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 499 U. S., at 166 (quoting \xc2\xa72679(d)(4)); (emphasis in Smith). The Smith Court held that the Liability Reform Act\xe2\x80\x99s reference to \xe2\x80\x9climitations and exceptions\xe2\x80\x9d was most naturally read to refer to the \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d section of the FTCA. And by taking note of the \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d section, the Smith court reasoned, the Liability Reform Act was intended to apply to those \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

In light of the unique language of the Liability Reform Act, Smith is distinguishable from this case. Nothing in the text of the judgment bar provision compels the same result.

\n

B

\n

The Government\xe2\x80\x99s remaining counterargument amounts to a parade of horribles that it believes will come to pass if every provision of Chapter 171 \xe2\x80\x9cshall not apply\xe2\x80\x9d to the \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d categories of claims. See Brief for Petitioners 52. If the Government is right about the other provisions of Chapter 171, the Court may hold so in the appropriate case. See Smith, 499 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 175. But this case deals only with the judgment bar provision, and, aside from a passing concern about duplicative litigation, the Government does not argue that any such cavalcade would follow if that provision does not apply to the excepted claims. It is enough for our purposes that the statute\xe2\x80\x99s clear directive would not lead to hard-to-explain results when applied to the judgment bar provision in particular.

\n

To the contrary, our holding that the judgment bar provision \xe2\x80\x9cshall not apply\xe2\x80\x9d to the categories of claims in the \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d section in fact allows the statute to operate in an utterly sensible manner. Ordinarily, the judgment bar provision prevents unnecessarily duplicative litigation. If the District Court in this case had issued a judgment dismissing Himmelreich\xe2\x80\x99s first suit because the prison employees were not negligent, because Himmelreich was not harmed, or because Himmelreich simply failed to prove his claim, it would make little sense to give Himmelreich a second bite at the money-damages apple by allowing suit against the employees: Himmelreich\xe2\x80\x99s first suit would have given him a fair chance to recover dam-ages for his beating.

\n

Where an FTCA claim is dismissed because it falls within one of the \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions,\xe2\x80\x9d by contrast, the judgment bar provision makes much less sense. The dismissal of a claim in the \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d section signals merely that the United States cannot be held liable for a particular claim; it has no logical bearing on whether an employee can be held liable instead.[5] To apply the judgment bar so as to foreclose a future suit against an employee thus would be passing strange.

\n

The Government\xe2\x80\x99s reading would yield another strange result. According to the Government, the viability of a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s meritorious suit against an individual employee should turn on the order in which the suits are filed (or the order in which the district court chooses to address motions). For example, had the District Court in this case addressed the individual employee suit first, there would be no FTCA judgment in the picture, and so the judgment bar provision would not affect the outcome of the suit. The Government\xe2\x80\x99s reading would thus encourage litigants to file suit against individual employees before suing the United States to avoid being foreclosed from recovery altogether. Yet this result is at odds with one of the FTCA\xe2\x80\x99s purposes, channeling liability away from individual employees and toward the United States. See Dalehite v. United States,\n\n346 U.\xc2\xa0S. 15,\n25 (1953)\n.

\n

We decline to ignore the text of the statute to achieve these imprudently restrictive results. Accordingly, we read \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he provisions of this chapter .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall not apply\xe2\x80\x9d as it was written. The judgment bar provision\xe2\x80\x94one of the \xe2\x80\x9cprovisions of this chapter\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94does not apply to the categories of claims in the \xe2\x80\x9cExceptions\xe2\x80\x9d sections of the FTCA. We therefore affirm the judgment of the Court of Appeals and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.
