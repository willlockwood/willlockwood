BRIDGE et\xc2\xa0al. v. PHOENIX BOND & INDEMNITY CO. et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the seventh circuit

\n

No. 07\xe2\x80\x93210.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 14, 2008\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 9, 2008

\n

Each year the Cook County Treasurer\xe2\x80\x99s Office holds a public auction to sell its tax liens on delinquent taxpayers\xe2\x80\x99 property. To prevent any one buyer from obtaining a disproportionate share of the liens, the county adopted the \xe2\x80\x9cSingle, Simultaneous Bidder Rule\xe2\x80\x9d (Rule), which requires each buyer to submit bids in its own name, prohibits a buyer from using \xe2\x80\x9capparent agents, employees, or related entities\xe2\x80\x9d to submit simultaneous bids for the same parcel, and requires a registered bidder to submit a sworn affidavit affirming its compliance with the Rule. Petitioners and respondents regularly participate in the tax sales. Respondents filed suit, alleging that petitioners fraudulently obtained a disproportionate share of liens by filing false compliance attestations. As relevant here, they claim that petitioners violated and conspired to violate the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act (RICO) through a pattern of racketeering activity involving mail fraud, which occurred when petitioners sent property owners various notices required by Illinois law. The District Court dismissed the RICO claims for lack of standing, finding that respondents were not protected by the mail fraud statute because they did not receive the alleged misrepresentations. Reversing, the Seventh Circuit based standing on the injury respondents suffered when they lost the chance to obtain more liens, and found that respondents had sufficiently alleged proximate cause because they were immediately injured by petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 scheme. The court also rejected petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 argument that respondents are not entitled to relief under RICO because they had not received, and therefore had not relied on, any false statements.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0A plaintiff asserting a RICO claim predicated on mail fraud need not show, either as an element of its claim or as a prerequisite to establishing proximate causation, that it relied on the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s alleged misrepresentations. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9321.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0In 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71964(c), RICO provides a private right of action for treble damages to \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny person injured in his business or property by reason of a violation,\xe2\x80\x9d as pertinent here, of \xc2\xa71962(c), which makes it \xe2\x80\x9cunlawful for any person employed by or associated with\xe2\x80\x9d a qualifying enterprise \xe2\x80\x9cto conduct or participate \xe2\x80\xa6 in the conduct of such enterprise\xe2\x80\x99s affairs through a pattern of racketeering activity,\xe2\x80\x9d including \xe2\x80\x9cmail fraud,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71961(1)(B). Mail fraud, in turn, occurs whenever a person, \xe2\x80\x9chaving devised or intending to devise any scheme or artifice to defraud,\xe2\x80\x9d uses the mail \xe2\x80\x9cfor the purpose of executing such scheme or artifice.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71341. The gravamen of the offense is the scheme to defraud, and any \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98mailing \xe2\x80\xa6 incident to an essential part of the scheme\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\xa6 satisfies the mailing element,\xe2\x80\x9d Schmuck v. United States, 489 U. S. 705, 712, even if the mailing \xe2\x80\x9ccontain[s] no false information,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 715. Once the relationship among these statutory provisions is understood, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 theory of the case is straightforward. Petitioners nonetheless argue that because the alleged pattern of racketeering activity is predicated on mail fraud, respondents must show that they relied on petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 fraudulent misrepresentations, which they cannot do because the misrepresentations were made to the county. Nothing on the statute\xe2\x80\x99s face imposes such a requirement. Using the mail to execute or attempt to execute a scheme to defraud is indictable as mail fraud, and hence a predicate racketeering act under RICO, even if no one relied on any misrepresentation, see Neder v. United States, 527 U. S. 1, 24\xe2\x80\x9325; and one can conduct the affairs of a qualifying enterprise through a pattern of such acts without anyone relying on a fraudulent misrepresentation. Thus, no reliance showing is required to establish that a person has violated \xc2\xa71962(c) by conducting an enterprise\xe2\x80\x99s affairs through a pattern of racketeering activity predicated on mail fraud. Nor can a first-party reliance requirement be derived from \xc2\xa71964(c), which, by providing a right of action to \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny person\xe2\x80\x9d injured by a violation of \xc2\xa71962, suggests a breadth of coverage not easily reconciled with an implicit first-party reliance requirement. Moreover, a person can be injured \xe2\x80\x9cby reason of\xe2\x80\x9d a pattern of mail fraud even if he has not relied on any misrepresentations. For example, accepting respondents\xe2\x80\x99 allegations as true, they were harmed by petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 scheme when they lost valuable liens they otherwise would have been awarded. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0None of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 arguments\xe2\x80\x94that under the \xe2\x80\x9ccommon-law meaning\xe2\x80\x9d rule, Congress should be presumed to have made reliance an element of a civil RICO claim predicated on a violation of the mail fraud statute; that a plaintiff bringing a RICO claim based on mail fraud must show reliance on the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s misrepresentations in order to establish proximate cause; and that RICO should be interpreted to require first-party reliance for fraud-based claims in order to avoid the \xe2\x80\x9coverfederalization\xe2\x80\x9d of traditional state-law claims\xe2\x80\x94persuades this Court to read a first-party reliance requirement into a statute that by its terms suggests none. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9321.

\n

477 F.\xc2\xa03d 928, affirmed.

JOHN BRIDGE, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. PHOENIX\nBOND & INDEMNITY CO. et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the seventh circuit

\n

[June 9, 2008]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Thomas delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act (RICO or Act), 18 U. S. C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71961\xe2\x80\x931968, provides a private right of action for treble damages to \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny person injured in his business or property by reason of a violation\xe2\x80\x9d of the Act\xe2\x80\x99s criminal prohibitions. \xc2\xa71964(c). The question presented in this case is whether a plaintiff asserting a RICO claim predicated on mail fraud must plead and prove that it relied on the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s alleged misrepresentations. Because we agree with the Court of Appeals that a showing of first-party reliance is not required, we affirm.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Each year the Cook County, Illinois, Treasurer\xe2\x80\x99s Office holds a public auction at which it sells tax liens it has acquired on the property of delinquent taxpayers.[Footnote 1] Prospective buyers bid on the liens, but not in cash amounts. Instead, the bids are stated as percentage penalties the property owner must pay the winning bidder in order to clear the lien. The bidder willing to accept the lowest penalty wins the auction and obtains the right to purchase the lien in exchange for paying the outstanding taxes on the property. The property owner may then redeem the property by paying the lienholder the delinquent taxes, plus the penalty established at the auction and an additional 12% penalty on any taxes subsequently paid by the lienholder. If the property owner does not redeem the property within the statutory redemption period, the lienholder may obtain a tax deed for the property, thereby in effect purchasing the property for the value of the delinquent taxes.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because property acquired in this manner can often be sold at a significant profit over the amount paid for the lien, the auctions are marked by stiff competition. As a result, most parcels attract multiple bidders willing to accept the lowest penalty permissible\xe2\x80\x940%, that is to say, no penalty at all. (Perhaps to prevent the perverse incentive taxpayers would have if they could redeem their property from a winning bidder for less than the amount of their unpaid taxes, the county does not accept negative bids.) The lower limit of 0% creates a problem: Who wins when the bidding results in a tie? The county\xe2\x80\x99s solution is to allocate parcels \xe2\x80\x9con a rotational basis\xe2\x80\x9d in order to ensure that liens are apportioned fairly among 0% bidders. App. 18.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But this creates a perverse incentive of its own: Bidders who, in addition to bidding themselves, send agents to bid on their behalf will obtain a disproportionate share of liens. To prevent this kind of manipulation, the county adopted the \xe2\x80\x9cSingle, Simultaneous Bidder Rule,\xe2\x80\x9d which requires each \xe2\x80\x9ctax buying entity\xe2\x80\x9d to submit bids in its own name and prohibits it from using \xe2\x80\x9capparent agents, employees, or related entities\xe2\x80\x9d to submit simultaneous bids for the same parcel.[Footnote 2] App. 67. Upon registering for an auction, each bidder must submit a sworn affidavit affirming that it complies with the Single, Simultaneous Bidder Rule.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners and respondents are regular participants in Cook County\xe2\x80\x99s tax sales. In July 2005, respondents filed a complaint in the United States District Court for the Northern District of Illinois, contending that petitioners had fraudulently obtained a disproportionate share of liens by violating the Single, Simultaneous Bidder Rule at the auctions held from 2002 to 2005. According to respondents, petitioner Sabre Group, LLC, and its principal Barrett Rochman arranged for related firms to bid on Sabre Group\xe2\x80\x99s behalf and directed them to file false attestations that they complied with the Single, Simultaneous Bidder Rule. Having thus fraudulently obtained the opportunity to participate in the auction, the related firms collusively bid on the same properties at a 0% rate. As a result, when the county allocated liens on a rotating basis,[Footnote 3] it treated the related firms as independent entities, allowing them collectively to acquire a greater number of liens than would have been granted to a single bidder acting alone. The related firms then purchased the liens and transferred the certificates of purchase to Sabre Group. In this way, respondents allege, petitioners deprived them and other bidders of their fair share of liens and the attendant financial benefits.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 complaint contains five counts. Counts I\xe2\x80\x93IV allege that petitioners violated and conspired to violate RICO by conducting their affairs through a pattern of racketeering activity involving numerous acts of mail fraud. In support of their allegations of mail fraud, respondents assert that petitioners \xe2\x80\x9cmailed or caused to be mailed hundreds of mailings in furtherance of the scheme,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 49, when they sent property owners various notices required by Illinois law. Count V alleges a state-law claim of tortious interference with prospective business advantage.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 motion, the District Court dismissed respondents\xe2\x80\x99 RICO claims for lack of standing. It observed that \xe2\x80\x9c[o]nly [respondents] and other competing buyers, as opposed to the Treasurer or the property owners, would suffer a financial loss from a scheme to violate the Single, Simultaneous Bidder Rule.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 17a. But it concluded that respondents \xe2\x80\x9care not in the class of individuals protected by the mail fraud statute, and therefore are not within the \xe2\x80\x98zone of interests\xe2\x80\x99 that the RICO statute protects,\xe2\x80\x9d because they \xe2\x80\x9cwere not recipients of the alleged misrepresentations and, at best were indirect victims of the alleged fraud.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 18a. The District Court declined to exercise supplemental jurisdiction over respondents\xe2\x80\x99 tortious-interference claim and dismissed it without prejudice.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit reversed. It first concluded that \xe2\x80\x9c[s]tanding is not a problem in this suit\xe2\x80\x9d because plaintiffs suffered a \xe2\x80\x9creal injury\xe2\x80\x9d when they lost the valuable chance to acquire more liens, and because \xe2\x80\x9cthat injury can be redressed by damages.\xe2\x80\x9d 477 F.\xc2\xa03d 928, 930 (2007). The Court of Appeals next concluded that respondents had sufficiently alleged proximate cause under Holmes v. Securities Investor Protection Corporation, 503 U. S. 258 (1992), and Anza v. Ideal Steel Supply Corp., 547 U. S. 451 (2006), because they (along with other losing bidders) were \xe2\x80\x9cimmediately injured\xe2\x80\x9d by petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 scheme. 477 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 930\xe2\x80\x93932. Finally, the Court of Appeals rejected petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 argument that respondents are not entitled to relief under RICO because they did not receive, and therefore did not rely on, any false statements: \xe2\x80\x9cA scheme that injures D by making false statements through the mail to E is mail fraud, and actionable by D through RICO if the injury is not derivative of someone else\xe2\x80\x99s.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 932.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0With respect to this last holding, the Court of Appeals acknowledged that courts have taken conflicting views. By its count, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]hree other circuits that have considered this question agree \xe2\x80\xa6 that the direct victim may recover through RICO whether or not it is the direct recipient of the false statements,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. (citing Mid Atlantic Telecom, Inc. v. Long Distance Servs., Inc., 18 F.\xc2\xa03d 260, 263\xe2\x80\x93264 (CA4 1994); Systems Management, Inc. v. Loiselle, 303 F.\xc2\xa03d 100, 103\xe2\x80\x93104 (CA1 2002); Ideal Steel Supply Corp. v. Anza, 373 F.\xc2\xa03d 251, 263 (CA2 2004)), whereas two Circuits hold that the plaintiff must show that it in fact relied on the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s misrepresentations, 477 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 932 (citing VanDenBroeck v. CommonPoint Mortgage Co., 210 F.\xc2\xa03d 696, 701 (CA6 2000); Sikes v. Teleline, Inc., 281 F.\xc2\xa03d 1350, 1360\xe2\x80\x931361 (CA11 2002)). Compare also Sandwich Chef of Texas, Inc. v. Reliance Nat\xe2\x80\x99l Indemnity Ins. Co., 319 F.\xc2\xa03d 205, 223 (CA5 2003) (recognizing \xe2\x80\x9ca narrow exception to the requirement that the plaintiff prove direct reliance on the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s fraudulent predicate act \xe2\x80\xa6 when the plaintiff can demonstrate injury as a direct and contemporaneous result of a fraud committed against a third party\xe2\x80\x9d), with Appletree Square I, L.\xc2\xa0P. v. W.\xc2\xa0R. Grace & Co., 29 F.\xc2\xa03d 1283, 1286\xe2\x80\x931287 (CA8 1994) (requiring the plaintiff to show that it detrimentally relied on the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s misrepresentations).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari, 552 U. S. ___ (2008), to resolve the conflict among the Courts of Appeals on \xe2\x80\x9cthe substantial question,\xe2\x80\x9d Anza, 547 U. S., at 461, whether first-party reliance is an element of a civil RICO claim predicated on mail fraud.[Footnote 4]

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We begin by setting forth the applicable statutory provisions. RICO\xe2\x80\x99s private right of action is contained in 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71964(c), which provides in relevant part that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny person injured in his business or property by reason of a violation of section 1962 of this chapter may sue therefor in any appropriate United States district court and shall recover threefold the damages he sustains and the cost of the suit, including a reasonable attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fee.\xe2\x80\x9d Section 1962 contains RICO\xe2\x80\x99s criminal prohibitions. Pertinent here is \xc2\xa71962(c), which makes it \xe2\x80\x9cunlawful for any person employed by or associated with\xe2\x80\x9d an enterprise engaged in or affecting interstate or foreign commerce \xe2\x80\x9cto conduct or participate, directly or indirectly, in the conduct of such enterprise\xe2\x80\x99s affairs through a pattern of racketeering activity.\xe2\x80\x9d The term \xe2\x80\x9cracketeering activity\xe2\x80\x9d is defined to include a host of so-called predicate acts, including \xe2\x80\x9cany act which is indictable under \xe2\x80\xa6 section 1341 (relating to mail fraud).\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71961(1)(B).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The upshot is that RICO provides a private right of action for treble damages to any person injured in his business or property by reason of the conduct of a qualifying enterprise\xe2\x80\x99s affairs through a pattern of acts indictable as mail fraud. Mail fraud, in turn, occurs whenever a person, \xe2\x80\x9chaving devised or intending to devise any scheme or artifice to defraud,\xe2\x80\x9d uses the mail \xe2\x80\x9cfor the purpose of executing such scheme or artifice or attempting so to do.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71341. The gravamen of the offense is the scheme to defraud, and any \xe2\x80\x9cmailing that is incident to an essential part of the scheme satisfies the mailing element,\xe2\x80\x9d Schmuck v. United States, 489 U. S. 705, 712 (1989) (citation and internal quotation marks omitted), even if the mailing itself \xe2\x80\x9ccontain[s] no false information,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 715.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Once the relationship among these statutory provisions is understood, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 theory of the case is straightforward. They allege that petitioners devised a scheme to defraud when they agreed to submit false attestations of compliance with the Single, Simultaneous Bidder Rule to the county. In furtherance of this scheme, petitioners used the mail on numerous occasions to send the requisite notices to property owners. Each of these mailings was an \xe2\x80\x9cact which is indictable\xe2\x80\x9d as mail fraud, and together they constituted a \xe2\x80\x9cpattern of racketeering activity.\xe2\x80\x9d By conducting the affairs of their enterprise through this pattern of racketeering activity, petitioners violated \xc2\xa71962(c). As a result, respondents lost the opportunity to acquire valuable liens. Accordingly, respondents were injured in their business or property by reason of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 violation of \xc2\xa71962(c), and RICO\xe2\x80\x99s plain terms give them a private right of action for treble damages.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners argue, however, that because the alleged pattern of racketeering activity consisted of acts of mail fraud, respondents must show that they relied on petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 fraudulent misrepresentations. This they cannot do, because the alleged misrepresentations\xe2\x80\x94petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 attestations of compliance with the Single, Simultaneous Bidder Rule\xe2\x80\x94were made to the county, not respondents. The county may well have relied on petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 misrepresentations when it permitted them to participate in the auction, but respondents, never having received the misrepresentations, could not have done so. Indeed, respondents do not even allege that they relied on petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 false attestations. Thus, petitioners submit, they fail to state a claim under RICO.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 proposed requirement of first-party reliance seems to come out of nowhere, there is a reason: Nothing on the face of the relevant statutory provisions imposes such a requirement. Using the mail to execute or attempt to execute a scheme to defraud is indictable as mail fraud, and hence a predicate act of racketeering under RICO, even if no one relied on any misrepresentation. See Neder v. United States, 527 U. S. 1, 24\xe2\x80\x9325 (1999) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe common-law requiremen[t] of \xe2\x80\x98justifiable reliance\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\xa6 plainly ha[s] no place in the [mail, wire, or bank] fraud statutes\xe2\x80\x9d). And one can conduct the affairs of a qualifying enterprise through a pattern of such acts without anyone relying on a fraudulent misrepresentation.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It thus seems plain\xe2\x80\x94and indeed petitioners do not dispute\xe2\x80\x94that no showing of reliance is required to establish that a person has violated \xc2\xa71962(c) by conducting the affairs of an enterprise through a pattern of racketeering activity consisting of acts of mail fraud. See Anza, 547 U. S., at 476 (Thomas, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part) (\xe2\x80\x9cBecause an individual can commit an indictable act of mail or wire fraud even if no one relies on his fraud, he can engage in a pattern of racketeering activity, in violation of \xc2\xa71962, without proof of reliance\xe2\x80\x9d). If reliance is required, then, it must be by virtue of \xc2\xa71964(c), which provides the right of action. But it is difficult to derive a first-party reliance requirement from \xc2\xa71964(c), which states simply that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny person injured in his business or property by reason of a violation of section 1962\xe2\x80\x9d may sue for treble damages. The statute provides a right of action to \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny person\xe2\x80\x9d injured by the violation, suggesting a breadth of coverage not easily reconciled with an implicit requirement that the plaintiff show reliance in addition to injury in his business or property.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, a person can be injured \xe2\x80\x9cby reason of\xe2\x80\x9d a pattern of mail fraud even if he has not relied on any misrepresentations. This is a case in point. Accepting their allegations as true, respondents clearly were injured by petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 scheme: As a result of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 fraud, respondents lost valuable liens they otherwise would have been awarded. And this is true even though they did not rely on petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 false attestations of compliance with the county\xe2\x80\x99s rules. Or, to take another example, suppose an enterprise that wants to get rid of rival businesses mails misrepresentations about them to their customers and suppliers, but not to the rivals themselves. If the rival businesses lose money as a result of the misrepresentations, it would certainly seem that they were injured in their business \xe2\x80\x9cby reason of\xe2\x80\x9d a pattern of mail fraud, even though they never received, and therefore never relied on, the fraudulent mailings. Yet petitioners concede that, on their reading of \xc2\xa71964(c), the rival businesses would have no cause of action under RICO, Tr. of Oral Arg. 4, even though they were the primary and intended victims of the scheme to defraud.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Lacking textual support for this counterintuitive position, petitioners rely instead on a combination of common-law rules and policy arguments in an effort to show that Congress should be presumed to have made first-party reliance an element of a civil RICO claim based on mail fraud. None of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 arguments persuades us to read a first-party reliance requirement into a statute that by its terms suggests none.

\n

III

\n

A

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners first argue that RICO should be read to incorporate a first-party reliance requirement in fraud cases \xe2\x80\x9cunder the rule that Congress intends to incorporate the well-settled meaning of the common-law terms it uses.\xe2\x80\x9d Neder, supra, at 23. It has long been settled, they contend, that only the recipient of a fraudulent misrepresentation may recover for common-law fraud, and that he may do so \xe2\x80\x9cif, but only if \xe2\x80\xa6 he relies on the misrepresentation in acting or refraining from action.\xe2\x80\x9d Restatement (Second) of Torts \xc2\xa7537 (1977). Given this background rule of common law, petitioners maintain, Congress should be presumed to have adopted a first-party reliance requirement when it created a civil cause of action under RICO for victims of mail fraud.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In support of this argument, petitioners point to our decision in Beck v. Prupis, 529 U. S. 494 (2000). There, we considered the scope of RICO\xe2\x80\x99s private right of action for violations of \xc2\xa71962(d), which makes it \xe2\x80\x9cunlawful for any person to conspire to violate\xe2\x80\x9d RICO\xe2\x80\x99s criminal prohibitions. The question presented was \xe2\x80\x9cwhether a person injured by an overt act in furtherance of a conspiracy may assert a civil RICO conspiracy claim under \xc2\xa71964(c) for a violation of \xc2\xa71962(d) even if the overt act does not constitute \xe2\x80\x98racketeering activity.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 500. Answering this question in the negative, we held that \xe2\x80\x9cinjury caused by an overt act that is not an act of racketeering or otherwise wrongful under RICO is not sufficient to give rise to a cause of action under \xc2\xa71964(c) for a violation of \xc2\xa71962(d).\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 505 (citation omitted). In so doing, we \xe2\x80\x9cturn[ed] to the well-established common law of civil conspiracy.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 500. Because it was \xe2\x80\x9cwidely accepted\xe2\x80\x9d by the time of RICO\xe2\x80\x99s enactment \xe2\x80\x9cthat a plaintiff could bring suit for civil conspiracy only if he had been injured by an act that was itself tortious,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 501, we presumed \xe2\x80\x9cthat when Congress established in RICO a civil cause of action for a person \xe2\x80\x98injured \xe2\x80\xa6 by reason of\xe2\x80\x99 a \xe2\x80\x98conspir[acy],\xe2\x80\x99 it meant to adopt these well-established common-law civil conspiracy principles.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 504 (quoting \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71964(c), 1962(d); alterations in original). We specifically declined to rely on the law of criminal conspiracy, relying instead on the law of civil conspiracy:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cWe have turned to the common law of criminal conspiracy to define what constitutes a violation of \xc2\xa71962(d), see Salinas v. United States, 522 U. S. 52, 63\xe2\x80\x9365 (1997), a mere violation being all that is necessary for criminal liability. This case, however, does not present simply the question of what constitutes a violation of \xc2\xa71962(d), but rather the meaning of a civil cause of action for private injury by reason of such a violation. In other words, our task is to interpret \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71964(c) and 1962(d) in conjunction, rather than \xc2\xa71962(d) standing alone. The obvious source in the common law for the combined meaning of these provisions is the law of civil conspiracy.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 501, n.\xc2\xa06.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners argue that, as in Beck, we should look to the common-law meaning of civil fraud in order to give content to the civil cause of action \xc2\xa71964(c) provides for private injury by reason of a violation of \xc2\xa71962(c) based on a pattern of mail fraud. The analogy to Beck, however, is misplaced. The critical difference between Beck and this case is that in \xc2\xa71962(d) Congress used a term\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cconspir[acy]\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94that had a settled common-law meaning, whereas Congress included no such term in \xc2\xa71962(c). Section 1962(c) does not use the term \xe2\x80\x9cfraud\xe2\x80\x9d; nor does the operative language of \xc2\xa71961(1)(B), which defines \xe2\x80\x9cracketeering activity\xe2\x80\x9d to include \xe2\x80\x9cany act which is indictable under \xe2\x80\xa6 section 1341.\xe2\x80\x9d And the indictable act under \xc2\xa71341 is not the fraudulent misrepresentation, but rather the use of the mails with the purpose of executing or attempting to execute a scheme to defraud. In short, the key term in \xc2\xa71962(c)\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cracketeering activity\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94is a defined term, and Congress defined the predicate act not as fraud simpliciter, but mail fraud\xe2\x80\x94a statutory offense unknown to the common law. In these circumstances, the presumption that Congress intends to adopt the settled meaning of common-law terms has little pull. Cf. Stoneridge Investment Partners, LLC v. Scientific-Atlanta, Inc., 552 U. S. ___, ___ (2008) (slip op., at 11) (rejecting the argument that \xc2\xa710(b) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778j(b), incorporates common-law fraud). There is simply no \xe2\x80\x9creason to believe that Congress would have defined \xe2\x80\x98racketeering activity\xe2\x80\x99 to include acts indictable under the mail and wire fraud statutes, if it intended fraud-related acts to be predicate acts under RICO only when those acts would have been actionable under the common law.\xe2\x80\x9d Anza, 547 U. S., at 477\xe2\x80\x93478 (Thomas, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor does it help petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 cause that here, as in Beck, the question is not simply \xe2\x80\x9cwhat constitutes a violation of \xc2\xa71962[(c)], but rather the meaning of a civil cause of action for private injury by reason of such a violation.\xe2\x80\x9d 529 U. S., at 501, n.\xc2\xa06. To be sure, Beck held that a plaintiff cannot state a civil claim for conspiracy under \xc2\xa71964(c) merely by showing a violation of \xc2\xa71962(d) and a resulting injury. But in so doing, Beck relied not only on the fact that the term \xe2\x80\x9cconspiracy\xe2\x80\x9d had a settled common-law meaning, but also on the well-established common-law understanding of what it means to be injured by a conspiracy for purposes of bringing a civil claim for damages. See id., at 501\xe2\x80\x93504. No comparable understanding exists with respect to injury caused by an enterprise conducting its affairs through a pattern of acts indictable as mail fraud. And even the common-law understanding of injury caused by fraud does not support petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 argument. As discussed infra, at 16\xe2\x80\x9317, the common law has long recognized that plaintiffs can recover in a variety of circumstances where, as here, their injuries result directly from the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s fraudulent misrepresentations to a third party.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For these reasons, we reject petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 contention that the \xe2\x80\x9ccommon-law meaning\xe2\x80\x9d rule dictates that reliance by the plaintiff is an element of a civil RICO claim predicated on a violation of the mail fraud statute. Congress chose to make mail fraud, not common-law fraud, the predicate act for a RICO violation. And \xe2\x80\x9cthe mere fact that the predicate acts underlying a particular RICO violation happen to be fraud offenses does not mean that reliance, an element of common-law fraud, is also incorporated as an element of a civil RICO claim.\xe2\x80\x9d Anza, supra, at 476 (Thomas, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners next argue that even if Congress did not make first-party reliance an element of a RICO claim predicated on mail fraud, a plaintiff who brings such a claim must show that it relied on the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s misrepresentations in order to establish the requisite element of causation. In Holmes, we recognized that \xc2\xa71964(c)\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9clanguage can, of course, be read to mean that a plaintiff is injured \xe2\x80\x98by reason of\xe2\x80\x99 a RICO violation, and therefore may recover, simply on showing that the defendant violated \xc2\xa71962, the plaintiff was injured, and the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s violation was a \xe2\x80\x98but for\xe2\x80\x99 cause of plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s injury.\xe2\x80\x9d 503 U. S., at 265\xe2\x80\x93266 (footnote omitted). We nonetheless held that not \xe2\x80\x9call factually injured plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x9d may recover under \xc2\xa71964(c). Id., at 266. Because Congress modeled \xc2\xa71964(c) on other provisions that had been interpreted to \xe2\x80\x9crequir[e] a showing that the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s violation not only was a \xe2\x80\x98but for\xe2\x80\x99 cause of his injury, but was the proximate cause as well,\xe2\x80\x9d we concluded that \xc2\xa71964(c) likewise requires the plaintiff to establish proximate cause in order to show injury \xe2\x80\x9cby reason of\xe2\x80\x9d a RICO violation. Id., at 268.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Proximate cause, we explained, is a flexible concept that does not lend itself to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a black-letter rule that will dictate the result in every case.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 272, n.\xc2\xa020 (quoting Associated Gen. Contractors of Cal., Inc. v. Carpenters, 459 U. S. 519, 536 (1983)). Instead, we \xe2\x80\x9cuse[d] \xe2\x80\x98proximate cause\xe2\x80\x99 to label generically the judicial tools used to limit a person\xe2\x80\x99s responsibility for the consequences of that person\xe2\x80\x99s own acts,\xe2\x80\x9d Holmes, 503 U. S., at 268, with a particular emphasis on the \xe2\x80\x9cdemand for some direct relation between the injury asserted and the injurious conduct alleged,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid.; see also Anza, supra, at 461 (\xe2\x80\x9cWhen a court evaluates a RICO claim for proximate causation, the central question it must ask is whether the alleged violation led directly to the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s injuries\xe2\x80\x9d). The direct-relation requirement avoids the difficulties associated with attempting \xe2\x80\x9cto ascertain the amount of a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s damages attributable to the violation, as distinct from other, independent, factors,\xe2\x80\x9d Holmes, 503 U. S., at 269; prevents courts from having \xe2\x80\x9cto adopt complicated rules of apportioning damages among plaintiffs removed at different levels of injury from the violative acts, to obviate the risk of multiple recoveries,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid.; and recognizes the fact that \xe2\x80\x9cdirectly injured victims can generally be counted on to vindicate the law as private attorneys general, without any of the problems attendant upon suits by plaintiffs injured more remotely,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 269\xe2\x80\x93270.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Pointing to our reliance on common-law proximate-causation principles in Holmes and Anza, petitioners argue that \xe2\x80\x9c[u]nder well-settled common-law principles, proximate cause is established for fraud claims only where the plaintiff can demonstrate that he relied on the misrepresentation.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 28. In support of this argument, petitioners cite Restatement (Second) of Torts \xc2\xa7548A, which provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] fraudulent misrepresentation is a legal cause of a pecuniary loss resulting from action or inaction in reliance upon it if, but only if, the loss might reasonably be expected to result from the reliance.\xe2\x80\x9d Thus, petitioners conclude, \xe2\x80\x9ca plaintiff asserting a civil RICO claim predicated on mail fraud cannot satisfy the proximate cause requirement unless he can establish that his injuries resulted from his reliance on the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s fraudulent misrepresentation.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 28.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 argument is twice flawed. First, as explained above, the predicate act here is not common-law fraud, but mail fraud. Having rejected petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 argument that reliance is an element of a civil RICO claim based on mail fraud, we see no reason to let that argument in through the back door by holding that the proximate-cause analysis under RICO must precisely track the proximate-cause analysis of a common-law fraud claim. \xe2\x80\x9cReliance is not a general limitation on civil recovery in tort; it \xe2\x80\x98is a specialized condition that happens to have grown up with common law fraud.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Anza, 547 U. S., at 477 (Thomas, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part) (quoting Systems Management, 303 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 104). That \xe2\x80\x9cspecialized condition,\xe2\x80\x9d whether characterized as an element of the claim or as a prerequisite to establishing proximate causation, simply has no place in a remedial scheme keyed to the commission of mail fraud, a statutory offense that is distinct from common-law fraud and that does not require proof of reliance.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, while it may be that first-party reliance is an element of a common-law fraud claim, there is no general common-law principle holding that a fraudulent misrepresentation can cause legal injury only to those who rely on it. The Restatement provision cited by petitioners certainly does not support that proposition. It provides only that the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s loss must be a foreseeable result of someone\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on the misrepresentation.[Footnote 6] It does not say that only those who rely on the misrepresentation can suffer a legally cognizable injury. And any such notion would be contradicted by the long line of cases in which courts have permitted a plaintiff directly injured by a fraudulent misrepresentation to recover even though it was a third party, and not the plaintiff, who relied on the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s misrepresentation.[Footnote 7] Indeed, so well established is the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s liability in such circumstances that the Restatement (Second) of Torts sets forth as a \xe2\x80\x9c[g]eneral [p]rinciple\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9c[o]ne who intentionally causes injury to another is subject to liability to the other for that injury, if his conduct is generally culpable and not justifiable under the circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7870. As an illustration, the Restatement provides the example of a defendant who \xe2\x80\x9cseeks to promote his own interests by telling a known falsehood to or about the plaintiff or his product.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., Comment h (emphasis added). And the Restatement specifically recognizes \xe2\x80\x9ca cause of action\xe2\x80\x9d in favor of the injured party where the defendant \xe2\x80\x9cdefrauds another for the purpose of causing pecuniary harm to a third person.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., \xc2\xa7435A, Comment\xc2\xa0a. Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 contention that proximate cause has traditionally incorporated a first-party reliance requirement for claims based on fraud cannot be reconciled with these authorities.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor is first-party reliance necessary to ensure that there is a sufficiently direct relationship between the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s wrongful conduct and the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s injury to satisfy the proximate-cause principles articulated in Holmes and Anza. Again, this is a case in point. Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 alleged injury\xe2\x80\x94the loss of valuable liens\xe2\x80\x94is the direct result of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 fraud. It was a foreseeable and natural consequence of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 scheme to obtain more liens for themselves that other bidders would obtain fewer liens. And here, unlike in Holmes and Anza, there are no independent factors that account for respondents\xe2\x80\x99 injury, there is no risk of duplicative recoveries by plaintiffs removed at different levels of injury from the violation, and no more immediate victim is better situated to sue. Indeed, both the District Court and the Court of Appeals concluded that respondents and other losing bidders were the only parties injured by petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 misrepresentations. App. to Pet. for Cert. 17a; 477 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 931. Petitioners quibble with that conclusion, asserting that the county would be injured too if the taint of fraud deterred potential bidders from participating in the auction. But that eventuality, in contrast to respondents\xe2\x80\x99 direct financial injury, seems speculative and remote.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Of course, none of this is to say that a RICO plaintiff who alleges injury \xe2\x80\x9cby reason of\xe2\x80\x9d a pattern of mail fraud can prevail without showing that someone relied on the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s misrepresentations. Cf. Field v. Mans, 516 U. S. 59, 66 (1995) (\xe2\x80\x9cNo one, of course, doubts that some degree of reliance is required to satisfy the element of causation inherent in the phrase \xe2\x80\x98obtained by\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(A), which prohibits the discharge of debts for money or property \xe2\x80\x9cobtained by\xe2\x80\x9d fraud). In most cases, the plaintiff will not be able to establish even but-for causation if no one relied on the misrepresentation. If, for example, the county had not accepted petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 false attestations of compliance with the Single, Simultaneous Bidder Rule, and as a result had not permitted petitioners to participate in the auction, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 injury would never have materialized. In addition, the complete absence of reliance may prevent the plaintiff from establishing proximate cause. Thus, for example, if the county knew petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 attestations were false but nonetheless permitted them to participate in the auction, then arguably the county\xe2\x80\x99s actions would constitute an intervening cause breaking the chain of causation between petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 misrepresentations and respondents\xe2\x80\x99 injury.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, it may well be that a RICO plaintiff alleging injury by reason of a pattern of mail fraud must establish at least third-party reliance in order to prove causation. \xe2\x80\x9cBut the fact that proof of reliance is often used to prove an element of the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s cause of action, such as the element of causation, does not transform reliance itself into an element of the cause of action.\xe2\x80\x9d Anza, 547 U. S., at 478 (Thomas, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part). Nor does it transform first-party reliance into an indispensable requisite of proximate causation. Proof that the plaintiff relied on the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s misrepresentations may in some cases be sufficient to establish proximate cause, but there is no sound reason to conclude that such proof is always necessary. By the same token, the absence of first-party reliance may in some cases tend to show that an injury was not sufficiently direct to satisfy \xc2\xa71964(c)\xe2\x80\x99s proximate-cause requirement, but it is not in and of itself dispositive. A contrary holding would ignore Holmes\xe2\x80\x99 instruction that proximate cause is generally not amenable to bright-line rules.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As a last resort, petitioners contend that we should interpret RICO to require first-party reliance for fraud-based claims in order to avoid the \xe2\x80\x9cover-federalization\xe2\x80\x9d of traditional state-law claims. In petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 view, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claim is essentially one for tortious interference with prospective business advantage, as evidenced by Count V of their complaint. Such claims have traditionally been handled under state law, and petitioners see no reason why Congress would have wanted to supplement traditional state-law remedies with a federal cause of action, complete with treble damages and attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees, in a statute designed primarily to combat organized crime. See Anza, supra, at 471\xe2\x80\x93475 (Thomas, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part); Beck, 529 U. S., at 496\xe2\x80\x93497. A first-party reliance requirement, they say, is necessary \xe2\x80\x9cto prevent garden-variety disputes between local competitors (such as this case) from being converted into federal racketeering actions.\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief for Petitioners 3.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Whatever the merits of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 arguments as a policy matter, we are not at liberty to rewrite RICO to reflect their\xe2\x80\x94or our\xe2\x80\x94views of good policy. We have repeatedly refused to adopt narrowing constructions of RICO in order to make it conform to a preconceived notion of what Congress intended to proscribe. See, e.g., National Organization for Women, Inc. v. Scheidler, 510 U. S. 249, 252 (1994) (rejecting the argument that \xe2\x80\x9cRICO requires proof that either the racketeering enterprise or the predicate acts of racketeering were motivated by an economic purpose\xe2\x80\x9d); H. J. Inc. v. Northwestern Bell Telephone Co., 492 U. S. 229, 244 (1989) (rejecting \xe2\x80\x9cthe argument for reading an organized crime limitation into RICO\xe2\x80\x99s pattern concept\xe2\x80\x9d); Sedima, S.\xc2\xa0P.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0L. v. Imrex Co., 473 U. S. 479, 481 (1985) (rejecting the view that RICO provides a private right of action \xe2\x80\x9conly against defendants who had been convicted on criminal charges, and only where there had occurred a \xe2\x80\x98racketeering injury\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We see no reason to change course here. RICO\xe2\x80\x99s text provides no basis for imposing a first-party reliance requirement. If the absence of such a requirement leads to the undue proliferation of RICO suits, the \xe2\x80\x9ccorrection must lie with Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 499. \xe2\x80\x9cIt is not for the judiciary to eliminate the private action in situations where Congress has provided it.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 499\xe2\x80\x93500.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For the foregoing reasons, we hold that a plaintiff asserting a RICO claim predicated on mail fraud need not show, either as an element of its claim or as a prerequisite to establishing proximate causation, that it relied on the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s alleged misrepresentations. Accordingly, the judgment of the Court of Appeals is affirmed.

\n

It is so ordered.
