SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

WITTMAN et\xc2\xa0al. v. PERSONHUBALLAH et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on appeal from the united states district court for the eastern district of virginia

\n

No. 14\xe2\x80\x931504.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 21, 2016\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 23, 2016

\n

Appellee voters from Virginia\xe2\x80\x99s Congressional District 3 filed suit challenging the Commonwealth\xe2\x80\x99s 2013 congressional redistricting plan on the ground that the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s redrawing of their district was an unconstitutional racial gerrymander. Appellant Members of Congress from Virginia, including, as relevant here, Representatives Randy Forbes, Robert Wittman, and David Brat, intervened to help defend the Commonwealth\xe2\x80\x99s plan. The District Court struck down the plan, and the intervenors appealed to this Court, which vacated the judgment below and remanded the case in light of one of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s recent decisions. Again, the District Court held that the redistricting plan was unconstitutional, and again, the intervenors appealed. This time, the Court directed the parties to address whether appellants lack standing, since none reside in or represent Congressional District 3.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Appellants lack standing to pursue this appeal. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0A party invoking a federal court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction can establish Article III standing only by showing that he has suffered an \xe2\x80\x9cinjury in fact,\xe2\x80\x9d that the injury is \xe2\x80\x9cfairly traceable\xe2\x80\x9d to the challenged conduct, and that the injury is likely to be \xe2\x80\x9credressed\xe2\x80\x9d by a favorable decision. Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife,\n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 555\n\xe2\x80\x93561. The need to satisfy these requirements persists throughout the life of the suit. Arizonans for Official English v. Arizona,\n\n520 U.\xc2\xa0S. 43\n. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x934.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0In light of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision striking down the redistricting plan, Representative Forbes, the Republican incumbent in District 4, decided to run in District 2. Originally, Representative Forbes argued that he would abandon his campaign in District 2 and run in District 4 if this Court ruled in his favor. Now, however, he has informed the Court that he will continue to seek election in District 2 regardless of this appeal\xe2\x80\x99s outcome. Given this change, this Court does not see how any injury that Forbes might have suffered is \xe2\x80\x9clikely to be redressed by a favorable judicial decision.\xe2\x80\x9d Hollingsworth v. Perry, 570 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. Regardless of whether Forbes had standing at the time he first intervened, he does not have standing now.

\n

Representatives Wittman and Brat, the incumbents in Congressional Districts 1 and 7, respectively, have not identified any record evidence to support their allegation that the redistricting plan has harmed their prospects of reelection. The allegation of an injury, without more, is not sufficient to satisfy Article III. See Lujan, supra, at 561. Given the complete lack of evidence of any injury in this case, the Court need not decide when, or whether, evidence of the kind of injury the Representatives allege would prove sufficient for Article III purposes. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

Appeal dismissed.

\n

Breyer, J., delivered the opinion for a unanimous Court.

SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

No. 14\xe2\x80\x931504

\n

ROBERT J. WITTMAN, et\xc2\xa0al., APPELLANTS v. GLORIA PERSONHUBALLAH, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on appeal from the united states district court for the eastern district of virginia

\n

[May 23, 2016]

\n

\n

Justice Breyer delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

Ten Members of Congress from Virginia, intervenors in the District Court below, have appealed a judgment from a three-judge panel striking down a congressional redistricting plan applicable to the November 2016 election. We conclude that the intervenors now lack standing to pursue the appeal. And we consequently order the appeal dismissed.

\n

I

\n

This lawsuit began in October 2013, after the then-Governor of Virginia signed into law a new congressional redistricting plan (which we shall call the \xe2\x80\x9cEnacted Plan\xe2\x80\x9d) designed to reflect the results of the 2010 census. Three voters from Congressional District 3 brought this lawsuit against the Commonwealth. They challenged the Enacted Plan on the ground that its redrawing of their district\xe2\x80\x99s lines was an unconstitutional racial gerrymander. The Members of Congress now before us intervened to help defend the Enacted Plan.

\n

After a bench trial, a divided three-judge District Court agreed with the voters. It concluded that the Commonwealth had used race as the predominant basis for modifying the boundaries of District 3. Page v. Virginia State Bd. of Elections, 58 F. Supp.\xc2\xa03d 533, 550 (ED Va. 2014). And it found that the Commonwealth\xe2\x80\x99s use of race, when scrutinized strictly, was not narrowly tailored to serve a compelling governmental interest. Id., at 553.

\n

The Commonwealth of Virginia did not appeal. Instead, the intervenor Members of Congress appealed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment to this Court. See\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71253 (granting the right to directly appeal certain three-judge district court orders to the Supreme Court). Having just decided a racial gerrymandering case, Alabama Legislative Black Caucus v. Alabama, 575 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015), we vacated the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment and remanded for reconsideration in light of that recent decision. Cantor v. Personhuballah, 575 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015).

\n

On remand the District Court again decided that District 3, as modified by the Enacted Plan, was an unconstitutional racial gerrymander. Page v. Virginia State Bd. of Elections, 2015 WL 3604029, *19 (ED Va., June 5, 2015). The court\xe2\x80\x99s order set forth a deadline of September 1, 2015, for the Virginia Legislature to adopt a new redistricting plan.

\n

Again, the Commonwealth of Virginia decided not to appeal. And again, the intervenor Members of Congress appealed to this Court. On September 28, 2015, we asked the parties to file supplemental briefs addressing whether the intervenors had standing to appeal the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision. 576 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___. As relevant here, the intervenors argued in their supplemental brief that they had standing because the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order, if allowed to stand, would necessarily result in a redrawing of their districts that would harm some of the intervenors\xe2\x80\x99 reelection prospects. On November 13, 2015, we issued an order explaining that the Court was \xe2\x80\x9cpostpon[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cconsideration of the question of jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d until \xe2\x80\x9cthe hearing of the case on the merits.\xe2\x80\x9d In addition, our order instructed the parties to dedicate a portion of their briefs and their oral argument time to the issue of standing\xe2\x80\x94specifically, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hether [the intervenors] lack standing because none reside in or represent the only congressional district whose constitutionality is at issue in this case.\xe2\x80\x9d 577 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___.

\n

In the meantime, the Virginia Legislature failed to meet the September 1 deadline imposed by the District Court. The District Court thus appointed a Special Master to develop a new districting plan. The Special Master did so, and on January 7, 2016, the District Court approved that plan (which we shall call the \xe2\x80\x9cRemedial Plan\xe2\x80\x9d). The intervenor Members of Congress asked this Court to stay implementation of the Remedial Plan pending resolution of their direct appeal to this Court. We declined to do so. 577 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2016). On March 21, we heard oral argument. That argument focused both on (1) the merits of intervenors\xe2\x80\x99 claims denying any racial gerrymander and (2) the question of standing. In respect to standing, the Court focused on whether the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s approval of the Remedial Plan on January 7 supported, or undermined, the intervenors\xe2\x80\x99 standing argument that, in the absence of the original Enacted Plan, they would suffer harm. Tr. of Oral Arg. 9\xe2\x80\x9323.

\n

II

\n

As our request for supplemental briefing, our order postponing consideration of jurisdiction, and our questions at oral argument suggested, we cannot decide the merits of this case unless the intervenor Members of Congress challenging the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s racial-gerrymandering decision have standing. We conclude that the intervenors now lack standing. We must therefore dismiss the appeal for lack of jurisdiction.

\n

Article III of the Constitution grants the federal courts the power to decide legal questions only in the presence of an actual \xe2\x80\x9cCas[e]\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cControvers[y].\xe2\x80\x9d This restriction requires a party invoking a federal court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction to demonstrate standing. Arizonans for Official English v. Arizona,\n\n520 U.\xc2\xa0S. 43,\n64 (1997)\n. A party has standing only if he shows that he has suffered an \xe2\x80\x9cinjury in fact,\xe2\x80\x9d that the injury is \xe2\x80\x9cfairly traceable\xe2\x80\x9d to the conduct being challenged, and that the injury will likely be \xe2\x80\x9credressed\xe2\x80\x9d by a favorable decision. Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife,\n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 555\n\xe2\x80\x93561 (1992) (internal quotation marks and ellipsis omitted). The need to satisfy these three requirements persists throughout the life of the lawsuit. Arizonans for Official English, 520 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 67.

\n

The relevant parties here are the intervenor Members of Congress. Since the Commonwealth of Virginia has not pursued an appeal, only the intervenors currently attack the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision striking down the Enacted Plan. And an \xe2\x80\x9cintervenor cannot step into the shoes of the original party\xe2\x80\x9d (here, the Commonwealth) \xe2\x80\x9cunless the intervenor independently \xe2\x80\x98fulfills the requirements of Article III.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 65 (quoting Diamond v. Charles,\n\n476 U.\xc2\xa0S. 54,\n68 (1986)\n).

\n

Although 10 current and former Members of Congress are technically intervenors, only 3 of the 10 now claim before this Court that they have standing. Those three Members are Representative Randy Forbes, Representative Robert Wittman, and Representative David Brat.

\n

Representative Forbes, the Republican incumbent in Congressional District 4, told us in his brief that, unless the Enacted Plan is upheld, District 4 will be \xe2\x80\x9ccompletely transform[ed] from a 48% Democratic district into a safe 60% Democratic district.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Appellants 58. According to Forbes, the threat of that kind of transformation compelled him to run in a different district, namely, Congressional District 2.

\n

At oral argument, Forbes\xe2\x80\x99 counsel told the Court that, if the Enacted Plan were reinstated, Representative Forbes would abandon his election effort in Congressional District 2 and run in his old district, namely, Congressional District 4. Tr. of Oral Arg. 10. Soon after oral argument, however, the Court received a letter from counsel stating that Representative Forbes would \xe2\x80\x9ccontinue to seek election in District 2 regardless of whether the Enacted Plan is reinstated.\xe2\x80\x9d Letter from Counsel for Appellants to Scott S. Harris, Clerk of Court (Mar. 25, 2016), p. 2. Given this letter, we do not see how any injury that Forbes might have suffered \xe2\x80\x9cis likely to be redressed by a favorable judicial decision.\xe2\x80\x9d Hollingsworth v. Perry, 570 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2013) (slip op., at 5\xe2\x80\x936). Consequently, we need not decide whether, at the time he first intervened, Representative Forbes possessed standing. Regardless, he does not possess standing now. See Arizonans for Official English, supra, at 65; Lewis v. Continental Bank Corp.,\n\n494 U.\xc2\xa0S. 472\n\xe2\x80\x93478 (1990).

\n

Representative Wittman and Representative Brat are Republicans representing Congressional District 1 and Congressional District 7, respectively. In their opening brief they argue that they have standing to challenge the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order because, unless the Enacted Plan is reinstated, \xe2\x80\x9ca portion of the[ir] \xe2\x80\x98base electorate\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d will necessarily be replaced with \xe2\x80\x9cunfavorable Democratic voters,\xe2\x80\x9d thereby reducing the likelihood of the Representatives\xe2\x80\x99 reelection. Brief for Appellants 58; see also Application for Stay of Remedial Plan Pending Resolution of Direct Appeal of Liability Judgment 25. Even assuming, without deciding, that this kind of injury is legally cognizable, Representatives Wittman and Brat have not identified record evidence establishing their alleged harm.

\n

We have made clear that the \xe2\x80\x9cparty invoking federal jurisdiction bears the burden of establishing\xe2\x80\x9d that he has suffered an injury by submitting \xe2\x80\x9caffidavit[s] or other evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Lujan, 504 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 561. When challenged by a court (or by an opposing party) concerned about standing, the party invoking the court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction cannot simply allege a nonobvious harm, without more. Ibid. Here, there is no \xe2\x80\x9cmore.\xe2\x80\x9d Representatives Wittman and Brat claim that unless the Enacted Plan is reinstated, their districts will be flooded with Democratic voters and their chances of reelection will accordingly be reduced. But we have examined the briefs, looking for any evidence that an alternative to the Enacted Plan (including the Remedial Plan) will reduce the relevant intervenors\xe2\x80\x99 chances of reelection, and have found none. The briefs focus on Congressional District 3 and Congressional District 4, districts with which Representatives Wittman and Brat are not associated.

\n

We need go no further. Given the lack of evidence that any of the three Representatives has standing, we need not decide when, or whether, evidence of the kind of injury they allege would prove sufficient for purposes of Article III\xe2\x80\x99s requirements. In light of the letter we have received about Representative Forbes, and the absence of any evidence in the briefs supporting any harm to the other two Representatives, we conclude that none of the intervenors has standing to bring an appeal in this case. We consequently lack jurisdiction and therefore dismiss this appeal.

\n

It is so ordered.
