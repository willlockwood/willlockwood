SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

Syllabus

\n

SHERIFF et\xc2\xa0al. v. GILLIE et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x93338.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 29, 2016\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 16, 2016

\n

The Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA or Act) aims to eliminate \xe2\x80\x9cabusive debt collection practices,\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71692(a)\xe2\x80\x93(d), by, as relevant here, barring \xe2\x80\x9cfalse, deceptive, or misleading representation[s] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in connection with the collection of any debt,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71692e. Governing \xe2\x80\x9cdebt collectors,\xe2\x80\x9d the Act excludes from the definition of that term \xe2\x80\x9cany officer .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any State to the extent that collecting .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any debt is in the performance of his official duties.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71692a(6)(C).

\n

Under Ohio law, overdue debts owed to state-owned agencies and instrumentalities are certified to the State\xe2\x80\x99s Attorney General for collection or disposition. Carrying out this responsibility, the Attorney General appoints, as independent contractors, private attorneys, naming them \xe2\x80\x9cspecial counsel\xe2\x80\x9d to act on the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s behalf. The Attorney General requires special counsel to use the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead in communicating with debtors. Among the special counsel appointed by the Attorney General in 2012 were petitioners Mark Sheriff and Eric Jones. Consistent with the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s direction, Sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s law firm and Jones sent debt collection letters on the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead to respondents Hazel Meadows and Pamela Gillie, respectively. The signature block of each letter contained the name and address of the signatory as well as the designation \xe2\x80\x9cspecial\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9coutside\xe2\x80\x9d counsel to the State Attorney General. Each letter also identified the sender as a debt collector seeking payment for debts to a state institution. Meadows and Gillie filed a putative class action in Federal District Court, alleging that defendants had, by using the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead, employed deceptive and misleading means to attempt to collect consumer debts, in violation of the FDCPA. The Ohio Attorney General intervened, seeking a declaratory judgment that special counsel\xe2\x80\x99s use of the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead is neither false nor misleading, and urging that special counsel be deemed officers of the State exempted from the Act. The District Court granted summary judgment for defendants, holding that special counsel are \xe2\x80\x9cofficers\xe2\x80\x9d of the State and, in any event, their use of the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead is not false or misleading. The Sixth Circuit vacated that judgment, concluding that special counsel, as independent contractors, are not entitled to the FDCPA\xe2\x80\x99s state-officer exemption. The appeals court remanded for trial the question whether use of the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead would mislead a debtor into believing that it is the Attorney General who is collecting the debt.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Assuming, arguendo, that special counsel do not rank as \xe2\x80\x9cstate officers\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning of the Act, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 use of the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead, nevertheless, does not offend \xc2\xa71692e.

\n

Special counsel\xe2\x80\x99s use of the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead at the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s direction does not offend \xc2\xa71692e\xe2\x80\x99s general prohibition against \xe2\x80\x9cfalse .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. or misleading representation[s].\xe2\x80\x9d The letterhead identifies the principal\xe2\x80\x94Ohio\xe2\x80\x99s Attorney General\xe2\x80\x94and the signature block names the agent\xe2\x80\x94a private lawyer hired as outside counsel to the Attorney General. The character of the relationship between special counsel and the Attorney General bolsters the Court\xe2\x80\x99s determination. Special counsel work closely with attorneys in the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s Office, providing legal services on the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s behalf in furtherance of the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s debt collection responsibilities for the State. A debtor\xe2\x80\x99s impression that a letter from special counsel is a letter from the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s Office is thus scarcely inaccurate.

\n

Special counsel\xe2\x80\x99s use of the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead is also consistent with \xc2\xa71692e(9)\xe2\x80\x99s specific prohibition against \xe2\x80\x9cfalsely represent[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d that a communication is \xe2\x80\x9cauthorized, issued, or approved\xe2\x80\x9d by a State. Because the Attorney General authorized\xe2\x80\x94indeed required\xe2\x80\x94special counsel to use his letterhead, special counsel create no false impression in doing just that. Nor did special counsel use an untrue name in their letters, in violation of \xc2\xa71692e(14). Special counsel do not employ a false name when they use the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead at his instruction, for special counsel act as the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s agents in debt-related matters. The Court sees no reason, furthermore, to construe the FDCPA in a manner that would interfere with the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s chosen method of fulfilling his statutory obligation to collect the State\xe2\x80\x99s debts.

\n

The Sixth Circuit raises the specter of consumer confusion and the risk of intimidation from special counsel\xe2\x80\x99s use of the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead, but its exposition is unconvincing. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

785 F.\xc2\xa03d 1091, reversed and remanded.

\n

MARK J. SHERIFF, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v.PAMELA GILLIE, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

[May 16, 2016]

\n

\n

Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

Ohio law authorizes the State\xe2\x80\x99s Attorney General to retain, as independent contractors, \xe2\x80\x9cspecial counsel\xe2\x80\x9d to act on the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s behalf in collecting certain debts owed to Ohio or an instrumentality of the State. Ohio Rev. Code Ann. \xc2\xa7109.08 (Lexis 2014). As required by the Attorney General, special counsel use the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead in communicating with debtors. App. 93. The Fair Debt Collection Practices Act,\n\n91Stat.\n874,\n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71692 et seq. (FDCPA or Act), aims to eliminate \xe2\x80\x9cabusive debt collection practices.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71692(a)\xe2\x80\x93(d). To that end, the Act imposes various procedural and substantive obligations on debt collectors. See, e.g., \xc2\xa71692d (prohibiting harassing, oppressive, or abusive conduct); \xc2\xa71692e (barring \xe2\x80\x9cfalse, deceptive, or misleading representation[s] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in connection with the collection of any debt\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa71692g(a) (setting out requirements for the contents of initial notices to consumers). The FDCPA excludes from the definition of \xe2\x80\x9cdebt collector\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cany officer or employee of the United States or any State to the extent that collecting .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any debt is in the performance of his official duties.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71692a(6)(C).

\n

This case involves litigation between debtors to Ohio institutions and special counsel who sought to collect money owed to the institutions. The petition raises two questions: (1) Do special counsel appointed by Ohio\xe2\x80\x99s Attorney General qualify as \xe2\x80\x9cstate officers\xe2\x80\x9d exempt from the FDCPA\xe2\x80\x99s governance? (2) Is special counsel\xe2\x80\x99s use of the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead a false or misleading representation proscribed by \xc2\xa71692e?

\n

Assuming, arguendo, that special counsel do not rank as \xe2\x80\x9cstate officers,\xe2\x80\x9d we hold, nevertheless, that their use of the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead does not offend \xc2\xa71692e. Not fairly described as \xe2\x80\x9cfalse\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cmisleading,\xe2\x80\x9d use of the letterhead accurately conveys that special counsel, in seeking to collect debts owed to the State, do so on behalf of, and as instructed by, the Attorney General.

\n

I

\n

Responding to reports of abusive practices by third-party collectors of consumer debts, Congress enacted the FDCPA \xe2\x80\x9cto eliminate abusive debt collection practices by debt collectors, to insure that those debt collectors who refrain from using abusive debt collection practices are not competitively disadvantaged, and to promote consistent State action to protect consumers against debt collection abuses.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71692(e). Primarily governing \xe2\x80\x9cdebt collector[s],\xe2\x80\x9d the Act defines that term to include \xe2\x80\x9cany person .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in any business the principal purpose of which is the collection of any debts, or who regularly collects or attempts to collect .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. debts owed or due or asserted to be owed or due another.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71692a(6). Excluded from the definition is \xe2\x80\x9cany offi-cer or employee of the United States or any State to the extent that collecting or attempting to collect any debt is in the performance of his official duties.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71692a(6)(C).

\n

Among other proscriptions, the FDCPA prohibits debt collectors from employing \xe2\x80\x9cfalse, deceptive, or misleading\xe2\x80\x9d practices. \xc2\xa71692e. \xe2\x80\x9cWithout limiting\xe2\x80\x9d this general ban, \xc2\xa71692e enumerates 16 categories of conduct that qualify as false or misleading. Two of those categories are pertinent to our review: \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he use or distribution of any written communication which simulates or is falsely represented to be a document authorized, issued, or approved by any court, official, or agency of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any State, or which creates a false impression as to its source, authorization, or approval,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71692e(9); and \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he use of any business, com-pany, or organization name other than the true name of the debt collector\xe2\x80\x99s business, company, or organization,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71692e(14). A debt collector who violates the Act is liable for both actual and statutory damages. \xc2\xa71692k(a).

\n

This case concerns the debt collection practices of those charged with collecting overdue debts owed to Ohio-owned agencies and instrumentalities. Among such debts are past-due tuition owed to public universities and unpaid medical bills from state-run hospitals. Under Ohio law, overdue debts are certified to the State\xe2\x80\x99s Attorney General, who is responsible for collecting, settling, or otherwise disposing of them. Ohio Rev. Code Ann. \xc2\xa7131.02(A), (C), (F). Carrying out this responsibility, the Attorney General may appoint private attorneys as \xe2\x80\x9cspecial counsel to represent the state\xe2\x80\x9d in collecting certified claims. \xc2\xa7109.08.

\n

Special counsel enter into year-long retention agreements \xe2\x80\x9con an independent contractor basis\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9cprovide legal services on behalf of the Attorney General to one or more State Clients.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 143\xe2\x80\x93144. The Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s Office assigns individual claims to special counsel, who are paid a set percentage of the funds they collect for the State. \xc2\xa7109.08; id., at 144\xe2\x80\x93145, 149\xe2\x80\x93152. With \xe2\x80\x9cthe prior approval of the Attorney General,\xe2\x80\x9d special counsel may litigate and settle claims on behalf of the State. Id., at 149. Special counsel may continue to represent private clients so long as doing so does not create a conflict of interest with their work for the Attorney General. Among the special counsel appointed by the Attorney General in 2012 were Mark Sheriff, a partner at the law firm of Wiles, Boyle, Burkholder, and Bringardner Co. LPA (Wiles firm), and Eric Jones, of the Law Offices of Eric A. Jones, LLC.

\n

When special counsel contact debtors on behalf of the State, the Attorney General requires them to use his letterhead. Id., at 93. Consistent with this requirement, Sarah Sheriff, an employee of the Wiles firm, sent respondent Hazel Meadows a debt collection letter on the Ohio Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead. The letter reads:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cSir/Madam: Per your request, this is a letter with the current balance owed for your University of Akron loan that has been placed with the Ohio Attorney General. Feel free to contact me at [telephone number] should you have any further questions.\xe2\x80\x9d Gillie v. Law Office of Eric A. Jones, LLC, 785 F.\xc2\xa03d 1091, 1119 (CA6 2015).

\n

The amount Meadows owed is listed in the letter\xe2\x80\x99s subject line. Ibid. After the body of the letter, Sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s signature appears, followed by the firm\xe2\x80\x99s name, its address, and the designation \xe2\x80\x9cSpecial Counsel to the Attorney General for the State of Ohio.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.[1] The letter concludes with a notice that it is \xe2\x80\x9can attempt to collect a debt\xe2\x80\x9d and that the senders \xe2\x80\x9care debt collectors.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

Respondent Pamela Gillie received a letter, also on the Ohio Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead, in relation to a debt she owed to a state-run hospital:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cDear Sir/Madam, You have chosen to ignore repeated attempts to resolv[e] the referenced .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. medical claim. If you cannot make immediate full payment call DENISE HALL at Eric A. Jones, L.L.C., [phone number] at my office to make arrangements to pay this debt.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1118.

\n

That text is followed by a bolded, all-caps notice that the letter is \xe2\x80\x9ca communication from a debt collector.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Signed by Eric A. Jones, \xe2\x80\x9cOutside Counsel for the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s Office,\xe2\x80\x9d the letter includes Jones\xe2\x80\x99s telephone and fax numbers. Ibid. A tear-away portion at the bottom of the page for return of payment is addressed to Jones\xe2\x80\x99s law office. Ibid.

\n

After receiving these letters, Meadows and Gillie filed a putative class action in the United States District Court for the Southern District of Ohio, asserting that Mark Sheriff, Sarah Sheriff, Jones, and their law firms had violated the FDCPA. By sending debt collection notices on the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead rather than the letterhead of their private firms, Meadows and Gillie alleged, defendants had employed deceptive and misleading means to attempt to collect consumer debts. The Ohio Attorney General intervened as a defendant and counterclaimant, seeking a declaratory judgment that special counsel\xe2\x80\x99s use of his letterhead, as authorized by Ohio law,[2] is neither false nor misleading. Further, the Attorney General urged, special counsel should be deemed officers of the State and therefore outside the FDCPA\xe2\x80\x99s compass.

\n

The District Court granted summary judgment for defendants, concluding that special counsel are \xe2\x80\x9cofficers\xe2\x80\x9d of the State of Ohio and, in any event, their use of the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead is not false or misleading. Gillie v. Law Office of Eric A. Jones, LLC, 37 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 3d 928 (2014).

\n

The Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit vacated the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment. Because special counsel are independent contractors, the court determined, they are not entitled to the FDCPA\xe2\x80\x99s state-officer exemption. 785 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1097\xe2\x80\x931098. Turning to the deceptive and misleading practices charge, the Court of Appeals concluded that there is a genuine issue of material fact as to whether an unsophisticated consumer would be misled \xe2\x80\x9cinto believing it is the Attorney General who is collecting on the account.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1106. The court therefore remanded the case for trial on this issue. Id., at 1110.

\n

Judge Sutton dissented from both holdings. In his view, \xe2\x80\x9cdeputizing .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. private lawyers to act as assistant attorneys general makes them \xe2\x80\x98officers\xe2\x80\x99 of the State for .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. collection purposes.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. He further concluded that special counsel\xe2\x80\x99s use of the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead \xe2\x80\x9caccurately describes the relevant legal realities\xe2\x80\x94that the law firm acts as an agent of the Attorney General and stands in [his] shoes .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in collecting money owed to the State.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1110\xe2\x80\x931111. The Sixth Circuit denied en banc rehearing. We granted certiorari, 577 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015), and now reverse.[3]

\n

II

\n

As they did below, petitioners maintain that, as special counsel appointed by the Attorney General, they are \xe2\x80\x9cofficers\xe2\x80\x9d exempt from the FDCPA\xe2\x80\x99s governance, and that, in any case, the debt collection letters they sent to respondents comply with the Act. We pretermit the question whether, as petitioners contend and Judge Sutton would have held, special counsel qualify as state officers. For purposes of this decision, we assume, arguendo, that special counsel are not \xe2\x80\x9cofficers\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning of the Act and, therefore, rank simply as \xe2\x80\x9cdebt collectors\xe2\x80\x9d within the FDCPA\xe2\x80\x99s compass. We conclude, nevertheless, that petitioners complied with the Act, as their use of the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead accurately conveys that special counsel act on behalf of the Attorney General.

\n

Special counsel\xe2\x80\x99s use of the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead at the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s direction does not offend \xc2\xa71692e\xe2\x80\x99s general prohibition against \xe2\x80\x9cfalse .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. or misleading representation[s].\xe2\x80\x9d The letterhead identifies the principal\xe2\x80\x94Ohio\xe2\x80\x99s Attorney General\xe2\x80\x94and the signature block names the agent\xe2\x80\x94a private lawyer hired as outside counsel to the Attorney General. It would not transgress \xc2\xa71692e, respondents acknowledge, if, in lieu of using the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead, special counsel\xe2\x80\x99s communications opened with a bold-face statement: \xe2\x80\x9cWe write to you as special counsel to the [A]ttorney [G]eneral who has authorized us to collect a debt you owe to [the State or an instrumentality thereof].\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 31 (internal quotation marks omitted). If that representation is accurate, i.e., not \xe2\x80\x9cfalse .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. or misleading,\xe2\x80\x9d it would make scant sense to rank as unlawful use of a letterhead conveying the very same message, particularly in view of the inclusion of special counsel\xe2\x80\x99s separate contact information and the conspicuous notation that the letter is sent by a debt collector.[4]

\n

Our conclusion is bolstered by the character of the relationship between special counsel and the Attorney General. As earlier recounted, special counsel \xe2\x80\x9cprovide legal services on behalf of the Attorney General to one or more State Clients\xe2\x80\x9d in furtherance of the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s responsibilities as debt collector for state-owned entities and instrumentalities. App. 143\xe2\x80\x93144. In performing this function, special counsel work closely with attorneys in the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s Office. For example, Assistant Attorneys General \xe2\x80\x9cfrequently assist Special Counsel in drafting pleadings, and sometimes join cases as co-counsel to assist Special Counsel with particularly sensitive or complex cases.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 102. Special counsel and Assistant Attorneys General even stand in one another\xe2\x80\x99s stead, as needed, to cover proceedings in ongoing litigation. Ibid. Given special counsel\xe2\x80\x99s alliance with attorneys within the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s Office, a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s impression that a letter from special counsel is a letter from the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s Office is scarcely inaccurate.[5]

\n

On safe ground with respect to \xc2\xa71692e\xe2\x80\x99s general proscription against false and misleading representations, special counsel\xe2\x80\x99s use of the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead is consistent too with \xc2\xa71692e(9)\xe2\x80\x99s specific prohibition against \xe2\x80\x9cfalsely represent[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d that a communication is \xe2\x80\x9cauthorized, issued, or approved\xe2\x80\x9d by a State. In enacting this provision, Congress sought to prevent debt collectors from \xe2\x80\x9cmisrepresenting\xe2\x80\x9d that they are \xe2\x80\x9cgovernment official[s].\xe2\x80\x9d S. Rep. No. 95\xe2\x80\x93382, p. 8 (1977). Here, the Attorney General authorized\xe2\x80\x94indeed required\xe2\x80\x94special counsel to use his letterhead in sending debt collection communications. Special counsel create no false impression in doing just what they have been instructed to do. Instead, their use of the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead conveys on whose authority special counsel writes to the debtor. As a whole, the communication alerts the debtor to both the basis for the payment obligation and the official responsible for enforcement of debts owed to the State, while the signature block conveys who the Attorney General has engaged to collect the debt.

\n

Nor did special counsel, in sending letters on the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead, use a name other than their \xe2\x80\x9ctrue name,\xe2\x80\x9d in violation of \xc2\xa71692e(14). Although the FDCPA does not say \xe2\x80\x9cwhat a \xe2\x80\x98true name\xe2\x80\x99 is, its import is straightforward: A debt collector may not lie about his institutional affiliation.\xe2\x80\x9d 785 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1115 (Sutton, J., dissenting). Special counsel do not employ a false name when using the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead at his instruction, for special counsel, as the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s agents, act for him in debt-related matters. Far from misrepresenting special counsel\xe2\x80\x99s identity, letters sent by special counsel accurately identify the office primarily responsible for collection of the debt (the Attorney General), special counsel\xe2\x80\x99s affiliation with that office, and the address (special counsel\xe2\x80\x99s law firm) to which payment should be sent.[6]

\n

We further note a federalism concern. \xe2\x80\x9cOhio\xe2\x80\x99s enforcement of its civil code\xe2\x80\x94by collecting money owed to it\xe2\x80\x94[is] a core sovereign function.\xe2\x80\x9d Gillie v. Law Office of Eric A. Jones, LLC, No. 14\xe2\x80\x933836 (CA6, July 14, 2015), App. to Pet. for Cert. 10a (Sutton, J., dissenting from denial of rehearing en banc). Ohio\xe2\x80\x99s Attorney General has chosen toappoint special counsel to assist him in fulfilling his obliga-tion to collect the State\xe2\x80\x99s debts, and he has instructed his appointees to use his letterhead when acting on his behalf. There is no cause, in this case, to construe federal law in a manner that interferes with \xe2\x80\x9cStates\xe2\x80\x99 arrangements for conducting their own governments.\xe2\x80\x9d Nixon v. Missouri Municipal League,\n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 125,\n140 (2004)\n (citing Greg-ory v. Ashcroft,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 452,\n460 (1991)\n).

\n

The Sixth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s contrary exposition is unconvincing. Use of the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead, the Court of Appeals emphasized, has led to confusion among debtors, as the Attorney General has received phone calls inquiring whether letters sent by special counsel are authentic. 785 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1107. But the Sixth Circuit overlooked that the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s prompt and invariable answer to those inquiries was \xe2\x80\x9cyes.\xe2\x80\x9d To the extent that consumers may be concerned that the letters are a \xe2\x80\x9cscam,\xe2\x80\x9d the solution is for special counsel to say more, not less, about their role as agents of the Attorney General. Special counsel\xe2\x80\x99s use of the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead, furthermore, encourages consumers to use official channels to ensure the legitimacy of the letters, assuaging the very concern the Sixth Circuit identified.

\n

In addition to the specter of consumer confusion, the Sixth Circuit stressed the risk of intimidation\xe2\x80\x94that the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead would \xe2\x80\x9cplace pressure on those individuals receiving the letters\xe2\x80\x9d to pay their state debts. Id., at 1105. There are two bases for this concern, neither of which is persuasive. First, invocation of the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s imprimatur could lead debtors to prioritize their debt to the State over other, private debts out of a belief that the consequences of failing to pay a state debt would be more severe. This impression is not false; the State does have enforcement powers beyond those afforded private creditors. A debtor\xe2\x80\x99s tax refund, for example, \xe2\x80\x9cmay be applied in satisfaction\xe2\x80\x9d of her debt, regardless of whether the State has obtained a judgment, Ohio Rev. Code Ann. \xc2\xa75747.12 (Lexis 2013), and a debt owed to the State takes priority over most private debts in state probate proceedings, \xc2\xa72117.25(A) (Lexis Supp. 2015). \xe2\x80\x9cThe special consequences of state debts explain why the Act bars debt collectors unaffiliated with a State from using the State\xe2\x80\x99s name to scare debtors into paying. When the State itself is doing the demanding, however, nothing about the resulting fear misleads.\xe2\x80\x9d 785 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1116 (Sutton, J., dissenting). In other words, \xc2\xa71692e bars debt collectors from deceiving or misleading consumers; it does not protect consumers from fearing the actual consequences of their debts.

\n

Second, debtors might worry that the letters imply that the Attorney General, as the State\xe2\x80\x99s top law enforcement official, intends to take punitive action against them. \xe2\x80\x9cBut neither of the milquetoast letters [received by respondents] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. threatens criminal prosecution, civil penalties, or any action whatsoever.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1116\xe2\x80\x931117. Use of the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s letterhead merely clarifies that the debt is owed to the State, and the Attorney General is the State\xe2\x80\x99s debt collector. The FDCPA is not sensibly read to require special counsel to obscure that reality.[7]

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

For the reasons stated, the judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

It is so ordered.
