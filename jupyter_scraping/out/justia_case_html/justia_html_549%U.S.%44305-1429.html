TRAVELERS CASUALTY & SURETY CO. OF AMERICA v. PACIFIC GAS & ELECTRIC CO.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 05\xe2\x80\x931429.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 16, 2007\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 20, 2007

\n

After respondent (PG&E) filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy, petitioner (Travelers), which had previously issued a surety bond to guarantee PG&E\xe2\x80\x99s payment of state workers\xe2\x80\x99 compensation benefits, asserted a claim in the bankruptcy action to protect itself should PG&E default on the benefits. With the Bankruptcy Court\xe2\x80\x99s approval, PG&E agreed to insert language into its reorganization plan and disclosure statement to protect Travelers in case of such a default. Additional litigation over the negotiated language nevertheless ensued and was ultimately resolved by a court-approved stipulation stating, inter alia, that Travelers could assert a general unsecured claim for attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees, which were authorized in the parties\xe2\x80\x99 original indemnity agreements. When Travelers filed an amended claim for such fees, PG&E objected based on the rule the Ninth Circuit adopted in its prior Fobian decision that where the litigated issues involve not basic contract enforcement questions, but issues peculiar to federal bankruptcy law, attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees generally will not be awarded. The Bankruptcy Court rejected Travelers\xe2\x80\x99 claim on that basis, and the District Court and the Ninth Circuit affirmed.

\n

Held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0Federal bankruptcy law does not disallow contract-based claims for attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees based solely on the fact that the fees were incurred litigating bankruptcy law issues. Because the Fobian rule finds no support in federal bankruptcy law, the Ninth Circuit erred in disallowing Travelers\xe2\x80\x99 claim. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The American rule that \xe2\x80\x9cthe prevailing litigant is ordinarily not entitled to collect a reasonable attorneys\xe2\x80\x99 fee from the loser,\xe2\x80\x9d Alyeska Pipeline Service Co. v. Wilderness Society, 421 U. S. 240, 247, may be overcome by, inter alia, an \xe2\x80\x9cenforceable contract\xe2\x80\x9d allocating such fees, Fleischmann Distilling Corp. v. Maier Brewing Co., 386 U. S. 714, 717. A contract allocating attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees that is enforceable under substantive, nonbankruptcy law is allowable in bankruptcy except where the Bankruptcy Code provides otherwise. Cf. Security Mortgage Co. v. Powers, 278 U. S. 149, 154. The Code does not do so here. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Under the Bankruptcy Code, the bankruptcy court \xe2\x80\x9cshall allow\xe2\x80\x9d a creditor\xe2\x80\x99s claim \xe2\x80\x9cexcept to the extent that\xe2\x80\x9d the claim implicates any of nine enumerated exceptions. 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7502(b). Because Travelers\xe2\x80\x99 attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees claim has nothing to do with the exceptions set forth in \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7502(b)(2)\xe2\x80\x93(9), it must be allowed unless it is unenforceable under \xc2\xa7502(b)(1), which disallows any claim that is \xe2\x80\x9cunenforceable against the debtor and property of the debtor, under any agreement or applicable law for a reason other than because such claim is contingent or unmatured.\xe2\x80\x9d Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Section 502(b)(1) is most naturally understood to provide that, with limited exceptions, any defense to a claim that is available outside of the bankruptcy context is also available in bankruptcy. This reading is consistent not only with the plain statutory text, but also with the settled principle that \xe2\x80\x9c[c]reditors\xe2\x80\x99 entitlements in bankruptcy arise in the first instance from the underlying substantive law creating the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s obligation, subject to any qualifying or contrary provisions of the Bankruptcy Code.\xe2\x80\x9d Raleigh v. Illinois Dept. of Revenue, 530 U. S. 15, 20. That principle requires bankruptcy courts to consult state law in determining the validity of most claims. See ibid. Thus, when the Code uses the word \xe2\x80\x9cclaim\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94i.e., a \xe2\x80\x9cright to payment,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7101(5)(A)\xe2\x80\x94it is usually referring to a right to payment recognized under state law, \xe2\x80\x9c[u]nless some federal interest requires a different result,\xe2\x80\x9d Butner v. United States, 440 U. S. 48, 55. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(d)\xc2\xa0The Fobian rule finds no support in \xc2\xa7502 or elsewhere in federal bankruptcy law. The Fobian court did not identify any Code provision as presenting such support, but instead cited three of its own prior decisions, none of which identified any basis for disallowing a contractual claim for attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees. Nor did the court have occasion to do so; in each of those cases, the attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees claim failed as a matter of state law. The absence of such textual support is fatal for the Fobian rule. See FCC v. NextWave Personal Communications Inc., 537 U. S. 293, 302. In light of \xc2\xa7502(b)(1)\xe2\x80\x99s broad, permissive scope, and the Court\xe2\x80\x99s prior recognition that \xe2\x80\x9cthe character of [a contractual] obligation to pay attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees presents no obstacle to enforcing it in bankruptcy,\xe2\x80\x9d it necessarily follows that the Fobian rule cannot stand. Security Mortgage, supra, at 154. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0The Court expresses no opinion as to PG&E\xe2\x80\x99s arguments that unsecured claims for contractual attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees, such as Travelers\xe2\x80\x99, are categorically disallowed by \xc2\xa7506(b), which expressly authorizes such fees \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o the extent that an allowed secured claim is secured by property [whose] value [exceeds] the amount of such claim,\xe2\x80\x9d and that such disallowance is confirmed by the Bankruptcy Code\xe2\x80\x99s structure and purpose, as examined against the backdrop of pre-Code bankruptcy law. The Court ordinarily does not consider arguments, such as these, that were neither raised nor addressed below, Cooper Industries, Inc. v. Aviall Services, Inc., 543 U. S. 157, 168\xe2\x80\x93169, and PG&E has not identified any circumstances warranting an exception to that rule here. PG&E\xe2\x80\x99s insistence that its arguments are \xe2\x80\x9cfairly included\xe2\x80\x9d within the question presented in the certiorari petition is not persuasive. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

167 Fed. Appx. 593, vacated and remanded.

\nOPINION OF THE COURT
\nTRAVELERS CASUALTY & SURETY CO. OF AMERICA V.PACIFIC GAS & ELEC. CO.
\n549 U. S. ____ (2007)
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 05-1429

\n

TRAVELERS CASUALTY AND SURETY COMPANY OF\nAMERICA, PETITIONER v. PACIFIC GAS AND\nELECTRIC COMPANY

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[March 20, 2007]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Alito delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We are asked to consider whether federal bankruptcy law precludes an unsecured creditor from recovering attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees authorized by a prepetition contract and incurred in postpetition litigation. The Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit held, based on a rule previously adopted by that court, that such fees are categorically prohibited\xe2\x80\x94even where the contractual allocation of attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees would be enforceable under applicable nonbankruptcy law\xe2\x80\x94to the extent the litigation involves issues of federal bankruptcy law. Because that rule finds no support in the Bankruptcy Code, we vacate and\nremand.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent Pacific Gas and Electric Company (PG&E) filed a voluntary Chapter 11 bankruptcy petition in April 2001, 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71101 et seq., and continued thereafter to operate its business as a \xe2\x80\x9cdebtor in possession.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71107(a), 1108. The bankruptcy filing caught the attention of petitioner Travelers Casualty & Surety Company (Travelers), which had previously issued a $100 million surety bond on PG&E\xe2\x80\x99s behalf to the California Department of Industrial Relations, guaranteeing PG&E\xe2\x80\x99s payment of state workers\xe2\x80\x99 compensation benefits to injured employees.[Footnote 1] In connection with the bond, PG&E executed a series of indemnity agreements in favor of Travelers. The indemnity agreements provide that PG&E will be responsible for any loss Travelers might incur in connection with the bonds, including any attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees incurred in pursuing, protecting, or litigating Travelers\xe2\x80\x99 rights in connection with those bonds.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although no default occurred, Travelers asserted a claim in the bankruptcy action to protect itself in case PG&E defaulted on its workers\xe2\x80\x99 compensation benefits at some point in the future, requiring Travelers to make payments under its bond. In response to Travelers\xe2\x80\x99 claim, and with the knowledge and approval of the Bankruptcy Court, PG&E agreed to insert language into its reorganization plan and disclosure statement to protect Travelers\xe2\x80\x99 right to indemnity and subrogation in the event of a default by PG&E.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Travelers claims, however, that PG&E then unilaterally altered the negotiated language in a way that substantially diminished the protection it had been seeking. According to Travelers, that development resulted in additional litigation, but Travelers and PG&E ultimately resolved the dispute by entering into a stipulation that was later approved by the Bankruptcy Court. In addition to accommodating Travelers\xe2\x80\x99 substantive concerns, the stipulation stated that Travelers \xe2\x80\x9cmay assert its claim for attorneys\xe2\x80\x99 fees under the [i]ndemnity [a]greements\xe2\x80\x9d (subject to PG&E\xe2\x80\x99s right to object) as a general unsecured claim against PG&E. Brief for Petitioner 17.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Travelers subsequently filed an amended proof of claim seeking to recover the attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees it incurred in connection with PG&E\xe2\x80\x99s bankruptcy proceedings. PG&E objected, arguing that Travelers could not recover attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees incurred while litigating issues of bankruptcy law.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Bankruptcy Court agreed and rejected Travelers\xe2\x80\x99 claim on that basis. App. to Pet. for Cert. 23a\xe2\x80\x9325a. Travelers appealed that ruling to the District Court. The District Court affirmed, relying on In\xc2\xa0re Fobian, 951 F.\xc2\xa02d 1149 (CA9 1991), which held that \xe2\x80\x9cwhere the litigated issues involve not basic contract enforcement questions, but issues peculiar to federal bankruptcy law, attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees will not be awarded absent bad faith or harassment by the losing party,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 1153. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 10a, 17a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Travelers appealed again, and the United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit affirmed. 167 Fed. Appx. 593 (2006). The panel acknowledged that, in at least some circumstances, a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98prevailing party in a bankruptcy proceeding may be entitled to an award of attorney fees in accordance with applicable state law . . . .\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 594 (quoting In\xc2\xa0re Baroff, 105 F.\xc2\xa03d 439, 441 (CA9 1997)). The panel nevertheless rejected Travelers\xe2\x80\x99 claim based on the Fobian rule, which it cited for the proposition that \xe2\x80\x9cattorney fees are not recoverable in bankruptcy for litigating issues \xe2\x80\x98peculiar to federal bankruptcy law.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 167 Fed. Appx., at 594 (quoting Fobian, supra, at 1153). The panel explained that, because the fees claimed by Travelers were incurred litigating issues that were \xe2\x80\x9cgoverned entirely by federal bankruptcy law,\xe2\x80\x9d Travelers\xe2\x80\x99 claim necessarily failed.[Footnote 2] 167 Fed. Appx., at 594.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Travelers sought review in this Court, noting a conflict among the Courts of Appeals regarding the validity of the Fobian rule. Compare Fobian, supra, at 1153, with In\xc2\xa0re Shangra-La, Inc., 167 F.\xc2\xa03d 843, 848\xe2\x80\x93849 (CA4 1999). We granted certiorari to resolve that conflict, 549 U. S. ___ (2006).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under the American Rule, \xe2\x80\x9cthe prevailing litigant is ordinarily not entitled to collect a reasonable attorneys\xe2\x80\x99 fee from the loser.\xe2\x80\x9d Alyeska Pipeline Service Co. v. Wilderness Society, 421 U. S. 240, 247 (1975); see Hauenstein v. Lynham, 100 U. S. 483, 490\xe2\x80\x93491 (1880); Arcambel v. Wiseman, 3 Dall. 306 (1796). This default rule can, of course, be overcome by statute. Fleischmann Distilling Corp. v. Maier Brewing Co., 386 U. S. 714, 717 (1967). It can also be overcome by an \xe2\x80\x9cenforceable contract\xe2\x80\x9d allocating attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees. Ibid.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In a case governed by the Bankruptcy Act of 1898, we observed that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he character of [a contractual] obligation to pay attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees presents no obstacle to enforcing it in bankruptcy, either as a provable claim or by way of a lien upon specific property.\xe2\x80\x9d Security Mortgage Co. v. Powers, 278 U. S. 149, 154 (1928). Similarly, under the terms of the current Bankruptcy Code, it remains true that an otherwise enforceable contract allocating attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees (i.e., one that is enforceable under substantive, nonbankruptcy law) is allowable in bankruptcy except where the Bankruptcy Code provides otherwise. See 4 Collier on Bankruptcy \xc2\xb6 506.04[3][a], p. 506\xe2\x80\x93118 (rev. 15th ed. 2006) (hereinafter Collier).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case requires us to consider whether the Bankruptcy Code disallows contract-based claims for attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees based solely on the fact that the fees at issue were incurred litigating issues of bankruptcy law. We conclude that it does not.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When a debtor declares bankruptcy, each of its creditors is entitled to file a proof of claim\xe2\x80\x94i.e., a document providing proof of a \xe2\x80\x9cright to payment,\xe2\x80\x9d 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7101(5)(A)\xe2\x80\x94against the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s estate. Once a proof of claim has been filed, the court must determine whether the claim is \xe2\x80\x9callowed\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa7502(a) of the Bankruptcy Code: \xe2\x80\x9cA claim or interest, proof of which is filed under section 501 . . . is deemed allowed, unless a party in interest . . . objects.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But even where a party in interest objects, the court \xe2\x80\x9cshall allow\xe2\x80\x9d the claim \xe2\x80\x9cexcept to the extent that\xe2\x80\x9d the claim implicates any of the nine exceptions enumerated in \xc2\xa7502(b). Ibid. Those exceptions apply where the claim at issue is \xe2\x80\x9cunenforceable against the debtor \xe2\x80\xa6 under any agreement or applicable law,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7502(b)(1); \xe2\x80\x9cis for unmatured interest,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7502(b)(2); \xe2\x80\x9cis for [property tax that] exceeds the value of the [estate\xe2\x80\x99s] interest\xe2\x80\x9d in the property, \xc2\xa7502(b)(3); \xe2\x80\x9cis for services of an insider or attorney of the debtor\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cexceeds the reasonable value of such services,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7502(b)(4); is for unmatured debt on certain alimony and child support obligations, \xc2\xa7502(b)(5); is for certain \xe2\x80\x9cdamages resulting from the termination\xe2\x80\x9d of a lease or employment contract, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7502(b)(6) and (7); \xe2\x80\x9cresults from a reduction, due to late payment, in the amount of \xe2\x80\xa6 credit available to the debtor in connection with an employment tax on wages, salaries, or commissions earned from the debtor,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7502(b)(8); or was brought to the court\xe2\x80\x99s attention through an untimely proof of claim, \xc2\xa7502(b)(9).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Travelers\xe2\x80\x99 claim for attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees has nothing to do with property tax, child support or alimony, services provided by an attorney of the debtor, damages resulting from the termination of a lease or employment contract, or the late payment of any employment tax. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7502(b)(2)\xe2\x80\x93(8). Nor does it appear that the proof of claim was untimely. See \xc2\xa7502(b)(9). Thus, Travelers\xe2\x80\x99 claim must be allowed under \xc2\xa7502(b) unless it is unenforceable within the meaning of \xc2\xa7502(b)(1).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 502(b)(1) disallows any claim that is \xe2\x80\x9cunenforceable against the debtor and property of the debtor, under any agreement or applicable law for a reason other than because such claim is contingent or unmatured.\xe2\x80\x9d This provision is most naturally understood to provide that, with limited exceptions, any defense to a claim that is available outside of the bankruptcy context is also available in bankruptcy. See 4 Collier \xc2\xb6\xc2\xa0502.03[2][b], at 502\xe2\x80\x9322 (explaining that \xc2\xa7502(b)(1) is generally understood to \xe2\x80\x9cmake available to the trustee any defense\xe2\x80\x9d available to the debtor \xe2\x80\x9cunder applicable nonbankruptcy law\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94i.e., any defense that the debtor \xe2\x80\x9ccould have interposed, absent bankruptcy, in a suit on the [same substantive] claim by the creditor\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This reading of \xc2\xa7502(b)(1) is consistent not only with the plain statutory text, but also with the settled principle that \xe2\x80\x9c[c]reditors\xe2\x80\x99 entitlements in bankruptcy arise in the first instance from the underlying substantive law creating the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s obligation, subject to any qualifying or contrary provisions of the Bankruptcy Code.\xe2\x80\x9d Raleigh v. Illinois Dept. of Revenue, 530 U. S. 15, 20 (2000). That principle requires bankruptcy courts to consult state law in determining the validity of most claims. See ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Indeed, we have long recognized that the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98basic federal rule\xe2\x80\x99 in bankruptcy is that state law governs the substance of claims, Congress having \xe2\x80\x98generally left the determination of property rights in the assets of a bankrupt\xe2\x80\x99s estate to state law.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Butner v. United States, 440 U. S. 48, 57, 54 (1979); citation omitted). Accordingly, when the Bankruptcy Code uses the word \xe2\x80\x9cclaim\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94which the Code itself defines as a \xe2\x80\x9cright to payment,\xe2\x80\x9d 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7101(5)(A)\xe2\x80\x94it is usually referring to a right to payment recognized under state law. As we stated in Butner, \xe2\x80\x9c[p]roperty interests are created and defined by state law,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c[u]nless some federal interest requires a different result, there is no reason why such interests should be analyzed differently simply because an interested party is involved in a bankruptcy proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d 440 U. S., at 55; accord, Vanston Bondholders Protective Comm. v. Green, 329 U. S. 156, 161 (1946) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhat claims of creditors are valid and subsisting obligations against the bankrupt at the time a petition in bankruptcy is filed is a question which, in the absence of overruling federal law, is to be determined by reference to state law\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In rejecting Travelers\xe2\x80\x99 claim for contractual attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees, the Court of Appeals did not conclude that the claim was \xe2\x80\x9cunenforceable\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa7502(b)(1) as a matter of applicable nonbankruptcy law. Nor did it conclude that Travelers\xe2\x80\x99 claim was rendered unenforceable by any provision of the Bankruptcy Code. To the contrary, the court acknowledged that, in at least some circumstances, a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98prevailing party in a bankruptcy proceeding may be entitled to an award of attorney fees in accordance with applicable state law . . . .\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 167 Fed. Appx., at 594 (quoting Baroff, 105 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 441).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The court nevertheless rejected Travelers\xe2\x80\x99 claim based solely on a rule of that court\xe2\x80\x99s own creation\xe2\x80\x94the so-called Fobian rule\xe2\x80\x94which dictates that \xe2\x80\x9cattorney fees are not recoverable in bankruptcy for litigating issues \xe2\x80\x98peculiar to federal bankruptcy law.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 167 Fed. Appx., at 594 (quoting Fobian, 951 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 1153). The court explained that, because the fees claimed by Travelers were incurred litigating issues that were \xe2\x80\x9cgoverned entirely by federal bankruptcy law,\xe2\x80\x9d 167 Fed. Appx., at 594, Travelers\xe2\x80\x99 claim necessarily failed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Fobian rule finds no support in the Bankruptcy Code, either in \xc2\xa7502 or elsewhere. In Fobian, the court did not identify any provision of the Bankruptcy Code as providing support for the new rule. See 951 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 1153. Instead, the court cited three of its own prior decisions, In\xc2\xa0re Johnson, 756 F.\xc2\xa02d 738 (1985); In\xc2\xa0re Coast Trading Co., 744 F.\xc2\xa02d 686 (1984); and In\xc2\xa0re Fulwiler, 624 F.\xc2\xa02d 908 (1980) (per curium). Significantly, in none of those cases did the court identify any basis for disallowing a contractual claim for attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees incurred litigating issues of federal bankruptcy law. Nor did the court have occasion to do so; in each of those cases, the claim for attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees failed as a matter of state law. See Johnson, supra, at 741\xe2\x80\x93742; Coast Trading, supra, at 693; Fulwiler, supra, at 910.[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The absence of textual support is fatal for the Fobian rule. Consistent with our prior statements regarding creditors\xe2\x80\x99 entitlements in bankruptcy, see, e.g., Raleigh, 530 U. S., at 20, we generally presume that claims enforceable under applicable state law will be allowed in bankruptcy unless they are expressly disallowed. See 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7502(b). Neither the court below nor PG&E has offered any reason why the fact that the attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees in this case were incurred litigating issues of federal bankruptcy law overcomes that presumption.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 502(b)(4) is instructive on this point. That provision expressly disallows claims for a particular category of attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees\xe2\x80\x94those \xe2\x80\x9cfor services of an \xe2\x80\xa6 attorney of the debtor,\xe2\x80\x9d to the extent the claimed fees \xe2\x80\x9cexcee[d] the reasonable value of such services.\xe2\x80\x9d The existence of that provision suggests that, in its absence, a claim for such fees would be allowed in bankruptcy to the extent enforceable under state law. The absence of an analogous provision excluding the category of fees covered by the Fobian rule likewise suggests that the Code does not categorically disallow them. See 4 Collier \xc2\xb6 506.04[3][a], at 506\xe2\x80\x93118 (concluding that Fobian \xe2\x80\x9cinverts the proper analysis\xe2\x80\x9d by allowing attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees only where they are expressly authorized by the Bankruptcy Code, and explaining that \xe2\x80\x9ca claim for attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees arising in the context of litigating bankruptcy issues must be allowed if valid under applicable state law\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress, of course, has the power to amend the Bankruptcy Code by adding a provision expressly disallowing claims for attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees incurred by creditors in the litigation of bankruptcy issues. But because no such provision exists, the Bankruptcy Code provides no basis for disallowing Travelers\xe2\x80\x99 claim on the grounds stated by the Ninth Circuit.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As we explained in FCC v. NextWave Personal Communications Inc., 537 U. S. 293 (2003), \xe2\x80\x9cwhere Congress has intended to provide . . . exceptions to provisions of the Bankruptcy Code, it has done so clearly and expressly.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 302. Here, the Bankruptcy Code does not \xe2\x80\x9cclearly and expressly\xe2\x80\x9d compel courts to follow the Fobian rule; on the contrary, the Code says nothing about unsecured claims for contractual attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees incurred while litigating issues of bankruptcy law. In light of the broad, permissive scope of \xc2\xa7502(b)(1), and our prior recognition that \xe2\x80\x9cthe character of [a contractual] obligation to pay attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees presents no obstacle to enforcing it in bankruptcy,\xe2\x80\x9d it necessarily follows that the Fobian rule cannot stand. Security Mortgage, 278 U. S., at 154; see Cohen v. de la Cruz, 523 U. S. 213, 221 (1998) (\xe2\x80\x9cWe . . . \xe2\x80\x98will not read the Bankruptcy Code to erode past bankruptcy practice absent a clear indication that Congress intended such a departure\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Pennsylvania Dept. of Public Welfare v. Davenport, 495 U. S. 552, 563 (1990))).

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0PG&E makes no effort to defend the Fobian rule. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 28 (conceding that PG&E does not defend the Fobian rule, and acknowledging that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Fobian rule is wrong . . . as to the distinction that it draws between State law and Federal litigation\xe2\x80\x9d). Instead, PG&E argues that \xc2\xa7506(b) categorically disallows unsecured claims for contractual attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees and\xe2\x80\x94noting that Travelers\xe2\x80\x99 claim is unsecured\xe2\x80\x94asks us to affirm on that basis. Section 506(b) provides as follows:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cTo the extent that an allowed secured claim is secured by property the value of which \xe2\x80\xa6 is greater than the amount of such claim, there shall be allowed to the holder of such claim, interest on such claim, and any reasonable fees, costs, or charges provided for under the agreement or State statute under which such claim arose.\xe2\x80\x9d 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0A. \xc2\xa7506(b) (Supp. 2006).

\n

\nAccording to PG&E, this provision authorizes claims for contractual attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees to the extent the creditor is oversecured, but disallows such claims to the extent the creditor is either not oversecured or (like Travelers) completely unsecured. This reading of the Code, PG&E argues, \xe2\x80\x9cis not a matter of negative implication, but of explicit negation.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 18. PG&E also argues that the structure and purpose of the Bankruptcy Code, examined against the backdrop of pre-Code bankruptcy law, confirm that Congress did not intend to allow unsecured creditors to recover attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees. See id., at 25\xe2\x80\x9338.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0PG&E did not raise these arguments below. Consequently, none of the lower courts had occasion to address them. Nor were these arguments presented in PG&E\xe2\x80\x99s brief in opposition to certiorari. PG&E nevertheless insists that we should address these arguments as though they were \xe2\x80\x9cfairly included\xe2\x80\x9d within the question presented in Travelers\xe2\x80\x99 petition for certiorari. See id., at 41. That contention appears to be premised on the theory that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Fobian rule reaches the correct conclusion in this case,\xe2\x80\x9d but \xe2\x80\x9cdoesn\xe2\x80\x99t go far enough in \xe2\x80\xa6 preventing creditors from requiring other creditors to pay for their attorneys\xe2\x80\x99 fees.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 25.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We are not persuaded. We granted certiorari to resolve a conflict among the lower courts regarding the Fobian rule, which is analytically distinct from, and fundamentally at odds with, PG&E\xe2\x80\x99s reading of \xc2\xa7506(b).[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In any event, we ordinarily do not consider claims that were neither raised nor addressed below, Cooper Industries, Inc. v. Aviall Services, Inc., 543 U. S. 157, 168\xe2\x80\x93169 (2004), and PG&E has failed to identify any circumstances that would warrant an exception to that rule in this case. We therefore will not consider these arguments.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, we express no opinion with regard to whether, following the demise of the Fobian rule, other principles of bankruptcy law might provide an independent basis for disallowing Travelers\xe2\x80\x99 claim for attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees. We conclude only that the Court of Appeals erred in disallowing that claim based on the fact that the fees at issue were incurred litigating issues of bankruptcy law.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment of the United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit is therefore vacated, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.
