Syllabus\n

\n

Under the Judiciary Act of 1789, a writ of error to this Court can only be taken out in the case of a final judgment.

\n

When the defendant had entered a plea of the statute of limitations to the call of the complainants and the plea was overruled and the defendant ordered to answer over, a writ of error will not lie.

\n

It appeared that the defendant in the circuit court had pleaded the statute of limitations to the bill of the complainants, and that the plea was overruled and the defendants ordered to answer the bill. On this decree, the present writ of error was sued out and it was moved to quash the writ because it was not a final decree upon which alone a writ of error would lie.

Syllabus\n

\n

Under the Judiciary Act of 1789, a writ of error to this Court can only be taken out in the case of a final judgment.

\n

When the defendant had entered a plea of the statute of limitations to the call of the complainants and the plea was overruled and the defendant ordered to answer over, a writ of error will not lie.

\n

It appeared that the defendant in the circuit court had pleaded the statute of limitations to the bill of the complainants, and that the plea was overruled and the defendants ordered to answer the bill. \n

CHASE, JUSTICE.

\n

In England, a writ of error may be brought upon an interlocutory decree or order, and until a decision obtained upon the writ, the proceedings in the court below are stayed. But here the words of the act which allow a writ of error allow it only in the case of a final judgment.

\n

By the Court:

\n

The writ must be quashed with costs.
