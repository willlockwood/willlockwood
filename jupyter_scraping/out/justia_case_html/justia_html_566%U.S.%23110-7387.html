\n\t\t\t\tSETSER v. UNITED STATES\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 10\xe2\x80\x937387.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 30, 2011\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 28, 2012\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tWhen petitioner Setser was indicted in a Texas court on drug charges, the State also moved to revoke the probation term that he was then serving for another drug offense. At about the same time, Setser pleaded guilty to federal drug charges. The Federal District Court imposed a 151-month sentence to run consecutively to any state sentence imposed for the probation violation, but concurrently with any state sentence imposed on the new drug charge. While Setser\xe2\x80\x99s federal appeal was pending, the state court sentenced him to 5 years for the probation violation and 10 years for the drug charge, but ordered the sentences to be served concurrently. The Fifth Circuit affirmed the federal sentence, holding that the District Court had authority to order a sentence consecutive to an anticipated state sentence, and that Setser\xe2\x80\x99s sentence was reasonable, even if the state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision made it unclear exactly how to administer it.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t1.\xc2\xa0The District Court had discretion to order that Setser\xe2\x80\x99s federal sentence run consecutively to his anticipated state sentence for the probation violation. Pp.\xc2\xa02\xe2\x80\x9312.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0Judges have traditionally had broad discretion in selecting whether the sentences they impose will run concurrently or consecutively with respect to other sentences that they impose, or that have been imposed in other proceedings, including state proceedings, see Oregon v. Ice, \n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 160\n\xe2\x80\x93169. The statutory text and structure do not foreclose a district court\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of this discretion with respect to anticipated state sentences. The Sentencing Reform Act of 1984 addresses the concurrent-vs.-consecutive decision, but not the situation here, since the District Court did not impose \xe2\x80\x9cmultiple terms of imprisonment .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. at the same time,\xe2\x80\x9d and Setser was not \xe2\x80\x9calready subject to\xe2\x80\x9d the state sentences at issue, \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73584(a). This does not mean, as Setser and the Government claim, that the District Court lacked authority to act as it did and that the Bureau of Prisons is to make the concurrent-vs.-consecutive decision after the federal sentence has been imposed. Section 3621(b), from which the Bureau claims to derive this authority, says nothing about concurrent or consecutive sentences. And it is more natural to read \xc2\xa73584(a) as leaving room for the exercise of judicial discretion in situations not covered than it is to read \xc2\xa73621(b) as giving the Bureau what amounts to sentencing authority. Setser\xe2\x80\x99s arguments to the contrary are unpersuasive. Pp.\xc2\xa02\xe2\x80\x938.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0None of the other objections raised by Setser and the Government requires a different result. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9312.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t2.\xc2\xa0The state court\xe2\x80\x99s subsequent decision to make the state sentences run concurrently does not establish that the Federal District Court imposed an unreasonable sentence. The difficulty here arises not from the federal-court sentence\xe2\x80\x94which is to run concurrently with one state sentence and consecutively with another\xe2\x80\x94but from the state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision. Deciding which of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s dispositions should prevail under these circumstances is a problem, but it does not show the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s sentence to be unlawful. The reasonableness standard for reviewing federal sentences asks whether the district court abused its discretion, see Gall v. United States, \n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 38\n, but Setser identifies no flaw in the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisionmaking process, nor anything available at the time of sentencing that the court failed to consider. Where late-onset facts make it difficult, or even impossible, to implement the sentence, the Bureau of Prisons may determine, in the first instance, how long the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s sentence authorizes it to continue Setser\xe2\x80\x99s confinement, subject to the potential for judicial review. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9314.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tMONROE ACE SETSER, PETITIONER v. UNITED STATES\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\ton writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t[March 28, 2012]\n\t\t\t

\n

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Scalia delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe consider whether a district court, in sentencing a de- fendant for a federal offense, has authority to order that the federal sentence be consecutive to an anticipated state sentence that has not yet been imposed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhen officers of the Lubbock Police Department ar- rested petitioner Monroe Setser for possessing methamphetamine, he was already serving a 5-year term of probation imposed by a Texas court for another drug offense. Setser was indicted in state court for possession with intent to deliver a controlled substance, and the State also moved to revoke his term of probation. As often happens in drug cases, the federal authorities also got involved. A federal grand jury indicted Setser for possessing with intent to distribute 50 grams or more of methamphetamine, \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7841(a)(1), (b)(1)(A)(viii), and he pleaded guilty.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBefore the federal sentencing hearing, a probation officer calculated the applicable Guidelines range to be 121 to 151 months\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment. Citing precedent from the United States Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit, United States v. Brown, 920 F.\xc2\xa02d 1212 (1991) (per curiam), he indicated that the District Court had discretion to make Setser\xe2\x80\x99s sentence either concurrent with or consecutive to any sentence anticipated in the separate state-court pro- ceedings. Setser objected, arguing that the District Court lacked such authority. The court nevertheless made the sentence of 151 months that it imposed consecutive to any state sentence imposed for probation violation, but concurrent with any state sentence imposed on the new drug charge. Setser appealed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhile Setser\xe2\x80\x99s appeal was pending, the state court sentenced him to a prison term of 5 years for probation violation and 10 years on the new drug charge. It ordered that these sentences be served concurrently. Setser then made before the Court of Appeals, in addition to the argument that the District Court had no authority to order a consecutive sentence, the argument that his federal sentence was unreasonable because it was impossible to implement in light of the concurrent state sentences.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit affirmed. 607 F.\xc2\xa03d 128 (2010). Following its earlier Brown decision, the court held that the District Court did have authority to order a consecutive sentence. 607 F. 3d, at 131\xe2\x80\x93132. It also held that Setser\xe2\x80\x99s sentence was reasonable, even if it was \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98partially foiled\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d by the state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision. Id., at 132\xe2\x80\x93133. We granted certiorari, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2011), and appointed an amicus curiae to brief and argue this case in support of the judgment below, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2011).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBefore proceeding further, it is important to be clear about what is at issue. Setser does not contend that his federal sentence must run concurrently with both state sentences imposed after his federal sentencing hearing. He acknowledges that someone must answer \xe2\x80\x9cthe consecutive versus concurrent question,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 27, and decide how the state and federal sentences will fit together. The issue here is who will make that decision, which in turn determines when that decision is made. One possible answer, and the one the Fifth Circuit gave, is that the decision belongs to the Federal District Court at the federal sentencing hearing.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe concurrent-vs.-consecutive decision has been addressed by \xc2\xa7212(a) of the Sentencing Reform Act of 1984, \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73584, reproduced in full as Appendix A, infra. The first subsection of that provision, which says when concurrent and consecutive sentences may be imposed, and specifies which of those dispositions will be assumed in absence of indication by the sentencing judge, does not cover the situation here. It addresses only \xe2\x80\x9cmultiple terms of imprisonment .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. imposed .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. at the same time\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ca term of imprisonment .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. imposed on a defendant who is already subject to an undischarged term of imprisonment.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73584(a). Here the state sentence is not imposed at the same time as the federal sentence, and the defendant was not already subject to that state sentence.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSetser, supported by the Government, argues that, be- cause \xc2\xa73584(a) does not cover this situation, the District Court lacked authority to act as it did; and that the concurrent-vs.-consecutive decision is therefore to be made by the Bureau of Prisons at any time after the federal sen- tence has been imposed. The Bureau of Prisons is said to derive this authority from \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73621(b) (2006 ed. and Supp. IV), reproduced in full as Appendix B, infra.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOn its face, this provision says nothing about concurrent or consecutive sentences, but the Government explains its position as follows: Section 3621(b) gives the Bureau the authority to order that a prisoner serve his federal sentence in any suitable prison facility \xe2\x80\x9cwhether maintained by the Federal Government or otherwise.\xe2\x80\x9d The Bureau may therefore order that a prisoner serve his federal sentence in a state prison. Thus, when a person subject to a federal sentence is serving a state sentence, the Bureau may designate the state prison as the place of impris- onment for the federal sentence\xe2\x80\x94effectively making the two sentences concurrent\xe2\x80\x94or decline to do so\xe2\x80\x94effec- tively making them consecutive.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Based on \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73584(a) and 3621(b), Setser and the Government argue that the concurrent-vs.-consecutive decision, under the circumstances presented here, is committed exclusively to the Bureau of Prisons.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is fundamental that we construe statutes governing the jurisdiction of the federal courts in light of \xe2\x80\x9cthe common-law background against which the statutes .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. were enacted,\xe2\x80\x9d New Orleans Public Service, Inc. v. Council of City of New Orleans, \n\n491 U.\xc2\xa0S. 350, \n359 (1989)\n, and the same approach is appropriate here, where the issue concerns a matter of discretion traditionally committed to the Judiciary. Judges have long been understood to have discretion to select whether the sentences they impose will run concurrently or consecutively with respect to other sentences that they impose, or that have been imposed in other proceedings, including state proceedings. See Oregon v. Ice, \n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 160\n\xe2\x80\x93169 (2009). And a large majority of the federal appellate courts addressing the question have recognized a similar authority in the context here, where a federal judge anticipates a state sentence that has not yet been imposed. See Salley v. United States, 786 F.\xc2\xa02d 546, 547 (CA2 1986); Anderson v. United States, 405 F.\xc2\xa02d 492, 493 (CA10 1969) (per\xc2\xa0curiam); United States ex\xc2\xa0rel. Lester v. Parker, 404 F.\xc2\xa02d 40, 41\xe2\x80\x9342 (CA3 1968) (per curiam); United States v. Kanton, 362 F.\xc2\xa02d 178, 179\xe2\x80\x93180 (CA7 1966) (per\xc2\xa0curiam); but see United States v. Eastman, 758 F.\xc2\xa02d 1315, 1317 (CA9 1985)\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t. We find nothing in the Sentencing Reform Act, or in any other provision of law, to show that Congress foreclosed the exercise of district courts\xe2\x80\x99 sentencing discretion in these circumstances.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSetser\xe2\x80\x99s main contention is that \xc2\xa73584(a) has this effect. But that provision cannot sustain the weight that Setser asks it to bear. In essence, he reads the first sentence in \xc2\xa73584(a) to say that \xe2\x80\x9cterms [of imprisonment] may run concurrently or consecutively\xe2\x80\x9d only \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f multiple terms of imprisonment are imposed .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. at the same time, or if a term of imprisonment is imposed on a defendant who is already subject to an undischarged term of imprisonment.\xe2\x80\x9d Since the District Court was not imposing the state sentence and since it was not already imposed, the sentence could not be ordered to run consecutively. But if the text is exclusive\xe2\x80\x94if the addition of only is correct\xe2\x80\x94the provision forbids not only the imposition of consecutive sentences, but the imposition of concurrent ones as well. And yet, as Setser acknowledges, it must be one or the other; someone must decide the issue.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSetser\xe2\x80\x99s response is that, read in context, the sentence speaks only to district courts. Under the circumstances at issue here, he says, the federal and state sentences still might run either concurrently or consecutively, but just not at the discretion of the District Court. That is an odd parsing of the text, which makes no distinction between the district court and the Bureau of Prisons. The placement of \xc2\xa73584 does indeed suggest that it is directed at district courts\xe2\x80\x94but that is likely because Congress contemplated that only district courts would have the authority to make the concurrent-vs.-consecutive decision, not because Congress meant to leave the Bureau unfettered. Indeed, the Bureau already follows the other directives in \xc2\xa73584(a). See Brief for United States 35. For example, if the district court imposes multiple terms of imprisonment at the same time, but fails to address the concurrent-vs.-consecutive issue, the terms \xe2\x80\x9crun concurrently,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73584(a), and the Bureau is not free to use its \xe2\x80\x9cplace of imprisonment\xe2\x80\x9d authority to achieve a different result.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Latin maxim on which Setser relies\xe2\x80\x94expressio unius est exclusio alterius\xe2\x80\x94might have application here if the provision in question were a conferral of authority on district courts. Giving sentencing authority in only specified circumstances could be said to imply that it is withheld in other circumstances. Section 3584, however, is framed not as a conferral of authority but as a limitation of authority that already exists (and a specification of what will be assumed when the exercise of that authority is ambiguous). It reads not \xe2\x80\x9cDistrict courts shall have authority to impose multiple terms of imprisonment on a defendant at the same time, etc.\xe2\x80\x9d but rather \xe2\x80\x9cIf multiple terms of imprisonment are imposed on a defendant at the same time, [etc.]\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94quite clearly assuming that such au- thority already exists. The mere acknowledgment of the existence of certain pre-existing authority (and regulation of that authority) in no way implies a repeal of other pre-existing authority. And that is especially true when there is an obvious reason for selecting the instances of pre-existing authority that are addressed\xe2\x80\x94to wit, that they are the examples of sentencing discretion most frequently encountered.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMoreover, expressio unius est exclusio alterius is a double-edged sword. Setser thinks it suggests that, because \xc2\xa73584(a) recognizes judicial discretion in scenario A and scenario B, there is no such discretion in scenario C. But the same maxim shows much more convincingly why \xc2\xa73621(b) cannot be read to give the Bureau of Prisons exclusive authority to make the sort of decision committed to the district court in \xc2\xa73584(a). When \xc2\xa73584(a) specific- ally addresses decisions about concurrent and consecutive sentences, and makes no mention of the Bureau\xe2\x80\x99s role in the process, the implication is that no such role exists. And that conclusion is reinforced by application of the same maxim (properly, in this instance) to \xc2\xa73621(b)\xe2\x80\x94which is a conferral of authority on the Bureau of Prisons, but does not confer authority to choose between concurrent and consecutive sentences. Put to the choice, we believe it is much more natural to read \xc2\xa73584(a) as not containing an implied \xe2\x80\x9conly,\xe2\x80\x9d leaving room for the exercise of judicial discretion in the situations not covered, than it is to read \xc2\xa73621(b) as giving the Bureau of Prisons what amounts to sentencing authority.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNone of the other objections to this approach raised by Setser and the Government require a different result.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur decision today follows the interpretive rule they invoke, that we must \xe2\x80\x9cgive effect .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to every clause and word\xe2\x80\x9d of the Act. United States v. Menasche, \n\n348 U.\xc2\xa0S. 528\n\xe2\x80\x93539 (1955) (internal quotation marks omitted). The first sentence in \xc2\xa73584(a) addresses the most common situations in which the decision between concurrent and consecutive sentences must be made: where two sentences are imposed at the same time, and where a sentence is imposed subsequent to a prior sentence that has not yet been fully served. It says that the district court has discretion whether to make the sentences concurrent or consecutive, except that it may not make consecutive a sentence for \xe2\x80\x9can attempt\xe2\x80\x9d and a sentence for an \xe2\x80\x9coffense that was the sole objective of the attempt.\xe2\x80\x9d And the last two sentences of \xc2\xa73584(a) say what will be assumed in those two common situations if the court does not specify that the sentence is concurrent or consecutive. Giving those dispositions full effect does not demand that we regard them as eliminating sentencing discretion in other situations.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSetser and the Government both suggest that, because \xc2\xa73584(b) directs courts to consider the sentencing factors in \xc2\xa73553(a) in making these decisions, and because some of those factors will be difficult to apply with respect to anticipated sentences, the Act cannot be read to allow judicial discretion in these circumstances. One cannot be sure that the sentence imposed is \xe2\x80\x9csufficient, but not greater than necessary,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73553(a), the argument goes, if one does not know how long it will actually be. But the district judge faces the same uncertainty if the concurrent-vs.-consecutive decision is left for later resolution by the Bureau of Prisons; he does not know, for example, whether the 5-year sentence he imposes will be an actual five years or will be simply swallowed within another sentence. To be sure, the Bureau of Prisons, if it waits to decide the matter until after the state court has imposed its sentence, will know for sure what sentences it is dealing with. But the Bureau is not charged with applying \xc2\xa73553(a). The factors that guide the agency\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cplace of imprisonment\xe2\x80\x9d decision do include \xe2\x80\x9cthe nature and circumstances of the offense\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cthe history and characteristics of the pris- oner,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73621(b)(2), (b)(3) (2006 ed.)\xe2\x80\x94factors that are, to be sure, relevant to sentencing but also relevant to selection of the place of confinement; but they also include factors that make little, if any, sense in the sentencing context, such as \xe2\x80\x9cthe resources of the facility contemplated\xe2\x80\x9d and whether the state facility \xe2\x80\x9cmeets minimum standards of health and habitability,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73621(b), (b)(1). (These factors confirm our view that \xc2\xa73621 is not a sentencing provision but a place-of-confinement provision.) It is much more natural for a judge to apply the \xc2\xa73553(a) factors in making all concurrent-vs.-consecutive decisions, than it is for some such decisions to be made by a judge applying \xc2\xa73553(a) factors and others by the Bureau of Prisons applying \xc2\xa73621(b) factors.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe final objection is that principles of federalism and good policy do not allow a district court to make the concurrent-vs.-consecutive decision when it does not have before it all of the information about the anticipated state sentence. As for principles of federalism, it seems to us they cut in precisely the opposite direction. In our American system of dual sovereignty, each sovereign\xe2\x80\x94whether the Federal Government or a State\xe2\x80\x94is responsible for \xe2\x80\x9cthe administration of [its own] criminal justice syste[m].\xe2\x80\x9d Ice, 555 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 170. If a prisoner like Setser starts in state custody, serves his state sentence, and then moves to federal custody, it will always be the Federal Government\xe2\x80\x94whether the district court or the Bureau of Prisons\xe2\x80\x94that decides whether he will receive credit for the time served in state custody. And if he serves his federal sentence first, the State will decide whether to give him credit against his state sentences without being bound by what the district court or the Bureau said on the matter. Given this framework, it is always more respectful of the State\xe2\x80\x99s sovereignty for the district court to make its decision up front rather than for the Bureau of Prisons to make the decision after the state court has acted. That way, the state court has all of the information before it when it acts.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t The Government\xe2\x80\x99s position does not promote the States\xe2\x80\x99 interest\xe2\x80\x94just the interests of the Bureau of Prisons.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs for good policy: The basic claim of Setser, the Government, and the dissent is that when it comes to sentencing, later is always better because the decisionmaker has more information. See, e.g., post, at 7 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] sentencing judge typically needs detailed information when constructing a multiple-count or multiple-conviction Guideline sentence\xe2\x80\x9d). That is undoubtedly true, but when that desideratum is applied to the statutory structure before us here it is overwhelmed by text, by our tradition of judicial sentencing,\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t and by the accompanying desideratum that sentencing not be left to employees of the same Department of Justice that conducts the prosecution.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Moreover, when the district court\xe2\x80\x99s failure to \xe2\x80\x9canticipat[e] developments that take place after the first sentencing,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 29, produces unfairness to the defendant, the Act provides a mechanism for relief. Section 3582(c)(1)(A) provides that a district court,\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cupon motion of the Director of the Bureau of Prisons, may reduce the term of imprisonment .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. after considering the factors set forth in section 3553(a) to the extent that they are applicable, if it finds that .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. extraordinary and compelling reasons warrant such a reduction [or that the defendant meets other criteria for relief].\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSetser argues that, even if the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s consecutive order was consistent with \xc2\xa73584(a), it made his sentence impossible to implement and therefore unreasonable under the Act, see United States v. Booker, \n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 220\n\xe2\x80\x93262 (2005),\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t7\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t in light of the State\xe2\x80\x99s decision to make his sentences concurrent. We think not. There is nothing unreasonable\xe2\x80\x94let alone inherently impossible\xe2\x80\x94about the sentence itself. Setser is ordered to serve a 151-month term in federal custody, and that sentence should run concurrently with one state sentence and consecutively with another.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe difficulty arises not from the sentence, but from the state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to make both state sentences concurrent. Which of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s dispositions should prevail: that his federal sentence run consecutively to the state sentence on the parole revocation charge, or that his federal sentence run concurrently with the state sentence on the new drug charge? If the federal sentence is added to the state sentence it will not be concurrent with the new drug charge, and if it is merged in the state sentence it will not be consecutive to the parole revocation charge. This is indeed a problem, but not, we think, one that shows the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s sentence to be unlawful. The reasonableness standard we apply in reviewing federal sentences asks whether the district court abused its discretion. See Gall v. United States, \n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 38, \n46 (2007)\n. Setser identifies no flaw in the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisionmaking process, nor anything available at the time of sentencing that the District Court failed to consider. That a sentence is thwarted does not mean that it was unreasonable. If a district court ordered, as a term of supervised release, that a defendant maintain a steady job, but a subsequent disability rendered gainful employment infeasible, we doubt that one would call the original sentence an abuse of discretion. There will often be late-onset facts that materially alter a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s position and that make it difficult, or even impossible, to implement his sentence.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis is where the Bureau of Prisons comes in\xe2\x80\x94which ultimately has to determine how long the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s sentence authorizes it to continue Setser\xe2\x80\x99s confinement. Setser is free to urge the Bureau to credit his time served in state court based on the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment that the federal sentence run concurrently with the state sentence for the new drug charges. If the Bureau initially declines to do so, he may raise his claim through the Bureau\xe2\x80\x99s Administrative Remedy Program. See 28 CFR \xc2\xa7542.10 et\xc2\xa0seq. (2011). And if that does not work, he may seek a writ of habeas corpus. See \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72241. We express no view on whether those proceedings would be successful.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Sentencing Reform Act of 1984 seeks to reform federal sentencing practices by creating a federal Sentencing Commission instructed to develop and to promulgate federal Sentencing Guidelines. The provision of the Act here at issue concerns \xe2\x80\x9cmultiple sentences.\xe2\x80\x9d See \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73584. It brings into focus a difficult Guidelines-related problem: How should a federal judge sentence an offender where the offender has been convicted of having violated several different statutes? The convictions may have taken place all at the same time. Or, some convictions might have taken place at an earlier time, the offender may already have been sentenced to prison, and indeed the offender may still be serving that sentence. The federal judge must decide the extent to which a sentence attached to one conviction should be served concurrently or consecutively with sentences attached to other convictions.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAn understanding of the nature of this general problem and the Sentencing Commission\xe2\x80\x99s statutorily foreseen solutions will help the reader understand why, in my view, the better legal answer to the question before us is that a federal sentencing judge does not have the power to order that a \xe2\x80\x9cfederal sentence be consecutive to an anticipated state sentence that has not yet been imposed.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 1.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Sentencing Reform Act (SRA) has two overall objectives. See Barber v. Thomas, 560 U.\xc2\xa0S. __, __ (2010) (slip op., at 7); see also United States Sentencing Commission, Guidelines Manual \xc2\xa71A3, p.\xc2\xa01.2 (Nov. 1987) (USSG) (addressing statutory objectives). First, it seeks greater honesty in sentencing. Instead of a parole commission and a judge trying to second-guess each other about the time an offender will actually serve in prison, the SRA tries to create a sentencing system that will require the offender actually to serve most of the sentence the judge imposes. See Mistretta v. United States, \n\n488 U.\xc2\xa0S. 361, \n367 (1989)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[The SRA] makes all sentences basically determinate\xe2\x80\x9d). Second, the Act seeks greater fairness in sentencing through the creation of Guidelines that will increase the likelihood that two offenders who engage in roughly similar criminal behavior will receive roughly similar sen-tences. See Barber, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 7) (noting that Congress sought to achieve, in part, \xe2\x80\x9cincreased sentencing uniformity\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo implement these reforms, the SRA instructs the Commission to write Guidelines that inevitably move in the direction of increased \xe2\x80\x9creal offense\xe2\x80\x9d sentencing. See USSG \xc2\xa71A2, p.\xc2\xa01.1. (describing how statute, e.g., by insisting upon categories of offense behavior and offender characteristics, leads to this result). In principle, real offense sentencing would impose the same sentence upon different offenders who engage in the same real conduct irrespective of the statutes under which they are charged. Real offense sentencing, for example, would mean that two individuals, both of whom rob a bank and injure a teller, would receive the same sentence even if the Government charges one of them under a bank robbery statute and the other under an assault statute. See, e.g., USSG App. A (listing federal statutory offenses, while keying them to specific individual Guidelines that determine sentence based upon likely actual behavior). In the event, the Guidelines move the sentencing system in this direction while simultaneously recognizing that other factors require considerable modification of the real offense principle. See USSG \xc2\xa71A4(a) (\xe2\x80\x9creal offense vs. charge offense sentencing\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNonetheless the \xe2\x80\x9creal offense\xe2\x80\x9d goal influenced the Act\xe2\x80\x99s, and the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s, objectives in respect to the sentencing of an offender with multiple convictions. Insofar as several convictions arise out of the same course of behavior, the sentencing judge should treat the crimes underlying the convictions as if they were all part of a single crime and sentence accordingly. But, insofar as the crimes underlying the convictions arise out of different courses of behavior, the sentencing judge should treat the crimes underlying the convictions as if they were not part of a single crime and should see that the ultimate sentence reflects that fact.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo achieve these objectives is easier said than done. For one thing, it requires a definition of what counts as the same course of behavior. The Guidelines set forth that definition in \xc2\xa71B1.3, p.\xc2\xa01.17 (\xe2\x80\x9cRelevant Conduct\xe2\x80\x9d). For another thing, statutes and Guidelines that set forth related instructions must take into account the fact that sentencing-related circumstances can prove highly complex. To take a fairly simple example, suppose that a defendant is convicted of both robbery and impersonating a federal official, that he has engaged in a single course of behavior, but that neither the robbery nor the impersonation Guidelines take account of the other. Instructions about concurrent/consecutive sentences must give the judge an idea about what to do in such a case. They must also take account of the fact that a maximum penalty contained in a statute will trump a greater penalty contained in a Guideline. And they must tell the judge (faced with multiple convictions) what to do where that is so.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tReflecting these, and other, complexities, the Guidelines contain complex instructions about how to sentence where the offender is convicted of \xe2\x80\x9cMultiple Counts,\xe2\x80\x9d see USSG \xc2\xa73D, or has previously been convicted of a crime for which he is \xe2\x80\x9csubject to an Un-discharged Term of Imprisonment,\xe2\x80\x9d see \xc2\xa75G1.3. The Guidelines also tell the sentencing judge how, through the use of partially concurrent and partially consecutive sentences, to build a total sentence that meets the Guidelines\xe2\x80\x99 requirements. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa75G1.2(d), 5G1.3.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWith this background it becomes easier to understand the statutory provisions before us. They reflect the fact that Congress expected sentencing judges, when faced with a defendant convicted of multiple crimes, to construct a sentence that would, at least to a degree, reflect the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s real underlying behavior. Where two convictions reflect in whole or in part the same behavior, the overall sentences should reflect that fact, say by running concurrently.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAccordingly, the statute says that \xe2\x80\x9c[m]ultiple terms of imprisonment imposed at the same time run concurrently unless the court orders or the statute mandates that the terms are to run consecutively.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73584(a). And that statement reflects the fact that often (but not always) multiple convictions after a single trial will reflect a sin-gle course of behavior (different aspects of which violate different criminal statutes). The statute also says that \xe2\x80\x9c[m]ultiple terms of imprisonment imposed at different times run consecutively unless the court orders that the terms are to run concurrently.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. This statement reflects the fact that several convictions imposed after different trials are more likely to reflect unrelated behaviors. In the first instance that the statute addresses, concurrent sentences are more likely to be appropriate; in the second, consecutive sentences are more likely to be appropriate. But that is not always so. Thus the statu- tory provisions assure sentencing judges that they retain the power to reach a different conclusion.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAt this point, I would ask the question that this case poses. Why does the statute say nothing about a sentencing judge imposing a sentence that might run consecu-tively with a sentence that a (typically different) judge has not yet imposed? The answer is this: Because the sentencing judge normally does not yet know enough about the behavior that underlies (or will underlie) a sentence that has not yet been imposed. Normally the sentencing judge does not know, for example, (a) what that sentence will be, (b) whether the behavior underlying that later sentence constitutes part of the same course of behavior that underlies the present sentence or, instead, is totally separate from the behavior underlying the present sentence, or (c) is partly the same and partly different. Even if the judge has an idea about what will happen, he does not know precisely what will happen; and precision in this matter is important.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn a word, the sentencing judge normally does not yet know enough about what will happen in the sentencing-proceeding-yet-to-come to be able to construct a sentence that meets the Guidelines\xe2\x80\x99 instructions and which, in doing so, helps to assure that different individuals who engage in the same criminal behavior will typically receive roughly comparable sentences.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOf course, the Court is correct when it says that eventually the sentences will run (either wholly in or in part) concurrently or consecutively. And someone must decide how they will run. Ante, at 2\xe2\x80\x933. But the Court is not correct when it says that this someone should be the first federal sentencing judge. Rather, the Executive and Judicial Branches have devised a system that can draw upon the intentions of that first federal judge, while applying them in light of actual knowledge about what later happened. The Bureau of Prisons (BOP) in effect makes the consecutive/concurrent decision after considering, among other things, \xe2\x80\x9cany statement by the court that imposed the sentence,\xe2\x80\x9d including statements \xe2\x80\x9cconcerning the purposes for which the sentence to imprisonment was determined to be warranted.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73621(b)(4)(A). And its program statement provides that it will review the \xe2\x80\x9cintent of the federal sentencing court\xe2\x80\x9d when deciding whether in effect to make an earlier federal, and later state, sentence concurrent or consecutive. Dept. of Justice, BOP, Program Statement 5160.05: Designation of State Institution for Service of Federal Sentence 4 (Jan. 16, 2003). The Bureau exercises this authority by designating (or refusing to designate) the state prison where an offender is or will be incarcerated pursuant to his state sentence as the place where he will serve his federal sentence. \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73621(b).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis exercise of authority by the Executive Branch is not constitutionally surprising. After all, \xe2\x80\x9cfederal sentencing\xe2\x80\x9d has \xe2\x80\x9cnever . . . been thought to be assigned by the Constitution to the exclusive jurisdiction of any one of the three Branches of Government.\xe2\x80\x9d Mistretta, 488 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 364. And, until fairly recently the federal BOP decided (via parole) the far more global question of just how long (within broad limits) each imprisoned offender would serve. See id., at 367. Thus, the present Bureau involvement represents a further practical accommodation to a fact about the world, namely that the initial sentencing judge typically lacks important sentencing-related information about a second sentence that has not yet been imposed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tGiven the purposes and the mechanics of the SRA\xe2\x80\x99s sentencing system, just described, the better reading of the \xe2\x80\x9cmultiple sentences\xe2\x80\x9d provision is a reading that denies a sentencing judge the authority to \xe2\x80\x9corder that the federal sentence be consecutive to an anticipated state sentence that has not yet been imposed.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 1. For one thing, nothing in the statute explicitly grants the judge that authority. The text refers to other circumstances, those that involve earlier or contemporaneous (multiple-count) convictions, while it does not refer to later imposed sentences.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor another, exercise of any such authority would more likely hinder than advance the basic objectives of the SRA. As I have explained, supra, at 2\xe2\x80\x935, a sentencing judge typically needs detailed information when constructing a multiple-count or multiple-conviction Guideline sentence. The fact that the future sentence has not yet been imposed means that information will often be lacking, and that in turn means that the exercise of such authority would risk confusion and error. A sentencing judge who believes, for example, that the future conviction will be based upon different relevant conduct (and consequently orders a consecutive sentence) could discover that the second conviction rests upon the same relevant conduct (warranting a concurrent sentence). Mistakes of this kind increase the risk of sentencing disparity and, insofar as the first judge guesses wrong, they can mean a less honest sentencing process as well.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFurther, I can find no significant tradition (pre-Guideline or post-Guideline) of federal judges imposing a sentence that runs consecutively with a sentence not yet imposed. The Court refers to four Courts of Appeals cases for the proposition that \xe2\x80\x9ctraditionally\xe2\x80\x9d a judge possessed this authority. Ante, at 4. The opinions in three of the cases are each about a page long and do not discuss the matter here at issue. (They assume, without significant discussion, the existence of the relevant sentencing authority.) See Anderson v. United States, 405 F.\xc2\xa02d 492, 493 (CA10 1969) (per\xc2\xa0curiam) (addressing the question whether a federal sentence runs from the date of its imposition or from the date of entry into federal custody); United States v. Kanton, 362 F.\xc2\xa02d 178, 179\xe2\x80\x93180 (CA7 1966) (per curiam) (same); United States ex\xc2\xa0rel. Lester v. Parker, 404 F.\xc2\xa02d 40, 41 (CA3 1968) (per\xc2\xa0curiam) (addressing the question whether a sentence was insufficiently certain for purposes of due process). The fourth case, Salley v. United States, 786 F.\xc2\xa02d 546, 548 (CA2 1986), discusses the issue directly and takes the Court\xe2\x80\x99s position. But, like the other three cases, it was decided before the Guidelines took effect (i.e., when the reasons for denying the authority were less strong). And, one judge on the panel disagreed in a separate opinion, and in my view has the better of the argument. See id., at 548\xe2\x80\x93550 (Newman, J., concurring in result); see also United States v. Eastman, 758 F.\xc2\xa02d 1315, 1317 (CA9 1985) (holding that a judge lacks the here-relevant sentencing power). In any event, these instances are too few to constitute a \xe2\x80\x9ctradition.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn fact the Senate Committee Report accompanying the SRA provides strong evidence that there was no such tradition. S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 98\xe2\x80\x93225 (1983). That Report thoroughly surveyed prior law. It says that the SRA is a \xe2\x80\x9ccomprehensive statement of the Federal law of sentencing,\xe2\x80\x9d that it \xe2\x80\x9cdescribes in detail the kinds of sentences that may be imposed,\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xc2\xa73584 \xe2\x80\x9cprovides the rules for determining the length of a term of imprisonment for a person convicted of more than one offense.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 50, 125\xe2\x80\x93126. It further states that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]xisting law permits the imposition of either concurrent or consecutive sentences,\xe2\x80\x9d which practice it then describes as limited to two scenarios: \xe2\x80\x9c[t]erms of imprisonment imposed at the same time,\xe2\x80\x9d and those \xe2\x80\x9cimposed on a person already serving a prison term.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 126. It says the same when describing how \xc2\xa73584 is supposed to work. In neither place does it refer to a practice of, or any authority for, imposing a prison term that runs consecutively with a future term not yet imposed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn addition, a grant of such authority risks at least occasional incoherence. For example, the statute, after setting forth the court\xe2\x80\x99s authority to impose a sentence of imprisonment that runs either concurrently or consecutively with other terms imposed in the same or in earlier proceedings, creates an exception that says: \xe2\x80\x9cexcept that the terms may not run consecutively for an attempt and for another offense that was the sole objective of the attempt.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73584(a). Now suppose the Court were right, and a sentencing judge had the authority to run a present term consecutively with a not-yet-imposed future term. Would it not be important to apply this same \xe2\x80\x9cattempt\xe2\x80\x9d exception in such instances as well? Indeed, the exception is phrased in categorical terms, and the legis-lative history in no way indicates that the exception ap-plies only occasionally. See S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 98\xe2\x80\x93225, at 126 (\xe2\x80\x9c[C]onsecutive terms of imprisonment may not, contrary to current law, be imposed for [attempt] and for an offense that was the sole objective of the attempt\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). Yet it is difficult, if not impossible, to read the statute\xe2\x80\x99s language as broadening the exception beyond the statutorily listed scenarios.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOr, consider, for example, an offender tried for arguably related crimes in two different federal courts at two different times. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s reading would not only allow the second judge to order concurrent service with the first sentence if warranted, as the statute explicitly permits, but it would also allow the first judge to make an analogous but anticipatory order based upon the sentence he expected the second judge would impose. But where complex forms of criminal behavior are at issue, these different judges may reach different conclusions. The result may well be conflict and confusion.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, as I said above, supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x936, a more practical solution to potential problems presented by a future sentencing proceeding lies closer at hand. The BOP has the statutory authority to effect concurrent service of federal and state sentences and is well situated to take into account both the intent of the first sentencing judge and the specific facts developed in the second sentencing. The relevant statute provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Bureau may designate any available penal or correctional facility .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0, whether maintained by the Federal Government or otherwise .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73621(b). And in reliance on this authority, the Bureau has concluded that it has the power to \xe2\x80\x9cdesignat[e] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a state institution for concurrent service of a federal sentence.\xe2\x80\x9d Program Statement 5160.05, at 1. The Program Statement further provides that exercise of this power will be guided by, in part, \xe2\x80\x9cthe intent of the federal sentencing court\xe2\x80\x9d in addition to \xe2\x80\x9cany other pertinent information regarding the inmate.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 4.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court\xe2\x80\x99s only criticism of this system is that it is less \xe2\x80\x9cnatural\xe2\x80\x9d to read the statute \xe2\x80\x9cas giving the Bureau of Prisons what amounts to sentencing authority.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8. But what is unnatural about giving the Bureau that authority? The sentencing process has long involved cooperation among the three branches of Government. Mistretta, 488 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 364. And until the Guidelines the BOP itself decided, within broad limits, precisely how much prison time every typical offender would serve. Even today, it still decides that question within certain limits. \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73624 (2006 ed. and Supp. IV) (delegating to the BOP authority to calculate \xe2\x80\x9cgood time credit,\xe2\x80\x9d which in effect reduces a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s term of incarceration); see also Barber, 560 U.\xc2\xa0S., at __ (slip op., at 1). Although Congress limited the Bureau\xe2\x80\x99s authority in this respect, there is nothing unnatural about leaving the Bureau with a small portion of that authority\xe2\x80\x94particularly where doing so helps significantly to alleviate a small, but important, technical problem in the application of the SRA\xe2\x80\x99s sentencing system.\n\t\t\t

Consequently, with respect, I dissent.\n\t\t\t
