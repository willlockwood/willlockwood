\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

CSX TRANSPORTATION, INC. v. GEORGIA STATE BOARD OF EQUALIZATION et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

No. 06\xe2\x80\x931287.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 5, 2007\xe2\x80\x94Decided December 4, 2007

\n

Under Georgia law, most commercial and industrial property is valued locally by county boards for tax purposes, but public utilities such as petitioner railroad (CSX) are initially valued by the State. In 2002, respondent Georgia state board used a different combination of methodologies than it had in 2001 to determine that the market value of CSX\xe2\x80\x99s in-state real property had increased 47 percent, resulting in a significantly higher ad\xc2\xa0valorem tax levy. CSX filed suit under the Railroad Revitalization and Regulatory Reform Act of 1976 (4\xe2\x80\x93R Act or Act), which bars States from, inter alia, \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ssess[ing] rail transportation property at a value that has a higher ratio to the [property\xe2\x80\x99s] true market value \xe2\x80\xa6 than the ratio\xe2\x80\x9d between the assessed and true market values of other commercial and industrial property in the same taxing jurisdiction, 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa711501(b)(1), and authorizes the federal district court to enjoin the tax if the railroad ratio exceeds the ratio for other property by at least five percent, \xc2\xa711501(c). CSX alleged that Georgia had grossly overestimated the market value of its in-state rail property while accurately valuing other commercial and industrial property in the State, so that CSX\xe2\x80\x99s property was taxed at a ratio of assessed-to-market value considerably more than 5 percent greater than the same ratio for the other in-state property. Ruling that Georgia had not discriminated against CSX in violation of the 4\xe2\x80\x93R Act because the State had used widely accepted valuation methods to arrive at its 2002 estimate of true market value, the District Court declared that the Act does not allow a railroad to challenge a State\xe2\x80\x99s chosen methodology if it is rational and not motivated by discriminatory intent. The Eleventh Circuit panel affirmed, reasoning that the Act does not clearly state that railroads may challenge valuation methodologies, and that such a clear statement was required in light of the intrusion on state taxing prerogatives.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The 4\xe2\x80\x93R Act allows a railroad to attempt to show that state methods for determining the value of railroad property result in a discriminatory determination of true market value. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The Act\xe2\x80\x99s language is clear. States may not tax railroad property at a ratio of assessed-to-true-market value higher than the ratio for other commercial and industrial property in the same jurisdiction. To apply the Act, district courts must calculate the true market value of in-state railroad property. A court cannot undertake the comparison of ratios the statute requires without that figure at hand, see Burlington Northern R. Co. v. Oklahoma Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 481 U. S. 454, 461, and the determination of true market value may be affected by the State\xe2\x80\x99s choice of valuation methods. Georgia\xe2\x80\x99s argument that valuation methodologies must be distinguished from their application, and that the Act allows courts to question only the latter, is rejected. There is no distinction between method and application in the Act\xe2\x80\x99s language and no passage limiting district court factfinding as the State proposes. Georgia\xe2\x80\x99s position is untenable given the way market value is calculated. Valuation is not a matter of mathematics, but an applied science, even a craft. Most appraisers estimate market value by employing not one methodology but a combination because no one approach is entirely accurate, at least in the absence of an established market for the type of property at issue. The individual methods yield sometimes more, sometimes less reliable results depending on the peculiar features of the property evaluated. Given the extent to which the chosen methods can affect the determination of value, preventing courts from scrutinizing state valuation methodologies would render \xc2\xa711501 a largely empty command, forcing district courts to accept as \xe2\x80\x9ctrue\xe2\x80\x9d the market value estimate of the State, one of the parties to the litigation. States, in turn, would be free to employ appraisal techniques that routinely overestimate the market worth of railroad assets. By then levying taxes based on those overestimates, States could implement the very discriminatory taxation Congress sought to eradicate. Courts would be powerless to stop them, and the Act would ultimately guarantee railroads nothing more than mathematically accurate discriminatory taxation.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The State\xe2\x80\x99s warning that allowing railroads to introduce their own valuation estimates based on different methodologies will inevitably lead to a futile clash of experts, which courts will have no reasonable way to settle, is not compelling, given that Congress was not similarly troubled. Rather, Congress directed courts to find true market value, however elusive, making that value the objective benchmark for courts\xe2\x80\x99 evaluation. Property valuation, though admittedly complex, is at bottom just \xe2\x80\x9can issue of fact about possible market prices,\xe2\x80\x9d Suitum v. Tahoe Regional Planning Agency, 520 U. S. 725, 741, an issue district courts are used to addressing. In light of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s directive making true market value a factual question to be determined by the district court, what Georgia really seeks is to limit the types of evidence courts may consider as part of their factual inquiry. Had Congress intended to impose such a limit, it could easily have included language insulating the State\xe2\x80\x99s chosen methodologies from judicial scrutiny. It did not. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The State argues that any interpretation of the Act allowing courts to question state valuation methods ignores the background principles of federalism against which the statute was enacted. Even if important state policy questions are intertwined with the selection of a valuation methodology, however, Congress clearly permitted courts to question such methodologies when it banned discriminatory assessment ratios and made true market value a question to be litigated in federal court. Department of Revenue of Ore. v. ACF Industries, Inc., 510 U. S. 332, 343\xe2\x80\x93344, distinguished. The Court also disagrees with Georgia\xe2\x80\x99s claim that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation will destroy the States\xe2\x80\x99 discretion to choose their own valuation methodologies. A State may use whatever method it likes, so long as the result is not discriminatory in violation of the Act. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

472 F.\xc2\xa03d 1281, reversed.

\nOPINION OF THE COURT
\nCSX TRANSP., INC. V. GEORGIA STATE BD. OFEQUALIZATION
\n552 U. S. ____ (2007)
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 06-1287
\n
\n

CSX TRANSPORTATION, INC., PETITIONER v.\nGEORGIA STATE BOARD OF\nEQUALIZATION et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

[December 4, 2007]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Chief Justice Roberts delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Railroad Revitalization and Regulatory Reform Act prohibits States from discriminating against railroads by taxing railroad property more heavily than other commercial property in the State. Two decades ago, we held that this statute permits an aggrieved railroad to challenge a State\xe2\x80\x99s valuation of its property for tax purposes. Burlington Northern R.\xc2\xa0Co. v. Oklahoma Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 481 U. S. 454, 462 (1987). Because the railroad in that case challenged only the State\xe2\x80\x99s application of its valuation methods, we expressly reserved the question whether a railroad may challenge the State\xe2\x80\x99s methods themselves. We answer that question today, and hold that railroads may challenge state methods for determining the value of railroad property, as well as how those methods are applied. The statute provides for nothing less.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress enacted the Railroad Revitalization and Regulatory Reform Act in 1976. 90 Stat. 31.[Footnote 1] Called the \xe2\x80\x9c4\xe2\x80\x93R Act\xe2\x80\x9d for brevity, the law aimed to halt the economic decline of the rail industry by, among other means, barring \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminatory state taxation of railroad property.\xe2\x80\x9d Burlington Northern, supra, at 457; see also Department of Revenue of Ore. v. ACF Industries, Inc., 510 U. S. 332, 336 (1994). The 4\xe2\x80\x93R Act prohibits four separate forms of discriminatory state taxation of railroads.[Footnote 2] Only the first is at issue here: States, the Act provides, may not \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ssess rail transportation property at a value that has a higher ratio to the [property\xe2\x80\x99s] true market value . . . than the ratio\xe2\x80\x9d between the assessed and true market values of other commercial and industrial property in the same taxing jurisdiction. 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa711501(b)(1). If the railroad ratio exceeds the ratio for other property by at least five percent, the district court may enjoin the tax. \xc2\xa711501(c).[Footnote 3]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner CSX Transportation, Inc., is a freight rail carrier with multiple routes across the State of Georgia. As a consequence, it is subject to Georgia\xe2\x80\x99s ad valorem tax on real property. Under Georgia law, most commercial and industrial property is valued locally by county boards. Public utilities such as railroads, however, are initially valued by the State, which then certifies the proposed valuations to the county boards for adoption or alteration. In 2001, Georgia\xe2\x80\x99s State Board of Equalization, a respondent here, put CSX\xe2\x80\x99s ad valorem tax liability at $4.6 million. A year later, the State\xe2\x80\x99s appraiser used a different combination of methodologies to determine the market value of CSX\xe2\x80\x99s in-state property.[Footnote 4] The result was a significantly higher tax levy. The State estimated the railroad\xe2\x80\x99s 2002 market value at approximately $7.8 billion, 472 F.\xc2\xa03d 1281, 1285 (CA11 2006), a 47 percent increase over the previous year. That brought the assessed value of CSX\xe2\x80\x99s Georgia property to $514.9 million, for a final property tax bill of $6.5 million. Brief for Petitioner 15.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0CSX filed suit in the United States District Court for the Northern District of Georgia, contending that the State\xe2\x80\x99s 2002 tax assessment violated the 4\xe2\x80\x93R Act. The railroad alleged that Georgia had grossly overestimated the market value of its in-state property while accurately valuing other commercial and industrial property in the State. The result, according to CSX, was that its rail property was taxed at a ratio of assessed-to-market value considerably more than 5 percent greater than the same ratio for the other property in the State.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To make its case, CSX submitted the testimony of its own expert appraiser, who relied on a combination of valuation methods different from those used by the appraiser for Georgia. The CSX appraiser calculated the 2002 market value of the railroad\xe2\x80\x99s property to be $6 billion, not the $7.8 billion figure used by the State. 472 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1285\xe2\x80\x931286. CSX maintained that the state appraiser\xe2\x80\x99s valuation methodologies were flawed, and urged the District Court to accept the market value estimated by its expert as more accurate.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court refused to do so. Following a bench trial, the court ruled Georgia had not discriminated against CSX in violation of the 4\xe2\x80\x93R Act because the State had used widely accepted valuation methods to arrive at its estimate of true market value. 448 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1330, 1341 (ND Ga. 2005). In the judgment of the District Court, the Act \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not generally allow a railroad to challenge the state\xe2\x80\x99s chosen methodology,\xe2\x80\x9d as long as the State\xe2\x80\x99s methods are rational and not motivated by discriminatory intent. Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A divided panel of the Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit affirmed. 472 F.\xc2\xa03d 1281 (2006). The majority reasoned that the \xe2\x80\x9ctext of the Act does not clearly state that railroads may challenge valuation methodologies,\xe2\x80\x9d and that such a clear statement was required in light of the intrusion on state taxing prerogatives. Id., at 1289. Judge Fay dissented. Id., at 1292. Recognizing the division on this question among the Circuits, compare Consolidated Rail Corp. v. Hyde Park, 47 F. 3d 473, 481\xe2\x80\x93482 (CA2 1995) (a railroad may challenge a State\xe2\x80\x99s valuation methodology), and Burlington Northern R.\xc2\xa0Co. v. Department of Revenue of Wash., 23 F. 3d 239, 240\xe2\x80\x93241 (CA9 1994) (same), with Chesapeake Western Ry. v. Forst, 938 F. 2d 528, 531 (CA4 1991) (a railroad may not challenge a State\xe2\x80\x99s valuation methodology), and 472 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1289 (decision below), we granted certiorari, 550 U. S. ___ (2007), and now reverse.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he language of \xc2\xa71150[1] plainly declares the congressional purpose.\xe2\x80\x9d Burlington Northern, 481 U. S., at 461. States may not tax railroad property at a ratio of assessed-to-true-market value higher than the ratio for other commercial and industrial property in the same jurisdiction. In order to apply the Act, district courts must calculate the true market value of in-state railroad property. A court cannot undertake the comparison of ratios the statute requires without that figure at hand. We said as much in Burlington Northern: \xe2\x80\x9cIt is clear from [the Act\xe2\x80\x99s] language that in order to compare the actual assessment ratios, it is necessary to determine what the \xe2\x80\x98true market values\xe2\x80\x99 are.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We do not see how a court can go about determining true market value if it may not look behind the State\xe2\x80\x99s choice of valuation methods. Georgia insists there is a clear and important distinction between valuation methodologies and their application. As the State would have it, the statute allows courts to question only the latter. We find no distinction between method and application in the language of the Act, and see no passage limiting district court factfinding in the manner the State proposes. The total lack of textual support for Georgia\xe2\x80\x99s position is not surprising. The dichotomy the State presses would eviscerate the statute by forcing courts to defer to the valuation estimate of the State, when discriminatory taxation by States was the very evil the Act aimed to ban.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Georgia\xe2\x80\x99s position is untenable given the way market value is calculated. Valuation is not a matter of mathematics, as if the district court could prevent discriminatory taxation simply by doublechecking the State\xe2\x80\x99s assessment equations. Rather, the calculation of true market value is an applied science, even a craft. Most appraisers estimate market value by employing not one methodology but a combination. These various methods generate a range of possible market values which the appraiser uses to derive what he considers to be an accurate estimate of market value, based on careful scrutiny of all the data available. Appraisal Institute, The Appraisal of Real Estate 49 (12th ed. 2001).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Georgia\xe2\x80\x99s appraiser in the instant case, for example, used three different valuation techniques\xe2\x80\x94the discounted cashflow approach, a market multiple approach, and a stock and debt approach. He derived five values from these three methods, ranging from $8.126 billion to $12.346 billion. After selecting a number at the low end of the range and then subtracting another $400 million to account for intangible property not subject to ad valorem taxation, he settled on $7.8 billion as his final estimate of the true market value. 472 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1284\xe2\x80\x931285.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Appraisers typically employ a combination of methods because no one approach is entirely accurate, at least in the absence of an established market for the type of property at issue. The individual methods yield sometimes more, sometimes less reliable results depending on the peculiar features of the property evaluated. As the variation in the state appraiser\xe2\x80\x99s market-value range reveals, different methods can produce substantially different estimates. W. Kinnard, Income Property Valuation: Principles and Techniques of Appraising Income-Producing Real Estate 52 (1971).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Given the extent to which the chosen methods can affect the determination of value, preventing courts from scrutinizing state valuation methodologies would render \xc2\xa711501 a largely empty command. It would force district courts to accept as \xe2\x80\x9ctrue\xe2\x80\x9d the market value estimated by the State, one of the parties to the litigation. States, in turn, would be free to employ appraisal techniques that routinely overestimate the market worth of railroad assets. By then levying taxes based on those overestimates, States could implement the very discriminatory taxation Congress sought to eradicate. On Georgia\xe2\x80\x99s reading of the statute, courts would be powerless to stop them, and the Act would ultimately guarantee railroads nothing more than mathematically accurate discriminatory taxation. We do not find this interpretation compelling. Instead, we agree with Judge Fay in dissent below: \xe2\x80\x9cSince the objective of any methodology is a determination of true market value, a railroad should be allowed to challenge the method[s] used [by the State] in an attempt to prove that the result \xe2\x80\xa6 was not the true market value of its property.\xe2\x80\x9d 472 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1294.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The State agrees that it may not be possible to fix true market value with any precision. But it draws a different conclusion from this premise. Because any number of estimates are plausible, Georgia argues, the court is as likely to get an accurate result by verifying the application of the State\xe2\x80\x99s methods\xe2\x80\x94so long as they are broadly reasonable\xe2\x80\x94as it is by employing another method altogether. The State warns that allowing railroads to introduce their own valuation estimates based on different methodologies will inevitably lead to a futile clash of experts, which courts will have no reasonable way to settle. At least one of the Courts of Appeals shares this concern. See Chesapeake Western, 938 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 532 (\xe2\x80\x9cThere is no absolute way to test the assertions of competing valuations . . .\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks and brackets omitted)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress was not similarly troubled. It directed courts to find true market value, however elusive. It made that value the objective benchmark for courts\xe2\x80\x99 evaluation of state taxes on railroad property. True market value may well not be a single, precise number, but Congress obviously believed it was susceptible to judicial inquiry and that some approximations were better than others.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Georgia\xe2\x80\x99s grim prophecies notwithstanding, the inquiry the statute mandates is not unfamiliar to courts. Valuation of property, though admittedly complex, is at bottom just \xe2\x80\x9can issue of fact about possible market prices,\xe2\x80\x9d Suitum v. Tahoe Regional Planning Agency, 520 U. S. 725, 741 (1997), an issue district courts are used to addressing. Railroad property is not frequently sold, but \xe2\x80\x9cdeterminations of market value are routinely made in judicial proceedings without the benefit of a market transaction.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 742. The District Court in this case made clear that it knew how to find true market value: \xe2\x80\x9cIn a more typical case, the court would look at both [the railroad expert\xe2\x80\x99s] appraisal and [the State\xe2\x80\x99s] appraisal to determine the true market value of [the railroad].\xe2\x80\x9d 448 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1338, n.\xc2\xa08. It refused to do so not because true market value is inherently elusive, but because it believed the Act did not allow it to question the State\xe2\x80\x99s methods.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In light of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s directive making true market value a factual question to be determined by the district court, what Georgia is really asking for is a limitation on the types of evidence courts may consider as part of their factual inquiry. If Congress had wanted to impose such a limit by reserving to States the prerogative of selecting which valuation methods may be used, it surely could have done so. Out of deference to the States, for example, \xc2\xa711501(c) provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he burden of proof in determining . . . true market value [shall be] governed by State law.\xe2\x80\x9d Congress could easily have included similar language insulating the State\xe2\x80\x99s chosen methodologies from judicial scrutiny. It did not. Like Oklahoma\xe2\x80\x99s argument in Burlington Northern, Georgia\xe2\x80\x99s position in this case ultimately \xe2\x80\x9cdepends upon the addition of words to a statutory provision which is complete as it stands.\xe2\x80\x9d 481 U. S.em>., at 463. We decline to find distinctions in the statute where they do not exist, especially where, as here, those distinctions would thwart the law\xe2\x80\x99s operation.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Considering the clarity of the statute, we are tempted to leave the discussion at that. \xe2\x80\x9cWhen we find the terms of a statue unambiguous, judicial inquiry is complete . . . .\xe2\x80\x9d Rubin v. United States, 449 U. S. 424, 430 (1981). Georgia, however, lodges two objections to our interpretation, each of which merits a reply. First, the State argues that any interpretation of the Act allowing courts to question state valuation methods ignores the background principles of federalism against which the statute was enacted. The majority below expressed a similar concern. \xe2\x80\x9cThe selection of a valuation methodology,\xe2\x80\x9d it ruled, \xe2\x80\x9cis part of th[e] fundamental power of a state [to tax],\xe2\x80\x9d 472 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1288, and should not be limited absent a clear statement from Congress. We have long held that the means States adopt to collect their taxes \xe2\x80\x9cshould be interfered with as little as possible.\xe2\x80\x9d Dows v. Chicago, 11 Wall. 108, 110 (1871). But we are persuaded that allowing railroads to challenge a State\xe2\x80\x99s valuation methodologies has been clearly authorized by the terms of the 4\xe2\x80\x93R Act.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As an initial matter, we question Georgia\xe2\x80\x99s contention that its selection of valuation methodologies is an important state policy choice intimately connected to its tax power. Georgia does not prescribe any particular methodology as a matter of state law. Its appraisers use different methodologies in different combinations, as they see fit. See 472 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1284\xe2\x80\x931285 (explaining that the state appraiser employed multiple methods and selected a value according to his best judgment). This suit, in fact, is the result of an individual appraiser\xe2\x80\x99s decision to employ a different combination of assessment techniques than that used by his immediate predecessors. The methods he selected were his choice, not the dictate of any state statute or regulation. Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But even if important questions of state policy are, as the Eleventh Circuit believed, \xe2\x80\x9cintertwined with the selection of a valuation methodology,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 1288, judicial scrutiny of those methodologies is authorized by the 4\xe2\x80\x93R Act\xe2\x80\x99s clear command to find true market value. As we explained above, the power to calculate true market value necessarily includes the power to look behind a State\xe2\x80\x99s valuation methods. That the statute should vest this authority in the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s courts is hardly surprising, given Congress\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that the States were assessing railroad property unfairly.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our decision in Department of Revenue of Ore. v. ACF Industries, Inc., 510 U. S. 332 (1994), is not to the contrary. That case concerned a different provision of the 4\xe2\x80\x93R Act\xe2\x80\x94namely, the command in \xc2\xa711501(b)(4) preventing a State from \xe2\x80\x9c[i]mpos[ing] another tax that discriminates against a rail carrier providing transportation\xe2\x80\x9d in the taxing jurisdiction. This bar on facially discriminatory taxes, we held, did not prevent a State from exempting certain nonrailroad property from otherwise generally applicable ad valorem taxes. ACF Industries, 510 U. S., at 343. At the time the 4\xe2\x80\x93R Act was adopted, a majority of States exempted one or more classes of business property from ad valorem taxation, \xe2\x80\x9cincluding business inventories, raw materials used in textile manufacturing, . . . and mechanics tools,\xe2\x80\x9d to name just a few. Id., at 344. The States had provided such property tax exemptions for years. In the face of this widespread and historical practice, we declined to read the 4\xe2\x80\x93R Act to prohibit a type of tax exemption the text did not expressly mention. Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0By contrast, we pointedly noted that the Act \xe2\x80\x9cprohibit[s] discriminatory tax rates and assessment ratios in no uncertain terms . . . and set[s] forth precise standards for judicial scrutiny of challenged rate and assessment practices.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 343. Georgia\xe2\x80\x99s claim that court review of state valuation methodologies is not authorized by a clear statement in the Act ignores the statute\xe2\x80\x99s explicit prohibition of discriminatory assessment ratios. A district court cannot accurately calculate or compare those ratios without determining true market value. Congress clearly permitted courts to question state valuation methodologies when it banned discriminatory assessment ratios and made true market value a question to be litigated in federal court.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Georgia also protests that our interpretation will destroy the States\xe2\x80\x99 discretion to choose their own valuation methodologies. We disagree. A State may use whatever method or methods it likes, so long as the result is not discriminatory. The Act does not prohibit the use of any valuation methodology. It prohibits discrimination. Far from requiring States to follow a particular method, we hold only that nothing in the statute prevents a railroad from attempting to show that the methods chosen by the State result in a discriminatory determination of true market value.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit is reversed.

\n

It is so ordered.
