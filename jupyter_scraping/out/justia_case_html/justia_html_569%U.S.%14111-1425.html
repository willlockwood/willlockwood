\n\t\t\t\tMISSOURI v. McNEELY\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the supreme court of missouri\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 11\xe2\x80\x931425.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 9, 2013\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 17, 2013\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tRespondent McNeely was stopped by a Missouri police officer for speeding and crossing the centerline. After declining to take a breath test to measure his blood alcohol concentration (BAC), he was arrested and taken to a nearby hospital for blood testing. The officer never attempted to secure a search warrant. McNeely refused to consent to the blood test, but the officer directed a lab technician to take a sample. McNeely\xe2\x80\x99s BAC tested well above the legal limit, and he was charged with driving while intoxicated (DWI). He moved to suppress the blood test result, arguing that taking his blood without a warrant violated his \n \nFourth Amendment rights. The trial court agreed, concluding that the exigency exception to the warrant requirement did not apply because, apart from the fact that McNeely\xe2\x80\x99s blood alcohol was dissipating, no circumstances suggested that the officer faced an emergency. The State Supreme Court affirmed, relying on Schmerber v. California, \n\n384 U.\xc2\xa0S. 757\n, in which this Court upheld a DWI suspect\xe2\x80\x99s warrantless blood test where the officer \xe2\x80\x9cmight reasonably have believed that he was confronted with an emergency, in which the delay necessary to obtain a warrant, under the circumstances, threatened \xe2\x80\x98the destruction of evidence,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 770. This case, the state court found, involved a routine DWI investigation where no factors other than the natural dissipation of blood alcohol suggested that there was an emergency, and, thus, the nonconsensual warrantless test violated McNeely\xe2\x80\x99s right to be free from unreasonable searches of his person.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0The judgment is affirmed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t358 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d 65, affirmed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Sotomayor delivered the opinion of the Court with respect to Parts I, II\xe2\x80\x93A, II\xe2\x80\x93B, and IV, concluding that in drunk-driving investigations, the natural dissipation of alcohol in the bloodstream does not constitute an exigency in every case sufficient to justify conducting a blood test without a warrant. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9313, 20\xe2\x80\x9323.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0The principle that a warrantless search of the person is reasonable only if it falls within a recognized exception, see, e.g., United States v. Robinson, \n\n414 U.\xc2\xa0S. 218\n, applies here, where the search involved a compelled physical intrusion beneath McNeely\xe2\x80\x99s skin and into his veins to obtain a blood sample to use as evidence in a criminal investigation. One recognized exception \xe2\x80\x9capplies when \xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cthe exigencies of the situation\xe2\x80\x9d make the needs of law enforcement so compelling that [a] warrantless search is objectively reasonable.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Kentucky v. King, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. This Court looks to the totality of circumstances in determining whether an exigency exits. See Brigham City v. Stuart, \n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 398\n. Applying this approach in Schmerber, the Court found a warrantless blood test reasonable after considering all of the facts and circumstances of that case and carefully basing its holding on those specific facts, including that alcohol levels decline after drinking stops and that testing was delayed while officers transported the injured suspect to the hospital and investigated the accident scene. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x938.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0The State nonetheless seeks a per\xc2\xa0se rule, contending that exigent circumstances necessarily exist when an officer has probable cause to believe a person has been driving under the influence of alcohol because BAC evidence is inherently evanescent. Though a person\xe2\x80\x99s blood alcohol level declines until the alcohol is eliminated, it does not follow that the Court should depart from careful case-by-case assessment of exigency. When officers in drunk-driving investigations can reasonably obtain a warrant before having a blood sample drawn without significantly undermining the efficacy of the search, the \n \nFourth Amendment mandates that they do so. See McDonald v. United States, \n\n335 U.\xc2\xa0S. 451\n. Circumstances may make obtaining a warrant impractical such that the alcohol\xe2\x80\x99s dissipation will support an exigency, but that is a reason to decide each case on its facts, as in Schmerber, not to accept the \xe2\x80\x9cconsiderable overgeneralization\xe2\x80\x9d that a per\xc2\xa0se rule would reflect, Richards v. Wisconsin, \n\n520 U.\xc2\xa0S. 385\n. Blood testing is different in critical respects from other destruction-of-evidence cases. Unlike a situation where, e.g., a suspect has control over easily disposable evidence, see Cupp v. Murphy, \n\n412 U.\xc2\xa0S. 291\n, BAC evidence naturally dissipates in a gradual and relatively predictable manner. Moreover, because an officer must typically take a DWI suspect to a medical facility and obtain a trained medical professional\xe2\x80\x99s assistance before having a blood test conducted, some delay between the time of the arrest or accident and time of the test is inevitable regardless of whether a warrant is obtained. The State\xe2\x80\x99s rule also fails to account for advances in the 47 years since Schmerber was decided that allow for the more expeditious processing of warrant applications, particularly in contexts like drunk-driving investigations where the evidence supporting probable cause is simple. The natural dissipation of alcohol in the blood may support an exigency finding in a specific case, as it did in Schmerber, but it does not do so categorically. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9313.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0Because the State sought a per se rule here, it did not argue that there were exigent circumstances in this particular case. The arguments and the record thus do not provide the Court with an adequate framework for a detailed discussion of all the relevant factors that can be taken into account in determining the reasonableness of acting without a warrant. It suffices to say that the metabolization of alcohol in the bloodstream and the ensuing loss of evidence are among the factors that must be considered in deciding whether a warrant is required. Pp.\xc2\xa020\xe2\x80\x9323.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Sotomayor, joined by Justice Scalia, Justice Ginsburg, and Justice Kagan, concluded in Part III that other arguments advanced by the State and amici in support of a per\xc2\xa0se rule are unpersuasive. Their concern that a case-by-case approach to exigency will not provide adequate guidance to law enforcement officers may make the desire for a bright-line rule understandable, but the \n \nFourth Amendment will not tolerate adoption of an overly broad categorical approach in this context. A fact-intensive, totality of the circumstances, approach is hardly unique within this Court\xe2\x80\x99s \n \nFourth Amendment jurisprudence. See, e.g., Illinois v. Wardlow, \n\n528 U.\xc2\xa0S. 119\n\xe2\x80\x93125. They also contend that the privacy interest implicated here is minimal. But motorists\xe2\x80\x99 diminished expectation of privacy does not diminish their privacy interest in preventing a government agent from piercing their skin. And though a blood test conducted in a medical setting by trained personnel is less intrusive than other bodily invasions, this Court has never retreated from its recognition that any compelled intrusion into the human body implicates significant, constitutionally protected privacy interests. Finally, the government\xe2\x80\x99s general interest in combating drunk driving does not justify departing from the warrant requirement without showing exigent circumstances that make securing a warrant impractical in a particular case. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9320.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Sotomayor announced the judgment of the Court and delivered the opinion of the Court with respect to Parts I, II\xe2\x80\x93A, II\xe2\x80\x93B, and IV, and an opinion with respect to Parts II\xe2\x80\x93C and III, in which Justice Scalia, Justice Ginsburg, and Justice Kagan join.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn Schmerber v. California, \n\n384 U.\xc2\xa0S. 757 (1966)\n, this Court upheld a warrantless blood test of an individual arrested for driving under the influence of alcohol because the officer \xe2\x80\x9cmight reasonably have believed that he was confronted with an emergency, in which the delay necessary to obtain a warrant, under the circumstances, threatened the destruction of evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 770 (internal quotation marks omitted). The question presented here is whether the natural metabolization of alcohol in the bloodstream presents a per\xc2\xa0se exigency that justifies an exception to the \n \nFourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s warrant requirement for nonconsensual blood testing in all drunk-driving cases. We conclude that it does not, and we hold, consistent with general \n \nFourth Amendment principles, that exigency in this context must be determined case by case based on the totality of the circumstances.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhile on highway patrol at approximately 2:08 a.m., a Missouri police officer stopped Tyler McNeely\xe2\x80\x99s truck after observing it exceed the posted speed limit and repeatedly cross the centerline. The officer noticed several signs that McNeely was intoxicated, including McNeely\xe2\x80\x99s bloodshot eyes, his slurred speech, and the smell of alcohol on his breath. McNeely acknowledged to the officer that he had consumed \xe2\x80\x9ca couple of beers\xe2\x80\x9d at a bar, App. 20, and he appeared unsteady on his feet when he exited the truck. After McNeely performed poorly on a battery of field-sobriety tests and declined to use a portable breath-test device to measure his blood alcohol concentration (BAC), the officer placed him under arrest.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe officer began to transport McNeely to the station house. But when McNeely indicated that he would again refuse to provide a breath sample, the officer changed course and took McNeely to a nearby hospital for blood testing. The officer did not attempt to secure a warrant. Upon arrival at the hospital, the officer asked McNeely whether he would consent to a blood test. Reading from a standard implied consent form, the officer explained to McNeely that under state law refusal to submit voluntar- ily to the test would lead to the immediate revocation of his driver\xe2\x80\x99s license for one year and could be used against him in a future prosecution. See Mo. Ann. Stat. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7577.020.1, 577.041 (West 2011). McNeely nonetheless refused. The officer then directed a hospital lab technician to take a blood sample, and the sample was secured at approximately 2:35 a.m. Subsequent laboratory testing measured McNeely\xe2\x80\x99s BAC at 0.154 percent, which was well above the legal limit of 0.08 percent. See \xc2\xa7577.012.1.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMcNeely was charged with driving while intoxicated (DWI), in violation of \xc2\xa7577.010.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t He moved to suppress the results of the blood test, arguing in relevant part that, under the circumstances, taking his blood for chemi- cal testing without first obtaining a search warrant vio- lated his rights under the \n \nFourth Amendment. The trial court agreed. It concluded that the exigency exception to the warrant requirement did not apply because, apart from the fact that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]s in all cases involving intoxication, [McNeely\xe2\x80\x99s] blood alcohol was being metabolized by his liver,\xe2\x80\x9d there were no circumstances suggesting the officer faced an emergency in which he could not practicably obtain a warrant. No. 10CG\xe2\x80\x93CR01849\xe2\x80\x9301 (Cir.\xc2\xa0Ct. Cape Giradeau Cty., Mo., Div. II, Mar. 3, 2011), App. to Pet. for Cert. 43a. On appeal, the Missouri Court of Appeals stated an intention to reverse but transferred the case directly to the Missouri Supreme Court. No. ED 96402 (June 21, 2011), id., at 24a.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Missouri Supreme Court affirmed. 358 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d 65 (2012) (per curiam). Recognizing that this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Schmerber v. California, \n\n384 U.\xc2\xa0S. 757\n, \xe2\x80\x9cprovide[d] the backdrop\xe2\x80\x9d to its analysis, the Missouri Supreme Court held that \xe2\x80\x9cSchmerber directs lower courts to engage in a totality of the circumstances analysis when determin- ing whether exigency permits a nonconsensual, warrantless blood draw.\xe2\x80\x9d 358 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d, at 69, 74. The court further concluded that Schmerber \xe2\x80\x9crequires more than the mere dissipation of blood-alcohol evidence to support a warrantless blood draw in an alcohol-related case.\xe2\x80\x9d 358 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d, at 70. According to the court, exigency depends heavily on the existence of additional \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98special facts,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d such as whether an officer was delayed by the need to investigate an ac- cident and transport an injured suspect to the hospital, as had been the case in Schmerber. 358 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d, at 70, 74. Finding that this was \xe2\x80\x9cunquestionably a routine DWI case\xe2\x80\x9d in which no factors other than the natural dissi- pation of blood-alcohol suggested that there was an emergency, the court held that the nonconsensual warrantless blood draw violated McNeely\xe2\x80\x99s \n \nFourth Amendment right to be free from unreasonable searches of his person. Id., at 74\xe2\x80\x9375.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe granted certiorari to resolve a split of authority on the question whether the natural dissipation of alcohol in the bloodstream establishes a per\xc2\xa0se exigency that suffices on its own to justify an exception to the warrant requirement for nonconsensual blood testing in drunk-driving investigations.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t See 567 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012). We now affirm.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe \n \nFourth Amendment provides in relevant part that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he right of the people to be secure in their persons, houses, papers, and effects, against unreasonable searches and seizures, shall not be violated, and no Warrants shall issue, but upon probable cause.\xe2\x80\x9d Our cases have held that a warrantless search of the person is reasonable only if it falls within a recognized exception. See, e.g., United States v. Robinson, \n\n414 U.\xc2\xa0S. 218, \n224 (1973)\n. That principle applies to the type of search at issue in this case, which involved a compelled physical intrusion beneath McNeely\xe2\x80\x99s skin and into his veins to obtain a sample of his blood for use as evidence in a criminal investigation. Such an invasion of bodily integrity implicates an individual\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cmost personal and deep-rooted expectations of privacy.\xe2\x80\x9d Winston v. Lee, \n\n470 U.\xc2\xa0S. 753, \n760 (1985)\n; see also Skinner v. Railway Labor Executives\xe2\x80\x99 Assn., \n\n489 U.\xc2\xa0S. 602, \n616 (1989)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe first considered the \n \nFourth Amendment restrictions on such searches in Schmerber, where, as in this case, a blood sample was drawn from a defendant suspected of driving while under the influence of alcohol. 384 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 758. Noting that \xe2\x80\x9c[s]earch warrants are ordinarily required for searches of dwellings,\xe2\x80\x9d we reasoned that \xe2\x80\x9cabsent an emergency, no less could be required where intrusions into the human body are concerned,\xe2\x80\x9d even when the search was conducted following a lawful arrest. Id., at 770. We explained that the importance of requiring authorization by a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98neutral and detached magistrate\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d before allowing a law enforcement officer to \xe2\x80\x9cinvade another\xe2\x80\x99s body in search of evidence of guilt is indisputable and great.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Johnson v. United States, \n\n333 U.\xc2\xa0S. 10\n\xe2\x80\x9314 (1948)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs noted, the warrant requirement is subject to ex- ceptions. \xe2\x80\x9cOne well-recognized exception,\xe2\x80\x9d and the one at issue in this case, \xe2\x80\x9capplies when the exigencies of the situation make the needs of law enforcement so compelling that a warrantless search is objectively reasonable under the \n \nFourth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d Kentucky v. King, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 6) (internal quotation marks and brackets omitted). A variety of circumstances may give rise to an exigency sufficient to justify a warrantless search, including law enforcement\xe2\x80\x99s need to provide emergency assistance to an occupant of a home, Michigan v. Fisher, \n\n558 U.\xc2\xa0S. 45\n\xe2\x80\x9348 (2009) (per curiam), engage in \xe2\x80\x9chot pursuit\xe2\x80\x9d of a fleeing suspect, United States v. San- tana, \n\n427 U.\xc2\xa0S. 38\n\xe2\x80\x9343 (1976), or enter a burning building to put out a fire and investigate its cause, Michigan v. Tyler, \n\n436 U.\xc2\xa0S. 499\n\xe2\x80\x93510 (1978). As is relevant here, we have also recognized that in some circumstances law enforcement officers may conduct a search without a warrant to prevent the imminent destruction of evidence. See Cupp v. Murphy, \n\n412 U.\xc2\xa0S. 291, \n296 (1973)\n; Ker v. California, \n\n374 U.\xc2\xa0S. 23\n\xe2\x80\x9341 (1963) (plurality opinion). While these contexts do not necessarily involve equiva- lent dangers, in each a warrantless search is potentially reasonable because \xe2\x80\x9cthere is compelling need for official action and no time to secure a warrant.\xe2\x80\x9d Tyler, 436 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 509.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo determine whether a law enforcement officer faced an emergency that justified acting without a warrant, this Court looks to the totality of circumstances. See Brigham City v. Stuart, \n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 398, \n406 (2006)\n (finding officers\xe2\x80\x99 entry into a home to provide emergency assistance \xe2\x80\x9cplainly reasonable under the circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d); Illinois v. Mc- Arthur, \n\n531 U.\xc2\xa0S. 326, \n331 (2001)\n (concluding that a warrantless seizure of a person to prevent him from returning to his trailer to destroy hidden contraband was reasonable \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n the circumstances of the case before us\xe2\x80\x9d due to exigency); Cupp, 412 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 296 (holding that a limited warrantless search of a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s fingernails to preserve evidence that the suspect was trying to rub off was justified \xe2\x80\x9c[o]n the facts of this case\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Richards v. Wisconsin, \n\n520 U.\xc2\xa0S. 385\n\xe2\x80\x93396 (1997) (rejecting a per\xc2\xa0se exception to the knock-and-announce requirement for felony drug investigations based on presumed exigency, and requiring instead evaluation of police conduct \xe2\x80\x9cin a particular case\xe2\x80\x9d). We apply this \xe2\x80\x9cfinely tuned approach\xe2\x80\x9d to \n \nFourth Amendment reasonableness in this context be- cause the police action at issue lacks \xe2\x80\x9cthe traditional justification that .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a warrant .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. provides.\xe2\x80\x9d Atwater v. Lago Vista, \n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 318, \n347, n. \n16 (2001)\n. Absent that established justification, \xe2\x80\x9cthe fact-specific nature of the reasonableness inquiry,\xe2\x80\x9d Ohio v. Robinette, \n\n519 U.\xc2\xa0S. 33, \n39 (1996)\n, demands that we evaluate each case of alleged exigency based \xe2\x80\x9con its own facts and circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Go-Bart Importing Co. v. United States, \n\n282 U.\xc2\xa0S. 344, \n357 (1931)\n.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur decision in Schmerber applied this totality of the circumstances approach. In that case, the petitioner had suffered injuries in an automobile accident and was taken to the hospital. 384 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 758. While he was there receiving treatment, a police officer arrested the petitioner for driving while under the influence of alcohol and ordered a blood test over his objection. Id., at 758\xe2\x80\x93759. After explaining that the warrant requirement applied generally to searches that intrude into the human body, we concluded that the warrantless blood test \xe2\x80\x9cin the present case\xe2\x80\x9d was nonetheless permissible because the officer \xe2\x80\x9cmight reasonably have believed that he was confronted with an emergency, in which the delay necessary to obtain a warrant, under the circumstances, threatened \xe2\x80\x98the destruction of evidence.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 770 (quoting Preston v. United States, \n\n376 U.\xc2\xa0S. 364, \n367 (1964)\n).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn support of that conclusion, we observed that evidence could have been lost because \xe2\x80\x9cthe percentage of alcohol in the blood begins to diminish shortly after drinking stops, as the body functions to eliminate it from the system.\xe2\x80\x9d 384 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 770. We added that \xe2\x80\x9c[p]articularly in a case such as this, where time had to be taken to bring the accused to a hospital and to investigate the scene of the accident, there was no time to seek out a magistrate and secure a warrant.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 770\xe2\x80\x93771. \xe2\x80\x9cGiven these special facts,\xe2\x80\x9d we found that it was appropriate for the police to act without a warrant. Id., at 771. We further held that the blood test at issue was a reasonable way to recover the evidence because it was highly effective, \xe2\x80\x9cinvolve[d] vir- tually no risk, trauma, or pain,\xe2\x80\x9d and was conducted in a reasonable fashion \xe2\x80\x9cby a physician in a hospital environment according to accepted medical practices.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. And in conclusion, we noted that our judgment that there had been no \n \nFourth Amendment violation was strictly based \xe2\x80\x9con the facts of the present record.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 772.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThus, our analysis in Schmerber fits comfortably within our case law applying the exigent circumstances exception. In finding the warrantless blood test reasonable in Schmerber, we considered all of the facts and circumstances of the particular case and carefully based our holding on those specific facts.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe State properly recognizes that the reasonableness of a warrantless search under the exigency exception to the warrant requirement must be evaluated based on the totality of the circumstances. Brief for Petitioner 28\xe2\x80\x9329. But the State nevertheless seeks a per\xc2\xa0se rule for blood testing in drunk-driving cases. The State contends that whenever an officer has probable cause to believe an individual has been driving under the influence of alcohol, exigent circumstances will necessarily exist because BAC evidence is inherently evanescent. As a result, the State claims that so long as the officer has probable cause and the blood test is conducted in a reasonable manner, it is categorically reasonable for law enforcement to obtain the blood sample without a warrant.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is true that as a result of the human body\xe2\x80\x99s natural metabolic processes, the alcohol level in a person\xe2\x80\x99s blood begins to dissipate once the alcohol is fully absorbed and continues to decline until the alcohol is eliminated. See Skinner, 489 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 623; Schmerber, 384 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 770\xe2\x80\x93771. Testimony before the trial court in this case indicated that the percentage of alcohol in an individual\xe2\x80\x99s blood typically decreases by approximately 0.015 percent to 0.02 percent per hour once the alcohol has been fully absorbed. App. 47. More precise calculations of the rate at which alcohol dissipates depend on various individual characteristics (such as weight, gender, and alcohol tolerance) and the circumstances in which the alcohol was consumed. See Stripp, Forensic and Clinical Issues in Alcohol Analysis, in Forensic Chemistry Handbook 437\xe2\x80\x93441 (L. Kobilinsky ed. 2012). Regardless of the exact elimination rate, it is sufficient for our purposes to note that because an individual\xe2\x80\x99s alcohol level gradually declines soon after he stops drinking, a significant delay in testing will negatively affect the probative value of the results. This fact was essential to our holding in Schmerber, as we recognized that, under the circumstances, further delay in order to secure a warrant after the time spent investigating the scene of the accident and transporting the injured suspect to the hospital to receive treatment would have threatened the destruction of evidence. 384 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 770\xe2\x80\x93771.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut it does not follow that we should depart from careful case-by-case assessment of exigency and adopt the categorical rule proposed by the State and its amici. In those drunk-driving investigations where police officers can reasonably obtain a warrant before a blood sample can be drawn without significantly undermining the efficacy of the search, the \n \nFourth Amendment mandates that they do so. See McDonald v. United States, \n\n335 U.\xc2\xa0S. 451, \n456 (1948)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cWe cannot .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. excuse the absence of a search warrant without a showing by those who seek exemption from the constitutional mandate that the exigencies of the situation made [the search] imperative\xe2\x80\x9d). We do not doubt that some circumstances will make obtaining a warrant impractical such that the dissipation of alcohol from the bloodstream will support an exigency justifying a properly conducted warrantless blood test. That, however, is a reason to decide each case on its facts, as we did in Schmerber, not to accept the \xe2\x80\x9cconsiderable overgeneralization\xe2\x80\x9d that a per\xc2\xa0se rule would reflect. Richards, 520 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 393.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe context of blood testing is different in critical respects from other destruction-of-evidence cases in which the police are truly confronted with a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98now or never\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d situation. Roaden v. Kentucky, \n\n413 U.\xc2\xa0S. 496, \n505 (1973)\n. In contrast to, for example, circumstances in which the suspect has control over easily disposable evidence, see Georgia v. Randolph, \n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 103\n, n.\xc2\xa06 (2006); Cupp, 412 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 296, BAC evidence from a drunk-driving suspect naturally dissipates over time in a gradual and relatively predictable manner. Moreover, because a police officer must typically transport a drunk-driving suspect to a medical facility and obtain the assistance of someone with appropriate medical training before conducting a blood test, some delay between the time of the arrest or accident and the time of the test is inevitable regardless of whether police officers are required to obtain a warrant. See State v. Shriner, 751 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 538, 554 (Minn. 2008) (Meyer, J., dissenting). This reality undermines the force of the State\xe2\x80\x99s contention, endorsed by the dissent, see post, at 3 (opinion of Thomas, J.), that we should recognize a categorical exception to the warrant requirement because BAC evidence \xe2\x80\x9cis actively being destroyed with every minute that passes.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 27. Consider, for example, a situation in which the warrant process will not significantly increase the delay before the blood test is conducted because an officer can take steps to secure a warrant while the suspect is being transported to a medical facility by another officer. In such a circumstance, there would be no plausible justification for an exception to the warrant requirement.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe State\xe2\x80\x99s proposed per\xc2\xa0se rule also fails to account for advances in the 47 years since Schmerber was decided that allow for the more expeditious processing of warrant applications, particularly in contexts like drunk-driving investigations where the evidence offered to establish probable cause is simple. The Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure were amended in 1977 to permit federal magistrate judges to issue a warrant based on sworn testimony communicated by telephone. See \n\n91Stat.\n319. As amended, the law now allows a federal magistrate judge to con- sider \xe2\x80\x9cinformation communicated by telephone or other reliable electronic means.\xe2\x80\x9d Fed. Rule Crim. Proc. 4.1. States have also innovated. Well over a majority of States allow police officers or prosecutors to apply for search warrants remotely through various means, including telephonic or radio communication, electronic communication such as e-mail, and video conferencing.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t And in addition to technology-based developments, jurisdictions have found other ways to streamline the warrant process, such as by using standard-form warrant applications for drunk-driving investigations.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe by no means claim that telecommunications inno- vations have, will, or should eliminate all delay from the warrant-application process. Warrants inevitably take some time for police officers or prosecutors to complete and for magistrate judges to review. Telephonic and electronic warrants may still require officers to follow time-consuming formalities designed to create an adequate record, such as preparing a duplicate warrant before calling the magistrate judge. See Fed. Rule Crim. Proc. 4.1(b)(3). And improvements in communications technology do not guarantee that a magistrate judge will be available when an officer needs a warrant after making a late-night arrest. But technological developments that enable police officers to secure warrants more quickly, and do so without undermining the neutral magistrate judge\xe2\x80\x99s essential role as a check on police discretion, are relevant to an assessment of exigency. That is particularly so in this context, where BAC evidence is lost gradually and relatively predictably.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOf course, there are important countervailing concerns. While experts can work backwards from the BAC at the time the sample was taken to determine the BAC at the time of the alleged offense, longer intervals may raise questions about the accuracy of the calculation. For that reason, exigent circumstances justifying a warrantless blood sample may arise in the regular course of law enforcement due to delays from the warrant application process. But adopting the State\xe2\x80\x99s per\xc2\xa0se approach would improperly ignore the current and future technological developments in warrant procedures, and might well diminish the incentive for jurisdictions \xe2\x80\x9cto pursue progressive approaches to warrant acquisition that preserve the protections afforded by the warrant while meeting the legitimate interests of law enforcement.\xe2\x80\x9d State v. Rodriguez, 2007 UT 15, \xc2\xb646, 156 P.\xc2\xa03d 771, 779.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t In short, while the natural dissipation of alcohol in the blood may support a finding of exigency in a specific case, as it did in Schmerber, it does not do so categorically. Whether a warrantless blood test of a drunk-driving suspect is reasonable must be determined case by case based on the totality of the circumstances.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tC\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn an opinion concurring in part and dissenting in part, The Chief Justice agrees that the State\xe2\x80\x99s proposed per\xc2\xa0se rule is overbroad because \xe2\x80\x9c[f]or exigent circumstances to justify a warrantless search .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. there must .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. be \xe2\x80\x98no time to secure a warrant.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 6 (quoting Tyler, 436 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 509). But The Chief Justice then goes on to suggest his own categorical rule under which a warrantless blood draw is permissible if the officer could not secure a warrant (or reasonably believed he could not secure a warrant) in the time it takes to transport the suspect to a hospital or similar facility and obtain medical assistance. Post, at 8\xe2\x80\x939. Although we agree that delay inherent to the blood-testing process is relevant to evaluating exigency, see supra, at 10, we decline to substitute The Chief Justice\xe2\x80\x99s modified per\xc2\xa0se rule for our traditional totality of the circumstances analysis.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor one thing, making exigency completely dependent on the window of time between an arrest and a blood test produces odd consequences. Under The Chief Justice\xe2\x80\x99s rule, if a police officer serendipitously stops a suspect near an emergency room, the officer may conduct a noncon- sensual warrantless blood draw even if all agree that a warrant could be obtained with very little delay under the circumstances (perhaps with far less delay than an average ride to the hospital in the jurisdiction). The rule would also distort law enforcement incentives. As with the State\xe2\x80\x99s per\xc2\xa0se rule, The Chief Justice\xe2\x80\x99s rule might discourage efforts to expedite the warrant process because it categorically authorizes warrantless blood draws so long as it takes more time to secure a warrant than to obtain medical assistance. On the flip side, making the requirement of independent judicial oversight turn exclusively on the amount of time that elapses between an arrest and BAC testing could induce police departments and individual officers to minimize testing delay to the detriment of other values. The Chief Justice correctly observes that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]his case involves medical personnel drawing blood at a medical facility, not police officers doing so by the side of the road.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 6\xe2\x80\x937, n.\xc2\xa02. But The Chief Justice does not say that roadside blood draws are necessarily un- reasonable, and if we accepted The Chief Justice\xe2\x80\x99s approach, they would become a more attractive option for the police. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe remaining arguments advanced in support of a per\xc2\xa0se exigency rule are unpersuasive.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe State and several of its amici, including the United States, express concern that a case-by-case approach to exigency will not provide adequate guidance to law enforcement officers deciding whether to conduct a blood test of a drunk-driving suspect without a warrant. The Chief Justice and the dissent also raise this concern. See post, at 1, 9\xe2\x80\x9310 (opinion of Roberts, C.\xc2\xa0J.); post, at 5\xe2\x80\x937 (opinion of Thomas, J.). While the desire for a bright-line rule is understandable, the \n \nFourth Amendment will not tolerate adoption of an overly broad categorical approach that would dilute the warrant requirement in a context where significant privacy interests are at stake. Moreover, a case-by-case approach is hardly unique within our \n \nFourth Amendment jurisprudence. Numerous police actions are judged based on fact-intensive, totality of the circumstances analyses rather than according to categorical rules, including in situations that are more likely to require police officers to make difficult split-second judgments. See, e.g., Illinois v. Wardlow, \n\n528 U.\xc2\xa0S. 119\n\xe2\x80\x93125 (2000) (whether an officer has reasonable suspicion to make an investigative stop and to pat down a suspect for weapons under Terry v. Ohio, \n\n392 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1 (1968)\n); Robinette, 519 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 39\xe2\x80\x9340 (whether valid consent has been given to search); Tennessee v. Garner, \n\n471 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n\xe2\x80\x939, 20 (1985) (whether force used to effectuate a seizure, including deadly force, is reasonable). As in those contexts, we see no valid substitute for careful case-by-case evaluation of reasonableness here.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t7\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNext, the State and the United States contend that the privacy interest implicated by blood draws of drunk-driving suspects is relatively minimal. That is so, they claim, both because motorists have a diminished expectation of privacy and because our cases have repeatedly indicated that blood testing is commonplace in society and typically involves \xe2\x80\x9cvirtually no risk, trauma, or pain.\xe2\x80\x9d Schmerber, 384 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 771. See also post, at 3, and n.\xc2\xa01 (opinion of Thomas, J.).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut the fact that people are \xe2\x80\x9caccorded less privacy in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. automobiles because of th[e] compelling governmental need for regulation,\xe2\x80\x9d California v. Carney, \n\n471 U.\xc2\xa0S. 386, \n392 (1985)\n, does not diminish a motorist\xe2\x80\x99s privacy interest in preventing an agent of the government from piercing his skin. As to the nature of a blood test conducted in a medical setting by trained personnel, it is concededly less intrusive than other bodily invasions we have found unreasonable. See Winston, 470 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 759\xe2\x80\x93766 (surgery to remove a bullet); Rochin v. California, \n\n342 U.\xc2\xa0S. 165\n\xe2\x80\x93174 (1952) (induced vomiting to extract narcotics capsules ingested by a suspect violated the Due Process Clause). For that reason, we have held that medically drawn blood tests are reasonable in appropriate circumstances. See Skinner, 489 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 618\xe2\x80\x93633 (upholding warrantless blood testing of railroad employees involved in certain train accidents under the \xe2\x80\x9cspecial needs\xe2\x80\x9d doctrine); Schmerber, 384 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 770\xe2\x80\x93772. We have never retreated, however, from our recognition that any compelled intrusion into the human body implicates significant, constitutionally protected privacy interests.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, the State and its amici point to the compelling governmental interest in combating drunk driving and contend that prompt BAC testing, including through blood testing, is vital to pursuit of that interest. They argue that is particularly so because, in addition to laws that make it illegal to operate a motor vehicle under the influence of alcohol, all 50 States and the District of Columbia have enacted laws that make it per\xc2\xa0se unlawful to operate a motor vehicle with a BAC of over 0.08 percent. See National Highway Traffic Safety Admin. (NHTSA), Al- cohol and Highway Safety: A Review of the State of Knowledge 167 (No. 811374, Mar. 2011) (NHTSA Review).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t8\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t To enforce these provisions, they reasonably assert, accurate BAC evidence is critical. See also post, at 4\xe2\x80\x935 (opinion of Roberts, C.\xc2\xa0J.); post, at 4\xe2\x80\x935 (opinion of Thomas, J.).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cNo one can seriously dispute the magnitude of the drunken driving problem or the States\xe2\x80\x99 interest in eradicating it.\xe2\x80\x9d Michigan Dept. of State Police v. Sitz, \n\n496 U.\xc2\xa0S. 444, \n451 (1990)\n. Certainly we do not. While some progress has been made, drunk driving continues to exact a terrible toll on our society. See NHTSA, Traffic Safety Facts, 2011 Data 1 (No. 811700, Dec. 2012) (reporting that 9,878 people were killed in alcohol-impaired driving crashes in 2011, an average of one fatality every 53 minutes).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut the general importance of the government\xe2\x80\x99s interest in this area does not justify departing from the warrant requirement without showing exigent circumstances that make securing a warrant impractical in a particular case. To the extent that the State and its amici contend that applying the traditional \n \nFourth Amendment totality-of-the-circumstances analysis to determine whether an exigency justified a warrantless search will undermine the governmental interest in preventing and prosecuting drunk-driving offenses, we are not convinced.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs an initial matter, States have a broad range of legal tools to enforce their drunk-driving laws and to secure BAC evidence without undertaking warrantless nonconsensual blood draws. For example, all 50 States have adopted implied consent laws that require motorists, as a condition of operating a motor vehicle within the State, to consent to BAC testing if they are arrested or otherwise detained on suspicion of a drunk-driving offense. See NHTSA Review 173; supra, at 2 (describing Missouri\xe2\x80\x99s implied consent law). Such laws impose significant consequences when a motorist withdraws consent; typically the motorist\xe2\x80\x99s driver\xe2\x80\x99s license is immediately suspended or revoked, and most States allow the motorist\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to take a BAC test to be used as evidence against him in a subsequent criminal prosecution. See NHTSA Review 173\xe2\x80\x93175; see also South Dakota v. Neville, \n\n459 U.\xc2\xa0S. 553\n\xe2\x80\x93564 (1983) (holding that the use of such an adverse inference does not violate the \n \nFifth Amendment right against self-incrimination).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is also notable that a majority of States either place significant restrictions on when police officers may obtain a blood sample despite a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s refusal (often limiting testing to cases involving an accident resulting in death or serious bodily injury) or prohibit nonconsensual blood tests altogether.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t9\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Among these States, several lift restrictions on nonconsensual blood testing if law enforcement officers first obtain a search warrant or similar court order.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t10\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Cf. Bullcoming v. New Mexico, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 3) (noting that the blood test was obtained pursuant to a warrant after the petitioner refused a breath test). We are aware of no evidence indicating that restrictions on nonconsensual blood testing have compromised drunk-driving enforcement efforts in the States that have them. And in fact, field studies in States that permit nonconsensual blood testing pursuant to a warrant have suggested that, although warrants do impose administrative burdens, their use can reduce breath-test-refusal rates and improve law enforcement\xe2\x80\x99s ability to recover BAC evidence. See NHTSA, Use of Warrants for Breath Test Refusal: Case Studies 36\xe2\x80\x9338 (No. 810852, Oct. 2007).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo be sure, \xe2\x80\x9cStates [may] choos[e] to protect privacy beyond the level that the \n \nFourth Amendment requires.\xe2\x80\x9d Virginia v. Moore, \n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 164, \n171 (2008)\n. But wide-spread state restrictions on nonconsensual blood testing provide further support for our recognition that compelled blood draws implicate a significant privacy interest. They also strongly suggest that our ruling today will not \xe2\x80\x9cseverely hamper effective law enforcement.\xe2\x80\x9d Garner, 471 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 19.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIV\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe State argued before this Court that the fact that alcohol is naturally metabolized by the human body creates an exigent circumstance in every case. The State did not argue that there were exigent circumstances in this particular case because a warrant could not have been obtained within a reasonable amount of time. In his testimony before the trial court, the arresting officer did not identify any other factors that would suggest he faced an emergency or unusual delay in securing a warrant. App. 40. He testified that he made no effort to obtain a search warrant before conducting the blood draw even though he was \xe2\x80\x9csure\xe2\x80\x9d a prosecuting attorney was on call and even though he had no reason to believe that a magistrate judge would have been unavailable. Id., at 39, 41\xe2\x80\x9342. The officer also acknowledged that he had obtained search warrants before taking blood samples in the past without difficulty. Id., at 42. He explained that he elected to forgo a warrant application in this case only because he believed it was not legally necessary to obtain a warrant. Id., at 39\xe2\x80\x9340. Based on this testimony, the trial court concluded that there was no exigency and specifically found that, although the arrest took place in the middle of the night, \xe2\x80\x9ca prosecutor was readily available to apply for a search warrant and a judge was readily available to issue a warrant.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 43a.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t11\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Missouri Supreme Court in turn affirmed that judgment, holding first that the dissipation of alcohol did not establish a per\xc2\xa0se exigency, and second that the State could not otherwise satisfy its burden of establishing exigent circumstances. 358 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d, at 70, 74\xe2\x80\x9375. In petitioning for certiorari to this Court, the State challenged only the first holding; it did not separately contend that the warrantless blood test was reasonable regardless of whether the natural dissipation of alcohol in a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s blood categorically justifies dispensing with the warrant requirement. See Pet. for Cert. i.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tHere and in its own courts the State based its case on an insistence that a driver who declines to submit to testing after being arrested for driving under the influence of alcohol is always subject to a nonconsensual blood test without any precondition for a warrant. That is incorrect.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAlthough the Missouri Supreme Court referred to this case as \xe2\x80\x9cunquestionably a routine DWI case,\xe2\x80\x9d 358 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d, at 74, the fact that a particular drunk-driving stop is \xe2\x80\x9croutine\xe2\x80\x9d in the sense that it does not involve \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98special facts,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., such as the need for the police to attend to a car accident, does not mean a warrant is required. Other factors present in an ordinary traffic stop, such as the procedures in place for obtaining a warrant or the avail- ability of a magistrate judge, may affect whether the police can obtain a warrant in an expeditious way and therefore may establish an exigency that permits a warrantless search. The relevant factors in determining whether a warrantless search is reasonable, including the practical problems of obtaining a warrant within a timeframe that still preserves the opportunity to obtain reliable evidence, will no doubt vary depending upon the circumstances in the case.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBecause this case was argued on the broad proposition that drunk-driving cases present a per\xc2\xa0se exigency, the arguments and the record do not provide the Court with an adequate analytic framework for a detailed discussion of all the relevant factors that can be taken into account in determining the reasonableness of acting without a warrant. It suffices to say that the metabolization of alcohol in the bloodstream and the ensuing loss of evidence are among the factors that must be considered in deciding whether a warrant is required. No doubt, given the large number of arrests for this offense in different jurisdictions nationwide, cases will arise when anticipated delays in obtaining a warrant will justify a blood test without judicial authorization, for in every case the law must be concerned that evidence is being destroyed. But that inquiry ought not to be pursued here where the question is not properly before this Court. Having rejected the sole argument presented to us challenging the Missouri Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision, we affirm its judgment.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe hold that in drunk-driving investigations, the natural dissipation of alcohol in the bloodstream does not con- stitute an exigency in every case sufficient to justify conducting a blood test without a warrant.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA police officer reading this Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion would have no idea\xe2\x80\x94no idea\xe2\x80\x94what the \n \nFourth Amendment requires of him, once he decides to obtain a blood sample from a drunk driving suspect who has refused a breathalyzer test. I have no quarrel with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ctotality of the circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d approach as a general matter; that is what our cases require. But the circumstances in drunk driving cases are often typical, and the Court should be able to offer guidance on how police should handle cases like the one before us.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn my view, the proper rule is straightforward. Our cases establish that there is an exigent circumstances exception to the warrant requirement. That exception applies when there is a compelling need to prevent the imminent destruction of important evidence, and there is no time to obtain a warrant. The natural dissipation of alcohol in the bloodstream constitutes not only the imminent but ongoing destruction of critical evidence. That would qualify as an exigent circumstance, except that there may be time to secure a warrant before blood can be drawn. If there is, an officer must seek a warrant. If an officer could reasonably conclude that there is not, the exigent circumstances exception applies by its terms, and the blood may be drawn without a warrant.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe \n \nFourth Amendment provides:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cThe right of the people to be secure in their persons, houses, papers, and effects, against unreasonable searches and seizures, shall not be violated, and no Warrants shall issue, but upon probable cause, supported by Oath or affirmation, and particularly describing the place to be searched, and the persons or things to be seized.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThat language does not state that warrants are required prior to searches, but this Court has long held that warrants must generally be obtained. See Kentucky v. King, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 5). We have also held that bodily intrusions like blood draws constitute searches and are subject to the warrant requirement. See Schmerber v. California, \n\n384 U.\xc2\xa0S. 757, \n767, \n770 (1966)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tHowever, \xe2\x80\x9cthe ultimate touchstone of the \n \nFourth Amendment is \xe2\x80\x98reasonableness,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brigham City v. Stuart, \n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 398, \n403 (2006)\n, and thus \xe2\x80\x9cthe warrant requirement is subject to certain reasonable exceptions,\xe2\x80\x9d King, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 6). One of those exceptions is known as the \xe2\x80\x9cexigent circumstances exception,\xe2\x80\x9d which \xe2\x80\x9capplies when the exigencies of the situation make the needs of law enforcement so compelling that a warrantless search is objectively reasonable under the \n \nFourth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks and alterations omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWithin the exigent circumstances exception, we have identified several sets of exigent circumstances excusing the need for a warrant. For example, there is an emergency aid exception to the warrant requirement. In Brigham City, supra, at 403, we held that \xe2\x80\x9claw enforcement officers may enter a home without a warrant to render emergency assistance to an injured occupant or to protect an occupant from imminent injury.\xe2\x80\x9d There is also a fire exception to the warrant requirement. In Michigan v. Tyler, \n\n436 U.\xc2\xa0S. 499, \n509 (1978)\n, we held that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] burning building clearly presents an exigency of sufficient proportions to render a warrantless entry \xe2\x80\x98reasonable.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d And there is a hot pur-suit exception to the warrant requirement as well. In United States v. Santana, \n\n427 U.\xc2\xa0S. 38 (1976)\n, and Warden, Md. Penitentiary v. Hayden, \n\n387 U.\xc2\xa0S. 294 (1967)\n, we recognized \xe2\x80\x9cthe right of police, who had probable cause to believe that an armed robber had entered a house a few minutes before, to make a warrantless entry to arrest the robber and to search for weapons.\xe2\x80\x9d Santana, supra, at 42. In each of these cases, the requirement that we base our decision on the \xe2\x80\x9ctotality of the circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d has not prevented us from spelling out a general rule for the police to follow.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe exigency exception most on point here is the one for imminent destruction of evidence. We have affirmed on several occasions that \xe2\x80\x9claw enforcement officers may make a warrantless entry onto private property .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to prevent the imminent destruction of evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Brigham City, supra, at 403 (citing Ker v. California, \n\n374 U.\xc2\xa0S. 23, \n40 (1963)\n (plurality opinion)); see also, e.g., King, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 6). For example, in Ker, the police had reason to believe that the defendant was in possession of marijuana and was expecting police pursuit. We upheld the officers\xe2\x80\x99 warrantless entry into the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s home, with the plurality explaining that the drugs \xe2\x80\x9ccould be quickly and easily destroyed\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cdistributed or hidden before a warrant could be obtained at that time of night.\xe2\x80\x9d 374 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 40, 42.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs an overarching principle, we have held that if there is a \xe2\x80\x9ccompelling need for official action and no time to secure a warrant,\xe2\x80\x9d the warrant requirement may be excused. Tyler, supra, at 509. The question here is whether and how this principle applies in the typical case of a police officer stopping a driver on suspicion of drunk driving.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe reasonable belief that critical evidence is being destroyed gives rise to a compelling need for blood draws in cases like this one. Here, in fact, there is not simply a belief that any alcohol in the bloodstream will be destroyed; it is a biological certainty. Alcohol dissipates from the bloodstream at a rate of 0.01 percent to 0.025 percent per hour. Stripp, Forensic and Clinical Issues in Alcohol Analysis, in Forensic Chemistry Handbook 440 (L. Kobilinsky ed. 2012). Evidence is literally disappearing by the minute. That certainty makes this case an even stronger one than usual for application of the exigent circumstances exception.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAnd that evidence is important. A serious and deadly crime is at issue. According to the Department of Transportation, in 2011, one person died every 53 minutes due to drinking and driving. National Highway Traffic Safety Admin. (NHTSA), Traffic Safety Facts, 2011 Data 1 (No. 811700, Dec. 2012). No surprise then that drinking and driving is punished severely, including with jail time. See generally Dept. of Justice, Bureau of Justice Statistics, L. Maruschak, Special Report, DWI Offenders under Correctional Supervision (1999). McNeely, for instance, faces up to four years in prison. See App. 22\xe2\x80\x9323 (citing Mo. Ann. Stat. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7558.011, 577.010, 577.023 (West 2011)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEvidence of a driver\xe2\x80\x99s blood alcohol concentration (BAC) is crucial to obtain convictions for such crimes. All 50 States and the District of Columbia have laws providing that it is per\xc2\xa0se illegal to drive with a BAC of 0.08 percent or higher. Most States also have laws establishing additional penalties for drivers who drive with a \xe2\x80\x9chigh BAC,\xe2\x80\x9d often defined as 0.15 percent or above. NHTSA, Digest of Impaired Driving and Selected Beverage Control Laws, pp. vii, x\xe2\x80\x93xviii (No. 811673, Oct. 2012). BAC evidence clearly matters. And when drivers refuse breathalyzers, as McNeely did here, a blood draw becomes necessary to obtain that evidence.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe need to prevent the imminent destruction of BAC evidence is no less compelling because the incriminating alcohol dissipates over a limited period of time, rather than all at once. As noted, the concentration of alcohol can make a difference not only between guilt and innocence, but between different crimes and different degrees of punishment. The officer is unlikely to know precisely when the suspect consumed alcohol or how much; all he knows is that critical evidence is being steadily lost. Fire can spread gradually, but that does not lessen the need and right of the officers to respond immediately. See Tyler, supra.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMcNeely contends that there is no compelling need for a warrantless blood draw, because if there is some alcohol left in the blood by the time a warrant is obtained, the State can use math and science to work backwards and identify a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s BAC at the time he was driving. See Brief for Respondent 44\xe2\x80\x9346. But that\xe2\x80\x99s not good enough. We have indicated that exigent circumstances justify warrantless entry when drugs are about to be flushed down the toilet. See, e.g., King, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 7\xe2\x80\x938). We have not said that, because there could well be drug paraphernalia elsewhere in the home, or because a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s co-conspirator might testify to the amount of drugs involved, the drugs themselves are not crucial and there is no compelling need for warrantless entry.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe same approach should govern here. There is a compelling need to search because alcohol\xe2\x80\x94the nearly conclusive evidence of a serious crime\xe2\x80\x94is dissipating from the bloodstream. The need is no less compelling because the police might be able to acquire second-best evidence some other way.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor exigent circumstances to justify a warrantless search, however, there must also be \xe2\x80\x9cno time to secure a warrant.\xe2\x80\x9d Tyler, 436 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 509; see Schmerber, 384 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 771 (warrantless search legal when \xe2\x80\x9cthere was no time to seek out a magistrate and secure a warrant\xe2\x80\x9d). In this respect, obtaining a blood sample from a suspected drunk driver differs from other exigent circumstances cases.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tImportantly, there is typically delay between the moment a drunk driver is stopped and the time his blood can be drawn. Drunk drivers often end up in an emergency room, but they are not usually pulled over in front of one. In most exigent circumstances situations, police are just outside the door to a home. Inside, evidence is about to be destroyed, a person is about to be injured, or a fire has broken out. Police can enter promptly and must do so to respond effectively to the emergency. But when police pull a person over on suspicion of drinking and driving, they cannot test his blood right away.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t There is a time-consuming obstacle to their search, in the form of a trip to the hospital and perhaps a wait to see a medical pro-fessional. In this case, for example, approximately 25 minutes elapsed between the time the police stopped McNeely and the time his blood was drawn. App. 36, 38.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs noted, the fact that alcohol dissipates gradually from the bloodstream does not diminish the compelling need for a search\xe2\x80\x94critical evidence is still disappearing. But the fact that the dissipation persists for some time means that the police\xe2\x80\x94although they may not be able to do anything about it right away\xe2\x80\x94may still be able to respond to the ongoing destruction of evidence later on.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThere might, therefore, be time to obtain a warrant in many cases. As the Court explains, police can often request warrants rather quickly these days. At least 30 States provide for electronic warrant applications. See ante, at 10\xe2\x80\x9312, and n.\xc2\xa04. In many States, a police officer can call a judge, convey the necessary information, and be authorized to affix the judge\xe2\x80\x99s signature to a warrant. See, e.g., Ala. Rule Crim. Proc. 3.8(b) (2012\xe2\x80\x932013); Alaska Stat. \xc2\xa712.35.015 (2012); Idaho Code \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa719\xe2\x80\x934404, 19\xe2\x80\x934406 (Lexis 2004); Minn. Rules Crim. Proc. 36.01\xe2\x80\x9336.08 (2010 and Supp. 2013); Mont. Code Ann. \xc2\xa746\xe2\x80\x935\xe2\x80\x93222 (2012); see generally NHTSA, Use of Warrants for Breath Test Refusal: Case Studies 6\xe2\x80\x9332 (No. 810852, Oct. 2007) (overview of procedures in Arizona, Michigan, Oregon, and Utah). Utah has an e-warrant procedure where a police officer enters information into a system, the system notifies a prosecutor, and upon approval the officer forwards the information to a magistrate, who can electronically re-turn a warrant to the officer. Utah, e-Warrants: Cross Boundary Collaboration 1 (2008). Judges have been known to issue warrants in as little as five minutes. Bergreen, Faster Warrant System Hailed, Salt Lake Tribune, Dec. 26, 2008, p. B1, col. 1. And in one county in Kansas, police officers can e-mail warrant requests to judges\xe2\x80\x99 iPads; judges have signed such warrants and e-mailed them back to officers in less than 15 minutes. Benefiel, DUI Search Warrants: Prosecuting DUI Refusals, 9 Kansas Prosecutor 17, 18 (Spring 2012). The police are presumably familiar with the mechanics and time involved in the warrant process in their particular jurisdiction.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn a case such as this, applying the exigent circumstances exception to the general warrant requirement of the \n \nFourth Amendment seems straightforward: If there is time to secure a warrant before blood can be drawn, the police must seek one. If an officer could reasonably conclude that there is not sufficient time to seek and receive a warrant, or he applies for one but does not receive a response before blood can be drawn, a warrantless blood draw may ensue. See Tyler, supra, at 509; see also Illinois v. Rodriguez, \n\n497 U.\xc2\xa0S. 177\n\xe2\x80\x93186 (1990) (\xe2\x80\x9cin order to satisfy the \xe2\x80\x98reasonableness\xe2\x80\x99 requirement of the \n \nFourth Amendment, what is generally demanded of the many factual determinations that must regularly be made by .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. police officer[s] conducting a search or seizure under one of the exceptions to the warrant requirement .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is not that they always be correct, but that they always be reasonable\xe2\x80\x9d); Terry v. Ohio, \n\n392 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1, \n20 (1968)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cpolice must, whenever practicable, obtain advance judicial approval of searches and seizures through the warrant procedure\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRequiring police to apply for a warrant if practicable increases the likelihood that a neutral, detached judicial officer will review the case, helping to ensure that there is probable cause for any search and that any search is reasonable. We have already held that forced blood draws can be constitutional\xe2\x80\x94that such searches can be reasonable\xe2\x80\x94 but that does not change the fact that they are significant bodily intrusions. See Schmerber, 384 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 770 (upholding a warrantless forced blood draw but noting the \xe2\x80\x9cimportance of informed, detached and deliberate determinations of the issue whether or not to invade another\xe2\x80\x99s body in search of evidence of guilt\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cindisputable and great\xe2\x80\x9d). Requiring a warrant whenever practicable helps ensure that when blood draws occur, they are indeed justified.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAt the same time, permitting the police to act without a warrant to prevent the imminent destruction of evidence is well established in \n \nFourth Amendment law. There is no reason to preclude application of that exception in drunk driving cases simply because it may take the police some time to be able to respond to the undoubted destruction of evidence, or because the destruction occurs continuously over an uncertain period.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAnd that is so even in situations where police have requested a warrant but do not receive a timely response. An officer who reasonably concluded there was no time to secure a warrant may have blood drawn from a suspect upon arrival at a medical facility. There is no reason an officer should be in a worse position, simply because he sought a warrant prior to his arrival at the hospital.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court resists the foregoing, contending that the question presented somehow inhibits such a focused analysis in this case. See ante, at 20\xe2\x80\x9323. It does not. The question presented is whether a warrantless blood draw is permissible under the \n \nFourth Amendment \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon the natural dissipation of alcohol in the bloodstream.\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Cert. i. The majority answers \xe2\x80\x9cIt depends,\xe2\x80\x9d and so do I. The difference is that the majority offers no ad-ditional guidance, merely instructing courts and police officers to consider the totality of the circumstances. I believe more meaningful guidance can be provided about how to handle the typical cases, and nothing about the question presented prohibits affording that guidance.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA plurality of the Court also expresses concern that my approach will discourage state and local efforts to expedite the warrant application process. See ante, at 14. That is not plausible: Police and prosecutors need warrants in a wide variety of situations, and often need them quickly. They certainly would not prefer a slower process, just because that might obviate the need to ask for a warrant in the occasional drunk driving case in which a blood draw is necessary. The plurality\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion also overlooks the interest of law enforcement in the protection a warrant provides.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court is correct when it says that every case must be considered on its particular facts. But the pertinent facts in drunk driving cases are often the same, and the police should know how to act in recurring factual situations. Simply put, when a drunk driving suspect fails field sobriety tests and refuses a breathalyzer, whether a warrant is required for a blood draw should come down to whether there is time to secure one.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSchmerber itself provides support for such an analysis. The Court there made much of the fact that \xe2\x80\x9cthere was no time to seek out a magistrate and secure a warrant.\xe2\x80\x9d 384 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 771. It did so in an era when cell phones and e-mail were unknown. It follows quite naturally that if cell phones and e-mail mean that there is time to contact a magistrate and secure a warrant, that must be done. At the same time, there is no need to jettison the well-established exception for the imminent destruction of evidence, when the officers are in a position to do something about it.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe \n \nFourth Amendment states that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he right of the people to be secure in their persons .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. against unreasonable searches and seizures, shall not be violated, and no Warrants shall issue, but upon probable cause.\xe2\x80\x9d Before a search occurs, \xe2\x80\x9ca warrant must generally be secured,\xe2\x80\x9d Kentucky v. King, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 5), but \xe2\x80\x9cthis presumption may be overcome in some circumstances because \xe2\x80\x98[t]he ultimate touchstone of the \n \nFourth Amendment is \xe2\x80\x9creasonableness.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Brig- ham City v. Stuart, \n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 398, \n403 (2006)\n; alteration in original).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe presence of \xe2\x80\x9cexigent circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d is one such exception to the warrant requirement. Exigency applies when \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the needs of law enforcement [are] so compelling that [a] warrantless search is objectively reasonable under the \n \nFourth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 6) (quoting Mincey v. Arizona, \n\n437 U.\xc2\xa0S. 385, \n394 (1978)\n; second alteration in original). Thus, when exigent circumstances are present, officers may take actions that would typically require a warrant, such as entering a home in hot pursuit of a fleeing suspect. 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 6). As relevant in this case, officers may also conduct a warrantless search when they have probable cause to believe that failure to act would result in \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98imminent destruction of evidence.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Brigham City, supra, at 403).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOnce police arrest a suspect for drunk driving, each passing minute eliminates probative evidence of the crime. The human liver eliminates alcohol from the bloodstream at a rate of approximately 0.015 percent to 0.020 percent per hour, ante, at 8, with some heavy drinkers as high as 0.022 percent per hour, Brief for Petitioner 21 (citing medical studies), depending on, among other things, a per- son\xe2\x80\x99s sex, weight, body type, and drinking history. Ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939; Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 23. The Court has acknowledged this fact since Schmerber v. California, \n\n384 U.\xc2\xa0S. 757, \n770 (1966)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cWe are told that the percentage of alcohol in the blood begins to diminish shortly after drinking stops, as the body functions to eliminate it from the system\xe2\x80\x9d). In that case, the Court recognized that destruction of evidence is inherent in drunk-driving cases and held that an officer investigating a drunk-driving crime \xe2\x80\x9cmight reasonably [believe] that he [is] confronted with an emergency, in which the delay necessary to obtain a warrant, under the circumstances, threaten[s] \xe2\x80\x98the destruction of evidence.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Preston v. United States, \n\n376 U.\xc2\xa0S. 364, \n367 (1964)\n). The Court explained that drawing a person\xe2\x80\x99s blood is \xe2\x80\x9ca highly ef- fective means of determining the degree to which [he] is under the influence of alcohol\xe2\x80\x9d and is a reasonable procedure because blood tests are \xe2\x80\x9ccommonplace\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cinvolv[e] virtually no risk, trauma, or pain.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t 384 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 771. The Court, therefore, held that dissipation of alcohol in the blood constitutes an exigency that allows a blood draw without a warrant.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe rapid destruction of evidence acknowledged by the parties, the majority, and Schmerber\xe2\x80\x99s exigency determination occurs in every situation where police have probable cause to arrest a drunk driver. In turn, that destruction of evidence implicates the exigent-circumstances doctrine. See Cupp v. Murphy, \n\n412 U.\xc2\xa0S. 291 (1973)\n. In Cupp, officers questioning a murder suspect observed a spot on the suspect\xe2\x80\x99s finger that they believed might be dried blood. Id., at 292. After the suspect began making obvious efforts to remove the spots from his hands, the officers took samples without obtaining either his consent or a warrant. Id., at 296. Following a \n \nFourth Amendment challenge to this search, the Court held that the \xe2\x80\x9cready destructibility of the evidence\xe2\x80\x9d and the suspect\xe2\x80\x99s observed efforts to destroy it \xe2\x80\x9cjustified the police in subjecting him to the very limited search necessary to preserve the highly evanescent evidence they found under his fingernails.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn this case, a similar exigency is present. Just as the suspect\xe2\x80\x99s efforts to destroy \xe2\x80\x9chighly evanescent evidence\xe2\x80\x9d gave rise to the exigency in Cupp, the natural metabolization of blood alcohol concentration (BAC) creates an exigency once police have probable cause to believe the driver is drunk. It naturally follows that police may conduct a search in these circumstances. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA hypothetical involving classic exigent circumstances further illustrates the point. Officers are watching a warehouse and observe a worker carrying bundles from the warehouse to a large bonfire and throwing them into the blaze. The officers have probable cause to believe the bundles contain marijuana. Because there is only one person carrying the bundles, the officers believe it will take hours to completely destroy the drugs. During that time the officers likely could obtain a warrant. But it is clear that the officers need not sit idly by and watch the destruction of evidence while they wait for a warrant. The fact that it will take time for the evidence to be destroyed and that some evidence may remain by the time the offi- cers secure a warrant are not relevant to the exigency. However, the ever-diminishing quantity of drugs may have an impact on the severity of the crime and the length of the sentence. See, e.g., \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(D) (lower penalties for less than 50 kilograms of marijuana); United States Sentencing Commission, Guidelines Manual \xc2\xa72D1.1(c) (Nov. 2012) (drug quantity table tying base offense level to drug amounts). Conducting a warrantless search of the warehouse in this situation would be entirely reasonable. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe same obtains in the drunk-driving context. Just because it will take time for the evidence to be completely destroyed does not mean there is no exigency. Congress has conditioned federal highway grants on states\xe2\x80\x99 adoption of laws penalizing the operation of a motor vehicle \xe2\x80\x9cwith a blood alcohol concentration of 0.08 percent or greater.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n23 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7163(a). See also 23 CFR \xc2\xa71225.1 (2012). All 50 States have acceded to this condition. National Highway Traffic Safety Admin. (NHTSA), Alcohol and Highway Safety: A Review of the State of Knowledge 167 (No. 811374, Mar. 2011) (NHTSA State Review); Mo. Ann. Stat. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7577.012(1)\xe2\x80\x93(2) (West 2011) (establishing Missouri\xe2\x80\x99s 0.08 percent BAC standard). Moreover, as of 2005, 32 States and the District of Columbia imposed additional penalties for BAC levels of 0.15 percent or higher. NHTSA State Review 175. Missouri is one such State. See, e.g., Mo. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7577.010(3)\xe2\x80\x93(4), 577.012(4)\xe2\x80\x93(5) (suspended sentence unavailable even for first offenders with BAC above 0.15 percent unless they complete drug treatment; mandatory jail time if treatment is not completed). As a result, the level of intoxication directly bears on enforcement of these laws. Nothing in the \n \nFourth Amendment requires officers to allow evidence essential to enforcement of drunk-driving laws to be destroyed while they wait for a warrant to issue.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn today\xe2\x80\x99s decision, the Court elides the certainty of evidence destruction in drunk-driving cases and focuses primarily on the time necessary for destruction. In doing so, it turns the exigency inquiry into a question about the amount of evidentiary destruction police must permit before they may act without a warrant. That inquiry is inconsistent with the actual exigency at issue: the un- contested destruction of evidence due to metabolization of alcohol. See Part I, supra. Moreover, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s facts-and-circumstances analysis will be difficult to administer, a particularly important concern in the \n \nFourth Amendment context.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment reflects nothing more than a vague notion that everything will come out right most of the time so long as the delay is not too lengthy. Ante, at 12 (justifying delays in part because \xe2\x80\x9cBAC evidence is lost gradually and relatively predictably\xe2\x80\x9d); ante, at 10 (same, quoting Brief for Petitioner 27). But hard percentage lines have meaningful legal consequences in the drunk-driving context. The fact that police will be able to retrieve some evidence before it is all destroyed is simply not relevant to the exigency inquiry.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority believes that, absent special facts and circumstances, some destruction of evidence is acceptable. See ante, at 9 (\xe2\x80\x9csufficient for our purposes to note that .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. significant delay in testing will negatively affect the probative value\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). This belief must rest on the assumption that whatever evidence remains once a warrant is obtained will be sufficient to prosecute the suspect. But that assumption is clearly wrong. Suspects\xe2\x80\x99 initial levels of intoxication and the time necessary to obtain warranted blood draws will vary widely from case to case. Even a slight delay may significantly affect probative value in borderline cases of suspects who are moderately intoxicated or suspects whose BAC is near a statutory threshold that triggers a more serious offense. See supra, at 4\xe2\x80\x935 (discussing laws penalizing heightened BAC levels). Similarly, the time to obtain a warrant can be ex- pected to vary, and there is no reason to believe it will do so in a predictable fashion.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFurther, the Court nowhere explains how an officer in the field is to apply the facts-and-circumstances test it adopts. First, officers do not have the facts needed to assess how much time can pass before too little evidence remains. They will never know how intoxicated a suspect is at the time of arrest. Otherwise, there would be no need for testing. Second, they will not know how long it will take to roust a magistrate from his bed, reach the hospital, or obtain a blood sample once there. As the Minnesota Supreme Court recognized in rejecting arguments like those adopted by the Court today:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he officer has no control over how long it would take to travel to a judge or the judge\xe2\x80\x99s availability. The officer also may not know the time of the suspect\xe2\x80\x99s last drink, the amount of alcohol consumed, or the rate at which the suspect will metabolize alcohol. Finally, an officer cannot know how long it will take to obtain the blood sample once the suspect is brought to the hospital. Under a totality of the circumstances test, an officer would be called upon to speculate on each of these considerations and predict how long the most probative evidence of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s blood-alcohol level would continue to exist before a blood sample was no longer reliable.\xe2\x80\x9d State v. Shriner, 751 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 538, 549 (2008) (footnote omitted). \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe Court should not adopt a rule that requires police to guess whether they will be able to obtain a warrant before \xe2\x80\x9ctoo much\xe2\x80\x9d evidence is destroyed, for the police lack reli- able information concerning the relevant variables.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis case demonstrates the uncertainty officers face with regard to the delay caused by obtaining a warrant. The arresting officer clearly had probable cause to believe respondent was drunk, but there was no way for the officer to quantify the level of intoxication to determine how quickly he needed to act in order to obtain probative evidence. Another officer testified at respondent\xe2\x80\x99s trial that it typically took 1 \xc2\xbd to 2 hours to obtain a drunk-driving warrant at night in Cape Girardeau County, Missouri. See App. 53\xe2\x80\x9354. Respondent submitted an exhibit summarizing six late afternoon and nighttime drunk-driving search warrants that suggests the time may be shorter. Brief for Respondent 56; App. 70. Ultimately this factual tiff is beside the point; the spotty evidence regarding timing itself illustrates the fact that delays in obtaining warrants are unpredictable and potentially lengthy. A rule that requires officers (and ultimately courts) to balance transportation delays, hospital availability, and ac- cess to magistrates is not a workable rule for cases where natural processes inevitably destroy the evidence with every passing minute.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe availability of telephonic warrant applications is not an answer to this conundrum. See ante, at 10\xe2\x80\x9312, and n.\xc2\xa04. For one thing, Missouri still requires written warrant applications and affidavits, Mo. Ann. Stat. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7542.276.2(1), 542.276.2.3 (West Supp. 2012), rendering the Court\xe2\x80\x99s 50-State survey irrelevant to the actual disposition of this case. Ante, at 11, n.\xc2\xa04. But even if telephonic applications were available in Missouri, the same difficulties would arise. As the majority correctly recognizes, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]arrants inevitably take some time for police officers or prosecutors to complete and for magistrate judges to review.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12. During that time, evidence is destroyed, and police who have probable cause to believe a crime has been committed should not have to guess how long it will take to secure a warrant. \n\t\t\t
