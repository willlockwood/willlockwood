MARYLAND v. SHATZER

\n

certiorari to the court of appeals of maryland

\n

No. 08\xe2\x80\x93680.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 5, 2009\xe2\x80\x94Decided February 24, 2010

\n

In 2003, a police detective tried to question respondent Shatzer, who was incarcerated at a Maryland prison pursuant to a prior conviction, about allegations that he had sexually abused his son. Shatzer invoked his Miranda right to have counsel present during interrogation, so the detective terminated the interview. Shatzer was released back into the general prison population, and the investigation was closed. Another detective reopened the investigation in 2006 and attempted to interrogate Shatzer, who was still incarcerated. Shatzer waived his Miranda rights and made inculpatory statements. The trial court refused to suppress those statements, reasoning that Edwards v. Arizona, 451 U. S. 477, did not apply because Shatzer had experienced a break in Miranda custody prior to the 2006 interrogation. Shatzer was convicted of sexual child abuse. The Court of Appeals of Maryland reversed, holding that the mere passage of time does not end the Edwards protections, and that, assuming, arguendo, a break-in-custody exception to Edwards existed, Shatzer\xe2\x80\x99s release back into the general prison population did not constitute such a break.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Because Shatzer experienced a break in Miranda custody lasting more than two weeks between the first and second attempts at interrogation, Edwards does not mandate suppression of his 2006 statements. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Edwards created a presumption that once a suspect invokes the Miranda right to the presence of counsel, any waiver of that right in response to a subsequent police attempt at custodial interrogation is involuntary. Edwards\xe2\x80\x99 fundamental purpose is to \xe2\x80\x9c[p]reserv[e] the integrity of an accused\xe2\x80\x99s choice to communicate with police only through counsel,\xe2\x80\x9d Patterson v. Illinois, 487 U. S. 285, 291, by \xe2\x80\x9cprevent[ing] police from badgering [him] into waiving his previously asserted Miranda rights,\xe2\x80\x9d Michigan v. Harvey, 494 U. S. 344, 350. It is easy to believe that a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s later waiver was coerced or badgered when he has been held in uninterrupted Miranda custody since his first refusal to waive. He remains cut off from his normal life and isolated in a \xe2\x80\x9cpolice-dominated atmosphere,\xe2\x80\x9d Miranda v. Arizona, 384 U. S. 436, 456, where his captors \xe2\x80\x9cappear to control [his] fate,\xe2\x80\x9d Illinois v. Perkins, 496 U. S. 292, 297. But where a suspect has been released from custody and returned to his normal life for some time before the later attempted interrogation, there is little reason to think that his change of heart has been coerced. Because the Edwards presumption has been established by opinion of this Court, it is appropriate for this Court to specify the period of release from custody that will terminate its application. See County of Riverside v. McLaughlin, 500 U. S. 44. The Court concludes that the appropriate period is 14 days, which provides ample time for the suspect to get reacclimated to his normal life, consult with friends and counsel, and shake off any residual coercive effects of prior custody. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Shatzer\xe2\x80\x99s release back into the general prison population constitutes a break in Miranda custody. Lawful imprisonment imposed upon conviction does not create the coercive pressures produced by investigative custody that justify Edwards. When previously incarcerated suspects are released back into the general prison population, they return to their accustomed surroundings and daily routine\xe2\x80\x94they regain the degree of control they had over their lives before the attempted interrogation. Their continued detention is relatively disconnected from their prior unwillingness to cooperate in an investigation. The \xe2\x80\x9cinherently compelling pressures\xe2\x80\x9d of custodial interrogation ended when Shatzer returned to his normal life. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We consider whether a break in custody ends the presumption of involuntariness established in Edwards v. Arizona, 451 U. S. 477 (1981).

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In August 2003, a social worker assigned to the Child Advocacy Center in the Criminal Investigation Division of the Hagerstown Police Department referred to the department allegations that respondent Michael Shatzer, Sr., had sexually abused his 3-year-old son. At that time, Shatzer was incarcerated at the Maryland Correctional Institution-Hagerstown, serving a sentence for an unrelated child-sexual-abuse offense. Detective Shane Blankenship was assigned to the investigation and interviewed Shatzer at the correctional institution on August 7, 2003. Before asking any questions, Blankenship reviewed Shatzer\xe2\x80\x99s Miranda rights with him, and obtained a written waiver of those rights. When Blankenship explained that he was there to question Shatzer about sexually abusing his son, Shatzer expressed confusion\xe2\x80\x94he had thought Blankenship was an attorney there to discuss the prior crime for which he was incarcerated. Blankenship clarified the purpose of his visit, and Shatzer declined to speak without an attorney. Accordingly, Blankenship ended the interview, and Shatzer was released back into the general prison population. Shortly thereafter, Blankenship closed the investigation.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Two years and six months later, the same social worker referred more specific allegations to the department about the same incident involving Shatzer. Detective Paul Hoover, from the same division, was assigned to the investigation. He and the social worker interviewed the victim, then eight years old, who described the incident in more detail. With this new information in hand, on March 2, 2006, they went to the Roxbury Correctional Institute, to which Shatzer had since been transferred, and interviewed Shatzer in a maintenance room outfitted with a desk and three chairs. Hoover explained that he wanted to ask Shatzer about the alleged incident involving Shatzer\xe2\x80\x99s son. Shatzer was surprised because he thought that the investigation had been closed, but Hoover explained they had opened a new file. Hoover then read Shatzer his Miranda rights and obtained a written waiver on a standard department form.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Hoover interrogated Shatzer about the incident for approximately 30 minutes. Shatzer denied ordering his son to perform fellatio on him, but admitted to masturbating in front of his son from a distance of less than three feet. Before the interview ended, Shatzer agreed to Hoover\xe2\x80\x99s request that he submit to a polygraph examination. At no point during the interrogation did Shatzer request to speak with an attorney or refer to his prior refusal to answer questions without one.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Five days later, on March 7, 2006, Hoover and another detective met with Shatzer at the correctional facility to administer the polygraph examination. After reading Shatzer his Miranda rights and obtaining a written waiver, the other detective administered the test and concluded that Shatzer had failed. When the detectives then questioned Shatzer, he became upset, started to cry, and incriminated himself by saying, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98I didn\xe2\x80\x99t force him. I didn\xe2\x80\x99t force him.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 405 Md. 585, 590, 954 A.\xc2\xa02d 1118, 1121 (2008). After making this inculpatory statement, Shatzer requested an attorney, and Hoover promptly ended the interrogation.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The State\xe2\x80\x99s Attorney for Washington County charged Shatzer with second-degree sexual offense, sexual child abuse, second-degree assault, and contributing to conditions rendering a child in need of assistance. Shatzer moved to suppress his March 2006 statements pursuant to Edwards. The trial court held a suppression hearing and later denied Shatzer\xe2\x80\x99s motion. The Edwards protections did not apply, it reasoned, because Shatzer had experienced a break in custody for Miranda purposes between the 2003 and 2006 interrogations. No. 21\xe2\x80\x93K\xe2\x80\x9306\xe2\x80\x9337799 (Cir. Ct. Washington Cty., Md., Sept. 14, 2006), App. 55. Shatzer pleaded not guilty, waived his right to a jury trial, and proceeded to a bench trial based on an agreed statement of facts. In accordance with the agreement, the State described the interview with the victim and Shatzer\xe2\x80\x99s 2006 statements to the detectives. Based on the proffered testimony of the victim and the \xe2\x80\x9cadmission of the defendant as to the act of masturbation,\xe2\x80\x9d the trial court found Shatzer guilty of sexual child abuse of his son.[Footnote 1] No. 21\xe2\x80\x93K\xe2\x80\x9306\xe2\x80\x9337799 (Cir. Ct. Washington Cty., Md., Sept. 21, 2006), id., at 70, 79.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Over the dissent of two judges, the Court of Appeals of Maryland reversed and remanded. The court held that \xe2\x80\x9cthe passage of time alone is insufficient to [end] the protections afforded by Edwards,\xe2\x80\x9d and that, assuming, arguendo, a break-in-custody exception to Edwards existed, Shatzer\xe2\x80\x99s release back into the general prison population between interrogations did not constitute a break in custody. 405 Md., at 606\xe2\x80\x93607, 954 A.\xc2\xa02d, at 1131. We granted certiorari, 555 U. S. ___ (2009).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Fifth Amendment, which applies to the States by virtue of the Fourteenth Amendment, Malloy v. Hogan, 378 U. S. 1, 6 (1964), provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o person . . . shall be compelled in any criminal case to be a witness against himself.\xe2\x80\x9d U. S. Const., Amdt. 5. In Miranda v. Arizona, 384 U. S. 436 (1966), the Court adopted a set of prophylactic measures to protect a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s Fifth Amendment right from the \xe2\x80\x9cinherently compelling pressures\xe2\x80\x9d of custodial interrogation. Id., at 467. The Court observed that \xe2\x80\x9cincommunicado interrogation\xe2\x80\x9d in an \xe2\x80\x9cunfamiliar,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cpolice-dominated atmosphere,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 456\xe2\x80\x93457, involves psychological pressures \xe2\x80\x9cwhich work to undermine the individual\xe2\x80\x99s will to resist and to compel him to speak where he would not otherwise do so freely,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 467. Consequently, it reasoned, \xe2\x80\x9c[u]nless adequate protective devices are employed to dispel the compulsion inherent in custodial surroundings, no statement obtained from the defendant can truly be the product of his free choice.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 458.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To counteract the coercive pressure, Miranda announced that police officers must warn a suspect prior to questioning that he has a right to remain silent, and a right to the presence of an attorney. Id., at 444. After the warnings are given, if the suspect indicates that he wishes to remain silent, the interrogation must cease. Id., at 473\xe2\x80\x93474. Similarly, if the suspect states that he wants an attorney, the interrogation must cease until an attorney is present. Id., at 474. Critically, however, a suspect can waive these rights. Id., at 475. To establish a valid waiver, the State must show that the waiver was knowing, intelligent, and voluntary under the \xe2\x80\x9chigh standar[d] of proof for the waiver of constitutional rights [set forth in] Johnson v. Zerbst, 304 U. S. 458 (1938).\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 475.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Edwards, the Court determined that Zerbst\xe2\x80\x99s traditional standard for waiver was not sufficient to protect a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s right to have counsel present at a subsequent interrogation if he had previously requested counsel; \xe2\x80\x9cadditional safeguards\xe2\x80\x9d were necessary. 451 U. S., at 484. The Court therefore superimposed a \xe2\x80\x9csecond layer of prophylaxis,\xe2\x80\x9d McNeil v. Wisconsin, 501 U. S. 171, 176 (1991). Edwards held:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hen an accused has invoked his right to have counsel present during custodial interrogation, a valid waiver of that right cannot be established by showing only that he responded to further police-initiated custodial interrogation even if he has been advised of his rights. . . . [He] is not subject to further interrogation by the authorities until counsel has been made available to him, unless the accused himself initiates further communication, exchanges, or conversations with the police.\xe2\x80\x9d 451 U. S., at 484\xe2\x80\x93485.

\n

\nThe rationale of Edwards is that once a suspect indicates that \xe2\x80\x9che is not capable of undergoing [custodial] questioning without advice of counsel,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cany subsequent waiver that has come at the authorities\xe2\x80\x99 behest, and not at the suspect\xe2\x80\x99s own instigation, is itself the product of the \xe2\x80\x98inherently compelling pressures\xe2\x80\x99 and not the purely voluntary choice of the suspect.\xe2\x80\x9d Arizona v. Roberson, 486 U. S. 675, 681 (1988). Under this rule, a voluntary Miranda waiver is sufficient at the time of an initial attempted interrogation to protect a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s right to have counsel present, but it is not sufficient at the time of subsequent attempts if the suspect initially requested the presence of counsel. The implicit assumption, of course, is that the subsequent requests for interrogation pose a significantly greater risk of coercion. That increased risk results not only from the police\xe2\x80\x99s persistence in trying to get the suspect to talk, but also from the continued pressure that begins when the individual is taken into custody as a suspect and sought to be interrogated\xe2\x80\x94pressure likely to \xe2\x80\x9cincrease as custody is prolonged,\xe2\x80\x9d Minnick v. Mississippi, 498 U. S. 146, 153 (1990). The Edwards presumption of involuntariness ensures that police will not take advantage of the mounting coercive pressures of \xe2\x80\x9cprolonged police custody,\xe2\x80\x9d Roberson, 486 U. S., at 686, by repeatedly attempting to question a suspect who previously requested counsel until the suspect is \xe2\x80\x9cbadgered into submission,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 690 (Kennedy, J., dissenting).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have frequently emphasized that the Edwards rule is not a constitutional mandate, but judicially prescribed prophylaxis. See, e.g., Montejo v. Louisiana, 556 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (slip op., at 7\xe2\x80\x938); Michigan v. Harvey, 494 U. S. 344, 349 (1990); Solem v. Stumes, 465 U. S. 638, 644, n.\xc2\xa04 (1984). Because Edwards is \xe2\x80\x9cour rule, not a constitutional command,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cit is our obligation to justify its expansion.\xe2\x80\x9d Roberson, supra, at 688 (Kennedy, J., dissenting). Lower courts have uniformly held that a break in custody ends the Edwards presumption, see, e.g., People v. Storm, 28 Cal. 4th 1007, 1023\xe2\x80\x931024, and n.\xc2\xa06, 52 P.\xc2\xa03d 52, 61\xe2\x80\x9362, and n.\xc2\xa06 (2002) (collecting state and federal cases), but we have previously addressed the issue only in dicta, see McNeil, supra, at 177 (Edwards applies \xe2\x80\x9cassuming there has been no break in custody\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A judicially crafted rule is \xe2\x80\x9cjustified only by reference to its prophylactic purpose,\xe2\x80\x9d Davis v. United States, 512 U. S. 452, 458 (1994) (internal quotation marks omitted), and applies only where its benefits outweigh its costs, Montejo, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 14). We begin with the benefits. Edwards\xe2\x80\x99 presumption of involuntariness has the incidental effect of \xe2\x80\x9cconserv[ing] judicial resources which would otherwise be expended in making difficult determinations of voluntariness.\xe2\x80\x9d Minnick, supra, at 151. Its fundamental purpose, however, is to \xe2\x80\x9c[p]reserv[e] the integrity of an accused\xe2\x80\x99s choice to communicate with police only through counsel,\xe2\x80\x9d Patterson v. Illinois, 487 U. S. 285, 291 (1988), by \xe2\x80\x9cprevent[ing] police from badgering a defendant into waiving his previously asserted Miranda rights,\xe2\x80\x9d Harvey, supra, at 350. Thus, the benefits of the rule are measured by the number of coerced confessions it suppresses that otherwise would have been admitted. See Montejo, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 14).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is easy to believe that a suspect may be coerced or badgered into abandoning his earlier refusal to be questioned without counsel in the paradigm Edwards case. That is a case in which the suspect has been arrested for a particular crime and is held in uninterrupted pretrial custody while that crime is being actively investigated. After the initial interrogation, and up to and including the second one, he remains cut off from his normal life and companions, \xe2\x80\x9cthrust into\xe2\x80\x9d and isolated in an \xe2\x80\x9cunfamiliar,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cpolice-dominated atmosphere,\xe2\x80\x9d Miranda, 384 U. S., at 456\xe2\x80\x93457, where his captors \xe2\x80\x9cappear to control [his] fate,\xe2\x80\x9d Illinois v. Perkins, 496 U. S. 292, 297 (1990). That was the situation confronted by the suspects in Edwards, Roberson, and Minnick, the three cases in which we have held the Edwards rule applicable. Edwards was arrested pursuant to a warrant and taken to a police station, where he was interrogated until he requested counsel. Edwards, 451 U. S.em>., at 478\xe2\x80\x93479. The officer ended the interrogation and took him to the county jail,[Footnote 2] but at 9:15 the next morning, two of the officer\xe2\x80\x99s colleagues reinterrogated Edwards at the jail. Id., at 479. Roberson was arrested \xe2\x80\x9cat the scene of a just-completed burglary\xe2\x80\x9d and interrogated there until he requested a lawyer. Roberson, 486 U. S., at 678. A different officer interrogated him three days later while he \xe2\x80\x9cwas still in custody pursuant to the arrest.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Minnick was arrested by local police and taken to the San Diego jail, where two FBI agents interrogated him the next morning until he requested counsel. Minnick, 498 U. S., at 148\xe2\x80\x93149. Two days later a Mississippi Deputy Sheriff reinterrogated him at the jail. Id., at 149. None of these suspects regained a sense of control or normalcy after they were initially taken into custody for the crime under investigation.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When, unlike what happened in these three cases, a suspect has been released from his pretrial custody and has returned to his normal life for some time before the later attempted interrogation, there is little reason to think that his change of heart regarding interrogation without counsel has been coerced. He has no longer been isolated. He has likely been able to seek advice from an attorney, family members, and friends.[Footnote 3] And he knows from his earlier experience that he need only demand counsel to bring the interrogation to a halt; and that investigative custody does not last indefinitely. In these circumstances, it is far fetched to think that a police officer\xe2\x80\x99s asking the suspect whether he would like to waive his Miranda rights will any more \xe2\x80\x9cwear down the accused,\xe2\x80\x9d Smith v. Illinois, 469 U. S. 91, 98 (1984) (per curiam), than did the first such request at the original attempted interrogation\xe2\x80\x94which is of course not deemed coercive. His change of heart is less likely attributable to \xe2\x80\x9cbadgering\xe2\x80\x9d than it is to the fact that further deliberation in familiar surroundings has caused him to believe (rightly or wrongly) that cooperating with the investigation is in his interest. Uncritical extension of Edwards to this situation would not significantly increase the number of genuinely coerced confessions excluded. The \xe2\x80\x9cjustification for a conclusive presumption disappears when application of the presumption will not reach the correct result most of the time.\xe2\x80\x9d Coleman v. Thompson, 501 U. S. 722, 737 (1991).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the same time that extending the Edwards rule yields diminished benefits, extending the rule also increases its costs: the in-fact voluntary confessions it excludes from trial, and the voluntary confessions it deters law enforcement officers from even trying to obtain. Voluntary confessions are not merely \xe2\x80\x9ca proper element in law enforcement,\xe2\x80\x9d Miranda, supra, at 478, they are an \xe2\x80\x9cunmitigated good,\xe2\x80\x9d McNeil, 501 U. S., at 181, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98essential to society\xe2\x80\x99s compelling interest in finding, convicting, and punishing those who violate the law,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. (quoting Moran v. Burbine, 475 U. S. 412, 426 (1986)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The only logical endpoint of Edwards disability is termination of Miranda custody and any of its lingering effects. Without that limitation\xe2\x80\x94and barring some purely arbitrary time-limit[Footnote 4]\xe2\x80\x94every Edwards prohibition of custodial interrogation of a particular suspect would be eternal. The prohibition applies, of course, when the subsequent interrogation pertains to a different crime, Roberson, supra, when it is conducted by a different law enforcement authority, Minnick, 498 U. S. 146, and even when the suspect has met with an attorney after the first interrogation, ibid. And it not only prevents questioning ex ante; it would render invalid ex\xc2\xa0post, confessions invited and obtained from suspects who (unbeknownst to the interrogators) have acquired Edwards immunity previously in connection with any offense in any jurisdiction.[Footnote 5] In a country that harbors a large number of repeat offenders,[Footnote 6] this consequence is disastrous.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We conclude that such an extension of Edwards is not justified; we have opened its \xe2\x80\x9cprotective umbrella,\xe2\x80\x9d Solem, 465 U. S., at 644, n.\xc2\xa04, far enough. The protections offered by Miranda, which we have deemed sufficient to ensure that the police respect the suspect\xe2\x80\x99s desire to have an attorney present the first time police interrogate him, adequately ensure that result when a suspect who initially requested counsel is reinterrogated after a break in custody that is of sufficient duration to dissipate its coercive effects.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If Shatzer\xe2\x80\x99s return to the general prison population qualified as a break in custody (a question we address in Part III, infra), there is no doubt that it lasted long enough (2 years) to meet that durational requirement. But what about a break that has lasted only one year? Or only one week? It is impractical to leave the answer to that question for clarification in future case-by-case adjudication; law enforcement officers need to know, with certainty and beforehand, when renewed interrogation is lawful. And while it is certainly unusual for this Court to set forth precise time limits governing police action, it is not unheard-of. In County of Riverside v. McLaughlin, 500 U. S. 44 (1991), we specified 48 hours as the time within which the police must comply with the requirement of Gerstein v. Pugh, 420 U. S. 103 (1975), that a person arrested without a warrant be brought before a magistrate to establish probable cause for continued detention.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Like McLaughlin, this is a case in which the requisite police action (there, presentation to a magistrate; here, abstention from further interrogation) has not been prescribed by statute but has been established by opinion of this Court. We think it appropriate to specify a period of time to avoid the consequence that continuation of the Edwards presumption \xe2\x80\x9cwill not reach the correct result most of the time.\xe2\x80\x9d Coleman, supra, at 737. It seems to us that period is 14 days. That provides plenty of time for the suspect to get reacclimated to his normal life, to consult with friends and counsel, and to shake off any residual coercive effects of his prior custody.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The 14-day limitation meets Shatzer\xe2\x80\x99s concern that a break-in-custody rule lends itself to police abuse. He envisions that once a suspect invokes his Miranda right to counsel, the police will release the suspect briefly (to end the Edwards presumption) and then promptly bring him back into custody for reinterrogation. But once the suspect has been out of custody long enough (14 days) to eliminate its coercive effect, there will be nothing to gain by such gamesmanship\xe2\x80\x94nothing, that is, except the entirely appropriate gain of being able to interrogate a suspect who has made a valid waiver of his Miranda rights.[Footnote 7]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Shatzer argues that ending the Edwards protections at a break in custody will undermine Edwards\xe2\x80\x99 purpose to conserve judicial resources. To be sure, we have said that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he merit of the Edwards decision lies in the clarity of its command and the certainty of its application.\xe2\x80\x9d Minnick, 498 U. S., at 151. But clarity and certainty are not goals in themselves. They are valuable only when they reasonably further the achievement of some substantive end\xe2\x80\x94here, the exclusion of compelled confessions. Confessions obtained after a 2-week break in custody and a waiver of Miranda rights are most unlikely to be compelled, and hence are unreasonably excluded. In any case, a break-in-custody exception will dim only marginally, if at all, the bright-line nature of Edwards. In every case involving Edwards, the courts must determine whether the suspect was in custody when he requested counsel and when he later made the statements he seeks to suppress. Now, in cases where there is an alleged break in custody, they simply have to repeat the inquiry for the time between the initial invocation and reinterrogation. In most cases that determination will be easy. And when it is determined that the defendant pleading Edwards has been out of custody for two weeks before the contested interrogation, the court is spared the fact-intensive inquiry into whether he ever, anywhere, asserted his Miranda right to counsel.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The facts of this case present an additional issue. No one questions that Shatzer was in custody for Miranda purposes during the interviews with Detective Blankenship in 2003 and Detective Hoover in 2006. Likewise, no one questions that Shatzer triggered the Edwards protections when, according to Detective Blankenship\xe2\x80\x99s notes of the 2003 interview, he stated that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98he would not talk about this case without having an attorney present,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 405 Md., at 589, 954 A.\xc2\xa02d, at 1120. After the 2003 interview, Shatzer was released back into the general prison population where he was serving an unrelated sentence. The issue is whether that constitutes a break in Miranda custody.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have never decided whether incarceration constitutes custody for Miranda purposes, and have indeed explicitly declined to address the issue. See Perkins, 496 U. S., at 299. See also Bradley v. Ohio, 497 U. S. 1011, 1013 (1990) (Marshall, J., dissenting from denial of certiorari). Whether it does depends upon whether it exerts the coercive pressure that Miranda was designed to guard against\xe2\x80\x94the \xe2\x80\x9cdanger of coercion [that] results from the interaction of custody and official interrogation.\xe2\x80\x9d Perkins, supra, at 297 (emphasis added). To determine whether a suspect was in Miranda custody we have asked whether \xe2\x80\x9cthere is a \xe2\x80\x98formal arrest or restraint on freedom of movement\xe2\x80\x99 of the degree associated with a formal arrest.\xe2\x80\x9d New York v. Quarles, 467 U. S. 649, 655 (1984); see also Stansbury v. California, 511 U. S. 318, 322 (1994) (per curiam). This test, no doubt, is satisfied by all forms of incarceration. Our cases make clear, however, that the freedom-of-movement test identifies only a necessary and not a sufficient condition for Miranda custody. We have declined to accord it \xe2\x80\x9ctalismanic power,\xe2\x80\x9d because Miranda is to be enforced \xe2\x80\x9conly in those types of situations in which the concerns that powered the decision are implicated.\xe2\x80\x9d Berkemer v. McCarty, 468 U. S. 420, 437 (1984). Thus, the temporary and relatively nonthreatening detention involved in a traffic stop or Terry stop, see Terry v. Ohio, 392 U. S. 1 (1968), does not constitute Miranda custody. McCarty, supra, at 439\xe2\x80\x93440. See also Perkins, supra, at 296.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Here, we are addressing the interim period during which a suspect was not interrogated, but was subject to a baseline set of restraints imposed pursuant to a prior conviction. Without minimizing the harsh realities of incarceration, we think lawful imprisonment imposed upon conviction of a crime does not create the coercive pressures identified in Miranda.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Interrogated suspects who have previously been convicted of crime live in prison. When they are released back into the general prison population, they return to their accustomed surroundings and daily routine\xe2\x80\x94they regain the degree of control they had over their lives prior to the interrogation. Sentenced prisoners, in contrast to the Miranda paradigm, are not isolated with their accusers. They live among other inmates, guards, and workers, and often can receive visitors and communicate with people on the outside by mail or telephone.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Their detention, moreover, is relatively disconnected from their prior unwillingness to cooperate in an investigation. The former interrogator has no power to increase the duration of incarceration, which was determined at sentencing.[Footnote 8] And even where the possibility of parole exists, the former interrogator has no apparent power to decrease the time served. This is in stark contrast to the circumstances faced by the defendants in Edwards, Roberson, and Minnick, whose continued detention as suspects rested with those controlling their interrogation, and who confronted the uncertainties of what final charges they would face, whether they would be convicted, and what sentence they would receive.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Shatzer\xe2\x80\x99s experience illustrates the vast differences between Miranda custody and incarceration pursuant to conviction. At the time of the 2003 attempted interrogation, Shatzer was already serving a sentence for a prior conviction. After that, he returned to the general prison population in the Maryland Correctional Institution-Hagerstown and was later transferred, for unrelated reasons, down the street to the Roxbury Correctional Institute. Both are medium-security state correctional facilities. See Maryland Div. of Correction Inmate Handbook 7 (2007), online at http://dpscs.md.gov/rehabservs/\ndoc/pdfs/2007_Inmate_Handbook.pdf (all Internet materials as visited Feb. 22, 2010, and available in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file). Inmates in these facilities generally can visit the library each week, id., at 28; have regular exercise and recreation periods, id., at 17; can participate\nin basic adult education and occupational training, id.,\nat 26, 7; are able to send and receive mail, id., at 21\xe2\x80\x9322, 16; and are allowed to receive visitors twice a week,\nsee http://dpscs.md.gov/locations/mcih.shtml; http://www.\ndpscs.state.md.us/locations/rci.shtml. His continued detention after the 2003 interrogation did not depend on what he said (or did not say) to Detective Blankenship, and he has not alleged that he was placed in a higher level of security or faced any continuing restraints as a result of the 2003 interrogation. The \xe2\x80\x9cinherently compelling pressures\xe2\x80\x9d of custodial interrogation ended when he returned to his normal life.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A few words in response to Justice Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 concurrence: It claims we ignore that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen police tell an indigent suspect that he has the right to an attorney\xe2\x80\x9d and then \xe2\x80\x9creinterrogate\xe2\x80\x9d him without providing a lawyer, \xe2\x80\x9cthe suspect is likely to feel that the police lied to him and that he really does not have any right to a lawyer.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 2 (opinion concurring in judgment) (hereinafter concurrence). See also post, at 4, 7, n.\xc2\xa011, 11, n.\xc2\xa016. The fallacy here is that we are not talking about \xe2\x80\x9creinterrogating\xe2\x80\x9d the suspect; we are talking about asking his permission to be interrogated. An officer has in no sense lied to a suspect when, after advising, as Miranda requires, \xe2\x80\x9cYou have the right to remain silent, and if you choose to speak you have the right to the presence of an attorney,\xe2\x80\x9d he promptly ends the attempted interrogation because the suspect declines to speak without counsel present, and then, two weeks later, reapproaches the suspect and asks, \xe2\x80\x9cAre you now willing to speak without a lawyer present?\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The \xe2\x80\x9cconcer[n] that motivated the Edwards line of cases,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 2\xe2\x80\x933, n.\xc2\xa02, is that the suspect will be coerced into saying yes. That concern guides our decision today. Contrary to the concurrence\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion, post, at 3, 5\xe2\x80\x936, there is no reason to believe a suspect will view confession as \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the only way to end his interrogation\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d when, before the interrogation begins, he is told that he can avoid it by simply requesting that he not be interrogated without counsel present\xe2\x80\x94an option that worked before. If, as the concurrence argues will often be the case, post, at 5, a break in custody does not change the suspect\xe2\x80\x99s mind, he need only say so.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The concurrence also accuses the Court of \xe2\x80\x9cignor[ing] that when a suspect asks for counsel, until his request is answered, there are still the same \xe2\x80\x98inherently compelling\xe2\x80\x99 pressures of custodial interrogation on which the Miranda line of cases is based.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 4. We do not ignore these pressures; nor do we suggest that they disappear when custody is recommenced after a break, see post, at 5. But if those pressures are merely \xe2\x80\x9cthe same\xe2\x80\x9d as before, then Miranda provides sufficient protection\xe2\x80\x94as it did before. The Edwards presumption of involuntariness is justified only in circumstances where the coercive pressures have increased so much that suspects\xe2\x80\x99 waivers of Miranda rights are likely to be involuntary most of the time. Contrary to the concurrence\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion, post, at 3, it is only in those narrow circumstances\xe2\x80\x94when custody is unbroken\xe2\x80\x94that the Court has concluded a \xe2\x80\x9cfresh se[t] of Miranda warnings\xe2\x80\x9d is not sufficient. See Roberson, 486 U. S., at 686.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the last analysis, it turns out that the concurrence accepts our principal points. It agrees that Edwards prophylaxis is not perpetual; it agrees that a break in custody reduces the inherently compelling pressure upon which Edwards was based; it agrees that Shatzer\xe2\x80\x99s release back into the general prison population constituted a break in custody; and it agrees that in this case the break was long enough to render Edwards inapplicable. Post, at 10\xe2\x80\x9312. We differ in two respects: Instead of terminating Edwards protection when the custodial pressures that were the basis for that protection dissipate, the concurrence would terminate it when the suspect would no longer \xe2\x80\x9cfeel that he has \xe2\x80\x98been denied the counsel he has clearly requested,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 11. This is entirely unrelated to the rationale of Edwards. If confidence in the police\xe2\x80\x99s promise to provide counsel were the touchstone, Edwards would not have applied in Minnick, where the suspect in continuing custody actually met with appointed counsel. The concurrence\xe2\x80\x99s rule is also entirely unrelated to the existence of a break in custody. While that may relieve the accumulated coercive pressures of custody that are the foundation for Edwards, it is hard to see how it bolsters the suspect\xe2\x80\x99s confidence that if he asks for counsel he will get one.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0And secondly, the concurrence differs from us in declining to say how long after a break in custody the termination of Edwards protection occurs. Two and one-half years, it says, is clearly enough\xe2\x80\x94but it gives law enforcement authorities no further guidance. The concurrence criticizes our use of 14 days as arbitrary and unexplained, post, at 5, and n.\xc2\xa07. But in fact that rests upon the same basis as the concurrence\xe2\x80\x99s own approval of a 212-year break in custody: how much time will justify \xe2\x80\x9ctreating the second interrogation as no more coercive than the first,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 10. Failure to say where the line falls short of 212 years, and leaving that for future case-by-case determination, is certainly less helpful, but not at all less arbitrary.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because Shatzer experienced a break in Miranda custody lasting more than two weeks between the first and second attempts at interrogation, Edwards does not mandate suppression of his March 2006 statements. Accordingly, we reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeals of Maryland, and remand the case for further proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I join Part III of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, which holds that release into the general prison population constitutes a break in custody. I do not join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to extend the presumption of involuntariness established in Edwards v. Arizona, 451 U. S. 477 (1981), for 14 days after custody ends.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is not apparent to me that the presumption of involuntariness the Court recognized in Edwards is justifiable even in the custodial setting to which Edwards applies it. See, e.g., Minnick v. Mississippi, 498 U. S. 146, 160 (1990) (Scalia, J., dissenting). Accordingly, I would not extend the Edwards rule \xe2\x80\x9cbeyond the circumstances present in Edwards itself.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 162. But even if one believes that the Court is obliged to apply Edwards to any case involving continuing custody, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion today goes well beyond that. It extends the presumption of involuntariness Edwards applies in custodial settings to interrogations that occur after custody ends.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court concedes that this extension, like the Edwards presumption itself, is not constitutionally required. The Court nevertheless defends the extension as a judicially created prophylaxis against compelled confessions. Even if one accepts that such prophylaxis is both permissible generally and advisable for some period following a break in custody,[Footnote 1] the Court\xe2\x80\x99s 14-day rule fails to satisfy the criteria our precedents establish for the judicial creation of such a safeguard.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our precedents insist that judicially created prophylactic rules like those in Edwards and Miranda v. Arizona, 384 U. S. 436 (1966), maintain \xe2\x80\x9cthe closest possible fit\xe2\x80\x9d between the rule and the Fifth Amendment interests they seek to protect. United States v. Patane, 542 U. S. 630, 640\xe2\x80\x93641 (2004) (plurality opinion); see generally Montejo v. Louisiana, 556 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (slip op., at 18); Chavez v. Martinez, 538 U. S. 760, 772 (2003) (plurality opinion). The Court\xe2\x80\x99s 14-day rule does not satisfy this test. The Court relates its 14-day rule to the Fifth Amendment simply by asserting that 14 days between release and recapture should provide \xe2\x80\x9cplenty of time for the suspect \xe2\x80\xa6 to shake off any residual coercive effects of his prior custody,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 11.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This ipse dixit does not explain why extending the Edwards presumption for 14 days following a break in custody\xe2\x80\x94as opposed to 0, 10, or 100 days\xe2\x80\x94provides the \xe2\x80\x9cclosest possible fit\xe2\x80\x9d with the Self-Incrimination Clause, Patane, supra, at 640\xe2\x80\x93641; see ante, at 11 (merely stating that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t seems to us that\xe2\x80\x9d the appropriate \xe2\x80\x9cperiod is 14 days\xe2\x80\x9d). Nor does it explain how the benefits of a prophylactic 14-day rule (either on its own terms or compared with other possible rules) \xe2\x80\x9coutweigh its costs\xe2\x80\x9d (which would include the loss of law enforcement information as well as the exclusion of confessions that are in fact voluntary). Ante, at 6 (citing Montejo, supra, at __ (slip op., at 14)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To be sure, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s rule has the benefit of providing a bright line. Ante, at 12. But bright-line rules are not necessary to prevent Fifth Amendment violations, as the Court has made clear when refusing to adopt such rules in cases involving other Miranda rights. See, e.g., Michigan v. Mosley, 423 U. S. 96, 103\xe2\x80\x93104 (1975). And an otherwise arbitrary rule is not justifiable merely because it gives clear instruction to law enforcement officers.[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the Court concedes, \xe2\x80\x9cclarity and certainty are not goals in themselves. They are valuable only when they reasonably further the achievement of some substantive end\xe2\x80\x94here, the exclusion of compelled confessions\xe2\x80\x9d that the Fifth Amendment prohibits. Ante, at 12. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s arbitrary 14-day rule fails this test, even under the relatively permissive criteria set forth in our precedents. Accordingly, I do not join that portion of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s\nopinion.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While I agree that the presumption from Edwards v. Arizona, 451 U. S. 477 (1981), is not \xe2\x80\x9ceternal,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310, and does not mandate suppression of Shatzer\xe2\x80\x99s statement made after a 2-year break in custody, I do not agree with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s newly announced rule: that Edwards always ceases to apply when there is a 14-day break in custody, ante, at 11.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In conducting its \xe2\x80\x9ccost-benefit\xe2\x80\x9d analysis, the Court demeans Edwards as a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98second layer\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9cjudicially prescribed prophylaxis,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 5, 6, 12, n.\xc2\xa07; see also ante, at 6 (describing Edwards as \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98our rule, not a constitutional command\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Arizona v. Roberson, 486 U. S. 675, 688 (1988) (Kennedy, J., dissenting))). The source of the holdings in the long line of cases that includes both Edwards and Miranda, however, is the Fifth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s protection against compelled self-incrimination applied to the \xe2\x80\x9ccompulsion inherent in custodial\xe2\x80\x9d interrogation, Miranda v. Arizona, 384 U. S. 436, 458 (1966), and the \xe2\x80\x9csignifican[ce]\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9cthe assertion of the right to counsel,\xe2\x80\x9d Edwards, 451 U. S., at 485.[Footnote 1] The Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis today is insufficiently sensitive to the concerns that motivated the Edwards line of cases.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The most troubling aspect of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s time-based rule is that it disregards the compulsion caused by a second (or third, or fourth) interrogation of an indigent suspect who was told that if he requests a lawyer, one will be provided for him. When police tell an indigent suspect that he has the right to an attorney, that he is not required to speak without an attorney present, and that an attorney will be provided to him at no cost before questioning, the police have made a significant promise. If they cease questioning and then reinterrogate the suspect 14 days later without providing him with a lawyer, the suspect is likely to feel that the police lied to him and that he really does not have any right to a lawyer.[Footnote 2]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When officers informed Shatzer of his rights during the first interrogation, they presumably informed him that if he requested an attorney, one would be appointed for him before he was asked any further questions. But if an indigent suspect requests a lawyer, \xe2\x80\x9cany further interrogation\xe2\x80\x9d (even 14 days later) \xe2\x80\x9cwithout counsel having been provided will surely exacerbate whatever compulsion to speak the suspect may be feeling.\xe2\x80\x9d Roberson, 486 U. S., at 686. When police have not honored an earlier commitment to provide a detainee with a lawyer, the detainee likely will \xe2\x80\x9cunderstan[d] his (expressed) wishes to have been ignored\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cmay well see further objection as futile and confession (true or not) as the only way to end his interrogation.\xe2\x80\x9d Davis v. United States, 512 U. S. 452, 472\xe2\x80\x93473 (1994) (Souter, J., concurring in judgment). Cf. Cooper v. Dupnik, 963 F.\xc2\xa02d 1220, 1225 (CA9 1992) (en banc) (describing an elaborate police task force plan to ignore a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s requests for counsel, on the theory that such would induce hopelessness and thereby elicit an admission). Simply giving a \xe2\x80\x9cfresh se[t] of Miranda warnings\xe2\x80\x9d will not \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98reassure\xe2\x80\x99 a suspect who has been denied the counsel he has clearly requested that his rights have remained untrammeled.\xe2\x80\x9d Roberson, 486 U. S., at 686.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court never explains why its rule cannot depend on, in addition to a break in custody and passage of time, a concrete event or state of affairs, such as the police having honored their commitment to provide counsel. Instead, the Court simply decides to create a time-based rule, and in so doing, disregards much of the analysis upon which Edwards and subsequent decisions were based. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he assertion of the right to counsel\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c[i]s a significant event.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 3] Edwards, 451 U. S., at 485. As the Court today acknowledges, the right to counsel, like the right to remain silent, is one that police may \xe2\x80\x9ccoerc[e] or badge[r],\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 7, a suspect into abandoning.[Footnote 4] However, as discussed above, the Court ignores the effects not of badgering but of reinterrogating a suspect who took the police at their word that he need not answer questions without an attorney present. See Roberson, 486 U. S., at 686. The Court, moreover, ignores that when a suspect asks for counsel, until his request is answered, there are still the same \xe2\x80\x9cinherently compelling\xe2\x80\x9d pressures of custodial interrogation on which the Miranda line of cases is based, see 486 U. S., at 681,[Footnote 5] and that the concern about compulsion is especially serious for a detainee who has requested a lawyer, an act that signals his \xe2\x80\x9cinability to cope with the pressures of custodial interrogation,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 686.[Footnote 6]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Instead of deferring to these well-settled understandings of the Edwards rule, the Court engages in its own speculation that a 14-day break in custody eliminates the compulsion that animated Edwards. But its opinion gives no strong basis for believing that this is the case.[Footnote 7] A 14-day break in custody does not eliminate the rationale for the initial Edwards rule: The detainee has been told that he may remain silent and speak only through a lawyer and that if he cannot afford an attorney, one will be provided for him. He has asked for a lawyer. He does not have one. He is in custody. And police are still questioning him. A 14-day break in custody does not change the fact that custodial interrogation is inherently compelling. It is unlikely to change the fact that a detainee \xe2\x80\x9cconsiders himself unable to deal with the pressures of custodial interrogation without legal assistance.\xe2\x80\x9d Roberson, 486 U. S., at 683.[Footnote 8] And in some instances, a 14-day break in custody may make matters worse\xc2\xa0[Footnote 9] \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen a suspect understands his (expressed) wishes to have been ignored\xe2\x80\x9d and thus \xe2\x80\x9cmay well see further objection as futile and confession (true or not) as the only way to end his interrogation.\xe2\x80\x9d Davis, 512 U. S., at 472\xe2\x80\x93473 (Souter, J., concurring in judgment).[Footnote 10]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court ignores these understandings from the Edwards line of cases and instead speculates that if a suspect is reinterrogated and eventually talks, it must be that \xe2\x80\x9cfurther deliberation in familiar surroundings has caused him to believe (rightly or wrongly) that cooperating with the investigation is in his interest.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9. But it is not apparent why that is the case. The answer, we are told, is that once a suspect has been out of Miranda custody for 14 days, \xe2\x80\x9c[h]e has likely been able to seek advice from an attorney, family members, and friends.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8. This speculation, however, is overconfident and only questionably relevant. As a factual matter, we do not know whether the defendant has been able to seek advice: First of all, suspects are told that if they cannot afford a lawyer, one will be provided for them. Yet under the majority\xe2\x80\x99s rule, an indigent suspect who took the police at their word when he asked for a lawyer will nonetheless be assumed to have \xe2\x80\x9cbeen able to seek advice from an attorney.\xe2\x80\x9d Second, even suspects who are not indigent cannot necessarily access legal advice (or social advice as the Court presumes) within 14 days. Third, suspects may not realize that they need to seek advice from an attorney. Unless police warn suspects that the interrogation will resume in 14 days, why contact a lawyer? When a suspect is let go, he may assume that the police were satisfied. In any event, it is not apparent why interim advice matters.[Footnote 11] In Minnick v. Mississippi, 498 U. S. 146, 153 (1990), we held that it is not sufficient that a detainee happened to speak at some point with a lawyer. See ibid. (noting that \xe2\x80\x9cconsultation with an attorney\xe2\x80\x9d does not prevent \xe2\x80\x9cpersistent attempts by officials to persuade [a suspect] to waive his rights\xe2\x80\x9d or shield against the \xe2\x80\x9ccoercive pressures that accompany custody\xe2\x80\x9d). If the actual interim advice of an attorney is not sufficient, the hypothetical, interim advice of \xe2\x80\x9can attorney, family members, and friends,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 8, is not enough.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The many problems with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s new rule are exacerbated in the very situation in this case: a suspect who is in prison. Even if, as the Court assumes, a trip to one\xe2\x80\x99s home significantly changes the Edwards calculus, a trip to one\xe2\x80\x99s prison cell is not the same. A prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s freedom is severely limited, and his entire life remains subject to government control. Such an environment is not conducive to \xe2\x80\x9cshak[ing] off any residual coercive effects of his prior custody.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11.[Footnote 12] Nor can a prisoner easily \xe2\x80\x9cseek advice from an attorney, family members, and friends,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 8, especially not within 14 days; prisoners are frequently subject to restrictions on communications. Nor, in most cases, can he live comfortably knowing that he cannot be badgered by police; prison is not like a normal situation in which a suspect \xe2\x80\x9cis in control, and need only shut his door or walk away to avoid police badgering.\xe2\x80\x9d Montejo v. Louisiana, 556 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (slip op., at 16). Indeed, for a person whose every move is controlled by the State, it is likely that \xe2\x80\x9chis sense of dependence on, and trust in, counsel as the guardian of his interests in dealing with government officials intensified.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Green, 592 A.\xc2\xa02d 985, 989 (D.\xc2\xa0C. 1991); cf. Minnick, 498 U. S., at 153 (explaining that coercive pressures \xe2\x80\x9cmay increase as custody is prolonged\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 13] The Court ignores these realities of prison, and instead rests its argument on the supposition that a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cdetention \xe2\x80\xa6 is relatively disconnected from their prior unwillingness to cooperate in an investigation.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14. But that is not necessarily the case. Prisoners are uniquely vulnerable to the officials who control every aspect of their lives; prison guards may not look kindly upon a prisoner who refuses to cooperate with police. And cooperation frequently is relevant to whether the prisoner can obtain parole. See, e.g., Code of Md. Regs., tit. 12, \xc2\xa708.01.18(A)(3) (2008). Moreover, even if it is true as a factual matter that a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s fate is not controlled by the police who come to interrogate him, how is the prisoner supposed to know that? As the Court itself admits, compulsion is likely when a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccaptors appear to control [his] fate,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 7 (internal quotation marks omitted). But when a guard informs a suspect that he must go speak with police, it will \xe2\x80\x9cappear\xe2\x80\x9d to the prisoner that the guard and police are not independent. \xe2\x80\x9cQuestioning by captors, who appear to control the suspect\xe2\x80\x99s fate, may create mutually reinforcing pressures that the Court has assumed will weaken the suspect\xe2\x80\x99s will.\xe2\x80\x9d Illinois v. Perkins, 496 U. S. 292, 297 (1990) (emphasis added).[Footnote 14]

III

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because, at the very least, we do not know whether Shatzer could obtain a lawyer, and thus would have felt that police had lied about providing one, I cannot join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion. I concur in today\xe2\x80\x99s judgment, however, on another ground: Even if Shatzer could not consult a lawyer and the police never provided him one, the 2-year break in custody is a basis for treating the second interrogation as no more coercive than the first. Neither a break in custody nor the passage of time has an inherent, curative power. But certain things change over time. An indigent suspect who took police at their word that they would provide an attorney probably will feel that he has \xe2\x80\x9cbeen denied the counsel he has clearly requested,\xe2\x80\x9d Roberson, 486 U. S., at 686, when police begin to question him, without a lawyer, only 14 days later.[Footnote 15] But, when a suspect has been left alone for a significant period of time, he is not as likely to draw such conclusions when the police interrogate him again.[Footnote 16] It is concededly \xe2\x80\x9cimpossible to determine with precision\xe2\x80\x9d where to draw such a line. Barker v. Wingo, 407 U. S. 514, 521 (1972). In the case before us, however, the suspect was returned to the general prison population for 2 years. I am convinced that this period of time is sufficient. I therefore concur in the judgment.
