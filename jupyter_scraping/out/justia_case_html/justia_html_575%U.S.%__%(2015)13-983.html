SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

Syllabus

\n

elonis v. united states

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the third circuit

\n

No. 13\xe2\x80\x93983.\xe2\x80\x83Argued December 1, 2014\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 1, 2015

\n

After his wife left him, petitioner Anthony Douglas Elonis, under the pseudonym \xe2\x80\x9cTone Dougie,\xe2\x80\x9d used the social networking Web siteFacebook to post self-styled rap lyrics containing graphically violent language and imagery concerning his wife, co-workers, a kindergarten class, and state and federal law enforcement. These posts were often interspersed with disclaimers that the lyrics were \xe2\x80\x9cfictitious\xe2\x80\x9d and not intended to depict real persons, and with statements that Elonis was exercising his\n \nFirst Amendment rights. Many who knew him saw his posts as threatening, however, including his boss, who fired him for threatening co-workers, and his wife, who sought and was granted a state court protection-from-abuse order against him.

\n

When Elonis\xe2\x80\x99s former employer informed the Federal Bureau of Investigation of the posts, the agency began monitoring Elonis\xe2\x80\x99s Face-book activity and eventually arrested him. He was charged with five counts of violating\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7875(c), which makes it a federal crime to transmit in interstate commerce \xe2\x80\x9cany communication containing any threat .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to injure the person of another.\xe2\x80\x9d At trial, Elonis requested a jury instruction that the Government was required to prove that he intended to communicate a \xe2\x80\x9ctrue threat.\xe2\x80\x9d Instead, the District Court told the jury that Elonis could be found guilty if a reasonable person would foresee that his statements would be interpreted as a threat. Elonis was convicted on four of the five counts and renewed his jury instruction challenge on appeal. The Third Circuit affirmed, holding that Section 875(c) requires only the intent to communicate words that the defendant understands, and that a reasonable person would view as a threat.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The Third Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s instruction, requiring only negligence with respect to the communication of a threat, is not sufficient to support a conviction under Section 875(c). Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0Section 875(c) does not indicate whether the defendant must intend that the communication contain a threat, and the parties can show no indication of a particular mental state requirement in the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text. Elonis claims that the word \xe2\x80\x9cthreat,\xe2\x80\x9d by definition, conveys the intent to inflict harm. But common definitions of \xe2\x80\x9cthreat\xe2\x80\x9d speak to what the statement conveys\xe2\x80\x94not to the author\xe2\x80\x99s mental state. The Government argues that the express \xe2\x80\x9cintent to extort\xe2\x80\x9d requirements in neighboring Sections 875(b) and (d) should preclude courts from implying an unexpressed \xe2\x80\x9cintent to threaten\xe2\x80\x9d requirement in Section 875(c). The most that can be concluded from such a comparison, however, is that Congress did not mean to confine Section 875(c) to crimes of extortion, not that it meant to exclude a mental state requirement. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0The Court does not regard \xe2\x80\x9cmere omission from a criminal enactment of any mention of criminal intent\xe2\x80\x9d as dispensing with such a requirement. Morissette v. United States,\n\n342 U.\xc2\xa0S. 246\n. This rule of construction reflects the basic principle that \xe2\x80\x9cwrongdoing must be conscious to be criminal,\xe2\x80\x9d and that a defendant must be \xe2\x80\x9cblameworthy in mind\xe2\x80\x9d before he can be found guilty. Id., at 252. The \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral rule\xe2\x80\x9d is that a guilty mind is \xe2\x80\x9ca necessary element in the indictment and proof of every crime.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Balint,\n\n258 U.\xc2\xa0S. 250\n. Thus, criminal statutes are generally interpreted \xe2\x80\x9cto include broadly applicable scienter requirements, even where the statute .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0does not contain them.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. X-Citement Video, Inc.,\n\n513 U.\xc2\xa0S. 64\n. This does not mean that a defendant must know that his conduct is illegal, but a defendant must have knowledge of \xe2\x80\x9cthe facts that make his conduct fit the definition of the offense.\xe2\x80\x9d Staples v. United States,\n\n511 U.\xc2\xa0S. 600\n, n.\xc2\xa03. Federal criminal statutes that are silent on the required mental state should be read to include \xe2\x80\x9conly that mens rea which is necessary to separate\xe2\x80\x9d wrongful from innocent conduct. Carter v. United States,\n\n530 U.\xc2\xa0S. 255\n. In some cases, a general requirement that a defendant act knowingly is sufficient, but where such a requirement \xe2\x80\x9cwould fail to protect the innocent actor,\xe2\x80\x9d the statute \xe2\x80\x9cwould need to be read to require .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. specific intent.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0The \xe2\x80\x9cpresumption in favor of a scienter requirement should apply to each of the statutory elements that criminalize otherwise innocent conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d X-Citement Video, 513 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 72. In the context of Section 875(c), that requires proof that a communication was transmitted and that it contained a threat. And because \xe2\x80\x9cthe crucial element separating legal innocence from wrongful conduct,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 73, is the threatening nature of the communication, the mental state requirement must apply to the fact that the communication contains a threat. Elonis\xe2\x80\x99s conviction was premised solely on how his posts would be viewed by a reasonable person, a standard feature of civil liability in tort law inconsistent with the conventional criminal conduct requirement of \xe2\x80\x9cawareness of some wrongdoing,\xe2\x80\x9d Staples, 511 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 606\xe2\x80\x93607. This Court \xe2\x80\x9cha[s] long been reluctant to infer that a negligence standard was intended in criminal statutes.\xe2\x80\x9d Rogers v. United States,\n\n422 U.\xc2\xa0S. 35\n (Marshall, J., concurring). And the Government fails to show that the instructions in this case required more than a mental state of negligence. Hamling v. United States,\n\n418 U.\xc2\xa0S. 87\n, distinguished. Section 875(c)\xe2\x80\x99s mental state requirement is satisfied if the defendant transmits a communication for the purpose of issuing a threat or with knowledge that the communication will be viewed as a threat. The Court declines to address whether a mental state of recklessness would also suffice. Given the disposition here, it is unnecessary to consider any\n \nFirst Amendment issues. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

730 F.\xc2\xa03d. 321, reversed and remanded.

Federal law makes it a crime to transmit in interstate commerce \xe2\x80\x9cany communication containing any threat .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to injure the person of another.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7875(c). B

\n

A grand jury indicted Elonis for making threats to injure patrons and employees of the park, his estranged wife, police officers, a kindergarten class, and an FBI agent, all in violation of\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7875(c). App. 14\xe2\x80\x9317. In the District Court, Elonis moved to dismiss the indictment for failing to allege that he had intended to threaten anyone. The District Court denied the motion, holding that Third Circuit precedent required only that Elonis \xe2\x80\x9cintentionally made the communication, not that he intended to make a threat.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 51a. At trial, Elonis testified that his posts emulated the rap lyrics of the well-known performer Eminem, some of which involve fantasies about killing his ex-wife. App. 225. In Elonis\xe2\x80\x99s view, he had posted \xe2\x80\x9cnothing .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that hasn\xe2\x80\x99t been said already.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 205. The Government presented as witnesses Elonis\xe2\x80\x99s wife and co-workers, all of whom said they felt afraid and viewed Elonis\xe2\x80\x99s posts as serious threats. See, e.g., id., at 153, 158.

Elonis requested a jury instruction that \xe2\x80\x9cthe government must prove that he intended to communicate a true threat.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 21. See also id., at 267\xe2\x80\x93269, 303. The District Court denied that request. The jury instructions instead informed the jury that

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cA statement is a true threat when a defendant intentionally makes a statement in a context or under such circumstances wherein a reasonable person would foresee that the statement would be interpreted by those to whom the maker communicates the statement as a serious expression of an intention to inflict bodily injury or take the life of an individual.\xe2\x80\x9d Id.,at 301.

The Government\xe2\x80\x99s closing argument emphasized that it was irrelevant whether Elonis intended the postings to be threats\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cit doesn\xe2\x80\x99t matter what he thinks.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 286. A jury convicted Elonis on four of the five counts against him, acquitting only on the charge of threatening park patrons and employees. Id., at 309. Elonis was sentenced to three years, eight months\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment and three years\xe2\x80\x99 supervised release.

\n

Elonis renewed his challenge to the jury instructions in the Court of Appeals, contending that the jury should have been required to find that he intended his posts to be threats. The Court of Appeals disagreed, holding that the intent required by Section 875(c) is only the intent to communicate words that the defendant understands, and that a reasonable person would view as a threat. 730 F.\xc2\xa03d 321, 332 (CA3 2013).

\n

We granted certiorari. 573 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014).

\n

II

\n

A

\n

An individual who \xe2\x80\x9ctransmits in interstate or foreign commerce any communication containing any threat to kidnap any person or any threat to injure the person of another\xe2\x80\x9d is guilty of a felony and faces up to five years\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment.\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7875(c). This statute requires that a communication be transmitted and that the communication contain a threat. It does not specify that the defendant must have any mental state with respect to these elements. In particular, it does not indicate whether the defendant must intend that his communication contain a threat.

\n

Elonis argues that the word \xe2\x80\x9cthreat\xe2\x80\x9d itself in Section 875(c) imposes such a requirement. According to Elonis, every definition of \xe2\x80\x9cthreat\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cthreaten\xe2\x80\x9d conveys the notion of an intent to inflict harm. Brief for Petitioner 23. See United States v. Jeffries, 692 F.\xc2\xa03d 473, 483 (CA6 2012) (Sutton, J., dubitante). E.g., 11 Oxford English Dictionary 353 (1933) (\xe2\x80\x9cto declare (usually conditionally) one\xe2\x80\x99s intention of inflicting injury upon\xe2\x80\x9d); Webster\xe2\x80\x99s New International Dictionary 2633 (2d ed. 1954) (\xe2\x80\x9cLaw, specif., an expression of an intention to inflict loss or harm on another by illegal means\xe2\x80\x9d); Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 1519 (8th ed. 2004) (\xe2\x80\x9cA communicated intent to inflict harm or loss on another\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

These definitions, however, speak to what the statement conveys\xe2\x80\x94not to the mental state of the author. For example, an anonymous letter that says \xe2\x80\x9cI\xe2\x80\x99m going to kill you\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9can expression of an intention to inflict loss or harm\xe2\x80\x9d regardless of the author\xe2\x80\x99s intent. A victim who receives that letter in the mail has received a threat, even if the author believes (wrongly) that his message will be taken as a joke.

\n

For its part, the Government argues that Section 875(c) should be read in light of its neighboring provisions, Sections 875(b) and 875(d). Those provisions also prohibit certain types of threats, but expressly include a mental state requirement of an \xe2\x80\x9cintent to extort.\xe2\x80\x9d See\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7875(b) (proscribing threats to injure or kidnap made \xe2\x80\x9cwith intent to extort\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa7875(d) (proscribing threats to property or reputation made \xe2\x80\x9cwith intent to extort\xe2\x80\x9d). According to the Government, the express \xe2\x80\x9cintent to extort\xe2\x80\x9d requirements in Sections 875(b) and (d) should preclude courts from implying an unexpressed \xe2\x80\x9cintent to threaten\xe2\x80\x9d requirement in Section 875(c). See Russello v. United States,\n\n464 U.\xc2\xa0S. 16,\n23 (1983)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]here Congress includes particular language in one section of a statute but omits it in another section of the same Act, it is generally presumed that Congress acts intentionally and purposely in the disparate inclusion or exclusion.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

The Government takes this expressio unius est exclusio alterius canon too far. The fact that Congress excluded the requirement of an \xe2\x80\x9cintent to extort\xe2\x80\x9d from Section 875(c) is strong evidence that Congress did not mean to confine Section 875(c) to crimes of extortion. But that does not suggest that Congress, at the same time, also meant to exclude a requirement that a defendant act with a certain mental state in communicating a threat. The most we can conclude from the language of Section 875(c) and its neighboring provisions is that Congress meant to proscribe a broad class of threats in Section 875(c), but did not iden-tify what mental state, if any, a defendant must have to be convicted.

\n

In sum, neither Elonis nor the Government has identified any indication of a particular mental state requirement in the text of Section 875(c).

\n

B

\n

The fact that the statute does not specify any required mental state, however, does not mean that none exists. We have repeatedly held that \xe2\x80\x9cmere omission from a criminal enactment of any mention of criminal intent\xe2\x80\x9d should not be read \xe2\x80\x9cas dispensing with it.\xe2\x80\x9d Morissette v. United States,\n\n342 U.\xc2\xa0S. 246,\n250 (1952)\n. This rule of construction reflects the basic principle that \xe2\x80\x9cwrongdoing must be conscious to be criminal.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 252. As Justice Jackson explained, this principle is \xe2\x80\x9cas universal and persistent in mature systems of law as belief in freedom of the human will and a consequent ability and duty of the normal individual to choose between good and evil.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 250. The \xe2\x80\x9ccentral thought\xe2\x80\x9d is that a defendant must be \xe2\x80\x9cblameworthy in mind\xe2\x80\x9d before he can be found guilty, a concept courts have expressed over time through various terms such as mens rea, scienter, malice aforethought, guilty knowledge, and the like. Id., at 252; 1 W. LaFave, Substantive Criminal Law \xc2\xa75.1, pp. 332\xe2\x80\x93333 (2d ed. 2003). Although there are exceptions, the \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral rule\xe2\x80\x9d is that a guilty mind is \xe2\x80\x9ca necessary element in the indictment and proof of every crime.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Balint,\n\n258 U.\xc2\xa0S. 250,\n251 (1922)\n. We therefore generally \xe2\x80\x9cinterpret[\xc2\xa0] criminal statutes to include broadly applicable scienter requirements, even where the statute by its terms does not contain them.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. X-Citement Video, Inc.,\n\n513 U.\xc2\xa0S. 64,\n70 (1994)\n.

\n

This is not to say that a defendant must know that his conduct is illegal before he may be found guilty. The familiar maxim \xe2\x80\x9cignorance of the law is no excuse\xe2\x80\x9d typi-cally holds true. Instead, our cases have explained that a defendant generally must \xe2\x80\x9cknow the facts that make his conduct fit the definition of the offense,\xe2\x80\x9d Staples v. United States,\n\n511 U.\xc2\xa0S. 600\n, n.\xc2\xa03 (1994), even if he does not know that those facts give rise to a crime.

\n

Morissette, for example, involved an individual who had taken spent shell casings from a Government bombing range, believing them to have been abandoned. Dur-ing his trial for \xe2\x80\x9cknowingly convert[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d property of the United States, the judge instructed the jury that the only question was whether the defendant had knowingly taken the property without authorization. 342 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 248\xe2\x80\x93249. This Court reversed the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conviction, ruling that he had to know not only that he was taking the casings, but also that someone else still had property rights in them. He could not be found liable \xe2\x80\x9cif he truly believed [the casings] to be abandoned.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 271; see id., at 276.

\n

By the same token, in Liparota v. United States, we considered a statute making it a crime to knowingly possess or use food stamps in an unauthorized manner.\n\n471 U.\xc2\xa0S. 419,\n420 (1985)\n. The Government\xe2\x80\x99s argument, similar to its position in this case, was that a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conviction could be upheld if he knowingly possessed or used the food stamps, and in fact his possession or use was unauthorized. Id., at 423. But this Court rejected that interpretation of the statute, because it would have criminalized \xe2\x80\x9ca broad range of apparently innocent conduct\xe2\x80\x9d and swept in individuals who had no knowledge of the facts that made their conduct blameworthy. Id., at 426. For example, the statute made it illegal to use food stamps at a store that charged higher prices to food stamp customers. Without a mental state requirement in the statute, an individual who unwittingly paid higher prices would be guilty under the Government\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation. Ibid. The Court noted that Congress could have intended to cover such a \xe2\x80\x9cbroad range of conduct,\xe2\x80\x9d but declined \xe2\x80\x9cto adopt such a sweeping interpretation\xe2\x80\x9d in the absence of a clear indication that Congress intended that result. Id., at 427. The Court instead construed the statute to require knowledge of the facts that made the use of the food stamps unauthorized. Id., at 425.

\n

To take another example, in Posters \xe2\x80\x98N\xe2\x80\x99 Things, Ltd. v. United States, this Court interpreted a federal statute prohibiting the sale of drug paraphernalia.\n\n511 U.\xc2\xa0S. 513 (1994)\n. Whether the items in question qualified as drug paraphernalia was an objective question that did not depend on the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s state of mind. Id., at 517\xe2\x80\x93522. But, we held, an individual could not be convicted of selling such paraphernalia unless he \xe2\x80\x9cknew that the items at issue [were] likely to be used with illegal drugs.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 524. Such a showing was necessary to establish the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s culpable state of mind.

\n

And again, in X-Citement Video, we considered a statute criminalizing the distribution of visual depictions of minors engaged in sexually explicit conduct. 513 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 68. We rejected a reading of the statute which would have required only that a defendant knowingly send the prohibited materials, regardless of whether he knew the age of the performers. Id., at 68\xe2\x80\x9369. We held instead that a defendant must also know that those depicted were minors, because that was \xe2\x80\x9cthe crucial element separating legal innocence from wrongful conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 73. See also Staples, 511 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 619 (defendant must know that his weapon had automatic firing capability to be convicted of possession of such a weapon).

\n

When interpreting federal criminal statutes that are silent on the required mental state, we read into the statute \xe2\x80\x9conly that mens rea which is necessary to separate wrongful conduct from \xe2\x80\x98otherwise innocent conduct.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Carter v. United States,\n\n530 U.\xc2\xa0S. 255,\n269 (2000)\n (quoting X-Citement Video, 513 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 72). In some cases, a general requirement that a defendant act knowingly is itself an adequate safeguard. For example, in Carter, we considered whether a conviction under\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72113(a), for taking \xe2\x80\x9cby force and violence\xe2\x80\x9d items of value belonging to or in the care of a bank, requires that a defendant have the intent to steal. 530 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 261. We held that once the Government proves the defendant forcibly took the money, \xe2\x80\x9cthe concerns underlying the presumption in favor of scienter are fully satisfied, for a forceful taking\xe2\x80\x94even by a defendant who takes under a good-faith claim of right\xe2\x80\x94falls outside the realm of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. \xe2\x80\x98otherwise innocent\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d conduct. Id., at 269\xe2\x80\x93270. In other instances, however, requiring only that the defendant act knowingly \xe2\x80\x9cwould fail to protect the innocent actor.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 269. A statute similar to Section 2113(a) that did not require a forcible taking or the intent to steal \xe2\x80\x9cwould run the risk of punishing seemingly innocent conduct in the case of a defendant who peaceably takes money believing it to be his.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. In such a case, the Court explained, the statute \xe2\x80\x9cwould need to be read to require\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0that the defendant take the money with \xe2\x80\x98intent to steal or purloin.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

C

\n

Section 875(c), as noted, requires proof that a communication was transmitted and that it contained a threat. The \xe2\x80\x9cpresumption in favor of a scienter requirement should apply to each of the statutory elements that criminalize otherwise innocent conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d X-Citement Video, 513 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 72 (emphasis added). The parties agree that a defendant under Section 875(c) must know that he is transmitting a communication. But communicating something is not what makes the conduct \xe2\x80\x9cwrongful.\xe2\x80\x9d Here \xe2\x80\x9cthe crucial element separating legal innocence from wrongful conduct\xe2\x80\x9d is the threatening nature of the communication. Id., at 73. The mental state requirement must therefore apply to the fact that the communication contains a threat.

\n

Elonis\xe2\x80\x99s conviction, however, was premised solely on how his posts would be understood by a reasonable person. Such a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable person\xe2\x80\x9d standard is a familiar feature of civil liability in tort law, but is inconsistent with \xe2\x80\x9cthe conventional requirement for criminal conduct\xe2\x80\x94awareness of some wrongdoing.\xe2\x80\x9d Staples, 511 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 606\xe2\x80\x93607 (quoting United States v. Dotterweich,\n\n320 U.\xc2\xa0S. 277,\n281 (1943)\n; emphasis added). Having liability turn on whether a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable person\xe2\x80\x9d regards the communication as a threat\xe2\x80\x94regardless of what the defendant thinks\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9creduces culpability on the all-important element of the crime to negligence,\xe2\x80\x9d Jeffries, 692 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 484 (Sutton, J., dubitante), and we \xe2\x80\x9chave long been reluctant to infer that a negligence standard was intended in criminal statutes,\xe2\x80\x9d Rogers v. United States,\n\n422 U.\xc2\xa0S. 35,\n47 (1975)\n (Marshall, J., concurring) (citing Morissette,\n\n342 U.\xc2\xa0S. 246\n). See 1 C. Torcia, Wharton\xe2\x80\x99s Criminal Law \xc2\xa727, pp.\xc2\xa0171\xe2\x80\x93172 (15th ed. 1993); Cochran v. United States,\n\n157 U.\xc2\xa0S. 286,\n294 (1895)\n (defendant could face \xe2\x80\x9cliability in a civil action for negligence, but he could only be held criminally for an evil intent actually existing in his mind\xe2\x80\x9d). Under these principles, \xe2\x80\x9cwhat [Elonis] thinks\xe2\x80\x9d does matter. App. 286.

\n

The Government is at pains to characterize its position as something other than a negligence standard, emphasizing that its approach would require proof that a defendant \xe2\x80\x9ccomprehended [the] contents and context\xe2\x80\x9d of the communication. Brief for United States 29. The Government gives two examples of individuals who, in its view, would lack this necessary mental state\xe2\x80\x94a \xe2\x80\x9cforeigner, ignorant of the English language,\xe2\x80\x9d who would not know the meaning of the words at issue, or an individual mailing a sealed envelope without knowing its contents. Ibid. But the fact that the Government would require a defendant to actu-ally know the words of and circumstances surrounding a communication does not amount to a rejection of negligence. Criminal negligence standards often incorporate \xe2\x80\x9cthe circumstances known\xe2\x80\x9d to a defendant. ALI, Model Penal Code \xc2\xa72.02(2)(d) (1985). See id., Comment 4, at 241; 1 LaFave, Substantive Criminal Law \xc2\xa75.4, at 372\xe2\x80\x93373. Courts then ask, however, whether a reasonable person equipped with that knowledge, not the actual defendant, would have recognized the harmfulness of his conduct. That is precisely the Government\xe2\x80\x99s position here: Elonis can be convicted, the Government contends, if he himself knew the contents and context of his posts, and a reason-able person would have recognized that the posts would be read as genuine threats. That is a negligence standard.

\n

In support of its position the Government relies most heavily on Hamling v. United States,\n\n418 U.\xc2\xa0S. 87 (1974)\n. In that case, the Court rejected the argument that individuals could be convicted of mailing obscene material only if they knew the \xe2\x80\x9clegal status of the materials\xe2\x80\x9d distributed. Id., at 121. Absolving a defendant of liability because he lacked the knowledge that the materials were legally obscene \xe2\x80\x9cwould permit the defendant to avoid prosecution by simply claiming that he had not brushed up on the law.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 123. It was instead enough for liability that \xe2\x80\x9ca defendant had knowledge of the contents of the materials he distributed, and that he knew the character and nature of the materials.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

This holding does not help the Government. In fact, the Court in Hamling approved a state court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that requiring a defendant to know the character of the material incorporated a \xe2\x80\x9cvital element of scienter\xe2\x80\x9d so that \xe2\x80\x9cnot innocent but calculated purveyance of filth .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is exorcised.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 122 (quoting Mishkin v. New York,\n\n383 U.\xc2\xa0S. 502,\n510 (1966)\n; internal quotation marks omitted). In this case, \xe2\x80\x9ccalculated purveyance\xe2\x80\x9d of a threat would require that Elonis know the threatening nature of his communication. Put simply, the mental state requirement the Court approved in Hamling turns on whether a defendant knew the character of what was sent, not simply its contents and context.

\n

Contrary to the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion, see post, at 4\xe2\x80\x935, 9\xe2\x80\x9310 (opinion of Thomas,\xc2\xa0J.), nothing in Rosen v. United States,\n\n161 U.\xc2\xa0S. 29 (1896)\n, undermines this reading. The defendant\xe2\x80\x99s contention in Rosen was that his indictment for mailing obscene material was invalid because it did not allege that he was aware of the contents of the mailing. Id., at 31\xe2\x80\x9333. That is not at issue here; there is no dispute that Elonis knew the words he communicated. The defendant also argued that he could not be convicted of mailing obscene material if he did not know that the material \xe2\x80\x9ccould be properly or justly characterized as obscene.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 41. The Court correctly rejected this \xe2\x80\x9cignorance of the law\xe2\x80\x9d defense; no such contention is at issue here. See supra, at 10.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

In light of the foregoing, Elonis\xe2\x80\x99s conviction cannot stand. The jury was instructed that the Government need prove only that a reasonable person would regard Elonis\xe2\x80\x99s communications as threats, and that was error. Federal criminal liability generally does not turn solely on the results of an act without considering the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s mental state. That understanding \xe2\x80\x9ctook deep and early root in American soil\xe2\x80\x9d and Congress left it intact here: Under Section 875(c), \xe2\x80\x9cwrongdoing must be conscious to be criminal.\xe2\x80\x9d Morissette, 342 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 252.

\n

There is no dispute that the mental state requirement in Section 875(c) is satisfied if the defendant transmits a communication for the purpose of issuing a threat, or with knowledge that the communication will be viewed as a threat. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 25, 56. In response to a question at oral argument, Elonis stated that a finding of recklessness would not be sufficient. See id., at 8\xe2\x80\x939. Neither Elonis nor the Government has briefed or argued that point, and we accordingly decline to address it. See Department of Treasury, IRS v. FLRA,\n\n494 U.\xc2\xa0S. 922,\n933 (1990)\n (this Court is \xe2\x80\x9cpoorly situated\xe2\x80\x9d to address an argument the Court of Appeals did not consider, the parties did not brief, and counsel addressed in \xe2\x80\x9conly the most cursory fashion at oral argument\xe2\x80\x9d). Given our disposition, it is not necessary to consider any\n \nFirst Amendment issues.

\n

Both Justice Alito and Justice Thomas complain about our not deciding whether recklessness suffices for liability under Section 875(c). Post, at 1\xe2\x80\x932 (Alito, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part); post, at 1\xe2\x80\x932 (opinion of Thomas, J.). Justice Alito contends that each party \xe2\x80\x9cargued\xe2\x80\x9d this issue, post, at 2, but they did not address it at all until oral argument, and even then only briefly. See Tr. of Oral Arg. at 8, 38\xe2\x80\x9339.

\n

Justice Alito also suggests that we have not clarified confusion in the lower courts. That is wrong. Our holding makes clear that negligence is not sufficient to support a conviction under Section 875(c), contrary to the view of nine Courts of Appeals. Pet. for Cert. 17. There was and is no circuit conflict over the question Justice Alito and Justice Thomas would have us decide\xe2\x80\x94whether recklessness suffices for liability under Section 875(c). No Court of Appeals has even addressed that question. We think that is more than sufficient \xe2\x80\x9cjustification,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 2 (opinion of Alito, J.), for us to decline to be the first appellate tribunal to do so.

\n

Such prudence is nothing new. See United States v. Bailey,\n\n444 U.\xc2\xa0S. 394,\n407 (1980)\n (declining to decide whether mental state of recklessness or negligence could suffice for criminal liability under\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7751, even though a \xe2\x80\x9ccourt may someday confront a case\xe2\x80\x9d presenting issue); Ginsberg v. New York,\n\n390 U.\xc2\xa0S. 629\n\xe2\x80\x93645 (1968) (rejecting defendant\xe2\x80\x99s challenge to obscenity law \xe2\x80\x9cmakes it unnecessary for us to define further today \xe2\x80\x98what sort of mental element is requisite to a constitutionally permissible prosecution\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d); Smith v. California,\n\n361 U.\xc2\xa0S. 147,\n154 (1959)\n (overturning conviction because lower court did not require any mental element under statute, but noting that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]e need not and most definitely do not pass today on what sort of mental element is requisite to a constitutionally permissible prosecution\xe2\x80\x9d); cf. Gulf Oil Co. v. Bernard,\n\n452 U.\xc2\xa0S. 89\n\xe2\x80\x93104 (1981) (finding a lower court\xe2\x80\x99s order impermissible under the\n \nFirst Amendment but not deciding \xe2\x80\x9cwhat standards are mandated by the\n \nFirst Amendment in this kind of case\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

We may be \xe2\x80\x9ccapable of deciding the recklessness issue,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 2 (opinion of Alito, J.), but following our usual practice of awaiting a decision below and hearing from the parties would help ensure that we decide it correctly.

\n

The judgment of the United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

ANTHONY DOUGLAS ELONIS, PETITIONER v. UNITED STATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the third circuit

\n

[June 1, 2015]

\n

\n

Justice Alito, concurring in part and dissenting in part.

\n

In Marbury v. Madison, 1 Cranch 137, 177 (1803), the Court famously proclaimed: \xe2\x80\x9cIt is emphatically the province and duty of the judicial department to say what the law is.\xe2\x80\x9d Today, the Court announces: It is emphatically the prerogative of this Court to say only what the law is not.

\n

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s disposition of this case is certain to cause confusion and serious problems. Attorneys and judges need to know which mental state is required for conviction under\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7875(c), an important criminal statute. This case squarely presents that issue, but the Court provides only a partial answer. The Court holds that the jury instructions in this case were defective because they required only negligence in conveying a threat. But the Court refuses to explain what type of intent was necessary. Did the jury need to find that Elonis had the purpose of conveying a true threat? Was it enough if he knew that his words conveyed such a threat? Would recklessness suffice? The Court declines to say. Attorneys and judges are left to guess.

\n

This will have regrettable consequences. While this Court has the luxury of choosing its docket, lower courts and juries are not so fortunate. They must actually decide cases, and this means applying a standard. If purpose or knowledge is needed and a district court instructs the jury that recklessness suffices, a defendant may be wrongly convicted. On the other hand, if recklessness is enough, and the jury is told that conviction requires proof of more, a guilty defendant may go free. We granted review in this case to resolve a disagreement among the Circuits. But the Court has compounded\xe2\x80\x94not clarified\xe2\x80\x94the confusion.

\n

There is no justification for the Court\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to provide an answer. The Court says that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]either Elonis nor the Government has briefed or argued\xe2\x80\x9d the question whether recklessness is sufficient. Ante, at 16. But in fact both parties addressed that issue. Elonis argued that recklessness is not enough, and the Government argued that it more than suffices. If the Court thinks that we cannot decide the recklessness question without additional help from the parties, we can order further briefing and argument. In my view, however, we are capable of deciding the recklessness issue, and we should resolve that question now.

\n

I

\n

Section 875(c) provides in relevant part:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cWhoever transmits in interstate or foreign commerce any communication containing .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any threat to injure the person of another, shall be fined under this title or imprisoned not more than five years, or both.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Thus, conviction under this provision requires proof that: (1)\xc2\xa0the defendant transmitted something, (2) the thing transmitted was a threat to injure the person of another, and (3) the transmission was in interstate or foreigncommerce.

\n

At issue in this case is the mens rea required with respect to the second element\xe2\x80\x94that the thing transmitted was a threat to injure the person of another. This Court has not defined the meaning of the term \xe2\x80\x9cthreat\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa7875(c), but in construing the same term in a related statute, the Court distinguished a \xe2\x80\x9ctrue \xe2\x80\x98threat\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d from facetious or hyperbolic remarks. Watts v. United States,\n\n394 U.\xc2\xa0S. 705,\n708 (1969)\n (per curiam). In my view, the term \xe2\x80\x9cthreat\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa7875(c) can fairly be defined as a statement that is reasonably interpreted as \xe2\x80\x9can expression of an intention to inflict evil, injury, or damage on another.\xe2\x80\x9d Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third New International Dictionary 2382 (1976). Conviction under \xc2\xa7875(c) demands proof that the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s transmission was in fact a threat, i.e., that it is reasonable to interpret the transmission as an expression of an intent to harm another. In addition, it must be shown that the defendant was at least reckless as to whether the transmission met that requirement.

\n

Why is recklessness enough? My analysis of the mens rea issue follows the same track as the Court\xe2\x80\x99s, as far as it goes. I agree with the Court that we should presume that criminal statutes require some sort of mens rea for conviction. See ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9313. To be sure, this presumption marks a departure from the way in which we generally interpret statutes. We \xe2\x80\x9cordinarily resist reading words or elements into a statute that do not appear on its face.\xe2\x80\x9d Bates v. United States,\n\n522 U.\xc2\xa0S. 23,\n29 (1997)\n. But this step is justified by a well-established pattern in our criminal laws. \xe2\x80\x9cFor several centuries (at least since 1600) the different common law crimes have been so defined as to require, for guilt, that the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s acts or omissions be accompanied by one or more of the various types of fault (intention, knowledge, recklessness or\xe2\x80\x94more rarely\xe2\x80\x94negligence).\xe2\x80\x9d 1 W. LaFave, Substantive Criminal Law \xc2\xa75.5, p.\xc2\xa0381 (2003). Based on these \xe2\x80\x9cbackground rules of the common law, in which the requirement of some mens rea for a crime is firmly embedded,\xe2\x80\x9d we require \xe2\x80\x9csome indication of congressional intent, express or implied, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to dispense with mens rea as an element of a crime.\xe2\x80\x9d Staples v. United States,\n\n511 U.\xc2\xa0S. 600\n\xe2\x80\x93606 (1994).

\n

For a similar reason, I agree with the Court that we should presume that an offense like that created by \xc2\xa7875(c) requires more than negligence with respect to a critical element like the one at issue here. See ante, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314. As the Court states, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen interpreting federal criminal statutes that are silent on the required mental state, we read into the statute \xe2\x80\x98only that mens rea which is necessary to separate wrongful conduct from \xe2\x80\x9cotherwise innocent conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12 (quoting Carter v. United States,\n\n530 U.\xc2\xa0S. 255,\n269 (2000)\n). Whether negligence is morally culpable is an interesting philosophical question, but the answer is at least sufficiently debatable to justify the presumption that a serious offense against the person that lacks any clear common-law counterpart should be presumed to require more.

\n

Once we have passed negligence, however, no further presumptions are defensible. In the hierarchy of mental states that may be required as a condition for criminal liability, the mens rea just above negligence is recklessness. Negligence requires only that the defendant \xe2\x80\x9cshould [have] be[en] aware of a substantial and unjustifiable risk,\xe2\x80\x9d ALI, Model Penal Code \xc2\xa72.02(2)(d), p.\xc2\xa0226 (1985), while recklessness exists \xe2\x80\x9cwhen a person disregards a risk of harm of which he is aware,\xe2\x80\x9d Farmer v. Brennan,\n\n511 U.\xc2\xa0S. 825,\n837 (1994)\n; Model Penal Code \xc2\xa72.02(2)(c). And when Congress does not specify a mens rea in a criminal statute, we have no justification for inferring that anything more than recklessness is needed. It is quite un-usual for us to interpret a statute to contain a requirement that is nowhere set out in the text. Once we have reached recklessness, we have gone as far as we can without stepping over the line that separates interpretation from amendment.

\n

There can be no real dispute that recklessness regarding a risk of serious harm is wrongful conduct. In a wide variety of contexts, we have described reckless conduct as morally culpable. See, e.g., Farmer, supra, at 835\xe2\x80\x93836 (deliberate indifference to an inmate\xe2\x80\x99s harm); Garrison v. Louisiana,\n\n379 U.\xc2\xa0S. 64,\n75 (1964)\n (criminal libel); New York Times Co. v. Sullivan,\n\n376 U.\xc2\xa0S. 254\n\xe2\x80\x93280 (1964) (civil libel). Indeed, this Court has held that \xe2\x80\x9creckless disregard for human life\xe2\x80\x9d may justify the death penalty. Tison v. Arizona,\n\n481 U.\xc2\xa0S. 137,\n157 (1987)\n. Someone who acts recklessly with respect to conveying a threat necessarily grasps that he is not engaged in innocent conduct. He is not merely careless. He is aware that others could regard his statements as a threat, but he delivers them anyway.

\n

Accordingly, I would hold that a defendant may be convicted under \xc2\xa7875(c) if he or she consciously disregards the risk that the communication transmitted will be interpreted as a true threat. Nothing in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s non-committal opinion prevents lower courts from adopting that standard.

\n

II

\n

There remains the question whether interpreting \xc2\xa7875(c) to require no more than recklessness with respect to the element at issue here would violate the\n \nFirst Amendment. Elonis contends that it would. I would reject that argument.

\n

It is settled that the Constitution does not protect true threats. See Virginia v. Black,\n\n538 U.\xc2\xa0S. 343\n\xe2\x80\x93360 (2003); R.\xc2\xa0A.\xc2\xa0V. v. St. Paul,\n\n505 U.\xc2\xa0S. 377,\n388 (1992)\n; Watts, 394 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 707\xe2\x80\x93708. And there are good reasons for that rule: True threats inflict great harm and have little if any social value. A threat may cause serious emotional stress for the person threatened and those who care about that person, and a threat may lead to a violent confrontation. It is true that a communication containing a threat may include other statements that have value and are entitled to protection. But that does not justify constitutional protection for the threat itself.

\n

Elonis argues that the\n \nFirst Amendment protects a threat if the person making the statement does not actually intend to cause harm. In his view, if a threat is madefor a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98therapeutic\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d purpose, \xe2\x80\x9cto \xe2\x80\x98deal with the pain\xe2\x80\x99 .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of a wrenching event,\xe2\x80\x9d or for \xe2\x80\x9ccathartic\xe2\x80\x9d reasons, the threat is protected. Brief for Petitioner 52\xe2\x80\x9353. But whether or not the person making a threat intends to cause harm, the damage is the same. And the fact that making a threat may have a therapeutic or cathartic effect for the speaker is not sufficient to justify constitutional protection. Some people may experience a therapeutic or cathartic benefit only if they know that their words will cause harm or only if they actually plan to carry out the threat, but surely the\n \nFirst Amendment does not protect them.

\n

Elonis also claims his threats were constitutionally protected works of art. Words like his, he contends, are shielded by the\n \nFirst Amendment because they are similar to words uttered by rappers and singers in public performances and recordings. To make this point, his brief includes a lengthy excerpt from the lyrics of a rap song in which a very well-compensated rapper imagines killing his ex-wife and dumping her body in a lake. If this celebrity can utter such words, Elonis pleads, amateurs like him should be able to post similar things on social media. But context matters. \xe2\x80\x9cTaken in context,\xe2\x80\x9d lyrics in songs that are performed for an audience or sold in recorded form are unlikely to be interpreted as a real threat to a real person. Watts, supra, at 708. Statements on social media that are pointedly directed at their victims, by contrast, are much more likely to be taken seriously. To hold otherwise would grant a license to anyone who is clever enough to dress up a real threat in the guise of rap lyrics, a parody, or something similar.

\n

The facts of this case illustrate the point. Imagine the effect on Elonis\xe2\x80\x99s estranged wife when she read this: \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98If I only knew then what I know now .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. I would have smothered your ass with a pillow, dumped your body in the back seat, dropped you off in Toad Creek and made it look like a rape and murder.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 730 F.\xc2\xa03d 321, 324 (CA3 2013). Or this: \xe2\x80\x9cThere\xe2\x80\x99s one way to love you but a thousand ways to kill you. I\xe2\x80\x99m not going to rest until your body is a mess, soaked in blood and dying from all the little cuts.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Or this: \xe2\x80\x9cFold up your [protection from abuse order] and put it in your pocket[.] Is it thick enough to stop a bullet?\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 325.

\n

There was evidence that Elonis made sure his wife saw his posts. And she testified that they made her feel \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98extremely afraid\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98like [she] was being stalked.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Considering the context, who could blame her? Threats of violence and intimidation are among the most favored weapons of domestic abusers, and the rise of social media has only made those tactics more commonplace. See Brief for The National Network to End Domestic Violence et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 4\xe2\x80\x9316. A fig leaf of artistic expression cannot convert such hurtful, valueless threats into protected speech.

\n

It can be argued that \xc2\xa7875(c), if not limited to threats made with the intent to harm, will chill statements that do not qualify as true threats, e.g., statements that may be literally threatening but are plainly not meant to be taken seriously. We have sometimes cautioned that it is necessary to \xe2\x80\x9cexten[d] a measure of strategic protection\xe2\x80\x9d to otherwise unprotected false statements of fact in order to ensure enough \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98breathing space\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d for protected speech. Gertz v. Robert Welch, Inc.,\n\n418 U.\xc2\xa0S. 323,\n342 (1974)\n (quoting NAACP v. Button,\n\n371 U.\xc2\xa0S. 415,\n433 (1963)\n). A similar argument might be made with respect to threats. But we have also held that the law provides adequate breathing space when it requires proof that false statements were made with reckless disregard of their falsity. See New York Times, 376 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 279\xe2\x80\x93280 (civil liability); Garrison, 379 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 74\xe2\x80\x9375 (criminal liability). Requiring proof of recklessness is similarly sufficient here.

\n

III

\n

Finally, because the jury instructions in this case did not require proof of recklessness, I would vacate the judgment below and remand for the Court of Appeals to decide in the first instance whether Elonis\xe2\x80\x99s conviction could be upheld under a recklessness standard.

\n

We do not lightly overturn criminal convictions, even where it appears that the district court might have erred. To benefit from a favorable ruling on appeal, a defendant must have actually asked for the legal rule the appellate court adopts. Rule 30(d) of the Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure requires a defendant to \xe2\x80\x9cinform the court of the specific objection and the grounds for the objection.\xe2\x80\x9d An objection cannot be vague or open-ended. It must specifically identify the alleged error. And failure to lodge a sufficient objection \xe2\x80\x9cprecludes appellate review,\xe2\x80\x9d except for plain error. Rule 30(d); see also 2A C. Wright & P. Henning, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa7484, pp.\xc2\xa0433\xe2\x80\x93435 (4th ed. 2009).

\n

At trial, Elonis objected to the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s instruction, but he did not argue for recklessness. Instead, he proposed instructions that would have required proof that he acted purposefully or with knowledge that his statements would be received as threats. See App. 19\xe2\x80\x9321. He advanced the same position on appeal and in this Court. See Brief for Petitioner 29 (\xe2\x80\x9cSection 875(c) requires proof that the defendant intended the charged statement to be a \xe2\x80\x98threat\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis in original)); Corrected Brief of Appellant in No. 12\xe2\x80\x933798 (CA3), p.\xc2\xa014 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] \xe2\x80\x98true threat\xe2\x80\x99 has been uttered only if the speaker acted with subjective intent to threaten\xe2\x80\x9d (same)). And at oral argument before this Court, he expressly disclaimed any agreement with a recklessness standard\xe2\x80\x94which the Third Circuit remains free to adopt. Tr. of Oral Arg. 8:22\xe2\x80\x9323 (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e would say that recklessness is not justif[ied]\xe2\x80\x9d). I would therefore remand for the Third Circuit to determine if Elonis\xe2\x80\x99s failure (indeed, refusal) to argue for recklessness prevents reversal of his conviction.

\n

The Third Circuit should also have the opportunity to consider whether the conviction can be upheld on harmless-error grounds. \xe2\x80\x9cWe have often applied harmless-error analysis to cases involving improper instructions.\xe2\x80\x9d Neder v. United States,\n\n527 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1,\n9 (1999)\n; see also, e.g., Pope v. Illinois,\n\n481 U.\xc2\xa0S. 497\n\xe2\x80\x93504 (1987) (remanding for harmless-error analysis after holding that jury instruction misstated obscenity standard). And the Third Circuit has previously upheld convictions where erroneous jury instructions proved harmless. See, e.g., United States v. Saybolt, 577 F.\xc2\xa03d 195, 206\xe2\x80\x93207 (2009). It should be given the chance to address that possibility here.

ANTHONY DOUGLAS ELONIS, PETITIONER v. UNITED STATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the third circuit

\n

[June 1, 2015]

\n

\n

Justice Thomas, dissenting.

\n

We granted certiorari to resolve a conflict in the lower courts over the appropriate mental state for threat prosecutions under\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7875(c). Save two, every Circuit to have considered the issue\xe2\x80\x9411 in total\xe2\x80\x94has held that this provision demands proof only of general intent, which here requires no more than that a defendant knew he transmitted a communication, knew the words used in that communication, and understood the ordinary meaning of those words in the relevant context. The outliers are the Ninth and Tenth Circuits, which have concluded that proof of an intent to threaten was necessary for conviction. Adopting the minority position, Elonis urges us to hold that \xc2\xa7875(c) and the\n \nFirst Amendment require proof of an intent to threaten. The Government in turn advocates a general-intent approach.

\n

Rather than resolve the conflict, the Court casts aside the approach used in nine Circuits and leaves nothing in its place. Lower courts are thus left to guess at the appropriate mental state for \xc2\xa7875(c). All they know after today\xe2\x80\x99s decision is that a requirement of general intent will not do. But they can safely infer that a majority of this Court would not adopt an intent-to-threaten requirement, as the opinion carefully leaves open the possibility that recklessness may be enough. See ante, at 16\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

This failure to decide throws everyone from appellate judges to everyday Facebook users into a state of uncertainty. This uncertainty could have been avoided had we simply adhered to the background rule of the common law favoring general intent. Although I am sympathetic to my colleagues\xe2\x80\x99 policy concerns about the risks associated with threat prosecutions, the answer to such fears is not to discard our traditional approach to state-of-mind requirements in criminal law. Because the Court of Appeals properly applied the general-intent standard, and because the communications transmitted by Elonis were \xe2\x80\x9ctrue threats\xe2\x80\x9d unprotected by the\n \nFirst Amendment, I would affirm the judgment below.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

Enacted in 1939, \xc2\xa7875(c) provides, \xe2\x80\x9cWhoever transmits in interstate or foreign commerce any communication containing any threat to kidnap any person or any threat to injure the person of another, shall be fined under this title or imprisoned not more than five years, or both.\xe2\x80\x9d Because \xc2\xa7875(c) criminalizes speech, the\n \nFirst Amendment requires that the term \xe2\x80\x9cthreat\xe2\x80\x9d be limited to a narrow class of historically unprotected communications called \xe2\x80\x9ctrue threats.\xe2\x80\x9d To qualify as a true threat, a communication must be a serious expression of an intention to commit unlawful physical violence, not merely \xe2\x80\x9cpolitical hyperbole\xe2\x80\x9d; \xe2\x80\x9cvehement, caustic, and sometimes unpleasantly sharp attacks\xe2\x80\x9d; or \xe2\x80\x9cvituperative, abusive, and inexact\xe2\x80\x9d statements. Watts v. United States,\n\n394 U.\xc2\xa0S. 705,\n708 (1969)\n (per curiam) (internal quotation marks omitted). It also cannot be determined solely by the reaction of the recipient, but must instead be \xe2\x80\x9cdetermined by the interpretation of a reasonable recipient familiar with the context of the communication,\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Darby, 37 F.\xc2\xa03d 1059, 1066 (CA4 1994) (emphasis added), lest historically protected speech be suppressed at the will of an eggshell observer, cf. Cox v. Louisiana,\n\n379 U.\xc2\xa0S. 536,\n551 (1965)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[C]onstitutional rights may not be denied simply because of hostility to their assertion or exercise\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). There is thus no dispute that, at a minimum, \xc2\xa7875(c) requires an objective showing: The communication must be one that \xe2\x80\x9ca reasonable observer would construe as a true threat to another.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Jeffries, 692 F.\xc2\xa03d 473, 478 (CA6 2012). And there is no dispute that the posts at issue here meet that objective standard.

\n

The only dispute in this case is about the state of mind necessary to convict Elonis for making those posts. On its face, \xc2\xa7875(c) does not demand any particular mental state. As the Court correctly explains, the word \xe2\x80\x9cthreat\xe2\x80\x9d does not itself contain a mens rea requirement. See ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939. But because we read criminal statutes \xe2\x80\x9cin light of the background rules of the common law, in which the requirement of some mens rea for a crime is firmly embedded,\xe2\x80\x9d we require \xe2\x80\x9csome indication of congressional intent, express or implied, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to dispense with mens rea as an element of a crime.\xe2\x80\x9d Staples v. United States,\n\n511 U.\xc2\xa0S. 600\n\xe2\x80\x93606 (1994) (citation omitted). Absent such indicia, we ordinarily apply the \xe2\x80\x9cpresumption in favor of scienter\xe2\x80\x9d to require only \xe2\x80\x9cproof of general intent\xe2\x80\x94that is, that the defendant [must] posses[s] knowledge with respect to the actus reus of the crime.\xe2\x80\x9d Carter v. United States,\n\n530 U.\xc2\xa0S. 255,\n268 (2000)\n.

\n

Under this \xe2\x80\x9cconventional mens rea element,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cthe defendant [must] know the facts that make his conduct illegal,\xe2\x80\x9d Staples, supra, at 605, but he need not know that those facts make his conduct illegal. It has long been settled that \xe2\x80\x9cthe knowledge requisite to knowing violation of a statute is factual knowledge as distinguished from knowledge of the law.\xe2\x80\x9d Bryan v. United States,\n\n524 U.\xc2\xa0S. 184,\n192 (1998)\n (internal quotation marks omitted). For instance, in Posters \xe2\x80\x98N\xe2\x80\x99 Things, Ltd. v. United States,\n\n511 U.\xc2\xa0S. 513 (1994)\n, the Court addressed a conviction for selling drug paraphernalia under a statute forbidding anyone to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98make use of the services of the Postal Service or other interstate conveyance as part of a scheme to sell drug paraphernalia,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 516 (quoting\n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7857(a)(1) (1988 ed.)). In applying the presumption in favor of scienter, the Court concluded that \xe2\x80\x9calthough the Government must establish that the defendant knew that the items at issue are likely to be used with illegal drugs, it need not prove specific knowledge that the items are \xe2\x80\x98drug paraphernalia\xe2\x80\x99 within the meaning of the statute.\xe2\x80\x9d 511 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 524.

\n

Our default rule in favor of general intent applies with full force to criminal statutes addressing speech. Well over 100 years ago, this Court considered a conviction under a federal obscenity statute that punished anyone \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98who shall knowingly deposit, or cause to be deposited, for mailing or delivery,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d any \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98obscene, lewd, or lascivious book, pamphlet, picture, paper, writing, print, or other publication of an indecent character.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Rosen v. United States,\n\n161 U.\xc2\xa0S. 29,\n30 (1896)\n (quoting Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa73893). In that case, as here, the defendant argued that, even if \xe2\x80\x9che may have had .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. actual knowledge or notice of [the paper\xe2\x80\x99s] contents\xe2\x80\x9d when he put it in the mail, he could not \xe2\x80\x9cbe convicted of the offence .\xc2\xa0. . unless he knew or believed that such paper could be properly or justly characterized as obscene, lewd, and lascivious.\xe2\x80\x9d 161 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 41. The Court rejected that theory, concluding that if the material was actually obscene and \xe2\x80\x9cdeposited in the mail by one who knew or had notice at the time of its contents, the offence is complete, although the defendant himself did not regard the paper as one that the statute forbade to be carried in the mails.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. As the Court explained, \xe2\x80\x9cCongress did not intend that the question as to the character of the paper should depend upon the opinion or belief of the person who, with knowledge or notice of [the paper\xe2\x80\x99s] contents, assumed the responsibility of putting it in the mails of the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9c[e]very one who uses the mails of the United States for carrying papers or publications must take notice of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. what must be deemed obscene, lewd, and lascivious.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 41\xe2\x80\x9342.

\n

This Court reaffirmed Rosen\xe2\x80\x99s holding in Hamling v. United States,\n\n418 U.\xc2\xa0S. 87 (1974)\n, when it considered a challenge to convictions under the successor federal statute, see id., at 98, n.\xc2\xa08 (citing\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71461 (1970 ed.)). Relying on Rosen, the Court rejected the argument that the statute required \xe2\x80\x9cproof both of knowledge of the contents of the material and awareness of the obscene character of the material.\xe2\x80\x9d 418 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 120 (internal quotation marks omitted). In approving the jury instruction that the defendants\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cbelief as to the obscenity or non-obscenity of the material is irrelevant,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court declined to hold \xe2\x80\x9cthat the prosecution must prove a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s knowledge of the legal status of the materials he distributes.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 120\xe2\x80\x93121 (internal quotation marks omitted). To rule otherwise, the Court observed, \xe2\x80\x9cwould permit the defendant to avoid prosecution by simply claiming that he had not brushed up on the law.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 123.

\n

Decades before \xc2\xa7875(c)\xe2\x80\x99s enactment, courts took the same approach to the first federal threat statute, which prohibited threats against the President. In 1917, Congress enacted a law punishing anyone

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cwho knowingly and willfully deposits or causes to be deposited for conveyance in the mail .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any letter, paper, writing, print, missive, or document containing any threat to take the life of or to inflict bodily harm upon the President of the United States, or who knowingly and willfully otherwise makes any such threat against the President.\xe2\x80\x9d Act of Feb. 14, 1917, ch. 64,\n\n39Stat.\n919.

\n

Courts applying this statute shortly after its enactment appeared to require proof of only general intent. In Ragansky v. United States, 253 F. 643 (CA7 1918), for instance, a Court of Appeals held that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] threat is knowingly made, if the maker of it comprehends the meaning of the words uttered by him,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cis willfully made, if in addition to comprehending the meaning of his words, the maker voluntarily and intentionally utters them as the declaration of an apparent determination to carry them into execution,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 645. The court consequently rejected the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s argument that he could not be convicted when his language \xe2\x80\x9c[c]oncededly .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. constituted such a threat\xe2\x80\x9d but was meant only \xe2\x80\x9cas a joke.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 644. Likewise, in United States v. Stobo, 251 F. 689 (Del. 1918), a District Court rejected the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s objection that there was no allegation \xe2\x80\x9cof any facts .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. indicating any intention .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. on the part of the defendant .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to menace the President of the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 693 (internal quotation marks omitted). As it explained, the defendant \xe2\x80\x9cis punishable under the act whether he uses the words lightly or with a set purpose to kill,\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he effect upon the minds of the hearers, who cannot read his inward thoughts, is precisely the same.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. At a minimum, there is no historical practice requiring more than general intent when a statute regulates speech.

\n

B

\n

Applying ordinary rules of statutory construction, I would read \xc2\xa7875(c) to require proof of general intent. To \xe2\x80\x9cknow the facts that make his conduct illegal\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa7875(c), see Staples, 511 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 605, a defendant must know that he transmitted a communication in interstate or foreign commerce that contained a threat. Knowing that the communication contains a \xe2\x80\x9cthreat\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94a serious expression of an intention to engage in unlawful physical violence\xe2\x80\x94does not, however, require knowing that a jury will conclude that the communication contains a threat as a matter of law. Instead, like one who mails an \xe2\x80\x9cobscene\xe2\x80\x9d publication and is prosecuted under the federal obscenity statute, a defendant prosecuted under \xc2\xa7875(c) must know only the words used in that communication, along with their ordinary meaning in context.

\n

General intent divides those who know the facts constituting the actus reus of this crime from those who do not. For example, someone who transmits a threat who does not know English\xe2\x80\x94or who knows English, but perhaps does not know a threatening idiom\xe2\x80\x94lacks the general intent required under \xc2\xa7875(c). See Ragansky, supra, at 645 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] foreigner, ignorant of the English language, repeating [threatening] words without knowledge of their meaning, may not knowingly have made a threat\xe2\x80\x9d). Likewise, the hapless mailman who delivers a threatening letter, ignorant of its contents, should not fear prosecution. A defendant like Elonis, however, who admits that he \xe2\x80\x9cknew that what [he] was saying was violent\xe2\x80\x9d but supposedly \xe2\x80\x9cjust wanted to express [him]self,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 205, acted with the general intent required under \xc2\xa7875(c), even if he did not know that a jury would conclude that his communication constituted a \xe2\x80\x9cthreat\xe2\x80\x9d as a matter of law.

\n

Demanding evidence only of general intent also corresponds to \xc2\xa7875(c)\xe2\x80\x99s statutory backdrop. As previously discussed, before the enactment of \xc2\xa7875(c), courts had read the Presidential threats statute to require proof only of general intent. Given Congress\xe2\x80\x99 presumptive awareness of this application of the Presidential threats statute\xe2\x80\x94not to mention this Court\xe2\x80\x99s similar approach in the obscenity context, see Rosen, 161 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 41\xe2\x80\x9342\xe2\x80\x94it is difficult to conclude that the Congress that enacted \xc2\xa7875(c) in 1939 understood it to contain an implicit mental-state requirement apart from general intent. There is certainly no textual evidence to support this conclusion. If anything, the text supports the opposite inference, as \xc2\xa7875(c), unlike the Presidential threats statute, contains no reference to knowledge or willfulness. Nothing in the statute suggests that Congress departed from the \xe2\x80\x9cconventional mens rea element\xe2\x80\x9d of general intent, Staples, supra, at 605; I would not impose a higher mental-state requirement here.

\n

C

\n

The majority refuses to apply these ordinary background principles. Instead, it casts my application of general intent as a negligence standard disfavored in the criminal law. Ante, at 13\xe2\x80\x9316. But that characterization misses the mark. Requiring general intent in this context is not the same as requiring mere negligence. Like the mental-state requirements adopted in many of the cases cited by the Court, general intent under \xc2\xa7875(c) prevents a defendant from being convicted on the basis of any fact beyond his awareness. See, e.g., United States v. X-Citement Video, Inc.,\n\n513 U.\xc2\xa0S. 64,\n73 (1994)\n (knowledge of age of persons depicted in explicit materials); Staples, supra, at 614\xe2\x80\x93615 (knowledge of firing capability of weapon); Morissette v. United States,\n\n342 U.\xc2\xa0S. 246\n\xe2\x80\x93271 (1952) (knowledge that property belonged to another). In other words, the defendant must know\xe2\x80\x94not merely be reckless or negligent with respect to the fact\xe2\x80\x94that he is committing the acts that constitute the actus reus of the offense.

\n

But general intent requires no mental state (not even a negligent one) concerning the \xe2\x80\x9cfact\xe2\x80\x9d that certain words meet the legal definition of a threat. That approach is particularly appropriate where, as here, that legal status is determined by a jury\xe2\x80\x99s application of the legal standard of a \xe2\x80\x9cthreat\xe2\x80\x9d to the contents of a communication. And convicting a defendant despite his ignorance of the legal\xe2\x80\x94or objective\xe2\x80\x94status of his conduct does not mean that he is being punished for negligent conduct. By way of example, a defendant who is convicted of murder despite claiming that he acted in self-defense has not been penalized under a negligence standard merely because he does not know that the jury will reject his argument that his \xe2\x80\x9cbelief in the necessity of using force to prevent harm to himself [was] a reasonable one.\xe2\x80\x9d See 2 W. LaFave, Substantive Criminal Law \xc2\xa710.4(c), p. 147 (2d ed. 2003).

\n

The Court apparently does not believe that our traditional approach to the federal obscenity statute involved a negligence standard. It asserts that Hamling \xe2\x80\x9capproved a state court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that requiring a defendant to know the character of the material incorporated a \xe2\x80\x98vital element of scienter\xe2\x80\x99 so that \xe2\x80\x98not innocent but calculated purveyance of filth .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is exorcised.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 15 (quoting Hamling, 418 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 122 (in turn quoting Mishkin v. New York,\n\n383 U.\xc2\xa0S. 502,\n510 (1966)\n). According to the Court, the mental state approved in Hamling thus \xe2\x80\x9cturns on whether a defendant knew the character of what was sent, not simply its contents and context.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 15. It is unclear what the Court means by its distinction between \xe2\x80\x9ccharacter\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ccontents and context.\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cCharacter\xe2\x80\x9d cannot mean legal obscenity, as Hamling rejected the argument that a defendant must have \xe2\x80\x9cawareness of the obscene character of the material.\xe2\x80\x9d 418 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 120 (internal quotation marks omitted). Moreover, this discussion was not part of Hamling\xe2\x80\x99s holding, which was primarily a reaffirmation of Rosen. See 418 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 120\xe2\x80\x93121; see also Posters \xe2\x80\x99N\xe2\x80\x99 Things, 511 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 524\xe2\x80\x93525 (characterizing Hamling as holding that a \xe2\x80\x9cstatute prohibiting mailing of obscene materials does not require proof that [the] defendant knew the materials at issue met the legal definition of \xe2\x80\x98obscenity\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

The majority\xe2\x80\x99s treatment of Rosen is even less persuasive. To shore up its position, it asserts that the critical portion of Rosen rejected an \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98ignorance of the law\xe2\x80\x99 defense,\xe2\x80\x9d and claims that \xe2\x80\x9cno such contention is at issue here.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 15. But the thrust of Elonis\xe2\x80\x99 challenge is that a \xc2\xa7875(c) conviction cannot stand if the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s subjective belief of what constitutes a \xe2\x80\x9cthreat\xe2\x80\x9d differs from that of a reasonable jury. That is akin to the argument the defendant made\xe2\x80\x94and lost\xe2\x80\x94in Rosen. That defendant insisted that he could not be convicted for mailing the paper \xe2\x80\x9cunless he knew or believed that such paper could be properly or justly characterized as obscene.\xe2\x80\x9d 161 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 41. The Court, however, held that the Government did not need to show that the defendant \xe2\x80\x9cregard[ed] the paper as one that the statute forbade to be carried in the mails,\xe2\x80\x9d because the obscene character of the material did not \xe2\x80\x9cdepend upon the opinion or belief of the person who .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. assumed the responsibility of putting it in the mails.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The majority\xe2\x80\x99s muddying of the waters cannot obscure the fact that today\xe2\x80\x99s decision is irreconcilable with Rosen and Hamling.

\n

D

\n

The majority today at least refrains from requiring an intent to threaten for \xc2\xa7875(c) convictions, as Elonis asks us to do. Elonis contends that proof of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s intent to put the recipient of a threat in fear is necessary for conviction, but that element cannot be found within the statutory text. \xe2\x80\x9c[W]e ordinarily resist reading words or elements into a statute that do not appear on its face,\xe2\x80\x9d including elements similar to the one Elonis proposes. E.g., Bates v. United States,\n\n522 U.\xc2\xa0S. 23,\n29 (1997)\n (declining to read an \xe2\x80\x9cintent to defraud\xe2\x80\x9d element into a criminal statute). As the majority correctly explains, nothing in the text of \xc2\xa7875(c) itself requires proof of an intent to threaten. See ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939. The absence of such a requirement is significant, as Congress knows how to require a heightened mens rea in the context of threat offenses. See \xc2\xa7875(b) (providing for the punishment of \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hoever, with intent to extort .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. , transmits in interstate or foreign commerce any communication containing any threat to kidnap any person or any threat to injure the person of another\xe2\x80\x9d); see also \xc2\xa7119 (providing for the punishment of \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hoever knowingly makes restricted personal information about [certain officials] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. publicly available .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. with the intent to threaten\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Elonis nonetheless suggests that an intent-to-threaten element is necessary in order to avoid the risk of punishing innocent conduct. But there is nothing absurd about punishing an individual who, with knowledge of the words he uses and their ordinary meaning in context, makes a threat. For instance, a high-school student who sends a letter to his principal stating that he will massacre his classmates with a machine gun, even if he intended the letter as a joke, cannot fairly be described as engaging in innocent conduct. But see ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x935, 16 (concluding that Elonis\xe2\x80\x99 conviction under \xc2\xa7875(c) for discussing a plan to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98initiate the most heinous school shooting ever imagined\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d against \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a Kindergarten class\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d cannot stand without proof of some unspecified heightened mental state).

\n

Elonis also insists that we read an intent-to-threaten element into \xc2\xa7875(c) in light of the\n \nFirst Amendment. But our practice of construing statutes \xe2\x80\x9cto avoid constitutional questions .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is not a license for the judiciary to rewrite language enacted by the legislature,\xe2\x80\x9d Salinas v. United States,\n\n522 U.\xc2\xa0S. 52\n\xe2\x80\x9360 (1997) (internal quotation marks omitted), and ordinary background principles of criminal law do not support rewriting \xc2\xa7875(c) to include an intent-to-threaten requirement. We have not altered our traditional approach to mens rea for other constitutional provisions. See, e.g., Dean v. United States,\n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 568\n\xe2\x80\x93574 (2009) (refusing to read an intent-to-discharge-the-firearm element into a mandatory minimum provision concerning the discharge of a firearm during a particular crime). The\n \nFirst Amendment should be treated no differently.

\n

II

\n

In light of my conclusion that Elonis was properly convicted under the requirements of \xc2\xa7875(c), I must address his argument that his threatening posts were nevertheless protected by the\n \nFirst Amendment.

\n

A

\n

Elonis does not contend that threats are constitutionally protected speech, nor could he: \xe2\x80\x9cFrom 1791 to the present, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. our society .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. has permitted restrictions upon the content of speech in a few limited areas,\xe2\x80\x9d true threats being one of them. R. A. V. v. St. Paul,\n\n505 U.\xc2\xa0S. 377\n\xe2\x80\x93383 (1992); see id., at 388. Instead, Elonis claims that only intentional threats fall within this particular historical exception.

\n

If it were clear that intentional threats alone have been punished in our Nation since 1791, I would be inclined to agree. But that is the not the case. Although the Federal Government apparently did not get into the business of regulating threats until 1917, the States have been doing so since the late 18th and early 19th centuries. See, e.g., 1795 N.\xc2\xa0J. Laws p. 108; Ill. Rev. Code of Laws, Crim. Code \xc2\xa7108 (1827) (1827 Ill. Crim. Code); 1832 Fla. Laws pp. 68\xe2\x80\x9369. And that practice continued even after the States amended their constitutions to include speech protections similar to those in the\n \nFirst Amendment. See, e.g., Fla. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa75 (1838); Ill. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0VIII, \xc2\xa722 (1818), Mich. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa77 (1835); N.\xc2\xa0J. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa75 (1844); J. Hood, Index of Colonial and State Laws of New Jersey 1203, 1235, 1257, 1265 (1905); 1 Ill. Stat., ch. 30, div. 9, \xc2\xa731 (3d ed. 1873). State practice thus provides at least some evidence of the original meaning of the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cfreedom of speech\xe2\x80\x9d in the\n \nFirst Amendment. See Roth v. United States,\n\n354 U.\xc2\xa0S. 476\n\xe2\x80\x93483 (1957) (engaging in a similar inquiry with respect to obscenity).

\n

Shortly after the founding, several States and Territories enacted laws making it a crime to \xe2\x80\x9cknowingly send or deliver any letter or writing, with or without a name subscribed thereto, or signed with a fictitious name, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. threatening to maim, wound, kill or murder any person, or to burn his or her [property], though no money, goods or chattels, or other valuable thing shall be demanded,\xe2\x80\x9d e.g., 1795 N.\xc2\xa0J. Laws \xc2\xa757, at 108; see also, e.g., 1816 Ga. Laws p. 178; 1816 Mich. Territory Laws p. 128; 1827 Ill. Crim. Code \xc2\xa7108; 1832 Fla. Laws, at 68\xe2\x80\x9369. These laws appear to be the closest early analogue to \xc2\xa7875(c), as they penalize transmitting a communication containing a threat without proof of a demand to extort something from the victim. Threat provisions explicitly requiring proof of a specific \xe2\x80\x9cintent to extort\xe2\x80\x9d appeared alongside these laws, see, e.g., 1795 N.\xc2\xa0J. Laws \xc2\xa757, at 108, but those provisions are simply the predecessors to \xc2\xa7875(b) and \xc2\xa7875(d), which likewise expressly contain an intent-to-extort requirement.

\n

The laws without that extortion requirement were copies of a 1754 English threat statute subject to only a general-intent requirement. The statute made it a capital offense to \xe2\x80\x9cknowingly send any Letter without any Name subscribed thereto, or signed with a fictitious Name .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. threatening to kill or murder any of his Majesty\xe2\x80\x99s Subject or Subjects, or to burn their [property], though no Money or Venison or other valuable Thing shall be demanded.\xe2\x80\x9d 27 Geo. II, ch. 15, in 7 Eng. Stat. at Large 61 (1754); see also 4 W. Blackstone, Commentaries on the Laws of England 144 (1768) (describing this statute). Early English decisions applying this threat statute indicated that the appropriate mental state was general intent. In King v. Girdwood, 1 Leach 142, 168 Eng. Rep. 173 (K. B. 1776), for example, the trial court instructed the jurors that, \xe2\x80\x9cif they were of opinion that\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9cterms of the letter conveyed an actual threat to kill or murder,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cand that the prisoner knew the contents of it, they ought to find him guilty; but that if they thought he did not know the contents, or that the words might import any thing less than to kill or murder, they ought to acquit,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 143, 168 Eng. Rep., at 173. On appeal following conviction, the judges \xe2\x80\x9cthought that the case had been properly left to the Jury.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid., 168 Eng. Rep., at 174. Other cases likewise appeared to consider only the import of the letter\xe2\x80\x99s language, not the intent of its sender. See, e.g., Rex v. Boucher, 4 Car. & P. 562, 563, 172 Eng. Rep. 826, 827 (K. B. 1831) (concluding that an indictment was sufficient because \xe2\x80\x9cth[e] letter very plainly conveys a threat to kill and murder\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o one who received it could have any doubt as to what the writer meant to threaten\xe2\x80\x9d); see also 2 E. East, A Treatise of the Pleas of the Crown 1116 (1806) (discussing Jepson and Springett\xe2\x80\x99s Case, in which the judges disagreed over whether \xe2\x80\x9cthe letter must be understood as .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. importing a threat\xe2\x80\x9d and whether that was \xe2\x80\x9ca necessary construction\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Unsurprisingly, these early English cases were well known in the legal world of the 19th century United States. For instance, Nathan Dane\xe2\x80\x99s A General Abridgement of American Law\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9ca necessary adjunct to the library of every American lawyer of distinction,\xe2\x80\x9d 1 C. Warren, History of the Harvard Law School and of Early Legal Conditions in America 414 (1908)\xe2\x80\x94discussed the English threat statute and summarized decisions such as Girdwood. 7 N. Dane, A General Abridgement of American Law 31\xe2\x80\x9332 (1824). And as this Court long ago recognized, \xe2\x80\x9cIt is doubtless true .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that where English statutes .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. have been adopted into our own legislation; the known and settled construction of those statutes by courts of law, has been considered as silently incorporated into the acts, or has been received with all the weight of authority.\xe2\x80\x9d Pennock v. Dialogue, 2 Pet. 1, 18 (1829); see also, e.g., Commonwealth v. Burdick, 2 Pa. 163, 164 (1846) (considering English cases persuasive authority in interpreting similar state statute creating the offense of obtaining property through false pretenses). In short, there is good reason to believe that States bound by their own Constitutions to protect freedom of speech long ago enacted general-intent threat statutes.

\n

Elonis disputes this historical analysis on two grounds, but neither is persuasive. He first points to a treatise stating that the 1754 English statute was \xe2\x80\x9clevelled against such whose intention it was, (by writing such letters, either without names or in fictitious names,) to conceal themselves from the knowledge of the party threatened, that they might obtain their object by creating terror in [the victim\xe2\x80\x99s] mind.\xe2\x80\x9d 2 W. Russell & D. Davis, A Treatise on Crimes & Misdemeanors 1845 (1st Am. ed. 1824). But the fact that the ordinary prosecution under this provision involved a defendant who intended to cause fear does not mean that such a mental state was required as a matter of law. After all, \xc2\xa7875(c) is frequently deployed against people who wanted to cause their victims fear, but that fact does not answer the legal question presented in this case. See, e.g., United States v. Sutcliffe, 505 F.\xc2\xa03d 944, 952 (CA9 2007); see also Tr. of Oral Arg. 53 (counsel for the Government noting that \xe2\x80\x9cI think Congress would well have understood that the majority of these cases probably [involved] people who intended to threaten\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Elonis also cobbles together an assortment of older American authorities to prove his point, but they fail to stand up to close scrutiny. Two of his cases address the offense of breaching the peace, Ware v. Loveridge, 75 Mich. 488, 490\xe2\x80\x93493, 42 N.\xc2\xa0W. 997, 998 (1889); State v. Benedict, 11 Vt. 236, 239 (1839), which is insufficiently similar to the offense criminalized in \xc2\xa7875(c) to be of much use. Another involves a prosecution under a blackmailing statute similar to \xc2\xa7875(b) and \xc2\xa7875(c) in that it expressly required an \xe2\x80\x9cintent to extort.\xe2\x80\x9d Norris v. State, 95 Ind. 73, 74 (1884). And his treatises do not clearly distinguish between the offense of making threats with the intent to extort and the offense of sending threatening letters without such a requirement in their discussions of threat statutes, making it difficult to draw strong inferences about the latter category. See 2 J. Bishop, Commentaries on the Criminal Law \xc2\xa71201, p. 664, and nn. 5\xe2\x80\x936 (1877); 2 J. Bishop, Commentaries on the Law of Criminal Procedure \xc2\xa7975, p. 546 (1866); 25 The American and English Encyclop\xc3\xa6dia of Law 1073 (C. Williams ed. 1894).

\n

Two of Elonis\xe2\x80\x99 cases appear to discuss an offense of sending a threatening letter without an intent to extort, but even these fail to make his point. One notes in passing that character evidence is admissible \xe2\x80\x9cto prove guilty knowledge of the defendant, when that is an essential element of the crime; that is, the quo animo, the intent or design,\xe2\x80\x9d and offers as an example that in the context of \xe2\x80\x9csending a threatening letter, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. prior and subsequent letters to the same person are competent in order to show the intent and meaning of the particular letter in question.\xe2\x80\x9d State v. Graham, 121 N.\xc2\xa0C. 623, 627, 28 S.\xc2\xa0E. 409, 409 (1897). But it is unclear from that statement whether that court thought an intent to threaten was required, especially as the case it cited for this proposition\xe2\x80\x94Rex v. Boucher, 4 Car. & P. 562, 563, 172 Eng. Rep. 826, 827 (K.\xc2\xa0B. 1831)\xe2\x80\x94supports a general-intent approach. The other case Elonis cites involves a statutory provision that had been judicially limited to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98pertain to one or the other acts which are denounced by the statute,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d namely, terroristic activities carried out by the Ku Klux Klan. Commonwealth v. Morton, 140 Ky. 628, 630, 131 S.\xc2\xa0W. 506, 507 (1910) (quoting Commonwealth v. Patrick, 127 Ky. 473, 478, 105 S.\xc2\xa0W. 981, 982 (1907)). That case thus provides scant historical support for Elonis\xe2\x80\x99 position.

\n

B

\n

Elonis also insists that our precedents require a mental state of intent when it comes to threat prosecutions under \xc2\xa7875(c), primarily relying on Watts,\n\n394 U.\xc2\xa0S. 705\n, and Virginia v. Black,\n\n538 U.\xc2\xa0S. 343 (2003)\n. Neither of those decisions, however, addresses whether the\n \nFirst Amendment requires a particular mental state for threat prosecutions.

\n

As Elonis admits, Watts expressly declined to address the mental state required under the\n \nFirst Amendment for a \xe2\x80\x9ctrue threat.\xe2\x80\x9d See 394 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 707\xe2\x80\x93708. True, the Court in Watts noted \xe2\x80\x9cgrave doubts\xe2\x80\x9d about Raganksy\xe2\x80\x99s construction of \xe2\x80\x9cwillfully\xe2\x80\x9d in the presidential threats statute. 394 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 707\xe2\x80\x93708. But \xe2\x80\x9cgrave doubts\xe2\x80\x9d do not make a holding, and that stray statement in Watts is entitled to no precedential force. If anything, Watts continued the long tradition of focusing on objective criteria in evaluating the mental requirement. See ibid.

\n

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s fractured opinion in Black likewise says little about whether an intent-to-threaten requirement is constitutionally mandated here. Black concerned a Virginia cross-burning law that expressly required \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98an intent to intimidate a person or group of persons,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 538 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 347 (quoting Va. Code Ann. \xc2\xa718.2\xe2\x80\x93423 (1996)), and the Court thus had no occasion to decide whether such an element was necessary in threat provisions silent on the matter. Moreover, the focus of the Black decision was on the statutory presumption that \xe2\x80\x9cany cross burning [w]as prima facie evidence of intent to intimidate.\xe2\x80\x9d 538 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 347\xe2\x80\x93348. A majority of the Court concluded that this presumption failed to distinguish unprotected threats from protected speech because it might allow convictions \xe2\x80\x9cbased solely on the fact of cross burning itself,\xe2\x80\x9d including cross burnings in a play or at a political rally. Id., at 365\xe2\x80\x93366 (plurality opinion); id., at 386 (Souter, J., concurring in judgment in part and dissenting in part) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe provision will thus tend to draw nonthreatening ideological expression within the ambit of the prohibition of intimidating expression\xe2\x80\x9d). The objective standard for threats under \xc2\xa7875(c), however, helps to avoid this problem by \xe2\x80\x9cforc[ing] jurors to examine the circumstances in which a statement is made.\xe2\x80\x9d Jeffries, 692 F. 3d, at 480.

\n

In addition to requiring a departure from our precedents, adopting Elonis\xe2\x80\x99 view would make threats one of the most protected categories of unprotected speech, thereby sowing tension throughout our\n \nFirst Amendment doctrine. We generally have not required a heightened mental state under the\n \nFirst Amendment for historically unprotected categories of speech. For instance, the Court has indicated that a legislature may constitutionally prohibit \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98fighting words,\xe2\x80\x99 those personally abusive epithets which, when addressed to the ordinary citizen, are, as a matter of common knowledge, inherently likely to provoke violent reaction,\xe2\x80\x9d Cohen v. California,\n\n403 U.\xc2\xa0S. 15,\n20 (1971)\n\xe2\x80\x94without proof of an intent to provoke a violent reaction. Because the definition of \xe2\x80\x9cfighting words\xe2\x80\x9d turns on how the \xe2\x80\x9cordinary citizen\xe2\x80\x9d would react to the language, ibid., this Court has observed that a defendant may be guilty of a breach of the peace if he \xe2\x80\x9cmakes statements likely to provoke violence and disturbance of good order, even though no such eventuality be intended,\xe2\x80\x9d and that the punishment of such statements \xe2\x80\x9cas a criminal act would raise no question under [the Constitution],\xe2\x80\x9d Cantwell v. Connecticut,\n\n310 U.\xc2\xa0S. 296\n\xe2\x80\x93310 (1940); see also Chaplinsky v. New Hampshire,\n\n315 U.\xc2\xa0S. 568\n\xe2\x80\x93573 (1942) (rejecting a\n \nFirst Amendment challenge to a general-intent construction of a state statute punishing \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98fighting\xe2\x80\x99 words\xe2\x80\x9d); State v. Chaplinsky, 91 N.\xc2\xa0H. 310, 318, 18 A.\xc2\xa02d 754, 758 (1941) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he only intent required for conviction .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. was an intent to speak the words\xe2\x80\x9d). The Court has similarly held that a defendant may be convicted of mailing obscenity under the\n \nFirst Amendment with-out proof that he knew the materials were legally obscene. Hamling, 418 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 120\xe2\x80\x93124. And our precedents allow liability in tort for false statements about private persons on matters of private concern even if the speaker acted negligently with respect to the falsity of those statements. See Philadelphia Newspapers, Inc. v. Hepps,\n\n475 U.\xc2\xa0S. 767\n\xe2\x80\x93775 (1986). I see no reason why we should give threats pride of place among unprotected speech.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

There is always a risk that a criminal threat statute may be deployed by the Government to suppress legitimate speech. But the proper response to that risk is to adhere to our traditional rule that only a narrow class of true threats, historically unprotected, may be constitutionally proscribed.

\n

The solution is not to abandon a mental-state requirement compelled by text, history, and precedent. Not only does such a decision warp our traditional approach to mens rea, it results in an arbitrary distinction between threats and other forms of unprotected speech. Had Elonis mailed obscene materials to his wife and a kindergarten class, he could have been prosecuted irrespective of whether he intended to offend those recipients or reck-lessly disregarded that possibility. Yet when he threatened to kill his wife and a kindergarten class, his intent to terrify those recipients (or reckless disregard of that risk) suddenly becomes highly relevant. That need not\xe2\x80\x94and should not\xe2\x80\x94be the case.

\n

Nor should it be the case that we cast aside the mental-state requirement compelled by our precedents yet offer nothing in its place. Our job is to decide questions, not create them. Given the majority\xe2\x80\x99s ostensible concern for protecting innocent actors, one would have expected it to announce a clear rule\xe2\x80\x94any clear rule. Its failure to do so reveals the fractured foundation upon which today\xe2\x80\x99s decision rests.

\n

I respectfully dissent.
