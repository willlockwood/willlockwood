\nEVANS V. CHAVIS

\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

EVANS, ACTING WARDEN v. CHAVIS

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 04\xe2\x80\x93721.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 9, 2005\xe2\x80\x94Decided January 10, 2006

\n

The Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA) gives a state prisoner whose conviction has become final one year to seek federal habeas corpus relief, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72244(d)(1)(A), but tolls this 1-year limitations period for the \xe2\x80\x9ctime during which a properly filed application for State \xe2\x80\xa6 collateral review . . . is pending,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72244(d)(2). Under California\xe2\x80\x99s collateral review scheme, the equivalent of a notice of appeal is timely if filed within a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable time.\xe2\x80\x9d In Carey v. Saffold, 536 U. S. 214, this Court held, inter alia, that (1) only a timely appeal tolls AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s limitations period for the time between the lower court\xe2\x80\x99s adverse decision and the filing of a notice of appeal; (2) in California, \xe2\x80\x9cunreasonable\xe2\x80\x9d delays are not timely; and (most pertinently) (3) a California Supreme Court order denying a petition \xe2\x80\x9con the merits\xe2\x80\x9d does not automatically indicate that the petition was timely filed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent Chavis, a California state prisoner, filed a state habeas petition on May 14, 1993, which the trial court denied. On September 29, 1994, the California Court of Appeal also held against him. He then waited more than three years before seeking review in the California Supreme Court. On April 29, 1998, that court issued an order stating simply that the petition was denied. On August 30, 2000, Chavis filed a federal habeas petition. After the case reached it, the Ninth Circuit concluded that the federal petition\xe2\x80\x99s timeliness depended on whether Chavis\xe2\x80\x99 state postconviction relief application was \xe2\x80\x9cpending,\xe2\x80\x9d therefore tolling AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s limitations period, during the 3-year period between the time the California Court of Appeal issued its opinion and the time he sought review in the State Supreme Court. The Ninth Circuit held that the state application was \xe2\x80\x9cpending\xe2\x80\x9d because under Circuit precedent a denial without comment or citation is treated as a denial on the merits, and a petition denied on the merits was not untimely.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit departed from Saffold\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of AEDPA as applied to California\xe2\x80\x99s system. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Contrary to Saffold, the Circuit in this case said in effect that the California Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s denial of a petition \xe2\x80\x9con the merits\xe2\x80\x9d did automatically mean that the petition was timely. More than that, it treated a State Supreme Court order that was silent on the grounds for the court\xe2\x80\x99s decision as equivalent to an order in which the words \xe2\x80\x9con the merits\xe2\x80\x9d appeared. If the appearance of \xe2\x80\x9con the merits\xe2\x80\x9d does not automatically warrant a holding that the filing was timely, the absence of those words could not automatically warrant such a holding. Absent (1) clear direction or explanation from the California Supreme Court about the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9creasonable time\xe2\x80\x9d in the present context, or (2) clear indication that a particular request for appellate review was timely or untimely, the Ninth Circuit must itself examine the delay in each case and determine what the state courts would have held in respect to timeliness. This is what this Court believes it asked the Circuit to do in Saffold. This is what this Court believes the Circuit should have done here. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Given the uncertain scope of California\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9creasonable time\xe2\x80\x9d standard, it may not be easy for the Ninth Circuit to decide in each of the several hundred federal habeas petitions from California prisoners it hears annually whether a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s state-court review petition was timely. However, for the reasons given in Saffold, the Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s attempt to create shortcuts looking to the label the California Supreme Court applied to the denial order, even where that label does not refer to timeliness, are not true, either to California\xe2\x80\x99s timeliness rule or to AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s intent to toll the 1-year limitations period only when the state collateral review proceeding is \xe2\x80\x9cpending.\xe2\x80\x9d Saffold, 536 U. S., at 220\xe2\x80\x93221, 225\xe2\x80\x93226. The California courts might alleviate the problem by clarifying the scope of \xe2\x80\x9creasonable time\xe2\x80\x9d or by indicating, when denying a petition, whether the filing was timely. And the Ninth Circuit might seek guidance by certifying a question to the State Supreme Court in an appropriate case. Id., at 226\xe2\x80\x93227. Alternatively, the California Legislature might decide to impose more determinate time limits, conforming California law with that of most other States. Absent any such guidance from state law, however, the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s only alternative is to simply ask and decide whether the state prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s filing was made within a reasonable time. In doing so, the Circuit must be mindful that, in Saffold, this Court held that timely filings in California fell within the federal tolling provision on the assumption that California\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9creasonable time\xe2\x80\x9d standard would not lead to filing delays substantially longer than those in States with determinate timeliness rules. Id., at 222\xe2\x80\x93223. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Chavis did not file his petition for review in the California Supreme Court within a reasonable time. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s examination of the record refutes his claim that his 3-year, 1-month, delay was reasonable because he could not use the prison library to work on his petition during this period. And since Chavis needs all but two days of that lengthy delay to survive the federal 1-year habeas filing period, he cannot succeed. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

382 F.\xc2\xa03d 921, reversed and remanded.

\n

\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

MIKE EVANS, ACTING WARDEN, PETITIONER v. REGINALD CHAVIS

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[January 10, 2006]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Breyer delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA or Act) requires a state prisoner whose conviction has become final to seek federal habeas corpus relief within one year. 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72244(d)(1)(A). The Act tolls this 1-year limitations period for the \xe2\x80\x9ctime during which a properly filed application for State post-conviction or other collateral review \xe2\x80\xa6 is pending.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72244(d)(2). The time that an application for state postconviction review is \xe2\x80\x9cpending\xe2\x80\x9d includes the period between (1) a lower court\xe2\x80\x99s adverse determination, and (2) the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s filing of a notice of appeal, provided that the filing of the notice of appeal is timely under state law. Carey v. Saffold, 536 U. S. 214 (2002).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In most States a statute sets out the number of days for filing a timely notice of appeal, typically a matter of a few days. See id., at 219. California, however, has a special system governing appeals when prisoners seek relief on collateral review. Under that system, the equivalent of a notice of appeal is timely if filed within a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable time.\xe2\x80\x9d In\xc2\xa0re Harris, 5 Cal. 4th 813, 828, n.\xc2\xa07, 855 P.\xc2\xa02d 391, 398, n.\xc2\xa07 (1993); see also Saffold, supra, at 221.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this case, the Ninth Circuit found timely a California prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s request for appellate review made three years after the lower state court ruled against him. Chavis v. LeMarque, 382 F.\xc2\xa03d 921 (2004). We conclude that the Circuit departed from our interpretation of the Act as applied to California\xe2\x80\x99s system, Carey v. Saffold, supra, and we therefore reverse its judgment.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We begin with our holding in Carey v. Saffold. In that case we addressed three questions.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We initially considered the question just mentioned: For purposes of tolling AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s 1-year limitations period, is a state habeas application \xe2\x80\x9cpending\xe2\x80\x9d during the interval between (1) the time a lower state court reaches an adverse decision, and (2) the day the prisoner timely files an appeal? We answered this question \xe2\x80\x9cyes.\xe2\x80\x9d 536 U. S., at 219\xe2\x80\x93221. If the filing of the appeal is timely, the period between the adverse lower court decision and the filing (typically just a few days) is not counted against the 1-year AEDPA time limit.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We then pointed out that in most States a prisoner who seeks review of an adverse lower court decision must file a notice of appeal in a higher court, and the timeliness of that notice of appeal is measured in terms of a determinate time period, such as 30 or 60 days. Id., at 219. As we explained, however, California has a different rule. In California, a state prisoner may seek review of an adverse lower court decision by filing an original petition (rather than a notice of appeal) in the higher court, and that petition is timely if filed within a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable time.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 221. We asked whether this distinction made a difference for AEDPA tolling purposes. We answered that question \xe2\x80\x9cno.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 222\xe2\x80\x93223. California\xe2\x80\x99s system is sufficiently analogous to appellate review systems in other States to treat it similarly. See id., at 222 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe upshot is that California\xe2\x80\x99s collateral review process functions very much like that of other States, but for the fact that its timeliness rule is indeterminate\xe2\x80\x9d). As long as the prisoner filed a petition for appellate review within a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable time,\xe2\x80\x9d he could count as \xe2\x80\x9cpending\xe2\x80\x9d (and add to the 1-year time limit) the days between (1) the time the lower state court reached an adverse decision, and (2) the day he filed a petition in the higher state court. Id., at 222\xe2\x80\x93223. We added, \xe2\x80\x9cThe fact that California\xe2\x80\x99s timeliness standard is general rather than precise may make it more difficult for federal courts to determine just when a review application (i.e., a filing in a higher court) comes too late.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 223. Nonetheless, the federal courts must undertake that task.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We considered finally whether the state habeas petition at issue in the case had itself been timely filed. Saffold had filed that petition (a petition for review by the California Supreme Court) not within 30 or even 60 days after the lower court (the California Court of Appeal) had reached its adverse decision, but, rather, 412 months later. The filing was not obviously late, however, because the delay might have been due to excusable neglect\xe2\x80\x94Saffold said he had taken 412 months because he had not received timely notice of the adverse lower court decision. Id., at 226.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We sent the case back to the Ninth Circuit to decide whether the prisoner had filed his California Supreme Court petition within a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable time,\xe2\x80\x9d thus making the filing timely under California law. We also set forth several legal propositions that set the boundaries within which the Ninth Circuit must answer this question.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, we pointed out that if \xe2\x80\x9cthe California Supreme Court had clearly ruled that Saffold\xe2\x80\x99s 412-month delay was \xe2\x80\x98unreasonable,\xe2\x80\x99 that would be the end of the matter.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, we noted that the California Supreme Court order denying Saffold\xe2\x80\x99s petition had stated that the denial was \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98on the merits and for lack of diligence.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 225. But, we added, these words alone did not decide the question. Id., at 225\xe2\x80\x93226.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Third, we stated that the words \xe2\x80\x9clack of diligence\xe2\x80\x9d did not prove that the California Supreme Court thought the petition was untimely. That is because those words might have referred to a totally different, earlier delay that was \xe2\x80\x9cirrelevant\xe2\x80\x9d to the timeliness of Saffold\xe2\x80\x99s California Supreme Court petition. Id., at 226.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Fourth, we stated that the words \xe2\x80\x9con the merits\xe2\x80\x9d did not prove that the California Supreme Court thought the petition was timely. That is because the California Supreme Court might have decided to address the merits of the petition even if the petition had been untimely. A \xe2\x80\x9ccourt,\xe2\x80\x9d we said,

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cwill sometimes address the merits of a claim that it believes was presented in an untimely way: for instance, where the merits present no difficult issue; where the court wants to give a reviewing court alternative grounds for decision; or where the court wishes to show a prisoner (who may not have a lawyer) that it was not merely a procedural technicality that precluded him from obtaining relief.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 225\xe2\x80\x93226.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We ultimately concluded that the Ninth Circuit must not take \xe2\x80\x9csuch words\xe2\x80\x9d (i.e., the words \xe2\x80\x9con the merits\xe2\x80\x9d) as \xe2\x80\x9can absolute bellwether\xe2\x80\x9d on the timeliness question. Id., at 226 (emphasis added). We pointed out that the Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s contrary approach (i.e., an approach that presumed that an order denying a petition \xe2\x80\x9con the merits\xe2\x80\x9d meant that the petition was timely) would lead to the tolling of AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s limitations period in circumstances where the law does not permit tolling. Ibid. And we gave as an example of the incorrect approach a case in which the Ninth Circuit had found timely a petition for review filed four years after the lower court reached its decision. Ibid. (citing Welch v. Newland, 267 F.\xc2\xa03d 1013 (CA9 2001)).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We turn now to the present case. Respondent Reginald Chavis, a California state prisoner, filed a state habeas corpus petition on May 14, 1993. The trial court denied the petition. He sought review in the California Court of Appeal, which also held against him. The Court of Appeal released its decision on September 29, 1994. Chavis then waited more than three years, until November 5, 1997, before filing a petition for review in the California Supreme Court. On April 29, 1998, the California Supreme Court denied the petition in an order stating simply, \xe2\x80\x9cPetition for writ of habeas corpus [i.e., review in the California Supreme Court] is DENIED.\xe2\x80\x9d App. G to Pet. for Cert. 1.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Subsequently, on August 30, 2000 (after bringing a second round of state habeas petitions), Chavis filed a federal habeas petition. The State asked the federal court to dismiss the petition on the ground that it was untimely. After all, AEDPA gives prisoners only one year to file their federal petitions, and Chavis had filed his federal petition more than four years after AEDPA became effective. Still, AEDPA also provides for tolling, adding to the one year those days during which an application for state collateral review is \xe2\x80\x9cpending.\xe2\x80\x9d And the federal courts consequently had to calculate how many days Chavis\xe2\x80\x99 state collateral review applications had been \xe2\x80\x9cpending\xe2\x80\x9d in the state courts and add those days to the 1-year limitations period.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Ultimately, after the case reached the Ninth Circuit, that court concluded that the timeliness of the federal petition turned upon whether the \xe2\x80\x9cpending\xe2\x80\x9d period included the 3-year period between (1) the time a lower state court, the California Court of Appeal issued its opinion (September 29, 1994), and (2) the time Chavis sought review in a higher state court, the California Supreme Court (on November 5, 1997). The Ninth Circuit held that the state collateral review application was \xe2\x80\x9cpending\xe2\x80\x9d during this time; hence, it should add those three years to the federal 1-year limitations period, and the addition of those three years, along with various other additions, rendered the federal filing timely.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning as to why it should add the three years consists of the following:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cUnder our decision in Saffold, because Chavis\xe2\x80\x99s November 1997 habeas petition to the California Supreme Court was denied on the merits, it was pending during the interval between the Court of Appeal decision and the Supreme Court petition and he is entitled to tolling. See [Saffold v. Carey, 312 F.\xc2\xa03d 1031, 1034\xe2\x80\x931036 (CA9 2002)]. When the California Supreme Court denies a habeas petition without comment or citation, we have long treated the denial as a decision on the merits. Hunter v. Aispuro, 982 F.\xc2\xa02d 344, 348 (9th Cir. 1992). Therefore, the California Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s summary denial was on the merits, and the petition was not dismissed as untimely. See id.; see also Delhomme v. Ramirez, 340 F.\xc2\xa03d 817, 819, 820 n.\xc2\xa02 (9th Cir. 2003) (noting that there was no indication that a state habeas petition was untimely where the California Supreme Court denied the petition without comment or citation). As a result, Chavis is entitled to tolling during [the relevant period].\xe2\x80\x9d 382 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 926 (emphasis added).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0California sought certiorari on the ground that the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision was inconsistent with our holding in Saffold. We granted the writ.

\n

III

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0California argues that the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision in this case is inconsistent with our decision in Saffold. Like California, we do not see how it is possible to reconcile the two cases.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Saffold, we held that (1) only a timely appeal tolls AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s 1-year limitations period for the time between the lower court\xe2\x80\x99s adverse decision and the filing of a notice of appeal in the higher court; (2) in California, \xe2\x80\x9cunreasonable\xe2\x80\x9d delays are not timely; and (3) (most pertinently) a California Supreme Court order denying a petition \xe2\x80\x9con the merits\xe2\x80\x9d does not automatically indicate that the petition was timely filed. In addition, we referred to a Ninth Circuit case holding that a 4-year delay was reasonable as an example of what the law forbids the Ninth Circuit to do.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nonetheless, the Ninth Circuit in this case said in effect that the California Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s denial of a petition \xe2\x80\x9con the merits\xe2\x80\x9d did automatically mean that the petition was timely (and thus that a 3-year delay was reasonable). More than that, it treated an order from the California Supreme Court that was silent on the grounds for the court\xe2\x80\x99s decision as if it were equivalent to an order in which the words \xe2\x80\x9con the merits\xe2\x80\x9d appeared. 382 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 926. If the appearance of the words \xe2\x80\x9con the merits\xe2\x80\x9d does not automatically warrant a holding that the filing was timely, the absence of those words could not automatically warrant a holding that the filing was timely. After all, the fact that the California Supreme Court did not include the words \xe2\x80\x9con the merits\xe2\x80\x9d in its order denying Chavis relief makes it less likely, not more likely, that the California Supreme Court believed that Chavis\xe2\x80\x99 3-year delay was reasonable. Thus, the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s presumption (\xe2\x80\x9cthat an order decided entirely on the merits indicates that the state court did not find the petition to be untimely,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 4 (opinion of Stevens, J.)) is not consistent with Saffold. See supra, at 4.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Neither do the cases cited by the Ninth Circuit provide it with the necessary legal support. The Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in Saffold (written on remand from this Court) said nothing about the significance of the words \xe2\x80\x9con the merits.\xe2\x80\x9d Saffold v. Carey, 312 F.\xc2\xa03d 1031 (2002). Hunter v. Aispuro, 982 F.\xc2\xa02d 344 (CA9 1992), predated AEDPA, not to mention our decision in Saffold, and in any event concerned an entirely different issue of federal habeas corpus law. Delhomme v. Ramirez, 340 F.\xc2\xa03d 817 (CA9 2003), addressed the timeliness issue in one sentence in a footnote, id., at 820, n.\xc2\xa02, and did not discuss at any length our opinion in Saffold, which must control the result here.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the absence of (1) clear direction or explanation from the California Supreme Court about the meaning of the term \xe2\x80\x9creasonable time\xe2\x80\x9d in the present context, or (2) clear indication that a particular request for appellate review was timely or untimely, the Circuit must itself examine the delay in each case and determine what the state courts would have held in respect to timeliness. That is to say, without using a merits determination as an \xe2\x80\x9cabsolute bellwether\xe2\x80\x9d (as to timeliness), the federal court must decide whether the filing of the request for state-court appellate review (in state collateral review proceedings) was made within what California would consider a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable time.\xe2\x80\x9d See supra, at 3. This is what we believe we asked the Circuit to do in Saffold. This is what we believe it should have done.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The discrepancy between the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s view of the matter and ours may reflect an administrative problem. The Ninth Circuit each year must hear several hundred petitions by California prisoners seeking federal habeas relief. Some of these cases will involve filing delays, and some of those delays will require the federal courts to determine whether a petition for appellate review in a related state collateral proceeding was timely. Given the uncertain scope of California\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9creasonable time\xe2\x80\x9d standard, it may not be easy for the Circuit to decide in each such case whether the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s state-court review petition was timely. And it is consequently not surprising that the Circuit has tried to create rules of thumb that look to the label the California Supreme Court applied to the denial order, even where that label does not refer to timeliness. For the reasons we gave in Saffold, however, we do not believe these shortcuts remain true, either to California\xe2\x80\x99s timeliness rule or to Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent in AEDPA to toll the 1-year limitations period only when the state collateral review proceeding is \xe2\x80\x9cpending.\xe2\x80\x9d 536 U. S., at 220\xe2\x80\x93221, 225\xe2\x80\x93226.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The California courts themselves might alleviate the problem by clarifying the scope of the words \xe2\x80\x9creasonable time\xe2\x80\x9d in this context or by indicating, when denying a petition, whether the filing was timely. And the Ninth Circuit might seek guidance on the matter by certifying a question to the California Supreme Court in an appropriate case. Id., at 226\xe2\x80\x93227. Alternatively, the California Legislature might itself decide to impose more determinate time limits, conforming California law in this respect with the law of most other States. Indeed, either state body might adopt a state-law presumption of the kind the concurrence here suggests. See post, at 8\xe2\x80\x939. In the absence of any such guidance, however, we see no alternative way of applying state law to a case like this one but for the Ninth Circuit simply to ask and to decide whether the state prisoner made the relevant filing within a reasonable time. In doing so, the Circuit must keep in mind that, in Saffold, we held that timely filings in California (as elsewhere) fell within the federal tolling provision on the assumption that California law in this respect did not differ significantly from the laws of other States, i.e., that California\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9creasonable time\xe2\x80\x9d standard would not lead to filing delays substantially longer than those in States with determinate timeliness rules. 536 U. S., at 222\xe2\x80\x93223. California, of course, remains free to tell us if, in this respect, we were wrong.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As we have pointed out, supra, at 5, Chavis had one year from the date AEDPA became effective (April 24, 1996) to file a federal habeas petition. Chavis did not actually file his petition in federal district court until August 30, 2000, four years and 128 days after AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s effective date. Hence Chavis\xe2\x80\x99 federal petition was timely only if \xe2\x80\x9ca properly filed application for State post-conviction or other collateral review [was] pending\xe2\x80\x9d for at least three years and 128 days of this time. 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72244(d)(2). Under the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning Chavis\xe2\x80\x99 state collateral review proceedings were \xe2\x80\x9cpending\xe2\x80\x9d for three years and 130 days, which period (when added to the 1-year federal limitations period) makes the federal petition timely.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As we have explained, however, we find the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning in conflict with our Saffold holding. And, after examining the record, we are convinced that the law does not permit a holding that Chavis\xe2\x80\x99 federal habeas petition was timely. Chavis filed his state petition for habeas review in the California Supreme Court approximately three years and one month after the California Court of Appeal released its decision denying him relief. Chavis tries to explain this long delay by arguing that he could not use the prison library to work on his petition during this time either because (1) his prison job\xe2\x80\x99s hours coincided with those of the library, or (2) prison lockdowns confined him to his cell. And, he adds, his inability to use the library excuses the three year and one month delay\xe2\x80\x94to the point where, despite the delay, he filed his petition for California Supreme Court review within a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable time.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Chavis concedes, however, that in March 1996, App. 38, about a year and a half after the California Court of Appeal denied his habeas petition, he was given a new prison job. He nowhere denies California\xe2\x80\x99s assertion, id., at 68, that this new job\xe2\x80\x99s working hours permitted him to use the library. And he also concedes that the prison \xe2\x80\x9cremained relatively lockdown free\xe2\x80\x9d between February 1997 and August 1997, id., at 39, a 6-month period. Thus, viewing every disputed issue most favorably to Chavis, there remains a totally unexplained, hence unjustified, delay of at least six months.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Six months is far longer than the \xe2\x80\x9cshort period[s] of time,\xe2\x80\x9d 30 to 60 days, that most States provide for filing an appeal to the state supreme court. Saffold, supra, at 219. It is far longer than the 10-day period California gives a losing party to file a notice of appeal in the California Supreme Court, see Cal. App. Ct. Rule 28(e)(1) (2004). We have found no authority suggesting, nor found any convincing reason to believe, that California would consider an unjustified or unexplained 6-month filing delay \xe2\x80\x9creasonable.\xe2\x80\x9d Nor do we see how an unexplained delay of this magnitude could fall within the scope of the federal statutory word \xe2\x80\x9cpending\xe2\x80\x9d as interpreted in Saffold. See 536 U. S., at 222\xe2\x80\x93223. Thus, since Chavis needs all but two days of the lengthy (three year and one month) delay to survive the federal 1-year habeas filing period, see 382 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 927, he cannot succeed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The concurrence reaches the same ultimate conclusion in a different way. Unlike the Ninth Circuit, it would not count in Chavis\xe2\x80\x99 favor certain days during which Chavis was pursuing a second round of state collateral review efforts. See post, at 10. Because, as the Ninth Circuit pointed out, the parties did not argue this particular matter below, 382 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 925, n.\xc2\xa03, we do not consider it here.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For these reasons, the judgment of the Ninth Circuit is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

MIKE EVANS, ACTING WARDEN, PETITIONER v. REGINALD CHAVIS

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[January 10, 2006]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens, concurring in the judgment.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Today the Court holds that, in the absence of a clear statement by a California state court that a petition for habeas corpus was timely or untimely, a federal court \xe2\x80\x9cmust itself examine the delay in each case\xe2\x80\x9d to determine whether the filing \xe2\x80\x9cwas made within what California would consider a \xe2\x80\x98reasonable time.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8. Contrary to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s admonition in its next sentence, this is not what we \xe2\x80\x9casked the Circuit to do in Saffold,\xe2\x80\x9d and it is not what \xe2\x80\x9cit should have done.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (citing Carey v. Saffold, 536 U. S. 214 (2002)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision in this case was both faithful to our decision in Saffold and consistent with our prior jurisprudence. Instead of endorsing an ad hoc approach to the interpretation of ambiguous judgments entered by California courts in the future, I believe we should direct the Ninth Circuit to apply the straightforward presumptions that I describe below. Rather than a de novo review of the record and California law, see ante, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311, it is the application of these presumptions, buttressed by an independent error made by the Ninth Circuit, that convinces me that the judgment must be reversed.

\n

I

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the Court has explained, both in Saffold and in its opinion today, California\xe2\x80\x99s postconviction procedures are unlike those employed by most other States. See 536 U. S., at 221\xe2\x80\x93222; ante, at 1, 2\xe2\x80\x933. California\xe2\x80\x99s time limit for the filing of a habeas corpus petition in a noncapital case is more forgiving and more flexible than that employed by most States. See Saffold, 536 U. S., at 222. Generally, such a petition \xe2\x80\x9cmust be filed within a reasonable time after the petitioner or counsel knew, or with due diligence should have known, the facts underlying the claim as well as the legal basis of the claim.\xe2\x80\x9d In re Harris, 5 Cal. 4th 813, 828, n.\xc2\xa07, 855 P. 2d 391, 398, n. 7 (1993). And the State Supreme Court apparently may exercise its jurisdiction to decide the merits of a petition for habeas corpus at any time whatsoever. See Cal. Const., Art. VI, \xc2\xa710 (giving California Supreme Court original jurisdiction over habeas petitions); In re Clark, 5 Cal. 4th 750, 764\xe2\x80\x93765, 855 P. 2d 729, 738 (1993) (noting procedural rules governing habeas petitions are judicially created).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is the existence of this flexible, discretionary timeliness standard in noncapital cases[Footnote 1] that gave rise to both the issue presented in Saffold and the issue the Court addresses today. In Saffold, we considered whether a habeas petition filed in the California Supreme Court 412 months after the lower state court made its decision was \xe2\x80\x9cpending\xe2\x80\x9d (and therefore tolled the federal statute of limitations in the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA)) during that period. See 536 U. S., at 217. After concluding that a state habeas application is pending during the interval between an adverse lower court decision and the filing in the California Supreme Court, and that California\xe2\x80\x99s virtually unique system made no difference for purposes of tolling AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s statute of limitations, we were faced with the question whether the state habeas petition in that case had been timely filed. See id., at 221, 223, 225.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Rather than answering the question ourselves, we remanded the case to the Court of Appeals with instructions that it do so. Id., at 226. We also explained why the answer was not entirely clear. In its order the California Supreme Court had stated that it had denied the petition both \xe2\x80\x9con the merits and for lack of diligence.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 218 (internal quotation marks omitted). We pointed out that the fact that the State Supreme Court had reached the merits did not preclude the possibility that its alternative basis for decision\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9clack of diligence\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94expressed a conclusion that the 412-month delay was unreasonable and therefore that it had considered the petition untimely as a matter of state law. On the other hand, we also recognized that \xe2\x80\x9clack of diligence\xe2\x80\x9d might have referred to the respondent\xe2\x80\x99s earlier failure to file his first postconviction petition more promptly, \xe2\x80\x9ca matter irrelevant to the question whether his application was \xe2\x80\x98pending\xe2\x80\x99 during the 412-month interval.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 226. Our opinion requested the Court of Appeals to resolve the ambiguity, noting that it might be \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate to certify a question to the California Supreme Court for the purpose of seeking clarification in this area of state law.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 226\xe2\x80\x93227.[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On remand in Saffold, after reviewing three fairly contemporaneous California Supreme Court orders that involved delays of 7 months, 18 months, and 15 months without mentioning any \xe2\x80\x9clack of diligence,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court of Appeals came to the quite reasonable conclusion that the State Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9clack of diligence\xe2\x80\x9d notation in the order denying Saffold\xe2\x80\x99s petition referred to an earlier 5-year delay that was irrelevant to the tolling issue rather than to the 412-month delay that had preceded his most recent filing. See Saffold v. Carey, 312 F. 3d 1031, 1035 (CA9 2002). It also noted \xe2\x80\x9cthat we have not been asked to provide any bright-line rule for determining what constitutes \xe2\x80\x98unreasonable\xe2\x80\x99 delay under California\xe2\x80\x99s indeterminate timeliness standard. While such a bright-line rule would certainly be welcomed, \xe2\x80\xa6 such an issue is more appropriately decided by the California Supreme Court or the California State Legislature.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1036, n.\xc2\xa01.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As both Judge O\xe2\x80\x99Scannlain\xe2\x80\x94who wrote for the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x94and I understood the rule of law that animated our remand, it was predicated on the assumption that the answer to the timeliness question depended on what the California Supreme Court had actually decided rather than on any conclusion that the Court of Appeals itself might reach concerning the reasonableness of the 412-month delay under California law. See id., at 1034. That assumption, also applied by the Ninth Circuit here, was consistent with the unequivocal assertion in our opinion that if the California Supreme Court had \xe2\x80\x9cclearly ruled\xe2\x80\x9d that the 412-month delay was unreasonable, \xe2\x80\x9cthat would be the end of the matter,\xe2\x80\x9d even if the court had also ruled on the merits. Saffold, 536 U. S., at 226.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Similarly, there is no inconsistency between our conclusion in Saffold that the merits ruling \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not automatically indicate that the petition was timely filed,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 7, and the presumption applied by the Court of Appeals in this case that an order decided entirely on the merits indicates that the state court did not find the petition to be untimely, see App. A to Pet. for Cert. 9, particularly when California allows the petitioner to advance a variety of reasons to excuse a late filing, see, e.g., In re Robbins, 18 Cal. 4th 770, 780\xe2\x80\x93782, 959 P. 2d 311, 318 (1998). Our rejection of the words \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98on the merits\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9can absolute bellwether\xe2\x80\x9d was made in a case in which the order itself indicated that the state court might have considered the petition untimely. Saffold, 536 U. S., at 226. Given that ambiguous order, Saffold did not foreclose the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 presumption that, by dismissing a petition solely on the merits, the state court necessarily found the filing to be timely. The Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 opinion in this case was therefore completely consistent with both our holding and our reasoning in Saffold.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 opinion was also consistent with our prior habeas jurisprudence. While the present question requires us to apply the tolling provision of a federal statute, application of that provision ultimately rests on state-law procedural rules. See 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72244(d)(2) (tolling federal statute while \xe2\x80\x9cproperly filed\xe2\x80\x9d application for state postconviction relief is pending). To the extent that a possibly decisive state-law requirement is at issue, application of AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s tolling provision is analogous to the question whether denial of a state postconviction petition rested upon an adequate and independent state ground.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Faced with such a question, it has been our general practice to try to determine the actual basis for the state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision rather than to resolve the state-law issue ourselves. The mere fact that a federal petitioner failed to abide by a state procedural rule does not prevent a federal court from resolving a federal claim unless the state court actually relied on the state procedural bar \xe2\x80\x9cas an independent basis for its disposition of the case.\xe2\x80\x9d Harris v. Reed, 489 U. S. 255, 261\xe2\x80\x93262 (1989) (internal quotation marks omitted). This practice is consistent with the rule of Michigan v. Long, 463 U. S. 1032, 1042 (1983), that unless it is \xe2\x80\x9cclear from the opinion itself\xe2\x80\x9d that the state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision rested on an adequate and independent state ground, we have appellate jurisdiction to review its resolution of a federal constitutional question. And in cases in which a state-court order is silent as to the basis for its decision, we have resorted to a presumption to reflect the role intended for such orders by the state court that issued it. See Ylst v. Nunnemaker, 501 U. S. 797, 803\xe2\x80\x93804 (1991).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Until today, however, we have not directed the lower federal courts to decide disputed issues of state procedural law for themselves instead of focusing on the actual basis for a state-court ruling. The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision in this case was entirely consistent with our past practice, and I would adhere to that practice in confronting the question whether habeas petitions advancing federal claims in California courts were filed within a reasonable time as a matter of California law. Cf. Brooks v. Walls, 279 F.\xc2\xa03d 518, 522 (CA7 2002) (Easterbrook, J.) (applying Harris and Ylst to AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cproperly filed\xe2\x80\x9d requirement). The inquiry, then, should focus on what the state court actually decided rather than what a federal court believes it could, or should, have done.

\n

III

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Determining what the California Supreme Court has \xe2\x80\x9cactually\xe2\x80\x9d decided is sometimes easy and sometimes difficult. Its rulings denying habeas corpus petitions generally fall into three broad categories: those expressly deciding the timeliness question, those deciding the merits without comment on timeliness, and those that do not disclose the basis for the decision.[Footnote 3] To simplify the inquiry, a straightforward rule can be applied to each type of order.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The easiest cases, of course, are those in which the state court-order expressly states that a petition was either untimely or timely. As we have explained, if the state court\xe2\x80\x99s untimeliness ruling is clear, \xe2\x80\x9cthat would be the end of the matter,\xe2\x80\x9d even if the court had also ruled on the merits. Saffold, 536 U. S., at 226. Conversely, an unequivocal holding that a delay was not unreasonable should be respected even if a federal judge would have decided the issue differently.[Footnote 4] The decision that a petition has been untimely filed need not be explicitly stated; citation to a case in which a petition was dismissed as untimely filed certainly would suffice.[Footnote 5] Cf. Brief for Petitioner 27; Robbins, 18 Cal. 4th, at 814, n. 34, 959 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 340, n. 34 (explaining California\xe2\x80\x99s practice of citing certain cases for certain propositions).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0More difficult are those cases in which the state court rules on the merits without any comment on timeliness. The Ninth Circuit deals with this situation by applying the presumption that a ruling on the merits, simpliciter, means that the state court has concluded that the petition was timely. The Court today seemingly assumes\xe2\x80\x94incorrectly\xe2\x80\x94that we rejected that presumption in Saffold. Even if we did so sub silentio, however, I am convinced that the Court should now endorse the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s presumption because it is both eminently sensible as a matter of judicial administration and entirely sound as a matter of law. Cf. Robbins, 18 Cal. 4th, at 814, n. 34, 959 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 340, n. 34 (explaining that when the State argues that a procedural bar applies, and the California Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s order does not cite a case imposing that bar, it means the claim is not barred on the asserted ground). The interest in the efficient processing of the dockets of overworked federal judges provides powerful support for relying on a presumption rather than engaging in de\xc2\xa0novo review of the questions whether the length of a delay was excessive, whether the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s explanation for the delay would be considered acceptable by a California court, and whether a nonetheless unreasonable delay should be excused because the petition raises an unusually serious constitutional question. Cf. id., at 779\xe2\x80\x93782, 959 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 317\xe2\x80\x93318.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There are, of course, cases in which the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s presumption may not be accurate. For example, a state court may find the deficiencies in a claim so clear that it is easier to deny it on the merits than to decide whether excuses for an apparently unreasonable delay are sufficient. But whereas California judges may continue to follow the easier route, under today\xe2\x80\x99s holding federal judges apparently must answer the timeliness question no matter how difficult it may be and no matter how easy it is to resolve the merits. A simple rule, applicable to all unambiguous rulings on the merits, is surely far wiser than the novel ad hoc approach that the Court appears to endorse today.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A general rule could also apply to the most difficult situation, which arises when the state court denies a petition with no explanation or citation whatsoever. Unlike an order that indicates that a state court has ruled on the merits, a silent order provides no evidence that the state court considered and passed upon the timeliness issue. To resolve such cases, I would adopt a presumption that, if a California court issues an unexplained order denying a petition filed after a delay of less than six months, the court considered that petition to be timely; unexplained orders following a longer delay should be presumed to be decisions on timeliness grounds. California\xe2\x80\x99s use of a 6-month period for determining presumptive timeliness in postconviction capital litigation\xe2\x80\x94the only specific time period mentioned in California\xe2\x80\x99s postconviction jurisprudence\xe2\x80\x94provides a principled basis for such a double-barreled presumption. See Cal. Rules of Court Policy Statement 3, std. 1\xe2\x80\x931.1 (Deering 2005) (\xe2\x80\x9cA petition for a writ of habeas corpus [in a capital case] will be presumed to be filed without substantial delay if it is filed within 180 days after the final due date for the filing of appellant\xe2\x80\x99s reply brief on the direct appeal\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xe2\x80\x9d). Moreover, a 6-month presumption would be fully consistent with our holding in Saffold that the 412-month delay in that case was not necessarily unreasonable.[Footnote 6]

\n

IV

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0 The above standards provide me with two independently sufficient reasons for concluding that the California Supreme Court actually decided\xe2\x80\x94not once, but twice\xe2\x80\x94that the petitions filed by respondent in that court were untimely. In one order, the State Supreme Court made its finding of untimeliness explicit; in the other, the 6-month presumption should control.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, as the Court notes ante, at 5, the California Supreme Court entered an order denying respondent habeas relief on April 29, 1998, and respondent did not file his federal petition for habeas corpus until August 30, 2000\xe2\x80\x94more than a year later. The Court of Appeals found that the federal statute of limitations was tolled during this 16-month period by a second set of state habeas petitions that respondent initiated in the California trial court on January 25, 1999, and that concluded with the entry of an order by the California Supreme Court on April 28, 2000. See App. A to Pet. for Cert. 11\xe2\x80\x9312. That finding was erroneous.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The California Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s April 28, 2000, order, unlike its 1998 order, was not silent. Instead, the April 2000 order cited three earlier California Supreme Court cases, two of which stand for the proposition that a petition has been untimely filed. See id., at 5; Robbins, 18 Cal. 4th, at 814, n. 34, 959 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 340, n. 34. Although the State did not argue that respondent\xe2\x80\x99s second habeas filing in the California Supreme Court was untimely, see App. A to Pet. for Cert.\xc2\xa08, n.\xc2\xa03, there is not even an arguable basis for disputing that the California Supreme Court found respondent\xe2\x80\x99s second habeas petition to have been untimely filed. Given this finding by the State Supreme Court, the Ninth Circuit clearly erred (although not for the reasons claimed by the Court).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, respondent\xe2\x80\x99s November 5, 1997, state habeas petition was filed with the California Supreme Court more than three years after the California Court of Appeal denied review. Ante, at 5. The State Supreme Court denied that petition without explanation. Ibid. The presumption I described above\xe2\x80\x94that an unexplained order following a delay longer than six months was based on the state court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that the petition was untimely\xe2\x80\x94provides me with a sufficient reason for concluding that respondent\xe2\x80\x99s state habeas petition was not pending during that 3-year interval. Consequently, respondent\xe2\x80\x99s federal habeas petition was also untimely and should have been denied.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, despite my profound disagreement with the reasoning in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, I concur in its judgment.
