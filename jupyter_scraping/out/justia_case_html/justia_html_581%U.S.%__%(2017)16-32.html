KINDRED NURSING CENTERS LIMITED PARTNERSHIP, dba WINCHESTER CENTRE FOR HEALTH AND REHABILITATION, nka FOUNTAIN CIRCLE HEALTH AND REHABILITATION, et\xc2\xa0al. v. CLARK et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the supreme court of kentucky

\n

No. 16\xe2\x80\x9332.\xe2\x80\x83Argued February 22, 2017\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 15, 2017

\n

Respondents Beverly Wellner and Janis Clark\xe2\x80\x94the wife and daughter, respectively, of Joe Wellner and Olive Clark\xe2\x80\x94each held a power of attorney affording her broad authority to manage her family member\xe2\x80\x99s affairs. When Joe and Olive moved into a nursing home operated by petitioner Kindred Nursing Centers L.\xc2\xa0P., Beverly and Janis used their powers of attorney to complete all necessary paperwork. As part of that process, each signed an arbitration agreement on her relative\xe2\x80\x99s behalf providing that any claims arising from the relative\xe2\x80\x99s stay at the facility would be resolved through binding arbitration. After Joe and Olive died, their estates (represented by Beverly and Janis) filed suits alleging that Kindred\xe2\x80\x99s substandard care had caused their deaths. Kindred moved to dismiss the cases, arguing that the arbitration agreements prohibited bringing the disputes to court. The trial court denied Kindred\xe2\x80\x99s motions, and the Kentucky Court of Appeals agreed that the suits could go forward.

\n

The Kentucky Supreme Court consolidated the cases and affirmed. The court initially found that the language of the Wellner power of attorney did not permit Beverly to enter into an arbitration agreement on Joe\xe2\x80\x99s behalf, but that the Clark document gave Janis the capacity to do so on behalf of Olive. Nonetheless, the court held, both arbitration agreements were invalid because neither power of attorney specifically entitled the representative to enter into an arbitration agreement. Because the Kentucky Constitution declares the rights of access to the courts and trial by jury to be \xe2\x80\x9csacred\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cinviolate,\xe2\x80\x9d the court determined, an agent could deprive her principal of such rights only if expressly provided in the power of attorney.

\n

Held: The Kentucky Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s clear-statement rule violates the Federal Arbitration Act by singling out arbitration agreements for disfavored treatment. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

(a) The FAA, which makes arbitration agreements \xe2\x80\x9cvalid, irrevocable, and enforceable, save upon such grounds as exist at law or in equity for the revocation of any contract,\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n9 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72, establishes an equal-treatment principle: A court may invalidate an arbitration agreement based on \xe2\x80\x9cgenerally applicable contract defenses,\xe2\x80\x9d but not on legal rules that \xe2\x80\x9capply only to arbitration or that derive their meaning from the fact that an agreement to arbitrate is at issue,\xe2\x80\x9d AT&T Mobility LLC v. Concepcion,\n\n563 U.\xc2\xa0S. 333\n. The Act thus preempts any state rule that discriminates on its face against arbitration or that covertly accomplishes the same objective by disfavoring contracts that have the defining features of arbitration agreements.

\n

The Kentucky Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s clear-statement rule fails to put arbitration agreements on an equal plane with other contracts. By requiring an explicit statement before an agent can relinquish her principal\xe2\x80\x99s right to go to court and receive a jury trial, the court did exactly what this Court has barred: adopt a legal rule hinging on the primary characteristic of an arbitration agreement. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

(b) In support of the decision below, respondents argue that the clear-statement rule affects only contract formation, and that the FAA does not apply to contract formation questions. But the Act\xe2\x80\x99s text says otherwise. The FAA cares not only about the \xe2\x80\x9cenforce[ment]\xe2\x80\x9d of arbitration agreements, but also about their initial \xe2\x80\x9cvalid[ity]\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94that is, about what it takes to enter into them.\n\n9 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72. Precedent confirms the point. In Concepcion, the Court noted the impermissibility of applying a contract defense like duress \xe2\x80\x9cin a fashion that disfavors arbitration.\xe2\x80\x9d 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 341. That discussion would have made no sense if the FAA had nothing to say about contract formation, because duress involves \xe2\x80\x9cunfair dealing at the contract formation stage.\xe2\x80\x9d Morgan Stanley Capital Group Inc. v. Public Util. Dist. No. 1 of Snohomish Cty.,\n\n554 U.\xc2\xa0S. 527\n. Finally, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 view would make it trivially easy for States to undermine the Act. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

(c) Because the Kentucky Supreme Court invalidated the Clark-Kindred arbitration agreement based exclusively on the clear-statement rule, the court must now enforce that agreement. But because it is unclear whether the court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the Wellner document was wholly independent of its rule, the court should determine on remand whether it adheres, in the absence of the rule, to its prior reading of that power of attorney. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

478 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d 306, reversed in part, vacated in part, and remanded.

KINDRED NURSING CENTERS LIMITED PARTNER-SHIP, dba WINCHESTER CENTRE FOR HEALTH AND REHABILITATION, nka FOUNTAIN CIRCLE HEALTH AND REHABILITA-TION, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. JANIS E. CLARK et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of kentucky

\n

[May 15, 2017]

\n

Justice Kagan delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

The Federal Arbitration Act (FAA or Act) requires courts to place arbitration agreements \xe2\x80\x9con equal footing with all other contracts.\xe2\x80\x9d DIRECTV, Inc. v. Imburgia, 577 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2015) (slip op., at 6) (quoting Buckeye Check Cashing, Inc. v. Cardegna,\n\n546 U.\xc2\xa0S. 440,\n443 (2006)\n); see\n\n9 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72. In the decision below, the Kentucky Supreme Court declined to give effect to two arbitration agreements executed by individuals holding \xe2\x80\x9cpowers of attorney\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94that is, authorizations to act on behalf of others. According to the court, a general grant of power (even if seemingly comprehensive) does not permit a legal representative to enter into an arbitration agreement for someone else; to form such a contract, the representative must possess specific authority to \xe2\x80\x9cwaive his principal\xe2\x80\x99s fundamental constitutional rights to access the courts [and] to trial by jury.\xe2\x80\x9d Extendicare Homes, Inc. v. Whisman, 478 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d 306, 327 (2015). Because that rule singles out arbitration agreements for disfavored treatment, we hold that it violates the FAA.

\n

I

\n

Petitioner Kindred Nursing Centers L.\xc2\xa0P. operates nursing homes and rehabilitation centers. Respondents Beverly Wellner and Janis Clark are the wife and daughter, respectively, of Joe Wellner and Olive Clark, two now-deceased residents of a Kindred nursing home called the Winchester Centre.

\n

At all times relevant to this case, Beverly and Janis each held a power of attorney, designating her as an \xe2\x80\x9cattorney-in-fact\xe2\x80\x9d (the one for Joe, the other for Olive) and affording her broad authority to manage her family member\xe2\x80\x99s affairs. In the Wellner power of attorney, Joe gave Beverly the authority, \xe2\x80\x9cin my name, place and stead,\xe2\x80\x9d to (among other things) \xe2\x80\x9cinstitute legal proceedings\xe2\x80\x9d and make \xe2\x80\x9ccontracts of every nature in relation to both real and personal property.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 10\xe2\x80\x9311. In the Clark power of attorney, Olive provided Janis with \xe2\x80\x9cfull power .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to transact, handle, and dispose of all matters affecting me and/or my estate in any possible way,\xe2\x80\x9d including the power to \xe2\x80\x9cdraw, make, and sign in my name any and all .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. contracts, deeds, or agreements.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 7.

\n

Joe and Olive moved into the Winchester Centre in 2008, with Beverly and Janis using their powers of attorney to complete all necessary paperwork. As part of that process, Beverly and Janis each signed an arbitration agreement with Kindred on behalf of her relative. The two contracts, worded identically, provided that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny and all claims or controversies arising out of or in any way relating to .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the Resident\xe2\x80\x99s stay at the Facility\xe2\x80\x9d would be resolved through \xe2\x80\x9cbinding arbitration\xe2\x80\x9d rather than a lawsuit. Id., at 14, 21.

\n

When Joe and Olive died the next year, their estates (represented again by Beverly and Janis) brought separate suits against Kindred in Kentucky state court. The complaints alleged that Kindred had delivered substandard care to Joe and Olive, causing their deaths. Kindred moved to dismiss the cases, arguing that the arbitration agreements Beverly and Janis had signed prohibited bringing their disputes to court. But the trial court denied Kindred\xe2\x80\x99s motions, and the Kentucky Court of Appeals agreed that the estates\xe2\x80\x99 suits could go forward. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 125a\xe2\x80\x93126a, 137a\xe2\x80\x93138a.

\n

The Kentucky Supreme Court, after consolidating the cases, affirmed those decisions by a divided vote. See 478 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 313. The court began with the language of the two powers of attorney. The Wellner document, the court stated, did not permit Beverly to enter into an arbitration agreement on Joe\xe2\x80\x99s behalf. In the court\xe2\x80\x99s view, neither the provision authorizing her to bring legal proceedings nor the one enabling her to make property-related contracts reached quite that distance. See id., at 325\xe2\x80\x93326; supra, at 2. By contrast, the court thought, the Clark power of attorney extended that far and beyond. Under that document, after all, Janis had the capacity to \xe2\x80\x9cdispose of all matters\xe2\x80\x9d affecting Olive. See supra, at 2. \xe2\x80\x9cGiven this extremely broad, universal delegation of authority,\xe2\x80\x9d the court acknowledged, \xe2\x80\x9cit would be impossible to say that entering into [an] arbitration agreement was not covered.\xe2\x80\x9d 478 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 327.

\n

And yet, the court went on, both arbitration agreements\xe2\x80\x94Janis\xe2\x80\x99s no less than Beverly\xe2\x80\x99s\xe2\x80\x94were invalid. That was because a power of attorney could not entitle a representative to enter into an arbitration agreement without specifically saying so. The Kentucky Constitution, the court explained, protects the rights of access to the courts and trial by jury; indeed, the jury guarantee is the sole right the Constitution declares \xe2\x80\x9csacred\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cinviolate.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 328\xe2\x80\x93329. Accordingly, the court held, an agent could deprive her principal of an \xe2\x80\x9cadjudication by judge or jury\xe2\x80\x9d only if the power of attorney \xe2\x80\x9cexpressly so provide[d].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 329. And that clear-statement rule\xe2\x80\x94so said the court\xe2\x80\x94complied with the FAA\xe2\x80\x99s demands. True enough that the Act precludes \xe2\x80\x9csingl[ing] out arbitration agreements.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted). But that was no problem, the court asserted, because its rule would apply not just to those agreements, but also to some other contracts implicating \xe2\x80\x9cfundamental constitutional rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 328. In the future, for example, the court would bar the holder of a \xe2\x80\x9cnon-specific\xe2\x80\x9d power of attorney from entering into a contract \xe2\x80\x9cbind[ing] the principal to personal servitude.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

Justice Abramson dissented, in an opinion joined by two of her colleagues. In their view, the Kentucky Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s new clear-statement rule was \xe2\x80\x9cclearly not .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. applicable to \xe2\x80\x98any contract\xe2\x80\x99 but [instead] single[d] out arbitration agreements for disfavored treatment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 344\xe2\x80\x93345. Accordingly, the dissent concluded, the rule \xe2\x80\x9cr[a]n afoul of the FAA.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 353.

\n

We granted certiorari. 580 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2016).

\n

II

\n

A

\n

The FAA makes arbitration agreements \xe2\x80\x9cvalid, irrevocable, and enforceable, save upon such grounds as exist at law or in equity for the revocation of any contract.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n9 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72. That statutory provision establishes an equal-treatment principle: A court may invalidate an arbitration agreement based on \xe2\x80\x9cgenerally applicable contract defenses\xe2\x80\x9d like fraud or unconscionability, but not on legal rules that \xe2\x80\x9capply only to arbitration or that derive their meaning from the fact that an agreement to arbitrate is at issue.\xe2\x80\x9d AT&T Mobility LLC v. Concepcion,\n\n563 U.\xc2\xa0S. 333,\n339 (2011)\n. The FAA thus preempts any state rule discriminating on its face against arbitration\xe2\x80\x94for example, a \xe2\x80\x9claw prohibit[ing] outright the arbitration of a particular type of claim.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 341. And not only that: The Act also displaces any rule that covertly accomplishes the same objective by disfavoring contracts that (oh so coinciden-tally) have the defining features of arbitration agreements. In Concepcion, for example, we described a hypothetical state law declaring unenforceable any contract that \xe2\x80\x9cdisallow[ed] an ultimate disposition [of a dispute] by a jury.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 342. Such a law might avoid referring to arbitration by name; but still, we explained, it would \xe2\x80\x9crely on the uniqueness of an agreement to arbitrate as [its] basis\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94and thereby violate the FAA. Id., at 341 (quoting Perry v. Thomas,\n\n482 U.\xc2\xa0S. 483\n, n.\xc2\xa09 (1987)).

\n

The Kentucky Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s clear-statement rule, in just that way, fails to put arbitration agreements on an equal plane with other contracts. By the court\xe2\x80\x99s own account, that rule (like the one Concepcion posited) serves to safeguard a person\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cright to access the courts and to trial by jury.\xe2\x80\x9d 478 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 327; see supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x934. In ringing terms, the court affirmed the jury right\xe2\x80\x99s unsurpassed standing in the State Constitution: The framers, the court explained, recognized \xe2\x80\x9cthat right and that right alone as a divine God-given right\xe2\x80\x9d when they made it \xe2\x80\x9cthe only thing\xe2\x80\x9d that must be \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98held sacred\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98inviolate.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 478 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 328\xe2\x80\x93329 (quoting Ky. Const. \xc2\xa77). So it was that the court required an explicit statement before an attorney-in-fact, even if possessing broad delegated powers, could relinquish that right on another\xe2\x80\x99s behalf. See 478 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 331 (\xe2\x80\x9cWe say only that an agent\xe2\x80\x99s authority to waive his principal\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional right to access the courts and to trial by jury must be clearly expressed by the principal\xe2\x80\x9d). And so it was that the court did exactly what Concepcion barred: adopt a legal rule hinging on the primary characteristic of an arbitration agreement\xe2\x80\x94namely, a waiver of the right to go to court and receive a jury trial. See 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 341\xe2\x80\x93342; see also 478 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 353 (Abramson, J., dissenting) (noting that the jury-trial right at the core of \xe2\x80\x9cthe majority\xe2\x80\x99s new rule\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cthe one right that just happens to be correlative to the right to arbitrate\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis deleted)). Such a rule is too tailor-made to arbitration agreements\xe2\x80\x94subjecting them, by virtue of their defining trait, to uncommon barriers\xe2\x80\x94to survive the FAA\xe2\x80\x99s edict against singling out those contracts for disfavored treatment.[1]

\n

And the state court\xe2\x80\x99s sometime-attempt to cast the rule in broader terms cannot salvage its decision. The clear-statement requirement, the court suggested, could also apply when an agent endeavored to waive other \xe2\x80\x9cfundamental constitutional rights\xe2\x80\x9d held by a principal. 478 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 331; see supra, at 4. But what other rights, really? No Kentucky court, so far as we know, has ever before demanded that a power of attorney explicitly confer authority to enter into contracts implicating constitutional guarantees. Nor did the opinion below indicate that such a grant would be needed for the many routine contracts\xe2\x80\x94executed day in and day out by legal representatives\xe2\x80\x94meeting that description. For example, the Kentucky Constitution protects the \xe2\x80\x9cinherent and inalienable\xe2\x80\x9d rights to \xe2\x80\x9cacquir[e] and protect[\xc2\xa0] property\xe2\x80\x9d and to \xe2\x80\x9cfreely communicat[e] thoughts and opinions.\xe2\x80\x9d Ky. Const. \xc2\xa71. But the state court nowhere cautioned that an attorney-in-fact would now need a specific authorization to, say, sell her principal\xe2\x80\x99s furniture or commit her principal to a non-disclosure agreement. (And were we in the business of giving legal advice, we would tell the agent not to worry.) Rather, the court hypothesized a slim set of both patently objectionable and utterly fanciful contracts that would be subject to its rule: No longer could a representative lacking explicit authorization waive her \xe2\x80\x9cprincipal\xe2\x80\x99s right to worship freely\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cconsent to an arranged marriage\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cbind [her] principal to personal servitude.\xe2\x80\x9d 478 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 328; see supra, at 4. Placing arbitration agreements within that class reveals the kind of \xe2\x80\x9chostility to arbitration\xe2\x80\x9d that led Congress to enact the FAA. Concepcion, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 339. And doing so only makes clear the arbitration-specific character of the rule, much as if it were made applicable to arbitration agreements and black swans.[2]

\n

B

\n

The respondents, Janis and Beverly, primarily advance a different argument\xe2\x80\x94based on the distinction between contract formation and contract enforcement\xe2\x80\x94to support the decision below. Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s clear-statement rule, they begin, affects only contract formation, because it bars agents without explicit authority from entering into arbitration agreements. And in their view, the FAA has \xe2\x80\x9cno application\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9ccontract formation issues.\xe2\x80\x9d Supp. Brief for Respondents 1. The Act, to be sure, requires a State to enforce all arbitration agreements (save on generally applicable grounds) once they have come into being. But, the respondents claim, States have free rein to decide\xe2\x80\x94irrespective of the FAA\xe2\x80\x99s equal-footing principle\xe2\x80\x94whether such contracts are validly created in the first instance. See id., at 3 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe FAA\xe2\x80\x99s statutory framework applies only after a court has determined that a valid arbitration agreement was formed\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Both the FAA\xe2\x80\x99s text and our case law interpreting it say otherwise. The Act\xe2\x80\x99s key provision, once again, states that an arbitration agreement must ordinarily be treated as \xe2\x80\x9cvalid, irrevocable, and enforceable.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n9 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72; see supra, at 4. By its terms, then, the Act cares not only about the \xe2\x80\x9cenforce[ment]\xe2\x80\x9d of arbitration agreements, but also about their initial \xe2\x80\x9cvalid[ity]\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94that is, about what it takes to enter into them. Or said otherwise: A rule selectively finding arbitration contracts invalid because improperly formed fares no better under the Act than a rule selectively refusing to enforce those agreements once properly made. Precedent confirms that point. In Concepcion, we noted the impermissibility of applying a contract defense like duress \xe2\x80\x9cin a fashion that disfavors arbitration.\xe2\x80\x9d 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 341. But the doctrine of duress, as we have elsewhere explained, involves \xe2\x80\x9cunfair dealing at the contract formation stage.\xe2\x80\x9d Morgan Stanley Capital Group Inc. v. Public Util. Dist. No. 1 of Snohomish Cty.,\n\n554 U.\xc2\xa0S. 527,\n547 (2008)\n. Our discussion of duress would have made no sense if the FAA, as the respondents contend, had nothing to say about contract formation.

\n

And still more: Adopting the respondents\xe2\x80\x99 view would make it trivially easy for States to undermine the Act\xe2\x80\x94indeed, to wholly defeat it. As the respondents have acknowledged, their reasoning would allow States to pronounce any attorney-in-fact incapable of signing an arbitration agreement\xe2\x80\x94even if a power of attorney specifically authorized her to do so. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 27. (After all, such a rule would speak to only the contract\xe2\x80\x99s formation.) And why stop there? If the respondents were right, States could just as easily declare everyone incompetent to sign arbitration agreements. (That rule too would address only formation.) The FAA would then mean nothing at all\xe2\x80\x94its provisions rendered helpless to prevent even the most blatant discrimination against arbitration.

\n

III

\n

As we did just last Term, we once again \xe2\x80\x9creach a conclusion that .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. falls well within the confines of (and goes no further than) present well-established law.\xe2\x80\x9d DIRECTV, 577 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 10). The Kentucky Supreme Court specially impeded the ability of attorneys-in-fact to enter into arbitration agreements. The court thus flouted the FAA\xe2\x80\x99s command to place those agreements on an equal footing with all other contracts.

\n

Our decision requires reversing the Kentucky Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment in favor of the Clark estate. As noted earlier, the state court held that the Clark power of attorney was sufficiently broad to cover executing an arbitration agreement. See supra, at 3. The court invalidated the agreement with Kindred only because the power of attorney did not specifically authorize Janis to enter into it on Olive\xe2\x80\x99s behalf. In other words, the decision below was based exclusively on the clear-statement rule that we have held violates the FAA. So the court must now enforce the Clark-Kindred arbitration agreement.

\n

By contrast, our decision might not require such a result in the Wellner case. The Kentucky Supreme Court began its opinion by stating that the Wellner power of attorney was insufficiently broad to give Beverly the authority to execute an arbitration agreement for Joe. See supra, at 3. If that interpretation of the document is wholly independent of the court\xe2\x80\x99s clear-statement rule, then nothing we have said disturbs it. But if that rule at all influenced the construction of the Wellner power of attorney, then the court must evaluate the document\xe2\x80\x99s meaning anew. The court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion leaves us uncertain as to whether such an impermissible taint occurred. We therefore vacate the judgment below and return the case to the state court for further consideration. See Marmet Health Care Center, Inc. v. Brown,\n\n565 U.\xc2\xa0S. 530,\n534 (2012)\n (per curiam) (vacating and remanding another arbitration decision because we could not tell \xe2\x80\x9cto what degree [an] alternative holding was influenced by\xe2\x80\x9d the state court\xe2\x80\x99s erroneous, arbitration-specific rule). On remand, the court should determine whether it adheres, in the absence of its clear-statement rule, to its prior reading of the Wellner power of attorney.

\n

For these reasons, we reverse in part and vacate in part the judgment of the Kentucky Supreme Court, and we remand the case for further proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n\n1\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0Making matters worse, the Kentucky Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s clear-statement rule appears not to apply to other kinds of agreements relinquishing the right to go to court or obtain a jury trial. Nothing in the decision below (or elsewhere in Kentucky law) suggests that explicit authorization is needed before an attorney-in-fact can sign a settlement agreement or consent to a bench trial on her principal\xe2\x80\x99s behalf. See 478 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 325 (discussing the Wellner power of attorney\xe2\x80\x99s provision for \xe2\x80\x9cmanaging a claim in litigation\xe2\x80\x9d without insisting that such commitments would require a clearer grant). Mark that as yet another indication that the court\xe2\x80\x99s demand for specificity in powers of attorney arises from the suspect status of arbitration rather than the sacred status of jury trials.\n\t\n
\n\n2\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0We do not suggest that a state court is precluded from announcing a new, generally applicable rule of law in an arbitration case. We simply reiterate here what we have said many times before\xe2\x80\x94that the rule must in fact apply generally, rather than single out arbitration.\n\t\n

I continue to adhere to the view that the Federal Arbitration Act (FAA),\n\n9 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71 et\xc2\xa0seq., does not apply to proceedings in state courts. See Allied-Bruce Terminix Cos. v. Dobson,\n\n513 U.\xc2\xa0S. 265\n\xe2\x80\x93297 (1995) (Thomas, J., dissenting); see also DIRECTV, Inc. v. Imburgia, 577 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2015) (same) (slip op., at 1); Preston v. Ferrer,\n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 346,\n363 (2008)\n (same); Buckeye Check Cashing, Inc. v. Cardegna,\n\n546 U.\xc2\xa0S. 440,\n449 (2006)\n (same); Green Tree Financial Corp. v. Bazzle,\n\n539 U.\xc2\xa0S. 444,\n460 (2003)\n (same); Doctor\xe2\x80\x99s Associates, Inc. v. Casarotto,\n\n517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 681,\n689 (1996)\n (same). In state-court proceedings, therefore, the FAA does not displace a rule that requires express authorization from a principal before an agent may waive the principal\xe2\x80\x99s right to a jury trial. Accordingly, I would affirm the judgment of the Kentucky Supreme Court.
