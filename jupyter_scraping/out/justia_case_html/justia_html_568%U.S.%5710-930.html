\n\t\t\t\tSyllabus\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tRYAN, DIRECTOR, ARIZONA DEPARTMENT OF CORRECTIONS v. VALENCIA GONZALES\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 10\xe2\x80\x93930.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 9, 2012\xe2\x80\x94Decided January 8, 2013\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tRespondent Valencia Gonzales, a death row inmate in Arizona, sought federal habeas relief. His counsel moved to stay the proceedings, contending that Gonzales\xe2\x80\x99 mental incompetence prevented him from rationally communicating with or assisting counsel, and that Gonzales was thus entitled to a stay because, under the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s Rohan decision, what is now \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73599(a)(2) requires a stay when a petitioner is adjudged incompetent. The District Court denied a stay, finding that the claims before it were record based or resolvable as a matter of law and thus would not benefit from Gonzales\xe2\x80\x99 input. Gonzales thereafter sought a writ of mandamus in the Ninth Circuit. Applying Rohan and its recent decision in Nash\xe2\x80\x94which gave habeas petitioners a right to competence even on record-based appeals\xe2\x80\x94the court granted the writ, concluding that \xc2\xa73599 gave Gonzales the right to a stay pending a competency determination.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondent Sean Carter, a death row inmate in Ohio, initiated federal habeas proceedings but eventually moved for a competency determination and stay of the proceedings. The District Court granted the motion and found Carter incompetent to assist counsel. Applying the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s Rohan test, it determined that Carter\xe2\x80\x99s assistance was required to develop four of his exhausted claims. It thus dismissed his habeas petition without prejudice and prospectively tolled the statute of limitations. On appeal, the Sixth Circuit, relying in part on Rees v. Peyton, \n\n384 U.\xc2\xa0S. 312\n (Rees I), located a statutory right to competence in \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa74241, and found that a court could employ that provision whenever a capital habeas petitioner seeks to forgo his petition. It thus ordered that Carter\xe2\x80\x99s petition be stayed indefinitely with respect to any claims requiring his assistance. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t1.\xc2\xa0Section 3599 does not provide a state prisoner a right to suspension of his federal habeas proceedings when he is adjudged incompetent. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9312.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0The assertion of such a right lacks any basis in the provision\xe2\x80\x99s text. Section 3599 guarantees federal habeas petitioners on death row the right to federally funded counsel, \xc2\xa73599(a)(2), and sets out various requirements that appointed counsel must meet, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73599(b)\xe2\x80\x93(e), but it does not direct district courts to stay proceedings when petitioners are found incompetent. The assertion is also difficult to square with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional precedents. If the \n \nSixth Amendment right carried with it an implied right to competence, the right to competence at trial would flow from that Amendment, not from the right to due process, see Cooper v. Oklahoma, \n\n517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 348\n. But while the benefits flowing from the right to counsel at trial could be affected if an incompetent defendant is unable to communicate with his attorney, this Court has never said that the right to competence derives from the right to counsel. And the Court will not assume or infer that Congress intended to depart from such precedent and locate a right to competence in federal habeas proceedings within the right to counsel. See Merck & Co. v. Reynolds, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x937.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit identified its rule in Rohan, concluding there that a petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s mental incompetency could \xe2\x80\x9ceviscerate the statutory right to counsel\xe2\x80\x9d in federal habeas proceedings. But given the backward-looking, record-based nature of \xc2\xa72254 proceedings, counsel can generally provide effective representation to a habeas petitioner regardless of the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s competence. Rees I, supra, Rees v. Peyton, \n\n386 U.\xc2\xa0S. 989\n, and Rees v. Superintendent of the Va. State Penitentiary, 516 U.\xc2\xa0S 802, which involved an incompetent death row inmate\xe2\x80\x99s attempt to withdraw his certiorari petition, offer no support for federal habeas petitioners seeking to stay district court proceedings or for the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s opinions in Rohan, Nash, or this case. The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation is also not supported by McFarland v. Scott, \n\n512 U.\xc2\xa0S. 849\n, in which this Court held that a district court could stay an execution after a capital prisoner had invoked his right to counsel but before he had filed his habeas petition. In contrast, Gonzales is seeking to stay the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s proceedings, and he sought a stay more than six years after initiating his habeas petition, certainly ample time for his attorney to research and present the claims. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9312.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t2.\xc2\xa0Section 4241 also does not provide a statutory right to competence during federal habeas proceedings. The Sixth Circuit based its conclusion largely on a misreading of Rees I, which did not recognize such a right. Moreover, \xc2\xa74241 does not even apply to habeas proceedings. By its terms, it applies only to trial proceedings prior to sentencing and \xe2\x80\x9cat any time after the commencement of probation or supervised release.\xe2\x80\x9d Federal habeas proceedings, however, commence after sentencing, and federal habeas petitioners are incarcerated, not on probation. Furthermore, \xc2\xa74241, like the rest of Title 18 generally, applies exclusively to federal defendants, not to state prisoners like Carter. Finally, \xc2\xa74241(a) authorizes a district court to grant a motion for a competency determination if there is reasonable cause to believe that the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s mental incompetence renders him \xe2\x80\x9cunable to understand .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the proceedings against him or to assist properly in his defense,\xe2\x80\x9d while a \xc2\xa72254 habeas proceeding is a civil action against a state-prison warden, in which the petitioner collaterally attacks his conviction in an earlier state trial. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9314.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t3.\xc2\xa0For purposes of resolving these cases, it is sufficient to address the outer limits of the district court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion to issue stays; it is unnecessary to determine the precise contours of that discretion. In Gonzales\xe2\x80\x99 case, the District Court did not abuse its discretion in denying a stay after finding that Gonzales\xe2\x80\x99 claims were all record based or resolvable as a matter of law, regardless of his competence. Review of a petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s record-based claims subject to \xc2\xa72254(d) is limited to the record before the state court that heard the case on the merits. Any evidence that Gonzales might have would be inadmissible. In Carter\xe2\x80\x99s case, three of his claims do not warrant a stay because they were adjudicated on the merits in state postconviction proceedings and thus subject to review under \xc2\xa72254(d). Thus, extrarecord evidence that he might have concerning these claims would be inadmissible. It is unclear from the record whether he exhausted his fourth claim. If it was exhausted, it too would be record based. But even if it was both unexhausted and not procedurally defaulted, an indefinite stay would be inappropriate, since such a stay would permit petitioners to \xe2\x80\x9cfrustrate [the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996\xe2\x80\x99s] goal of finality by dragging out indefinitely their federal habeas review.\xe2\x80\x9d Rhines v. Weber, \n\n544 U.\xc2\xa0S. 269\n\xe2\x80\x93278. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9318.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t623 F.\xc2\xa03d 1242, No. 10\xe2\x80\x93930, reversed; 644 F.\xc2\xa03d 329, No. 11\xe2\x80\x93218, reversed and remanded.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Thomas delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThese two cases present the question whether the incompetence of a state prisoner requires suspension of the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s federal habeas corpus proceedings. We hold that neither \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73599 nor \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa74241 provides such a right and that the Courts of Appeals for the Ninth and Sixth Circuits both erred in holding that district courts must stay federal habeas proceedings when petitioners are adjudged incompetent.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tErnest Valencia Gonzales was convicted by an Arizona jury of felony murder, armed robbery, aggravated assault, first-degree burglary, and theft. The convictions arose from Gonzales\xe2\x80\x99 repeated stabbing of Darrel and Deborah Wagner in front of their 7-year-old son during a burglary of the Wagners\xe2\x80\x99 home. Darrel Wagner died from the stabbing, while Deborah Wagner survived but spent five days in intensive care. The trial court sentenced Gonzales to death on the murder charge and to various prison terms for the other crimes.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAfter exhausting state remedies, Gonzales filed a petition for a writ of habeas corpus in Federal District Court on November 15, 1999. While the petition was pending, Gonzales\xe2\x80\x99 appointed counsel moved to stay the proceedings, contending that Gonzales was no longer capable of rationally communicating with or assisting counsel. He argued that mental incompetence entitled Gonzales to a stay under Ninth Circuit precedent. See Rohan v. Woodford, 334 F.\xc2\xa03d 803 (2003). In Rohan, the Ninth Circuit held that the federal statute guaranteeing state capital prisoners a right to counsel in federal habeas proceedings, \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7848(q)(4)(B) (2000 ed.) (now codified as \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73599(a)(2)), could not \xe2\x80\x9cbe faithfully enforced unless courts ensure that a petitioner is competent,\xe2\x80\x9d 334 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 813. Rohan thus concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cwhere an incompetent capital habeas petitioner raises claims that could potentially benefit from his ability to communicate rationally, refusing to stay proceedings pending restoration of competence denies him his statutory right to assistance of counsel, whether or not counsel can identify with precision the information sought.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 819.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tApplying Rohan, the District Court denied a stay after concluding that the claims properly before it were record based or resolvable as a matter of law and thus would not benefit from Gonzales\xe2\x80\x99 input. The court found it unnec-essary to determine whether Gonzales was incompetent, though it did find that he possessed \xe2\x80\x9cat least a limited capacity for rational communication.\xe2\x80\x9d Gonzales v. Schriro, 617 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 849, 863 (Ariz. 2008).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tGonzales thereafter filed an emergency petition for a writ of mandamus in the Ninth Circuit. While Gonzales\xe2\x80\x99 petition was pending, the Ninth Circuit decided Nash v. Ryan, 581 F.\xc2\xa03d 1048 (2009), which held that habeas petitioners have a right to competence on appeal, even though appeals are entirely record based. Id., at 1050 (\xe2\x80\x9cWhile an appeal is record-based, that does not mean that a habeas petitioner in a capital case is relegated to a nonexistent role. Meaningful assistance of appellate counsel may require rational communication between counsel and a habeas petitioner\xe2\x80\x9d). Applying Nash and Rohan, the court granted the writ of mandamus, concluding that even though Gonzales\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cexhausted claims are record-based or legal in nature, he is entitled to a stay pending a competency determination\xe2\x80\x9d under \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73599. In\xc2\xa0re Gonzales, 623 F.\xc2\xa03d 1242, 1244 (2010).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe granted certiorari to determine whether \xc2\xa73599 provides a statutory right to competence in federal habeas proceedings. 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSean Carter was convicted by an Ohio jury of aggra-vated murder, aggravated robbery, and rape, and sentenced to death for anally raping his adoptive grandmother, Veader Prince, and stabbing her to death. After exhausting his state-court appeals, Carter initiated federal habeas proceedings on March 19, 2002, in the Northern District of Ohio. Carter eventually filed a third amended petition, along with a motion requesting a competency determi-nation and a stay of the proceedings. The District Court granted the motion.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFollowing several psychiatric evaluations and a com-petency determination, the District Court found Carter incompetent to assist counsel. Applying the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s test in Rohan, it determined that Carter\xe2\x80\x99s assistance was required to develop four of his exhausted claims. As a result, the court dismissed his habeas petition without prejudice and prospectively tolled the statute of limitations. Carter v. Bradshaw, 583 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 872, 884 (2008). The State appealed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Sixth Circuit acknowledged that \xe2\x80\x9c[f]ederal habeas petitioners facing the death penalty for state criminal convictions do not enjoy a constitutional right to competence.\xe2\x80\x9d Carter v. Bradshaw, 644 F.\xc2\xa03d 329, 332 (2011). It nevertheless located a statutory right to competence in \xc2\xa74241, relying, in part, on this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Rees v. Peyton, \n\n384 U.\xc2\xa0S. 312 (1966)\n (per\xc2\xa0curiam) (Rees I\xc2\xa0).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t 644 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 332. The Sixth Circuit explained:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cBy applying section 4241 to habeas actions, Rees addresses the situation where a habeas petitioner awaiting the death penalty may seek to forego any collateral attacks on his conviction or sentence, and defines a statutory right for the petitioner to be competent enough to (1) understand the nature and consequences of the proceedings against him, and (2) assist properly in his defense.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 333.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe court concluded that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]nytime a capital habeas petitioner affirmatively seeks to forego his habeas petition, whether by action or inaction, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a district court may employ section 4241.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 334.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe court therefore amended the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment and ordered that Carter\xe2\x80\x99s petition be stayed in-definitely with respect to any claims that required his assistance. Id., at 336\xe2\x80\x93337. Judge Rogers dissented, arguing that there was no constitutional or statutory basis for the court\xe2\x80\x99s decision. Id., at 337\xe2\x80\x93342.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe granted certiorari to determine whether \xc2\xa74241 provides a statutory right to competence in federal habeas proceedings. 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBoth the Ninth and Sixth Circuits have concluded that death row inmates pursuing federal habeas are entitled to a suspension of proceedings when found incompetent. The Ninth Circuit located this right in \xc2\xa73599, while the Sixth Circuit located it in \xc2\xa74241. Neither section provides such a right.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSection 3599(a)(2) guarantees federal habeas petitioners on death row the right to federally funded counsel.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t The statute provides that petitioners who are \xe2\x80\x9cfinancially unable to obtain adequate representation .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall be entitled to the appointment of one or more attorneys.\xe2\x80\x9d Appointed attorneys are required to have experience in death penalty litigation, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73599(b)\xe2\x80\x93(d), and, once appointed, are directed to \xe2\x80\x9crepresent the defendant throughout every subsequent stage of available judicial proceedings,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73599(e). The statute also gives district courts the power to authorize funding for \xe2\x80\x9cinvestigative, expert, or other services\xe2\x80\x9d as are \xe2\x80\x9creasonably necessary for the representation of the defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73599(f). But \xc2\xa73599 does not direct district courts to stay proceedings when habeas petitioners are found incompetent.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn addition to lacking any basis in the statutory text, the assertion that the right to counsel implies a right to competence is difficult to square with our constitutional precedents. The right to counsel is located in the \n \nSixth Amendment. (\xe2\x80\x9cIn all criminal prosecutions, the accused shall enjoy the right .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to have the Assistance of Counsel for his defence.\xe2\x80\x9d) If the right to counsel carried with it an implied right to competence, the right to competence at trial would flow from the \n \nSixth Amendment. But \xe2\x80\x9c[w]e have repeatedly and consistently recognized that \xe2\x80\x98the criminal trial of an incompetent defendant violates due process,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d not the \n \nSixth Amendment. Cooper v. Oklahoma, \n\n517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 348, \n354 (1996)\n (quoting Medina v. California, \n\n505 U.\xc2\xa0S. 437, \n453 (1992)\n; emphasis added); see also Drope v. Missouri, \n\n420 U.\xc2\xa0S. 162, \n172 (1975)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he failure to observe procedures adequate to protect a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s right not to be tried or convicted while incompetent to stand trial deprives him of his due process right to a fair trial\xe2\x80\x9d (citing Pate v. Robinson, \n\n383 U.\xc2\xa0S. 375, \n385 (1966)\n)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt stands to reason that the benefits flowing from the right to counsel at trial could be affected if an incompe-tent defendant is unable to communicate with his attorney. For example, an incompetent defendant would be unable to assist counsel in identifying witnesses and deciding on a trial strategy. For this reason, \xe2\x80\x9c[a] defendant may not be put to trial unless he \xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9chas sufficient present ability to consult with his lawyer with a reasonable degree of rational understanding .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [and] a rational as well as factual understanding of the proceedings against him.\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Cooper, supra, at 354 (quoting Dusky v. United States, \n\n362 U.\xc2\xa0S. 402 (1960)\n (per curiam)). Notwithstanding the connection between the right to competence at trial and the right to counsel at trial, we have never said that the right to competence derives from the right to counsel. We will not assume or infer that Congress intended to depart from our precedents and locate a right to competence in federal habeas proceedings within the right to counsel. \xe2\x80\x9cWe normally assume that, when Congress enacts statutes, it is aware of relevant judicial precedent.\xe2\x80\x9d Merck & Co. v. Reynolds, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 12).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Ninth Circuit located a statutory right to competence in \xc2\xa73599. 623 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1245 (citing Rohan, 334 F.\xc2\xa03d 803, and Nash, 581 F.\xc2\xa03d 1048). Because Rohan is the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s controlling precedent, we briefly address that decision.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn Rohan, a habeas petitioner asserted a right to com-petency based both on the Due Process Clause and on \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7848(q)(4)(B) (2000 ed.). After discussing the history of the common law, which prohibited the indictment, trial and execution of mentally incompetent defendants,\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t the Court of Appeals stated that the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s due process claim raised \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutional ques-tions.\xe2\x80\x9d Rohan, 334 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 814. This conclusion is puzzling in light of the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s acknowledgment that there is \xe2\x80\x9cno constitutional right to counsel on habeas,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 810 (citing Murray v. Giarratano, \n\n492 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1, \n10 (1989)\n (plurality opinion)), and that \xe2\x80\x9cthere is no due process right to collateral review at all,\xe2\x80\x9d 334 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 810 (citing United States v. MacCollom, \n\n426 U.\xc2\xa0S. 317, \n323 (1976)\n (plurality opinion)). The Ninth Circuit was simply incorrect in suggesting that, in this case, there might be a constitutional concern\xe2\x80\x94much less a \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial\xe2\x80\x9d one\xe2\x80\x94raised by the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s due process claim.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tInvoking the canon of constitutional avoidance, the Ninth Circuit gave the petitioner the practical benefit of a due process right to competence in federal habeas proceedings through its interpretation of \xc2\xa7848(q)(4)(B).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t 334 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 814. In analyzing that statute, the Rohan court relied on a Ninth Circuit en banc opinion in Calderon v. United\xc2\xa0States Dist. Court for Central Dist. of Cal., 163 F.\xc2\xa03d 530 (1998) (Kelly V), overruled in unrelated part, Woodford v. Garceau, \n\n538 U.\xc2\xa0S. 202 (2003)\n, which held that a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s incompetence is grounds for equitably tolling the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996\xe2\x80\x99s (AEDPA) 1-year statute of limitations for filing habeas petitions. The Rohan court purported to be bound by the \xe2\x80\x9crationale\xe2\x80\x9d of Kelly V\xe2\x80\x94that a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s incompetence could \xe2\x80\x9ceviscerate the statutory right to counsel,\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Kelly V, supra, at 541\xe2\x80\x94and concluded that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f a petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s statutory rights depend on his ability to communicate rationally, compelling him to pursue relief while incompetent is no less an infringement than dismissing his late petition.\xe2\x80\x9d 334 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 814.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe are not persuaded by the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s assertion that a habeas petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s mental incompetency could \xe2\x80\x9cevis-cerate the statutory right to counsel\xe2\x80\x9d in federal habeas proceedings. Given the backward-looking, record-based nature of most federal habeas proceedings, counsel can generally provide effective representation to a habeas petitioner regardless of the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s competence. Indeed, where a claim is \xe2\x80\x9cadjudicated on the merits in State court proceedings,\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d) (2006 ed.), counsel should, in most circumstances, be able to identify whether the \xe2\x80\x9cadjudication .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. resulted in a decision that was contrary to, or involved an unreasonable application of, clearly established Federal law, as determined by the Supreme Court of the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72254(d)(1), without any evi-dence outside the record. See Cullen v. Pinholster, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 9) (\xe2\x80\x9c[R]eview under [28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C.] \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) is limited to the record that was before the state court that adjudicated the claim on the merits. . . . This backward-looking language requires an examination of the state-court decision at the time it was made. It follows that the record under review is limited to the record in existence at that same time\xe2\x80\x94i.e., the record before the state court\xe2\x80\x9d). Attorneys are quite capable of reviewing the state-court record, identifying legal errors, and marshaling relevant arguments, even without their clients\xe2\x80\x99 assistance.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRohan also cited Rees I, \n\n384 U.\xc2\xa0S. 312\n, in support of its conclusion. 334 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 815. In Rees I, a state inmate on death row filed a petition for a writ of habeas corpus in District Court, alleging that the state-court conviction violated his constitutional rights. 384 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 313. The District Court denied his petition, and the Court of Appeals affirmed. Ibid. Shortly after Rees\xe2\x80\x99 counsel filed a petition for certiorari with this Court, Rees directed his counsel to withdraw the petition and to forgo any further proceedings. Counsel advised the Court that he could not accede to these instructions without a psychiatric evaluation of Rees, because there was some doubt as to Rees\xe2\x80\x99 mental competency. Ibid. In response, the Court directed the District Court to determine Rees\xe2\x80\x99 mental competence. Id., at 313\xe2\x80\x93314. After the District Court conducted a hearing and found Rees incompetent, the Court issued a one-sentence order directing that the petition for certiorari be \xe2\x80\x9cheld without action.\xe2\x80\x9d Rees v. Peyton, \n\n386 U.\xc2\xa0S. 989 (1967)\n (Rees II\xc2\xa0).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t7\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t When Rees died several decades later, the Court dismissed the petition. Rees v. Superintendent of Va. State Penitentiary, \n\n516 U.\xc2\xa0S. 802 (1995)\n (Rees III\xc2\xa0).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Ninth Circuit concluded that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he record in Rees II shows that incompetence is grounds for staying habeas proceedings.\xe2\x80\x9d Rohan, supra, at 815. This conclusion is unwarranted. Rees I concerned whether an incompetent habeas petitioner may withdraw his certiorari petition, and it provides no clear answer even to that question. Likewise, the unique, one-sentence order in Rees II offered no rationale for the decision to hold Rees\xe2\x80\x99 petition. As a result, Rees offers no support for federal habeas petitioners seeking to stay district court proceedings or for the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s opinions in Rohan, Nash, or this case.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t8\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tGonzales barely defends the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of \xc2\xa73599.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t9\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t He offers a single, halfhearted argument in support of the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s opinion based on our statement in McFarland v. Scott, \n\n512 U.\xc2\xa0S. 849, \n858 (1994)\n, that \xe2\x80\x9cthe right to counsel necessarily includes a right for that counsel meaningfully to research and present a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s habeas claims.\xe2\x80\x9d But McFarland was addressing whether a district court could issue a stay of execution after a capital prisoner had filed a request for counsel but before he had filed his habeas petition. Id., at 854\xe2\x80\x93858. We held that a district court may stay a capital prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s execution once the prisoner has invoked his statutory right to counsel. Id., at 859. McFarland has no relevance here where Gonzales is not seeking a stay of execution, but rather a stay of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s proceedings. Moreover, Gonzales moved for a stay more than six years after initiating his habeas petition. This was certainly ample time for his attorney to research and present the claims.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor the foregoing reasons, we hold that \xc2\xa73599 does not provide federal habeas petitioners with a \xe2\x80\x9cstatutory right\xe2\x80\x9d to competence.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t10\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Sixth Circuit reached the same conclusion as the Ninth Circuit but located the statutory right to competence during habeas proceedings in \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa74241. Relying largely on Rees I, the Sixth Circuit concluded that \xc2\xa74241 provides a statutory right to competence. 644 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 333. But as discussed, Part II\xe2\x80\x93A, supra, Rees I did not recognize a statutory right to competence in federal ha-beas proceedings.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t11\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Moreover, \xc2\xa74241 does not even apply to such proceedings. Section 4241(a) provides:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cAt any time after the commencement of a prosecution for an offense and prior to the sentencing of the defendant, or at any time after the commencement of probation or supervised release and prior to the completion of the sentence, the defendant or the attorney for the Government may file a motion for a hearing to determine the mental competency of the defendant. The court shall grant the motion, or shall order such a hearing on its own motion, if there is reasonable cause to believe that the defendant may presently be suf-fering from a mental disease or defect rendering him mentally incompetent to the extent that he is unable to understand the nature and consequences of the proceedings against him or to assist properly in his defense.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tBy its own terms, \xc2\xa74241 applies only to trial proceedings prior to sentencing and \xe2\x80\x9cat any time after the commencement of probation or supervised release.\xe2\x80\x9d Federal habeas proceedings, however, commence after sentencing, and federal habeas petitioners, by definition, are incarcerated, not on probation.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFurthermore, \xc2\xa74241, like the rest of Title 18 generally, applies exclusively to federal defendants and probationers subject to prosecution by the United States. Carter is not, and does not claim to be, a federal defendant. Rather, he is a state prisoner challenging the basis of his conviction in a federal civil action. See Blair v. Martel, 645 F.\xc2\xa03d 1151, 1155 (CA9 2011) (\xe2\x80\x9cBy its own terms, \xc2\xa74241 does not apply unless a federal criminal defendant is on trial or is released on probation\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, \xc2\xa74241(a) authorizes the district court to grant a motion for a competency determination if there is reasonable cause to believe that the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s mental incompetence renders him \xe2\x80\x9cunable to understand the nature and consequences of the proceedings against him or to assist properly in his defense.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.) See also \xc2\xa74241(d).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t12\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t A habeas proceeding under \xc2\xa72254, however, is not a \xe2\x80\x9cproceedin[g] against\xe2\x80\x9d the habeas petitioner; this, on the other hand, is a civil action against the warden of the state prison. And, a federal habeas petitioner does not mount a \xe2\x80\x9cdefense\xe2\x80\x9d to the government\xe2\x80\x99s prosecution. Rather, the petitioner collaterally attacks his conviction at an earlier state trial. Accordingly, the statutory right to competence provided in \xc2\xa74241 is simply inapplicable to federal habeas proceedings. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe would address Carter\xe2\x80\x99s arguments in defense of the Sixth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision, but, there are none. Carter\xe2\x80\x99s brief informed us that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]his Court need not consider the statutory argument with which the [petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s] brief begins\xe2\x80\x94i.e., that there is no \xe2\x80\x98statutory right\xe2\x80\x99 under \n\n18 U. S. C. \xc2\xa74241 to be competent in habeas proceedings.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent in No. 11\xe2\x80\x93218, p. 15. Apparently, Carter found the Sixth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning indefensible. We agree.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBoth Gonzales and Carter argued at length in their briefs and at oral argument that district courts have the equitable power to stay proceedings when they determine that habeas petitioners are mentally incompetent.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t13\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Neither petitioner disputes that \xe2\x80\x9c[d]istrict courts . . . ordinarily have authority to issue stays, where such a stay would be a proper exercise of discretion.\xe2\x80\x9d Rhines v. Weber, \n\n544 U.\xc2\xa0S. 269, \n276 (2005)\n (citation omitted); see also Enelow v. New York Life Ins. Co., \n\n293 U.\xc2\xa0S. 379, \n382 (1935)\n (explaining that a district court may stay a case \xe2\x80\x9cpending before it by virtue of its inherent power to control the progress of the cause so as to maintain the orderly processes of justice\xe2\x80\x9d). Similarly, both petitioners agree that \xe2\x80\x9cAEDPA does not deprive district courts of [this] authority.\xe2\x80\x9d Rhines, supra, at 276. Petitioners and respondents disagree, however, about the types of situations in which a stay would be appropriate and about the permissible duration of a competency-based stay. We do not presume that district courts need unsolicited advice from us on how to manage their dockets. Rather, the decision to grant a stay, like the decision to grant an evidentiary hearing, is \xe2\x80\x9cgenerally left to the sound discretion of district courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Schriro v. Landrigan, \n\n550 U.\xc2\xa0S. 465, \n473 (2007)\n. For pur-poses of resolving these cases, it is unnecessary to determine the precise contours of the district court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion to issue stays. We address only its outer limits.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn Gonzales\xe2\x80\x99 case, the District Court correctly found that all of Gonzales\xe2\x80\x99 properly exhausted claims were record based or resolvable as a matter of law, irrespective of Gonzales\xe2\x80\x99 competence.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t14\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t 617 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 863; see also State v. Gonzales, 181 Ariz. 502, 509\xe2\x80\x93515, 892 P.\xc2\xa02d 838, 845\xe2\x80\x93851 (1995) (adjudicating Gonzales\xe2\x80\x99 claims on the merits). The court therefore denied Gonzales\xe2\x80\x99 motion for a stay. The District Court did not abuse its discretion in so holding, because a stay is not generally warranted when a petitioner raises only record-based claims subject to \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d). As previously noted, review of such claims \xe2\x80\x9cis limited to the record that was before the state court that adjudicated the claim on the merits.\xe2\x80\x9d Pin-holster, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 9). Accordingly, any evidence that a petitioner might have would be inadmis-sible. Ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he record under review is limited to the record in existence at that same time\xe2\x80\x94i.e., the record before the state court\xe2\x80\x9d). Because federal habeas is \xe2\x80\x9ca \xe2\x80\x98guard against extreme malfunctions in the state criminal justice systems,\xe2\x80\x99 not a substitute for ordinary error correction through appeal,\xe2\x80\x9d the types of errors redressable under \xc2\xa72254(d) should be apparent from the record. Harrington v. Richter, 562 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 13) (quoting Jackson v. Virginia, \n\n443 U.\xc2\xa0S. 307, \n332, n. \n5 (1979)\n (Stevens, J., concurring in judgment)). Counsel can read the record.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn Carter\xe2\x80\x99s case, the District Court concluded that four of Carter\xe2\x80\x99s claims could potentially benefit from Carter\xe2\x80\x99s assistance.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t15\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t \n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tHowever, three of these claims were adjudicated on the merits in state postconviction proceedings and, thus, were subject to review under \xc2\xa72254(d). See State v. Carter, No. 99\xe2\x80\x93T\xe2\x80\x930133, 2000 Ohio App. LEXIS 5935, *5\xe2\x80\x93*13 (Dec. 15, 2000). Any extrarecord evidence that Carter might have concerning these claims would therefore be inadmissible. Pinholster, supra, at ___. Consequently, these claims do not warrant a stay.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is unclear from the record whether Carter exhausted the fourth claim.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t16\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t If that claim was exhausted, it too would be record based. But even if Carter could show that the claim was both unexhausted and not procedurally defaulted,\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t17\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t an indefinite stay would be inappropriate. \xe2\x80\x9cAEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s acknowledged purpose\xe2\x80\x9d is to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98reduc[e] delays in the execution of state and federal criminal sentences.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Schriro, supra, at 475 (quoting Woodford, 538 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 206). \xe2\x80\x9cStaying a federal habeas petition frustrates AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s objective of encouraging finality by allowing a petitioner to delay the resolution of the federal proceedings.\xe2\x80\x9d Rhines, 544 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 277. In the context of discussing stay and abeyance procedures, we observed:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c[N]ot all petitioners have an incentive to obtain federal relief as quickly as possible. In particular, capital petitioners might deliberately engage in dilatory tactics to prolong their incarceration and avoid execution of the sentence of death. Without time limits [on stays], petitioners could frustrate AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s goal of finality by dragging out indefinitely their federal ha-beas review.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 277\xe2\x80\x93278.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe same principle obtains in the context of competency-based stays. At some point, the State must be allowed to defend its judgment of conviction.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t18\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIf a district court concludes that the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claim could substantially benefit from the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s assistance, the district court should take into account the likelihood that the petitioner will regain competence in the foreseeable future. Where there is no reasonable hope of competence, a stay is inappropriate and merely frustrates the State\xe2\x80\x99s attempts to defend its presumptively valid judgment.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIV\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe judgment of the Ninth Circuit is reversed. We vacate the judgment of the Sixth Circuit and remand the case for proceedings consistent with this opinion.\n\t\t\t
