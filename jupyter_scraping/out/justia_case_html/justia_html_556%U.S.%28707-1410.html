UNITED STATES v. NAVAJO NATION

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the federal circuit

\n

No. 07\xe2\x80\x931410.\xe2\x80\x83Argued February 23, 2009\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 6, 2009

\n

The Navajo Nation has long sought damages under the Indian Tucker Act (ITA) for an asserted breach of fiduciary duty by the Secretary of the Interior in connection with his failure promptly to approve a royalty rate increase under a coal lease (Lease 8580) the Tribe executed in 1964. Six years ago, this Court held that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s claim for compensation \xe2\x80\xa6 fails.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Navajo Nation, 537 U. S. 488, 493 (Navajo I). The Court explained that in order to invoke the ITA and thereby bypass federal sovereign immunity, a tribe \xe2\x80\x9cmust identify a substantive source of law that establishes specific fiduciary or other duties, and allege that the Government has failed faithfully to perform those duties.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 506. Holding that such duties were not imposed by the Indian Mineral Leasing Act of 1938 (IMLA), by the Indian Mineral Development Act of 1982 (IMDA), or by 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7399, the Court reversed a judgment for the Tribe and remanded. The Court of Federal Claims then dismissed the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s claim, but the Federal Circuit reversed, finding violations of duties imposed by the Navajo-Hopi Rehabilitation Act of 1950, 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7635(a), 638, and the Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act of 1977, 30 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71300(e), as well as common-law duties arising from the Government\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccomprehensive control\xe2\x80\x9d over tribal coal.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s claim for compensation fails. None of the sources of law cited by the Federal Circuit and relied upon by the Tribe provides any more sound a basis for its lawsuit than those analyzed in Navajo I. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Navajo I did not definitively terminate the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s claim. Because the Court in that case did not analyze statutes other than the IMLA, the IMDA, and \xc2\xa7399, it is conceivable, albeit unlikely, that another relevant statute might have provided a basis for the suit. However, Navajo I\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning\xe2\x80\x94particularly its instruction to \xe2\x80\x9ctrain on specific rights-creating or duty-imposing statutory or regulatory prescriptions,\xe2\x80\x9d 537 U. S., at 506\xe2\x80\x94left no room for that result based on the sources of law relied on below. P.\xc2\xa08.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Lease 8580 was not issued under \xc2\xa7635(a), so the Tribe cannot invoke that law as a source of money-mandating duties. Section 635(a) authorizes leases only for terms of up to 25 years, renewable for up to another 25 years. In contrast, the IMLA allows \xe2\x80\x9cterms not to exceed ten years and as long thereafter as minerals are produced in paying quantities.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7396a. Mirroring the latter language, Lease 8580\xe2\x80\x99s indefinite term strongly suggests that it was negotiated and approved under the IMLA. This conclusion is not refuted by \xc2\xa7635(a)\xe2\x80\x99s saving clause or by testimony that coal leasing was a centerpiece of the Rehabilitation Act\xe2\x80\x99s program. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Also unavailing is the argument that the Secretary violated \xc2\xa7638\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that he follow the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s recommendations in administering the \xe2\x80\x9cprogram authorized by this subchapter.\xe2\x80\x9d The word \xe2\x80\x9cprogram\xe2\x80\x9d refers back to \xc2\xa7631, which directs the Secretary to undertake \xe2\x80\x9ca program of basic improvements for the conservation and development of the [Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s] resources\xe2\x80\x9d and lists various projects to be included in the program. The statute certainly does not require the Secretary to follow recommendations of the Tribe as to royalty rates under coal leases executed pursuant to another Act. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(d)\xc2\xa0Title 30 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71300(e) is irrelevant. That provision applies only \xe2\x80\x9c[w]ith respect to leases issued after\xe2\x80\x9d the statute was enacted in 1977. Lease 8580 was issued in 1964; \xc2\xa71300(e) is therefore inapplicable. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(e)\xc2\xa0The Government\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccomprehensive control\xe2\x80\x9d over Indian coal, alone, does not create enforceable fiduciary duties. The ITA limits cognizable claims to those arising under, inter alia, \xe2\x80\x9cthe \xe2\x80\xa6 laws \xe2\x80\xa6 of the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71505, and Navajo I reiterated that the analysis must begin with \xe2\x80\x9cspecific rights-creating or duty-imposing statutory or regulatory prescriptions,\xe2\x80\x9d 537 U. S., at 506. If a statute or regulation imposes a trust relationship, then common-law principles are relevant in determining whether damages are available for breach of the duty, but the Tribe cannot identify a specific, applicable, trust-creating statute or regulation that the Government violated, so trust principles do not come into play here. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

501 F. 3d 1327, reversed and remanded.

\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 07-1410

UNITED STATES, PETITIONER v. NAVAJO NATION

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the federal circuit

\n

[April 6, 2009]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For over 15 years, the Indian Tribe known as the Navajo Nation has been pursuing a claim for money damages against the Federal Government based on an asserted breach of trust by the Secretary of the Interior in connection with his approval of amendments to a coal lease executed by the Tribe. The original lease took effect in 1964. The amendments were approved in 1987. The litigation was initiated in 1993. Six years ago, we held that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s claim for compensation . . . fails,\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Navajo Nation, 537 U. S. 488, 493 (2003) (Navajo I), but after further proceedings on remand the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit resuscitated it. 501 F.\xc2\xa03d 1327 (2007). Today we hold, once again, that the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s claim for compensation fails. This matter should now be regarded as closed.

\n

I. Legal Background

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Federal Government cannot be sued without its consent. FDIC v. Meyer, 510 U. S. 471, 475 (1994). Limited consent has been granted through a variety of statutes, including one colloquially referred to as the Indian Tucker Act:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cThe United States Court of Federal Claims shall have jurisdiction of any claim against the United States accruing after August 13, 1946, in favor of any tribe . . . whenever such claim is one arising under the Constitution, laws or treaties of the United States, or Executive orders of the President, or is one which otherwise would be cognizable in the Court of Federal Claims if the claimant were not an Indian tribe, band or group.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71505.

\n

\nThe last clause refers to the (ordinary) Tucker Act, which waives immunity with respect to any claim \xe2\x80\x9cfounded either upon the Constitution, or any Act of Congress or any regulation of an executive department, or upon any express or implied contract with the United States, or for liquidated or unliquidated damages in cases not sounding in tort.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71491(a)(1).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Neither the Tucker Act nor the Indian Tucker Act creates substantive rights; they are simply jurisdictional provisions that operate to waive sovereign immunity for claims premised on other sources of law (e.g., statutes or contracts). United States v. Testan, 424 U. S. 392, 400 (1976); United States v. Mitchell, 445 U. S. 535, 538 (1980) (Mitchell I). The other source of law need not explicitly provide that the right or duty it creates is enforceable through a suit for damages, but it triggers liability only if it \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98can fairly be interpreted as mandating compensation by the Federal Government.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Testan, supra, at 400 (quoting Eastport S.\xc2\xa0S. Corp. v. United States, 178 Ct. Cl. 599, 607, 372 F.\xc2\xa02d 1002, 1009 (1967)); see also United States v. Mitchell, 463 U. S. 206, 218 (1983) (Mitchell II); Navajo I, 537 U. S., at 503.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As we explained in Navajo I, there are thus two hurdles that must be cleared before a tribe can invoke jurisdiction under the Indian Tucker Act. First, the tribe \xe2\x80\x9cmust identify a substantive source of law that establishes specific fiduciary or other duties, and allege that the Government has failed faithfully to perform those duties.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 506. \xe2\x80\x9cIf that threshold is passed, the court must then determine whether the relevant source of substantive law \xe2\x80\x98can fairly be interpreted as mandating compensation for damages sustained as a result of a breach of the duties [the governing law] impose[s].\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (alteration in original). At the second stage, principles of trust law might be relevant \xe2\x80\x9cin drawing the inference that Congress intended damages to remedy a breach.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. White Mountain Apache Tribe, 537 U. S. 465, 477 (2003).

\n

II. History of the Present Case

\n

A. The Facts

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A comprehensive recitation of the facts can be found in Navajo I, supra, at 495\xe2\x80\x93502. By way of executive summary: The Tribe occupies a large Indian reservation in the American Southwest, on which there are significant coal deposits. In 1964 the Secretary of the Interior approved a lease (Lease 8580), executed by the Tribe and the predecessor of Peabody Coal Company, allowing the company to engage in coal mining on a tract of the reservation in exchange for royalty payments to the Tribe. The term of the lease was set at \xe2\x80\x9cten (10) years from the date hereof, and for so long thereafter as the substances produced are being mined by the Lessee in accordance with its terms, in paying quantities,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 189; it is still in effect today. The royalty rates were originally set at a maximum of 37.5 cents per ton of coal, but the lease also said that the rates were \xe2\x80\x9csubject to reasonable adjustment by the Secretary of the Interior\xe2\x80\x9d after 20 years and again \xe2\x80\x9cat the end of each successive ten-year period thereafter.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 194.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dispute in this case concerns the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s attempt to secure such an adjustment to the royalty rate after the initial 20-year period elapsed in 1984. At that point, the Tribe requested that the Secretary exercise his power to increase the royalty rate, and the Director of the Bureau of Indian Affairs for the Navajo Area issued an opinion letter imposing a new rate of 20 percent of gross proceeds. Id., at 8\xe2\x80\x939. But Peabody filed an administrative appeal, and while it was pending the Tribe and the company reached a negotiated agreement to set a rate of 12.5 percent of gross proceeds instead. As a result, the Area Director\xe2\x80\x99s decision was vacated, the administrative appeal was dismissed, and the Secretary approved the amendments to the lease.

\n

B. This Litigation through Navajo I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Tribe launched the present lawsuit in 1993, claiming that the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s actions in connection with the approval of the lease amendments constituted a breach of trust. In particular, the Tribe alleged that the Secretary, following upon improper ex parte contacts with Peabody, had delayed action on Peabody\xe2\x80\x99s administrative appeal in order to pressure the economically desperate Tribe to return to the bargaining table. This, the complaint charged, was in violation of the United States\xe2\x80\x99 fiduciary duty to act in the Indians\xe2\x80\x99 best interests. The Tribe sought $600 million in damages, invoking the Indian Tucker Act to bypass sovereign immunity.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Federal Claims granted summary judgment to the United States, concluding that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Navajo Nation has failed to present statutory authority which can be fairly interpreted as mandating compensation for the government\xe2\x80\x99s fiduciary wrongs,\xe2\x80\x9d Navajo Nation v. United States, 46 Fed. Cl. 217, 236 (2000), and therefore could not sue under the Indian Tucker Act. The Federal Circuit reversed that ruling and held that the Indian Mineral Leasing Act of 1938 (IMLA), 52 Stat. 347, 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7396a et seq., among other statutes, gave the Government broad control over mineral leasing on Indian lands, thus creating a fiduciary duty enforceable through suits for monetary damages. Navajo Nation v. United States, 263 F.\xc2\xa03d 1325, 1330\xe2\x80\x931332 (2001). Finding that the Government had in fact violated its obligations, the Court of Appeals reinstated the suit.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari, United States v. Navajo Nation, 535 U. S. 1111 (2002), and (as described by the author of the ensuing opinion, concurring in a companion case) considered \xe2\x80\x9cthe threshold question\xe2\x80\x9d presented by the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s attempt to invoke the Indian Tucker Act: \xe2\x80\x9c[W]hether the IMLA and its regulations impose any concrete substantive obligations, fiduciary or otherwise, on the Government,\xe2\x80\x9d White Mountain, supra, at 480 (Ginsburg, J., concurring). The answer was an unequivocal no.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The relevant provision of the IMLA provided as follows:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[U]nallotted lands within any Indian reservation or lands owned by any tribe . . . may, with the approval of the Secretary of the Interior, be leased for mining purposes, by authority of the tribal council or other authorized spokesmen for such Indians, for terms not to exceed ten years and as long thereafter as minerals are produced in paying quantities.\xe2\x80\x9d 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7396a.

\n

\nAnother provision of the IMLA authorized the Secretary to promulgate regulations governing operations under such leases, \xc2\xa7396d, but during the relevant period the regulations applicable to coal leases, beyond setting a minimum royalty rate of 10 cents per ton, 25 CFR \xc2\xa7211.15(c) (1985), did not limit the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s approval authority.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We construed the IMLA in light of its purpose: to \xe2\x80\x9cenhance tribal self-determination by giving Tribes, not the Government, the lead role in negotiating mining leases with third parties.\xe2\x80\x9d Navajo I, 537 U. S., at 508. Consistent with that goal, the IMLA gave the Secretary not a \xe2\x80\x9ccomprehensive managerial role,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 507, but only the power to approve coal leases already negotiated by Tribes. That authority did not create, expressly or otherwise, a trust duty with respect to coal leasing and so there existed no enforceable fiduciary obligations that the Tribe could sue the Government for having neglected. Id., at 507\xe2\x80\x93508.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We distinguished Mitchell II, which involved a series of statutes and regulations that gave the Federal Government \xe2\x80\x9cfull responsibility to manage Indian resources and land for the benefit of the Indians.\xe2\x80\x9d 463 U. S., at 224. Title 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7406(a) permitted Indians to sell timber with the consent of the Secretary of the Interior, but directed the Secretary to base his decisions on \xe2\x80\x9ca consideration of the needs and best interests of the Indian owner and his heirs\xe2\x80\x9d and enumerated specific factors to guide that decisionmaking. We understood that statute\xe2\x80\x94in combination with several other provisions and the applicable regulations\xe2\x80\x94to create a fiduciary duty with respect to Indian timber. Mitchell II, supra, at 219\xe2\x80\x93224. But neither the IMLA nor its regulations established any analogous duties or obligations in the coal context. Navajo I, supra, at 507\xe2\x80\x93508.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor did the other statutes cited by the Tribe\xe2\x80\x9425 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7399 and the Indian Mineral Development Act of 1982 (IMDA), 96 Stat. 1938, 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72101 et seq.\xe2\x80\x94help its case. Section 399 \xe2\x80\x9cis not part of the IMLA and [did] not govern Lease 8580,\xe2\x80\x9d Navajo I, 537 U. S., at 509; rather, it granted to the Secretary the power to lease Indian land on his own say-so. We therefore found it irrelevant to the question whether \xe2\x80\x9cthe Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s more limited approval role under the IMLA\xe2\x80\x9d created any enforceable duties. Ibid. And while the IMDA did set standards to govern the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s approval of other mining-related agreements, Lease 8580 \xe2\x80\x9cfalls outside the IMDA\xe2\x80\x99s domain,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid.; that law was accordingly beside the point.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Having resolved that \xe2\x80\x9cwe ha[d] no warrant from any relevant statute or regulation to conclude that [the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s] conduct implicated a duty enforceable in an action for damages under the Indian Tucker Act,\xe2\x80\x9d this Court reversed the Federal Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment in favor of the Tribe and \xe2\x80\x9cremanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 514.

\n

C. Proceedings on Remand

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On remand, the Tribe argued that even if its suit could not be maintained on the basis of the IMLA, the IMDA, or \xc2\xa7399, a \xe2\x80\x9cnetwork\xe2\x80\x9d of other statutes, treaties, and regulations could provide the basis for its claims. The Government objected that our opinion foreclosed that possibility, but the Federal Circuit disagreed and remanded for consideration of the argument in the first instance. 347 F.\xc2\xa03d 1327 (2003). The Court of Federal Claims, however, persisted in its original decision to dismiss the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s claim, explaining that nothing in the suggested \xe2\x80\x9cnetwork\xe2\x80\x9d succeeded in tying \xe2\x80\x9cspecific laws or regulatory provisions to the issue at hand,\xe2\x80\x9d namely, the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s approval of royalty rates in coal leases negotiated by tribes. 68 Fed. Cl. 805, 811 (2005).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Once again the Federal Circuit reversed, this time relying primarily on three statutory provisions\xe2\x80\x94two sections of the Navajo-Hopi Rehabilitation Act of 1950, 64 Stat. 46, 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7635(a), 638; and one section of the Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act of 1977, 30 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71300(e)\xe2\x80\x94to allow the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s claim to proceed. The Court held that the Government had violated the specific duties created by those statutes, as well as \xe2\x80\x9ccommon law trust duties of care, candor, and loyalty\xe2\x80\x9d that arise from the comprehensive control over tribal coal that is exercised by the Government. 501 F.\xc2\xa03d 1327, 1346 (2007).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Once again we granted the Government\xe2\x80\x99s petition for a writ of certiorari. 554 U. S. ___ (2008).

\n

III. Analysis

\n

A. Threshold Matter

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government points to our categorical concluding language in Navajo I: \xe2\x80\x9c[W]e have no warrant from any relevant statute or regulation to conclude that [the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s] conduct implicated a duty enforceable in an action for damages under the Indian Tucker Act,\xe2\x80\x9d 537 U. S., at 514. This proves, the Government claims, that this Court definitively terminated the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s claim last time around, so that the lower court\xe2\x80\x99s later resurrection of the suit was flatly inconsistent with our mandate. But, to be fair, our opinion (like the Court of Appeals decision we were reviewing, Navajo Nation, 263 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1327, 1330\xe2\x80\x931331) did not analyze any statutes beyond the IMLA, the IMDA, and \xc2\xa7399. It is thus conceivable, albeit unlikely, that some other relevant statute, though invoked by the Tribe at the outset of the litigation, might have gone unmentioned by the Federal Circuit and unanalyzed by this Court.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0So we cannot say that our mandate completely foreclosed the possibility that such a statute might allow for the Tribe to succeed on remand. What we can say, however, is that our reasoning in Navajo I\xe2\x80\x94in particular, our emphasis on the need for courts to \xe2\x80\x9ctrain on specific rights-creating or duty-imposing statutory or regulatory prescriptions,\xe2\x80\x9d 537 U. S., at 506\xe2\x80\x94left no room for that result based on the sources of law that the Court of Appeals relied upon.

\n

B. 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7635(a)

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The first of the two discussed provisions of the Navajo-Hopi Rehabilitation Act of 1950\xe2\x80\x94like the IMLA\xe2\x80\x94permits Indians to lease reservation lands if the Secretary approves of the deal:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cAny restricted Indian lands owned by the Navajo Tribe, members thereof, or associations of such members . . . may be leased by the Indian owners, with the approval of the Secretary of the Interior, for public, religious, educational, recreational, or business purposes, including the development or utilization of natural resources in connection with operations under such leases. All leases so granted shall be for a term of not to exceed twenty-five years, but may include provisions authorizing their renewal for an additional term of not to exceed twenty-five years, and shall be made under such regulations as may be prescribed by the Secretary. . . . Nothing contained in this section shall be construed to repeal or affect any authority to lease restricted Indian lands conferred by or pursuant to any other provision of law.\xe2\x80\x9d 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7635(a).

\n

\nThe Tribe contends that this section renders the Government liable for any breach of trust in connection with the approval of leases executed pursuant to the authority it grants. Whether or not that is so, the provision only even arguably matters if Lease 8580 was issued under its authority.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Navajo I we presumed, as did the parties, that the lease had been issued pursuant to the IMLA. 537 U. S., at 495. But now the Tribe has changed its tune, and contends that Lease 8580 was approved under \xc2\xa7635(a), not under the IMLA at all. Brief for Respondent 39. The Government says otherwise. Section 635(a) permits leasing only for \xe2\x80\x9cpublic, religious, educational, recreational, or business purposes,\xe2\x80\x9d and the Government contends that mining is not embraced by those terms. While leases under \xc2\xa7635(a) may provide for \xe2\x80\x9cthe development or utilization of natural resources,\xe2\x80\x9d they may do so only \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with operations under such leases,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., in connection with operations for the enumerated purposes. By contrast, mining leases were permitted and governed by the IMLA even before the Navajo-Hopi Rehabilitation Act was enacted in 1950.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We need not decide whether the Government is correct on that point, or whether mining could ever qualify as a \xe2\x80\x9cbusiness purpose\xe2\x80\x9d under the statute, because the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s argument suffers from a more fundamental problem. Section 635(a) authorizes leases only for terms of up to 25 years, renewable for up to another 25 years. In contrast, the IMLA allows \xe2\x80\x9cfor terms not to exceed ten years and as long thereafter as minerals are produced in paying quantities.\xe2\x80\x9d 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7396a. Lease 8580, mirroring the latter language, sets a term of \xe2\x80\x9cten (10) years from the date hereof, and for so long thereafter as the substances produced are being mined by the Lessee in accordance with its terms, in paying quantities.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 189. That indefinite lease term strongly suggests that it was negotiated by the Tribe and approved by the Secretary under the powers authorized by the IMLA, not the Rehabilitation Act.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s only responses to this apparently fatal defect in its argument are (1) that \xc2\xa7635(a) expressly leaves unaffected \xe2\x80\x9cany authority to lease restricted Indian lands conferred by or pursuant to any other provision of law,\xe2\x80\x9d including the authority to lease for indefinite terms; and (2) that Stewart Udall, who served as Secretary of the Interior during the 1960\xe2\x80\x99s, recently testified that \xe2\x80\x9ccoal leasing and related development was the centerpiece of the resources development program\xe2\x80\x9d under the Rehabilitation Act, id., at 569.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As to the former: That is precisely the point. Section 635(a) creates a supplemental authority for leasing Indian land; it does not displace authority granted elsewhere. But in light of the different conditions attached to the different grants, it is apparent that a particular lease must be executed and approved pursuant to a particular authorization. The saving clause in \xc2\xa7635(a) does not allow the Tribe to mix-and-match, to combine the (allegedly) duty-creating mechanism of the Rehabilitation Act with the indefinite lease term of the IMLA. It must be one or the other, and the record persuasively demonstrates that Lease 8580 is an IMLA lease.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As to Secretary Udall\xe2\x80\x99s testimony: That is not inconsistent with our conclusion. The Interior Department may have viewed coal leasing as an important part of the program to rehabilitate the Navajo Tribe but that does not prove that Lease 8580 was issued pursuant to the supplemental leasing authority granted by the Rehabilitation Act, rather than the pre-existing leasing authority of the IMLA preserved by the Rehabilitation Act. The latter, perhaps because of its longer lease terms, was evidently preferable to the Tribe or the coal company or both.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because the lease in this case \xe2\x80\x9cfalls outside\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7635(a)\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cdomain,\xe2\x80\x9d Navajo I, supra, at 509, the Tribe cannot invoke it as a source of money-mandating rights or duties.

\n

C. 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7638

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Next, the Tribe points to a second provision in the Navajo-Hopi Rehabilitation Act:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cThe Tribal Councils of the Navajo and Hopi Tribes and the Indian communities affected shall be kept informed and afforded opportunity to consider from their inception plans pertaining to the program authorized by this subchapter. In the administration of the program, the Secretary of the Interior shall consider the recommendations of the tribal councils and shall follow such recommendations whenever he deems them feasible and consistent with the objectives of this subchapter.\xe2\x80\x9d 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7638.

\n

\nIn the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s view, the Secretary violated this provision by failing promptly to abide by its wishes to affirm the Area Director\xe2\x80\x99s order increasing the royalty rate under Lease 8580 to a full 20 percent of gross proceeds.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We cannot agree. The \xe2\x80\x9cprogram\xe2\x80\x9d twice mentioned in \xc2\xa7638 refers back to the Act\xe2\x80\x99s opening provision, which directs the Secretary to undertake \xe2\x80\x9ca program of basic improvements for the conservation and development of the resources of the Navajo and Hopi Indians, the more productive employment of their manpower, and the supplying of means to be used in their rehabilitation.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7631. The statute then enumerates various projects to be included in that program, and authorizes appropriation of funds (in specific amounts) for each. E.g., \xe2\x80\x9cSoil and water conservation and range improvement work, $10,000,000.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7631(1).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The only listed project even remotely related to this case is \xe2\x80\x9c[s]urveys and studies of timber, coal, mineral, and other physical and human resources.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7631(3). Of course a lease is neither a survey nor a study. To read \xc2\xa7638 as imposing a money-mandating duty on the Secretary to follow recommendations of the Tribe as to royalty rates under coal leases executed pursuant to another Act, and to allow for the enforcement of that duty through the Indian Tucker Act, would simply be too far a stretch.

\n

D. 30 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71201 et seq.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The final statute invoked by the Tribe is the most easily dispensed with. The Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act of 1977 (SMCRA), 91 Stat. 445, 30 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71201 et seq., is a comprehensive statute that regulates all surface coal mining operations. See generally \xc2\xa71202; Hodel v. Virginia Surface Mining & Reclamation Assn., Inc., 452 U. S. 264, 268\xe2\x80\x93272 (1981). One section of the Act, \xc2\xa71300, deals with coal mining specifically on Indian lands, and the Tribe cites subsection (e): \xe2\x80\x9cWith respect to leases issued after [the date of enactment of this Act], the Secretary shall include and enforce terms and conditions in addition to those required by subsections (c) and (d) of this section as may be requested by the Indian tribe in such leases.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0According to the Tribe, this provision requires the Secretary to enforce whatever terms the Indians request with respect to coal leases. In light of the fact that the referenced subsections (c) and (d) refer exclusively to environmental protection standards, that interpretation is highly suspect. In any event, because Lease 8580 was issued in 1964\xe2\x80\x94some 13 years before the date of enactment of the SMCRA\xe2\x80\x94the provision is categorically inapplicable. The Federal Circuit concluded otherwise on the theory that the amendments to the lease were approved after 1977. But \xc2\xa71300(e) is limited to leases \xe2\x80\x9cissued\xe2\x80\x9d after that date; and even the Tribe does not contend that a lease is \xe2\x80\x9cissued\xe2\x80\x9d whenever it is amended. The SMCRA is irrelevant here.

\n

E. Government\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cComprehensive Control\xe2\x80\x9d over Coal

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Federal Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s opinion also suggested that the Government\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccomprehensive control\xe2\x80\x9d over coal on Indian land gives rise to fiduciary duties based on common-law trust principles. It noted that the Government had conducted surveys and studies of the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s coal resources, 501 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1341; that the Interior Department imposed various requirements on coal mining operations on Indian land\xe2\x80\x94regulating, for example, \xe2\x80\x9csigns and markers, postmining use of land, backfilling and grading, waste disposal, topsoil handling, protection of hydrologic systems, revegetation, and steep-slope mining,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 1342; and that the Government in practice exercised control over the calculation of coal values and quantities for royalty purposes, even though such control was codified by regulation only after the events at issue here, id., at 1342\xe2\x80\x931343.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Federal Government\xe2\x80\x99s liability cannot be premised on control alone. The text of the Indian Tucker Act makes clear that only claims arising under \xe2\x80\x9cthe Constitution, laws or treaties of the United States, or Executive orders of the President\xe2\x80\x9d are cognizable (unless the claim could be brought by a non-Indian plaintiff under the ordinary Tucker Act). 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71505. In Navajo I we reiterated that the analysis must begin with \xe2\x80\x9cspecific rights-creating or duty-imposing statutory or regulatory prescriptions.\xe2\x80\x9d 537 U. S., at 506. If a plaintiff identifies such a prescription, and if that prescription bears the hallmarks of a \xe2\x80\x9cconventional fiduciary relationship,\xe2\x80\x9d White Mountain, 537 U. S., at 473, then trust principles (including any such principles premised on \xe2\x80\x9ccontrol\xe2\x80\x9d) could play a role in \xe2\x80\x9cinferring that the trust obligation [is] enforceable by damages,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 477. But that must be the second step of the analysis, not (as the Federal Circuit made it) the starting point.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Navajo I determined that the IMLA, which governs the lease at issue here, does not create even a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98limited trust relationship\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d with respect to coal leasing. Navajo I, supra, at 508 (quoting Mitchell I, 445 U. S., at 542). Since the statutes discussed in the preceding subparts, supra, at 8\xe2\x80\x9313, do not apply to the lease at all, they likewise create no such relationship. Because the Tribe cannot identify a specific, applicable, trust-creating statute or regulation that the Government violated, we do not reach the question whether the trust duty was money mandating. Thus, neither the Government\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccontrol\xe2\x80\x9d over coal nor common-law trust principles matter.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0None of the sources of law cited by the Federal Circuit and relied upon by the Tribe provides any more sound a basis for its breach-of-trust lawsuit against the Federal Government than those we analyzed in Navajo I. This case is at an end. The judgment of the Court of Appeals is reversed, and the case is remanded with instructions to affirm the Court of Federal Claims\xe2\x80\x99 dismissal of the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s complaint.

\n

It is so ordered.
