\nSYLLABUS
\nOCTOBER TERM, 2008
\nFITZGERALD V. BARNSTABLE SCHOOL COMM.
\n
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\n
\n

FITZGERALD et vir v. BARNSTABLE SCHOOL\nCOMMITTEE et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the first circuit

\n

No. 07\xe2\x80\x931125.\xe2\x80\x83Argued December 2, 2008\xe2\x80\x94Decided January 21, 2009

\n

Petitioners filed suit against respondents, the local school district\xe2\x80\x99s governing board and superintendent, alleging that their response to allegations of sexual harassment of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 daughter by an older student was inadequate, raising claims under, inter alia, Title IX of the Education Amendments of 1972, 20 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71681(a), and 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983 for violation of the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. Among its rulings, the District Court dismissed the \xc2\xa71983 claim. The First Circuit affirmed, holding that, under this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents, Title IX\xe2\x80\x99s implied private remedy was sufficiently comprehensive to preclude the use of \xc2\xa71983 to advance constitutional claims.

\n

Held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0Title IX does not preclude a \xc2\xa71983 action alleging unconstitutional gender discrimination in schools. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0In Middlesex County Sewerage Authority v. National Sea Clammers Assn., 453 U. S. 1; Smith v. Robinson, 468 U. S. 992; and Rancho Palos Verdes v. Abrams, 544 U. S. 113, this Court found that particular statutory enactments precluded \xc2\xa71983 claims where it was established that Congress intended the statute\xe2\x80\x99s remedial scheme to \xe2\x80\x9cbe the exclusive avenue through which a plaintiff may assert [such] claims,\xe2\x80\x9d Smith, supra, at 1009. In determining whether Congress intended for a subsequent statute to preclude the enforcement of a federal right under \xc2\xa71983, the Court has placed primary emphasis on the nature and extent of that statute\xe2\x80\x99s remedial scheme. See Sea Clammers, 453 U. S., at 20. Where the \xc2\xa71983 claim alleges a constitutional violation, a lack of congressional intent to preclude may also be inferred from a comparison of the rights and protections of the other statute and those existing under the Constitution. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0In the absence of a comprehensive remedial scheme comparable to those at issue in Sea Clammers, Smith, and Rancho Palos Verdes, and in light of the divergent coverage of Title IX and the Equal Protection Clause, it must be concluded that Title IX was not meant to be an exclusive mechanism for addressing gender discrimination in schools, or a substitute for \xc2\xa71983 suits as a means of enforcing constitutional rights. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(i)\xc2\xa0Title IX\xe2\x80\x99s only express enforcement mechanism, 20 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71682, is an administrative procedure resulting in the withdrawal of federal funding from noncompliant institutions. This Court has also recognized an implied private right of action, Cannon v. University of Chicago, 441 U. S. 677, 717, for which both injunctive relief and damages are available, Franklin v. Gwinnett County Public Schools, 503 U. S. 60, 76. These remedies stand in stark contrast to the \xe2\x80\x9cunusually elaborate,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ccarefully tailored,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9crestrictive\xe2\x80\x9d enforcement schemes of the statutes in Sea Clammers, Smith, and Rancho Palos Verdes. Unlike those statutes, Title IX has no administrative exhaustion requirement and no notice provisions. Plaintiffs can file directly in court under its implied private right of action and can obtain the full range of remedies. Accordingly, parallel and concurrent \xc2\xa71983 claims will neither circumvent required procedures nor allow access to new remedies. Moreover, under Rancho Palos Verdes, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he provision of an express, private means of redress in the statute itself\xe2\x80\x9d is a key consideration in determining congressional intent, and \xe2\x80\x9cthe existence of a more restrictive private remedy for statutory violations has been the dividing line between those cases in which \xe2\x80\xa6 an action would lie under \xc2\xa71983 and those in which we have held that it would not.\xe2\x80\x9d 544 U. S., at 121. Title IX contains no express private remedy, much less a more restrictive one. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(ii)\xc2\xa0Because Title IX\xe2\x80\x99s protections are narrower in some respects and broader in others than those guaranteed under the Equal Protection Clause, the Court cannot agree with the First Circuit that Congress saw Title IX as the sole means of correcting unconstitutional gender discrimination in schools. Title IX reaches institutions and programs that receive federal funds, 20 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71681(a), which may include nonpublic institutions, \xc2\xa71681(c), but it has consistently been interpreted as not authorizing suit against school officials, teachers, and other individuals. Moreover, while the constitutional provision reaches only state actors, \xc2\xa71983 equal protection claims may be brought against individuals as well as state entities. West v. Atkins, 487 U. S. 42, 48\xe2\x80\x9351. And Title IX exempts from its restrictions several activities that may be challenged on constitutional grounds. See, e.g., \xc2\xa71681(a)(5). Even where particular activities and particular defendants are subject to both Title IX and the Equal Protection Clause, the standards for establishing liability may not be wholly congruent. Compare Gebser v. Lago Vista Independent School Dist., 524 U. S. 274, 290, with Monell v. New York City Dept. of Social Servs., 436 U. S. 658, 694. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(iii)\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion is consistent with Title IX\xe2\x80\x99s context and history. Because the Congress that enacted Title IX authorized the Attorney General to intervene in private suits alleging sex discrimination violative of the Equal Protection Clause, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000h\xe2\x80\x932, Congress must have explicitly envisioned that private plaintiffs would bring constitutional claims to challenge gender discrimination via \xc2\xa71983. Moreover, Title IX was modeled after Title VI of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, Cannon, supra, at 694\xe2\x80\x93695, and, at the time of Title IX\xe2\x80\x99s 1972 enactment, the lower courts routinely interpreted Title VI to allow for parallel and concurrent \xc2\xa71983 claims. Absent contrary evidence, it follows that Congress intended Title IX to be interpreted similarly to allow for parallel and concurrent \xc2\xa71983 claims. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0As neither of the courts below addressed the merits of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 constitutional claims or even the sufficiency of their pleadings, this Court will not do so in the first instance here. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

504 F.\xc2\xa03d 165, reversed and remanded.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Alito, J., delivered the opinion for a unanimous Court.

\nOPINION OF THE COURT
\nFITZGERALD V. BARNSTABLE SCHOOL COMM.
\n555 U. S. ____ (2009)
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 07-1125
\n
\n

LISA FITZGERALD, et vir, PETITIONERS v. BARN-\nSTABLE SCHOOL COMMITTEE et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the first circuit

\n

[January 21, 2009]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Alito delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The issue in this case of peer-on-peer sexual harassment is whether Title IX of the Education Amendments of 1972, 86 Stat. 373, 20 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71681(a), precludes an action under Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa71979, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983, alleging unconstitutional gender discrimination in schools. The Court of Appeals for the First Circuit held that it does. 504 F.\xc2\xa03d 165 (2007). We reverse.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because this case comes to us on a motion to dismiss under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 12(b)(6), we assume the truth of the facts as alleged in petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 complaint. During the 2000\xe2\x80\x932001 school year, the daughter of petitioners Lisa and Robert Fitzgerald was a kindergarten student in the Barnstable, Massachusetts, school system, and rode the bus to school each morning. One day she told her parents that, whenever she wore a dress, a third-grade boy on the school bus would bully her into lifting her skirt. Lisa Fitzgerald immediately called the school principal, Frederick Scully, who arranged a meeting later that day with the Fitzgeralds, their daughter, and another school official, Lynda Day. Scully and Day then questioned the alleged bully, who denied the allegations. Day also interviewed the bus driver and several students who rode the bus. She concluded that she could not corroborate the girl\xe2\x80\x99s version of the events.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Fitzgeralds\xe2\x80\x99 daughter then provided new details of the alleged abuse to her parents, who relayed them to Scully. Specifically, she told her parents that in addition to bullying her into raising her skirt, the boy coerced her into pulling down her underpants and spreading her legs. Scully scheduled a second meeting with the Fitzgeralds to discuss the additional details and again questioned the boy and other students.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Meanwhile, the local police department conducted an independent investigation and concluded there was insufficient evidence to bring criminal charges against the boy. Based partly on the police investigation and partly on the school\xe2\x80\x99s own investigation, Scully similarly concluded there was insufficient evidence to warrant discipline. Scully did propose remedial measures to the Fitzgeralds. He suggested transferring their daughter to a different bus or leaving rows of empty seats between the kindergarteners and older students on the original bus. The Fitzgeralds felt that these proposals punished their daughter instead of the boy and countered with alternative proposals. They suggested transferring the boy to a different bus or placing a monitor on the original bus. The Barnstable school system\xe2\x80\x99s superintendent, Russell Dever, did not act on these proposals.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Fitzgeralds began driving their daughter to school to avoid further bullying on the bus, but she continued to report unsettling incidents at school. The Fitzgeralds reported each incident to Scully. The Fitzgeralds\xe2\x80\x99 daughter had an unusual number of absences during the remainder of the school year.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In April 2002, the Fitzgeralds filed suit in District Court, alleging that the school system\xe2\x80\x99s response to their allegations of sexual harassment had been inadequate, resulting in further harassment to their daughter. Their complaint included: (1) a claim for violation of Title IX against the Barnstable School Committee (the school system\xe2\x80\x99s governing body), (2) claims under 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983 for violations of Title IX and the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment against the school committee and Dever, and (3) Massachusetts state-law claims against the school committee and Dever. The school committee and Dever (respondents here), filed a motion to dismiss, which the District Court granted as to the \xc2\xa71983 claims and the state-law claims. On the Title IX claim, the school committee filed a motion for summary judgment, which the District Court also granted. Hunter v. Barnstable School Committee, 456 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 255, 266 (Mass. 2006).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals for the First Circuit affirmed. 504 F.\xc2\xa03d 165. Turning first to the Title IX claim against the school committee, the court noted three points that were not in dispute: (1) the school committee was the recipient of federal funds and was therefore subject to Title IX, (2) the school committee had actual knowledge of the harassment the Fitzgeralds\xe2\x80\x99 daughter suffered, and (3) if the allegations of the complaint were true, the harassment was \xe2\x80\x9csevere, pervasive and objectively offensive.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 172. The court concluded that the Fitzgeralds\xe2\x80\x99 Title IX claim lacked merit, however, because the response of the school committee and Dever to the reported harassment had been objectively reasonable. Id., at 175.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals turned next to the Fitzgeralds\xe2\x80\x99 \xc2\xa71983 claims. Relying on this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents in Middlesex County Sewerage Authority v. National Sea Clammers Assn., 453 U. S. 1 (1981), Smith v. Robinson, 468 U. S. 992 (1984), and Rancho Palos Verdes v. Abrams, 544 U. S. 113 (2005), the court characterized Title IX\xe2\x80\x99s implied private remedy as \xe2\x80\x9csufficiently comprehensive\xe2\x80\x9d to preclude use of \xc2\xa71983 to advance statutory claims based on Title IX itself. 504 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 179. This reasoning, the court held, \xe2\x80\x9cappl[ied] with equal force\xe2\x80\x9d to the constitutional claims. Ibid. The court concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress saw Title IX as the sole means of vindicating the constitutional right to be free from gender discrimination perpetrated by educational institutions.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 decision deepened a conflict among the Circuits regarding whether Title IX precludes use of \xc2\xa71983 to redress unconstitutional gender discrimination in schools. Compare Bruneau ex rel. Schofield v. South Kortright Central School Dist., 163 F. 3d 749, 758\xe2\x80\x93759 (CA2 1998); Waid v. Merrill Area Public Schools 91 F.\xc2\xa03d 857, 862\xe2\x80\x93863 (CA7 1996); Pfeiffer v. Marion Center Area School Dist., 917 F. 2d 779, 789 (CA3 1990), with Communities for Equity v. Michigan High School Athletic Assn., 459 F. 3d 676, 691 (CA6 2006); Crawford v. Davis, 109 F. 3d 1281, 1284 (CA8 1997); Seamons v. Snow, 84 F.\xc2\xa03d 1226, 1234 (CA10 1996). We granted certiorari to resolve this conflict, 553 U. S. ___ (2008), and we now reverse.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In relevant part, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983, provides:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cEvery person who, under color of any statute, ordinance, regulation, custom, or usage, of any State or Territory or the District of Columbia, subjects, or causes to be subjected, any citizen of the United States or other person within the jurisdiction thereof to the deprivation of any rights, privileges, or immunities secured by the Constitution and laws, shall be liable to the party injured in an action at law, suit in equity, or other proper proceeding for redress.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\nIn three cases, this Court has found that statutory enactments precluded claims under this statute. Sea Clammers, supra; Smith, supra; Rancho Palos Verdes, supra. These cases establish that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he crucial consideration is what Congress intended.\xe2\x80\x9d Smith, 468 U. S., at 1012. If Congress intended a statute\xe2\x80\x99s remedial scheme to \xe2\x80\x9cbe the exclusive avenue through which a plaintiff may assert [the] claim,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 1009, the \xc2\xa71983 claims are precluded. See Rancho Palos Verdes, 544 U. S., at 120\xe2\x80\x93121 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe critical question, then, is whether Congress meant the judicial remedy authorized by [the statute] to coexist with an alternative remedy available in a \xc2\xa71983 action\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In those cases in which the \xc2\xa71983 claim is based on a statutory right, \xe2\x80\x9cevidence of such congressional intent may be found directly in the statute creating the right, or inferred from the statute\xe2\x80\x99s creation of a comprehensive enforcement scheme that is incompatible with individual enforcement under \xc2\xa71983.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 120 (internal quotation marks omitted). In cases in which the \xc2\xa71983 claim alleges a constitutional violation, lack of congressional intent may be inferred from a comparison of the rights and protections of the statute and those existing under the Constitution. Where the contours of such rights and protections diverge in significant ways, it is not likely that Congress intended to displace \xc2\xa71983 suits enforcing constitutional rights. Our conclusions regarding congressional intent can be confirmed by a statute\xe2\x80\x99s context. Id., at 127 (Breyer, J., concurring) (\xe2\x80\x9c[C]ontext, not just literal text, will often lead a court to Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent in respect to a particular statute\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In determining whether a subsequent statute precludes the enforcement of a federal right under \xc2\xa71983, we have placed primary emphasis on the nature and extent of that statute\xe2\x80\x99s remedial scheme. See Sea Clammers, supra, at 20 (\xe2\x80\x9cWhen the remedial devices provided in a particular Act are sufficiently comprehensive, they may suffice to demonstrate congressional intent to preclude the remedy of suits under \xc2\xa71983\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Sea Clammers illustrates this approach. The plaintiffs brought suit under \xc2\xa71983 for violations of the Federal Water Pollution Control Act and the Marine Protection, Research, and Sanctuaries Act of 1972. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis focused on these two statutes\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cunusually elaborate enforcement provisions,\xe2\x80\x9d which authorized the Environmental Protection Agency to seek civil and criminal penalties for violations, permitted \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98any interested person\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to seek judicial review, and contained detailed citizen suit provisions allowing for injunctive relief. 453 U. S., at 13\xe2\x80\x9314. Allowing parallel \xc2\xa71983 claims to proceed, we concluded, would have thwarted Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent in formulating and detailing these provisions.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Smith, the plaintiffs alleged deprivation of a free, appropriate public education for their handicapped child, in violation of the Education of the Handicapped Act (EHA) and the Due Process and Equal Protection Clauses of the Fourteenth Amendment. Departing from the pattern of the plaintiffs in Sea Clammers, the Smith plaintiffs relied on \xc2\xa71983 to assert independent constitutional rights, not to assert the statutory rights guaranteed by the EHA. As in Sea Clammers, however, this Court focused on the statute\xe2\x80\x99s detailed remedial scheme in concluding that Congress intended the statute to provide the sole avenue for relief. Smith, 468 U. S., at 1011 (noting \xe2\x80\x9cthe comprehensive nature of the procedures and guarantees set out in the [the statute] and Congress\xe2\x80\x99 express efforts to place on local and state educational agencies the primary responsibility for developing a plan to accommodate the needs of each individual handicapped child\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Rancho Palos Verdes, we again focused on a statute\xe2\x80\x99s remedial scheme in inferring congressional intent for exclusivity. After being denied a permit to build a radio tower on his property, the plaintiff brought claims for injunctive relief under the Telecommunications Act of 1996 (TCA) and for damages and attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees under \xc2\xa71983. Noting that the TCA provides highly detailed and restrictive administrative and judicial remedies, and explaining that \xe2\x80\x9climitations upon the remedy contained in the statute are deliberate and are not to be evaded through \xc2\xa71983,\xe2\x80\x9d we again concluded that Congress must have intended the statutory remedies to be exclusive. 544 U. S., at 124.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In all three cases, the statutes at issue required plaintiffs to comply with particular procedures and/or to exhaust particular administrative remedies prior to filing suit. Sea Clammers, supra, at 6; Smith, supra, at 1011\xe2\x80\x931012; Rancho Palos Verdes, supra, at 122. Offering plaintiffs a direct route to court via \xc2\xa71983 would have circumvented these procedures and given plaintiffs access to tangible benefits\xe2\x80\x94such as damages, attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees, and costs\xe2\x80\x94that were unavailable under the statutes.[Footnote 1] \xe2\x80\x9cAllowing a plaintiff to circumvent\xe2\x80\x9d the statutes\xe2\x80\x99 provisions in this way would have been \xe2\x80\x9cinconsistent with Congress\xe2\x80\x99 carefully tailored scheme.\xe2\x80\x9d Smith, supra, at 1012.

\n

B

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 901(a) of Title IX provides:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cNo person in the United States shall, on the basis of sex, be excluded from participation in, be denied the benefits of, or be subjected to discrimination under any education program or activity receiving Federal financial assistance.\xe2\x80\x9d 20 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71681(a).

\n

\nThe statute\xe2\x80\x99s only express enforcement mechanism, \xc2\xa71682, is an administrative procedure resulting in the withdrawal of federal funding from institutions that are not in compliance. In addition, this Court has recognized an implied private right of action. Cannon v. University of Chicago, 441 U. S. 677, 717 (1979). In a suit brought pursuant to this private right, both injunctive relief and damages are available. Franklin v. Gwinnett County Public Schools, 503 U. S. 60, 76 (1992).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These remedies\xe2\x80\x94withdrawal of federal funds and an implied cause of action\xe2\x80\x94stand in stark contrast to the \xe2\x80\x9cunusually elaborate,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ccarefully tailored,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9crestrictive\xe2\x80\x9d enforcement schemes of the statutes at issue in Sea Clammers, Smith, and Rancho Palos Verdes. Unlike those statutes, Title IX has no administrative exhaustion requirement and no notice provisions. Under its implied private right of action, plaintiffs can file directly in court, Cannon, supra, at 717, and can obtain the full range of remedies, see Franklin, supra, at 72 (concluding that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress did not intend to limit the remedies available in a suit brought under Title IX\xe2\x80\x9d). As a result, parallel and concurrent \xc2\xa71983 claims will neither circumvent required procedures, nor allow access to new remedies.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, this Court explained in Rancho Palos Verdes that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he provision of an express, private means of redress in the statute itself\xe2\x80\x9d is a key consideration in determining congressional intent, and that \xe2\x80\x9cthe existence of a more restrictive private remedy for statutory violations has been the dividing line between those cases in which we have held that an action would lie under \xc2\xa71983 and those in which we have held that it would not.\xe2\x80\x9d 544 U. S., at 121 (emphasis added). As noted, Title IX contains no express private remedy, much less a more restrictive one. This Court has never held that an implied right of action had the effect of precluding suit under \xc2\xa71983, likely because of the difficulty of discerning congressional intent in such a situation. See Franklin, supra, at 76 (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9cQuite obviously, the search for what was Congress\xe2\x80\x99 remedial intent as to a right whose very existence Congress did not expressly acknowledge is unlikely to succeed\xe2\x80\x9d). Mindful that we should \xe2\x80\x9cnot lightly conclude that Congress intended to preclude reliance on \xc2\xa71983 as a remedy for a substantial equal protection claim,\xe2\x80\x9d Smith, 468 U. S., at 1012, we see no basis for doing so here.

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A comparison of the substantive rights and protections guaranteed under Title IX and under the Equal Protection Clause lends further support to the conclusion that Congress did not intend Title IX to preclude \xc2\xa71983 constitutional suits. Title IX\xe2\x80\x99s protections are narrower in some respects and broader in others. Because the protections guaranteed by the two sources of law diverge in this way, we cannot agree with the Court of Appeals that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress saw Title IX as the sole means of vindicating the constitutional right to be free from gender discrimination perpetrated by educational institutions.\xe2\x80\x9d 504 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 179.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Title IX reaches institutions and programs that receive federal funds, 20 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71681(a), which may include nonpublic institutions, \xc2\xa71681(c), but it has consistently been interpreted as not authorizing suit against school officials, teachers, and other individuals, see, e.g., Hartley v. Parnell, 193 F.\xc2\xa03d 1263, 1270 (CA11 1999). The Equal Protection Clause reaches only state actors, but \xc2\xa71983 equal protection claims may be brought against individuals as well as municipalities and certain other state entities. West v. Atkins, 487 U. S. 42, 48\xe2\x80\x9351 (1988).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Title IX exempts from its restrictions several activities that may be challenged on constitutional grounds. For example, Title IX exempts elementary and secondary schools from its prohibition against discrimination in admissions, \xc2\xa71681(a)(1); it exempts military service schools and traditionally single-sex public colleges from all of its provisions, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71681(a)(4)\xe2\x80\x93(5). Some exempted activities may form the basis of equal protection claims. See United States v. Virginia, 518 U. S. 515, 534 (1996) (men-only admissions policy at Virginia Military Institute violated the Equal Protection Clause); Mississippi Univ. for Women v. Hogan, 458 U. S. 718, 731 (1982) (women-only admission policy at a traditionally single-sex public college violated the Equal Protection Clause).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even where particular activities and particular defendants are subject to both Title IX and the Equal Protection Clause, the standards for establishing liability may not be wholly congruent. For example, a Title IX plaintiff can establish school district liability by showing that a single school administrator with authority to take corrective action responded to harassment with deliberate indifference. Gebser v. Lago Vista Independent School Dist., 524 U. S. 274, 290 (1998). A plaintiff stating a similar claim via \xc2\xa71983 for violation of the Equal Protection Clause by a school district or other municipal entity must show that the harassment was the result of municipal custom, policy, or practice. Monell v. New York City Dept. of Social Servs., 436 U. S. 658, 694 (1978).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In light of the divergent coverage of Title IX and the Equal Protection Clause, as well as the absence of a comprehensive remedial scheme comparable to those at issue in Sea Clammers, Smith, and Rancho Palos Verdes, we conclude that Title IX was not meant to be an exclusive mechanism for addressing gender discrimination in schools, or a substitute for \xc2\xa71983 suits as a means of enforcing constitutional rights. Accordingly, we hold that \xc2\xa71983 suits based on the Equal Protection Clause remain available to plaintiffs alleging unconstitutional gender discrimination in schools.

\n

3

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This conclusion is consistent with Title IX\xe2\x80\x99s context and history. In enacting Title IX, Congress amended \xc2\xa7902, 78 Stat. 266\xe2\x80\x93267, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000h\xe2\x80\x932 to authorize the Attorney General to intervene in private suits alleging discrimination on the basis of sex in violation of the Equal Protection Clause. See \xc2\xa7906, 86 Stat. 375 (adding the term \xe2\x80\x9csex\xe2\x80\x9d to the listed grounds, which already included race, color, religion or national origin). Accordingly, it appears that the Congress that enacted Title IX explicitly envisioned that private plaintiffs would bring constitutional claims to challenge gender discrimination; it must have recognized that plaintiffs would do so via 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, Congress modeled Title IX after Title VI of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, Cannon, 441 U. S., at 694\xe2\x80\x93695, and passed Title IX with the explicit understanding that it would be interpreted as Title VI was, id., at 696. At the time of Title IX\xe2\x80\x99s enactment in 1972, Title VI was routinely interpreted to allow for parallel and concurrent \xc2\xa71983 claims, see, e.g., Alvarado v. El Paso Independent School Dist., 445 F.\xc2\xa02d 1011 (CA5 1971); Nashville I\xe2\x80\x9340 Steering Comm. v. Ellington, 387 F.\xc2\xa02d 179 (CA6 1967); Bossier Parish School Bd. v. Lemon, 370 F.\xc2\xa02d 847 (CA5 1967), and we presume Congress was aware of this when it passed Title IX, see Franklin, 503 U. S., at 71 (in assessing Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent, \xe2\x80\x9cwe evaluate the state of the law when the Legislature passed Title IX\xe2\x80\x9d). In the absence of any contrary evidence, it follows that Congress intended Title IX to be interpreted similarly to allow for parallel and concurrent \xc2\xa71983 claims. At the least, this indicates that Congress did not affirmatively intend Title IX to preclude such claims.[Footnote 2]

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0One matter remains. Respondents contend that the judgment of the Court of Appeals should be affirmed on independent grounds\xe2\x80\x94namely, that the Fitzgeralds have no actionable \xc2\xa71983 claim on which to proceed. They contend that the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 holding that neither the school committee nor Dever acted with deliberate indifference is conclusive and forecloses a \xc2\xa71983 constitutional claim based on a similar theory of liability. They contend that all other \xc2\xa71983 constitutional claims on these facts are precluded by the Fitzgeralds\xe2\x80\x99 failure to allege such claims adequately or to preserve them on appeal.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Fitzgeralds respond that they have no intention of relitigating the issue of deliberate indifference. They intend, they say, to advance claims of discriminatory treatment in the investigation of student behavior and in the treatment of student complaints, which they were foreclosed from developing at the earliest stages of litigation by the dismissal of the \xc2\xa71983 claims.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the Fitzgeralds note, no court has addressed the merits of their constitutional claims or even the sufficiency of their pleadings. Ordinarily, \xe2\x80\x9cwe do not decide in the first instance issues not decided below,\xe2\x80\x9d National Collegiate Athletic Assn. v. Smith, 525 U. S. 459, 470 (1999), and we see no reason for doing so here.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, we reverse the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 judgment that the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s dismissal of the \xc2\xa71983 claims was proper and remand this case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.
