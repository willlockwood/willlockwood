\n\t\t\t\tChapter 12 of the Bankruptcy Code allows farmer debtors with regular annual income to adjust their debts subject to a reorganization plan. The plan must provide for full payment of priority claims. \n\n11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71222(a)(2). Under \xc2\xa71222(a)(2)(A), however, certain governmental claims arising from the disposition of farm assets are stripped of priority status and downgraded to general, unsecured claims that are dischargeable after less than full payment. That exception applies only to claims \xe2\x80\x9centitled to priority under [\n\n11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7507]\xe2\x80\x9d in the first place. As relevant here, \xc2\xa7507(a)(2) covers \xe2\x80\x9cadministrative expenses allowed under \xc2\xa7503(b),\xe2\x80\x9d which includes \xe2\x80\x9cany tax .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. incurred by the estate.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7503(b)(B)(i).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners filed for Chapter 12 bankruptcy and then sold their farm. They proposed a plan under which they would pay off outstanding liabilities with proceeds from the sale. The Internal Revenue Service (IRS) objected, asserting a tax on the capital gains from the sale. Petitioners then proposed treating the tax as an unsecured claim to be paid to the extent funds were available, with the unpaid balance being discharged. The Bankruptcy Court sustained an IRS objection, the District Court reversed, and the Ninth Circuit reversed the District Court. The Ninth Circuit held that because a Chapter 12 estate is not a separate taxable entity under the Internal Revenue Code (IRC), 26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71398, 1399, it does not \xe2\x80\x9cincur\xe2\x80\x9d postpetition federal income taxes. The Ninth Circuit concluded that because the tax was not \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the estate\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa7503(b), it was not a priority claim eligible for the \xc2\xa71222(a)(2)(A) exception.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0The federal income tax liability resulting from petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 postpetition farm sale is not \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the estate\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa7503(b) of the Bankruptcy Code and thus is neither collectible nor dischargeable in the Chapter 12 plan. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x88\x9217.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0The phrase \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the estate\xe2\x80\x9d bears a plain and natural reading. A tax \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the estate\xe2\x80\x9d is a tax for which the estate itself is liable. Only certain estates are liable for federal income taxes. IRC \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71398 and 1399 define the division of responsibilities for the payment of taxes between the estate and the debtor on a chapter-by-chapter basis. Under those provisions, a Chapter 12 estate is not a separately taxable entity. The debtor\xe2\x80\x94not the trustee\xe2\x80\x94is generally liable for taxes and files the only tax return. The postpetition income taxes are thus not \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the estate.\xe2\x80\x9d Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x88\x925.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0Section 346 of the Bankruptcy Code and its longstanding interplay with IRC \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71398 and 1399 reinforce that whether an estate \xe2\x80\x9cincurs\xe2\x80\x9d taxes turns on Congress\xe2\x80\x99 chapter-specific guidance on which estates are separately taxable. The original \xc2\xa7346 established that state or local income taxes could be imposed only on the estate in an individual-debtor Chapter 7 or 11 bankruptcy, and only on the debtor in a Chapter 13 bankruptcy. Congress applied the framework of \xc2\xa7346 to federal taxes two years later: IRC \xc2\xa71398 and 1399 established that the estate is separately taxable in individual-debtor Chapter 7 or 11 cases, and not separately taxable in Chapter 13 (and now Chapter 12) cases. The Bankruptcy Abuse Prevention and Consumer Protection Act of 2005 subsequently amended \xc2\xa7346, expressly aligning its assignment of state or local taxes with the IRC separate taxable entity rules for federal taxes. This Court assumes that Congress is aware of existing law when it passes legislation, and the existing law at the enactment of \xc2\xa71222(a)(2)(A) indicated that an estate\xe2\x80\x99s liability for taxes turned on separate taxable entity rules. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x88\x929.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0Chapter 13, on which Chapter 12 was modeled, further bolsters this Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding. Established understandings hold that postpetition income taxes are not \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the [Chapter 13] estate\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa7503(b) because they are the liability of the Chapter 13 debtor alone. The Government has also long hewed to this position. Section 1305(a)(1), which gives holders of postpetition claims the option of collecting postpetition taxes within the bankruptcy case, would be superfluous if postpetition tax liabilities were automatically collectible inside the bankruptcy. It is thus clear that postpetition income taxes are not automatically collectible in a Chapter 13 plan and are not administrative expenses under \xc2\xa7503(b). To hold otherwise in Chapter 12 would disrupt settled practices in Chapter 13 cases. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x88\x9212.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(d)\xc2\xa0None of the contrary arguments by petitioners and the dissent overcomes the statute\xe2\x80\x99s plain language, context, and structure. There is no textual basis for giving \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the estate\xe2\x80\x9d a temporal meaning, such that it refers to all taxes \xe2\x80\x9cincurred postpetition.\xe2\x80\x9d Nor does the text support deeming a tax \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the estate\xe2\x80\x9d whenever it is paid by the debtor out of property of the estate. Section 503\xe2\x80\x99s legislative history is not inconsistent with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding, and the Court has cautioned against allowing ambiguous legislative history to muddy clear statutory language. See Milner v. Department of Navy, 562 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. Meanwhile, any cases suggesting that postpetition taxes were treated as administrative expenses are inapposite because they involve corporate debtors, which Congress has singled out for responsibilities paralleling those borne by a separate taxable entity\xe2\x80\x99s trustee. Finally, petitioners contend that the purpose of \xc2\xa71222(a)(2)(A) was to provide debtors with robust relief from tax debts. There may be compelling policy reasons for treating postpetition income tax liabilities as dischargeable. But if Congress intended petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 result, it did not so provide in the statute. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x88\x9217.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUnder Chapter 12 of the Bankruptcy Code, farmer debtors may treat certain claims owed to a governmental unit resulting from the disposition of farm assets as dischargeable, unsecured liabilities. \n\n11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71222(a) (2)(A). One such claim is for \xe2\x80\x9cany tax .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. incurred by the estate.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7503(b)(B)(i). The question presented is whether a federal income tax liability resulting from individual debtors\xe2\x80\x99 sale of a farm during the pendency of a Chapter 12 bankruptcy is \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the estate\xe2\x80\x9d and thus dischargeable. We hold that it is not.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 1986, Congress enacted Chapter 12 of the Bankruptcy Code, \xc2\xa71201 et\xc2\xa0seq., to allow farmer debtors with regu- lar annual income to adjust their debts. Chapter 12 was modeled on Chapter 13, \xc2\xa71301 et\xc2\xa0seq., which permits individual debtors with regular annual income to preserve existing assets subject to a \xe2\x80\x9ccourt-approved plan under which they pay creditors out of their future income.\xe2\x80\x9d Hamilton v. Lanning, 560 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 1). Chapter 12 debtors similarly file a plan of reorganization. \xc2\xa71221. To be confirmed, the plan must provide for the full payment of priority claims. \xc2\xa71222(a)(2).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn the Bankruptcy Abuse Prevention and Consumer Protection Act of 2005 (BAPCPA), \xc2\xa71003, \n\n119Stat.\n186, Congress created an exception to that requirement:\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c(A) the claim is a claim owed to a governmental unit that arises as a result of the sale, transfer, exchange, or other disposition of any farm asset used in the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s farming operation, in which case the claim shall be treated as an unsecured claim that is not entitled to priority under section 507, but the debt shall be treated in such manner only if the debtor receives a discharge.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71222.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tUnder \xc2\xa71222(a)(2)(A), certain governmental claims resulting from the disposition of farm assets are downgraded to general, unsecured claims that are dischargeable after less than full payment. See \xc2\xa71228(a). The claims are stripped of their priority status.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThat exception, however, applies only to claims in the plan that are \xe2\x80\x9centitled to priority under section 507\xe2\x80\x9d in the first place. Section 507 lists 10 categories of such claims. Two pertain to taxes: One category, \xc2\xa7507(a)(8), covers prepetition taxes, and is inapplicable in this case. The other, \xc2\xa7507(a)(2), covers \xe2\x80\x9cadministrative expenses allowed under section 503(b),\xe2\x80\x9d which in turn includes \xe2\x80\x9cany tax .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. incurred by the estate.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7503(b)(B)(i). Thus, for postpetition taxes to be entitled to priority under \xc2\xa7507 and eligible for the \xc2\xa71222(a)(2)(A) exception, the taxes must be \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the estate.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners Lynwood and Brenda Hall petitioned for bankruptcy under Chapter 12 and sold their farm shortly thereafter. Petitioners initially proposed a plan of reorganization under which they would pay off outstanding liabilities with proceeds from the sale. The Internal Revenue Service (IRS) objected, asserting a federal income tax of $29,000 on the capital gains from the farm sale.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners amended their proposal to treat the income tax as a general, unsecured claim to be paid to the extent funds were available, with the unpaid balance discharged. Again the IRS objected. Taxes on income from a postpetition farm sale, the IRS argued, remain the debtors\xe2\x80\x99 independent responsibility because they are neither collectible nor dischargeable in bankruptcy.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Bankruptcy Court sustained the objection. The court reasoned that because a Chapter 12 estate is not a separate taxable entity under the Internal Revenue Code (IRC), see 26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71398, 1399, it cannot \xe2\x80\x9cincur\xe2\x80\x9d taxes for purposes of \n\n11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7503(b).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe District Court reversed, expressing doubt that IRC provisions are relevant to interpreting \xc2\xa7503(b). Based on its reading of legislative history, the District Court determined that Congress intended \xc2\xa71222(a)(2)(A) to extend to petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 postpetition taxes.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit reversed. 617 F.\xc2\xa03d 1161 (2010). The Court of Appeals held that the Chapter 12 estate does not \xe2\x80\x9cincur\xe2\x80\x9d the postpetition federal income taxes for purposes of \xc2\xa7503(b) because it is not a separate taxable entity under the IRC, and noted that Congress repeatedly has indicated the relevance of the IRC\xe2\x80\x99s taxable entity provisions to the Bankruptcy Code. Although \xe2\x80\x9csympathetic\xe2\x80\x9d to the view that the postpetition tax liabilities should be dischargeable, the Court of Appeals held that \xe2\x80\x9cthe operative language simply failed to make its way into the statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1167. The Court of Appeals concluded that because the taxes do not qualify under \xc2\xa7503(b), they are not priority claims in the plan eligible for the \xc2\xa71222(a)(2)(A) exception.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJudge Paez dissented, siding with a sister Circuit that had concluded that Congress intended \xc2\xa71222(a)(2)(A) to extend to such postpetition federal income taxes. We granted certiorari to resolve the split of authority.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2011).\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur resolution of this case turns on the meaning of a phrase in \xc2\xa7503(b) of the Bankruptcy Code: \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the estate.\xe2\x80\x9d The parties agree that \xc2\xa71222(a)(2)(A) applies only to priority claims collectible in the bankruptcy plan and that postpetition federal income taxes so qualify only if they constitute a \xe2\x80\x9ctax .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. incurred by the estate.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7503(b)(B)(i).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe phrase \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the estate\xe2\x80\x9d bears a plain and natural reading. See FCC v. AT&T Inc., 562 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 5) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhen a statute does not define a term, we typically \xe2\x80\x98give the phrase its ordinary meaning\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). To \xe2\x80\x9cincur,\xe2\x80\x9d one must \xe2\x80\x9csuffer or bring on oneself (a liability or expense).\xe2\x80\x9d Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 836 (9th ed. 2009); see also Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third New International Dictionary 1146 (1976) (\xe2\x80\x9cto .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. become liable or subject to: bring down upon oneself\xe2\x80\x9d); Random House Dictionary 722 (1966) (\xe2\x80\x9cto become liable or subject to through one\xe2\x80\x99s own action; bring upon oneself\xe2\x80\x9d). A tax \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the estate\xe2\x80\x9d is a tax for which the estate itself is liable.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs the IRC makes clear, only certain estates are liable for federal income taxes. Title 26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71398 and 1399 address taxation in bankruptcy and define the division of responsibilities for the payment of taxes between the estate and the debtor on a chapter-by-chapter basis. Section 1398 provides that when an individual debtor files for Chapter 7 or 11 bankruptcy, the estate shall be liable for taxes. In such cases, the trustee files a separate re- turn on the estate\xe2\x80\x99s behalf and \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he tax\xe2\x80\x9d on \xe2\x80\x9cthe taxable income of the estate .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall be paid by the trustee.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71398(c)(1); see also \xc2\xa76012(b)(4) (\xe2\x80\x9cReturns of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. an estate of an individual under chapter 7 or 11 .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall be made by the fiduciary thereof\xe2\x80\x9d). Section 1399 provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]xcept in any case to which section 1398 applies, no separate taxable entity shall result from the commencement of a [bankruptcy] case.\xe2\x80\x9d In Chapter 12 and 13 cases, then, there is no separately taxable estate. The debtor\xe2\x80\x94not the trustee\xe2\x80\x94is generally liable for taxes and files the only tax return. See In\xc2\xa0re Lindsey, 142 B.\xc2\xa0R. 447, 448 (Bkrtcy. Ct. WD Okla. 1992) (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is clear that, pursuant to \n\n26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71398 and 1399, the standing Chapter 12 trustee neither files a return nor pays federal income tax\xe2\x80\x9d); cf. infra, at 15 (discussing special trustee duties in corporate-debtor cases).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThese provisions suffice to resolve this case: Chapter 12 estates are not taxable entities. Petitioners, not the estate itself, are required to file the tax return and are liable for the taxes resulting from their postpetition farm sale. The postpetition federal income tax liability is not \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the estate\xe2\x80\x9d and thus is neither collectible nor discharge- able in the Chapter 12 plan.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur reading of \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the estate\xe2\x80\x9d as informed by the IRC\xe2\x80\x99s separate taxable entity rules draws support from a related provision of the Bankruptcy Code, \n\n11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7346, and its longstanding interplay with 26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71398 and 1399. That relationship illustrates that from the inception of the current Bankruptcy Code, Congress has specified on a chapter-by-chapter basis which estates are separately taxable and therefore liable for taxes. That relationship also refutes the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion that applying such rules is an incongruous importation of \xe2\x80\x9ctax law\xe2\x80\x9d unconnected to \xe2\x80\x9cbankruptcy principles (as Congress understood them).\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 8\xe2\x80\x939 (opinion of Breyer, J.). And it reinforces the reason- ableness of our view that whether an estate \xe2\x80\x9cincurs\xe2\x80\x9d taxes under \xc2\xa7503(b) turns on such chapter-by-chapter distinctions.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn the original Bankruptcy Code, Congress included a provision, \xc2\xa7346, that set out a chapter-specific division of tax liabilities between the estate and the debtor. Bankruptcy Reform Act of 1978, \n\n92Stat.\n2565. Section 346(b)(1) provided that in an individual-debtor Chapter 7 or 11 bankruptcy, \xe2\x80\x9cany income of the estate may be taxed under a State or local law imposing a tax .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. only to the estate, and may not be taxed to such individual.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n92Stat.\n2565 (emphasis added); see also 11 Collier on Bankruptcy \xc2\xb6TX12.03[5][b][i], p. TX12\xe2\x80\x9321 (16th ed. 2011) (hereinafter Collier) (\xc2\xa7346(b) \xe2\x80\x9cprovided that in a case under chapter 7 [or] 11 .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the estate of an individual is a taxable entity\xe2\x80\x9d). Section 346(d) provided, meanwhile, that in a Chapter 13 bankruptcy, \xe2\x80\x9cany income of the estate or the debtor may be taxed under a State or local law imposing a tax .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. only to the debtor, and may not be taxed to the estate.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n92Stat.\n2566 (emphasis added). Congress thus established that the estate in an individual-debtor Chapter 7 or 11 bankruptcy is a separate taxable entity; the estate in a Chapter 13 bankruptcy is not.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAlthough \xc2\xa7346 concerned state or local taxes,\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Congress applied its framework to federal taxes two years later. In the Bankruptcy Tax Act of 1980, \n\n94Stat.\n3397, Congress enacted 26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71398 and 1399. Section 1398 of the IRC, much like \xc2\xa7346(b) in the Bankruptcy Code, established that the estate is separately taxable in individual-debtor Chapter 7 or 11 cases. Section 1399 of the IRC, much like \xc2\xa7346(d) in the Bankruptcy Code, clarified that the estate is not separately taxable in Chapter 13 (and now Chapter 12) cases.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 2005, Congress in BAPCPA amended \xc2\xa7346 and crystallized the connection between the Bankruptcy Code and the IRC. Section 346 now expressly aligns its assignment of state or local taxes with the rules for federal taxes, providing in relevant part:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c(a) Whenever the Internal Revenue Code of 1986 provides that a separate taxable estate or entity is created in a case concerning a debtor under this title, and the income .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of such estate shall be taxed to or claimed by the estate, a separate taxable estate is also created for purposes of any State and local law imposing a tax on or measured by income and such income .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall be taxed to or claimed by the estate and may not be taxed to or claimed by the debtor.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c(b) Whenever the Internal Revenue Code of 1986 provides that no separate taxable estate shall be created in a case concerning a debtor under this title, and the income .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of an estate shall be taxed to or claimed by the debtor, such income .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall be taxed to or claimed by the debtor under a State or local law imposing a tax on or measured by income and may not be taxed to or claimed by the estate.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.)\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThus, whenever the estate is separately taxable under federal income tax law, that \xe2\x80\x9cis also\xe2\x80\x9d the case under state or local income tax law, \xc2\xa7346(a), and vice versa, \xc2\xa7346(b). And given that the Bankruptcy Code instructs that the as- signment of state or local tax liabilities shall turn on the IRC\xe2\x80\x99s separate taxable entity rules, there is parity in turning to such rules in assigning federal tax liabilities.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn the same Act, Congress added \xc2\xa71222(a)(2)(A). Section 1222(a)(2)(A) carves out an exception to the ordinary priority classification scheme. But \xc2\xa71222(a)(2)(A) did not purport to redefine which claims are otherwise entitled to priority, much less alter the underlying division of tax liability between the estate and the debtor in Chapter 12 cases. \xe2\x80\x9cWe assume that Congress is aware of existing law when it passes legislation,\xe2\x80\x9d Miles v. Apex Marine Corp., \n\n498 U.\xc2\xa0S. 19, \n32 (1990)\n, and the existing law at the enactment of \xc2\xa71222(a)(2)(A) indicated that an estate\xe2\x80\x99s liability for taxes turned on chapter-by-chapter separate taxable entity rules.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tC\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe statutory structure further reinforces our holding that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 postpetition income taxes are not \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the estate.\xe2\x80\x9d As a leading bankruptcy treatise and lower courts recognize, \xe2\x80\x9c[b]ecause chapter 12 was modeled on chapter 13, and because so many of the provisions are identical, chapter 13 cases construing provisions corresponding to chapter 12 provisions may be relied on as authority in chapter 12 cases.\xe2\x80\x9d 8 Collier \xc2\xb61200.01[5], at 1200\xe2\x80\x9310; In re Lopez, 372 B.\xc2\xa0R. 40, 45, n. 13 (Bkrtcy. App. Panel CA9 2007); Justice v. Valley Nat. Bank, 849 F.\xc2\xa02d 1078, 1083 (CA8 1988). We agree. Section 1322(a)(2), like \xc2\xa71222(a)(2), requires full payment of \xe2\x80\x9call claims entitled to priority under section 507\xe2\x80\x9d under the plan. Both provisions cross-reference the same section of the Code, \xc2\xa7507, and in turn, the same subsection, \xc2\xa7503(b). Both are treated alike by IRC \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71398 and 1399. Whether postpetition taxes qualify under \xc2\xa7503(b) in Chapter 13 thus sheds light on whether they so qualify in petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 Chapter 12 case.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBankruptcy courts and commentators have reasoned that postpetition income taxes are not \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the estate\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa7503(b) because \xe2\x80\x9ca tax on postpetition income of the debtor or of the chapter 13 estate is not a liability of the chapter 13 estate; it is a liability of the debtor alone.\xe2\x80\x9d 8 Collier \xc2\xb61305.02[1], at 1305\xe2\x80\x935 and 1305\xe2\x80\x936.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t For over a decade, the Government has likewise hewed to the position that \xe2\x80\x9csince post-petition tax liabilities are, in Chapter 13 cases, incurred by the debtor, rather than the bankruptcy estate, characterizing such liabilities as administrative expenses is inconsistent with section 503.\xe2\x80\x9d IRS Chief Counsel Advice No. 200113027, p.\xc2\xa06 (Mar. 30, 2001), 2001 WL 307746, *4; see also Internal Revenue Manual \xc2\xa75.9.10.9.2(3) (2006) (hereinafter IRM); IRS Litigation Guideline Memorandum GL\xe2\x80\x9326, p.\xc2\xa09 (Dec. 16, 1996), 1996 WL 33107107, *6. We see no reason to depart from those established understandings. To \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98hold the Chapter 13 estate liable for [a] tax when it does not exist as a taxable entity defies common sense as well as Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d In\xc2\xa0re Whall, 391 B.\xc2\xa0R. 1, 4 (Bkrtcy. Ct. Mass. 2008). The same holds true for a Chapter 12 estate.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA provision in Chapter 13 confirms that postpetition income taxes fall outside \xc2\xa7503(b). Section 1305(a)(1) pro- vides that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] proof of claim may be filed by any entity that holds a claim against the debtor .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. for taxes that become payable to a governmental unit while the case is pending.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.) That provision gives holders of postpetition claims the option of collecting postpetition taxes within the bankruptcy case\xe2\x80\x94an option that the Government would never need to invoke if postpetition tax liabilities were already collectible inside the bankruptcy. Accordingly, lest we render \xc2\xa71305 \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98inoperative or superfluous,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Hibbs v. Winn, \n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 88, \n101 (2004)\n, it is clear that postpetition income taxes are not automatically collectible in a Chapter 13 plan and, a\xc2\xa0fortiori, are not administrative expenses under \xc2\xa7503(b).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt follows that postpetition income taxes are not automatically collectible in petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 Chapter 12 plan.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Because both chapters cross-reference \xc2\xa7503(b) in an identical manner, see \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71222(a)(2), 1322(a)(2), we are cognizant that any conflicting reading of \xc2\xa7503(b) here could disrupt settled Chapter 13 practices. See Cohen v. de la Cruz, \n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 213, \n221 (1998)\n (the Court \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98will not read the Bankruptcy Code to erode past bankruptcy practice absent a clear indication that Congress intended such a departure\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). Chapter 13 filings outnumber Chapter 12 filings six-hundred-fold. See U.\xc2\xa0S. Bankruptcy Courts\xe2\x80\x94Cases Commenced During the 12-Month Period Ending September 30, 2011 (Table F\xe2\x80\x932), http://www.uscourts.gov/ Statistics/BankruptcyStatistics.aspx (estimating 676 and 417,503 annual Chapter 12 and 13 filings, respectively) (as visited May 14, 2012, and available in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file). Yet adopting petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 reading of \xc2\xa7503(b) would mean that, in every Chapter 13 case, the Government could ignore \xc2\xa71305 and expect priority payment of postpetition income taxes in every plan.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAt bottom, \xe2\x80\x9cidentical words and phrases within the same statute should normally be given the same meaning.\xe2\x80\x9d Powerex Corp. v. Reliant Energy Services, Inc., \n\n551 U.\xc2\xa0S. 224, \n232 (2007)\n. Absent any indication that Congress intended a conflict between two closely related chapters, we decline to create one.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t7\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners and the dissent advance several arguments for why the postpetition income taxes at issue should be considered \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the estate,\xe2\x80\x9d notwithstanding the IRC\xe2\x80\x99s separate taxable entity rules. But none provides sufficient reason to overcome the statute\xe2\x80\x99s plain language, context, and structure.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners primarily argue that \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the estate\xe2\x80\x9d has a temporal meaning. Petitioners emphasize that the estate only comes into existence after a bankruptcy petition is filed. Thus, they reason, taxes \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the estate\xe2\x80\x9d refers to all taxes \xe2\x80\x9cincurred postpetition,\xe2\x80\x9d regardless of whether the estate is liable for the tax and regardless of the chapter under which a case is filed. Although all taxes \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the estate\xe2\x80\x9d are necessarily incurred postpetition, not all taxes incurred postpetition are \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the estate.\xe2\x80\x9d That an estate cannot incur liability until it exists does not mean that every liability that arises after that point automatically becomes the estate\xe2\x80\x99s liability. And there is no textual basis to focus on when the liability is incurred, as opposed to whether the liability is incurred \xe2\x80\x9cby the estate.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAlternately, petitioners contend that a tax should be considered \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the estate\xe2\x80\x9d so long as it is payable out of estate assets. Income from postpetition sales of farm assets is considered property of the estate. See \xc2\xa71207(a). Petitioners argue that even if the debtor\xe2\x80\x94and not the estate\xe2\x80\x94is liable for a tax, the tax is still \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the estate\xe2\x80\x9d because the funds the debtor uses to pay the tax are property of the estate. But that too strains the text beyond what it can bear. To concede that someone other than the estate is liable for filing the return and paying the tax, and yet maintain that the estate is the one that has \xe2\x80\x9cincurred\xe2\x80\x9d the tax, defies the ordinary meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cincur\xe2\x80\x9d as bringing a liability upon oneself.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe dissent, echoing both of these points, urges that we \xe2\x80\x9csimply .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. consider the debtor and estate as merged.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 11. \xe2\x80\x9cThe English language,\xe2\x80\x9d the dissent reasons, \xe2\x80\x9cpermits this reading\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cdo[es] not require\xe2\x80\x9d our reading. Post, at 8\xe2\x80\x939. But any reading of \xe2\x80\x9ctax .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. incurred by the estate\xe2\x80\x9d that is contingent on merging the debtor and estate\xe2\x80\x94despite Congress\xe2\x80\x99 longstanding efforts to distinguish between when tax liabilities are borne by the debtor or borne by the estate\xe2\x80\x94is not a natural construction of the statute as written.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMoreover, these alternative readings create a conflict between \xc2\xa7503(b) and \xc2\xa7346(b). Petitioners consider postpetition state or local income taxes, like federal income taxes, to be \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the estate\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa7503(b). See Tr. of Oral Arg. 4\xe2\x80\x935. But \xc2\xa7346(b) requires that such taxes be borne by the Chapter 12 debtor, not the estate. It is implausible to maintain that taxes are \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the estate\xe2\x80\x9d when \xc2\xa7346(b) specifically prohibits such taxes from being \xe2\x80\x9ctaxed to or claimed by the estate.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo buttress their counterintuitive readings of the text, petitioners and the dissent suggest that there is a long history of treating postpetition taxes as administrative expenses entitled to priority. Both point to two legislative Reports accompanying the 1978 enactment of \xc2\xa7503. But neither snippet from which they quote is inconsistent with today\xe2\x80\x99s holding,\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t8\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t and we have cautioned against \xe2\x80\x9callowing ambiguous legislative history to muddy clear statutory language.\xe2\x80\x9d Milner v. Department of Navy, 562 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 9).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners also point to cases suggesting that postpetition taxes were treated as administrative expenses. E.g., United States v. Noland, \n\n517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 535, \n543 (1996)\n (cor- porate Chapter 11 debtor); Nicholas v. United States, \n\n384 U.\xc2\xa0S. 678\n\xe2\x80\x93688 (1966) (corporate Chapter XI case under predecessor Bankruptcy Act). But those cases involve corporate debtors and are therefore inapposite. Among estates that are not separately taxable, those involving corporate debtors have long been singled out by Congress for special responsibilities.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t9\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t See H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep., at 277 (even \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f the estate is not a separate taxable entity,\xe2\x80\x9d administrative responsibility can \xe2\x80\x9cvar[y] according to the nature of the debtor\xe2\x80\x9d). Although estates of corporate debtors are not separate taxable entities under 26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71398 and 1399, the IRC requires a trustee that \xe2\x80\x9chas possession of or holds title to all or substantially all the property or business of a corporation\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9cmake the return of income for such corporation.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa76012(b)(3). In effect, Congress provided that the trustee in a corporate-debtor case may shoulder responsibility that parallels that borne by the trustee of a separate taxable entity. In any event, petitioners do not deny that neither the separate taxable entity provisions nor the special provisions for corporate debtors apply to them.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, petitioners and the dissent contend that the purpose of \n\n11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71222(a)(2)(A) was to provide debtors with robust relief from tax debts, relying on statements by a single Senator on unenacted bills introduced in years preceding the enactment. See Brief for Petitioners 23\xe2\x80\x9336. They argue that deeming \xc2\xa71222(a)(2)(A) inapplicable to their postpetition income taxes would undermine that purpose and confine the exception to prepetition taxes. But we need not resolve here what other claims, if any, are covered by \xc2\xa71222(a)(2)(A).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t10\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Whatever the 2005 Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent with respect to \xc2\xa71222(a)(2)(A), that provision merely carved out an exception to the pre-existing priority classification scheme. The exception could only apply to claims \xe2\x80\x9centitled to priority under section 507\xe2\x80\x9d in the first place. That pre-existing scheme was in turn premised on antecedent, decades-old understandings about the scope of \xc2\xa7503(b) and the division of tax liabilities between estates and debtors. See Dewsnup v. Timm, \n\n502 U.\xc2\xa0S. 410, \n419 (1992)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cWhen Congress amends the bankruptcy laws, it does not write \xe2\x80\x98on a clean slate\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). If Congress wished to alter these background norms, it needed to enact a provision to enable postpetition income taxes to be collected in the Chapter 12 plan in the first place.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe dissent concludes otherwise by an inverted analysis. Rather than demonstrate that such claims were treated as \xc2\xa7507 priority claims in the first place, the dissent begins with the single Senator\xe2\x80\x99s stated purpose for the exception to that priority scheme. Post, at 7. It then reasons backwards from there, and in the process upsets background norms in both Chapters 12 and 13.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCertainly, there may be compelling policy reasons for treating postpetition income tax liabilities as discharge- able. But if Congress intended that result, it did not so provide in the statute. Given the statute\xe2\x80\x99s plain language, context, and structure, it is not for us to rewrite the statute, particularly in this complex terrain of interconnected provisions and exceptions enacted over nearly three decades. Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 position threatens ripple effects beyond this individual case for debtors in Chapter 13 and the broader bankruptcy scheme that we need not invite. As the Court of Appeals noted, \xe2\x80\x9cCongress is entirely free to change the law by amending the text.\xe2\x80\x9d 617 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1167.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe hold that the federal income tax liability resulting from petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 postpetition farm sale is not \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the estate\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa7503(b) and thus is neither collectible nor dischargeable in the Chapter 12 plan. We therefore affirm the judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tChapter 12 of the Bankruptcy Code helps family farmers in economic difficulty reorganize their debts without losing their farms. Consistent with the chapter\xe2\x80\x99s pur- poses, Congress amended \xc2\xa71222(a) of the Code to enable the debtor to treat certain capital gains tax claims as ordinary unsecured claims. \n\n11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71222(a)(2)(A). The Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding prevents the Amendment from carrying out this basic objective. I would read the statute differently, interpreting it in a way that, in my view, both is consistent with its language and allows the Amendment better to achieve its purposes.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tChapter 12 of the Bankruptcy Code helps indebted family farmers (and fishermen) keep their farms by making commitments to pay those debts (in part) out of future income. An eligible farmer whose debts exceed his assets may enter Chapter 12 bankruptcy, at which point he must develop a detailed Plan setting forth how he will pay his debts. That Plan must satisfy certain statutory criteria. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71221, 1222, 1225.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA brief overview of these requirements helps to illuminate what is at stake in this case. Roughly speaking, the chapter requires that a holder of a secured claim receive the full amount of that claim up to the value of the collateral securing the loan. The claim may be paid over an extended period. If the claim exceeds the value of the collateral, the creditor is given an unsecured claim in the remainder. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7506(a), 1225(a)(5).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe holder of a \xc2\xa7507 priority claim (a category that includes, among other things, domestic support obligations, debts for taxes incurred before filing the bankruptcy petition, and administrative expenses) must receive the full amount of the priority claim in deferred cash payments paid over the life of the Plan. \xc2\xa71222(a)(2).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe holder of an ordinary unsecured claim\xe2\x80\x94i.e., an unsecured claim of a kind not listed in \xc2\xa7507\xe2\x80\x94may receive at least a partial payment from the amount left over after the payment of the secured and \xc2\xa7507 priority claims. This amount may well be more than zero, for the Plan must provide that the farmer will devote all \xe2\x80\x9cdisposable income\xe2\x80\x9d (as defined by \xc2\xa71225(b)(2)) or property of equivalent value to the repayment of his debts over the next three years (sometimes extended to five years). \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71222(c), 1225(b)(1). And that amount must prove sufficient to provide the un- secured creditor with no less than that creditor would re- ceive in a Chapter 7 liquidation. \xc2\xa71225(a)(4).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOnce the farmer completes his Plan payments, he will receive a discharge even if his payments did not fully satisfy all unsecured claims. The Code does not, however, permit all debts to be discharged. There are categories of nondischargeable debts (including, for example, secured claims), which creditors can pursue after bankruptcy. \xc2\xa71228(a).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor present purposes, it is important to understand that if the debtor owes too much money to his \xc2\xa7507 priority creditors, he may not have sufficient assets or future income to pay all his secured creditors and his \xc2\xa7507 prior- ity creditors while leaving enough funds over to guarantee unsecured creditors the minimum amounts that Chapter 12 requires. If so, the farmer may not be able to proceed under Chapter 12. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(a)(1), (6) (bankruptcy court will not confirm Plan unless it satisfies statutory criteria and debtor will be able to make good on his commitments under the Plan).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is also important to understand that the same kind of insufficient-assets-and-income problem might occur where the debtor owes the Government a large post-petition tax debt. In general, postpetition claims are not part of the bankruptcy proceedings. See 7 Norton Bankruptcy Law and Practice \xc2\xa7135:14 (3d ed. 2011) (hereinafter Norton). Unless the Government\xe2\x80\x99s debt falls within an exception to this general rule, bankruptcy law would leave the Government to collect its postpetition claim outside of bankruptcy as best it could. Again, the result will be to leave the farmer with fewer assets and income to devote to his Chapter 12 Plan\xe2\x80\x94perhaps to the point where he cannot proceed under Chapter 12 at all.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe Amendment consists of subparagraph (A).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAt first blush, the Amendment seems to relegate the capital gains tax collector to the status of an ordinary unsecured creditor. See ibid. (exception applies to claims \xe2\x80\x9cowed to a governmental unit that arises as a result of the sale .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of any farm asset\xe2\x80\x9d). If, as petitioners claim, that is so, then it is unlikely that such a debt could stop a farmer from proceeding under Chapter 12, since its treatment as an ordinary unsecured claim means that the farmer will not necessarily have to pay the debt in full.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut if the Government and the majority are right, then the capital gains tax falls outside the category of \xc2\xa7507 priority claims\xe2\x80\x94and therefore falls outside the scope of the Amendment; in fact, it falls outside the bankruptcy proceeding altogether. And the Government then might well be able to collect the debt in full outside the bankruptcy proceeding\xe2\x80\x94even if doing so would reduce the farmer\xe2\x80\x99s assets and future income to the point where the farmer would not be able to proceed under Chapter 12. The question before us is whether we must interpret the Amendment in a way that could bring about this result.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tC\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress did not intend this result. In a significant number of instances a Chapter 12 farmer, in order to have enough money to pay his creditors, might have to sell farmland or other farm assets at a price that would give rise to considerable capital gains taxes (particularly if the family has held the land or assets for many years). If the resulting tax debt were treated as a \xc2\xa7507 priority claim, then it might well absorb much of the money raised to the point where (depending upon the size of his other debts) the farmer might be unable to proceed under Chapter 12. The Amendment accordingly seeks to place the tax authorities farther back in the creditor queue, requiring them, like ordinary unsecured creditors, to seek payment from the funds that remain after the \xc2\xa7507 priority creditors (and secured claim holders) have been paid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s chief legislative sponsor, Senator Charles Grassley, explained this well when he told the Senate:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cUnder current law, farmers often face a crushing tax liability if they need to sell livestock or land in order to reorganize their business affairs.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [H]igh taxes have caused farmers to lose their farms. Under the bankruptcy code, the I.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0S. must be paid in full for any tax liabilities generated during a bankruptcy reorganization. If the farmer can\xe2\x80\x99t pay the I.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0S. in full, then he can\xe2\x80\x99t keep his farm. This isn\xe2\x80\x99t sound policy. Why should the I.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0S. be allowed to veto a farmer\xe2\x80\x99s reorganization plan? [The Amendment] takes this power away from the I.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0S. by reducing the priority of taxes during proceedings. This will free up capital for investment in the farm, and help farmers stay in the business of farming.\xe2\x80\x9d 145 Cong. Rec. 1113 (1999).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tSee also 14A J. Mertens, Law of Federal Income Taxation \xc2\xa754:61, p.\xc2\xa011 (Oct. 2011 Supp.) (\xe2\x80\x9cThis provision attempts to mitigate the tax expense often incurred by farmers who have significant taxable capital gains or depreciation re- capture when their low basis farm assets are foreclosed, sold, or otherwise disposed of by their creditors\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority, following the Government\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion, interprets the relevant language in a way that denies the Amendment its intended effect. It holds that the only income tax claims to which \xc2\xa7507 accords priority are claims for taxes due for years prior to the taxable year in which the farmer filed for bankruptcy. (We shall call these \xe2\x80\x9cprepetition tax claims.\xe2\x80\x9d) In the majority\xe2\x80\x99s view, \xc2\xa7507 does not cover income tax liabilities that arise during the year of filing or during the Chapter 12 proceedings. (We shall call these \xe2\x80\x9cpostpetition tax claims.\xe2\x80\x9d) Ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x935; see Brief for United States 8 (the Amendment \xe2\x80\x9cprovides farmers relief from [only] those tax claims that are otherwise entitled to priority under 11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. 507(a)(8), namely pre-petition claims arising from the sale of farm as- sets\xe2\x80\x9d); Bankruptcy Abuse Prevention and Consumer Protection Act of 2005, \xc2\xa7705(1)(A), \n\n119Stat.\n126 (amending \xc2\xa7507(a)(8) to clarify that it only covers income tax claims for taxable years that end on or before the date of the filing of the bankruptcy petition).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority then observes that the Amendment creates an exception only in respect to \xc2\xa7507 priority claims. \xc2\xa71222(a) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe plan shall .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. provide for the full payment .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of all claims entitled to priority under section 507, unless .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.)). Ante, at 2. Thus, if (without the Amendment) \xc2\xa7507 would not cover postpetition capital gains taxes in the first place, the Amendment (creating only a \xc2\xa7507 exception) cannot affect postpeti- tion tax claims. An exception from nothing amounts to nothing.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tConsequently, the majority concludes that postpetition tax claims fall outside the bankruptcy proceeding entirely; the tax authorities can collect them as if they were ordinary tax debts; and the Government\xe2\x80\x99s efforts to collect them can lead to the very results (blocking the use of Chapter 12) that the Amendment sought to avoid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTherein lies the problem. These results are the very opposite of what Congress intended. Congress did not want to relegate to ordinary-unsecured-claim status only prepetition tax claims, i.e., tax claims that accrued well before the Chapter 12 proceedings began. Rather, Congress was concerned about the effect on the farmer of collecting capital gains tax debts that arose during (and were connected with) the Chapter 12 proceedings themselves. See 145 Cong. Rec. 1113 (the Amendment will have the effect of \xe2\x80\x9creducing the priority of taxes during proceedings\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added) (statement of Sen. Grassley during a failed attempt to enact the Amendment)); Hearing on the Bankruptcy Reform Act of 2001 before the Senate Committee on the Judiciary, 107th Cong., 1st Sess., 121 (statement of Sen. Grassley) (\xe2\x80\x9c[The Amendment] also reduces the priority of capital gains tax liabilities for farm assets sold as a part of a reorganization plan\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). The majority does not deny the importance of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 objective. Rather, it feels compelled to hold that Congress put the Amendment in the wrong place.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUnlike the majority, I believe the relevant Bankruptcy Code language can be and is better interpreted in a way that would give full effect to the Amendment. In particular, the relevant language is better interpreted so that in the absence of the Amendment \xc2\xa7507 would cover these postpetition tax claims. Hence the Amendment creates an exception from what otherwise would amount to a \xc2\xa7507 priority claim. And it can take effect as written.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is common ground that subsection (a)(2) of \xc2\xa7507 cov- ers, and gives \xc2\xa7507 priority to, \xe2\x80\x9cadministrative expenses allowed under section 503(b).\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7507(a)(2) (2006 ed., Supp. IV). It is also common ground that the relevant definitional section, namely \xc2\xa7503(b), defines allowed \xe2\x80\x9cadministrative expenses\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cincluding .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any tax .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. incurred by the estate.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7503(b)(1)(B)(i) (2006 ed.). But after this point, we part company.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority believes that the words any tax \xe2\x80\x9cincur- red by the estate\xe2\x80\x9d cannot include postpetition taxes. It emphasizes that tax law does not treat a Chapter 12 bankruptcy estate as a \xe2\x80\x9cseparate taxable entity,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., as separate from the farmer-debtor for federal income tax purposes. 26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71398, 1399. This means that there is just one entity\xe2\x80\x94the debtor\xe2\x80\x94for these purposes. And \xc2\xa7346 of the Bankruptcy Code makes clear that any state and local income tax liabilities incurred by a Chapter 12 estate must also be taxed to the debtor. The majority says that these provisions mean that only the debtor, and not the estate, can \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98incu[r]\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d taxes within the meaning of \n\n11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7503(b)(1)(B)(i). Ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x935.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn my view, however, these tax law circumstances do not require the majority\xe2\x80\x99s narrow reading of this Bankruptcy Code provision. That is to say, the phrase tax \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the [bankruptcy] estate\xe2\x80\x9d can include a tax incurred by the farmer while managing his estate in the midst of his bankruptcy proceedings, i.e., between the time the farmer files for Chapter 12 bankruptcy and the time the bankruptcy court confirms the farmer\xe2\x80\x99s Chapter 12 Plan.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe bankruptcy estate is in existence during this time. Cf. \xc2\xa71227(b) (property of the estate vests in the debtor at confirmation unless the Plan provides otherwise). The bankruptcy court has jurisdiction over the farmer\xe2\x80\x99s assets during this time. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7541, 1207; 4 Norton \xc2\xa761:1, at 61\xe2\x80\x932 (\xc2\xa7541\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cbroad definition of estate property .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. centralizes all of the estate\xe2\x80\x99s assets under the jurisdiction of the bankruptcy court\xe2\x80\x9d). And, as a matter of both the English language and bankruptcy principles, one can consider a tax liability that the farmer incurs during this period (such as a capital gains tax arising from a sale of a portion of his farm assets to raise funds for creditors) as a liability that, in a bankruptcy sense, the estate incurs.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe English language permits this reading of the phrase tax \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the estate.\xe2\x80\x9d When the farmer, in the midst of Chapter 12 proceedings, sells a portion of his farm to raise money to help pay his creditors, one can say, as a matter of English, that the bankruptcy estate has \xe2\x80\x9cincurred\xe2\x80\x9d the associated tax, even if it is ultimately taxed to the farmer, just as one can say that an employee who makes purchases using a company credit card \xe2\x80\x9cincurs costs\xe2\x80\x9d for which his employer is liable.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs a matter of general bankruptcy principles (as Congress understood them), the history of the 1978 Bank- ruptcy Code revision is replete with statements to the effect that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]axes arising from the operation of the estate after bankruptcy are entitled to priority as administrative expenses.\xe2\x80\x9d H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 95\xe2\x80\x93595, p.\xc2\xa0193 (1977) (emphasis added). See S. Rep. No. 95\xe2\x80\x931106, p.\xc2\xa013 (1978) (administrative expenses include \xe2\x80\x9c[t]axes incurred during the administration of the estate\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); S. Rep. No. 95\xe2\x80\x93989, p.\xc2\xa066 (1978) (\xe2\x80\x9cIn general, administrative expenses include taxes which the trustee incurs in administering the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s estate, including taxes on capital gains from sales of property by the trustee and taxes on income earned by the estate during the case\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); 124 Cong. Rec. 32415 (1978) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe amendment generally follows the Senate amendment in providing expressly that taxes incurred during the administration of the estate share the first priority given to administrative expenses generally\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); id., at 34014 (Senate version of the joint floor statement saying exactly the same).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAnd importantly, as the majority concedes, ante, at 14\xe2\x80\x9315, bankruptcy law treats taxes incurred by corporate debtors while they are in bankruptcy proceedings as \xe2\x80\x9ctax[es] incurred by the estate,\xe2\x80\x9d even though the Tax Code does not treat the bankruptcy estate of a corporate debtor as a \xe2\x80\x9cseparate taxable entity.\xe2\x80\x9d See, e.g., United States v. Noland, \n\n517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 535, \n543 (1996)\n (treating Chapter 11 corporate debtor\xe2\x80\x99s postpetition taxes as administrative expenses); In\xc2\xa0re Pacific-Atlantic Trading Co., 64 F.\xc2\xa03d 1292, 1298 (CA9 1995) (same); In\xc2\xa0re L.\xc2\xa0J. O\xe2\x80\x99Neil Shoe Co., 64 F.\xc2\xa03d 1146, 1151\xe2\x80\x931152 (CA8 1995) (same); In\xc2\xa0re Hillsborough Holdings Corp., 156 B.\xc2\xa0R. 318, 320 (Bkrtcy. Ct. MD Fla. 1993) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]dministrative expenses should include taxes which the trustee, and, in Chapter 11 cases, the Debtor-in-Possession, incurs in administering the estate, including taxes based on capital gains from sales of property and taxes on income earned by the estate during the case post-petition\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEven though, as the majority says, corporate bankruptcies have some special features (in particular, a trustee in a corporate bankruptcy is required to file the estate\xe2\x80\x99s income tax return), it is unclear why these features should have any bearing on the definition of administrative expenses. See ante, at 15 (discussing \n\n26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa76012(b)(3)). Indeed, in many corporate Chapter 11 bankruptcies, there is no trustee, in which case the debtor-in-possession, just like an individual Chapter 12 debtor, must file the tax return. See 11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71104, 1107 (2006 ed. and Supp. IV); 5 Norton \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa791:3, 93:1 (typically, no trustee is appointed in a Chapter 11 bankruptcy, and the debtor-in-possession assumes most of the duties and powers of a trustee, continuing in possession and managing the business until the court determines, upon request of a party in interest, that grounds exist for the appointment of a trustee); Holywell Corp. v. Smith, \n\n503 U.\xc2\xa0S. 47, \n54 (1992)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cAs the assignee of \xe2\x80\x98all\xe2\x80\x99 or \xe2\x80\x98substantially all\xe2\x80\x99 of the property of the corporate debtors, the trustee must file the re- turns that the corporate debtors would have filed had the plan not assigned their property to the trustee\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tConsequently, I can find no strong bankruptcy law reason for treating taxes incurred by a corporate debtor differently from those incurred by an individual Chapter 12 debtor. To the contrary, since corporations can file for bankruptcy under Chapter 12, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s argument implies that the treatment of postpetition taxes in Chapter 12 proceedings turns on whether the debtor happens to be a corporation. See \xc2\xa7101(18)(B) (2006 ed.) (defining \xe2\x80\x9cfamily farmer\xe2\x80\x9d to include certain corporations); \xc2\xa7109(f) (\xe2\x80\x9cOnly a family farmer or family fisherman with regular annual income may be a debtor under chapter 12\xe2\x80\x9d); Brief for United States 26, n.\xc2\xa09 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he estate of a corporate (as opposed to individual) Chapter 12 debtor .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. could be viewed as incurring post-petition income taxes .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. collectible as administrative expenses .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. rather than outside the bankruptcy case as required for an individual Chapter 12 debtor\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority does not point to any adverse consequences that might arise were bankruptcy law to treat taxes incurred in administering the bankruptcy estate (i.e., taxes incurred after filing and before Plan confirmation) as administrative expenses. The effect of doing so would simply be to consider the debtor and estate as merged for purposes of determining which taxes fall within the Bankruptcy\xe2\x80\x99s Code\xe2\x80\x99s definition of \xe2\x80\x9cadministrative expenses,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., determining for that purpose that the estate may \xe2\x80\x9cincur\xe2\x80\x9d tax liabilities on behalf of the whole (with the ul- timate liability assigned to the debtor), much like a married couple filing jointly, \n\n26 U.\xc2\xa0S. C. \xc2\xa76013(a), or an affiliated group of corporations filing a consolidated tax return, \xc2\xa71501. Cf. In re Lumara Foods of America, Inc., 50 B.\xc2\xa0R. 809, 815 (Bkrtcy. Ct. ND Ohio 1985) (describing the history of \xc2\xa7503(b)(1)(B)(i) and concluding that \xe2\x80\x9cthe elevation [of a tax] to an administrative priority is dependent upon when the tax accrued\xe2\x80\x9d). In fact, the very tax provisions that separate the estate from the individual debtor in Chapter 7 and Chapter 11 proceedings, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71398 and 1399, say that the Chapter 12 estate is not separate from the debtor for tax purposes\xe2\x80\x94a concept consistent, not at odds, with merging the two for this bankruptcy purpose.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNor is the majority\xe2\x80\x99s reading free of conceptual problems. If we read the phrase tax \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the estate\xe2\x80\x9d as excluding tax liabilities incurred while the farmer is in Chapter 12 bankruptcy, we must read it as excluding not only capital gains taxes but also other kinds of taxes, such as an employer\xe2\x80\x99s share of Social Security taxes, Medicare taxes, or other employee taxes. But no one claims that all of these taxes fall outside the scope of the term \xe2\x80\x9cadministrative expenses.\xe2\x80\x9d See In\xc2\xa0re Ryan, 228 B.\xc2\xa0R. 746 (Bkrtcy. Ct. Ore. 1999) (treating postpetition employment taxes as administrative expenses in a Chapter 12 proceeding); IRS Chief Counsel Advice No. 200518002 (May 6, 2005), 2005 WL 1060956 (assuming that some postpetition fed- eral taxes can be treated as administrative expenses in a Chapter 12 bankruptcy).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn fact, the Government, realizing it cannot go this far, concedes that many of these other (e.g., employer) taxes are \xe2\x80\x9cadministrative expenses,\xe2\x80\x9d but only, it suggests, because they fall within a different part of the \xe2\x80\x9cadministrative expenses\xe2\x80\x9d definition, namely \n\n11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7503(b)(1)(A), which says that \xe2\x80\x9cadministrative expenses\xe2\x80\x9d include \xe2\x80\x9cthe actual, necessary costs and expenses of preserving the estate including .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. wages, salaries, and commissions for services rendered after the commencement of the case.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.) See Brief for United States 27\xe2\x80\x9328, n.\xc2\xa011. Employment taxes, however, do not fit easily within the rubric \xe2\x80\x9cwages, salaries, and commissions.\xe2\x80\x9d They may well be \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary costs and expenses of preserving the estate.\xe2\x80\x9d But then so are the capital gains taxes at issue here.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, the majority makes what I believe to be its strongest argument. Ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9312. Chapter 13, it points out, allows individuals (typically those who are not farmers or fishermen) to reorganize their debts in much the same way as does Chapter 12. And there is authority holding that taxes on income earned between the time the Chapter 13 debtor files for bankruptcy and the time the bankruptcy Plan is confirmed are not \xe2\x80\x9ctax[es] incurred by the estate.\xe2\x80\x9d See In\xc2\xa0re Whall, 391 B.\xc2\xa0R. 1, 5\xe2\x80\x936 (Bkrtcy. Ct. Mass. 2008); In\xc2\xa0re Brown, No. 05\xe2\x80\x9341071, 2006 WL 3370867, *3 (Bkrtcy. Ct. Mass. 2006); In\xc2\xa0re Jagours, 236 B.\xc2\xa0R. 616, 620, n.\xc2\xa04 (Bkrtcy. Ct. ED Tex. 1999); In\xc2\xa0re Gyulafia, 65 B.\xc2\xa0R. 913, 916 (Bkrtcy. Ct. Kan. 1986). Why, asks the majority, should the law treat Chapter 12 taxes differently?\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor one thing, the issue is less important in a Chapter 13 case, for the relevant time period\xe2\x80\x94between filing and Plan confirmation\xe2\x80\x94is typically very short. Compare H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 95\xe2\x80\x93595, at 276 (\xe2\x80\x9cmost chapter 13 estates will only remain open for 1 or 2 months until confirmation of the plan\xe2\x80\x9d), with Brief for Neil E. Harl et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 32\xe2\x80\x9333 (survey of Chapter 12 bankruptcies found the average time from filing to confirmation in a district ranged from nearly five months to over three years). See also 7 Norton \xc2\xa7122:14, at 122\xe2\x80\x9327 (\xe2\x80\x9cIn Chapter 13, the plan must be filed within 15 days after the filing of the petition, unless the time is extended for cause. A Chapter 12 Plan must be filed no later than 90 days after the order for relief, unless the court finds that an extension is substantially justified\xe2\x80\x9d (footnote omitted)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor another, the issue arises differently in a Chapter 13 case. That chapter, unlike Chapter 12, contains a special provision that permits the Government to seek \xc2\xa7507 priority treatment of all taxes incurred while the bankruptcy case is pending. \xc2\xa71305 (Government can file proof of claim to have postpetition taxes treated as if they had arisen before the petition was filed).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, if uniformity of interpretation between these two chapters is critical, I do not see the serious harm in treating the relevant taxes as \xe2\x80\x9cadministrative expenses\xe2\x80\x9d in both Chapter 12 and Chapter 13 cases rather than in neither. The majority apparently believes that this would render \xc2\xa71305 (the provision permitting the Government to seek \xc2\xa7507 priority treatment) superfluous. Ante, at 10\xe2\x80\x9312. But that is not so. This interpretation would simply limit the scope of operation of \xc2\xa71305 to the period of time after the Chapter 13 Plan is confirmed but while the Chapter 13 case is still pending. And that is likely to be a significant period of time relative to the preconfirmation period. See H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 95\xe2\x80\x93595, at 276 (\xe2\x80\x9c[M]ost chapter 13 estates will only remain open for 1 or 2 months until confirmation of the plan\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71325(b)(1), (4) (debtor must commit all his projected disposable income over a 3-year period (sometimes extended to five) to the Plan, unless all unsecured claims can be paid off over a shorter period). The greatest Chapter 13 harm this interpretation could cause is to re- quire the Government to pursue those tax liabilities as \xc2\xa7507 priority administrative expense claims (rather than allow it to choose between \xc2\xa7507 priority treatment and pursuing those claims outside bankruptcy) during the relatively brief period of time between the filing of a petition and the Plan\xe2\x80\x99s confirmation.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn sum, I would treat a postpetition/preconfirmation tax liability as a tax \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the estate,\xe2\x80\x9d hence as an \xe2\x80\x9cadministrative expense,\xe2\x80\x9d hence as a \xe2\x80\x9cclai[m] entitled to priority under section 507, unless .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0,\xe2\x80\x9d hence as a claim falling within the scope of the Amendment. Doing so would allow the Amendment to take effect as Congress intended.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government argues that, even if tax liabilities arising during the bankruptcy proceedings are \xe2\x80\x9cadministrative expenses,\xe2\x80\x9d they still do not fall within the Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s scope. It says that neither the Amendment nor anything else in \xc2\xa71222(a) provides for the payment of administrative expenses. Rather, that section and its Amendment provide only for the payment of \xe2\x80\x9cclaims.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71222(a)(2) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe plan shall .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. provide for the full payment .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of all claims entitled to priority under section 507, unless .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). And administrative expenses, the Government says, like all debts that are incurred postpetition, are not \xe2\x80\x9cclaims.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government finds support for its view in the fact that that \xc2\xa71222 deals with the contents of a \xe2\x80\x9cplan,\xe2\x80\x9d while a later section, \xc2\xa71227(a), says that the provisions of a \xe2\x80\x9cconfirmed plan bind the debtor, each creditor, [and certain others of no relevance here].\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.) This is because the Code defines \xe2\x80\x9ccreditor\xe2\x80\x9d to include only hold- ers of pre-petition claims, thus excluding holders of post-petition claims, such as administrative expenses. \xc2\xa7101(10).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government points out that a different Code section, namely \xc2\xa71226(b)(1), provides for the payment of administrative expenses. That section says that \xe2\x80\x9c[b]efore or at the time of each payment to creditors under the plan, there shall be paid .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any unpaid claim of the kind specified in section 507(a)(2),\xe2\x80\x9d namely \xe2\x80\x9cadministrative ex- penses.\xe2\x80\x9d And Congress did not amend \xc2\xa71226(b)(1); it amended the earlier section, \xc2\xa71222(a).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn short, the Government says, the Plan only covers those \xc2\xa7507 priority \xe2\x80\x9cexpenses and claims\xe2\x80\x9d that are described as \xe2\x80\x9cclaims\xe2\x80\x9d and can be held by \xe2\x80\x9ccreditors.\xe2\x80\x9d Section 1226(b)(1), not \xc2\xa71222, deals with administrative expenses. The bottom line of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s chain of logic is, once again, that Congress put the Amendment in the wrong place.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI concede that there is some text and legislative history that supports the Government\xe2\x80\x99s view that the word \xe2\x80\x9cclaim\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa71222(a) does not include \xe2\x80\x9cadministrative expenses.\xe2\x80\x9d See, e.g., \xc2\xa7507(a) (referring to \xe2\x80\x9cexpenses and claims\xe2\x80\x9d as if they are separate categories); S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 95\xe2\x80\x931106, at 20 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe committee amendments contain several changes designed to clarify the distinction between a \xe2\x80\x98claim\xe2\x80\x99 (which generally relates to a debt incurred before the bankruptcy petition is filed) and an administrative expense (which is an expense incurred by the trustee after the filing of the petition)\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut the language does not demand the Government\xe2\x80\x99s reading. For the Code also uses the word \xe2\x80\x9cclaim\xe2\x80\x9d to cover both prepetition and postpetition claims (such as administrative expenses). E.g., \xc2\xa7101(5)(A) (defining a claim as a \xe2\x80\x9cright to payment\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa7726(b) (2006 ed., Supp. IV) (refer- ring to \xe2\x80\x9cclaims\xe2\x80\x9d that include administrative expenses). In- deed, the very section that the Government says permits separate collection of administrative expenses, namely \xc2\xa71226(b)(1), refers to \xe2\x80\x9cany unpaid claim\xe2\x80\x9d for administrative expenses. (Emphasis added.) And one can easily read that section as setting forth when, not whether, administrative expenses will be paid under the Plan (i.e., as specifying that the Plan must provide for the payment of administrative expenses before payments to other creditors are made). Thus, reading \xc2\xa71222(a)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s reference to \xe2\x80\x9cclaims\xe2\x80\x9d as including administrative expenses need not render \xc2\xa71226(b)(1) surplusage.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhat about \xc2\xa71227(a), which refers only to \xe2\x80\x9ccreditor[s]\xe2\x80\x9d? One must read it in conjunction with \xc2\xa71228(a), which provides that once the debtor has completed all payments under the Plan, \xe2\x80\x9cthe court shall grant the debtor a discharge of [1] all debts provided for by the plan[,] [2] allowed under section 503 of this title [which describes \xe2\x80\x98administrative expenses\xe2\x80\x99] or [3] disallowed under section 502 of this title .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.) (The first few words of \xc2\xa71227(a)\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9c[e]xcept as provided in section 1228(a)\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94explain why I say \xe2\x80\x9cmust\xe2\x80\x9d; the comma comes from 7 Norton \xc2\xa7137:2, at 137\xe2\x80\x933, n.\xc2\xa01, which says that its omission was a typographical error).
