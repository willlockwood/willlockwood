zivotofsky, by his parents and guardians, zivotofsky et ux. v. kerry, secretary of state

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the district of columbia circuit

\n

No. 13\xe2\x80\x93628.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 3, 2014\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 8, 2015

\n

Petitioner Zivotofsky was born to United\xc2\xa0States citizens living in Jerusalem. Pursuant to \xc2\xa7214(d) of the Foreign Relations Authorization Act, Fiscal Year 2003, his mother asked American Embassy officials to list his place of birth as \xe2\x80\x9cIsrael\xe2\x80\x9d on, inter alia, his passport. Section 214(d) states for \xe2\x80\x9cpurposes of the registration of birth, certification of nationality, or issuance of a passport of a United States citizen born in the city of Jerusalem, the Secretary shall, upon the request of the citizen or the citizen\xe2\x80\x99s legal guardian, record the place of birth as Israel.\xe2\x80\x9d The Embassy officials refused to list Zivotofsky\xe2\x80\x99s place of birth as \xe2\x80\x9cIsrael\xe2\x80\x9d on his passport, citing the Executive Branch\xe2\x80\x99s longstanding position that the United States does not recognize any country as having sovereignty over Jerusalem. Zivotofsky\xe2\x80\x99s parents brought suit on his behalf in federal court, seeking to enforce \xc2\xa7214(d). Ultimately, the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit held the statute unconstitutional, concluding that it contradicts the Executive Branch\xe2\x80\x99s exclusive power to recognize foreign sovereigns.

\n

Held:

\n

1.\xc2\xa0The President has the exclusive power to grant formal recognition to a foreign sovereign. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9326.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0Where, as here, the President\xe2\x80\x99s action is \xe2\x80\x9cincompatible with the expressed or implied will of Congress,\xe2\x80\x9d the President \xe2\x80\x9ccan rely [for his authority] only upon his own constitutional powers minus any constitutional powers of Congress over the matter,\xe2\x80\x9d Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer,\n\n343 U.\xc2\xa0S. 579\n (Jackson, J., concurring). His asserted power must be both \xe2\x80\x9cexclusive\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cconclusive\xe2\x80\x9d on the issue, id., at 637\xe2\x80\x93638, and he may rely solely on powers the Constitution grants to him alone, id., at 638. To determine whether the President\xe2\x80\x99s power of recognition is exclusive, this Court examines the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s text and structure and relevant precedent and history. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0The Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s text and structure grant the President the power to recognize foreign nations and governments. The Reception Clause directs that the President \xe2\x80\x9cshall receive Ambassadors and other public Ministers,\xe2\x80\x9d Art. II, \xc2\xa73. And at the time of the founding, receiving an ambassador was considered tantamount to recognizing the sending state\xe2\x80\x99s sovereignty. It is thus logical and proper to infer that the Reception Clause would be understood to acknowledge the President\xe2\x80\x99s power to recognize other nations. This inference is further supported by the President\xe2\x80\x99s additional Article II powers: to negotiate treaties and to nominate the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s ambassadors and dispatch other diplomatic agents. Though ratifying a treaty and confirming an ambassador require congressional approval, Congress lacks authority to initiate the actions without the President\xe2\x80\x99s involvement. The President, unlike Congress, also has the power to open diplomatic channels simply by engaging in direct diplomacy with foreign heads of state and their ministers. The Constitution thus assigns the President, not Congress, means to effect recognition on his own initiative.

\n

Functional considerations also suggest that the President\xe2\x80\x99s recognition power is exclusive. The Nation must \xe2\x80\x9cspeak .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. with one voice\xe2\x80\x9d regarding which governments are legitimate in the eyes of the United States and which are not, American Insurance Assn. v. Garamendi,\n\n539 U.\xc2\xa0S. 396\n, and only the Executive has the characteristic of unity at all times. Unlike Congress, the President is also capable of engaging in the delicate and often secret diplomatic contacts that may lead to a recognition decision, see, e.g., United States v. Pink,\n\n315 U.\xc2\xa0S. 203\n, and is better positioned to take the decisive, unequivocal action necessary to recognize other states at international law. The President has also exercised unilateral recognition power since the founding, a practice endorsed by this Court, see, e.g., Banco Nacional de Cuba v. Sabbatino,\n\n376 U.\xc2\xa0S. 398\n.

\n

Under basic separation-of-powers principles, Congress, which has the central role in making laws, see Art. I, \xc2\xa78, cl.\xc2\xa018, does have substantial authority regarding many policy determinations that precede and follow an act of recognition. The President\xe2\x80\x99s recognition determination is thus only one part of a political process. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0A fair reading of relevant precedent illustrates that this Court has long considered recognition to be the exclusive prerogative of the Executive. See, e.g., Williams v. Suffolk Ins. Co., 13 Pet. 415, 420; United States v. Belmont, \n\n301 U.\xc2\xa0S. 324\n; United States v. Pink, supra, at 229; Banco Nacional de Cuba v. Sabbatino, supra, at 410; National City Bank of N. Y. v. Republic of China,\n\n348 U.\xc2\xa0S. 356\n. United States v. Curtiss-Wright Export Corp.,\n\n299 U.\xc2\xa0S. 304\n, does not support a broader definition of the Executive\xe2\x80\x99s power over foreign relations that would permit the President alone to determine the whole content of the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s foreign policy. The Executive is not free from the ordinary controls and checks of Congress merely because foreign affairs are at issue. See, e.g., Medell\xc3\xadn v. Texas,\n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 491\n\xe2\x80\x93532. Nonetheless, it is for the President alone to make the specific decision of what foreign power he will recognize as legitimate, and his position must be clear. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9320.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0The weight of historical evidence also indicates Congress has accepted that the recognition power is exclusive to the Presidency. Cf. NLRB v. Noel Canning, 573 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___. Since the first Administration, the President has claimed unilateral authority to recognize foreign sovereigns. And Congress, for the most part, has acquiesced, generally respecting the Executive\xe2\x80\x99s policies and positions on formal recognition and even defending the President\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional prerogative. Pp.\xc2\xa020\xe2\x80\x9326.

\n

2.\xc2\xa0Because the power to recognize foreign states resides in the President alone, \xc2\xa7214(d) infringes on the Executive\xe2\x80\x99s consistent decision to withhold recognition with respect to Jerusalem. See Nixon v. Administrator of General Services,\n\n433 U.\xc2\xa0S. 425\n. The provision forces the President, through the Secretary of State, to identify, upon request, citizens born in Jerusalem as being born in Israel when, as a matter of United States policy, neither Israel nor any other country is acknowledged as having sovereignty over Jerusalem.

\n

If the recognition power is to mean anything, it must mean that the President not only makes the initial, formal recognition determination but also may maintain that determination in his and his agent\xe2\x80\x99s statements. Under international law, recognition may be effected by written or oral declaration. In addition, an act of recognition must leave no doubt as to the intention to grant it. Thus, if Congress could alter the President\xe2\x80\x99s statements on matters of recognition or force him to contradict them, Congress in effect would exercise the recognition power. An \xe2\x80\x9cexclusive\xe2\x80\x9d Presidential power \xe2\x80\x9cdisabl[es] the Congress from acting upon the subject.\xe2\x80\x9d Youngstown, supra, at 638 (Jackson, J., concurring). If Congress may not pass a law, speaking in its own voice, effecting formal recognition, then it may not force the President, through \xc2\xa7214(d), to contradict his prior recognition determination in an official document issued by the Secretary of State. See Urtetiqui v. D\xe2\x80\x99Arcy, 9 Pet. 692, 698.

\n

Section 214(d)\xe2\x80\x99s flaw is further underscored by the fact that the statute\xe2\x80\x99s purpose was to infringe on the President\xe2\x80\x99s exclusive recognition power. While Congress may have power to enact passport legislation of wide scope, it may not \xe2\x80\x9caggrandiz[e] its power at the expense of another branch\xe2\x80\x9d by requiring the President to contradict an earlier recognition determination in an official Executive Branch document. Freytag v. Commissioner,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 868\n. Pp.\xc2\xa026\xe2\x80\x9329.

\n

725 F.\xc2\xa03d 197, affirmed.

MENACHEM BINYAMIN ZIVOTOFSKY, by his parents and guardians, ARI Z. AND NAOMI SIEGMAN ZIVOTOFSKY, PETITIONER v. JOHN KERRY, SECRETARY OF STATE

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the district of columbia circuit

\n

[June 8, 2015]

\n

\n

Justice Kennedy delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

A delicate subject lies in the background of this case. That subject is Jerusalem. Questions touching upon the history of the ancient city and its present legal and international status are among the most difficult and complex in international affairs. In our constitutional system these matters are committed to the Legislature and the Executive, not the Judiciary. As a result, in this opinion the Court does no more, and must do no more, than note the existence of international debate and tensions respecting Jerusalem. Those matters are for Congress and the President to discuss and consider as they seek to shape the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s foreign policies.

\n

The Court addresses two questions to resolve the interbranch dispute now before it. First, it must determine whether the President has the exclusive power to grant formal recognition to a foreign sovereign. Second, if he has that power, the Court must determine whether Congress can command the President and his Secretary of State to issue a formal statement that contradicts the earlier recognition. The statement in question here is a congressional mandate that allows a United States citizen born in Jerusalem to direct the President and Secretary of State, when issuing his passport, to state that his place of birth is \xe2\x80\x9cIsrael.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

I

\n

A

\n

Jerusalem\xe2\x80\x99s political standing has long been, and remains, one of the most sensitive issues in American foreign policy, and indeed it is one of the most delicate issues in current international affairs. In 1948, President Truman formally recognized Israel in a signed statement of \xe2\x80\x9crecognition.\xe2\x80\x9d See Statement by the President Announcing Recognition of the State of Israel, Public Papers of the Presidents, May 14, 1948, p. 258 (1964). That statement did not recognize Israeli sovereignty over Jerusalem. Over the last 60 years, various actors have sought to assert full or partial sovereignty over the city, including Israel, Jordan, and the Palestinians. Yet, in contrast to a consistent policy of formal recognition of Israel, neither President Truman nor any later United States President has issued an official statement or declaration acknowledging any country\xe2\x80\x99s sovereignty over Jerusalem. Instead, the Executive Branch has maintained that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the status of Jerusalem . . . should be decided not unilaterally but in consultation with all concerned.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d United Nations Gen. Assembly Official Records, 5th Emergency Sess., 1554th Plenary Meetings, United Nations Doc. No. 1 A\xe2\x81\x84PV.1554, p.\xc2\xa010 (July 14, 1967); see, e.g., Remarks by President Obama in Address to the United Nations Gen. Assembly (Sept. 21, 2011), 2011 Daily Comp. of Pres. Doc. No. 00661, p. 4 (\xe2\x80\x9cUltimately, it is the Israelis and the Palestinians, not us, who must reach agreement on the issues that divide them,\xe2\x80\x9d including \xe2\x80\x9cJerusalem\xe2\x80\x9d). In a letter to Congress then-Secretary of State Warren Christopher expressed the Executive\xe2\x80\x99s concern that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here is no issue related to the Arab-Israeli negotiations that is more sensitive than Jerusalem.\xe2\x80\x9d See 141 Cong. Rec. 28967 (1995) (letter to Robert Dole, Majority Leader, (June 20, 1995)). He further noted the Executive\xe2\x80\x99s opinion that \xe2\x80\x9cany effort .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to bring it to the forefront\xe2\x80\x9d could be \xe2\x80\x9cvery damaging to the success of the peace process.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

The President\xe2\x80\x99s position on Jerusalem is reflected in State Department policy regarding passports and consular reports of birth abroad. Understanding that passports will be construed as reflections of American policy, the State Department\xe2\x80\x99s Foreign Affairs Manual instructs its employees, in general, to record the place of birth on a passport as the \xe2\x80\x9ccountry [having] present sovereignty over the actual area of birth.\xe2\x80\x9d Dept. of State, 7 Foreign Affairs Manual (FAM) \xc2\xa71383.4 (1987). If a citizen objects to the country listed as sovereign by the State Department, he or she may list the city or town of birth rather than the country. See id., \xc2\xa71383.6. The FAM, however, does not allow citizens to list a sovereign that conflicts with Executive Branch policy. See generally id., \xc2\xa71383. Because the United States does not recognize any country as having sovereignty over Jerusalem, the FAM instructs employees to record the place of birth for citizens born there as \xe2\x80\x9cJerusalem.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., \xc2\xa71383.5\xe2\x80\x936 (emphasis deleted).

\n

In 2002, Congress passed the Act at issue here, the Foreign Relations Authorization Act, Fiscal Year 2003,\n\n116Stat.\n1350. Section 214 of the Act is titled \xe2\x80\x9cUnited States Policy with Respect to Jerusalem as the Capital of Israel.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1365. The subsection that lies at the heart of this case, \xc2\xa7214(d), addresses passports. That subsection seeks to override the FAM by allowing citizens born in Jerusalem to list their place of birth as \xe2\x80\x9cIsrael.\xe2\x80\x9d Titled \xe2\x80\x9cRecord of Place of Birth as Israel for Passport Purposes,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7214(d) states \xe2\x80\x9c[f\xc2\xa0]or purposes of the registration of birth, certification of nationality, or issuance of a passport of a United States citizen born in the city of Jerusalem, the Secretary shall, upon the request of the citizen or the citizen\xe2\x80\x99s legal guardian, record the place of birth as Israel.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1366.

\n

When he signed the Act into law, President George W. Bush issued a statement declaring his position that \xc2\xa7214 would, \xe2\x80\x9cif construed as mandatory rather than advisory, impermissibly interfere with the President\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional authority to formulate the position of the United States, speak for the Nation in international affairs, and determine the terms on which recognition is given to foreign states.\xe2\x80\x9d Statement on Signing the Foreign Relations Authorization Act, Fiscal Year 2003, Public Papers of the Presidents, George W. Bush, Vol. 2, Sept. 30, 2002, p. 1698 (2005). The President concluded, \xe2\x80\x9cU.\xc2\xa0S. policy regarding Jerusalem has not changed.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

Some parties were not reassured by the President\xe2\x80\x99s statement. A cable from the United States Consulate in Jerusalem noted that the Palestine Liberation Organization Executive Committee, Fatah Central Committee, and the Palestinian Authority Cabinet had all issued statements claiming that the Act \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98undermines the role of the U.\xc2\xa0S. as a sponsor of the peace process.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d App. 231. In the Gaza Strip and elsewhere residents marched in protest. See The Associated Press and Reuters, Palestinians Stone Police Guarding Western Wall, The Seattle Times, Oct. 5, 2002, p. A7.

\n

In response the Secretary of State advised diplomats to express their understanding of \xe2\x80\x9cJerusalem\xe2\x80\x99s importance to both sides and to many others around the world.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 228. He noted his belief that America\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cpolicy towards Jerusalem\xe2\x80\x9d had not changed. Ibid.

\n

B

\n

In 2002, petitioner Menachem Binyamin Zivotofsky was born to United\xc2\xa0States citizens living in Jerusalem. App. 24\xe2\x80\x9325. In December 2002, Zivotofsky\xe2\x80\x99s mother visited the American Embassy in Tel Aviv to request both a passport and a consular report of birth abroad for her son. Id., at 25. She asked that his place of birth be listed as \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Jerusalem, Israel.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The Embassy clerks explained that, pursuant to State Department policy, the passport would list only \xe2\x80\x9cJerusalem.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Zivotofsky\xe2\x80\x99s parents objected and, as his guardians, brought suit on his behalf in the United States District Court for the District of Columbia, seeking to enforce \xc2\xa7214(d).

\n

Pursuant to \xc2\xa7214(d), Zivotofsky claims the right to have \xe2\x80\x9cIsrael\xe2\x80\x9d recorded as his place of birth in his passport. See Zivotofsky v. Clinton, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 4) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hile Zivotofsky had originally asked that \xe2\x80\x98Jerusalem, Israel\xe2\x80\x99 be recorded on his passport, \xe2\x80\x98[b]oth sides agree that the question now is whether \xc2\xa7214(d) entitles [him] to have just \xe2\x80\x98Israel\xe2\x80\x99 listed\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). The arguments in Zivotofsky\xe2\x80\x99s brief center on his passport claim, as opposed to the consular report of birth abroad. Indeed, in the court below, Zivotofsky waived any argument that his consular report of birth abroad should be treated differently than his passport. Zivotofsky v. Secretary of State, 725 F. 3d 197, 203, n. 3 (CADC 2013). He has also waived the issue here by failing to differentiate between the two documents. As a result, the Court addresses Zivotofsky\xe2\x80\x99s passport arguments and need not engage in a separate analysis of the validity of \xc2\xa7214(d) as applied to consular reports of birth abroad.

\n

After Zivotofsky brought suit, the District Court dismissed his case, reasoning that it presented a nonjusticiable political question and that Zivotofsky lacked standing. App. 28\xe2\x80\x9339. The Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit reversed on the standing issue, Zivotofsky v. Secretary of State, 444 F. 3d 614, 617\xe2\x80\x93619 (2006), but later affirmed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s political question determination. See Zivotofsky v. Secretary of State, 571 F. 3d 1227, 1228 (2009).

\n

This Court granted certiorari, vacated the judgment, and remanded the case. Whether \xc2\xa7214(d) is constitutional, the Court held, is not a question reserved for the politi-cal branches. In reference to Zivotofsky\xe2\x80\x99s claim the Court observed \xe2\x80\x9cthe Judiciary must decide if Zivotofsky\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the statute is correct, and whether the statute is constitutional\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94not whether Jerusalem is, in fact, part of Israel. Zivotofsky v. Clinton, supra, at___ (slip op., at 7).

\n

On remand the Court of Appeals held the statute unconstitutional. It determined that \xe2\x80\x9cthe President exclusively holds the power to determine whether to recognize a foreign sovereign,\xe2\x80\x9d 725 F. 3d, at 214, and that \xe2\x80\x9csection 214(d) directly contradicts a carefully considered exercise of the Executive branch\xe2\x80\x99s recognition power.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 217.

\n

This Court again granted certiorari. 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014).

\n

II

\n

In considering claims of Presidential power this Court refers to Justice Jackson\xe2\x80\x99s familiar tripartite framework from Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer,\n\n343 U. S. 579\n\xe2\x80\x93638 (1952) (concurring opinion). The framework divides exercises of Presidential power into three categories: First, when \xe2\x80\x9cthe President acts pursuant to an express or implied authorization of Congress, his authority is at its maximum, for it includes all that he possesses in his own right plus all that Congress can delegate.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 635. Second, \xe2\x80\x9cin absence of either a congressional grant or denial of authority\xe2\x80\x9d there is a \xe2\x80\x9czone of twilight in which he and Congress may have concurrent authority,\xe2\x80\x9d and where \xe2\x80\x9ccongressional inertia, indifference or quiescence may\xe2\x80\x9d invite the exercise of executive power. Id., at 637. Finally, when \xe2\x80\x9cthe President takes measures incompatible with the expressed or implied will of Congress . . . he can rely only upon his own constitutional powers minus any constitutional powers of Congress over the matter.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. To succeed in this third category, the President\xe2\x80\x99s asserted power must be both \xe2\x80\x9cexclusive\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cconclusive\xe2\x80\x9d on the issue. Id., at 637\xe2\x80\x93638.

\n

In this case the Secretary contends that \xc2\xa7214(d) in-fringes on the President\xe2\x80\x99s exclusive recognition power by \xe2\x80\x9crequiring the President to contradict his recognition posi-tion regarding Jerusalem in official communications with foreign sovereigns.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 48. In so doing the Secretary acknowledges the President\xe2\x80\x99s power is \xe2\x80\x9cat its lowest ebb.\xe2\x80\x9d Youngstown, 343 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 637. Because the President\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to implement \xc2\xa7214(d) falls into Justice Jackson\xe2\x80\x99s third category, his claim must be \xe2\x80\x9cscrutinized with caution,\xe2\x80\x9d and he may rely solely on powers the Constitution grants to him alone. Id., at 638.

\n

To determine whether the President possesses the exclusive power of recognition the Court examines the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s text and structure, as well as precedent and history bearing on the question.

\n

A

\n

Recognition is a \xe2\x80\x9cformal acknowledgement\xe2\x80\x9d that a particular \xe2\x80\x9centity possesses the qualifications for statehood\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cthat a particular regime is the effective government of a state.\xe2\x80\x9d Restatement (Third) of Foreign Relations Law of the United States \xc2\xa7203, Comment a, p. 84 (1986). It may also involve the determination of a state\xe2\x80\x99s territorial bounds. See 2 M. Whiteman, Digest of International Law \xc2\xa71, p. 1 (1963) (Whiteman) (\xe2\x80\x9c[S]tates may recognize or decline to recognize territory as belonging to, or under the sovereignty of, or having been acquired or lost by, other states\xe2\x80\x9d). Recognition is often effected by an express \xe2\x80\x9cwritten or oral declaration.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 J. Moore, Digest of International Law \xc2\xa727, p. 73 (1906) (Moore). It may also be implied\xe2\x80\x94for example, by concluding a bilateral treaty or by sending or receiving diplomatic agents. Ibid.; I. Brownlie, Prin-ciples of Public International Law 93 (7th ed. 2008) (Brownlie).

\n

Legal consequences follow formal recognition. Recognized sovereigns may sue in United\xc2\xa0States courts, see Guaranty Trust Co. v. United States,\n\n304 U. S. 126,\n137 (1938)\n, and may benefit from sovereign immunity when they are sued, see National City Bank of N. Y. v. Republic of China,\n\n348 U. S. 356\n\xe2\x80\x93359 (1955). The actions of a recognized sovereign committed within its own territory also receive deference in domestic courts under the act of state doctrine. See Oetjen v. Central Leather Co.,\n\n246 U.\xc2\xa0S. 297\n\xe2\x80\x93303 (1918). Recognition at international law, furthermore, is a precondition of regular diplomatic relations. 1 Moore \xc2\xa727, at 72. Recognition is thus \xe2\x80\x9cuseful, even necessary,\xe2\x80\x9d to the existence of a state. Ibid.

\n

Despite the importance of the recognition power in foreign relations, the Constitution does not use the term \xe2\x80\x9crecognition,\xe2\x80\x9d either in Article II or elsewhere. The Secretary asserts that the President exercises the recognition power based on the Reception Clause, which directs that the President \xe2\x80\x9cshall receive Ambassadors and other public Ministers.\xe2\x80\x9d Art. II, \xc2\xa73. As Zivotofsky notes, the Reception Clause received little attention at the Constitutional Convention. See Reinstein, Recognition: A Case Study on the Original Understanding of Executive Power, 45 U. Rich. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 801, 860\xe2\x80\x93862 (2011). In fact, during the ratification debates, Alexander Hamilton claimed that the power to receive ambassadors was \xe2\x80\x9cmore a matter of dignity than of authority,\xe2\x80\x9d a ministerial duty largely \xe2\x80\x9cwithout consequence.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 69, p. 420 (C. Rossiter ed. 1961).

\n

At the time of the founding, however, prominent international scholars suggested that receiving an ambassador was tantamount to recognizing the sovereignty of the sending state. See E. de Vattel, The Law of Nations \xc2\xa778, p. 461 (1758) (J. Chitty ed. 1853) (\xe2\x80\x9c[E]very state, truly possessed of sovereignty, has a right to send ambassadors\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cto contest their right in this instance\xe2\x80\x9d is equivalent to \xe2\x80\x9ccontesting their sovereign dignity\xe2\x80\x9d); see also 2 C. van Bynkershoek, On Questions of Public Law 156\xe2\x80\x93157 (1737) (T. Frank ed. 1930) (\xe2\x80\x9cAmong writers on public law it is usually agreed that only a sovereign power has a right to send ambassadors\xe2\x80\x9d); 2 H. Grotius, On the Law of War and Peace 440\xe2\x80\x93441 (1625) (F. Kelsey ed. 1925) (discussing the duty to admit ambassadors of sovereign powers). It is a logical and proper inference, then, that a Clause directing the President alone to receive ambassadors would be understood to acknowledge his power to recognize other nations.

\n

This in fact occurred early in the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s history when President Washington recognized the French Revolutionary Government by receiving its ambassador. See A. Hamilton, Pacificus No. 1, in The Letters of Pacificus and Helvidius 5, 13\xe2\x80\x9314 (1845) (reprint 1976) (President \xe2\x80\x9cacknowledged the republic of France, by the reception of its minister\xe2\x80\x9d). After this incident the import of the Reception Clause became clear\xe2\x80\x94causing Hamilton to change his earlier view. He wrote that the Reception Clause \xe2\x80\x9cincludes th[e power] of judging, in the case of a revolution of government in a foreign country, whether the new rulers are competent organs of the national will, and ought to be recognised, or not.\xe2\x80\x9d See id., at 12; see also 3 J. Story, Commentaries on the Constitution of the United States \xc2\xa71560, p. 416 (1833) (\xe2\x80\x9cIf the executive receives an ambassador, or other minister, as the representative of a new nation . . . it is an acknowledgment of the sovereign authority de facto of such new nation, or party\xe2\x80\x9d). As a result, the Reception Clause provides support, although not the sole authority, for the President\xe2\x80\x99s power to recognize other nations.

\n

The inference that the President exercises the recognition power is further supported by his additional Article II powers. It is for the President, \xe2\x80\x9cby and with the Advice and Consent of the Senate,\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9cmake Treaties, provided two thirds of the Senators present concur.\xe2\x80\x9d Art. II, \xc2\xa72, cl. 2. In addition, \xe2\x80\x9che shall nominate, and by and with the Advice and Consent of the Senate, shall appoint Ambassadors\xe2\x80\x9d as well as \xe2\x80\x9cother public Ministers and Consuls.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

As a matter of constitutional structure, these additional powers give the President control over recognition decisions. At international law, recognition may be effected by different means, but each means is dependent upon Presidential power. In addition to receiving an ambassador, recognition may occur on \xe2\x80\x9cthe conclusion of a bilateral treaty,\xe2\x80\x9d or the \xe2\x80\x9cformal initiation of diplomatic relations,\xe2\x80\x9d including the dispatch of an ambassador. Brownlie 93; see also 1 Moore \xc2\xa727, at 73. The President has the sole power to negotiate treaties, see United States v. Curtiss-Wright Export Corp.,\n\n299 U. S. 304,\n319 (1936)\n, and the Senate may not conclude or ratify a treaty without Presidential action. The President, too, nominates the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s ambassadors and dispatches other diplomatic agents. Congress may not send an ambassador without his involvement. Beyond that, the President himself has the power to open diplomatic channels simply by engaging in direct diplo-macy with foreign heads of state and their ministers. The Constitution thus assigns the President means to effect recognition on his own initiative. Congress, by contrast, has no constitutional power that would enable it to initiate diplomatic relations with a foreign nation. Because these specific Clauses confer the recognition power on the President, the Court need not consider whether or to what extent the Vesting Clause, which provides that the \xe2\x80\x9cexecutive Power\xe2\x80\x9d shall be vested in the President, provides further support for the President\xe2\x80\x99s action here. Art. II, \xc2\xa71, cl.\xc2\xa01.

\n

The text and structure of the Constitution grant the President the power to recognize foreign nations and governments. The question then becomes whether that power is exclusive. The various ways in which the President may unilaterally effect recognition\xe2\x80\x94and the lack of any similar power vested in Congress\xe2\x80\x94suggest that it is. So, too, do functional considerations. Put simply, the Nation must have a single policy regarding which governments are legitimate in the eyes of the United States and which are not. Foreign countries need to know, before entering into diplomatic relations or commerce with the United States, whether their ambassadors will be received; whether their officials will be immune from suit in federal court; and whether they may initiate lawsuits here to vindicate their rights. These assurances cannot be equivocal.

\n

Recognition is a topic on which the Nation must \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98speak .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. with one voice.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d American Ins. Assn. v. Garamendi,\n\n539 U. S. 396,\n424 (2003)\n (quoting Crosby v. National Foreign Trade Council,\n\n530 U. S. 363,\n381 (2000)\n). That voice must be the President\xe2\x80\x99s. Between the two political branches, only the Executive has the characteristic of unity at all times. And with unity comes the ability to exercise, to a greater degree, \xe2\x80\x9c[d]ecision, activity, secrecy, and dispatch.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 70, p. 424 (A. Hamilton). The President is capable, in ways Congress is not, of engaging in the delicate and often secret diplomatic contacts that may lead to a decision on recognition. See, e.g., United States v. Pink,\n\n315 U.\xc2\xa0S. 203,\n229 (1942)\n. He is also better positioned to take the decisive, unequivocal action necessary to recognize other states at international law. 1 Oppenheim\xe2\x80\x99s International Law \xc2\xa750, p. 169 (R. Jennings & A. Watts eds., 9th ed. 1992) (act of recognition must \xe2\x80\x9cleave no doubt as to the intention to grant it\xe2\x80\x9d). These qualities explain why the Framers listed the traditional avenues of recognition\xe2\x80\x94receiving ambassadors, making treaties, and sending ambassadors\xe2\x80\x94as among the President\xe2\x80\x99s Article II powers.

\n

As described in more detail below, the President since the founding has exercised this unilateral power to recognize new states\xe2\x80\x94and the Court has endorsed the practice. See Banco Nacional de Cuba v. Sabbatino,\n\n376 U. S. 398,\n410 (1964)\n; Pink, supra, at 229; Williams v. Suffolk Ins. Co., 13 Pet. 415, 420 (1839). Texts and treatises on international law treat the President\xe2\x80\x99s word as the final word on recognition. See, e.g., Restatement (Third) of Foreign Relations Law \xc2\xa7204, at 89 (\xe2\x80\x9cUnder the Constitution of the United States the President has exclusive authority to recognize or not to recognize a foreign state or government\xe2\x80\x9d); see also L. Henkin, Foreign Affairs and the U. S. Constitution 43 (2d ed. 1996) (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is no longer questioned that the President does not merely perform the ceremony of receiving foreign ambassadors but also determines whether the United States should recognize or refuse to recognize a foreign government\xe2\x80\x9d). In light of this author-ity all six judges who considered this case in the Court of Appeals agreed that the President holds the exclusive recognition power. See 725 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 214 (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e conclude that the President exclusively holds the power to determine whether to recognize a foreign sovereign\xe2\x80\x9d); Zivotofsky, 571 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1231 (\xe2\x80\x9cThat this power belongs solely to the President has been clear from the earliest days of the Republic\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 1240 (Edwards, J., concurring) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Executive has exclusive and unreviewable authority to recognize foreign sovereigns\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

It remains true, of course, that many decisions affecting foreign relations\xe2\x80\x94including decisions that may determine the course of our relations with recognized countries\xe2\x80\x94require congressional action. Congress may \xe2\x80\x9cregulate Commerce with foreign Nations,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cestablish an uniform Rule of Naturalization,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cdefine and punish Piracies and Felonies committed on the high Seas, and Offences against the Law of Nations,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cdeclare War,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cgrant Letters of Marque and Reprisal,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cmake Rules for the Government and Regulation of the land and naval Forces.\xe2\x80\x9d U. S. Const., Art. I, \xc2\xa78. In addition, the President cannot make a treaty or appoint an ambassador without the approval of the Senate. Art. II, \xc2\xa72, cl. 2. The President, furthermore, could not build an American Embassy abroad without congressional appropriation of the necessary funds. Art. I, \xc2\xa78, cl.\xc2\xa01. Under basic separation-of-powers principles, it is for the Congress to enact the laws, including \xe2\x80\x9call Laws which shall be necessary and proper for carrying into Execution\xe2\x80\x9d the powers of the Federal Government. \xc2\xa78, cl.\xc2\xa018.

\n

In foreign affairs, as in the domestic realm, the Constitution \xe2\x80\x9cenjoins upon its branches separateness but interdependence, autonomy but reciprocity.\xe2\x80\x9d Youngstown, 343 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 635 (Jackson, J., concurring). Although the President alone effects the formal act of recognition, Congress\xe2\x80\x99 powers, and its central role in making laws, give it substantial authority regarding many of the policy determinations that precede and follow the act of recognition itself. If Congress disagrees with the President\xe2\x80\x99s recognition policy, there may be consequences. Formal recognition may seem a hollow act if it is not accompanied by the dispatch of an ambassador, the easing of trade restrictions, and the conclusion of treaties. And those decisions require action by the Senate or the whole Congress.

\n

In practice, then, the President\xe2\x80\x99s recognition determination is just one part of a political process that may require Congress to make laws. The President\xe2\x80\x99s exclusive recognition power encompasses the authority to acknowledge, in a formal sense, the legitimacy of other states and governments, including their territorial bounds. Albeit limited, the exclusive recognition power is essential to the conduct of Presidential duties. The formal act of recognition is an executive power that Congress may not qualify. If the President is to be effective in negotiations over a formal recognition determination, it must be evident to his counterparts abroad that he speaks for the Nation on that precise question.

\n

A clear rule that the formal power to recognize a foreign government subsists in the President therefore serves a necessary purpose in diplomatic relations. All this, of course, underscores that Congress has an important role in other aspects of foreign policy, and the President may be bound by any number of laws Congress enacts. In this way ambition counters ambition, ensuring that the democratic will of the people is observed and respected in foreign affairs as in the domestic realm. See The Federalist No. 51, p. 322 (J. Madison).

\n

B

\n

No single precedent resolves the question whether the President has exclusive recognition authority and, if so, how far that power extends. In part that is because, until today, the political branches have resolved their disputes over questions of recognition. The relevant cases, though providing important instruction, address the division of recognition power between the Federal Government and the States, see, e.g., Pink,\n\n315 U.\xc2\xa0S. 203\n, or between the courts and the political branches, see, e.g., Banco Nacional de Cuba, 376 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 410\xe2\x80\x94not between the President and Congress. As the parties acknowledge, some isolated statements in those cases lend support to the position that Congress has a role in the recognition process. In the end, however, a fair reading of the cases shows that the President\xe2\x80\x99s role in the recognition process is both central and exclusive.

\n

During the administration of President Van Buren, in a case involving a dispute over the status of the Falkland Islands, the Court noted that \xe2\x80\x9cwhen the executive branch of the government\xe2\x80\x9d assumes \xe2\x80\x9ca fact in regard to the sovereignty of any island or country, it is conclusive on the judicial department.\xe2\x80\x9d Williams, 13 Pet., at 420. Once the President has made his determination, it \xe2\x80\x9cis enough to know, that in the exercise of his constitutional functions, he has decided the question. Having done this under the responsibilities which belong to him, it is obligatory on the people and government of the Union.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

Later, during the 1930\xe2\x80\x99s and 1940\xe2\x80\x99s, the Court addressed issues surrounding President Roosevelt\xe2\x80\x99s decision to recognize the Soviet Government of Russia. In United States v. Belmont, \n\n301 U. S. 324 (1937)\n, and Pink,\n\n315 U.\xc2\xa0S. 203\n, New York state courts declined to give full effect to the terms of executive agreements the President had concluded in negotiations over recognition of the Soviet regime. In particular the state courts, based on New York public policy, did not treat assets that had been seized by the Soviet Government as property of Russia and declined to turn those assets over to the United States. The Court stated that it \xe2\x80\x9cmay not be doubted\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9crecognition, establishment of diplomatic relations, . . . and agreements with respect thereto\xe2\x80\x9d are \xe2\x80\x9cwithin the competence of the President.\xe2\x80\x9d Belmont, 301 U. S., at 330. In these matters, \xe2\x80\x9cthe Executive ha[s] authority to speak as the sole organ of th[e] government.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The Court added that the President\xe2\x80\x99s authority \xe2\x80\x9cis not limited to a determination of the government to be recognized. It includes the power to determine the policy which is to govern the question of recognition.\xe2\x80\x9d Pink, supra, at 229; see also Guaranty Trust Co., 304 U. S., at 137\xe2\x80\x93138 (The \xe2\x80\x9cpolitical department[\xe2\x80\x99s] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. action in recognizing a foreign government and in receiving its diplomatic representatives is conclusive on all domestic courts\xe2\x80\x9d). Thus, New York state courts were required to respect the executive agreements.

\n

It is true, of course, that Belmont and Pink are not direct holdings that the recognition power is exclusive. Those cases considered the validity of executive agreements, not the initial act of recognition. The President\xe2\x80\x99s determination in those cases did not contradict an Act of Congress. And the primary issue was whether the executive agreements could supersede state law. Still, the language in Pink and Belmont, which confirms the President\xe2\x80\x99s competence to determine questions of recognition, is strong support for the conclusion that it is for the President alone to determine which foreign governments are legitimate.

\n

Banco Nacional de Cuba contains even stronger statements regarding the President\xe2\x80\x99s authority over recognition. There, the status of Cuba\xe2\x80\x99s Government and its acts as a sovereign were at issue. As the Court explained, \xe2\x80\x9cPolitical recognition is exclusively a function of the Executive.\xe2\x80\x9d 376 U. S., at 410. Because the Executive had recognized the Cuban Government, the Court held that it should be treated as sovereign and could benefit from the \xe2\x80\x9cact of state\xe2\x80\x9d doctrine. See also Baker v. Carr,\n\n369 U. S. 186,\n213 (1962)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is the executive that determines a person\xe2\x80\x99s status as representative of a foreign government\xe2\x80\x9d); National City Bank of N. Y., 348 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 358 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe status of the Republic of China in our courts is a matter for determination by the Executive and is outside the competence of this Court\xe2\x80\x9d). As these cases illustrate, the Court has long considered recognition to be the exclusive prerogative of the Executive.

\n

The Secretary now urges the Court to define the executive power over foreign relations in even broader terms. He contends that under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent the President has \xe2\x80\x9cexclusive authority to conduct diplomatic relations,\xe2\x80\x9d along with \xe2\x80\x9cthe bulk of foreign-affairs powers.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 18, 16. In support of his submission that the President has broad, undefined powers over foreign affairs, the Secretary quotes United States v. Curtiss-Wright Export Corp., which described the President as \xe2\x80\x9cthe sole organ of the federal government in the field of international relations.\xe2\x80\x9d 299 U. S., at 320. This Court declines to acknowledge that unbounded power. A formulation broader than the rule that the President alone determines what nations to formally recognize as legitimate\xe2\x80\x94and that he consequently controls his statements on matters of recognition\xe2\x80\x94presents different issues and is unnecessary to the resolution of this case.

\n

The Curtiss-Wright case does not extend so far as the Secretary suggests. In Curtiss-Wright, the Court considered whether a congressional delegation of power to the President was constitutional. Congress had passed a joint resolution giving the President the discretion to prohibit arms sales to certain militant powers in South America. The resolution provided criminal penalties for violation of those orders. Id., at 311\xe2\x80\x93312. The Court held that the delegation was constitutional, reasoning that Congress may grant the President substantial authority and discretion in the field of foreign affairs. Id., at 315\xe2\x80\x93329. Describing why such broad delegation may be appropriate, the opinion stated:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIn this vast external realm, with its important, complicated, delicate and manifold problems, the President alone has the power to speak or listen as a representative of the nation. He makes treaties with the advice and consent of the Senate; but he alone negotiates. Into the field of negotiation the Senate cannot intrude; and Congress itself is powerless to invade it. As Marshall said in his great argument of March 7, 1800, in the House of Representatives, \xe2\x80\x98The President is the sole organ of the nation in its external relations, and its sole representative with foreign nations.\xe2\x80\x99 [10 Annals of Cong.] 613. \xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 319.

\n

This description of the President\xe2\x80\x99s exclusive power was not necessary to the holding of Curtiss-Wright\xe2\x80\x94which, after all, dealt with congressionally authorized action, not a unilateral Presidential determination. Indeed, Curtiss-Wright did not hold that the President is free from Congress\xe2\x80\x99 lawmaking power in the field of internationalrelations. The President does have a unique role in communi-cating with foreign governments, as then-Congressman John Marshall acknowledged. See 10 Annals of Cong. 613 (1800) (cited in Curtiss-Wright, supra, at 319). But whether the realm is foreign or domestic, it is still the Legislative Branch, not the Executive Branch, that makes the law.

\n

In a world that is ever more compressed and interdependent, it is essential the congressional role in foreign affairs be understood and respected. For it is Congress that makes laws, and in countless ways its laws will and should shape the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s course. The Executive is not free from the ordinary controls and checks of Congress merely because foreign affairs are at issue. See, e.g., Medell\xc3\xadn v. Texas,\n\n552 U. S. 491\n\xe2\x80\x93532 (2008); Youngstown, 343 U. S., at 589; Little v. Barreme, 2 Cranch 170, 177\xe2\x80\x93179 (1804); Glennon, Two Views of Presidential Foreign Affairs Power: Little v. Barreme or Curtiss-Wright? 13 Yale J. Int\xe2\x80\x99l L. 5, 19\xe2\x80\x9320 (1988); cf. Dames & Moore v. Regan,\n\n453 U. S. 654\n\xe2\x80\x93681 (1981). It is not for the President alone to determine the whole content of the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s foreign policy.

\n

That said, judicial precedent and historical practice teach that it is for the President alone to make the specific decision of what foreign power he will recognize as legitimate, both for the Nation as a whole and for the purpose of making his own position clear within the context of recognition in discussions and negotiations with foreign nations. Recognition is an act with immediate and powerful significance for international relations, so the President\xe2\x80\x99s position must be clear. Congress cannot require him to contradict his own statement regarding a determination of formal recognition.

\n

Zivotofsky\xe2\x80\x99s contrary arguments are unconvincing. The decisions he relies upon are largely inapposite. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases do not hold that the recognition power is shared. Jones v. United States,\n\n137 U. S. 202 (1890)\n, and Boumediene v. Bush,\n\n553 U. S. 723 (2008)\n, each addressed the status of territories controlled or acquired by the United States\xe2\x80\x94not whether a province ought to be recognized as part of a foreign country. See also Vermilya-Brown Co. v. Connell,\n\n335 U.\xc2\xa0S. 377,\n380 (1948)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[D]etermination of [American] sovereignty over an area is for the legislative and executive departments\xe2\x80\x9d). And no one disputes that Congress has a role in determining the status of United States territories. See U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Art. IV, \xc2\xa73, cl.\xc2\xa02 (Congress may \xe2\x80\x9cdispose of and make all needful Rules and Regulations respecting the Territory or other Property belonging to the United States\xe2\x80\x9d). Other cases describing a shared power address the recognition of Indian tribes\xe2\x80\x94which is, similarly, a distinct issue from the recognition of foreign countries. See Cherokee Nation v. Georgia, 5 Pet. 1 (1831).

\n

To be sure, the Court has mentioned both of the political branches in discussing international recognition, but it has done so primarily in affirming that the Judiciary is not responsible for recognizing foreign nations. See Oetjen, 246 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 302 (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Who is the sovereign, de\xc2\xa0jure or de\xc2\xa0facto, of a territory is not a judicial, but is a political question, the determination of which by the legislative and executive departments of any government conclusively binds the judges\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Jones, supra, at 212)); United States v. Palmer, 3 Wheat. 610, 643 (1818) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he courts of the union must view [a] newly constituted government as it is viewed by the legislative and executive departments of the government of the United States\xe2\x80\x9d). This is consistent with the fact that Congress, in the ordinary course, does support the President\xe2\x80\x99s recognition policy, for instance by confirming an ambassador to the recognized foreign government. Those cases do not cast doubt on the view that the Executive Branch determines whether the United States will recognize foreign states and governments and their territorial bounds.

\n

C

\n

Having examined the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s text and this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent, it is appropriate to turn to accepted understandings and practice. In separation-of-powers cases this Court has often \xe2\x80\x9cput significant weight upon historical practice.\xe2\x80\x9d NLRB v. Noel Canning, 573 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 6) (emphasis deleted). Here, history is not all on one side, but on balance it provides strong support for the conclusion that the recognition power is the President\xe2\x80\x99s alone. As Zivotofsky argues, certain historical incidents can be interpreted to support the position that recognition is a shared power. But the weight of historical evidence supports the opposite view, which is that the formal determination of recognition is a power to be exercised only by the President.

\n

The briefs of the parties and amici, which have been of considerable assistance to the Court, give a more complete account of the relevant history, as do the works of scholars in this field. See, e.g., Brief for Respondent 26\xe2\x80\x9339; Brief for Petitioner 34\xe2\x80\x9357; Brief for American Jewish Committee as Amicus Curiae 6\xe2\x80\x9324; J. Goebel, The Recognition Policy of the United States 97\xe2\x80\x93170 (1915) (Goebel); 1 Moore \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa728\xe2\x80\x9358, 74\xe2\x80\x93164; Reinstein, Is the President\xe2\x80\x99s Recognition Power Exclusive? 86 Temp. L. Rev. 1, 3\xe2\x80\x9350 (2013). But even a brief survey of the major historical examples, with an emphasis on those said to favor Zivotofsky, establishes no more than that some Presidents have chosen to cooperate with Congress, not that Congress itself has exercised the recognition power.

\n

From the first Administration forward, the President has claimed unilateral authority to recognize foreign sovereigns. For the most part, Congress has acquiesced in the Executive\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of the recognition power. On occasion, the President has chosen, as may often be prudent, to consult and coordinate with Congress. As Judge Tatel noted in this case, however, \xe2\x80\x9cthe most striking thing\xe2\x80\x9d about the history of recognition \xe2\x80\x9cis what is absent from it: a situation like this one,\xe2\x80\x9d where Congress has enacted a statute contrary to the President\xe2\x80\x99s formal and considered statement concerning recognition. 725 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 221 (concurring opinion).

\n

The first debate over the recognition power arose in 1793, after France had been torn by revolution. See Prakash & Ramsey, The Executive Power over Foreign Affairs, 111 Yale L. J. 231, 312 (2001). Once the Revolutionary Government was established, Secretary of State Jefferson and President Washington, without consulting Congress, authorized the American Ambassador to resume relations with the new regime. See Letter to Gouverneur Morris (Mar. 12, 1793), in 25 Papers of Thomas Jefferson 367, 367\xe2\x80\x93368 (J. Catanzariti ed. 1992); Goebel 99\xe2\x80\x93104. Soon thereafter, the new French Government proposed to send an ambassador, Citizen Genet, to the United States. See id., at 105. Members of the President\xe2\x80\x99s Cabinet agreed that receiving Genet would be a binding and public act of recognition. See Opinion on the Treaties with France (Apr. 28, 1793), in 25 Papers of Thomas Jefferson, at 608, 612 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe reception of the Minister at all .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is an ackno[w]le[d]gement of the legitimacy of their government\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Letter from A. Hamilton to G. Washington (Cabinet Paper) (Apr. 1793), in 4 Works of Alexander Hamilton 369, 369\xe2\x80\x93396 (H. Lodge ed. 1904). They de-cided, however, both that Genet should be received and that consultation with Congress was not necessary. See T. Jefferson, Anas (Apr. 18, 1793), in 1 Writings of Thomas Jefferson 226, 227 (P. Ford ed. 1892); Cabinet Opinion on Washington\xe2\x80\x99s Questions on Neutrality and the Alliance with France (Apr. 19, 1793), in 25 Papers of Thomas Jefferson, at 570. Congress expressed no disagreement with this position, and Genet\xe2\x80\x99s reception marked the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s first act of recognition\xe2\x80\x94one made by the President alone. See Prakash, supra, at 312\xe2\x80\x93313.

\n

The recognition power again became relevant when yet another revolution took place\xe2\x80\x94this time, in South America, as several colonies rose against Spain. In 1818, Speakerof the House Henry Clay announced he \xe2\x80\x9cintended mov-ing the recognition of Buenos Ayres and probably of Chile.\xe2\x80\x9d Goebel 121. Clay thus sought to appropriate money \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[f\xc2\xa0]or one year\xe2\x80\x99s salary\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d for \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a Minister\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d topresent-day Argentina. 32 Annals of Cong. 1500 (1818). President Monroe, however, did not share that view. Although Clay gave \xe2\x80\x9cone of the most remarkable speeches of his career,\xe2\x80\x9d his proposed bill was defeated. Goebel 123;\xc2\xa032 Annals of Cong. 1655. That action has been attributed, in part, to the fact that Congress agreed the recognition power rested solely with the President. Goebel 124; see, e.g., 32 Annals of Cong. 1570 (statement of Rep. Alexander Smyth) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he acknowledgment of the independence of a new Power is an exercise of Executive authority; consequently, for Congress to direct the Executive how he shall exercise this power, is an act of usurpation\xe2\x80\x9d). Four years later, after the President had decided to recognize the South American republics, Congress did pass a resolution, on his request, appropriating funds for \xe2\x80\x9csuch missions to the independent nations on the American continent, as the President of the United States may deem proper.\xe2\x80\x9d Act of May 4, 1822, ch. 52,\n\n3Stat.\n678.

\n

A decade later, President Jackson faced a recognition crisis over Texas. In 1835, Texas rebelled against Mexico and formed its own government. See Goebel 144\xe2\x80\x93147. But the President feared that recognizing the new government could ignite a war. See A. Jackson, To the Senate and House of Representatives of the United States (Dec. 21, 1836), in 3 Messages and Papers of the Presidents 265, 266\xe2\x80\x93267 (J. Richardson ed. 1899). After Congress urged him to recognize Texas, see Cong. Globe, 24th Cong., 1st Sess., 453 (1836); H. R. Rep. No. 854, 24th Cong., 1st Sess. (1836), the President delivered a message to the Legislature. He concluded there had not been a \xe2\x80\x9cdeliberate inquiry\xe2\x80\x9d into whether the President or Congress possessed the recognition power. See A. Jackson, in 3 Messages and Papers of the Presidents, at 267. He stated, however, \xe2\x80\x9con the ground of expediency, I am disposed to concur\xe2\x80\x9d with Congress\xe2\x80\x99 preference regarding Texas. Ibid. In response Congress appropriated funds for a \xe2\x80\x9cdiplomatic agent to be sent to the Republic of Texas, whenever the President of the United States .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall deem it expedient to appoint such minister.\xe2\x80\x9d Act of Mar. 3, 1837,\n\n5Stat.\n170. Thus, although he cooperated with Congress, the President was left to execute the formal act of recognition.

\n

President Lincoln, too, sought to coordinate with Congress when he requested support for his recognition of Liberia and Haiti. In his first annual message to Congress he said he could see no reason \xe2\x80\x9cwhy we should persevere longer in withholding our recognition of the independence and sovereignty of Hayti and Liberia.\xe2\x80\x9d Lincoln\xe2\x80\x99s First Annual Message to Congress (Dec. 3, 1861), in 6 Messages and Papers of the Presidents 44, 47. Nonetheless, he was \xe2\x80\x9c[u]nwilling\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9cinaugurate a novel policy in regard to them without the approbation of Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. In response Congress concurred in the President\xe2\x80\x99s recognition determination and enacted a law appropriating funds to appoint diplomatic representatives to the two countries\xe2\x80\x94leaving, as usual, the actual dispatch of ambassadors and formal statement of recognition to the President. Act of June 5, 1862,\n\n12Stat.\n421.

\n

Three decades later, the branches again were able to reach an accord, this time with regard to Cuba. In 1898, an insurgency against the Spanish colonial government was raging in Cuba. President McKinley determined to ask Congress for authorization to send armed forces to Cuba to help quell the violence. See 31 Cong. Rec. 3699\xe2\x80\x933702 (1898). Although McKinley thought Spain was to blame for the strife, he opposed recognizing either Cuba or its insurgent government. Id., at 3701. At first, the House proposed a resolution consistent with McKinley\xe2\x80\x99s wishes. Id., at 3810. The Senate countered with a resolution that authorized the use of force but that did recognize both Cuban independence and the insurgent government. Id., at 3993. When the Senate\xe2\x80\x99s version reached the House, the House again rejected the language recognizing Cuban independence. Id., at 4017. The resolution went to Conference, which, after debate, reached a compromise. See Reinstein, 86 Temp. L.\xc2\xa0Rev., at 40\xe2\x80\x9341. The final resolution stated \xe2\x80\x9cthe people of the Island of Cuba are, and of right ought to be, free and independent,\xe2\x80\x9d but made no mention of recognizing a new Cuban Government. Act of Apr. 20, 1898,\n\n30Stat.\n738. Accepting the compromise, the President signed the joint resolution. See Reinstein, 86 Temp. L.\xc2\xa0Rev., at 41.

\n

For the next 80 years, \xe2\x80\x9c[P]residents consistently recognized new states and governments without any serious opposition from, or activity in, Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; see 2 Whiteman \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa76\xe2\x80\x9360, at 133\xe2\x80\x93242 (detailing over 50 recognition decisions made by the Executive). The next debate over recognition did not occur until the late 1970\xe2\x80\x99s. It concerned China.

\n

President Carter recognized the People\xe2\x80\x99s Republic of China (PRC) as the government of China, and derecognized the Republic of China, located on Taiwan. See S. Kan, Cong. Research Serv., China/Taiwan: Evolution of the \xe2\x80\x9cOne China\xe2\x80\x9d Policy\xe2\x80\x94Key Statements from Washington, Beijing, and Taipei 1, 10 (Oct. 10, 2014). As to the status of Taiwan, the President \xe2\x80\x9cacknowledge[d] the Chinese position\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cTaiwan is part of China,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 39 (text of U. S.\xe2\x80\x93PRC Joint Communique on the Establishment of Diplomatic Relations (Jan. 1, 1979)), but he did not accept that claim. The President proposed a new law defining how the United States would conduct business with Taiwan. See Hearings on Taiwan Legislation before the House Committee on Foreign Affairs, 96th Cong., 1st Sess., 2\xe2\x80\x936 (1979) (statement of Warren Christopher, Dep-uty Secretary of State). After extensive revisions, Congress passed, and the President signed, the Taiwan Relations Act,\n\n93Stat.\n14 (1979) (codified as amended at 22 U. S. C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73301\xe2\x80\x933316). The Act (in a simplified summary) treated Taiwan as if it were a legally distinct entity from China\xe2\x80\x94an entity with which the United States intended to maintain strong ties. See, e.g., \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73301, 3303(a), (b)(1), (b)(7).

\n

Throughout the legislative process, however, no one raised a serious question regarding the President\xe2\x80\x99s exclusive authority to recognize the PRC\xe2\x80\x94or to decline to grant formal recognition to Taiwan. See, e.g., 125 Cong. Rec. 6709 (1979) (statement of Sen. Jacob Javits) (\xe2\x80\x9cNeither bill [proposed by either Chamber] sought to reestablish official relations between the United States and the Republic of China on Taiwan; Congress .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. does not have the author-ity to do that even if it wanted to do so\xe2\x80\x9d). Rather, Congress accepted the President\xe2\x80\x99s recognition determination as a completed, lawful act; and it proceeded to outline the trade and policy provisions that, in its judgment, were appropriate in light of that decision.

\n

This history confirms the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion in the instant case that the power to recognize or decline to recognize a foreign state and its territorial bounds resides in the President alone. For the most part, Congress has respected the Executive\xe2\x80\x99s policies and positions as to formal recognition. At times, Congress itself has defended the President\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional prerogative. Over the last 100 years, there has been scarcely any debate over the President\xe2\x80\x99s power to recognize foreign states. In this respect the Legislature, in the narrow context of recognition, on balance has acknowledged the importance of speaking \xe2\x80\x9cwith one voice.\xe2\x80\x9d Crosby, 530 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 381. The weight of historical evidence indicates Congress has accepted that the power to recognize foreign states and governments and their territorial bounds is exclusive to the Presidency.

\n

III

\n

As the power to recognize foreign states resides in the President alone, the question becomes whether \xc2\xa7214(d) infringes on the Executive\xe2\x80\x99s consistent decision to withhold recognition with respect to Jerusalem. See Nixon v. Administrator of General Services,\n\n433 U. S. 425,\n443 (1977)\n (action unlawful when it \xe2\x80\x9cprevents the Executive Branch from accomplishing its constitutionally assigned functions\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Section 214(d) requires that, in a passport or consular report of birth abroad, \xe2\x80\x9cthe Secretary shall, upon the request of the citizen or the citizen\xe2\x80\x99s legal guardian, record the place of birth as Israel\xe2\x80\x9d for a \xe2\x80\x9cUnited States citizen born in the city of Jerusalem.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n116Stat.\n1366. That is, \xc2\xa7214(d) requires the President, through the Secretary, to identify citizens born in Jerusalem who so request as being born in Israel. But according to the President, those citizens were not born in Israel. As a matter of United States policy, neither Israel nor any other country is acknowledged as having sovereignty over Jerusalem. In this way, \xc2\xa7214(d) \xe2\x80\x9cdirectly contradicts\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9ccarefully calibrated and longstanding Executive branch policy of neutrality toward Jerusalem.\xe2\x80\x9d 725 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 217, 216.

\n

If the power over recognition is to mean anything, it must mean that the President not only makes the initial, formal recognition determination but also that he may maintain that determination in his and his agent\xe2\x80\x99s statements. This conclusion is a matter of both common sense and necessity. If Congress could command the President to state a recognition position inconsistent with his own, Congress could override the President\xe2\x80\x99s recognition determination. Under international law, recognition may be effected by \xe2\x80\x9cwritten or oral declaration of the recognizing state.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 Moore \xc2\xa727, at 73. In addition an act of recognition must \xe2\x80\x9cleave no doubt as to the intention to grant it.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 Oppenheim\xe2\x80\x99s International Law \xc2\xa750, at 169. Thus, if Congress could alter the President\xe2\x80\x99s statements on matters of recognition or force him to contradict them, Congress in effect would exercise the recognition power.

\n

As Justice Jackson wrote in Youngstown, when a Presidential power is \xe2\x80\x9cexclusive,\xe2\x80\x9d it \xe2\x80\x9cdisabl[es] the Congress from acting upon the subject.\xe2\x80\x9d 343 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 637\xe2\x80\x93638 (concurring opinion). Here, the subject is quite narrow: The Executive\xe2\x80\x99s exclusive power extends no further than his formal recognition determination. But as to that determination, Congress may not enact a law that directly contradicts it. This is not to say Congress may not express its disagreement with the President in myriad ways. For example, it may enact an embargo, decline to confirm an ambassador, or even declare war. But none of these acts would alter the President\xe2\x80\x99s recognition decision.

\n

If Congress may not pass a law, speaking in its own voice, that effects formal recognition, then it follows that it may not force the President himself to contradict his earlier statement. That congressional command would not only prevent the Nation from speaking with one voice but also prevent the Executive itself from doing so in conducting foreign relations.

\n

Although the statement required by \xc2\xa7214(d) would not itself constitute a formal act of recognition, it is a mandate that the Executive contradict his prior recognition determination in an official document issued by the Secretary of State. See Urtetiqui v. D\xe2\x80\x99Arcy, 9 Pet. 692, 699 (1835) (a passport \xe2\x80\x9cfrom its nature and object, is addressed to foreign powers\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cis to be considered .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in the character of a political document\xe2\x80\x9d). As a result, it is unconstitu-tional. This is all the more clear in light of the longstanding treatment of a passport\xe2\x80\x99s place-of-birth section as an official executive statement implicating recognition. See 725 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 224 (Tatel, J., concurring). The Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s position on this point has been consistent: He will not place information in the place-of-birth section of a passport that contradicts the President\xe2\x80\x99s recognition policy. See 7 FAM \xc2\xa71383. If a citizen objects to the country listed as sovereign over his place of birth, then the Secretary will accommodate him by listing the city or town of birth rather than the country. See id., \xc2\xa71383.6. But the Secretary will not list a sovereign that contradicts the President\xe2\x80\x99s recognition policy in a passport. Thus, the Secretary will not list \xe2\x80\x9cIsrael\xe2\x80\x9d in a passport as the country containing Jerusalem.

\n

The flaw in \xc2\xa7214(d) is further underscored by the undoubted fact that that the purpose of the statute was to infringe on the recognition power\xe2\x80\x94a power the Court now holds is the sole prerogative of the President. The statute is titled \xe2\x80\x9cUnited States Policy with Respect to Jerusalem as the Capital of Israel.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7214,\n\n116Stat.\n1365. The House Conference Report proclaimed that \xc2\xa7214 \xe2\x80\x9ccontains four provisions related to the recognition of Jerusalem as Israel\xe2\x80\x99s capital.\xe2\x80\x9d H. R. Conf. Rep. No. 107\xe2\x80\x93671, p. 123 (2002). And, indeed, observers interpreted \xc2\xa7214 as altering United States policy regarding Jerusalem\xe2\x80\x94which led to protests across the region. See supra, at 4. From the face of \xc2\xa7214, from the legislative history, and from its reception, it is clear that Congress wanted to express its displeasure with the President\xe2\x80\x99s policy by, among other things, commanding the Executive to contradict his own, earlier stated position on Jerusalem. This Congress may not do.

\n

It is true, as Zivotofsky notes, that Congress has substantial authority over passports. See Haig v. Agee, 453 U.\xc2\xa0S. 280 (1981); Zemel v. Rusk,\n\n381 U. S. 1 (1965)\n; Kent v. Dulles,\n\n357 U. S. 116 (1958)\n. The Court does not question the power of Congress to enact passport legislation of wide scope. In Kent v. Dulles, for example, the Court held that if a person\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98liberty\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to travel \xe2\x80\x9cis to be regulated\xe2\x80\x9d through a passport, \xe2\x80\x9cit must be pursuant to the law-making functions of the Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d See id., at 129. Later cases, such as Zemel v. Rusk and Haig v. Agee, also proceeded on the assumption that Congress must authorize the grounds on which passports may be approved or denied. See Zemel, supra, at 7\xe2\x80\x9313; Haig, supra, at 289\xe2\x80\x93306. This is consistent with the extensive lawmaking power the Constitution vests in Congress over the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s foreign affairs.

\n

The problem with \xc2\xa7214(d), however, lies in how Congress exercised its authority over passports. It was an improper act for Congress to \xe2\x80\x9caggrandiz[e] its power at the expense of another branch\xe2\x80\x9d by requiring the President to contradict an earlier recognition determination in an official document issued by the Executive Branch. Freytag v. Commissioner,\n\n501 U. S. 868,\n878 (1991)\n. To allow Congress to control the President\xe2\x80\x99s communication in the context of a formal recognition determination is to allow Congress to exercise that exclusive power itself. As a result, the statute is unconstitutional.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

In holding \xc2\xa7214(d) invalid the Court does not question the substantial powers of Congress over foreign affairs in general or passports in particular. This case is confined solely to the exclusive power of the President to control recognition determinations, including formal statements by the Executive Branch acknowledging the legitimacy of a state or government and its territorial bounds. Congress cannot command the President to contradict an earlier recognition determination in the issuance of passports.

\n

The judgment of the Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit is

\n

Affirmed.

Justice Scalia, with whom The Chief Justice and Justice Alito join, dissenting.

\n

Before this country declared independence, the law of England entrusted the King with the exclusive care of his kingdom\xe2\x80\x99s foreign affairs. The royal prerogative included the \xe2\x80\x9csole power of sending ambassadors to foreign states, and receiving them at home,\xe2\x80\x9d the sole authority to \xe2\x80\x9cmake treaties, leagues, and alliances with foreign states and princes,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cthe sole prerogative of making war and peace,\xe2\x80\x9d and the \xe2\x80\x9csole power of raising and regulating fleets and armies.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 W. Blackstone, Commentaries *253, *257, *262. The People of the United States had other ideas when they organized our Government. They considered a sound structure of balanced powers essential to the preservation of just government, and international relations formed no exception to that principle.

\n

The People therefore adopted a Constitution that divides responsibility for the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s foreign concerns between the legislative and executive departments. The Constitution gave the President the \xe2\x80\x9cexecutive Power,\xe2\x80\x9d authority to send and responsibility to receive ambassadors, power to make treaties, and command of the Army and Navy\xe2\x80\x94though they qualified some of these powers by requiring consent of the Senate. Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71\xe2\x80\x933. At the same time, they gave Congress powers over war, foreign commerce, naturalization, and more. Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa78. \xe2\x80\x9cFully eleven of the powers that Article I, \xc2\xa78 grants Congress deal in some way with foreign affairs.\xe2\x80\x9d L. Tribe, American Constitutional Law, \xc2\xa75\xe2\x80\x9318, p. 965.

\n

This case arises out of a dispute between the Executive and Legislative Branches about whether the United States should treat Jerusalem as a part of Israel. The Constitution contemplates that the political branches will make policy about the territorial claims of foreign nations the same way they make policy about other international matters: The President will exercise his powers on the basis of his views, Congress its powers on the basis of its views. That is just what has happened here.

\n

I

\n

The political branches of our Government agree on the real-world fact that Israel controls the city of Jerusalem. See Jerusalem Embassy Act of 1995,\n\n109Stat.\n398; Brief for Respondent 3. They disagree, however, about how official documents should record the birthplace of an American citizen born in Jerusalem. The Executive does not accept any state\xe2\x80\x99s claim to sovereignty over Jerusalem, and it maintains that the birthplace designation \xe2\x80\x9cIsrael\xe2\x80\x9d would clash with this stance of neutrality. But the National Legislature has enacted a statute that provides: \xe2\x80\x9cFor purposes of the registration of birth, certification of nationality, or issuance of a passport of a United States citizen born in the city of Jerusalem, the Secretary [of State] shall, upon the request of the citizen or the citizen\xe2\x80\x99s legal guardian, record the place of birth as Israel.\xe2\x80\x9d Foreign Relations Authorization Act, Fiscal Year 2003, \xc2\xa7214(d),\n\n116Stat.\n1366. Menachem Zivotofsky\xe2\x80\x99s parents seek enforcement of this statutory right in the issuance of their son\xe2\x80\x99s passport and consular report of birth abroad. They regard their son\xe2\x80\x99s birthplace as a part of Israel and insist as \xe2\x80\x9ca matter of conscience\xe2\x80\x9d that his Israeli nativity \xe2\x80\x9cnot be erased\xe2\x80\x9d from his identity documents. App. 26.

\n

Before turning to Presidential power under Article II, I think it well to establish the statute\xe2\x80\x99s basis in congressional power under Article I. Congress\xe2\x80\x99s power to \xe2\x80\x9cestablish an uniform Rule of Naturalization,\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa78, cl.\xc2\xa04, enables it to grant American citizenship to someone born abroad. United States v. Wong Kim Ark,\n\n169 U.\xc2\xa0S. 649\n\xe2\x80\x93703 (1898). The naturalization power also enables Congress to furnish the people it makes citizens with papers verifying their citizenship\xe2\x80\x94say a consular report of birth abroad (which certifies citizenship of an American born outside the United States) or a passport (which certifies citizenship for purposes of international travel). As the Necessary and Proper Clause confirms, every congressional power \xe2\x80\x9ccarries with it all those incidental powers which are necessary to its complete and effectual execution.\xe2\x80\x9d Cohens v. Virginia, 6 Wheat. 264, 429 (1821). Even on a miserly understanding of Congress\xe2\x80\x99s incidental authority, Congress may make grants of citizenship \xe2\x80\x9ceffec-tual\xe2\x80\x9d by providing for the issuance of certificates authenticating them.

\n

One would think that if Congress may grant Zivotofsky a passport and a birth report, it may also require these papers to record his birthplace as \xe2\x80\x9cIsrael.\xe2\x80\x9d The birthplace specification promotes the document\xe2\x80\x99s citizenship-authenticating function by identifying the bearer, distinguish-ing people with similar names but different birthplaces from each other, helping authorities uncover identity fraud, and facilitating retrieval of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s cit-izenship records. See App. 70. To be sure, recording Zivotovsky\xe2\x80\x99s birthplace as \xe2\x80\x9cJerusalem\xe2\x80\x9d rather than \xe2\x80\x9cIsrael\xe2\x80\x9d would fulfill these objectives, but when faced with alternative ways to carry its powers into execution, Congress has the \xe2\x80\x9cdiscretion\xe2\x80\x9d to choose the one it deems \xe2\x80\x9cmost beneficial to the people.\xe2\x80\x9d McCulloch v. Maryland, 4 Wheat. 316, 421 (1819). It thus has the right to decide that recording birthplaces as \xe2\x80\x9cIsrael\xe2\x80\x9d makes for better foreign policy. Or that regardless of international politics, a passport or birth report should respect its bearer\xe2\x80\x99s conscientious belief that Jerusalem belongs to Israel.

\n

No doubt congressional discretion in executing legislative powers has its limits; Congress\xe2\x80\x99s chosen approach must be not only \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary\xe2\x80\x9d to carrying its powers into execution, but also \xe2\x80\x9cproper.\xe2\x80\x9d Congress thus may not transcend boundaries upon legislative authority stated or implied elsewhere in the Constitution. But as we shall see, \xc2\xa7214(d) does not transgress any such restriction.

\n

II

\n

The Court frames this case as a debate about recognition. Recognition is a sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s official acceptance of a status under international law. A sovereign might recognize a foreign entity as a state, a regime as the other state\xe2\x80\x99s government, a place as part of the other state\xe2\x80\x99s territory, rebel forces in the other state as a belligerent power, and so on. 2 M. Whiteman, Digest of International Law \xc2\xa71 (1963) (hereinafter Whiteman). President Truman recognized Israel as a state in 1948, but Presidents have consistently declined to recognize Jerusalem as a part of Israel\xe2\x80\x99s (or any other state\xe2\x80\x99s) sovereign territory.

\n

The Court holds that the Constitution makes the President alone responsible for recognition and that \xc2\xa7214(d) invades this exclusive power. I agree that the Constitution empowers the President to extend recognition on behalf of the United States, but I find it a much harder question whether it makes that power exclusive. The Court tells us that \xe2\x80\x9cthe weight of historical evidence\xe2\x80\x9d supports exclusive executive authority over \xe2\x80\x9cthe formal determination of recognition.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 20. But even with its attention confined to formal recognition, the Court is forced to admit that \xe2\x80\x9chistory is not all on one side.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. To take a stark example, Congress legislated in 1934 to grant independence to the Philippines, which were then an American colony.\n\n48Stat.\n456. In the course of doing so, Congress directed the President to \xe2\x80\x9crecognize the independence of the Philippine Islands as a separate and self-governing nation\xe2\x80\x9d and to \xe2\x80\x9cacknowledge the authority and control over the same of the government instituted by the people thereof.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa710, id., at 463. Constitutional? And if Congress may control recognition when exercising its power \xe2\x80\x9cto dispose of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the Territory or other Property belonging to the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d Art. IV, \xc2\xa73, cl.\xc2\xa02, why not when exercising other enumerated powers? Neither text nor history nor precedent yields a clear answer to these questions. Fortunately, I have no need to confront these matters today\xe2\x80\x94nor does the Court\xe2\x80\x94because \xc2\xa7214(d) plainly does not concern recognition.

\n

Recognition is more than an announcement of a policy. Like the ratification of an international agreement or the termination of a treaty, it is a formal legal act with effects under international law. It signifies acceptance of an international status, and it makes a commitment to continued acceptance of that status and respect for any attendant rights. See, e.g., Convention on the Rights and Duties of States, Art. 6, Dec. 26, 1933,\n\n49Stat.\n3100, T. S. No. 881. \xe2\x80\x9cIts legal effect is to create an estoppel. By granting recognition, [states] debar themselves from challenging in future whatever they have previously acknowledged.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 G. Schwarzenberger, International Law 127 (3d ed. 1957). In order to extend recognition, a state must perform an act that unequivocally manifests that intention. Whiteman \xc2\xa73. That act can consist of an express conferral of recognition, or one of a handful of acts that by international custom imply recognition\xe2\x80\x94chiefly, entering into a bilateral treaty, and sending or receiving an ambassador. Ibid.

\n

To know all this is to realize at once that \xc2\xa7214(d) has nothing to do with recognition. Section 214(d) does not require the Secretary to make a formal declaration about Israel\xe2\x80\x99s sovereignty over Jerusalem. And nobody suggests that international custom infers acceptance of sovereignty from the birthplace designation on a passport or birth report, as it does from bilateral treaties or exchanges of ambassadors. Recognition would preclude the United States (as a matter of international law) from later contesting Israeli sovereignty over Jerusalem. But making a notation in a passport or birth report does not encumber the Republic with any international obligations. It leaves the Nation free (so far as international law is concerned) to change its mind in the future. That would be true even if the statute required all passports to list \xe2\x80\x9cIsrael.\xe2\x80\x9d But in fact it requires only those passports to list \xe2\x80\x9cIsrael\xe2\x80\x9d for which the citizen (or his guardian) requests \xe2\x80\x9cIsrael\xe2\x80\x9d; all the rest, under the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s policy, list \xe2\x80\x9cJerusalem.\xe2\x80\x9d It is utterly impossible for this deference to private requests to constitute an act that unequivocally manifests an intention to grant recognition.

\n

Section 214(d) performs a more prosaic function than extending recognition. Just as foreign countries care about what our Government has to say about their borders, so too American citizens often care about what our Government has to say about their identities. Cf. Bowen v. Roy,\n\n476 U.\xc2\xa0S. 693 (1986)\n. The State Department does not grant or deny recognition in order to accommodate these individuals, but it does make exceptions to its rules about how it records birthplaces. Although normal protocol requires specifying the bearer\xe2\x80\x99s country of birth in his passport, Dept. of State, 7 Foreign Affairs Manual (FAM) \xc2\xa71300, App. D, \xc2\xa71330(a) (2014), the State Department will, if the bearer protests, specify the city of birth instead\xe2\x80\x94so that an Irish nationalist may have his birthplace recorded as \xe2\x80\x9cBelfast\xe2\x80\x9d rather than \xe2\x80\x9cUnited Kingdom,\xe2\x80\x9d id., \xc2\xa71380(a). And although normal protocol requires specifying the country with present sovereignty over the bearer\xe2\x80\x99s place of birth, id., \xc2\xa71330(b), a special exception allows a bearer born before 1948 in what was then Palestine to have his birthplace listed as \xe2\x80\x9cPalestine,\xe2\x80\x9d id., \xc2\xa71360(g). Section 214(d) requires the State Department to make a further accommodation. Even though the Department normally refuses to specify a country that lacks recognized sovereignty over the bearer\xe2\x80\x99s birthplace, it must suspend that policy upon the request of an American citizen born in Jerusalem. Granting a request to specify \xe2\x80\x9cIsrael\xe2\x80\x9d rather than \xe2\x80\x9cJerusalem\xe2\x80\x9d does not recognize Israel\xe2\x80\x99s sovereignty over Jerusalem, just as granting a request to specify \xe2\x80\x9cBelfast\xe2\x80\x9d rather than \xe2\x80\x9cUnited Kingdom\xe2\x80\x9d does not derecognize the United Kingdom\xe2\x80\x99s sovereignty over Northern Ireland.

\n

The best indication that \xc2\xa7214(d) does not concern recognition comes from the State Department\xe2\x80\x99s policies concerning Taiwan. According to the Solicitor General, theUnited States \xe2\x80\x9cacknowledges the Chinese position\xe2\x80\x9d that Taiwan is a part of China, but \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not take a position\xe2\x80\x9d of its own on that issue. Brief for Respondent 51\xe2\x80\x9352. Even so, the State Department has for a long time recorded the birthplace of a citizen born in Taiwan as \xe2\x80\x9cChina.\xe2\x80\x9d It indeed insisted on doing so until Congress passed a law (on which \xc2\xa7214(d) was modeled) giving citizens the option to have their birthplaces recorded as \xe2\x80\x9cTaiwan.\xe2\x80\x9d See \xc2\xa7132,\n\n108Stat.\n395, as amended by \xc2\xa71(r),\n\n108Stat.\n4302. The Solicitor General explains that the designation \xe2\x80\x9cChina\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cinvolves a geographic description, not an assertion that Taiwan is .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. part of sovereign China.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 51\xe2\x80\x9352. Quite so. Section 214(d) likewise calls for nothing beyond a \xe2\x80\x9cgeographic description\xe2\x80\x9d; it does not require the Executive even to assert, never mind formally recognize, that Jerusalem is a part of sovereign Israel. Since birthplace specifications in citizenship documents are matters within Congress\xe2\x80\x99s control, Congress may treat Jerusalem as a part of Israel when regulating the recording of birthplaces, even if the President does not do so when extending recognition. Section 214(d), by the way, expressly directs the Secretary to \xe2\x80\x9crecord the place of birth as Israel\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c[f]or purposes of the registration of birth, certification of nationality, or issuance of a passport.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.) And the law bears the caption, \xe2\x80\x9cRecord of Place of Birth as Israel for Passport Purposes.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.) Finding recognition in this provision is rather like finding admission to the Union in a provision that treats American Samoa as a State for purposes of a federal highway safety program,\n\n23 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7401.

\n

III

\n

The Court complains that \xc2\xa7214(d) requires the Secretary of State to issue official documents implying that Jerusalem is a part of Israel; that it appears in a section of the statute bearing the title \xe2\x80\x9cUnited States Policy with Respect to Jerusalem as the Capital of Israel\xe2\x80\x9d; and that foreign \xe2\x80\x9cobservers interpreted [it] as altering United States policy regarding Jerusalem.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 28. But these features do not show that \xc2\xa7214(d) recognizes Israel\xe2\x80\x99s sovereignty over Jerusalem. They show only that the law displays symbolic support for Israel\xe2\x80\x99s territorial claim. That symbolism may have tremendous significance as a matter of international diplomacy, but it makes no difference as a matter of constitutional law.

\n

Even if the Constitution gives the President sole power to extend recognition, it does not give him sole power to make all decisions relating to foreign disputes over sovereignty. To the contrary, a fair reading of Article I allows Congress to decide for itself how its laws should handle these controversies. Read naturally, power to \xe2\x80\x9cregulate Commerce with foreign Nations,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa78, cl.\xc2\xa03, includes power to regulate imports from Gibraltar as British goods or as Spanish goods. Read naturally, power to \xe2\x80\x9cregulate the Value .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of foreign Coin,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa78, cl.\xc2\xa05, includes power to honor (or not) currency issued by Taiwan. And so on for the other enumerated powers. These are not airy hypotheticals. A trade statute from 1800, for example, pro-vided that \xe2\x80\x9cthe whole of the island of Hispaniola\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94whose status was then in controversy\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cshall for purposes of [the] act be considered as a dependency of the French Republic.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa77,\n\n2Stat.\n10. In 1938, Congress allowed admission of the Vatican City\xe2\x80\x99s public records in federal courts, decades before the United States extended formal recognition. ch. 682,\n\n52Stat.\n1163; Whiteman \xc2\xa768. The Taiwan Relations Act of 1979 grants Taiwan capacity to sue and be sued, even though the United States does not recognize it as a state.\n\n22 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73303(b)(7). Section 214(d) continues in the same tradition.

\n

The Constitution likewise does not give the President exclusive power to determine which claims to statehood and territory \xe2\x80\x9care legitimate in the eyes of the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 11. Congress may express its own views about these matters by declaring war, restricting trade, denying foreign aid, and much else besides. To take just one example, in 1991, Congress responded to Iraq\xe2\x80\x99s invasion of Kuwait by enacting a resolution authorizing use of military force.\n\n105Stat.\n3. No doubt the resolution reflected Congress\xe2\x80\x99s views about the legitimacy of Iraq\xe2\x80\x99s territorial claim. The preamble referred to Iraq\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cillegal occupation\xe2\x80\x9d and stated that \xe2\x80\x9cthe international community has demanded .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that Kuwait\xe2\x80\x99s independence and legitimate government be restored.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. These statements are far more categorical than the caption \xe2\x80\x9cUnited States Policy with Respect to Jerusalem as the Capital of Israel.\xe2\x80\x9d Does it follow that the authorization of the use of military force invaded the President\xe2\x80\x99s exclusive powers? Or that it would have done so had the President recognized Iraqi sovereignty over Kuwait?

\n

History does not even support an exclusive Presidential power to make what the Court calls \xe2\x80\x9cformal statements\xe2\x80\x9d about \xe2\x80\x9cthe legitimacy of a state or government and its territorial bounds,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 29. For a long time, the Houses of Congress have made formal statements announcing their own positions on these issues, again without provoking constitutional objections. A recent resolution expressed the House of Representatives\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cstrong support for the legitimate, democratically-elected Government of Lebanon\xe2\x80\x9d and condemned an \xe2\x80\x9cillegitimate\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cunjustifiable\xe2\x80\x9d insurrection by \xe2\x80\x9cthe terrorist group Hizballah.\xe2\x80\x9d H.\xc2\xa0Res. 1194, 110th Cong, 2d Sess., 1, 4 (2008). An earlier enactment declared \xe2\x80\x9cthe sense of the Congress that .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. Tibet .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is an occupied country under the established principles of international law\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9cTibet\xe2\x80\x99s true representatives are the Dalai Lama and the Tibetan Government in exile.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7355,\n\n105Stat.\n713 (1991). After Texas won independence from Mexico, the Senate resolved that \xe2\x80\x9cthe State of Texas having established and maintained an independent Government, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. it is expedient and proper .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that the independent political existence of the said State be acknowledged by the Government of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Cong. Globe, 24th Cong., 2d Sess., 83 (1837); see id., at 270.

\n

In the final analysis, the Constitution may well deny Congress power to recognize\xe2\x80\x94the power to make an international commitment accepting a foreign entity as a state, a regime as its government, a place as a part ofits territory, and so on. But whatever else \xc2\xa7214(d) maydo, it plainly does not make (or require the President to make) a commitment accepting Israel\xe2\x80\x99s sovereignty over Jerusalem.

\n

IV

\n

The Court does not try to argue that \xc2\xa7214(d) extends recognition; nor does it try to argue that the President holds the exclusive power to make all nonrecognition decisions relating to the status of Jerusalem. As just shown, these arguments would be impossible to make with a straight face.

\n

The Court instead announces a rule that is blatantly gerrymandered to the facts of this case. It concludes that, in addition to the exclusive power to make the \xe2\x80\x9cformal recognition determination,\xe2\x80\x9d the President holds an ancillary exclusive power \xe2\x80\x9cto control .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. formal statements by the Executive Branch acknowledging the legitimacy of a state or government and its territorial bounds.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 29. It follows, the Court explains, that Congress may not \xe2\x80\x9crequir[e] the President to contradict an earlier recognition determination in an official document issued by the Executive Branch.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. So requiring imports from Jerusalem to be taxed like goods from Israel is fine, but requiring Customs to issue an official invoice to that effect is not? Nonsense.

\n

Recognition is a type of legal act, not a type of statement. It is a leap worthy of the Mad Hatter to go from exclusive authority over making legal commitments about sovereignty to exclusive authority over making statements or issuing documents about national borders. The Court may as well jump from power over issuing declaratory judgments to a monopoly on writing law-review articles.

\n

No consistent or coherent theory supports the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision. At times, the Court seems concerned with the possibility of congressional interference with the President\xe2\x80\x99s ability to extend or withhold legal recognition. The Court concedes, as it must, that the notation required by \xc2\xa7214(d) \xe2\x80\x9cwould not itself constitute a formal act of recognition.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 27. It still frets, however, that Congress could try to regulate the President\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cstatements\xe2\x80\x9d in a way that \xe2\x80\x9coverride[s] the President\xe2\x80\x99s recognition determination.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. But \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he circumstance, that .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [a] power may be abused, is no answer. All powers may be abused.\xe2\x80\x9d 2 J. Story, Commentaries on the Constitution of theUnited States \xc2\xa7921, p. 386 (1833). What matters is whether this law interferes with the President\xe2\x80\x99s ability to withhold recognition. It would be comical to claim that it does. The Court identifies no reason to believe that the United States\xe2\x80\x94or indeed any other country\xe2\x80\x94uses the place-of-birth field in passports and birth reports as a forum for performing the act of recognition. That is why nobody thinks the United States withdraws recognition from Canada when it accommodates a Quebec nationalist\xe2\x80\x99s request to have his birthplace recorded as \xe2\x80\x9cMontreal.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

To the extent doubts linger about whether the United States recognizes Israel\xe2\x80\x99s sovereignty over Jerusalem, \xc2\xa7214(d) leaves the President free to dispel them by issuing a disclaimer of intent to recognize. A disclaimer always suffices to prevent an act from effecting recognition. Restatement (Second) of Foreign Relations Law of the United States \xc2\xa7104(1) (1962). Recall that an earlier law grants citizens born in Taiwan the right to have their birthplaces recorded as \xe2\x80\x9cTaiwan.\xe2\x80\x9d The State Department has complied with the law, but states in its Foreign Affairs Manual: \xe2\x80\x9cThe United States does not officially recognize Taiwan as a \xe2\x80\x98state\xe2\x80\x99 or \xe2\x80\x98country,\xe2\x80\x99 although passport issuing officers may enter \xe2\x80\x98Taiwan\xe2\x80\x99 as a place of birth.\xe2\x80\x9d 7 FAM \xc2\xa71300, App. D, \xc2\xa71340(d)(6). Nothing stops a similar disclaimer here.

\n

At other times, the Court seems concerned with Congress\xe2\x80\x99s failure to give effect to a recognition decision that the President has already made. The Court protests, for instance, that \xc2\xa7214(d) \xe2\x80\x9cdirectly contradicts\xe2\x80\x9d the President\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to recognize Israel\xe2\x80\x99s sovereignty over Jerusalem. Ante, at 27. But even if the Constitution empowers the President alone to extend recognition, it nowhere obliges Congress to align its laws with the President\xe2\x80\x99s recognition decisions. Because the President and Congress are \xe2\x80\x9cperfectly co-ordinate by the terms of their common commission,\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 49, p. 314 (C. Rossiter ed. 1961) (Madison), the President\xe2\x80\x99s use of the recognition power does not constrain Congress\xe2\x80\x99s use of its legislative powers.

\n

Congress has legislated without regard to recognition for a long time and in a range of settings. For example, responding in 1817 and 1818 to revolutions in Latin America, Congress amended federal neutrality laws\xe2\x80\x94which originally prohibited private military action for or against recognized states\xe2\x80\x94to prohibit private hostilities against unrecognized states too. ch. 58,\n\n3Stat.\n370; ch. 88,\n\n3Stat.\n447; see The Three Friends,\n\n166 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n\xe2\x80\x9359 (1897). Legislation from 90 years ago provided for the revision of national immigration quotas upon one country\xe2\x80\x99s surrender of territory to another, even if \xe2\x80\x9cthe transfer . . . has not been recognized by the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa712(c),\n\n43Stat.\n161 (1924). Federal law today prohibits murdering a foreign government\xe2\x80\x99s officials,\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71116, counterfeiting a foreign government\xe2\x80\x99s bonds, \xc2\xa7478, and using American vessels to smuggle goods in violation of a foreign government\xe2\x80\x99s laws, \xc2\xa7546\xe2\x80\x94all \xe2\x80\x9cirrespective of recognition by the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa711, 1116. Just as Congress may legislate independently of recognition in all of those areas, so too may it legislate independently of recognition when regulating the recording of birthplaces.

\n

The Court elsewhere objects that \xc2\xa7214(d) interferes with the autonomy and unity of the Executive Branch, setting the branch against itself. The Court suggests, for instance, that the law prevents the President from maintaining his neutrality about Jerusalem in \xe2\x80\x9chis and his agent\xe2\x80\x99s statements.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 26. That is of no constitutional significance. As just shown, Congress has power to legislate without regard to recognition, and where Congress has the power to legislate, the President has a duty to \xe2\x80\x9ctake Care\xe2\x80\x9d that its legislation \xe2\x80\x9cbe faithfully executed,\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa73. It is likewise \xe2\x80\x9cthe duty of the secretary of state to conform to the law\xe2\x80\x9d; where Congress imposes a responsibility on him, \xe2\x80\x9che is so far the officer of the law; is amenable to the laws for his conduct; and cannot at his discretion sport away the vested rights of others.\xe2\x80\x9d Marbury v. Madison, 1 Cranch 137, 158, 166 (1803). The Executive\xe2\x80\x99s involvement in carrying out this law does not affect its constitutionality; the Executive carries out every law.

\n

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s error could be made more apparent by applying its reasoning to the President\xe2\x80\x99s power \xe2\x80\x9cto make Treaties,\xe2\x80\x9d Art. II, \xc2\xa72, cl.\xc2\xa02. There is no question that Congress may, if it wishes, pass laws that openly flout treaties made by the President. Head Money Cases,\n\n112 U.\xc2\xa0S. 580,\n597 (1884)\n. Would anyone have dreamt that the President may refuse to carry out such laws\xe2\x80\x94or, to bring the point closer to home, refuse to execute federal courts\xe2\x80\x99 judgments under such laws\xe2\x80\x94so that the Executive may \xe2\x80\x9cspeak with one voice\xe2\x80\x9d about the country\xe2\x80\x99s international obligations? To ask is to answer. Today\xe2\x80\x99s holding puts the implied power to recognize territorial claims (which the Court infers from the power to recognize states, which it infers from the responsibility to receive ambassadors) on a higher footing than the express power to make treaties. Andthis, even though the Federalist describes the making of treaties as a \xe2\x80\x9cdelicate and important prerogative,\xe2\x80\x9d but the reception of ambassadors as \xe2\x80\x9cmore a matter of dignity than of authority,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ca circumstance which will be without consequence in the administration of the government.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 69, p. 420 (Hamilton).

\n

In the end, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision does not rest on text or history or precedent. It instead comes down to \xe2\x80\x9cfunctional considerations\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94principally the Court\xe2\x80\x99s perception that the Nation \xe2\x80\x9cmust speak with one voice\xe2\x80\x9d about the status of Jerusalem. Ante, at 11 (ellipsis and internal quotation marks omitted). The vices of this mode of analysis go beyond mere lack of footing in the Constitution. Functionalism of the sort the Court practices today will systematically favor the unitary President over the plural Congress in disputes involving foreign affairs. It is possible that this approach will make for more effective foreign policy, perhaps as effective as that of a monarchy. It is certain that, in the long run, it will erode the structure of separated powers that the People established for the protection of their liberty.

\n

V

\n

Justice Thomas\xe2\x80\x99s concurrence deems \xc2\xa7214(d) constitutional to the extent it regulates birth reports, but unconstitutional to the extent it regulates passports. Ante, at 10 (opinion concurring in judgment in part and dissenting in part). The concurrence finds no congressional power that would extend to the issuance or contents of passports. Including the power to regulate foreign commerce\xe2\x80\x94even though passports facilitate the transportation of passengers, \xe2\x80\x9ca part of our commerce with foreign nations,\xe2\x80\x9d Henderson v. Mayor of New York,\n\n92 U.\xc2\xa0S. 259,\n270 (1876)\n. Including the power over naturalization\xe2\x80\x94even though passports issued to citizens, like birth reports, \xe2\x80\x9chave the same force and effect as proof of United States citizenship as certificates of naturalization,\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n22 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72705. Including the power to enforce the\n \nFourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s guarantee that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ll persons born or naturalized in the United States .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. are citizens of the United States\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94even though a passport provides evidence of citizenship and so helps enforce this guarantee abroad. Including the power to exclude persons from the territory of the United States, see Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa79, cl. 1\xe2\x80\x94even though passports are the principal means of identifying citizens entitled to entry. Including the powers under which Congress has restricted the ability of various people to leave the country (fugitives from justice, for example, see\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71073)\xe2\x80\x94even though passports are the principal means of controlling exit. Including the power to \xe2\x80\x9cmake all needful Rules and Regulations respecting the Territory or other Property belonging to the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d Art. IV, \xc2\xa73, cl.\xc2\xa02\xe2\x80\x94even though \xe2\x80\x9c[a] passport remains at all times the property of the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d 7 FAM \xc2\xa71317 (2013). The concurrence\xe2\x80\x99s stingy interpretation of the enumerated powers forgets that the Constitution does not \xe2\x80\x9cpartake of the prolixity of a legal code,\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9conly its great outlines [are] marked, its important objects designated, and the minor ingredients which compose those objects [left to] be deduced from the nature of the objects themselves.\xe2\x80\x9d McCulloch, 4 Wheat., at 407. It forgets, in other words, \xe2\x80\x9cthat it is a constitution we are expounding.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

Defending Presidential primacy over passports, the concurrence says that the royal prerogative in England included the power to issue and control travel documents akin to the modern passport. Ante, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311. Perhaps so, but that power was assuredly not exclusive. The Aliens Act 1793, for example, enacted almost contemporaneously with our Constitution, required an alien traveling within England to obtain \xe2\x80\x9ca passport from [a] mayor or .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [a] justice of [the] peace,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cin which passport shall be expressed the name and rank, occupation or description, of such alien.\xe2\x80\x9d 33 Geo. III, ch. 4, \xc2\xa78, in 39 Eng. Stat. at Large 12. The Aliens Act 1798 prohibited aliens from leaving the country without \xe2\x80\x9ca passport .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. first obtained from one of his Majesty\xe2\x80\x99s principal secretaries of state,\xe2\x80\x9d and instructed customs officers to mark, sign, and date passports before allowing their bearers to depart. 38 Geo. III, ch. 50, \xc2\xa78, in 41 Eng. Stat. at Large 684. These and similar laws discredit any claim that, in the \xe2\x80\x9cAnglo-American legal tradition,\xe2\x80\x9d travel documents have \xe2\x80\x9cconsistently been issued and controlled by the body exercising executive power,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 10 (emphasis added).

\n

Returning to this side of the Atlantic, the concurrence says that passports have a \xe2\x80\x9chistorical pedigree uniquely associated with the President.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 28. This statement overlooks the reality that, until Congress restricted the issuance of passports to the State Department in 1856, \xe2\x80\x9cpassports were also issued by governors, mayors, and even .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. notaries public.\xe2\x80\x9d Assn. of the Bar of the City of New York, Special Committee to Study Passport Procedures, Freedom to Travel 6 (1958). To be sure, early Presidents granted passports without express congressional authorization. Ante, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312. But this point establishes Presidential authority over passports in the face of congressional silence, not Presidential authority in the face of congressional opposition. Early in the Republic\xe2\x80\x99s history, Congress made it a crime for a consul to \xe2\x80\x9cgrant a passport or other paper certifying that any alien, knowing him or her to be such, is a citizen of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa78,\n\n2Stat.\n205 (1803). Closer to the Civil War, Congress expressly authorized the granting of passports, regulated passport fees, and prohibited the issuance of passports to foreign citizens. \xc2\xa723,\n\n11Stat.\n60\xe2\x80\x9361 (1856). Since then, Congress has made laws about eligibility to receive passports, the duration for which passports remain valid, and even the type of paper used to manufacture passports. 22 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7212, 217a; \xc2\xa7617(b),\n\n102Stat.\n1755. (The concurrence makes no attempt to explain how these laws were supported by congressional powers other than those it rejects in the present case.) This Court has held that the President may not curtail a citizen\xe2\x80\x99s travel by withholding a passport, except on grounds approved by Congress. Kent v. Dulles,\n\n357 U.\xc2\xa0S. 116,\n129 (1958)\n. History and precedent thus refute any suggestion that the Constitution disables Congress from regulating the President\xe2\x80\x99s issuance and formulation of passports.

\n

The concurrence adds that a passport \xe2\x80\x9ccontains [a] communication directed at a foreign power.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 28. The \xe2\x80\x9ccommunication\xe2\x80\x9d in question is a message that traditionally appears in each passport (though no statute, to my knowledge, expressly requires its inclusion): \xe2\x80\x9cThe Secretary of State of the United States of America hereby requests all whom it may concern to permit the citizen/national of the United States named herein to pass without delay or hindrance and in case of need to giveall lawful aid and protection.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 22. I leave it to the reader to judge whether a request to \xe2\x80\x9call whom it may con-cern\xe2\x80\x9d qualifies as a \xe2\x80\x9ccommunication directed at a foreign power.\xe2\x80\x9d Even if it does, its presence does not affect \xc2\xa7214(d)\xe2\x80\x99s constitutionality. Requesting protection is only a \xe2\x80\x9csub-ordinate\xe2\x80\x9d function of a passport. Kent, supra, at 129. This subordinate function has never been thought to invalidate other laws regulating the contents of passports; why then would it invalidate this one?

\n

That brings me, in analytic crescendo, to the concurrence\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion that even if Congress\xe2\x80\x99s enumerated powers otherwise encompass \xc2\xa7214(d), and even if the President\xe2\x80\x99s power to regulate the contents of passports is not exclusive, the law might still violate the Constitution, because it \xe2\x80\x9cconflict[s]\xe2\x80\x9d with the President\xe2\x80\x99s passport policy. Ante, at 24. It turns the Constitution upside-down to suggest that in areas of shared authority, it is the executive policy that preempts the law, rather than the other way around. Congress may make laws necessary and proper for carrying into execution the President\xe2\x80\x99s powers, Art. I, \xc2\xa78, cl. 18, but the President must \xe2\x80\x9ctake Care\xe2\x80\x9d that Congress\xe2\x80\x99s legislation \xe2\x80\x9cbe faithfully executed,\xe2\x80\x9d Art. II, \xc2\xa73. And Acts of Congress made in pursuance of the Constitution are the \xe2\x80\x9csupreme Law of the Land\xe2\x80\x9d; acts of the President (apart from treaties) are not. Art.\xc2\xa0VI, cl.\xc2\xa02. That is why Chief Justice Marshall was right to think that a law prohibiting the seizure of foreign ships trumped a military order requiring it. Little v. Barreme, 2 Cranch 170, 178\xe2\x80\x93179 (1804). It is why Justice Jackson was right to think that a President who \xe2\x80\x9ctakes measures incompatible with the expressed or implied will of Congress\xe2\x80\x9d may \xe2\x80\x9crely only upon his own constitutional powers minus any constitutional powers of Congress over the matter.\xe2\x80\x9d Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer,\n\n343 U.\xc2\xa0S. 579,\n637 (1952)\n (concurring opinion) (emphasis added). And it is why Justice Thomas is wrong to think that even if \xc2\xa7214(d) operates in a field of shared authority the President might still prevail.

\n

Whereas the Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis threatens congressional power over foreign affairs with gradual erosion, the concurrence\xe2\x80\x99s approach shatters it in one stroke. The combination of (a) the concurrence\xe2\x80\x99s assertion of broad, unenumerated \xe2\x80\x9cresidual powers\xe2\x80\x9d in the President, see ante, at2\xe2\x80\x939; (b) its parsimonious interpretation of Congress\xe2\x80\x99s enu-merated powers, see ante, at 13\xe2\x80\x9317; and (c) its even more parsimonious interpretation of Congress\xe2\x80\x99s authority to enact laws \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary and proper for carrying into Execution\xe2\x80\x9d the President\xe2\x80\x99s executive powers, see ante, at 17\xe2\x80\x9320; produces (d) a presidency more reminiscent of George III than George Washington.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

International disputes about statehood and territory are neither rare nor obscure. Leading foreign debates during the 19th century concerned how the United States should respond to revolutions in Latin America, Texas, Mexico, Hawaii, Cuba. During the 20th century, attitudes toward Communist governments in Russia and China became conspicuous subjects of agitation. Disagreements about Taiwan, Kashmir, and Crimea remain prominent today. A President empowered to decide all questions relating to these matters, immune from laws embodying congres-sional disagreement with his position, would have un-controlled mastery of a vast share of the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s foreign affairs.

\n

That is not the chief magistrate under which the American People agreed to live when they adopted the national charter. They believed that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he accumulation of all powers, legislative, executive, and judiciary, in the same hands, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. may justly be pronounced the very definition of tyranny.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 47, p. 301 (Madison). For this reason, they did not entrust either the President or Congress with sole power to adopt uncontradictable policies about any subject\xe2\x80\x94foreign-sovereignty disputes included. They instead gave each political department its own powers, and with that the freedom to contradict the other\xe2\x80\x99s policies. Under the Constitution they approved, Congress may require Zivotofsky\xe2\x80\x99s passport and birth report to record his birthplace as Israel, even if that requirement clashes with the President\xe2\x80\x99s preference for neutrality about the status of Jerusalem.

\n

I dissent.

[June 8, 2015]

\n

\n

Chief Justice Roberts, with whom Justice Alito joins, dissenting.

\n

Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision is a first: Never before has this Court accepted a President\xe2\x80\x99s direct defiance of an Act of Congress in the field of foreign affairs. We have instead stressed that the President\xe2\x80\x99s power reaches \xe2\x80\x9cits lowest ebb\xe2\x80\x9d when he contravenes the express will of Congress, \xe2\x80\x9cfor what is at stake is the equilibrium established by our constitutional system.\xe2\x80\x9d Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer,\n\n343 U.\xc2\xa0S. 579\n\xe2\x80\x93638 (1952) (Jackson, J.,concurring).

\n

Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s principal dissent, which I join in full, refutes the majority\xe2\x80\x99s unprecedented holding in detail. I write separately to underscore the stark nature of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s error on a basic question of separation of powers.

\n

The first principles in this area are firmly established. The Constitution allocates some foreign policy powers to the Executive, grants some to the Legislature, and enjoins the President to \xe2\x80\x9ctake Care that the Laws be faithfully executed.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa73. The Executive may disregard \xe2\x80\x9cthe expressed or implied will of Congress\xe2\x80\x9d only if the Constitution grants him a power \xe2\x80\x9cat once so conclusive and preclusive\xe2\x80\x9d as to \xe2\x80\x9cdisabl[e] the Congress from acting upon the subject.\xe2\x80\x9d Youngstown, 343 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 637\xe2\x80\x93638 (Jackson, J., concurring).

\n

Assertions of exclusive and preclusive power leave the Executive \xe2\x80\x9cin the least favorable of possible constitutional postures,\xe2\x80\x9d and such claims have been \xe2\x80\x9cscrutinized with caution\xe2\x80\x9d throughout this Court\xe2\x80\x99s history. Id., at 640, 638; see Dames & Moore v. Regan,\n\n453 U.\xc2\xa0S. 654\n\xe2\x80\x93669 (1981). For our first 225 years, no President prevailed when contradicting a statute in the field of foreign affairs. See Medell\xc3\xadn v. Texas,\n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 491\n\xe2\x80\x93532 (2008); Hamdan v. Rumsfeld,\n\n548 U.\xc2\xa0S. 557\n\xe2\x80\x93595, 613\xe2\x80\x93625 (2006); Youngstown, 343 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 587\xe2\x80\x93589 (majority opinion); Little v. Barreme, 2 Cranch 170, 177\xe2\x80\x93179 (1804).

\n

In this case, the President claims the exclusive and preclusive power to recognize foreign sovereigns. The Court devotes much of its analysis to accepting the Executive\xe2\x80\x99s contention. Ante, at 6\xe2\x80\x9326. I have serious doubts about that position. The majority places great weight on the Reception Clause, which directs that the Executive \xe2\x80\x9cshall receive Ambassadors and other public Ministers.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa73. But that provision, framed as an obligation rather than an authorization, appears alongside the duties imposed on the President by Article II, Section 3, not the powers granted to him by Article II, Section 2. Indeed, the People ratified the Constitution with Alexander Hamilton\xe2\x80\x99s assurance that executive reception of ambassadors \xe2\x80\x9cis more a matter of dignity than of authority\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cwill be without consequence in the administration of the government.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 69, p. 420 (C. Rossiter ed. 1961). In short, at the time of the founding, \xe2\x80\x9cthere was no reason to view the reception clause as a source of discretionary authority for the president.\xe2\x80\x9d Adler, The President\xe2\x80\x99s Recognition Power: Ministerial or Discretionary? 25 Presidential Studies Q. 267, 269 (1995).

\n

The majority\xe2\x80\x99s other asserted textual bases are even more tenuous. The President does have power to make treaties and appoint ambassadors. Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa72. Butthose authorities are shared with Congress, ibid., so they hardly support an inference that the recognition power is exclusive.

\n

Precedent and history lend no more weight to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s position. The majority cites dicta suggesting an exclusive executive recognition power, but acknowledges contrary dicta suggesting that the power is shared. See, e.g., United States v. Palmer, 3 Wheat. 610, 643 (1818) (\xe2\x80\x9cthe courts of the union must view [a] newly constituted government as it is viewed by the legislative and executive departments of the government of the United States\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). When the best you can muster is conflicting dicta, precedent can hardly be said to support your side.

\n

As for history, the majority admits that it too points in both directions. Some Presidents have claimed an exclusive recognition power, but others have expressed uncertainty about whether such preclusive authority exists. Those in the skeptical camp include Andrew Jackson and Abraham Lincoln, leaders not generally known for their cramped conceptions of Presidential power. Congress has also asserted its authority over recognition determinations at numerous points in history. The majority therefore falls short of demonstrating that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress has accepted\xe2\x80\x9d the President\xe2\x80\x99s exclusive recognition power. Ante, at 26. In any event, we have held that congressional acquiescence is only \xe2\x80\x9cpertinent\xe2\x80\x9d when the President acts in the absence of express congressional authorization, not when he asserts power to disregard a statute, as the Executive does here. Medell\xc3\xadn, 552 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 528; see Dames & Moore, 453 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 678\xe2\x80\x93679.

\n

In sum, although the President has authority over recognition, I am not convinced that the Constitution provides the \xe2\x80\x9cconclusive and preclusive\xe2\x80\x9d power required to justify defiance of an express legislative mandate. Youngstown, 343 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 638 (Jackson, J., concurring). As the leading scholar on this issue has concluded, the \xe2\x80\x9ctext, original understanding, post-ratification history, and structure of the Constitution do not support the .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. expansive claim that this executive power is plenary.\xe2\x80\x9d Reinstein, Is the President\xe2\x80\x99s Recognition Power Exclusive? 86 Temp. L. Rev. 1, 60 (2013).

\n

But even if the President does have exclusive recognition power, he still cannot prevail in this case, because the statute at issue does not implicate recognition. See Zivotofsky v. Clinton, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (Alito, J., concurring in judgment) (slip op., at 1); post, at 5\xe2\x80\x9310 (Scalia, J., dissenting). The relevant provision, \xc2\xa7214(d), simply gives an American citizen born in Jerusalem the option to designate his place of birth as Israel \xe2\x80\x9c[f\xc2\xa0]or purposes of\xe2\x80\x9d passports and other documents. Foreign Relations Authorization Act, Fiscal Year 2003,\n\n116Stat.\n1366. The State Department itself has explained that \xe2\x80\x9cidentification\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94not recognition\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cis the principal reason that U.\xc2\xa0S. passports require \xe2\x80\x98place of birth.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d App. 42. Congress has not disputed the Executive\xe2\x80\x99s assurances that \xc2\xa7214(d) does not alter the longstanding United States position on Jerusalem. And the annals of diplomatic history record no examples of official recognition accomplished via optional passport designation.

\n

The majority acknowledges both that the \xe2\x80\x9cExecutive\xe2\x80\x99s exclusive power extends no further than his formal recognition determination\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xc2\xa7214(d) does \xe2\x80\x9cnot itself constitute a formal act of recognition.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 27. Taken together, these statements come close to a confession of error. The majority attempts to reconcile its position by reconceiving \xc2\xa7214(d) as a \xe2\x80\x9cmandate that the Executive contradict his prior recognition determination in an official document issued by the Secretary of State.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 27. But as just noted, neither Congress nor the Executive Branch regards \xc2\xa7214(d) as a recognition determination, so it is hard to see how the statute could contradict any such determination.

\n

At most, the majority worries that there may be a perceived contradiction based on a mistaken understanding of the effect of \xc2\xa7214(d), insisting that some \xe2\x80\x9cobservers interpreted \xc2\xa7214 as altering United States policy regarding Jerusalem.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 28. To afford controlling weight to such impressions, however, is essentially to subject a duly enacted statute to an international heckler\xe2\x80\x99s veto.

\n

Moreover, expanding the President\xe2\x80\x99s purportedly exclusive recognition power to include authority to avoid potential misunderstandings of legislative enactments proves far too much. Congress could validly exercise its enumerated powers in countless ways that would create more severe perceived contradictions with Presidential recognition decisions than does \xc2\xa7214(d). If, for example, the President recognized a particular country in opposition to Congress\xe2\x80\x99s wishes, Congress could declare war or impose a trade embargo on that country. A neutral observer might well conclude that these legislative actions had, to put it mildly, created a perceived contradiction with the President\xe2\x80\x99s recognition decision. And yet each of them would undoubtedly be constitutional. See ante, at 27. So too would statements by nonlegislative actors that might be seen to contradict the President\xe2\x80\x99s recognition positions, such as the declaration in a political party platform that \xe2\x80\x9cJerusalem is and will remain the capital of Israel.\xe2\x80\x9d Landler, Pushed by Obama, Democrats Alter Platform Over Jerusalem, N.\xc2\xa0Y. Times, Sept. 6, 2012, p. A14.

\n

Ultimately, the only power that could support the President\xe2\x80\x99s position is the one the majority purports to reject: the \xe2\x80\x9cexclusive authority to conduct diplomatic relations.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 18. The Government offers a single citation for this allegedly exclusive power: United States v. Curtiss-Wright Export Corp.,\n\n299 U.\xc2\xa0S. 304\n\xe2\x80\x93320 (1936). But as the majority rightly acknowledges, Curtiss-Wright did not involve a claim that the Executive could contravene a statute; it held only that he could act pursuant to a legislative delegation. Ante, at 17.

\n

The expansive language in Curtiss-Wright casting the President as the \xe2\x80\x9csole organ\xe2\x80\x9d of the Nation in foreign affairs certainly has attraction for members of the Executive Branch. The Solicitor General invokes the case no fewer than ten times in his brief. Brief for Respondent 9, 10, 18, 19, 23, 24, 53, 54. But our precedents have never accepted such a sweeping understanding of executive power. See Hamdan, 548 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 591\xe2\x80\x93592; Dames & Moore, 453 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 661\xe2\x80\x93662; Youngstown, 343 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 587 (majority opinion); id., at 635, n.\xc2\xa02 (Jackson, J., concurring); cf. Little, 2 Cranch, at 179 (Marshall, C.\xc2\xa0J.) (\xe2\x80\x9cI confess the first bias of my mind was very strong in favour of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the executive .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [b]ut I have been convinced that I was mistaken.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Just a few Terms ago, this Court rejected the President\xe2\x80\x99s argument that a broad foreign relations power allowed him to override a state court decision that contradicted U.\xc2\xa0S. international law obligations. Medell\xc3\xadn, 552 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 523\xe2\x80\x93532. If the President\xe2\x80\x99s so-called general foreign relations authority does not permit him to countermand a State\xe2\x80\x99s lawful action, it surely does not authorize him to disregard an express statutory directive enacted by Congress, which\xe2\x80\x94unlike the States\xe2\x80\x94has extensive foreign relations powers of its own. Unfortunately, despite its protest to the contrary, the majority today allows the Executive to do just that.

\n

Resolving the status of Jerusalem may be vexing, but resolving this case is not. Whatever recognition power the President may have, exclusive or otherwise, is not implicated by \xc2\xa7214(d). It has not been necessary over the past 225 years to definitively resolve a dispute between Congress and the President over the recognition power. Perhaps we could have waited another 225 years. But instead the majority strains to reach the question based on the mere possibility that observers overseas might misperceive the significance of the birthplace designation at issue in this case. And in the process, the Court takes the perilous step\xe2\x80\x94for the first time in our history\xe2\x80\x94of allowing the President to defy an Act of Congress in the field of foreign affairs.

\n

I respectfully dissent.

Justice Thomas, concurring in the judgment in part and dissenting in part.

\n

Our Constitution allocates the powers of the Federal Government over foreign affairs in two ways. First, it expressly identifies certain foreign affairs powers and vests them in particular branches, either individually or jointly. Second, it vests the residual foreign affairs powers of the Federal Government\xe2\x80\x94i.e., those not specifically enumerated in the Constitution\xe2\x80\x94in the President by way of Article II\xe2\x80\x99s Vesting Clause.

\n

Section 214(d) of the Foreign Relations Authorization Act, Fiscal Year 2003, ignores that constitutional allocation of power insofar as it directs the President, contrary to his wishes, to list \xe2\x80\x9cIsrael\xe2\x80\x9d as the place of birth of Jerusalem-born citizens on their passports. The President haslong regulated passports under his residual foreign affairs power, and this portion of \xc2\xa7214(d) does not fall within any of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 enumerated powers.

\n

By contrast, \xc2\xa7214(d) poses no such problem insofar as it regulates consular reports of birth abroad. Unlike passports, these reports were developed to effectuate the naturalization laws, and they continue to serve the role of identifying persons who need not be naturalized to obtain U.\xc2\xa0S. citizenship. The regulation of these reports does not fall within the President\xe2\x80\x99s foreign affairs powers, but within Congress\xe2\x80\x99 enumerated powers under the Naturalization and Necessary and Proper Clauses.

\n

Rather than adhere to the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s division of powers, the Court relies on a distortion of the President\xe2\x80\x99s recognition power to hold both of these parts of \xc2\xa7214(d) unconstitutional. Because I cannot join this faulty analysis, I concur only in the portion of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment holding \xc2\xa7214(d) unconstitutional as applied to passports. I respectfully dissent from the remainder of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

The Constitution specifies a number of foreign affairs powers and divides them between the political branches. Among others, Article I allocates to Congress the powers \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o regulate Commerce with foreign Nations,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o establish an uniform Rule of Naturalization,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o define and punish Piracies and Felonies committed on the high Seas, and Offenses against the Law of Nations,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o declare War, grant Letters of Marque and Reprisal, and make Rules concerning Captures on Land and Water.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa78. For his part, the President has certain express powers relating to foreign affairs, including the powers, \xe2\x80\x9cby and with the Advice and Consent of the Senate,\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9cappoint Ambassadors,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cto make Treaties, provided two thirds of the Senators present concur.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa72. He is also assigned certain duties with respect to foreign affairs, including serving as \xe2\x80\x9cCommander in Chief ofthe Army and Navy of the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., and\xe2\x80\x9creceiv[ing] Ambassadors and other public Ministers,\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa73.

\n

These specific allocations, however, cannot account for the entirety of the foreign affairs powers exercised by the Federal Government. Neither of the political branches is expressly authorized, for instance, to communicate with foreign ministers, to issue passports, or to repel sudden attacks. Yet the President has engaged in such conduct, with the support of Congress, since the earliest days of the Republic. Prakash & Ramsey, The Executive Power Over Foreign Affairs, 111 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J. 231, 298\xe2\x80\x93346 (2001) (Prakash & Ramsey).

\n

The President\xe2\x80\x99s longstanding practice of exercising unenumerated foreign affairs powers reflects a constitutional directive that \xe2\x80\x9cthe President ha[s] primary responsibility\xe2\x80\x94along with the necessary power\xe2\x80\x94to protect the national security and to conduct the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s foreign relations.\xe2\x80\x9d Hamdi v. Rumsfeld,\n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 507,\n580 (2004)\n (Thomas, J., dissenting). Specifically, the Vesting Clause of Article II provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he executive Power shall be vested in a President of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa71. This Clause is notably different from the Vesting Clause of Article I, which provides only that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ll legislative Powers herein granted shall be vested in a Congress of the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa71 (emphasis added). By omitting the words \xe2\x80\x9cherein granted\xe2\x80\x9d in Article II, the Constitution indicates that the \xe2\x80\x9cexecutive Power\xe2\x80\x9d vested in the President is not confined to those powers expressly identified in the document. Instead, it includes all powers originally understood as falling within the \xe2\x80\x9cexecutive Power\xe2\x80\x9d of the Federal Government.

\n

B

\n

Founding-era evidence reveals that the \xe2\x80\x9cexecutivePower\xe2\x80\x9d included the foreign affairs powers of a sovereign State. See Prakash & Ramsey 253. John Locke\xe2\x80\x99s 17th-century writings laid the groundwork for this understanding of executive power. Locke described foreign affairs powers\xe2\x80\x94including the powers of \xe2\x80\x9cwar and peace, leagues and alliances, and all the transactions with all persons and communities without the commonwealth\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94as \xe2\x80\x9cfederative\xe2\x80\x9d power. Second Treatise of Civil Government \xc2\xa7146,p. 73 (J. Gough ed. 1947). He defined the \xe2\x80\x9cexecutive\xe2\x80\x9d power as \xe2\x80\x9ccomprehending the execution of the municipal laws of the society within itself upon all that are parts of it.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., \xc2\xa7147, at 73. Importantly, however, Locke explained that the federative and executive powers must be lodged together, lest \xe2\x80\x9cdisorder and ruin\xe2\x80\x9d erupt from the division of the \xe2\x80\x9cforce of the public.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., \xc2\xa7148, at 73\xe2\x80\x9374.

\n

Subsequent thinkers began to refer to both of these powers as aspects of \xe2\x80\x9cexecutive power.\xe2\x80\x9d William Blackstone, for example, described the executive power in England as including foreign affairs powers, such as the\xe2\x80\x9cpower of sending embassadors to foreign states, and receiv-ing embassadors at home\xe2\x80\x9d; making \xe2\x80\x9ctreaties, leagues, and alliances with foreign states and princes\xe2\x80\x9d; \xe2\x80\x9cmaking war and peace\xe2\x80\x9d; and \xe2\x80\x9cissu[ing] letters of marque and reprisal.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 Commentaries on the Laws of England 245, 249, 250, 242\xe2\x80\x93252 (1765) (Blackstone). Baron de Montesquieu similarly described executive power as including the power to \xe2\x80\x9cmak[e] peace or war, sen[d] or receiv[e] embassies, establis[h] the public security, and provid[e] against invasions.\xe2\x80\x9d The Spirit of the Laws bk. XI, ch. 6, p. 151 (O. Piest ed., T. Nugent transl. 1949). In fact, \xe2\x80\x9cmost writers of [Montesquieu\xe2\x80\x99s] tim[e] w[ere] inclined to think of the executive branch of government as being concerned nearly entirely with foreign affairs.\xe2\x80\x9d W. Gwyn, The Meaning of the Separation of Powers 103 (1965).

\n

That understanding of executive power prevailed in America. Following independence, Congress assumed control over foreign affairs under the Articles of Confederation. See, e.g., Articles of Confederation, Art. IX, cl. 1. At that time, many understood that control to be an exercise of executive power. See Prakash & Ramsey 272, 275\xe2\x80\x93278. Letters among Members of the Continental Congress, for instance, repeatedly referred to the Department of Foreign Affairs, established under the control of the Continental Congress, as an \xe2\x80\x9cExecutive departmen[t]\xe2\x80\x9d and to its officers as \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Executives or Ministers.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 276, and nn. 194\xe2\x80\x93196. Similarly, the Essex Result of 1778\xe2\x80\x94an influential report on the proposed Constitution for Massachusetts\xe2\x80\x94described executive power as including both \xe2\x80\x9cexternal\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cinternal\xe2\x80\x9d powers: The external executive power \xe2\x80\x9ccomprehends war, peace, the sending and receiving ambassadors, and whatever concerns the transactions of the state with any other independent state,\xe2\x80\x9d while the internal executive power \xe2\x80\x9cis employed in the peace, security and protection of the subject and his property.\xe2\x80\x9d Essex Result, in The Popular Sources of Political Authority: Documents on the Massachusetts Constitution of 1780, pp. 324, 337 (O. Handlin & M. Handlin eds. 1966).

\n

This view of executive power was widespread at the time of the framing of the Constitution. Thomas Rutherforth\xe2\x80\x99s Institutes of Natural Law\xe2\x80\x94a treatise routinely cited by the Founders, McDowell, The Limits of Natural Law: Thomas Rutherforth and the American Legal Tradition, 37 Am. J. Juris. 57, 59, and n. 10 (1992)\xe2\x80\x94explained that \xe2\x80\x9cexternal executive power\xe2\x80\x9d includes \xe2\x80\x9cnot only what is properly called military power, but the power likewise of making war or peace, the power of engaging in alliances for an encrease of strength, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the power of entering into treaties, and of making leagues to restore peace .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and the power of adjusting the rights of a nation in respect of navigation, trade, etc.,\xe2\x80\x9d 2 Institutes of Natural Law 55\xe2\x80\x9356, 54\xe2\x80\x9361 (1756). During the ratification debates, James Wilson likewise referred to the \xe2\x80\x9cexecutive powers of government\xe2\x80\x9d as including the external powers of a nation. 2 J. Elliot, The Debates in the Several State Conventions on the Adoption of the Federal Constitution 500\xe2\x80\x93502 (1863). And Alexander Hamilton, writing as Publius, asserted that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he actual conduct of foreign negotiations,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cthe arrangement of the army and navy, the directions of the operations of war .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and other matters of a like nature\xe2\x80\x9d are \xe2\x80\x9cexecutive details\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cfal[l] peculiarly within the province of the executive department.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 72, pp. 435\xe2\x80\x93436 (C. Rossiter ed. 1961).

\n

Given this pervasive view of executive power, it is unsurprising that those who ratified the Constitution understood the \xe2\x80\x9cexecutive Power\xe2\x80\x9d vested by Article II to include those foreign affairs powers not otherwise allocated in the Constitution. James Iredell, for example, told the North Carolina ratifying convention that, under the new Constitution, the President would \xe2\x80\x9cregulate all intercourse with foreign powers\xe2\x80\x9d and act as the \xe2\x80\x9cprimary agent\xe2\x80\x9d of the United States, though no specific allocation of foreign affairs powers in the document so provided. 4 Elliot, supra, at 127, 128. And Alexander Hamilton presumed as much when he argued that the \xe2\x80\x9c[e]nergy\xe2\x80\x9d created in the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s Executive would be \xe2\x80\x9cessential to the protection of the community against foreign attacks,\xe2\x80\x9d even though no specific allocation of foreign affairs powers provided for the Executive to repel such assaults. See The Federalist No. 70, p. 423. These statements confirm that the \xe2\x80\x9cexecutive Power\xe2\x80\x9d vested in the President by Article II includes the residual foreign affairs powers of the Federal Government not otherwise allocated by the Constitution.[1]

\n

C

\n

Early practice of the founding generation also supports this understanding of the \xe2\x80\x9cexecutive Power.\xe2\x80\x9d Upon taking office, President Washington assumed the role of chief diplomat; began to direct the Secretary of Foreign Affairs who, under the Articles of Confederation, had reported to the Congress; and established the foreign policy of the United States. Prakash & Ramsey 296\xe2\x80\x93297. At the same time, he respected Congress\xe2\x80\x99 prerogatives to declare war, regulate foreign commerce, and appropriate funds. Id.,at 296.

\n

For its part, Congress recognized a broad Presidential role in foreign affairs. Id., at 297\xe2\x80\x93298. It created an \xe2\x80\x9cExecutive department\xe2\x80\x9d called the \xe2\x80\x9cDepartment of Foreign Affairs,\xe2\x80\x9d with a Secretary wholly subordinate to the President. An Act for Establishing an Executive Department, to be denominated the Department of Foreign Affairs,\n\n1Stat.\n28. The enabling Act provided that the Secretary was to \xe2\x80\x9cperform and execute such duties as shall from time to time be enjoined on or intrusted to him by the President,\xe2\x80\x9d including those \xe2\x80\x9crelative to correspondences, commissions or instructions to or with public ministers or consuls, from the United States, or to negotiations with public ministers from foreign states or princes, or to memorials or other applications from foreign public ministers or other foreigners, or to such other matters respecting foreign affairs.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71, id., at 29. By referring to those duties as those \xe2\x80\x9cthe President of the United States shall assign to the said department,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., the Act presumed the President inherently possessed power to engage in those tasks.

\n

Subsequent interactions between President Washington and Congress indicated that the parties involved believed the Constitution vested the President with authority to regulate dealings with foreign nations. In his first State of the Union Address, President Washington told Congress that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he interests of the United States require, that our intercourse with other nations should be facilitated by such provisions as will enable me to fulfil my duty in that respect.\xe2\x80\x9d First Annual Message (Jan. 8, 1790), in George Washington: A Collection 467, 468 (W. Allen ed. 1988). To that end, he asked for compensation for employees and a fund designated for \xe2\x80\x9cdefraying the expenses incident to the conduct of our foreign affairs.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Congress responded by passing \xe2\x80\x9cAn Act providing the means of intercourse between the United States and foreign nations.\xe2\x80\x9d Ch. 22,\n\n1Stat.\n128.

\n

During the congressional debate over that bill, the President sought an opinion from Thomas Jefferson\xe2\x80\x94at that time, Secretary of State\xe2\x80\x94about the scope of the Senate\xe2\x80\x99s power in this area. Jefferson responded that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he transaction of business with foreign nations is executive altogether.\xe2\x80\x9d Opinion on the Powers of the Senate (Apr. 24, 1790), in 5 Writings of Thomas Jefferson 161 (P. Ford ed. 1895). As such, Jefferson concluded that it properly belonged \xe2\x80\x9cto the head\xe2\x80\x9d of the executive department, \xe2\x80\x9cexcept as to such portions of it as are specially submitted to the senate.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. According to Washington\xe2\x80\x99s diaries, he received similar advice from John Jay and James Madison about \xe2\x80\x9cthe propriety of consulting the Senate on the places to which it would be necessary to send persons in the Diplomatic line, and Consuls.\xe2\x80\x9d 6 The Diaries of George Washington 68 (D. Jackson & D. Twohig eds. 1979). All agreed that the Senate lacked a \xe2\x80\x9cConstitutional right to interfere with either, & that it might be impolitic to draw it into a precedent their powers extending no farther than to an approbation or disapprobation of the person nominated by the President all the rest being Executive and vested in the President by the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

Washington followed this advice. He corresponded directly with U.\xc2\xa0S. ministers, moved them among countries, and removed them from their positions at will. Prakash & Ramsey 308\xe2\x80\x93309. He also corresponded with foreign leaders, representing that his role as the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98supreme executive authority\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d authorized him to receive and respond to their letters on behalf of the United States. Id., at 317. When foreign ministers addressed their communications to Congress, he informed them of their error. Id., at 321.

\n

Washington\xe2\x80\x99s control over foreign affairs extended beyond communications with other governments. When confronted with the question whether to recognize the French Republic as the lawful government of France, he received the French Republic\xe2\x80\x99s emissary without the involvement of Congress. Id., at 312. When he later concluded that the emissary had acted inappropriately, he again acted without the involvement of Congress to ask the French executive to recall him. Id., at 314\xe2\x80\x93315. Washington also declared neutrality on behalf of the United States during the war between England and France in 1793, see Proclamation of Neutrality (Apr. 22, 1793), an action Hamilton pseudonymously defended as a proper exercise of the power vested in the President by the \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral grant\xe2\x80\x9d of executive power in the Vesting Clause. Pacificus No. 1 (June 29, 1793), Letters of Pacificus and Helvidius 10 (1845); id., at 3. For its part, Congress applauded the President\xe2\x80\x99s decision. 4 Annals of Cong. 18, 138 (1793).

\n

In short, the practices of the Washington administration and First Congress confirm that Article II\xe2\x80\x99s Vesting Clause was originally understood to include a grant of residual foreign affairs power to the Executive.

\n

II

\n

The statutory provision at issue implicates the President\xe2\x80\x99s residual foreign affairs power. Section 214(d) instructs the Secretary of State, upon request of a citizen born in Jerusalem (or that citizen\xe2\x80\x99s legal guardian), to list that citizen\xe2\x80\x99s place of birth as Israel on his passport and consular report of birth abroad, even though it is the undisputed position of the United States that Jerusalem is not a part of Israel. The President argues that this provision violates his foreign affairs powers generally and his recognition power specifically. Zivotofsky rejoins that Congress passed \xc2\xa7214(d) pursuant to its enumerated powers and its action must therefore take precedence.

\n

Neither has it quite right. The President is not constitutionally compelled to implement \xc2\xa7214(d) as it appliesto passports because passport regulation falls squarely within his residual foreign affairs power and Zivotofsky has identified no source of congressional power to require the President to list Israel as the place of birth for a citizen born in Jerusalem on that citizen\xe2\x80\x99s passport. Section 214(d) can, however, be constitutionally applied to con-sular reports of birth abroad because those documentsdo not fall within the President\xe2\x80\x99s foreign affairs authority but do fall within Congress\xe2\x80\x99 enumerated powers over naturalization.[2]

\n

A

\n

1

\n

In the Anglo-American legal tradition, passports have consistently been issued and controlled by the body exercising executive power\xe2\x80\x94in England, by the King; in the colonies, by the Continental Congress; and in the United States, by President Washington and every President since.

\n

Historically, \xe2\x80\x9cpassports were classed with those documents known as safe conducts or letters of protection, by which the person of an enemy might be rendered safe and inviolable.\xe2\x80\x9d G. Hunt, U.\xc2\xa0S. Dept. of State, The American Passport: Its History 3 (1898). Letters of safe conduct and passports performed different functions in England, but both grew out of the King\xe2\x80\x99s prerogative to regulate the \xe2\x80\x9cnation\xe2\x80\x99s intercourse with foreign nations,\xe2\x80\x9d see 1 Blackstone 251\xe2\x80\x93253. The King issued letters of safe conduct during times of war, id., at 252, whereas passports were heirs to a tradition of requiring the King\xe2\x80\x99s license to depart the country, see, e.g., Richard II, Feb. 26, 1383, 2 Calendar of Close Rolls, pp. 281\xe2\x80\x93282 (1920); 1 E. Turner, The Privy Council of England in the Seventeenth and Eighteenth Centuries 1603\xe2\x80\x931784, p. 151 (1927); see also K. Diplock, Passports and Protection in International Law, in 32 The Grotius Society, Transactions for the Year 1946, Problems of Public and Private International Law 42, 44 (1947).

\n

Both safe conducts and passports were in use at the time of the founding. Passports were given \xe2\x80\x9cfor greater security\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9con ordinary occasions [to] persons who meet with no special interference in going and coming,\xe2\x80\x9d whereas \xe2\x80\x9csafe-conduct[s]\xe2\x80\x9d were \xe2\x80\x9cgiven to persons who could not otherwise enter with safety the dominions of the sovereign granting it.\xe2\x80\x9d 3 E. de Vattel, The Law of Nations \xc2\xa7265, p. 331 (1758 ed. C. Fenwick transl. 1916) (emphasis deleted). Both were issued by the person exercising the external sovereign power of a state. See id., \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7162, 275, at 69, 332. In the absence of a separate executive branch of government, the Continental Congress issued passports during the American Revolution, see, e.g., Resolution (May 9, 1776), in 4 Journals of the Continental Congress 340\xe2\x80\x93341; Resolution (May 24, 1776), in id., at 385; as did the Congress under the Articles of Confederation, see, e.g., 25 id., at 859 (Jan. 24, 1783) (discussing its authority to issue passports under the war power).

\n

After the ratification of the Constitution, President Washington immediately took responsibility for issuing passports. Hunt, supra, at 3. Although \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[p]ast practice does not, by itself, create power,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ca governmental practice [that] has been open, widespread, and unchallenged since the early days of the Republic .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. should guide our interpretation of an ambiguous constitutional provision.\xe2\x80\x9d NLRB v. Noel Canning, 573 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment) (slip op., at 5) (alteration in original; some internal quotation marks omitted). The history of the President\xe2\x80\x99s passport regulation in this country is one such practice. From the ratification until the end of the Civil War, the President issued passports without any authorization from Congress. As the Department of State later remarked, \xe2\x80\x9cIn the absence of any law upon the subject, the issuing of passports to Americans going abroad naturally fell to the Department of State, as one of its manifestly proper functions.\xe2\x80\x9d Hunt, supra, at 37. To that end, the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s authority was \xe2\x80\x9centirely discretionary.\xe2\x80\x9d Urtetiqui v. D\xe2\x80\x99Arcy, 9 Pet. 692, 699 (1835). Congress acted in support of that authority by criminalizing the \xe2\x80\x9cviolat[ion] [of] any safe-conduct or passport duly obtained and issued under the authority of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d An Act for the Punishment of certain Crimes against the United States, \xc2\xa728,\n\n1Stat.\n118.[3] Congress only purported to authorize the President to issue such passports in 1856 and, even under that statute, it provided that passports should be issued \xe2\x80\x9cunder such rules as the President shall designate and prescribe for and on behalf of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d An Act to regulate the Diplomatic and Consular Systems of the United States, \xc2\xa723,\n\n11Stat.\n60. The President has continued to designate and prescribe the rules for passports ever since.

\n

2

\n

That the President has the power to regulate passports under his residual foreign affairs powers does not, however, end the matter, for Congress has repeatedly legislatedon the subject of passports. These laws have always been narrow in scope. For example, Congress enacted laws prohibiting the issuance of passports to noncitizens, id., at 61, created an exception to that rule for \xe2\x80\x9cpersons liable to military duty,\xe2\x80\x9d Act of Mar. 3, 1863, \xc2\xa723,\n\n12Stat.\n754, and then eliminated that exception, Act of May 30, 1866, ch. 102,\n\n14Stat.\n54. It passed laws regulating the fees that the State Department should impose for issuance of the passports. Act of May 16, 1932, ch. 187,\n\n47Stat.\n157; Act of June 4, 1920, \xc2\xa71,\n\n41Stat.\n750; Act of June 15, 1917, ch. 30, Title IX, \xc2\xa71,\n\n40Stat.\n227; Act of Aug. 18, 1856, \xc2\xa723,\n\n11Stat.\n60; Act of Mar. 1, 1855, \xc2\xa712,\n\n10Stat.\n624. It also enacted legislation addressing the duration for which passports may remain valid. \xc2\xa7116,\n\n96Stat.\n279; Pub. L. 90\xe2\x80\x93428,\n\n82Stat.\n446; Pub. L. 86\xe2\x80\x93267,\n\n73Stat.\n552; Act of July 3, 1926,\n\n44Stat.\n887. And it passed laws imposing criminal penalties for false statements made when applying for passports, along with misuse of passports and counterfeiting or forgery of them. Act of June 25, 1948,\n\n62Stat.\n771; Act of Mar. 28, 1940, \xc2\xa77,\n\n54Stat.\n80;\n\n40Stat.\n227.[4]

\n

As with any congressional action, however, such legislation is constitutionally permissible only insofar as it is promulgated pursuant to one of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 enumerated powers. I must therefore address whether Congress had constitutional authority to enact \xc2\xa7214(d)\xe2\x80\x99s regulation of passports.

\n

a

\n

Zivotofsky and congressional amici identify three potential sources of congressional power to enact the portion of \xc2\xa7214(d) dealing with passports. Zivotofsky first argues that it falls within Congress\xe2\x80\x99 power \xe2\x80\x9cto regulate the issuance and content of United States passports.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 17. The U.\xc2\xa0S. Senate, as amicus curiae, likewise contends that it can be justified under Congress\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cplenary authority over passports,\xe2\x80\x9d which it derives from the penumbras of its powers \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[t]o regulate Commerce with foreign Nations\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[t]o establish an uniform Rule of Naturalization.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States Senate 3 (quoting U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Art. I, \xc2\xa78, cls. 3, 4). None of these arguments withstands scrutiny.

\n

The Constitution contains no Passport Clause, nor does it explicitly vest Congress with \xe2\x80\x9cplenary authority over passports.\xe2\x80\x9d Because our Government is one of enumerated powers, \xe2\x80\x9cCongress has no power to act unless the Constitution authorizes it to do so.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Comstock,\n\n560 U.\xc2\xa0S. 126,\n159 (2010)\n (Thomas, J., dissenting). And \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Constitution plainly sets forth the \xe2\x80\x98few and defined\xe2\x80\x99 powers that Congress may exercise.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. A \xe2\x80\x9cpassport power\xe2\x80\x9d is not one of them.

\n

Section 214(d)\xe2\x80\x99s passport directive fares no better under those powers actually included in Article I. To start, it does not fall within the power \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o regulate Commerce with foreign Nations.\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cAt the time the original Constitution was ratified, \xe2\x80\x98commerce\xe2\x80\x99 consisted of selling, buying, and bartering, as well as transporting for these purposes.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Lopez,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 549,\n585 (1995)\n (Thomas, J., concurring). The listing of the place of birth of an applicant\xe2\x80\x94whether born in Jerusalem or not\xe2\x80\x94does not involve selling, buying, bartering, or transporting for those purposes. Cf. United States v. Morrison,\n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 598,\n613 (2000)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[O]ur cases have upheld Commerce Clause regulation of intrastate activity [under the power to regulate commerce among the several States] only where that activity is economic in nature\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

True, a passport is frequently used by persons who may intend to engage in commerce abroad, but that use is insufficient to bring \xc2\xa7214(d)\xe2\x80\x99s passport directive within the scope of this power. The specific conduct at issue here\xe2\x80\x94the listing of the birthplace of a U.\xc2\xa0S. citizen born in Jerusalem on a passport by the President\xe2\x80\x94is not a commercial activity. Any commercial activities subsequently undertaken by the bearer of a passport are yet further removed from that regulation.

\n

The power \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o establish an uniform Rule of Naturalization\xe2\x80\x9d is similarly unavailing. At the founding, the word \xe2\x80\x9cnaturalization\xe2\x80\x9d meant \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he act of investing aliens with the privileges of native subjects.\xe2\x80\x9d 2 S. Johnson, A Dictionary of the English Language 1293 (4th ed. 1773); see also T. Dyche & W. Pardon, A New General English Dictionary (1771) (\xe2\x80\x9cthe making a foreigner or alien, a denizen or freeman of any kingdom or city, and so becoming, as it were, both a subject and a native of a king or country, that by nature he did not belong to\xe2\x80\x9d). A passport has never been issued as part of the naturalization process. It is\xe2\x80\x94and has always been\xe2\x80\x94a \xe2\x80\x9ctravel document,\xe2\x80\x9d Dept. of State, 7 Foreign Affairs Manual (or FAM) \xc2\xa71311(b) (2013), issued for the same purpose it has always served: a request from one sovereign to another for the protection of the bearer. See supra, at 10\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

b

\n

For similar reasons, the Necessary and Proper Clause gives Congress no authority here. That Clause provides, \xe2\x80\x9cThe Congress shall have Power .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [t]o make all Laws which shall be necessary and proper for carrying into Execution the foregoing Powers, and all other Powers vested by this Constitution in the Government of the United States, or in any Department or Officer thereof.\xe2\x80\x9d U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa78, cl. 18. As an initial matter, \xe2\x80\x9cCongress lacks authority to legislate [under this provision] if the objective is anything other than \xe2\x80\x98carrying into Execution\xe2\x80\x99 one or more of the Federal Government\xe2\x80\x99s enumerated powers.\xe2\x80\x9d Comstock, supra, at 161 (Thomas, J., dissenting). The \xe2\x80\x9cend [must] be legitimate\xe2\x80\x9d under our constitutional structure. McCulloch v. Maryland, 4 Wheat. 316, 421 (1819).

\n

But even if the objective of a law is carrying into execution one of the Federal Government\xe2\x80\x99s enumerated powers, the law must be both necessary and proper to that objective. The \xe2\x80\x9cClause is not a warrant to Congress to enact any law that bears some conceivable connection to the exercise of an enumerated power.\xe2\x80\x9d Gonzales v. Raich,\n\n545 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1,\n60 (2005)\n (Thomas, J., dissenting). Instead, \xe2\x80\x9cthere must be a necessary and proper fit between the \xe2\x80\x98means\xe2\x80\x99 (the federal law) and the \xe2\x80\x98end\xe2\x80\x99 (the enumerated power or powers) it is designed to serve.\xe2\x80\x9d Comstock, supra, at 160 (Thomas, J., dissenting). The \xe2\x80\x9cmeans\xe2\x80\x9d chosen by Congress \xe2\x80\x9cwill be deemed \xe2\x80\x98necessary\xe2\x80\x99 if they are \xe2\x80\x98appropriate\xe2\x80\x99 and \xe2\x80\x98plainly adapted\xe2\x80\x99 to the exercise of an enumerated power, and \xe2\x80\x98proper\xe2\x80\x99 if they are not otherwise \xe2\x80\x98prohibited\xe2\x80\x99 by the Constitution and not \xe2\x80\x98[in]consistent\xe2\x80\x99 with its \xe2\x80\x98letter and spirit.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 160\xe2\x80\x93161 (alteration in original).

\n

The argument that \xc2\xa7214(d), as applied to passports, could be an exercise of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 power to carry into execution its foreign commerce or naturalization powers falters because this aspect of \xc2\xa7214(d) is directed at neither of the ends served by these powers. Although at a high level of generality, a passport could be related to foreign commerce and naturalization, that attenuated relationship is insufficient. The law in question must be \xe2\x80\x9cdirectly link[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d to the enumerated power. Id., at 169, n.\xc2\xa08. As applied to passports, \xc2\xa7214(d) fails that test because it does not \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98carr[y] into Execution\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Congress\xe2\x80\x99 foreign commerce or naturalization powers. Id., at 160. At most, it bears a tertiary relationship to an activity Congress is permitted to regulate: It directs the President\xe2\x80\x99s formulation of a document, which, in turn, may be used to facilitate travel, which, in turn, may facilitate foreign commerce. And the distinctive history of the passport as a travel rather than citizenship document makes its connection to naturalization even more tenuous.

\n

Nor can this aspect of \xc2\xa7214(d) be justified as an exercise of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 power to enact laws to carry into execution the President\xe2\x80\x99s residual foreign affairs powers. Simply put, \xc2\xa7214(d)\xe2\x80\x99s passport directive is not a \xe2\x80\x9cproper\xe2\x80\x9d means of carrying this power into execution.

\n

To be \xe2\x80\x9cproper,\xe2\x80\x9d a law must fall within the peculiar competence of Congress under the Constitution. Though \xe2\x80\x9cproper\xe2\x80\x9d was susceptible of several definitions at the time of the founding, only two are plausible candidates for use in the Necessary and Proper Clause\xe2\x80\x94(1) \xe2\x80\x9c[f]it; accommodated; adapted; suitable; qualified\xe2\x80\x9d and (2) \xe2\x80\x9c[p]eculiar; not belonging to more; not common.\xe2\x80\x9d See 2 Johnson, supra, at 1537. Because the former would render the word \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary\xe2\x80\x9d superfluous, McCulloch, supra, at 413, and we ordinarily attempt to give effect \xe2\x80\x9cto each word of the Constitution,\xe2\x80\x9d Knowlton v. Moore,\n\n178 U.\xc2\xa0S. 41,\n87 (1900)\n, the latter is the more plausible. That is particularly true because the Constitution elsewhere uses the term \xe2\x80\x9cproper\xe2\x80\x9d by itself, Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa79, Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72, 3; the term \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary\xe2\x80\x9d by itself, Art. I, \xc2\xa77; Art.\xc2\xa0V; and the term \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary\xe2\x80\x9d as part of the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary and expedient,\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa73. Thus, the best interpretation of \xe2\x80\x9cproper\xe2\x80\x9d is that a law must fall within the peculiar jurisdiction of Congress.

\n

Our constitutional structure imposes three key limitations on that jurisdiction: It must conform to (1) the allocation of authority within the Federal Government, (2) the allocation of power between the Federal Government and the States, and (3) the protections for retained individual rights under the Constitution. See Lawson & Granger, The \xe2\x80\x9cProper\xe2\x80\x9d Scope of Federal Power: A Jurisdictional Interpretation of the Sweeping Clause, 43 Duke L.\xc2\xa0J. 267, 291, 297 (1993). In other words, to be \xe2\x80\x9cproper,\xe2\x80\x9d a law \xe2\x80\x9cmust be consistent with principles of separation of powers, principles of federalism, and individual rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Id.,at 297.

\n

Commentators during the ratification debates treated \xe2\x80\x9cproper\xe2\x80\x9d as having this meaning. Writing as Publius, Hamilton posed the question who would \xe2\x80\x9cjudge .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the necessity and propriety of the laws to be passed for executing the powers of the Union\xe2\x80\x9d and responded that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he propriety of a law, in a constitutional light, must always be determined by the nature of the powers upon which it is founded.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist, No. 33, pp. 203\xe2\x80\x93204. For example, a law that \xe2\x80\x9cexceeded [Congress\xe2\x80\x99] jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d and invaded the authority of the States would not meet that standard. Id., at 204. Similarly, an \xe2\x80\x9cimpartial citizen\xe2\x80\x9d wrote in a Virginia newspaper that, even if the governmental powers could not \xe2\x80\x9cbe executed without the aid of a law, granting commercial monopolies, inflicting un-usual punishments, creating new crimes, or commanding any unconstitutional act,\xe2\x80\x9d thus making the law necessary to the execution of a power, \xe2\x80\x9csuch a law would be manifestly not proper,\xe2\x80\x9d and not \xe2\x80\x9cwarranted by this clause, without absolutely departing from the usual acceptation of words.\xe2\x80\x9d An Impartial Citizen V, Petersburg Va. Gazette, Feb. 28, 1788, in 8 Documentary History of the Ratification of the Constitution 428, 431 (J. Kaminski & G. Saladino eds. 1988) (emphasis deleted).

\n

Early interpretations of the Clause following ratification largely confirm that view. Lawson & Granger, supra, at 298\xe2\x80\x93308. During debate on the Bank of the United States in the First Congress, for example, Representative Ames declared that the correct construction of the Necessary and Proper Clause \xe2\x80\x9cpromotes the good of the society, and the ends for which the Government was adopted, without impairing the rights of any man, or the powers of any State.\xe2\x80\x9d 2 Annals of Cong. 1906 (1791). During the Second Congress, Representative Niles railed against a bill that would have authorized federal mail carriers to transport passengers for hire in order to reduce the cost of the mails. He said that such a law would not be \xe2\x80\x9cproper\xe2\x80\x9d to the power to establish post offices and post roads because some States had \xe2\x80\x9can exclusive right of carrying passengers for hire\xe2\x80\x9d and an interpretation of the word \xe2\x80\x9cproper\xe2\x80\x9d that would allow the bill would render \xe2\x80\x9cas nugatory, all [the States\xe2\x80\x99] deliberations on the Constitution\xe2\x80\x9d and effectively vest Congress with \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral authority to legislate on every subject.\xe2\x80\x9d 3 id., at 308\xe2\x80\x93310 (1792) (emphasis deleted). Each of these comments presumed that the word \xe2\x80\x9cproper\xe2\x80\x9d imposed a jurisdictional limit on congressional activity.

\n

This evidence makes sense in light of the Framers\xe2\x80\x99 efforts to ensure a separation of powers, reinforced by checks and balances, as \xe2\x80\x9cpractical and real protectio[n] for individual liberty in the new Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Perez v. Mortgage Bankers Assn., 575 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2015) (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment) (slip op., at 8). If Congress could rely on the Necessary and Proper Clause to exercise power expressly allocated to the other branches or to prevent the exercise of such power by other branches, it could undermine the constitutional allocation of powers.

\n

That the evidence thus points to a definition of \xe2\x80\x9cproper\xe2\x80\x9d that protects the separation of powers does not fully explain the way that the \xe2\x80\x9cproper\xe2\x80\x9d requirement operates when Congress seeks to facilitate the exercise of a power allocated to another branch. I can see two potential mechanisms, either or both of which may accurately reflect the original understanding of the Clause. First, a law could be \xe2\x80\x9cimproper\xe2\x80\x9d if it purports to direct another branch\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of its power. See Calabresi & Prakash, The President\xe2\x80\x99s Power to Execute the Laws, 104 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J. 541, 591 (1994) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he Clause .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. does [not] allow Congress to tell constitutionally empowered actors how they can implement their exclusive powers\xe2\x80\x9d). Second, a law could be \xe2\x80\x9cimproper\xe2\x80\x9d if it takes one of those actions and the branch to which the power is allocated objects to the action. See Prakash & Ramsey 255\xe2\x80\x93256 (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress has the general power to legislate in support of the President\xe2\x80\x99s foreign policy goals. But .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [s]ince it is derivative of the President\xe2\x80\x99s power, it must be exercised in coordination with, and not in opposition to, the President\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

I need not resolve that question today, as the application of \xc2\xa7214(d) to passports would be improper under either approach. The President has made a determination that the \xe2\x80\x9cplace of birth\xe2\x80\x9d on a passport should list the country of present sovereignty. 7 FAM, \xc2\xa71300, App. D, \xc2\xa71330 (2014). And the President has determined that no country is presently exercising sovereignty over the area of Jerusalem. Thus, the President has provided that passports for persons born in Jerusalem should list \xe2\x80\x9cJerusalem\xe2\x80\x9d as the place of birth in the passport. Id., \xc2\xa71360(f). Section 214(d) directs the President to exercise his power to issue and regulate the content of passports in a particular way, and the President has objected to that direction. Under either potential mechanism for evaluating the propriety of a law under the separation-of-powers limitation, this law would be improper.[5]

c

\n

In support of his argument that the President must enforce \xc2\xa7214(d), Zivotofsky relies heavily on a similar statute addressing the place of birth designation for persons born in Taiwan. See Foreign Relations Authorization Act, Fiscal Years 1994 and 1995, \xc2\xa7132,\n\n108Stat.\n395. That statute provided, \xe2\x80\x9cFor purposes of the registration of birth or certification of nationality of a United States citizen born in Taiwan, the Secretary of State shall permit the place of birth to be recorded as Taiwan.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The President has adopted that practice.

\n

The President\xe2\x80\x99s decision to adopt that practice, however, says nothing about the constitutionality of the Taiwan provision in the first place. The constitutional allocation of powers \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not depend on the views of individual Presidents, nor on whether the encroached upon branch approves the encroachment.\xe2\x80\x9d Free Enterprise Fund v. Public Company Accounting Oversight Bd.,\n\n561 U.\xc2\xa0S. 477,\n497 (2010)\n (citation and internal quotation marks omitted).[6] And the argument from Presidential acquiescence here is particularly weak, given that the Taiwan statute is consistent with the President\xe2\x80\x99s longstanding policy on Taiwan. At the time Congress enacted the statute, the Foreign Affairs Manual permitted consular officials to list \xe2\x80\x9cthe city or area of birth\xe2\x80\x9d on a passport \xe2\x80\x9c[w]here the birthplace of the applicant is located in territory disputed by another country,\xe2\x80\x9d 7 FAM \xc2\xa71383.5\xe2\x80\x932 (1987), and to list \xe2\x80\x9cthe city or town, rather than the country\xe2\x80\x9d of an applicant\xe2\x80\x99s birth \xe2\x80\x9cwhen there are objections to the listing shown on the birthplace guide,\xe2\x80\x9d id., \xc2\xa71383.6. Because the President otherwise treats Taiwan as a geographical area within the People\xe2\x80\x99s Republic of China, listing Taiwan as the place of birth did not directly conflict with the President\xe2\x80\x99s prevailing practices. Section 214(d) does so conflict, as it requires the President to list citizens born in Jerusalem as born in \xe2\x80\x9cIsrael,\xe2\x80\x9d even though the Foreign Affairs Manual has long prohibited that action.

\n

Justice Scalia would locate Congress\xe2\x80\x99 power to enact the passport directive of \xc2\xa7214(d) in Congress\xe2\x80\x99 power under the Necessary and Proper Clause to bring into effect its enumerated power over naturalization. Post, at 3\xe2\x80\x934 (dissenting opinion). As an initial matter, he asserts that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he naturalization power .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. enables Congress to furnish the people it makes citizens with papers verifying their citizenship,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 3, yet offers no support for this interpretation of a clause that, by its terms, grants Congress only the \xe2\x80\x9cPower .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. To establish an uniform Rule of Naturalization,\xe2\x80\x9d U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa78, cl. 4. He then concludes that, if Congress can grant such documents, \xe2\x80\x9cit may also require these [documents] to record his birthplace as \xe2\x80\x98Israel\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d pursuant to its power under the Necessary and Proper Clause, post, at 3. But this theory does not account for the President\xe2\x80\x99s power to act in this area, nor does it confront difficult questions about the application of the Necessary and Proper Clause in the case of conflict among the branches.

\n

Justice Scalia disapproves of my \xe2\x80\x9cassertion of broad, unenumerated \xe2\x80\x98residual powers\xe2\x80\x99 in the President,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 19, but offers no response to my interpretation of the words \xe2\x80\x9cexecutive Power\xe2\x80\x9d in the Constitution. Instead, he claims that I have argued for \xe2\x80\x9cPresidential primacy over passports\xe2\x80\x9d and then rejects that position based on two postratification English statutes, the early practice of nonfederal actors issuing passports in this country, and the same congressional statutes that I have already discussed, most of which were enacted after the Civil War. Post, at 16\xe2\x80\x9317; supra, at 13, and n.\xc2\xa04. But I do not argue that the President possesses primary power over passports. I need not argue that. I argue only that Congress did not act according to any of the powers granted to it in the Constitution and, in such circumstances, the question of primacy does not arise.

\n

In any event, the historical evidence cited in Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s dissent does not conflict with my analysis of the President\xe2\x80\x99s power in this area. The two postratification English statutes implicitly acknowledged that passports are issued by executive officers in the exercise of executive power, see 38 Geo. III, ch. 50, \xc2\xa78, in 41 Eng. Stat. at Large 684; 33 Geo. III, ch. 4, \xc2\xa78, in 39 Eng. Stat. at Large 12, and the practice of executive officials in the States of this country confirms that relationship. In addition, neither piece of historical evidence speaks to the scope of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 power to regulate passports under our federal system. Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s final piece of historical support\xe2\x80\x94the increased congressional regulation of passports following the Civil War\xe2\x80\x94is perhaps more on point from an institutional perspective, but still does not resolve the issue. Those regulations were, as I have already described, narrow in scope and continued to leave primary regulation of the content of passports to the President. To draw an inference from these \xe2\x80\x9clate-arising historical practices that are ambiguous at best\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94and that might conflict with the original meaning of the \xe2\x80\x9cexecutive Power\xe2\x80\x9d and the \xe2\x80\x9cproper\xe2\x80\x9d requirement in the Necessary and Proper Clause\xe2\x80\x94is a dubious way to undertake constitutional analysis. See Noel Canning, 573 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment) (slip op., at 2).

\n

Even more dubious, however, is the cursory treatment of the Necessary and Proper Clause in Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s dissent. He asserts that, in acting pursuant to that Clause, \xe2\x80\x9cCongress .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. may not transcend boundaries upon legislative authority stated or implied elsewhere in the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 4. But he offers no explanation for what those implied limits might be or how they would operate. Does he, for example, agree that the word \xe2\x80\x9cproper\xe2\x80\x9d requires Congress to act in a manner \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98consistent with principles of separation of powers, principles of federalism, and individual rights\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d? Supra, at 18 (quoting Lawson & Grainger, 43 Duke L.\xc2\xa0J., at 297). If so, then why does he find that requirement satisfied in this case? Is it because he views the President as having no constitutional authority to act in this area? Or is it because he views Congress\xe2\x80\x99 directive to the President as consistent with the separation of powers, irrespective of the President\xe2\x80\x99s authority? If the latter, is that because he perceives no separation-of-powers limitations on Congress when it acts to carry into execution one of its enumerated powers, as opposed to the enumerated powers of another branch? And if that is the case, what textual, structural, or historical evidence exists for that interpretation? Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s dissent raises more questions than it answers.

\n

Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s dissent does at least answer how, in his view, the Constitution would resolve a conflict between the political branches, each acting pursuant to the powers granted them under the Constitution. He believes that congressional power should trump in any such conflict. Post, at 18. I see nothing in the Constitution that clearly mandates that solution to a difficult separation-of-powers question, and I need not opine on it. I find no power under which Congress could lawfully have enacted the passport directive of \xc2\xa7214(d), apart from its power under the Necessary and Proper Clause to carry into effect the President\xe2\x80\x99s powers. And I have offered textual and historical support for my conclusion that the Clause does not include the power to direct the President\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of his passport power.

\n

Finally, Justice Scalia faults me for failing to consider a number of potential sources of congressional power for \xc2\xa7214(d) not argued by any of the parties, ranging from the\n \nFourteenth Amendment; to the Migration or Importation Clause, Art. I, \xc2\xa79, cl. 1; to the Territories Clause, Art. IV, \xc2\xa73, cl. 2. Post, at 15. But no one\xe2\x80\x94not even Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x94has seriously contended that those provisions would afford a basis for the passport provision of \xc2\xa7214(d).

\n

In the end, Justice Scalia characterizes my interpretation of the executive power, the naturalization power, and the Necessary and Proper Clause as producing \xe2\x80\x9ca presidency more reminiscent of George III than George Washington.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 19. But he offers no competing interpretation of either the Article II Vesting Clause or the Necessary and Proper Clause. And his decision about the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s resolution of conflict among the branches could itself be criticized as creating a supreme legislative body more reminiscent of the Parliament in England than the Congress in America.

B

\n

Although the consular report of birth abroad shares some features with a passport, it is historically associated with naturalization, not foreign affairs. In order to establish a \xe2\x80\x9cuniform Rule of Naturalization,\xe2\x80\x9d Congress must be able to identify the categories of persons who are eligible for naturalization, along with the rules for that process. Congress thus has always regulated the \xe2\x80\x9cacquisition of citizenship by being born abroad of American parents . . . in the exercise of the power conferred by the Constitution to establish a uniform rule of naturalization.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Wong Kim Ark,\n\n169 U.\xc2\xa0S. 649,\n688 (1898)\n; see also Miller v. Albright,\n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 420,\n456 (1998)\n (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment) (recognizing that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress has the power to set the requirements for acquisition of citizenship by persons not born within the territory of the United States\xe2\x80\x9d). It has determined that children born abroad to U.\xc2\xa0S. parents, subject to some exceptions, are natural-born citizens who do not need to go through the naturalization process. 8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7 1401(c), (d), (g).

\n

The consular report of birth abroad is well suited to carrying into execution the power conferred on Congress in the Naturalization Clause. The report developed in response to Congress\xe2\x80\x99 requirement that children born abroad to U.\xc2\xa0S. citizens register with the consulate or lose their citizenship. And it continues to certify the acquisition of U.\xc2\xa0S. citizenship at birth by a person born abroad to a U.\xc2\xa0S. citizen. See\n\n22 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72705(2).

\n

Although such persons have possessed a statutory right to citizenship at birth for much of this country\xe2\x80\x99s history,[7] the process by which that citizenship is evidenced has varied over time. Under the 1870 consular regulations, for instance, children born abroad to U.\xc2\xa0S. citizens were issued no certificates. If they applied for a U.\xc2\xa0S. passport, then they were issued one \xe2\x80\x9cqualified by the obligations and duties\xe2\x80\x9d that attached to those citizens by virtue of their residence in a foreign nation. Regulations Prescribed For The Use Of The Consular Service of the UnitedStates App. No. IV, p. 288 (1870); see also id., \xc2\xa7109, at38\xe2\x80\x9339. Congress acted in 1907 to require children residing abroad to register with their local consulate at the age of 18. Act of Mar. 2, 1907, \xc2\xa76,\n\n34Stat.\n1229. Because of the importance of this registration requirement, consular officials began to issue reports to citizens confirming their registration. See generally National Archives, General Records of the Dept. of State, Record Group 59, Passport Office, Decimal File, 1910\xe2\x80\x931949.

\n

In 1919, the Department of State acted to standardize the consular registration of children born abroad. Report of Birth of Children to American Citizens Residing Abroad, General Instruction No. 652. It urged consulates to impress upon U.\xc2\xa0S. citizens abroad the need to record the birth of their children within two years. Id., at 2. To encourage that effort, the Department permitted consular officials to issue reports attesting that the parents of U.\xc2\xa0S. citizens born abroad had presented sufficient evidence of citizenship for their children. Ibid.

\n

The 1960\xe2\x80\x99s brought additional regulations of consular reports of birth abroad, 31 Fed. Reg. 13538 (1966), which continue in a substantially similar form to this day. See 22 CFR \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa750.5, 50.7 (2014). As currently issued, the consular report of birth abroad includes the applicant\xe2\x80\x99s name, sex, place of birth, date of birth, and parents. It has had the \xe2\x80\x9csame force and effect as proof of United States citizenship as [a] certificat[e] of naturalization\xe2\x80\x9d since 1982. \xc2\xa7117,\n\n96Stat.\n279.

\n

Thus, although registration is no longer required to maintain birthright citizenship, the consular report of birth abroad remains the primary means by which children born abroad may obtain official acknowledgement of their citizenship. See 22 CFR \xc2\xa751.43. Once acknowledged as U.\xc2\xa0S. citizens, they need not pursue the naturalization process to obtain the rights and privileges of citizenship in this country. Regulation of the report is thus \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cplainly adapted\xe2\x80\x9d to the exercise of the naturalization power. See Comstock, 560 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 161 (Thomas, J., dissenting).

\n

By contrast, regulation of the report bears no relationship to the President\xe2\x80\x99s residual foreign affairs power. It has no historical pedigree uniquely associated with the President, contains no communication directed at a foreign power, and is primarily used for domestic purposes. To the extent that a citizen born abroad seeks a document to use as evidence of his citizenship abroad, he must obtain a passport. See generally 7 FAM \xc2\xa71311.

\n

Because regulation of the consular report of birth abroad is justified as an exercise of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 powers under the Naturalization and Necessary and Proper Clauses and does not fall within the President\xe2\x80\x99s foreign affairs powers, \xc2\xa7214(d)\xe2\x80\x99s treatment of that document is constitutional.[8]

III

\n

The majority does not perform this analysis, but instead relies on a variation of the recognition power. That power is among the foreign affairs powers vested in the President by Article II\xe2\x80\x99s Vesting Clause, as is confirmed by Article II\xe2\x80\x99s express assignment to the President of the duty of receiving foreign Ambassadors, Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa73. But I cannot join the majority\xe2\x80\x99s analysis because no act of recognition is implicated here.[9]

\n

Under international law, \xe2\x80\x9crecognition of a state signifies acceptance of its position within the international community and the possession by it of the full range of rights and obligations which are the normal attributes of statehood.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 Oppenheim\xe2\x80\x99s International Law \xc2\xa747, 158 (R. Jennings & A. Watts eds., 9th ed. 1992) (footnote omitted) (Oppenheim).[10] It can be accomplished expressly or implicitly, but the key is to discern a clear intention on the part of one state to recognize another. Id., \xc2\xa750, at 169. Important consequences are understood to flow from one state\xe2\x80\x99s recognition of another: The new state, for instance, acquires the capacity to engage in diplomatic relations, including the negotiation of treaties, with the recognizing state. Id., \xc2\xa747, at 158. The new state is also entitled to sue in, invoke sovereign immunity from, and demand acceptance of official acts in the courts of the recognizing state. Ibid.; see also I. Brownlie, Principles of Public International Law 95\xe2\x80\x9396 (7th ed. 2008).

\n

Changes in territory generally do not affect the status of a state as an international person. Oppenheim \xc2\xa757, at 204\xe2\x80\x93205. France, for example, \xe2\x80\x9chas over the centuries retained its identity although it acquired, lost and regained parts of its territory, changed its dynasty, was a kingdom, a republic, an empire, again a kingdom, again a republic, again an empire, and is now once more a republic.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. \xe2\x80\x9cEven such loss of territory as occasions the reduction of a major power to a lesser status does not affect the state as an international person.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., \xc2\xa757, at 205. Changes that would affect the status as an international person include the union of two separate international persons or a partial loss of independence. Id., \xc2\xa758, at 206.

\n

Assuming for the sake of argument that listing a non-recognized foreign sovereign as a citizen\xe2\x80\x99s place of birth on a U.\xc2\xa0S. passport could have the effect of recognizing that sovereign under international law, no such recognition would occur under the circumstances presented here. The United States has recognized Israel as a foreign sovereign since May 14, 1948. Statement by the President Announcing the Recognition of the State of Israel, Public Papers of the Presidents, Harry S. Truman, p. 258 (1964). That the United States has subsequently declined to acknowledge Israel\xe2\x80\x99s sovereignty over Jerusalem has not changed its recognition of Israel as a sovereign state. And even if the United States were to acknowledge Israel\xe2\x80\x99s sovereignty over Jerusalem, that action would not change its recognition of Israel as a sovereign state. That is because the United States has already afforded Israel the rights and responsibilities attendant to its status as a sovereign State. Taking a different position on the Jerusalem question will have no effect on that recognition.[11]

\n

Perhaps recognizing that a formal recognition is not implicated here, the majority reasons that, if the Executive\xe2\x80\x99s exclusive recognition power \xe2\x80\x9cis to mean anything, it must mean that the President not only makes the initial, formal recognition determination but also that he may maintain that determination in his and his agent\xe2\x80\x99s statements.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 26. By \xe2\x80\x9calter[ing] the President\xe2\x80\x99s statements on matters of recognition or forc[ing] him to contradict them,\xe2\x80\x9d the majority reasons, \xe2\x80\x9cCongress in effect would exercise the recognition power.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 27. This argument stretches the recognition power beyond all recognition. Listing a Jerusalem-born citizen\xe2\x80\x99s place of birth as \xe2\x80\x9cIsrael\xe2\x80\x9d cannot amount to recognition because the United States already recognizes Israel as an international person. Rather than adopt a novel definition of the recognition power, the majority should have looked to other foreign affairs powers in the Constitution to resolve this dispute.
