GONZALEZ v. CROSBY, SECRETARY, FLORIDA DEPARTMENT OF CORRECTIONS

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

No. 04\xe2\x80\x936432.Argued April 25, 2005\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 23, 2005

\n

Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s federal habeas corpus petition was dismissed as time barred when the District Court concluded that the federal limitations period was not tolled while petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s motion for postconviction relief was pending in state court. After petitioner abandoned his attempt to seek review of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision, this Court decided that a state postconviction relief petition can toll the federal statute of limitations even if, like petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s, the petition is ultimately dismissed as procedurally barred. Artuz v. Bennett, 531 U. S. 4. Petitioner filed a Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 60(b)(6) motion for relief from the judgment, which the District Court denied. The Eleventh Circuit affirmed the denial, holding that the Rule 60(b) motion was in substance a second or successive habeas petition, which under the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA), 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72244(b), cannot be filed without precertification by the court of appeals.

\n

Held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0Because petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 60(b) motion challenged only the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s previous ruling on AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s statute of limitations, it is not the equivalent of a successive habeas petition and can be ruled upon by the District Court without precertification by the Eleventh Circuit. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Rule 60(b) applies in \xc2\xa72254 habeas proceedings only \xe2\x80\x9cto the extent that [it is] not inconsistent with\xe2\x80\x9d applicable federal statutes and rules. \xc2\xa72254 Rule 11. Because \xc2\xa72244(b) applies only where a court acts pursuant to a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9chabeas corpus application,\xe2\x80\x9d the question here is whether a Rule 60(b) motion is such an application. The text of \xc2\xa72244(b) shows that, for these purposes, a habeas application is a filing containing one or more \xe2\x80\x9cclaims.\xe2\x80\x9d Other federal habeas statutes and this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions also make clear that a \xe2\x80\x9cclaim\xe2\x80\x9d is an asserted federal basis for relief from a state-court conviction. If a Rule 60(b) motion contains one or more \xe2\x80\x9cclaims,\xe2\x80\x9d the motion is, if not in substance a \xe2\x80\x9chabeas corpus application,\xe2\x80\x9d at least similar enough that failing to subject it to AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s restrictions on successive habeas petitions would be \xe2\x80\x9cinconsistent with\xe2\x80\x9d the statute. A Rule 60(b) motion can be said to bring a \xe2\x80\x9cclaim\xe2\x80\x9d if it seeks to add a new ground for relief from the state conviction or attacks the federal court\xe2\x80\x99s previous resolution of a claim on the merits, though not if it merely attacks a defect in the federal habeas proceedings\xe2\x80\x99 integrity. Pp. 3\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0When no \xe2\x80\x9cclaim\xe2\x80\x9d is presented, there is no basis for contending that a Rule 60(b) motion should be treated like a habeas petition. If neither the motion itself nor the federal judgment from which it seeks relief substantively addresses federal grounds for setting aside the movant\xe2\x80\x99s state conviction, allowing the motion to proceed on its own terms creates no inconsistency with the habeas statute or rules. Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s motion, which alleges that the federal courts misapplied \xc2\xa72244(d)\xe2\x80\x99s statute of limitations, fits this description. Nothing in Calderon v. Thompson, 523 U. S. 538, suggests that entertaining a filing confined to a nonmerits aspect of the first federal habeas proceeding is \xe2\x80\x9cinconsistent with\xe2\x80\x9d AEDPA. Pp. 8\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0Under the proper Rule 60(b) standards, the District Court was correct to deny relief. The change in the law worked by Artuz is not an \xe2\x80\x9cextraordinary circumstance\xe2\x80\x9d justifying relief under Rule 60(b)(6), and it is made all the less extraordinary by the lack of diligence that petitioner showed in seeking direct appellate review of the statute-of-limitations issue. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

366 F.\xc2\xa03d 1253, affirmed.

AURELIO O. GONZALEZ, PETITIONER v. JAMES V. \nCROSBY, Jr., SECRETARY, FLORIDA \nDEPARTMENT OF CORRECTIONS

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

[June 23, 2005]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After the federal courts denied petitioner habeas corpus relief from his state conviction, he filed a motion for relief from that judgment, pursuant to Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 60(b). The question presented is whether, in a habeas case, such motions are subject to the additional restrictions that apply to \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive\xe2\x80\x9d habeas corpus petitions under the provisions of the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA) codified at 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72244(b).

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner Aurelio Gonzalez pleaded guilty in Florida Circuit Court to one count of robbery with a firearm. He filed no appeal and began serving his 99-year sentence in 1982. Some 12 years later, petitioner began to seek relief from his conviction. He filed two motions for state postconviction relief, which the Florida courts denied. Thereafter, in June 1997, petitioner filed a federal habeas petition in the United\xc2\xa0States District Court for the Southern District of Florida, alleging that his guilty plea had not been entered knowingly and voluntarily.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Upon the State\xe2\x80\x99s motion, the District Court dismissed petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s habeas petition as barred by AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s statute of limitations, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72244(d). Under Eleventh Circuit precedent, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s filing deadline, absent tolling, was April 23, 1997, one year after AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s statute of limitations took effect. Wilcox v. Florida Dept. of Corrections, 158 F.\xc2\xa03d 1209, 1211 (CA11 1998) (per curiam). Adopting a Magistrate Judge\xe2\x80\x99s recommendation, the District Court concluded that the limitations period was not tolled during the 163-day period while petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s second motion for state postconviction relief was pending. Section 2244(d)(2) tolls the statute of limitations during the pendency of \xe2\x80\x9cproperly filed\xe2\x80\x9d applications only, and the District Court thought petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s motion was not \xe2\x80\x9cproperly filed\xe2\x80\x9d because it was both untimely and successive. Without tolling, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s federal habeas petition was two months late, so the District Court dismissed it as time barred. A judge of the Eleventh Circuit denied a certificate of appealability (COA) on April 6, 2000, and petitioner did not file for rehearing or review of that decision.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On November 7, 2000, we held in Artuz v. Bennett, 531 U. S. 4, that an application for state postconviction relief can be \xe2\x80\x9cproperly filed\xe2\x80\x9d even if the state courts dismiss it as procedurally barred. See id., at 8\xe2\x80\x939. Almost nine months later, petitioner filed in the District Court a pro se \xe2\x80\x9cMotion to Amend or Alter Judgment,\xe2\x80\x9d contending that the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s time-bar ruling was incorrect under Artuz\xe2\x80\x99s construction of \xc2\xa72244(d), and invoking Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 60(b)(6), which permits a court to relieve a party from the effect of a final judgment.[Footnote 1] The District Court denied the motion, and petitioner appealed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A judge of the Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit granted petitioner a COA, but a panel quashed the certificate as improvidently granted. 317 F.\xc2\xa03d 1308, 1310, 1314 (2003). The full court vacated that order and reheard the case en banc. It granted petitioner a COA but held, by a vote of 7 to 4, that the District Court was correct to deny his Rule 60(b) motion. The en banc majority determined that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s motion\xe2\x80\x94indeed, any postjudgment motion under Rule 60(b) save one alleging fraud on the court under Rule 60(b)(3)\xe2\x80\x94was in substance a second or successive habeas corpus petition. 366 F.\xc2\xa03d 1253, 1278, 1281\xe2\x80\x931282 (2004). A state prisoner may not file such a petition without precertification by the court of appeals that the petition meets certain stringent criteria. \xc2\xa72244(b). Because petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s motion did not satisfy these requirements, the Eleventh Circuit affirmed its denial. Id., at 1282.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari. 543 U. S. __ (2005).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Rule 60(b) allows a party to seek relief from a final judgment, and request reopening of his case, under a limited set of circumstances including fraud, mistake, and newly discovered evidence.[Footnote 2] Rule 60(b)(6), the particular provision under which petitioner brought his motion, permits reopening when the movant shows \xe2\x80\x9cany \xe2\x80\xa6 reason justifying relief from the operation of the judgment\xe2\x80\x9d other than the more specific circumstances set out in Rules 60(b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(5). See Liljeberg v. Health Services Acquisition Corp., 486 U. S. 847, 863, n.\xc2\xa011 (1988); Klapprott v. United States, 335 U. S. 601, 613 (1949) (opinion of Black, J.). The mere recitation of these provisions shows why we give little weight to respondent\xe2\x80\x99s appeal to the virtues of finality. That policy consideration, standing alone, is unpersuasive in the interpretation of a provision whose whole purpose is to make an exception to finality. The issue here is whether the text of Rule 60(b) itself, or of some other provision of law, limits its application in a manner relevant to the case before us.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0AEDPA did not expressly circumscribe the operation of Rule 60(b). (By contrast, AEDPA directly amended other provisions of the Federal Rules. See, e.g., AEDPA, \xc2\xa7103, 110 Stat. 1218 (amending Fed. Rule App. Proc. 22).) The new habeas restrictions introduced by AEDPA are made indirectly relevant, however, by the fact that Rule 60(b), like the rest of the Rules of Civil Procedure, applies in habeas corpus proceedings under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254[Footnote 3] only \xe2\x80\x9cto the extent that [it is] not inconsistent with\xe2\x80\x9d applicable federal statutory provisions and rules. 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254 Rule 11; see Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 81(a)(2). The relevant provisions of the AEDPA-amended habeas statutes, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72244(b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(3), impose three requirements on second or successive habeas petitions: First, any claim that has already been adjudicated in a previous petition must be dismissed. \xc2\xa72244(b)(1). Second, any claim that has not already been adjudicated must be dismissed unless it relies on either a new and retroactive rule of constitutional law or new facts showing a high probability of actual innocence. \xc2\xa72244(b)(2). Third, before the district court may accept a successive petition for filing, the court of appeals must determine that it presents a claim not previously raised that is sufficient to meet \xc2\xa72244(b)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s new-rule or actual-innocence provisions. \xc2\xa72244(b)(3). We proceed to consider whether these provisions limit the application of Rule 60(b) to the present case.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cAs a textual matter, \xc2\xa72244(b) applies only where the court acts pursuant to a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x98application\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d for a writ of habeas corpus. Calderon v. Thompson, 523 U. S. 538, 554 (1998). We therefore must decide whether a Rule 60(b) motion filed by a habeas petitioner is a \xe2\x80\x9chabeas corpus application\xe2\x80\x9d as the statute uses that term.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under \xc2\xa72244(b), the first step of analysis is to determine whether a \xe2\x80\x9cclaim presented in a second or successive habeas corpus application\xe2\x80\x9d was also \xe2\x80\x9cpresented in a prior application.\xe2\x80\x9d If so, the claim must be dismissed; if not, the analysis proceeds to whether the claim satisfies one of two narrow exceptions. In either event, it is clear that for purposes of \xc2\xa72244(b) an \xe2\x80\x9capplication\xe2\x80\x9d for habeas relief is a filing that contains one or more \xe2\x80\x9cclaims.\xe2\x80\x9d That definition is consistent with the use of the term \xe2\x80\x9capplication\xe2\x80\x9d in the other habeas statutes in chapter 153 of title 28. See, e.g., Woodford v. Garceau, 538 U. S. 202, 207 (2003) (for purposes of \xc2\xa72254(d), an application for habeas corpus relief is a filing that seeks \xe2\x80\x9can adjudication on the merits of the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claims\xe2\x80\x9d). These statutes, and our own decisions, make clear that a \xe2\x80\x9cclaim\xe2\x80\x9d as used in \xc2\xa72244(b) is an asserted federal basis for relief from a state court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment of conviction.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In some instances, a Rule 60(b) motion will contain one or more \xe2\x80\x9cclaims.\xe2\x80\x9d For example, it might straightforwardly assert that owing to \xe2\x80\x9cexcusable neglect,\xe2\x80\x9d Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 60(b)(1), the movant\xe2\x80\x99s habeas petition had omitted a claim of constitutional error, and seek leave to present that claim. Cf. Harris v. United States, 367 F.\xc2\xa03d 74, 80\xe2\x80\x9381 (CA2 2004) (petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 60(b) motion sought relief from judgment because habeas counsel had failed to raise a Sixth Amendment claim). Similarly, a motion might seek leave to present \xe2\x80\x9cnewly discovered evidence,\xe2\x80\x9d Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 60(b)(2), in support of a claim previously denied. E.g., Rodwell v. Pepe, 324 F.\xc2\xa03d 66, 69 (CA1 2003). Or a motion might contend that a subsequent change in substantive law is a \xe2\x80\x9creason justifying relief,\xe2\x80\x9d Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 60(b)(6), from the previous denial of a claim. E.g., Dunlap v. Litscher, 301 F.\xc2\xa03d 873, 876 (CA7 2002). Virtually every Court of Appeals to consider the question has held that such a pleading, although labeled a Rule 60(b) motion, is in substance a successive habeas petition and should be treated accordingly. E.g., Rodwell, supra, at 71\xe2\x80\x9372; Dunlap, supra, at 876.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We think those holdings are correct. A habeas petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s filing that seeks vindication of such a claim is, if not in substance a \xe2\x80\x9chabeas corpus application,\xe2\x80\x9d at least similar enough that failing to subject it to the same requirements would be \xe2\x80\x9cinconsistent with\xe2\x80\x9d the statute. 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254 Rule 11. Using Rule 60(b) to present new claims for relief from a state court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment of conviction\xe2\x80\x94even claims couched in the language of a true Rule 60(b) motion\xe2\x80\x94circumvents AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that a new claim be dismissed unless it relies on either a new rule of constitutional law or newly discovered facts. \xc2\xa72244(b)(2). The same is true of a Rule 60(b)(2) motion presenting new evidence in support of a claim already litigated: even assuming that reliance on a new factual predicate causes that motion to escape \xc2\xa72244(b)(1)\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition of claims \xe2\x80\x9cpresented in a prior application,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72244(b)(2)(B) requires a more convincing factual showing than does Rule 60(b). Likewise, a Rule 60(b) motion based on a purported change in the substantive law governing the claim could be used to circumvent \xc2\xa72244(b)(2)(A)\xe2\x80\x99s dictate that the only new law on which a successive petition may rely is \xe2\x80\x9ca new rule of constitutional law, made retroactive to cases on collateral review by the Supreme Court, that was previously unavailable.\xe2\x80\x9d In addition to the substantive conflict with AEDPA standards, in each of these three examples use of Rule 60(b) would impermissibly circumvent the requirement that a successive habeas petition be precertified by the court of appeals as falling within an exception to the successive-petition bar. \xc2\xa72244(b)(3).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In most cases, determining whether a Rule 60(b) motion advances one or more \xe2\x80\x9cclaims\xe2\x80\x9d will be relatively simple. A motion that seeks to add a new ground for relief, as in Harris, supra, will of course qualify. A motion can also be said to bring a \xe2\x80\x9cclaim\xe2\x80\x9d if it attacks the federal court\xe2\x80\x99s previous resolution of a claim on the merits,[Footnote 4] since alleging that the court erred in denying habeas relief on the merits is effectively indistinguishable from alleging that the movant is, under the substantive provisions of the statutes, entitled to habeas relief. That is not the case, however, when a Rule 60(b) motion attacks, not the substance of the federal court\xe2\x80\x99s resolution of a claim on the merits, but some defect in the integrity of the federal habeas proceedings.[Footnote 5]

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When no \xe2\x80\x9cclaim\xe2\x80\x9d is presented, there is no basis for contending that the Rule 60(b) motion should be treated like a habeas corpus application. If neither the motion itself nor the federal judgment from which it seeks relief substantively addresses federal grounds for setting aside the movant\xe2\x80\x99s state conviction, allowing the motion to proceed as denominated creates no inconsistency with the habeas statute or rules. Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s motion in the present case, which alleges that the federal courts misapplied the federal statute of limitations set out in \xc2\xa72244(d), fits this description.[Footnote 6]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Like the Court of Appeals, respondent relies heavily on our decision in Calderon v. Thompson, 523 U. S. 538 (1998). In that case we reversed the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision to recall its mandate and reconsider the denial of Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s first federal habeas petition; the recall was, we held, an abuse of discretion because of its inconsistency with the policies embodied in AEDPA. Id., at 554\xe2\x80\x93559. Analogizing an appellate court\xe2\x80\x99s recall of its mandate to a district court\xe2\x80\x99s grant of relief from judgment, the Eleventh Circuit thought that Calderon\xe2\x80\x99s disposition applied to Rule 60(b). 366 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1272\xe2\x80\x931277. We think otherwise. To begin with, as the opinion said, compliance with the actual text of AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s successive-petition provision was not at issue in Calderon\xe2\x80\x94because the Court of Appeals considered only the claims and evidence presented in Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s first federal habeas petition. 523 U. S., at 554. Calderon did state, however, that \xe2\x80\x9ca prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s motion to recall the mandate on the basis of the merits of the underlying decision can be regarded as a second or successive application.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 553 (emphasis added). But that is entirely consonant with the proposition that a Rule 60(b) motion that seeks to revisit the federal court\xe2\x80\x99s denial on the merits of a claim for relief should be treated as a successive habeas petition. The problem for respondent is that this case does not present a revisitation of the merits. The motion here, like some other Rule 60(b) motions in \xc2\xa72254 cases, confines itself not only to the first federal habeas petition, but to a nonmerits aspect of the first federal habeas proceeding. Nothing in Calderon suggests that entertaining such a filing is \xe2\x80\x9cinconsistent with\xe2\x80\x9d AEDPA.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Rule 60(b) has an unquestionably valid role to play in habeas cases. The Rule is often used to relieve parties from the effect of a default judgment mistakenly entered against them, e.g., Klapprott, 335 U. S., at 615 (opinion of Black, J.), a function as legitimate in habeas cases as in run-of-the-mine civil cases. The Rule also preserves parties\xe2\x80\x99 opportunity to obtain vacatur of a judgment that is void for lack of subject-matter jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x94a consideration just as valid in habeas cases as in any other, since absence of jurisdiction altogether deprives a federal court of the power to adjudicate the rights of the parties. Steel Co. v. Citizens for Better Environment, 523 U. S. 83, 94, 101 (1998). In some instances, we may note, it is the State, not the habeas petitioner, that seeks to use Rule 60(b), to reopen a habeas judgment granting the writ. See, e.g., Ritter v. Smith, 811 F.\xc2\xa02d 1398, 1400 (CA11 1987).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, several characteristics of a Rule 60(b) motion limit the friction between the Rule and the successive-petition prohibitions of AEDPA, ensuring that our harmonization of the two will not expose federal courts to an avalanche of frivolous postjudgment motions. First, Rule 60(b) contains its own limitations, such as the requirement that the motion \xe2\x80\x9cbe made within a reasonable time\xe2\x80\x9d and the more specific 1-year deadline for asserting three of the most open-ended grounds of relief (excusable neglect, newly discovered evidence, and fraud). Second, our cases have required a movant seeking relief under Rule 60(b)(6) to show \xe2\x80\x9cextraordinary circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d justifying the reopening of a final judgment. Ackermann v. United States, 340 U. S. 193, 199 (1950); accord, id., at 202; Liljeberg, 486 U. S., at 864; id., at 873 (Rehnquist, C.\xc2\xa0J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cThis very strict interpretation of Rule 60(b) is essential if the finality of judgments is to be preserved\xe2\x80\x9d). Such circumstances will rarely occur in the habeas context. Third, Rule 60(b) proceedings are subject to only limited and deferential appellate review. Browder v. Director, Dept. of Corrections of Ill., 434 U. S. 257, 263, n.\xc2\xa07 (1978). Many Courts of Appeals have construed 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72253 to impose an additional limitation on appellate review by requiring a habeas petitioner to obtain a COA as a prerequisite to appealing the denial of a Rule 60(b) motion.[Footnote 7]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 60(b) motion challenges only the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s previous ruling on the AEDPA statute of limitations, it is not the equivalent of a successive habeas petition. The Eleventh Circuit therefore erred in holding that petitioner did not qualify even to seek Rule 60(b) relief.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although the Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning is inconsistent with our holding today, we nonetheless affirm its denial of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 60(b) motion.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s only ground for reopening the judgment denying his first federal habeas petition is that our decision in Artuz showed the error of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s statute-of-limitations ruling. We assume for present purposes that the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling was incorrect.[Footnote 8] As we noted above, however, relief under Rule 60(b)(6)\xe2\x80\x94the only subsection petitioner invokes\xe2\x80\x94requires a showing of \xe2\x80\x9cextraordinary circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Petitioner contends that Artuz\xe2\x80\x99s change in the interpretation of the AEDPA statute of limitations meets this description. We do not agree. The District Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation was by all appearances correct under the Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s then-prevailing interpretation of 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72244(d)(2). It is hardly extraordinary that subsequently, after petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s case was no longer pending, this Court arrived at a different interpretation. Although our constructions of federal statutes customarily apply to all cases then pending on direct review, see, e.g., Harper v. Virginia Dept. of Taxation, 509 U. S. 86, 97 (1993), not every interpretation of the federal statutes setting forth the requirements for habeas provides cause for reopening cases long since final.[Footnote 9] If Artuz justified reopening long-ago dismissals based on a lower court\xe2\x80\x99s unduly parsimonious interpretation of \xc2\xa72244(d)(2), then Pace v. DiGuglielmo, 544 U. S. __ (2005), would justify reopening long-ago grants of habeas relief based on a lower court\xe2\x80\x99s unduly generous interpretation of the same tolling provision.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The change in the law worked by Artuz is all the less extraordinary in petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s case, because of his lack of diligence in pursuing review of the statute-of-limitations issue. At the time Artuz was decided, petitioner had abandoned any attempt to seek review of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision on this statute-of-limitations issue. Although the District Court relied on Eleventh Circuit precedent holding that a state postconviction application is not \xe2\x80\x9cproperly filed\xe2\x80\x9d if it is procedurally defaulted, and although that precedent was at odds with the rule in several other Circuits, petitioner neither raised that issue in his application for a COA, nor filed a petition for rehearing of the Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s denial of a COA, nor sought certiorari review of that denial.[Footnote 10] This lack of diligence confirms that Artuz is not an extraordinary circumstance justifying relief from the judgment in petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s case. Indeed, in one of the cases in which we explained Rule 60(b)(6)\xe2\x80\x99s extraordinary-circumstances requirement, the movant had failed to appeal an adverse ruling by the District Court, whereas another party to the same judgment had appealed and won reversal. Ackermann, 340 U. S., at 195. Some years later, the petitioner sought Rule 60(b) relief, which the District Court denied. We affirmed the denial of Rule 60(b) relief, noting that the movant\xe2\x80\x99s decision not to appeal had been free and voluntary, although the favorable ruling in the companion case made it appear mistaken in hindsight. See id., at 198.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under the Rule 60(b) standards that properly govern petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s motion, the District Court was correct to deny relief.

\n

***

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We hold that a Rule 60(b)(6) motion in a \xc2\xa72254 case is not to be treated as a successive habeas petition if it does not assert, or reassert, claims of error in the movant\xe2\x80\x99s state conviction. A motion that, like petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s, challenges only the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s failure to reach the merits does not warrant such treatment, and can therefore be ruled upon by the District Court without precertification by the Court of Appeals pursuant to \xc2\xa72244(b)(3). In this case, however, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 60(b)(6) motion fails to set forth an \xe2\x80\x9cextraordinary circumstance\xe2\x80\x9d justifying relief. For that reason, we affirm the judgment of the Court of Appeals.

\n

It is so ordered.

AURELIO O. GONZALEZ, PETITIONER v. JAMES V. \nCROSBY, Jr., SECRETARY, FLORIDA \nDEPARTMENT OF CORRECTIONS

[June 23, 2005]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens, with whom Justice Souter joins, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The most significant aspect of today\xe2\x80\x99s decision is the Court\xe2\x80\x99s unanimous rejection of the view that all postjudgment motions under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 60(b) except those alleging fraud under Rule 60(b)(3) should be treated as second or successive habeas corpus petitions. Not only do I agree with that holding, I believe that we should have more promptly made clear that the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA) and Rule 60(b) can coexist in harmony. See Abdur\xe2\x80\x99Rahman v. Bell, 537 U. S. 88, 90 (2002) (Stevens, J., dissenting from dismissal of certiorari as improvidently granted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the Court recognizes, whether a Rule 60(b) motion may proceed in the habeas context depends on the nature of the relief the motion seeks. See ante, at 8.[Footnote 1] Given the substance of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s motion, I agree with the Court that this was a \xe2\x80\x9ctrue\xe2\x80\x9d Rule 60(b) motion and that the District Court and the Court of Appeals therefore erred in treating it as a successive habeas petition. And while I also agree with much of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning in Parts I and II of its opinion, I believe the Court goes too far in commenting on issues that are not directly before us and that have not been fully briefed. See, e.g., ante, at 6\xe2\x80\x937 (discussing various court of appeals cases). My main disagreement, however, pertains to Part III of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court reaches beyond the question on which we granted certiorari (whether petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 60(b) motion should be treated as a successive habeas petition) and adjudicates the merits of that motion. In my judgment, however, \xe2\x80\x9ccorrect procedure requires that the merits of the Rule 60(b) motion be addressed in the first instance by the District Court.\xe2\x80\x9d Abdur\xe2\x80\x99Rahman, 537 U. S., at 97 (Stevens, J., dissenting). A district court considering a Rule 60(b) motion will often take into account a variety of factors in addition to the specific ground given for reopening the judgment. These factors include the diligence of the movant, the probable merit of the movant\xe2\x80\x99s underlying claims, the opposing party\xe2\x80\x99s reliance interests in the finality of the judgment, and other equitable considerations. See 11 C. Wright, A. Miller, & M. Kane, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa72857 (2d ed. 1995 and Supp. 2004) see ibid. (noting that appellate courts will reverse a district court\xe2\x80\x99s decision only for an abuse of discretion); Plaut v. Spendthrift Farm, Inc., 514 U. S. 211, 233\xe2\x80\x93234 (1995) (Rule 60(b) \xe2\x80\x9creflects and confirms the courts\xe2\x80\x99 own inherent and discretionary power, \xe2\x80\x98firmly established in English practice long before the foundation of our Republic,\xe2\x80\x99 to set aside a judgment whose enforcement would work inequity\xe2\x80\x9d (citation omitted)). In light of the equitable, often fact-intensive nature of the Rule 60(b) inquiry, it is inappropriate for an appellate court to undertake it in the first instance. This is especially so in this case, in which both the briefing and the record before us are insufficient with regard to the merits issue.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Orderly procedure aside, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s truncated analysis is unsatisfactory. At least in some circumstances, a supervening change in AEDPA procedural law can be the kind of \xe2\x80\x9cextraordinary circumstanc[e],\xe2\x80\x9d Ackermann v. United States, 340 U. S. 193, 199 (1950), that constitutes a \xe2\x80\x9creason justifying relief from the operation of the judgment\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning of Rule 60(b)(6). In this case, the District Court dismissed petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s habeas petition as time barred after concluding that his second motion for state postconviction relief did not toll AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s statute of limitations. See 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72244(d). After that judgment became final, however, we decided Artuz v. Bennett, 531 U. S. 4 (2000), which made clear that the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling on tolling was erroneous and that the habeas petition should therefore not have been dismissed.[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Unfortunately, the Court underestimates the significance of the fact that petitioner was effectively shut out of federal court\xe2\x80\x94without any adjudication of the merits of his claims\xe2\x80\x94because of a procedural ruling that was later shown to be flatly mistaken. As we have stressed, \xe2\x80\x9c[d]ismissal of a first federal habeas petition is a particularly serious matter, for that dismissal denies the petitioner the protections of the Great Writ entirely, risking injury to an important interest in human liberty.\xe2\x80\x9d Lonchar v. Thomas, 517 U. S. 314, 324 (1996); see also Slack v. McDaniel, 529 U. S. 473, 483 (2000) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe writ of habeas corpus plays a vital role in protecting constitutional rights\xe2\x80\x9d). When a habeas petition has been dismissed on a clearly defective procedural ground, the State can hardly claim a legitimate interest in the finality of that judgment. Indeed, the State has experienced a windfall, while the state prisoner has been deprived\xe2\x80\x94contrary to congressional intent\xe2\x80\x94of his valuable right to one full round of federal habeas review.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While this type of supervening change in procedural law may not alone warrant the reopening of a habeas judgment, there may be special factors that allow a prisoner to satisfy the high standard of Rule 60(b)(6). For instance, when a prisoner has shown reasonable diligence in seeking relief based on a change in procedural law, and when that prisoner can show that there is probable merit to his underlying claims, it would be well in keeping with a district court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion under Rule 60(b)(6) for that court to reopen the habeas judgment and give the prisoner the one fair shot at habeas review that Congress intended that he have. After all, we have consistently recognized that Rule 60(b)(6) \xe2\x80\x9cprovides courts with authority \xe2\x80\x98adequate to enable them to vacate judgments whenever such action is appropriate to accomplish justice.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Liljeberg v. Health Services Acquisition Corp., 486 U. S. 847, 864 (1988) (quoting Klapprott v. United States, 335 U. S. 601, 614\xe2\x80\x93615 (1949)). Here, petitioner, who is serving a 99-year term in Florida prison, filed his Rule 60(b) motion approximately eight months after this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Artuz. A district court could reasonably conclude that this period reveals no lack of diligence on the part of an incarcerated pro se litigant.[Footnote 3] And while we have received scant briefing on the probable merit of his petition, his allegation\xe2\x80\x94that his guilty plea was not knowing and voluntary because it was based on grossly inaccurate advice about the actual time he would serve in prison\xe2\x80\x94at least states a colorable claim of a constitutional violation. See Finch v. Vaughn, 67 F. 3d 909 (CA11 1995); see also Mabry v. Johnson, 467 U. S. 504 (1984).[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court relies on petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s supposed lack of diligence in pursuing review of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s initial statute-of-limitations ruling. See ante, at 12. In fact, petitioner did appeal the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling, which the Court of Appeals correctly interpreted as a request for a certificate of appealability (COA).[Footnote 5] As for petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s failure to seek rehearing or certiorari, he alleged in his Rule 60(b) motion, App. 16, and again in his reply brief, that he filed a timely petition for rehearing on April 18, 2000, but that the clerk of the Court of Appeals returned the motion unfiled, \xe2\x80\x9cexplaining, erroneously, that his appeal was dismissed and closed on October 28, 1999.\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief for Petitioner 13 (emphasis deleted). According to petitioner, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]his official misinformation carried the weight of a court decision and was enough to convince a pro se litigant (and some lawyers) that the 90-day window for filing a certiorari petition expired, as well.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The State, however, represents that petitioner erroneously filed the petition for rehearing under the case number of an earlier, dismissed appeal. Brief for Respondent 4. I do not know how to resolve these allegations, but this only highlights the propriety of a remand. Even on the State\xe2\x80\x99s version of events, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s attempt at filing for rehearing is proof of diligence on his part.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Putting these allegations aside, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning is too parsimonious. While petitioner could have shown even greater diligence by seeking rehearing for a second time and then filing for certiorari, we have never held pro se prisoners to the standards of counseled litigants. See, e.g., Haines v. Kerner, 404 U. S. 519 (1972) (per curiam). Indeed, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s situation contrasts dramatically with that of the movant in the case the Court relies on, Ackermann v. United States, 340 U. S. 193 (1950). See ante, at 13. In upholding the denial of Rule 60(b)(6) relief in Ackermann, the Court put great emphasis on the fact that the movant had the benefit of paid counsel and that, for much of the relevant period, he was not detained, but rather enjoyed \xe2\x80\x9cfreedom of movement and action,\xe2\x80\x9d 340 U. S., at 201.[Footnote 6] In any event, I believe that our rules governing prisoner litigation should favor a policy of repose rather than a policy that encourages multiple filings with a low probability of success.[Footnote 7]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, I agree with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that petitioner filed a \xe2\x80\x9ctrue\xe2\x80\x9d Rule 60(b) motion. I respectfully dissent, however, because of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to rule on the merits of the motion in the first instance.
