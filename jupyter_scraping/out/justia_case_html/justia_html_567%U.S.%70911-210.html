\n\t\t\t\tUNITED STATES v. ALVAREZ\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 11\xe2\x80\x93210.\xe2\x80\x83Argued February 22, 2012\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 28, 2012\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe Stolen Valor Act makes it a crime to falsely claim receipt of military decorations or medals and provides an enhanced penalty if the Congressional Medal of Honor is involved. 18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7704 (b), (c). Respondent pleaded guilty to a charge of falsely claiming that he had received the Medal of Honor, but reserved his right to appeal his claim that the Act is unconstitutional. The Ninth Circuit reversed, finding the Act invalid under the \n \nFirst Amendment.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0The judgment is affirmed. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x88\x9218.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t617 F. 3d 1198, affirmed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Kennedy, joined by The Chief Justice, Justice Ginsburg, and Justice Sotomayor, concluded that the Act infringes upon speech protected by the \n \nFirst Amendment. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9318.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0The Constitution \xe2\x80\x9cdemands that content-based restrictions on speech be presumed invalid .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and that the Government bear the burden of showing their constitutionality.\xe2\x80\x9d Ashcroft v. American Civil Liberties Union, \n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 656\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tContent-based restrictions on speech have been permitted only for a few historic categories of speech, including incitement, obscenity, defamation, speech integral to criminal conduct, so-called \xe2\x80\x9cfighting words,\xe2\x80\x9d child pornography, fraud, true threats, and speech presenting some grave and imminent threat the Government has the power to prevent.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAbsent from these few categories is any general exception for false statements. The Government argues that cases such as Hustler Magazine, Inc., v. Falwell, \n\n485 U.\xc2\xa0S. 46\n, support its claim that false statements have no value and hence no \n \nFirst Amendment protection. But all the Government\xe2\x80\x99s quotations derive from cases discussing defamation, fraud, or some other legally cognizable harm associated with a false statement. In those decisions the falsity of the speech at issue was not irrelevant to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis, but neither was it determinative. These prior decisions have not confronted a measure, like the Stolen Valor Act, that targets falsity and nothing more.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEven when considering some instances of defamation or fraud, the Court has instructed that falsity alone may not suffice to bring the speech outside the \n \nFirst Amendment; the statement must be a knowing and reckless falsehood. See New York Times v. Sullivan, \n\n376 U.\xc2\xa0S. 254\n. Here, the Government seeks to convert a rule that limits liability even in defamation cases where the law permits recovery for tortious wrongs into a rule that expands liability in a different, far greater realm of discourse and expression.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government\xe2\x80\x99s three examples of false-speech regulation that courts generally have found permissible do not establish a principle that all proscriptions of false statements are exempt from rigorous \n \nFirst Amendment scrutiny. The criminal prohibition of a false statement made to Government officials in communications concerning official matters, \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71001, does not lead to the broader proposition that false statements are unprotected when made to any person, at any time, in any context. As for perjury statutes, perjured statements lack \n \nFirst Amendment protection not simply because they are false, but because perjury undermines the function and province of the law and threatens the integrity of judgments. Finally, there are statutes that prohibit falsely representing that one is speaking on behalf of the Government, or prohibit impersonating a Government officer. These examples, to the extent that they implicate fraud or speech integral to criminal conduct, are inapplicable here.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhile there may exist \xe2\x80\x9csome categories of speech that have been historically unprotected,\xe2\x80\x9d but that the Court has not yet specifically identified or discussed, United States v. Stevens, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, the Government has not demonstrated that false statements should constitute a new category. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x88\x9210.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0The Act seeks to control and suppress all false statements on this one subject in almost limitless times and settings without regard to whether the lie was made for the purpose of material gain. Permitting the Government to decree this speech to be a criminal offense would endorse government authority to compile a list of subjects about which false statements are punishable. That governmental power has no clear limiting principle. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x88\x9211.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0The Court applies the \xe2\x80\x9cmost exacting scrutiny\xe2\x80\x9d in assessing content-based restrictions on protected speech. Turner Broadcasting System Inc. v. FCC, \n\n512 U.\xc2\xa0S. 622\n. The Act does not satisfy that scrutiny. While the Government\xe2\x80\x99s interest in protecting the integrity of the Medal of Honor is beyond question, the \n \nFirst Amendment requires that there be a direct causal link between the restriction imposed and the injury to be prevented. Here, that link has not been shown. The Government points to no evidence supporting its claim that the public\xe2\x80\x99s general perception of military awards is diluted by false claims such as those made by respondent. And it has not shown, and cannot show, why counterspeech, such as the ridicule respondent received online and in the press, would not suffice to achieve its interest.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn addition, when the Government seeks to regulate protected speech, the restriction must be the \xe2\x80\x9cleast restrictive means among available, effective alternatives.\xe2\x80\x9d Ashcroft, 542 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 666. Here, the Government could likely protect the integrity of the military awards system by creating a database of Medal winners accessible and searchable on the Internet, as some private individuals have already done. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x88\x9218.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Breyer, joined by Justice Kagan, concluded that because the Stolen Valor Act, as presently drafted, works disproportionate constitutional harm, it fails intermediate scrutiny, and thus violates the \n \nFirst Amendment. Pp. 1\xe2\x88\x9210.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0In determining whether a statute violates the \n \nFirst Amendment, the Court has often found it appropriate to examine the fit between statutory ends and means, taking into account the seriousness of the speech-related harm the provision will likely cause, the nature and importance of the provision\xe2\x80\x99s countervailing objectives, the extent to which the statute will tend to achieve those objectives, and whether there are other, less restrictive alternatives. \xe2\x80\x9cIntermediate scrutiny\xe2\x80\x9d describes this approach. Since false factual statements are less likely than true factual statements to make a valuable contribution to the marketplace of ideas, and the government often has good reason to prohibit such false speech, but its regulation can threaten speech-related harm, such an approach is applied here. Pp.\xc2\xa01\xe2\x88\x923.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0The Act should be read as criminalizing only false factual statements made with knowledge of their falsity and with intent that they be taken as true. Although the Court has frequently said or implied that false factual statements enjoy little \n \nFirst Amendment protection, see, e.g., Gertz v. Robert Welch, Inc., \n\n418 U.\xc2\xa0S. 323\n, those statements cannot be read to mean \xe2\x80\x9cno protection at all.\xe2\x80\x9d False factual statements serve useful human objectives in many contexts. Moreover, the threat of criminal prosecution for making a false statement can inhibit the speaker from making true statements, thereby \xe2\x80\x9cchilling\xe2\x80\x9d a kind of speech that lies at the \n \nFirst Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s heart. See id., at 340\xe2\x88\x92341. And the pervasiveness of false factual statements provides a weapon to a government broadly empowered to prosecute falsity without more. Those who are unpopular may fear that the government will use that weapon selectively against them.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAlthough there are many statutes and common-law doctrines making the utterance of certain kinds of false statements unlawful, they tend to be narrower than the Act, in that they limit the scope of their application in various ways, for example, by requiring proof of specific harm to identifiable victims. The Act lacks any such limiting features. Although it prohibits only knowing and intentional falsehoods about readily verifiable facts within the personal knowledge of the speaker, it otherwise ranges broadly, and that breadth means that it creates a significant risk of \n \nFirst Amendment harm. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x88\x928.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0The Act nonetheless has substantial justification. It seeks to protect the interests of those who have sacrificed their health and life for their country by seeking to preserve intact the country\xe2\x80\x99s recognition of that sacrifice in the form of military honors. P.\xc2\xa08.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(d)\xc2\xa0It may, however, be possible substantially to achieve the Government\xe2\x80\x99s objective in less burdensome ways. The \n \nFirst Amendment risks flowing from the Act\xe2\x80\x99s breadth of coverage could be diminished or eliminated by a more finely tailored statute, for example, a statute that requires a showing that the false statement caused specific harm or is focused on lies more likely to be harmful or on contexts where such lies are likely to cause harm. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x88\x9210.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tUNITED STATES, PETITIONER v. XAVIER ALVAREZ\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\ton writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t[June 28, 2012]\n\t\t\t

\n

\n

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Kennedy announced the judgment of the Court and delivered an opinion, in which The Chief Justice, Justice Ginsburg, and Justice Sotomayor join.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tLying was his habit. Xavier Alvarez, the respondent here, lied when he said that he played hockey for the Detroit Red Wings and that he once married a starlet from Mexico. But when he lied in announcing he held the Con-gressional Medal of Honor, respondent ventured onto new ground; for that lie violates a federal criminal statute, the Stolen Valor Act of 2005. \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7704.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 2007, respondent attended his first public meeting as a board member of the Three Valley Water District Board. The board is a governmental entity with headquarters in Claremont, California. He introduced himself as follows: \xe2\x80\x9cI\xe2\x80\x99m a retired marine of 25 years. I retired in the year 2001. Back in 1987, I was awarded the Congressional Medal of Honor. I got wounded many times by the same guy.\xe2\x80\x9d 617 F.\xc2\xa03d 1198, 1201\xe2\x80\x931202 (CA9 2010). None of this was true. For all the record shows, respondent\xe2\x80\x99s statements were but a pathetic attempt to gain respect that eluded him. The statements do not seem to have been made to secure employment or financial benefits or admission to privileges reserved for those who had earned the Medal.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondent was indicted under the Stolen Valor Act for lying about the Congressional Medal of Honor at the meeting. The United States District Court for the Central District of California rejected his claim that the statute is invalid under the \n \nFirst Amendment. Respondent pleaded guilty to one count, reserving the right to appeal on his \n \nFirst Amendment claim. The United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit, in a decision by a divided panel, found the Act invalid under the \n \nFirst Amendment and reversed the conviction. Id., at 1218. With further opinions on the issue, and over a dissent by seven judges, rehearing en banc was denied. 638 F.\xc2\xa03d 666 (2011). This Court granted certiorari. 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2011).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAfter certiorari was granted, and in an unrelated case, the United States Court of Appeals for the Tenth Circuit, also in a decision by a divided panel, found the Act constitutional. United States v. Strandlof, 667 F.\xc2\xa03d 1146 (2012). So there is now a conflict in the Courts of Appeals on the question of the Act\xe2\x80\x99s validity.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis is the second case in two Terms requiring the Court to consider speech that can disparage, or attempt to steal, honor that belongs to those who fought for this Nation in battle. See Snyder v. Phelps, 562 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2011) (hateful protests directed at the funeral of a serviceman who died in Iraq). Here the statement that the speaker held the Medal was an intended, undoubted lie.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is right and proper that Congress, over a century ago, established an award so the Nation can hold in its high- est respect and esteem those who, in the course of carrying out the \xe2\x80\x9csupreme and noble duty of contributing to the defense of the rights and honor of the nation,\xe2\x80\x9d Selective Draft Law Cases, \n\n245 U.\xc2\xa0S. 366, \n390 (1918)\n, have acted with extraordinary honor. And it should be uncontested that this is a legitimate Government objective, indeed a most valued national aspiration and purpose. This does not end the inquiry, however. Fundamental constitutional principles require that laws enacted to honor the brave must be consistent with the precepts of the Constitution for which they fought.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government contends the criminal prohibition is a proper means to further its purpose in creating and awarding the Medal. When content-based speech regulation is in question, however, exacting scrutiny is required. Statutes suppressing or restricting speech must be judged by the sometimes inconvenient principles of the \n \nFirst Amendment. By this measure, the statutory provisions under which respondent was convicted must be held invalid, and his conviction must be set aside.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondent\xe2\x80\x99s claim to hold the Congressional Medal of Honor was false. There is no room to argue about in-terpretation or shades of meaning. On this premise, respondent violated \xc2\xa7704(b); and, because the lie concerned the Congressional Medal of Honor, he was subject to an enhanced penalty under subsection (c). Those statutory provisions are as follows:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c(b) False Claims About Receipt of Military Decorations or Medals.\xe2\x80\x93\xe2\x80\x93Whoever falsely represents himself or herself, verbally or in writing, to have been awarded any decoration or medal authorized by Congress for the Armed Forces of the United States .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall be fined under this title, imprisoned not more than six months, or both.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c(c) Enhanced Penalty for Offenses Involving Congressional Medal of Honor.\xe2\x80\x93\xe2\x80\x93\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c(1) In General.\xe2\x80\x93\xe2\x80\x93If a decoration or medal involved in an offense under subsection (a) or (b) is a Congressional Medal of Honor, in lieu of the punishment provided in that subsection, the offender shall be fined under this title, imprisoned not more than 1 year, or both.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondent challenges the statute as a content-based suppression of pure speech, speech not falling within any of the few categories of expression where content-based regulation is permissible. The Government defends the statute as necessary to preserve the integrity and purpose of the Medal, an integrity and purpose it contends are compromised and frustrated by the false statements the statute prohibits. It argues that false statements \xe2\x80\x9chave no \n \nFirst Amendment value in themselves,\xe2\x80\x9d and thus \xe2\x80\x9care protected only to the extent needed to avoid chilling fully protected speech.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 18, 20. Al-though the statute covers respondent\xe2\x80\x99s speech, the Government argues that it leaves breathing room for pro-tected speech, for example speech which might criticize the idea of the Medal or the importance of the military. The Government\xe2\x80\x99s arguments cannot suffice to save the statute.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c[A]s a general matter, the \n \nFirst Amendment means that government has no power to restrict expression because of its message, its ideas, its subject matter, or its content.\xe2\x80\x9d Ashcroft v. American Civil Liberties Union, \n\n535 U.\xc2\xa0S. 564, \n573 (2002)\n (internal quotation marks omitted). As a result, the Constitution \xe2\x80\x9cdemands that content-based restrictions on speech be presumed invalid .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and that the Government bear the burden of showing their constitutionality.\xe2\x80\x9d Ashcroft v. American Civil Liberties Union, \n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 656, \n660 (2004)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn light of the substantial and expansive threats to free expression posed by content-based restrictions, this Court has rejected as \xe2\x80\x9cstartling and dangerous\xe2\x80\x9d a \xe2\x80\x9cfree-floating test for \n \nFirst Amendment coverage .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [based on] an ad\xc2\xa0hoc balancing of relative social costs and benefits.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Stevens, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 7). Instead, content-based restrictions on speech have been permitted, as a general matter, only when confined to the few \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98historic and traditional categories [of expression] long familiar to the bar,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 5) (quoting Simon & Schuster, Inc. v. Members of N. Y. State Crime Victims Bd., \n\n502 U.\xc2\xa0S. 105, \n127 (1991)\n (Kennedy, J., concurring in judgment)). Among these categories are advocacy intended, and likely, to incite imminent lawless action, see Brandenburg v. Ohio, \n\n395 U.\xc2\xa0S. 444 (1969)\n (per curiam); obscenity, see, e.g., Miller v. California, \n\n413 U.\xc2\xa0S. 15 (1973)\n; defamation, see, e.g., New York Times Co. v. Sullivan, \n\n376 U.\xc2\xa0S. 254 (1964)\n (providing substantial protection for speech about public figures); Gertz v. Robert Welch, Inc., \n\n418 U.\xc2\xa0S. 323 (1974)\n (imposing some limits on liability for defaming a private figure); speech integral to criminal conduct, see, e.g., Giboney v. Empire Storage & Ice Co., \n\n336 U.\xc2\xa0S. 490 (1949)\n; so-called \xe2\x80\x9cfighting words,\xe2\x80\x9d see Chaplinsky v. New Hampshire, \n\n315 U.\xc2\xa0S. 568 (1942)\n; child pornography, see New York v. Ferber, \n\n458 U.\xc2\xa0S. 747 (1982)\n; fraud, see Virginia Bd. of Pharmacy v. Virginia Citizens Consumer Council, Inc., \n\n425 U.\xc2\xa0S. 748, \n771 (1976)\n; true threats, see Watts v. United States, \n\n394 U.\xc2\xa0S. 705 (1969)\n (per curiam); and speech presenting some grave and imminent threat the government has the power to prevent, see Near v. Minnesota ex rel. Olson, \n\n283 U.\xc2\xa0S. 697, \n716 (1931)\n, although a restriction under the last category is most difficult to sustain, see New York Times Co. v. United States, \n\n403 U.\xc2\xa0S. 713 (1971)\n (per curiam). These categories have a historical foundation in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s free speech tradition. The vast realm of free speech and thought always protected in our tradition can still thrive, and even be furthered, by adherence to those categories and rules.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAbsent from those few categories where the law allows content-based regulation of speech is any general exception to the \n \nFirst Amendment for false statements. This comports with the common understanding that some false statements are inevitable if there is to be an open and vigorous expression of views in public and private con-versation, expression the \n \nFirst Amendment seeks to guarantee. See Sullivan, supra, at 271 (\xe2\x80\x9cTh[e] erroneous statement is inevitable in free debate\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government disagrees with this proposition. It cites language from some of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents to support its contention that false statements have no value and hence no \n \nFirst Amendment protection. See also Brief for Eugene Volokh et al. as Amici Curiae 2\xe2\x80\x9311. These isolated statements in some earlier decisions do not support the Government\xe2\x80\x99s submission that false statements, as a general rule, are beyond constitutional protection. That conclusion would take the quoted language far from its proper context. For instance, the Court has stated \xe2\x80\x9c[f]alse statements of fact are particularly valueless [because] they interfere with the truth-seeking function of the marketplace of ideas,\xe2\x80\x9d Hustler Magazine, Inc. v. Falwell, \n\n485 U.\xc2\xa0S. 46, \n52 (1988)\n, and that false statements \xe2\x80\x9care not protected by the \n \nFirst Amendment in the same manner as truthful statements,\xe2\x80\x9d Brown v. Hartlage, \n\n456 U.\xc2\xa0S. 45\n\xe2\x80\x9361 (1982). See also, e.g., Virginia Bd. of Pharmacy, supra, at 771 (\xe2\x80\x9cUntruthful speech, commercial or otherwise, has never been protected for its own sake\xe2\x80\x9d); Herbert v. Lando, \n\n441 U.\xc2\xa0S. 153, \n171 (1979)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cSpreading false information in and of itself carries no \n \nFirst Amendment credentials\xe2\x80\x9d); Gertz, supra, at 340 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]here is no constitutional value in false statements of fact\xe2\x80\x9d); Garrison v. Louisiana, \n\n379 U.\xc2\xa0S. 64, \n75 (1964)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he knowingly false statement and the false statement made with reckless disregard of the truth, do not enjoy constitutional protection\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThese quotations all derive from cases discussing def-amation, fraud, or some other legally cognizable harm associated with a false statement, such as an invasion of privacy or the costs of vexatious litigation. See Brief for United States 18\xe2\x80\x9319. In those decisions the falsity of the speech at issue was not irrelevant to our analysis, but neither was it determinative. The Court has never endorsed the categorical rule the Government advances: that false statements receive no \n \nFirst Amendment protection. Our prior decisions have not confronted a measure, like the Stolen Valor Act, that targets falsity and nothing more.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEven when considering some instances of defamation and fraud, moreover, the Court has been careful to instruct that falsity alone may not suffice to bring the speech outside the \n \nFirst Amendment. The statement must be a knowing or reckless falsehood. See Sullivan, supra, at 280 (prohibiting recovery of damages for a defamatory falsehood made about a public official unless the statement was made \xe2\x80\x9cwith knowledge that it was false or with reckless disregard of whether it was false or not\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Garrison, supra, at 73 (\xe2\x80\x9c[E]ven when the utterance is false, the great principles of the Constitution which secure freedom of expression .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. preclude attaching adverse consequences to any except the knowing or reckless falsehood\xe2\x80\x9d); Illinois ex rel. Madigan v. Telemarketing Associates, Inc., \n\n538 U.\xc2\xa0S. 600, \n620 (2003)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cFalse statement alone does not subject a fundraiser to fraud liability\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government thus seeks to use this principle for a new purpose. It seeks to convert a rule that limits liability even in defamation cases where the law permits recovery for tortious wrongs into a rule that expands liability in a different, far greater realm of discourse and expression. That inverts the rationale for the exception. The requirements of a knowing falsehood or reckless disregard for the truth as the condition for recovery in certain defamation cases exists to allow more speech, not less. A rule designed to tolerate certain speech ought not blossom to become a rationale for a rule restricting it.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government then gives three examples of regulations on false speech that courts generally have found per-missible: first, the criminal prohibition of a false statement made to a Government official, \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71001; second, laws punishing perjury; and third, prohibi-tions on the false representation that one is speaking as a Government official or on behalf of the Government, see, e.g., \xc2\xa7912; \xc2\xa7709. These restrictions, however, do not establish a principle that all proscriptions of false statements are exempt from exacting \n \nFirst Amendment scrutiny.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe federal statute prohibiting false statements to Government officials punishes \xe2\x80\x9cwhoever, in any matter within the jurisdiction of the executive, legislative, or judicial branch of the Government .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. makes any mate-rially false, fictitious, or fraudulent statement or repre-sentation.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71001. Section 1001\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition on false statements made to Government officials, in communications concerning official matters, does not lead to the broader proposition that false statements are unprotected when made to any person, at any time, in any context.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe same point can be made about what the Court has confirmed is the \xe2\x80\x9cunquestioned constitutionality of perjury statutes,\xe2\x80\x9d both the federal statute, \xc2\xa71623, and its state-law equivalents. United States v. Grayson, \n\n438 U.\xc2\xa0S. 41, \n54 (1978)\n. See also Konigsberg v. State Bar of Cal., \n\n366 U.\xc2\xa0S. 36\n, n.\xc2\xa010 (1961). It is not simply because perjured statements are false that they lack \n \nFirst Amendment protection. Perjured testimony \xe2\x80\x9cis at war with justice\xe2\x80\x9d because it can cause a court to render a \xe2\x80\x9cjudgment not resting on truth.\xe2\x80\x9d In re Michael, \n\n326 U.\xc2\xa0S. 224, \n227 (1945)\n. Perjury undermines the function and province of the law and threatens the integrity of judgments that are the basis of the legal system. See United States v. Dunnigan, \n\n507 U.\xc2\xa0S. 87, \n97 (1993)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cTo uphold the integrity of our trial system .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the constitutionality of perjury statutes is unquestioned\xe2\x80\x9d). Unlike speech in other contexts, testi-mony under oath has the formality and gravity necessary to remind the witness that his or her statements will be the basis for official governmental action, action that often affects the rights and liberties of others. Sworn testimony is quite distinct from lies not spoken under oath and sim-ply intended to puff up oneself.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tStatutes that prohibit falsely representing that one is speaking on behalf of the Government, or that prohibit im-personating a Government officer, also protect the integrity of Government processes, quite apart from merely restricting false speech. Title \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7912, for ex-ample, prohibits impersonating an officer or employee of the United States. Even if that statute may not require proving an \xe2\x80\x9cactual financial or property loss\xe2\x80\x9d resulting from the deception, the statute is itself confined to \xe2\x80\x9cmaintain[ing] the general good repute and dignity of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. government .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. service itself.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Lepowitch, \n\n318 U.\xc2\xa0S. 702, \n704 (1943)\n (internal quotation marks omitted). The same can be said for prohibitions on the unauthorized use of the names of federal agencies such as the Federal Bureau of Investigation in a manner calculated to convey that the communication is approved, see \xc2\xa7709, or using words such as \xe2\x80\x9cFederal\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cUnited States\xe2\x80\x9d in the collection of private debts in order to convey that the communication has official authorization, see \xc2\xa7712. These examples, to the extent that they implicate fraud or speech integral to criminal conduct, are inapplicable here.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs our law and tradition show, then, there are instances in which the falsity of speech bears upon whether it is protected. Some false speech may be prohibited even if analogous true speech could not be. This opinion does not imply that any of these targeted prohibitions are somehow vulnerable. But it also rejects the notion that false speech should be in a general category that is presumptively unprotected.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAlthough the \n \nFirst Amendment stands against any \xe2\x80\x9cfreewheeling authority to declare new categories of speech outside the scope of the \n \nFirst Amendment,\xe2\x80\x9d Stevens, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 9), the Court has acknowledged that perhaps there exist \xe2\x80\x9csome categories of speech that have been historically unprotected .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. but have not yet been specifically identified or discussed .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in our case law.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Before exempting a category of speech from the normal prohibition on content-based re-strictions, however, the Court must be presented with \xe2\x80\x9cper-suasive evidence that a novel restriction on content is part of a long (if heretofore unrecognized) tradition of proscription,\xe2\x80\x9d Brown v. Entertainment Merchants Assn., 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 4). The Government has not demonstrated that false statements generally should constitute a new category of unprotected speech on this basis.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe probable, and adverse, effect of the Act on free- dom of expression illustrates, in a fundamental way, the reasons for the Law\xe2\x80\x99s distrust of content-based speech prohibitions.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Act by its plain terms applies to a false statement made at any time, in any place, to any person. It can be assumed that it would not apply to, say, a theatrical performance. See Milkovich v. Lorain Journal Co., \n\n497 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1, \n20 (1990)\n (recognizing that some statements nominally purporting to contain false facts in reality \xe2\x80\x9ccannot reasonably be interpreted as stating actual facts about an individual\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks and brackets omitted)). Still, the sweeping, quite unprecedented reach of the statute puts it in conflict with the \n \nFirst Amendment. Here the lie was made in a public meeting, but the statute would apply with equal force to personal, whispered conversations within a home. The statute seeks to control and suppress all false statements on this one subject in almost limitless times and settings. And it does so en-tirely without regard to whether the lie was made for the purpose of material gain. See San Francisco Arts & Athletics, Inc. v. United States Olympic Comm., \n\n483 U.\xc2\xa0S. 522\n\xe2\x80\x93540 (1987) (prohibiting a nonprofit corporation from exploiting the \xe2\x80\x9ccommercial magnetism\xe2\x80\x9d of the word \xe2\x80\x9cOlym-pic\xe2\x80\x9d when organizing an athletic competition (internal quotation marks omitted)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPermitting the government to decree this speech to be a criminal offense, whether shouted from the rooftops or made in a barely audible whisper, would endorse government authority to compile a list of subjects about which false statements are punishable. That governmental power has no clear limiting principle. Our constitutional tradition stands against the idea that we need Oceania\xe2\x80\x99s Ministry of Truth. See G. Orwell, Nineteen Eighty-Four (1949) (Centennial ed. 2003). Were this law to be sustained, there could be an endless list of subjects the National Government or the States could single out. Where false claims are made to effect a fraud or secure moneys or other valuable considerations, say offers of employment, it is well established that the Government may restrict speech without affronting the \n \nFirst Amendment. See, e.g., Virginia Bd. of Pharmacy, 425 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 771 (noting that fraudulent speech generally falls outside the protections of the \n \nFirst Amendment). But the Stolen Valor Act is not so limited in its reach. Were the Court to hold that the interest in truthful discourse alone is sufficient to sustain a ban on speech, absent any evidence that the speech was used to gain a material advantage, it would give government a broad censorial power unprecedented in this Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases or in our constitutional tradition. The mere potential for the exercise of that power casts a chill, a chill the \n \nFirst Amendment cannot permit if free speech, thought, and discourse are to remain a foundation of our freedom.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIV\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe previous discussion suffices to show that the Act conflicts with free speech principles. But even when examined within its own narrow sphere of operation, the Act cannot survive. In assessing content-based restrictions on protected speech, the Court has not adopted a free-wheeling approach, see Stevens, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 7) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe \n \nFirst Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s guarantee of free speech does not extend only to categories of speech that survive an ad\xc2\xa0hoc balancing of relative social costs and benefits\xe2\x80\x9d), but rather has applied the \xe2\x80\x9cmost exacting scrutiny.\xe2\x80\x9d Turner Broadcasting System, Inc. v. FCC, \n\n512 U.\xc2\xa0S. 622, \n642 (1994)\n. Although the objectives the Government seeks to further by the statute are not without significance, the Court must, and now does, find the Act does not satisfy exacting scrutiny.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government is correct when it states military medals \xe2\x80\x9cserve the important public function of recognizing and expressing gratitude for acts of heroism and sacrifice in military service,\xe2\x80\x9d and also \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98foste[r] morale, mission accomplishment and esprit de corps\xe2\x80\x99 among service members.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 37, 38. General George Washington observed that an award for valor would \xe2\x80\x9ccherish a virtuous ambition in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. soldiers, as well as foster and encourage every species of military merit.\xe2\x80\x9d General Orders of George Washington Issued at Newburgh on the Hudson, 1782\xe2\x80\x931783 (Aug. 7, 1782), p.\xc2\xa030 (E. Boynton ed. 1883). Time has not diminished this idea. In periods of war and peace alike public recognition of valor and noble sacrifice by men and women in uniform reinforces the pride and national resolve that the military relies upon to fulfill its mission.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThese interests are related to the integrity of the military honors system in general, and the Congressional Medal of Honor in particular. Although millions have served with brave resolve, the Medal, which is the highest military award for valor against an enemy force, has been given just 3,476 times. Established in 1861, the Medal is reserved for those who have distinguished themselves \xe2\x80\x9cconspicuously by gallantry and intrepidity at the risk of his life above and beyond the call of duty.\xe2\x80\x9d 10 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73741 (Army), 6241 (Navy and Marine Corps), 8741 (Air Force), \n\n14 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7491 (Coast Guard). The stories of those who earned the Medal inspire and fascinate, from Dakota Meyer who in 2009 drove five times into the midst of a Taliban ambush to save 36 lives, see Curtis, President Obama Awards Medal of Honor to Dakota Meyer, The White House Blog (Sept. 15, 2011) (all Internet materials as visited June 25, 2012, and available in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file); to Desmond Doss who served as an army medic on Okinawa and on June 5, 1945, rescued 75 fellow soldiers, and who, after being wounded, gave up his own place on a stretcher so others could be taken to safety, see America\xe2\x80\x99s Heroes 88\xe2\x80\x9390 (J. Willbanks ed. 2011); to William Carney who sustained multiple gunshot wounds to the head, chest, legs, and arm, and yet carried the flag to ensure it did not touch the ground during the Union army\xe2\x80\x99s assault on Fort Wagner in July 1863, id., at 44\xe2\x80\x9345. The rare acts of courage the Medal celebrates led President Truman to say he would \xe2\x80\x9crather have that medal round my neck than .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. be president of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Truman Gives No. 1 Army Medal to 15 Heroes, Washington Post, Oct. 13, 1945, p.\xc2\xa05. The Government\xe2\x80\x99s interest in protecting the integrity of the Medal of Honor is beyond question.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut to recite the Government\xe2\x80\x99s compelling interests is not to end the matter. The \n \nFirst Amendment requires that the Government\xe2\x80\x99s chosen restriction on the speech at issue be \xe2\x80\x9cactually necessary\xe2\x80\x9d to achieve its interest. En-tertainment Merchants Assn., 564 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 12). There must be a direct causal link between the restriction imposed and the injury to be prevented. See ibid. The link between the Government\xe2\x80\x99s interest in protecting the integrity of the military honors system and the Act\xe2\x80\x99s restriction on the false claims of liars like respondent has not been shown. Although appearing to concede that \xe2\x80\x9can isolated misrepresentation by itself would not tarnish the meaning of military honors,\xe2\x80\x9d the Government asserts it is \xe2\x80\x9ccommon sense that false representations have the tendency to dilute the value and meaning of military awards,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 49, 54. It must be acknowledged that when a pretender claims the Medal to be his own, the lie might harm the Government by demeaning the high purpose of the award, diminishing the honor it confirms, and creating the appearance that the Medal is awarded more often than is true. Furthermore, the lie may offend the true holders of the Medal. From one perspective it in-sults their bravery and high principles when falsehood puts them in the unworthy company of a pretender.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tYet these interests do not satisfy the Government\xe2\x80\x99s heavy burden when it seeks to regulate protected speech. See United States v. Playboy Entertainment Group, Inc., \n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 803, \n818 (2000)\n. The Government points to no evidence to support its claim that the public\xe2\x80\x99s general perception of military awards is diluted by false claims such as those made by Alvarez. Cf. Entertainment Merchants Assn., supra, at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 12\xe2\x80\x9313) (analyzing and rejecting the findings of research psychologists demonstrating the causal link between violent video games and harmful effects on children). As one of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s amici notes \xe2\x80\x9cthere is nothing that charlatans such as Xavier Alvarez can do to stain [the Medal winners\xe2\x80\x99] honor.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Veterans of Foreign Wars of the United States et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 1. This general proposition is sound, even if true holders of the Medal might experience anger and frustration.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe lack of a causal link between the Government\xe2\x80\x99s stated interest and the Act is not the only way in which the Act is not actually necessary to achieve the Government\xe2\x80\x99s stated interest. The Government has not shown, and cannot show, why counterspeech would not suffice to achieve its interest. The facts of this case indicate that the dynamics of free speech, of counterspeech, of refutation, can overcome the lie. Respondent lied at a public meeting. Even before the FBI began investigating him for his false statements \xe2\x80\x9cAlvarez was perceived as a phony,\xe2\x80\x9d 617 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1211. Once the lie was made public, he was ridiculed online, see Brief for Respondent 3, his actions were reported in the press, see Ortega, Alvarez Again Denies Claim, Ontario, CA, Inland Valley Daily Bulletin (Sept. 27, 2007), and a fellow board member called for his resignation, see, e.g., Bigham, Water District Rep Requests Alvarez Resign in Wake of False Medal Claim, San Bernardino Cty., CA, The Sun (May 21, 2008). There is good reason to believe that a similar fate would befall other false claimants. See Brief for Reporters Committee for Freedom of the Press et al. as Amici Curiae 30\xe2\x80\x9333 (listing numerous examples of public exposure of false claimants). Indeed, the outrage and contempt expressed for respondent\xe2\x80\x99s lies can serve to reawaken and reinforce the public\xe2\x80\x99s respect for the Medal, its recipients, and its high purpose. The acclaim that recipients of the Congressional Medal of Honor receive also casts doubt on the proposition that the public will be misled by the claims of charlatans or become cynical of those whose heroic deeds earned them the Medal by right. See, e.g., Well Done, Washington Post, Feb. 5, 1943, p.\xc2\xa08 (reporting on Pres-ident Roosevelt\xe2\x80\x99s awarding the Congressional Medal of Honor to Maj. Gen. Alexander Vandegrift); Devroy, Medal of Honor Given to 2 Killed in Somalia, Washington Post, May 24, 1994, p.\xc2\xa0A6 (reporting on President Clinton\xe2\x80\x99s awarding the Congressional Medal of Honor to two special forces soldiers killed during operations in Somalia).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe remedy for speech that is false is speech that is true. This is the ordinary course in a free society. The response to the unreasoned is the rational; to the uninformed, the enlightened; to the straight-out lie, the simple truth. See Whitney v. California, \n\n274 U.\xc2\xa0S. 357, \n377 (1927)\n (Brandeis, J., concurring) (\xe2\x80\x9cIf there be time to expose through discussion the falsehood and fallacies, to avert the evil by the processes of education, the remedy to be ap-plied is more speech, not enforced silence\xe2\x80\x9d). The theory of our Constitution is \xe2\x80\x9cthat the best test of truth is the power of the thought to get itself accepted in the competition of the market,\xe2\x80\x9d Abrams v. United States, \n\n250 U.\xc2\xa0S. 616, \n630 (1919)\n (Holmes, J., dissenting). The \n \nFirst Amendment itself ensures the right to respond to speech we do not like, and for good reason. Freedom of speech and thought flows not from the beneficence of the state but from the inalienable rights of the person. And suppression of speech by the government can make exposure of falsity more difficult, not less so. Society has the right and civic duty to engage in open, dynamic, rational discourse. These ends are not well served when the government seeks to orchestrate public discussion through content-based mandates.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tExpressing its concern that counterspeech is insuf- ficient, the Government responds that because \xe2\x80\x9csome military records have been lost .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. some claims [are] un-verifiable,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 50. This proves little, however; for without verifiable records, successful criminal prosecution under the Act would be more difficult in any event. So, in cases where public refutation will not serve the Government\xe2\x80\x99s interest, the Act will not either. In addition, the Government claims that \xe2\x80\x9cmany [false claims] will remain unchallenged.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 55. The Government provides no support for the contention. And in any event, in order to show that public refutation is not an adequate alternative, the Government must demonstrate that unchallenged claims undermine the public\xe2\x80\x99s perception of the military and the integrity of its awards system. This showing has not been made.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is a fair assumption that any true holders of the Medal who had heard of Alvarez\xe2\x80\x99s false claims would have been fully vindicated by the community\xe2\x80\x99s expression of outrage, showing as it did the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s high regard for the Medal. The same can be said for the Government\xe2\x80\x99s interest. The American people do not need the assistance of a government prosecution to express their high regard for the special place that military heroes hold in our tradi-tion. Only a weak society needs government protection or intervention before it pursues its resolve to preserve the truth. Truth needs neither handcuffs nor a badge for its vindication.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn addition, when the Government seeks to regulate protected speech, the restriction must be the \xe2\x80\x9cleast restrictive means among available, effective alternatives.\xe2\x80\x9d Ashcroft, 542 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 666. There is, however, at least one less speech-restrictive means by which the Government could likely protect the integrity of the military awards system.\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t A Government-created database could list Congressional Medal of Honor winners. Were a database accessible through the Internet, it would be easy to verify and expose false claims. It appears some private individuals have already created databases similar to this, see Brief for Respondent 25, and at least one data- base of past winners is online and fully searchable, see Congressional Medal of Honor Society, Full Archive, http://www.cmohs.org/recipient-archive.php. The Solicitor General responds that although Congress and the Department of Defense investigated the feasibility of establishing a database in 2008, the Government \xe2\x80\x9cconcluded that such a database would be impracticable and insuf-ficiently comprehensive.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 55. Without more explanation, it is difficult to assess the Gov-ernment\xe2\x80\x99s claim, especially when at least one database of Congressional Medal of Honor winners already exists.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government may have responses to some of these criticisms, but there has been no clear showing of the necessity of the statute, the necessity required by exacting scrutiny.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Nation well knows that one of the costs of the \n \nFirst Amendment is that it protects the speech we detest as well as the speech we embrace. Though few might find respondent\xe2\x80\x99s statements anything but contemptible, his right to make those statements is protected by the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s guarantee of freedom of speech and expression. The Stolen Valor Act infringes upon speech protected by the \n \nFirst Amendment.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI agree with the plurality that the Stolen Valor Act of 2005 violates the \n \nFirst Amendment. But I do not rest my conclusion upon a strict categorical analysis. Ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x9310. Rather, I base that conclusion upon the fact that the statute works \n \nFirst Amendment harm, while the Government can achieve its legitimate objectives in less restrictive ways.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn determining whether a statute violates the \n \nFirst Amendment, this Court has often found it appropriate to examine the fit between statutory ends and means. In doing so, it has examined speech-related harms, justifications, and potential alternatives. In particular, it has taken account of the seriousness of the speech-related harm the provision will likely cause, the nature and importance of the provision\xe2\x80\x99s countervailing objectives, the extent to which the provision will tend to achieve those objectives, and whether there are other, less restrictive ways of doing so. Ultimately the Court has had to determine whether the statute works speech-related harm that is out of proportion to its justifications.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSometimes the Court has referred to this approach as \xe2\x80\x9cintermediate scrutiny,\xe2\x80\x9d sometimes as \xe2\x80\x9cproportionality\xe2\x80\x9d review, sometimes as an examination of \xe2\x80\x9cfit,\xe2\x80\x9d and sometimes it has avoided the application of any label at all. See, e.g., Turner Broadcasting System, Inc. v. FCC, \n\n512 U.\xc2\xa0S. 622\n\xe2\x80\x93652 (1994) (intermediate scrutiny); Randall v. Sorrell, \n\n548 U.\xc2\xa0S. 230, \n249 (2006)\n (plurality opinion) (proportionality); Board of Trustees of State Univ. of N.\xc2\xa0Y. v. Fox, \n\n492 U.\xc2\xa0S. 469, \n480 (1989)\n (requiring a \xe2\x80\x9cfit\xe2\x80\x9d be- tween means and ends that is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98in proportion to the in- terest served\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d); In re R. M. J., \n\n455 U.\xc2\xa0S. 191, \n203 (1982)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]nterference with speech must be in proportion to the [substantial governmental] interest served\xe2\x80\x9d); Pickering v. Board of Ed. of Township High School Dist. 205, Will Cty., \n\n391 U.\xc2\xa0S. 563, \n568 (1968)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRegardless of the label, some such approach is necessary if the \n \nFirst Amendment is to offer proper protection in the many instances in which a statute adversely affects constitutionally protected interests but warrants neither near-automatic condemnation (as \xe2\x80\x9cstrict scrutiny\xe2\x80\x9d implies) nor near-automatic approval (as is implicit in \xe2\x80\x9crational basis\xe2\x80\x9d review). See, e.g., Turner Broadcasting System, Inc., supra, at 641\xe2\x80\x93652 (\xe2\x80\x9cmust-carry\xe2\x80\x9d cable regulations); Central Hudson Gas & Elec. Corp. v. Public Serv. Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of N.\xc2\xa0Y., \n\n447 U.\xc2\xa0S. 557, \n566 (1980)\n (nonmisleading commercial speech); Burdick v. Takushi, \n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 428\n\xe2\x80\x93434 (1992) (election regulation); Pickering, supra, at 568 (government employee speech); United States v. O\xe2\x80\x99Brien, \n\n391 U.\xc2\xa0S. 367, \n377 (1968)\n (application of generally appli- cable laws to expressive conduct). I have used the term \xe2\x80\x9cproportionality\xe2\x80\x9d to describe this approach. Thompson v. Western States Medical Center, \n\n535 U.\xc2\xa0S. 357, \n388 (2002)\n (dissenting opinion); see also Bartnicki v. Vopper, \n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 514, \n536 (2001)\n (concurring opinion); Nixon v. Shrink Missouri Government PAC, \n\n528 U.\xc2\xa0S. 377\n\xe2\x80\x93403 (2000) (concurring opinion). But in this case, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s term \xe2\x80\x9cintermediate scrutiny\xe2\x80\x9d describes what I think we should do.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs the dissent points out, \xe2\x80\x9cthere are broad areas in which any attempt by the state to penalize purportedly false speech would present a grave and unacceptable dan- ger of suppressing truthful speech.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 14. Laws restricting false statements about philosophy, religion, history, the social sciences, the arts, and the like raise such concerns, and in many contexts have called for strict scrutiny. But this case does not involve such a law. The dangers of suppressing valuable ideas are lower where, as here, the regulations concern false statements about easily verifiable facts that do not concern such subject matter. Such false factual statements are less likely than are true factual statements to make a valuable contribution to the marketplace of ideas. And the government often has good reasons to prohibit such false speech. See infra, at 5\xe2\x80\x937 (listing examples of statutes and doctrines regulating false factual speech). But its regulation can nonetheless threaten speech-related harms. Those circumstances lead me to apply what the Court has termed \xe2\x80\x9cintermediate scrutiny\xe2\x80\x9d here.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Stolen Valor Act makes it a crime \xe2\x80\x9cfalsely\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9crepresen[t]\xe2\x80\x9d oneself \xe2\x80\x9cto have been awarded any decoration or medal authorized by Congress for the Armed Forces of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7704(b). I would read the statute favorably to the Government as criminalizing only false factual statements made with knowledge of their fal- sity and with the intent that they be taken as true. See Staples v. United States, \n\n511 U.\xc2\xa0S. 600, \n605 (1994)\n (courts construe statutes \xe2\x80\x9cin light of the background rules of the common law, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in which the requirement of some mens rea for a crime is firmly embedded\xe2\x80\x9d); cf. New York Times Co. v. Sullivan, \n\n376 U.\xc2\xa0S. 254\n\xe2\x80\x93280 (1964) (\n \nFirst Amendment allows a public official to recover for defamation only upon a showing of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98actual malice\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). As so interpreted the statute covers only lies. But although this interpretation diminishes the extent to which the statute endangers \n \nFirst Amendment values, it does not eliminate the threat.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI must concede, as the Government points out, that this Court has frequently said or implied that false factual statements enjoy little \n \nFirst Amendment protection. See, e.g., BE&K Constr. Co. v. NLRB, \n\n536 U.\xc2\xa0S. 516, \n531 (2002)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[F]alse statements may be unprotected for their own sake\xe2\x80\x9d); Hustler Magazine, Inc. v. Falwell, \n\n485 U.\xc2\xa0S. 46, \n52 (1988)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cFalse statements of fact are particularly valueless\xe2\x80\x9d); Gertz v. Robert Welch, Inc., \n\n418 U.\xc2\xa0S. 323, \n340 (1974)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he erroneous statement of fact is not worthy of constitutional protection\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut these judicial statements cannot be read to mean \xe2\x80\x9cno protection at all.\xe2\x80\x9d False factual statements can serve useful human objectives, for example: in social contexts, where they may prevent embarrassment, protect privacy, shield a person from prejudice, provide the sick with comfort, or preserve a child\xe2\x80\x99s innocence; in public contexts, where they may stop a panic or otherwise preserve calm in the face of danger; and even in technical, philosophical, and scientific contexts, where (as Socrates\xe2\x80\x99 methods suggest) examination of a false statement (even if made deliberately to mislead) can promote a form of thought that ultimately helps realize the truth. See, e.g., 638 F.\xc2\xa03d 666, 673\xe2\x80\x93675 (CA9 2011) (Kozinski, J., concurring in denial of rehearing en banc) (providing numerous examples); S. Bok, Lying: Moral Choice in Public and Private Life (1999) (same); New York Times Co., supra, at 279, n.\xc2\xa019 (\xe2\x80\x9cEven a false statement may be deemed to make a valuable contribution to public debate, since it brings about \xe2\x80\x98the clearer perception and livelier impression of truth, produced by its collision with error\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting J. Mill, On Liberty 15 (Blackwell ed. 1947))).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMoreover, as the Court has often said, the threat of criminal prosecution for making a false statement can inhibit the speaker from making true statements, thereby \xe2\x80\x9cchilling\xe2\x80\x9d a kind of speech that lies at the \n \nFirst Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s heart. See, e.g., Gertz, supra, at 340\xe2\x80\x93341. Hence, the Court emphasizes mens rea requirements that provide \xe2\x80\x9cbreathing room\xe2\x80\x9d for more valuable speech by reducing an honest speaker\xe2\x80\x99s fear that he may accidentally incur liability for speaking.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFurther, the pervasiveness of false statements, made for better or for worse motives, made thoughtlessly or de- liberately, made with or without accompanying harm, provides a weapon to a government broadly empowered to prosecute falsity without more. And those who are unpopular may fear that the government will use that weapon selectively, say by prosecuting a pacifist who sup- ports his cause by (falsely) claiming to have been a war hero, while ignoring members of other political groups who might make similar false claims.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI also must concede that many statutes and common-law doctrines make the utterance of certain kinds of false statements unlawful. Those prohibitions, however, tend to be narrower than the statute before us, in that they limit the scope of their application, sometimes by requiring proof of specific harm to identifiable victims; sometimes by specifying that the lies be made in contexts in which a tangible harm to others is especially likely to occur; and sometimes by limiting the prohibited lies to those that are particularly likely to produce harm.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFraud statutes, for example, typically require proof of a misrepresentation that is material, upon which the victim relied, and which caused actual injury. See Restatement (Second) of Torts \xc2\xa7525 (1976). Defamation statutes focus upon statements of a kind that harm the reputation of another or deter third parties from association or dealing with the victim. See id., \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7558, 559. Torts involving the intentional infliction of emotional distress (like torts involving placing a victim in a false light) concern falsehoods that tend to cause harm to a specific victim of an emotional-, dignitary-, or privacy-related kind. See id., \xc2\xa7652E.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPerjury statutes prohibit a particular set of false statements\xe2\x80\x94those made under oath\xe2\x80\x94while requiring a showing of materiality. See, e.g., \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71621. Statutes forbidding lying to a government official (not under oath) are typically limited to circumstances where a lie is likely to work particular and specific harm by interfering with the functioning of a government department, and those statutes also require a showing of materiality. See, e.g., \xc2\xa71001.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tStatutes prohibiting false claims of terrorist attacks, or other lies about the commission of crimes or catastrophes, require proof that substantial public harm be directly foreseeable, or, if not, involve false statements that are very likely to bring about that harm. See, e.g., 47 CFR \xc2\xa773.1217 (2011) (requiring showing of foreseeability and actual substantial harm); \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71038(a)(1) (prohibiting knowing false statements claiming that terrorist attacks have taken, are taking, or will take, place).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tStatutes forbidding impersonation of a public official typically focus on acts of impersonation, not mere speech, and may require a showing that, for example, someone was deceived into following a \xe2\x80\x9ccourse [of action] he would not have pursued but for the deceitful conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Lepowitch, \n\n318 U.\xc2\xa0S. 702, \n704 (1943)\n; see, e.g., \xc2\xa7912 (liability attaches to \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hoever falsely assumes or pretends to be an officer or employee acting under the authority of the United States .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and acts as such\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tStatutes prohibiting trademark infringement present, perhaps, the closest analogy to the present statute. Trademarks identify the source of a good; and infringement causes harm by causing confusion among potential customers (about the source) and thereby diluting the value of the mark to its owner, to consumers, and to the econ- omy. Similarly, a false claim of possession of a medal or other honor creates confusion about who is entitled to wear it, thus diluting its value to those who have earned it, to their families, and to their country. But trademark statutes are focused upon commercial and promotional activities that are likely to dilute the value of a mark. Indeed, they typically require a showing of likely confusion, a showing that tends to assure that the feared harm will in fact take place. See \n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71114(1)(a); KP Permanent Make-Up, Inc. v. Lasting Impression I, Inc., \n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 111, \n117 (2004)\n; see also San Francisco Arts & Athletics, Inc. v. United States Olympic Comm., \n\n483 U.\xc2\xa0S. 522\n\xe2\x80\x93540, 548 (1987) (upholding statute giving the United States Olympic Committee the right to prohibit certain commercial and promotional uses of the word \xe2\x80\x9cOlympic\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhile this list is not exhaustive, it is sufficient to show that few statutes, if any, simply prohibit without limitation the telling of a lie, even a lie about one particular matter. Instead, in virtually all these instances limitations of context, requirements of proof of injury, and the like, narrow the statute to a subset of lies where specific harm is more likely to occur. The limitations help to make certain that the statute does not allow its threat of liability or criminal punishment to roam at large, discouraging or forbidding the telling of the lie in contexts where harm is unlikely or the need for the prohibition is small.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe statute before us lacks any such limiting features. It may be construed to prohibit only knowing and intentional acts of deception about readily verifiable facts within the personal knowledge of the speaker, thus reducing the risk that valuable speech is chilled. Supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x934. But it still ranges very broadly. And that breadth means that it creates a significant risk of \n \nFirst Amendment harm. As written, it applies in family, social, or other private contexts, where lies will often cause little harm. It also applies in political contexts, where although such lies are more likely to cause harm, the risk of censorious se- lectivity by prosecutors is also high. Further, given the potential haziness of individual memory along with the large number of military awards covered (ranging from medals for rifle marksmanship to the Congressional Medal of Honor), there remains a risk of chilling that is not completely eliminated by mens rea requirements; a speaker might still be worried about being prosecuted for a careless false statement, even if he does not have the intent required to render him liable. And so the prohibition may be applied where it should not be applied, for example, to bar stool braggadocio or, in the political arena, subtly but selectively to speakers that the Government does not like. These considerations lead me to believe that the statute as written risks significant \n \nFirst Amendment harm.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tLike both the plurality and the dissent, I believe the statute nonetheless has substantial justification. It seeks to protect the interests of those who have sacrificed their health and life for their country. The statute serves this interest by seeking to preserve intact the country\xe2\x80\x99s recognition of that sacrifice in the form of military honors. To permit those who have not earned those honors to claim otherwise dilutes the value of the awards. Indeed, the Nation cannot fully honor those who have sacrificed so much for their country\xe2\x80\x99s honor unless those who claim to have received its military awards tell the truth. Thus, the statute risks harming protected interests but only in order to achieve a substantial countervailing objective.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tC\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe must therefore ask whether it is possible substantially to achieve the Government\xe2\x80\x99s objective in less burdensome ways. In my view, the answer to this question is \xe2\x80\x9cyes.\xe2\x80\x9d Some potential \n \nFirst Amendment threats can be alleviated by interpreting the statute to require knowledge of falsity, etc. Supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x934. But other \n \nFirst Amendment risks, primarily risks flowing from breadth of coverage, remain. Supra, at 4\xe2\x80\x935, 7\xe2\x80\x938. As is indicated by the limitations on the scope of the many other kinds of statutes regulating false factual speech, supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x937, it should be possible significantly to diminish or eliminate these re- maining risks by enacting a similar but more finely tailored statute. For example, not all military awards are alike. Congress might determine that some warrant greater protection than others. And a more finely tailored statute might, as other kinds of statutes prohibiting false factual statements have done, insist upon a showing that the false statement caused specific harm or at least was material, or focus its coverage on lies most likely to be harmful or on contexts where such lies are most likely to cause harm.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI recognize that in some contexts, particularly political contexts, such a narrowing will not always be easy to achieve. In the political arena a false statement is more likely to make a behavioral difference (say, by leading the listeners to vote for the speaker) but at the same time criminal prosecution is particularly dangerous (say, by radically changing a potential election result) and consequently can more easily result in censorship of speakers and their ideas. Thus, the statute may have to be significantly narrowed in its applications. Some lower courts have upheld the constitutionality of roughly comparable but narrowly tailored statutes in political contexts. See, e.g., United We Stand America, Inc. v. United We Stand, America New York, Inc., 128 F.\xc2\xa03d 86, 93 (CA2 1997) (upholding against \n \nFirst Amendment challenge application of Lanham Act to a political organization); Treasure of the Committee to Elect Gerald D. Lostracco v. Fox, 150 Mich. App. 617, 389 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 446 (1986) (upholding under \n \nFirst Amendment statute prohibiting campaign material falsely claiming that one is an incumbent). Without expressing any view on the validity of those cases, I would also note, like the plurality, that in this area more accurate information will normally counteract the lie. And an accurate, publicly available register of military awards, easily obtainable by political opponents, may well adequately protect the integrity of an award against those who would falsely claim to have earned it. See ante, at 17\xe2\x80\x9318. And so it is likely that a more narrowly tailored statute combined with such information-disseminating devices will effectively serve Congress\xe2\x80\x99 end.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government has provided no convincing explanation as to why a more finely tailored statute would not work. In my own view, such a statute could significantly reduce the threat of \n \nFirst Amendment harm while permitting the statute to achieve its important protective objective. That being so, I find the statute as presently drafted works disproportionate constitutional harm. It consequently fails intermediate scrutiny, and so violates the \n \nFirst Amendment.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor these reasons, I concur in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Alito, with whom Justice Scalia and Jus- tice Thomas join, dissenting.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOnly the bravest of the brave are awarded the Congressional Medal of Honor, but the Court today holds that every American has a constitutional right to claim to have received this singular award. The Court strikes down the Stolen Valor Act of 2005, which was enacted to stem an epidemic of false claims about military decorations. These lies, Congress reasonably concluded, were undermining our country\xe2\x80\x99s system of military honors and inflicting real harm on actual medal recipients and their families.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBuilding on earlier efforts to protect the military awards system, Congress responded to this problem by crafting a narrow statute that presents no threat to the freedom of speech. The statute reaches only knowingly false statements about hard facts directly within a speaker\xe2\x80\x99s per- sonal knowledge. These lies have no value in and of themselves, and proscribing them does not chill any valuable speech.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBy holding that the \n \nFirst Amendment nevertheless shields these lies, the Court breaks sharply from a long line of cases recognizing that the right to free speech does not protect false factual statements that inflict real harm and serve no legitimate interest. I would adhere to that principle and would thus uphold the constitutionality of this valuable law.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Stolen Valor Act makes it a misdemeanor to \xe2\x80\x9cfalsely represen[t]\xe2\x80\x9d oneself as having been awarded a medal, decoration, or badge for service in the Armed Forces of the United States. \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7704(b). Properly construed, this statute is limited in five significant respects. First, the Act applies to only a narrow category of false representations about objective facts that can almost always be proved or disproved with near certainty. Second, the Act concerns facts that are squarely within the speaker\xe2\x80\x99s personal knowledge. Third, as the Government maintains, see Brief for United States 15\xe2\x80\x9317, and both the plurality, see ante, at 7, and the concurrence, see ante, at 3 (Breyer, J., concurring in judgment), seemingly accept, a conviction under the Act requires proof beyond a reasonable doubt that the speaker actually knew that the representation was false.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Fourth, the Act applies only to statements that could reasonably be interpreted as communicating actual facts; it does not reach dramatic performances, satire, parody, hyperbole, or the like.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Finally, the Act is strictly viewpoint neutral. The false statements proscribed by the Act are highly unlikely to be tied to any particular political or ideological message. In the rare cases where that is not so, the Act applies equally to all false statements, whether they tend to disparage or commend the Government, the military, or the system of mil- itary honors.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Stolen Valor Act follows a long tradition of efforts to protect our country\xe2\x80\x99s system of military honors. When George Washington, as the commander of the Continental Army, created the very first \xe2\x80\x9chonorary badges of distinction\xe2\x80\x9d for service in our country\xe2\x80\x99s military, he established a rigorous system to ensure that these awards would be received and worn by only the truly deserving. See General Orders of George Washington Issued at Newburgh on the Hudson, 1782\xe2\x80\x931783, p. 35 (E. Boynton ed. 1883) (reprint 1973) (requiring the submission of \xe2\x80\x9cincontest- ible proof\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9csingularly meritorious action\xe2\x80\x9d to the Commander in Chief). Washington warned that anyone with the \xe2\x80\x9cinsolence to assume\xe2\x80\x9d a badge that had not actually been earned would be \xe2\x80\x9cseverely punished.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 34.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBuilding on this tradition, Congress long ago made it a federal offense for anyone to wear, manufacture, or sell certain military decorations without authorization. See Act of Feb. 24, 1923, ch. 110, \n\n42Stat.\n1286 (codified as amended at \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7704(a)). Although this Court has never opined on the constitutionality of that particular provision, we have said that \xc2\xa7702, which makes it a crime to wear a United States military uniform without authorization, is \xe2\x80\x9ca valid statute on its face.\xe2\x80\x9d Schacht v. United States, \n\n398 U.\xc2\xa0S. 58, \n61 (1970)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress passed the Stolen Valor Act in response to a proliferation of false claims concerning the receipt of military awards. For example, in a single year, more than 600 Virginia residents falsely claimed to have won the Medal of Honor.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t An investigation of the 333 people listed in the online edition of Who\xe2\x80\x99s Who as having received a top military award revealed that fully a third of the claims could not be substantiated.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t When the Library of Congress compiled oral histories for its Veterans History Project, 24 of the 49 individuals who identified themselves as Medal of Honor recipients had not actually received that award.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t The same was true of 32 individuals who claimed to have been awarded the Distinguished Service Cross and 14 who claimed to have won the Navy Cross.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Notorious cases brought to Congress\xe2\x80\x99 attention included the case of a judge who falsely claimed to have been awarded two Medals of Honor and displayed counterfeit medals in his courtroom;\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t7\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t a television network\xe2\x80\x99s military consultant who falsely claimed that he had received the Silver Star;\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t8\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t and a former judge advocate in the Marine Corps who lied about receiving the Bronze Star and a Purple Heart.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t9\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs Congress recognized, the lies proscribed by the Stolen Valor Act inflict substantial harm. In many instances, the harm is tangible in nature: Individuals often falsely represent themselves as award recipients in order to obtain financial or other material rewards, such as lucrative contracts and government benefits.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t10\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t An investigation of false claims in a single region of the United States, for example, revealed that 12 men had defrauded the Department of Veterans Affairs out of more than $1.4 million in veteran\xe2\x80\x99s benefits.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t11\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t In other cases, the harm is less tangible, but nonetheless significant. The lies proscribed by the Stolen Valor Act tend to debase the distinctive honor of military awards. See Stolen Valor Act of 2005, \xc2\xa72, \n\n120Stat.\n3266, note following \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7704 (finding that \xe2\x80\x9c[f]raudulent claims surrounding the receipt of [military decorations and medals] damage the reputation and meaning of such decorations and medals\xe2\x80\x9d). And legitimate award recipients and their families have expressed the harm they endure when an imposter takes credit for he- roic actions that he never performed. One Medal of Honor recipient described the feeling as a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98slap in the face of veterans who have paid the price and earned their medals.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t12\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is well recognized in trademark law that the proliferation of cheap imitations of luxury goods blurs the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98signal\xe2\x80\x99 given out by the purchasers of the originals.\xe2\x80\x9d Landes & Posner, Trademark Law: An Economic Perspective, 30 J. Law & Econ. 265, 308 (1987). In much the same way, the proliferation of false claims about military awards blurs the signal given out by the actual awards by making them seem more common than they really are, and this diluting effect harms the military by hampering its efforts to foster morale and esprit de corps. Surely it was reasonable for Congress to conclude that the goal of preserving the in- tegrity of our country\xe2\x80\x99s top military honors is at least as worthy as that of protecting the prestige associated with fancy watches and designer handbags. Cf. San Francisco Arts & Athletics, Inc. v. United States Olympic Comm., \n\n483 U.\xc2\xa0S. 522\n\xe2\x80\x93541 (1987) (rejecting \n \nFirst Amendment challenge to law prohibiting certain unauthorized uses of the word \xe2\x80\x9cOlympic\xe2\x80\x9d and recognizing that such uses harm the U.\xc2\xa0S. Olympic Committee by \xe2\x80\x9clessening the distinctiveness\xe2\x80\x9d of the term).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBoth the plurality and Justice Breyer argue that Congress could have preserved the integrity of military honors by means other than a criminal prohibition, but Congress had ample reason to believe that alternative approaches would not be adequate. The chief alternative that is recommended is the compilation and release of a comprehensive list or database of actual medal recipients. If the public could readily access such a resource, it is argued, imposters would be quickly and easily exposed, and the proliferation of lies about military honors would come to an end.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis remedy, unfortunately, will not work. The Department of Defense has explained that the most that it can do is to create a database of recipients of certain top military honors awarded since 2001. See Office of Undersecretary of Defense, Report to the Senate and House Armed Services Committees on a Searchable Military Valor Decorations Database 4\xe2\x80\x935 (2009).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t13\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBecause a sufficiently comprehensive database is not practicable, lies about military awards cannot be remedied by what the plurality calls \xe2\x80\x9ccounterspeech.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 15. Without the requisite database, many efforts to refute false claims may be thwarted, and some legitimate award recipients may be erroneously attacked. In addition, a steady stream of stories in the media about the exposure of imposters would tend to increase skepticism among members of the public about the entire awards system. This would only exacerbate the harm that the Stolen Valor Act is meant to prevent.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe plurality and the concurrence also suggest that Congress could protect the system of military honors by enacting a narrower statute. The plurality recommends a law that would apply only to lies that are intended to \xe2\x80\x9csecure moneys or other valuable considerations.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11. In a similar vein, the concurrence comments that \xe2\x80\x9ca more finely tailored statute might .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. insist upon a showing that the false statement caused specific harm.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9 (opinion of Breyer, J.). But much damage is caused, both to real award recipients and to the system of mili- tary honors, by false statements that are not linked to any financial or other tangible reward. Unless even a small financial loss\xe2\x80\x94say, a dollar given to a homeless man falsely claiming to be a decorated veteran\xe2\x80\x94is more important in the eyes of the \n \nFirst Amendment than the damage caused to the very integrity of the military awards system, there is no basis for distinguishing between the Stolen Valor Act and the alternative statutes that the plurality and concurrence appear willing to sustain.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Breyer also proposes narrowing the statute so that it covers a shorter list of military awards, ante, at 9 (opinion concurring in judgment), but he does not provide a hint about where he thinks the line must be drawn. Perhaps he expects Congress to keep trying until it eventually passes a law that draws the line in just the right place.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTime and again, this Court has recognized that as a general matter false factual statements possess no intrinsic \n \nFirst Amendment value. See Illinois ex\xc2\xa0rel. Madigan v. Telemarketing Associates, Inc., \n\n538 U.\xc2\xa0S. 600, \n612 (2003)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cLike other forms of public deception, fraudulent charitable solicitation is unprotected speech\xe2\x80\x9d); BE&K Constr. Co. v. NLRB, \n\n536 U.\xc2\xa0S. 516, \n531 (2002)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[F]alse statements may be unprotected for their own sake\xe2\x80\x9d); Hustler Magazine, Inc. v. Falwell, \n\n485 U.\xc2\xa0S. 46, \n52 (1988)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cFalse statements of fact are particularly valueless; they interfere with the truth-seeking function of the marketplace of ideas, and they cause damage to an individual\xe2\x80\x99s reputation that cannot easily be repaired by counterspeech, however persuasive or effective\xe2\x80\x9d); Keeton v. Hustler Magazine, Inc., \n\n465 U.\xc2\xa0S. 770, \n776 (1984)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cThere is \xe2\x80\x98no constitutional value in false statements of fact\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Gertz v. Robert Welch, Inc., \n\n418 U.\xc2\xa0S. 323, \n340 (1974)\n)); Bill John- son\xe2\x80\x99s Restaurants, Inc. v. NLRB, \n\n461 U.\xc2\xa0S. 731, \n743 (1983)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[F]alse statements are not immunized by the \n \nFirst Amendment right to freedom of speech\xe2\x80\x9d); Brown v. Hartlage, \n\n456 U.\xc2\xa0S. 45, \n60 (1982)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cOf course, demonstrable falsehoods are not protected by the \n \nFirst Amendment in the same manner as truthful statements\xe2\x80\x9d); Herbert v. Lando, \n\n441 U.\xc2\xa0S. 153, \n171 (1979)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cSpreading false information in and of itself carries no \n \nFirst Amendment credentials\xe2\x80\x9d); Virginia Bd. of Pharmacy v. Virginia Citizens Consumer Council, Inc., \n\n425 U.\xc2\xa0S. 748, \n771 (1976)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cUntruthful speech, commercial or otherwise, has never been protected for its own sake\xe2\x80\x9d); Gertz, supra, at 340 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he erroneous statement of fact is not worthy of constitutional protection\xe2\x80\x9d); Time, Inc. v. Hill, \n\n385 U.\xc2\xa0S. 374, \n389 (1967)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he constitutional guarantees [of the \n \nFirst Amendment] can tolerate sanctions against calculated falsehood without significant impairment of their essential function\xe2\x80\x9d); Garrison v. Louisiana, \n\n379 U.\xc2\xa0S. 64, \n75 (1964)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he knowingly false statement and the false statement made with reckless disregard of the truth, do not enjoy constitutional protection\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tConsistent with this recognition, many kinds of false factual statements have long been proscribed without \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98rais[ing] any Constitutional problem.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Stevens, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 6) (quoting Chaplinsky v. New Hampshire, \n\n315 U.\xc2\xa0S. 568\n\xe2\x80\x93572 (1942)). Laws prohibiting fraud, perjury, and defamation, for example, were in existence when the \n \nFirst Amendment was adopted, and their constitutionality is now beyond question. See, e.g., Donaldson v. Read Magazine, Inc., \n\n333 U.\xc2\xa0S. 178, \n190 (1948)\n (explaining that the government\xe2\x80\x99s power \xe2\x80\x9cto protect people against fraud\xe2\x80\x9d has \xe2\x80\x9calways been recognized in this country and is firmly established\xe2\x80\x9d); United States v. Dunnigan, \n\n507 U.\xc2\xa0S. 87, \n97 (1993)\n (observing that \xe2\x80\x9cthe constitutionality of perjury statutes is unquestioned\xe2\x80\x9d); Beauharnais v. Illinois, \n\n343 U.\xc2\xa0S. 250, \n256 (1952)\n (noting that the \xe2\x80\x9cprevention and punishment\xe2\x80\x9d of libel \xe2\x80\x9chave never been thought to raise any Constitutional problem\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe have also described as falling outside the \n \nFirst Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s protective shield certain false factual statements that were neither illegal nor tortious at the time of the Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s adoption. The right to freedom of speech has been held to permit recovery for the intentional infliction of emotional distress by means of a false statement, see Falwell, supra, at 56, even though that tort did not enter our law until the late 19th century, see W. Keeton, D. Dobbs, R. Keeton, & D. Owen, Prosser and Keeton on Law of Torts \xc2\xa712, p. 60, and n. 47. (5th ed. 1984) (hereinafter Prosser and Keeton). And in Hill, supra, at 390, the Court concluded that the free speech right allows recovery for the even more modern tort of false-light invasion of privacy, see Prosser and Keeton \xc2\xa7117, at 863.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn line with these holdings, it has long been assumed that the \n \nFirst Amendment is not offended by prominent criminal statutes with no close common-law analog. The most well known of these is probably \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71001, which makes it a crime to \xe2\x80\x9cknowingly and willfully\xe2\x80\x9d make any \xe2\x80\x9cmaterially false, fictitious, or fraudulent statement or representation\xe2\x80\x9d in \xe2\x80\x9cany matter within the jurisdiction of the executive, legislative, or judicial branch of the Government of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Unlike perjury, \xc2\xa71001 is not limited to statements made under oath or before an official government tribunal. Nor does it require any showing of \xe2\x80\x9cpecuniary or property loss to the government.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Gilliland, \n\n312 U.\xc2\xa0S. 86, \n93 (1941)\n. Instead, the statute is based on the need to protect \xe2\x80\x9cagencies from the perversion which might result from the deceptive practices described.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tStill other statutes make it a crime to falsely represent that one is speaking on behalf of, or with the approval of, the Federal Government. See, e.g., \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7912 (making it a crime to falsely impersonate a federal officer); \xc2\xa7709 (making it a crime to knowingly use, without authorization, the names of enumerated federal agencies, such as \xe2\x80\x9cFederal Bureau of Investigation,\xe2\x80\x9d in a manner reasonably calculated to convey the impression that a communication is approved or authorized by the agency). We have recognized that \xc2\xa7912, like \xc2\xa71001, does not require a showing of pecuniary or property loss and that its purpose is to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98maintain the general good repute and dignity\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of Government service. United States v. Lepowitch, \n\n318 U.\xc2\xa0S. 702, \n704 (1943)\n (quoting United States v. Barnow, \n\n239 U.\xc2\xa0S. 74, \n80 (1915)\n). All told, there are more than 100 federal criminal statutes that punish false statements made in connection with areas of federal agency concern. See United States v. Wells, \n\n519 U.\xc2\xa0S. 482\n\xe2\x80\x93507, and nn.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9310 (1997) (Stevens, J., dissenting) (citing \xe2\x80\x9cat least 100 federal false statement statutes\xe2\x80\x9d in the United States Code).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThese examples amply demonstrate that false statements of fact merit no \n \nFirst Amendment protection in their own right.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t14\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t It is true, as Justice Breyer notes, that many in our society either approve or condone certain discrete categories of false statements, including false statements made to prevent harm to innocent victims and so-called \xe2\x80\x9cwhite lies.\xe2\x80\x9d See ante, at 4. But respondent\xe2\x80\x99s false claim to have received the Medal of Honor did not fall into any of these categories. His lie did not \xe2\x80\x9cprevent embarrassment, protect privacy, shield a person from prejudice, provide the sick with comfort, or preserve a child\xe2\x80\x99s innocence.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Nor did his lie \xe2\x80\x9cstop a panic or otherwise preserve calm in the face of danger\xe2\x80\x9d or further philosophical or scientific debate. Ibid. Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s claim, like all those covered by the Stolen Valor Act, served no valid purpose.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondent and others who join him in attacking the Stolen Valor Act take a different view. Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s brief features a veritable paean to lying. According to respondent, his lie about the Medal of Honor was nothing out of the ordinary for 21st-century Americans. \xe2\x80\x9cEveryone lies,\xe2\x80\x9d he says. Brief for Respondent 10. \xe2\x80\x9cWe lie all the time.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. \xe2\x80\x9c[H]uman beings are constantly forced to choose the persona we present to the world, and our choices nearly always involve intentional omissions and misrepresentations, if not outright deception.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 39. An academic amicus tells us that the \n \nFirst Amendment protects the right to construct \xe2\x80\x9cself-aggrandizing fabrications such as having been awarded a military decoration.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Jonathan D. Varat as Amicus Curiae 5.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis radical interpretation of the \n \nFirst Amendment is not supported by any precedent of this Court. The lies covered by the Stolen Valor Act have no intrinsic value and thus merit no \n \nFirst Amendment protection unless their prohibition would chill other expression that falls within the Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s scope. I now turn to that question.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhile we have repeatedly endorsed the principle that false statements of fact do not merit \n \nFirst Amendment protection for their own sake, we have recognized that it is sometimes necessary to \xe2\x80\x9cexten[d] a measure of strategic protection\xe2\x80\x9d to these statements in order to ensure sufficient \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98breathing space\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d for protected speech. Gertz, 418 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 342 (quoting NAACP v. Button, \n\n371 U.\xc2\xa0S. 415, \n433 (1963)\n). Thus, in order to prevent the chilling of truthful speech on matters of public concern, we have held that liability for the defamation of a public official or figure requires proof that defamatory statements were made with knowledge or reckless disregard of their falsity. See New York Times Co. v. Sullivan, \n\n376 U.\xc2\xa0S. 254\n\xe2\x80\x93280 (1964) (civil liability); Garrison, 379 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 74\xe2\x80\x9375 (criminal liability). This same requirement applies when public officials and figures seek to recover for the tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress. See Falwell, 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 55\xe2\x80\x9356. And we have imposed \xe2\x80\x9c[e]xacting proof requirements\xe2\x80\x9d in other contexts as well when necessary to ensure that truthful speech is not chilled. Madigan, 538 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 620 (complainant in a fraud action must show that the defendant made a knowingly false statement of material fact with the intent to mislead the listener and that he succeeded in doing so); see also BE&K Constr., 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 531 (regulation of baseless lawsuits limited to those that are both \xe2\x80\x9cobjectively baseless and subjectively motivated by an unlawful purpose\xe2\x80\x9d); Hartlage, 456 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 61 (sustaining as-applied \n \nFirst Amendment challenge to law prohibiting certain \xe2\x80\x9cfactual misstatements in the course of political debate\xe2\x80\x9d where there had been no showing that the disputed statement was made \xe2\x80\x9cother than in good faith and without knowledge of its falsity, or .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. with reckless disregard as to whether it was false or not\xe2\x80\x9d). All of these proof requirements inevitably have the effect of bringing some false factual statements within the protection of the \n \nFirst Amendment, but this is justified in order to prevent the chilling of other, valuable speech.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThese examples by no means exhaust the circumstances in which false factual statements enjoy a degree of instrumental constitutional protection. On the contrary, there are broad areas in which any attempt by the state to penalize purportedly false speech would present a grave and unacceptable danger of suppressing truthful speech. Laws restricting false statements about philosophy, religion, history, the social sciences, the arts, and other matters of public concern would present such a threat. The point is not that there is no such thing as truth or falsity in these areas or that the truth is always impossible to ascertain, but rather that it is perilous to permit the state to be the arbiter of truth.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEven where there is a wide scholarly consensus concerning a particular matter, the truth is served by allowing that consensus to be challenged without fear of reprisal. Today\xe2\x80\x99s accepted wisdom sometimes turns out to be mistaken. And in these contexts, \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ven a false statement may be deemed to make a valuable contribution to public debate, since it brings about \xe2\x80\x98the clearer perception and livelier impression of truth, produced by its collision with error.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Sullivan, supra, at 279, n.\xc2\xa019 (quoting J. Mill, On Liberty 15 (R. McCallum ed. 1947)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAllowing the state to proscribe false statements in these areas also opens the door for the state to use its power for political ends. Statements about history illustrate this point. If some false statements about historical events may be banned, how certain must it be that a statement is false before the ban may be upheld? And who should make that calculation? While our cases prohibiting viewpoint discrimination would fetter the state\xe2\x80\x99s power to some degree, see R. A. V. v. St. Paul, \n\n505 U.\xc2\xa0S. 377\n\xe2\x80\x93390 (1992) (explaining that the \n \nFirst Amendment does not permit the government to engage in viewpoint discrimination under the guise of regulating unprotected speech), the potential for abuse of power in these areas is simply too great.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn stark contrast to hypothetical laws prohibiting false statements about history, science, and similar matters, the Stolen Valor Act presents no risk at all that valuable speech will be suppressed. The speech punished by the Act is not only verifiably false and entirely lacking in intrinsic value, but it also fails to serve any instrumen- tal purpose that the \n \nFirst Amendment might protect. Tell- ingly, when asked at oral argument what truthful speech the Stolen Valor Act might chill, even respondent\xe2\x80\x99s counsel conceded that the answer is none. Tr. of Oral Arg. 36.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tC\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNeither of the two opinions endorsed by Justices in the majority claims that the false statements covered by the Stolen Valor Act possess either intrinsic or instrumental value. Instead, those opinions appear to be based on the distinct concern that the Act suffers from overbreadth. See ante, at 10 (plurality opinion) (the Act applies to \xe2\x80\x9cpersonal, whispered conversations within a home\xe2\x80\x9d); ante, at 8 (Breyer, J., concurring in judgment) (the Act \xe2\x80\x9capplies in family, social, or other private contexts\xe2\x80\x9d and in \xe2\x80\x9cpolitical contexts\xe2\x80\x9d). But to strike down a statute on the basis that it is overbroad, it is necessary to show that the statute\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9coverbreadth [is] substantial, not only in an absolute sense, but also relative to [its] plainly legitimate sweep.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Williams, \n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 285, \n292 (2008)\n; see also ibid. (noting that this requirement has been \xe2\x80\x9cvigorously enforced\xe2\x80\x9d). The plurality and the concurrence do not even attempt to make this showing.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe plurality additionally worries that a decision sustaining the Stolen Valor Act might prompt Congress and the state legislatures to enact laws criminalizing lies about \xe2\x80\x9can endless list of subjects.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11. The plurality apparently fears that we will see laws making it a crime to lie about civilian awards such as college degrees or certificates of achievement in the arts and sports.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis concern is likely unfounded. With very good reason, military honors have traditionally been regarded as quite different from civilian awards. Nearly a century ago, Congress made it a crime to wear a military medal without authorization; we have no comparable tradition regarding such things as Super Bowl rings, Oscars, or Phi Beta Kappa keys.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn any event, if the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s concern is not entirely fanciful, it falls outside the purview of the \n \nFirst Amendment. The problem that the plurality foresees\xe2\x80\x94that legislative bodies will enact unnecessary and overly intrusive criminal laws\xe2\x80\x94applies regardless of whether the laws in question involve speech or nonexpressive conduct. If there is a problem with, let us say, a law making it a criminal offense to falsely claim to have been a high school valedictorian, the problem is not the suppression of speech but the misuse of the criminal law, which should be reserved for conduct that inflicts or threatens truly serious societal harm. The objection to this hypothetical law would be the same as the objection to a law making it a crime to eat potato chips during the graduation ceremony at which the high school valedictorian is recognized. The safeguard against such laws is democracy, not the \n \nFirst Amendment. Not every foolish law is unconstitutional.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Stolen Valor Act represents the judgment of the people\xe2\x80\x99s elected representatives that false statements about military awards are very different from false statements about civilian awards. Certainly this is true with respect to the high honor that respondent misappropri- ated. Respondent claimed that he was awarded the Medal of Honor in 1987 for bravery during the Iran hostage crisis. This singular award, however, is bestowed only on those members of the Armed Forces who \xe2\x80\x9cdistinguis[h] [themselves] conspicuously by gallantry and intrepidity at the risk of [their lives] above and beyond the call of duty.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n10 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73741; see also \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa76241, 8741. More than half of the heroic individuals to have been awarded the Medal of Honor after World War I received it posthumously.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t15\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Congress was entitled to conclude that falsely claiming to have won the Medal of Honor is qualitatively different from even the most prestigious civilian awards and that the misappropriation of that honor warrants criminal sanction.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Stolen Valor Act is a narrow law enacted to address an important problem, and it presents no threat to freedom of expression. I would sustain the constitutionality of the Act, and I therefore respectfully dissent.\n\t\t\t
