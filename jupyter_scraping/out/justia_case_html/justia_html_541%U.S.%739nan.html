CENTRAL LABORERS\xe2\x80\x99 PENSION FUND v. HEINZ \net al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the seventh circuit

\n

No. 02\xe2\x80\x93891. Argued April 19, 2004\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 7, 2004

\n

Respondents (collectively, Heinz) are retired participants in a multiemployer pension plan (hereinafter Plan) administered by petitioner. Heinz retired from the construction industry after accruing enough pension credits to qualify for early retirement payments under a \xe2\x80\x9cservice only\xe2\x80\x9d pension scheme that pays him the same monthly benefit he would have received had he retired at the usual age. The Plan prohibits such beneficiaries from certain \xe2\x80\x9cdisqualifying employment\xe2\x80\x9d after they retire, suspending monthly payments until they stop the forbidden work. When Heinz retired, the Plan defined \xe2\x80\x9cdisqualifying employment\xe2\x80\x9d to include a job as a construction worker but not as a supervisor, the job Heinz took. In 1998, the Plan expanded its definition to include any construction industry job and stopped Heinz\xe2\x80\x99s payments when he did not leave his supervisor\xe2\x80\x99s job. Heinz sued to recover the suspended benefits, claiming that the suspension violated the \xe2\x80\x9canti-cutback\xe2\x80\x9d rule of the Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974 (ERISA), which prohibits any pension plan amendment that would reduce a participant\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9caccrued benefit,\xe2\x80\x9d ERISA \xc2\xa7204(g), 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71054(g). The District Court granted the Plan judgment on the pleadings, but the Seventh Circuit reversed, holding that imposing new conditions on rights to benefits already accrued violates the anti-cutback rule.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0ERISA \xc2\xa7204(g) prohibits a plan amendment expanding the categories of postretirement employment that triggers suspension of the payment of early retirement benefits already accrued. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The anti-cutback provision is crucial to ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s central object of protecting employees\xe2\x80\x99 justified expectations of receiving the benefits that they have been promised, see Lockheed Corp. v. Spink, 517 U. S. 882, 887. The provision prohibits plan amendments that have \xe2\x80\x9cthe effect of \xe2\x80\xa6 eliminating or reducing an early retirement benefit.\xe2\x80\x9d 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71054(g)(2). The question here is whether the Plan\xe2\x80\x99s amendment had such an effect. Although the statutory text is not as helpful as it might be, it is clear as a matter of common sense that a benefit has suffered under the amendment. Heinz accrued benefits under a plan allowing him to supplement his retirement income, and he reasonably relied on that plan\xe2\x80\x99s terms in planning his retirement. The 1998 amendment undercut that reliance, paying benefits only if he accepted a substantial curtailment of his opportunity to do the kind of work he knew. There is no way that, in any practical sense, this change of terms could not be viewed as shrinking the value of Heinz\xe2\x80\x99s pension rights and reducing his promised benefits. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The Plan\xe2\x80\x99s technical responses are rejected. To give the anti-cutback rule the constricted reading urged by the Plan\xe2\x80\x94applying it only to amendments directly altering the monthly payment\xe2\x80\x99s nominal dollar amount and not to a suspension when the amount that would be paid is unaltered\xe2\x80\x94would take textual force majeure, and certainly something closer to irresistible than language in 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71002(23)(A) to the effect that accrued benefits are ordinarily \xe2\x80\x9cexpressed in the form of an annual benefit commencing at normal retirement age.\xe2\x80\x9d And the Plan\xe2\x80\x99s argument that \xc2\xa7204(g)\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9celiminat[e] or reduc[e]\xe2\x80\x9d language does not apply to mere suspensions misses the point. ERISA permits conditions that are elements of the benefit itself but the question here is whether a new condition may be imposed after a benefit has accrued. The right to receive certain money on a certain date may not be limited by a new condition narrowing that right. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0This Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion is confirmed by an Internal Revenue Service regulation that adopts the reading of \xc2\xa7204(g) approved here. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(d)\xc2\xa0ERISA \xc2\xa7203(a)(3)(B), 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71053(a)(3)(B)\xe2\x80\x94which provides that the right to an accrued benefit \xe2\x80\x9cshall not be treated as forfeitable solely because the plan\xe2\x80\x9d suspends benefit payments when beneficiaries like respondents are employed in the same industry and the same geographic area covered by the plan\xe2\x80\x94is irrelevant to the question here. Section 203(a) addresses the entirely distinct concept of benefit forfeitures. And read most simply and in context, \xc2\xa7203(a)(3)(B) is a statement about the terms that can be offered to plan participants up front, not as an authorization to adopt retroactive amendments. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

303 F.\xc2\xa03d 802, affirmed.

\nOPINION OF THE COURT
\nCENTRAL LABORERS\' PENSION FUND V. HEINZ
\n541 U. S. ____ (2004)

\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 02-891

\n

CENTRAL LABORERS\xe2\x80\x99 PENSION FUND, PETITIONER v. THOMAS E. HEINZ et al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the seventh circuit

\n

[June 7, 2004]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Souter delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0With few exceptions, the \xe2\x80\x9canti-cutback\xe2\x80\x9d rule of the Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974 (ERISA) prohibits any amendment of a pension plan that would reduce a participant\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9caccrued benefit.\xe2\x80\x9d 88 Stat. 858, 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71054(g). The question is whether the rule prohibits an amendment expanding the categories of postretirement employment that triggers suspension of payment of early retirement benefits already accrued. We hold such an amendment prohibited.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents Thomas Heinz and Richard Schmitt (collectively, Heinz) are retired participants in a multiemployer pension plan (hereinafter Plan) administered by petitioner Central Laborers\xe2\x80\x99 Pension Fund. Like most other participants in the Plan, Heinz worked in the construction industry in central Illinois before retiring, and by 1996, he had accrued enough pension credits to qualify for early retirement payments under a defined benefit \xe2\x80\x9cservice only\xe2\x80\x9d pension. This scheme pays him the same monthly retirement benefit he would have received if he had retired at the usual age, and is thus a form of subsidized benefit, since monthly payments are not discounted even though they start earlier and are likely to continue longer than the average period.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Heinz\xe2\x80\x99s entitlement is subject to a condition on which this case focuses: the Plan prohibits beneficiaries of service only pensions from certain \xe2\x80\x9cdisqualifying employment\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d after they retire. The Plan provides that if beneficiaries accept such employment their monthly payments will be suspended until they stop the forbidden work.[Footnote 1] When Heinz retired in 1996, the Plan defined \xe2\x80\x9cdisqualifying employment\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d as any job as \xe2\x80\x9ca union or non-union construction worker.\xe2\x80\x9d This condition did not cover employment in a supervisory capacity, however, and when Heinz took a job in central Illinois as a construction supervisor after retiring, the Plan continued to pay out his monthly benefit.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1998, the Plan\xe2\x80\x99s definition of disqualifying employment was expanded by amendment to include any job \xe2\x80\x9cin any capacity in the construction industry (either as a union or non-union construction worker).\xe2\x80\x9d The Plan took the amended definition to cover supervisory work and warned Heinz that if he continued on as a supervisor, his monthly pension payments would be suspended. Heinz kept working, and the Plan stopped paying.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Heinz sued to recover the suspended benefits on the ground that applying the amended definition of disqualifying employment so as to suspend payment of his accrued benefits violated ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s anti-cutback rule. On cross-motions for judgment on the pleadings under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure\xc2\xa012(c), the District Court granted judgment for the Plan, only to be reversed by a divided panel of the Seventh Circuit, which held that imposing new conditions on rights to benefits already accrued was a violation of the anti-cutback rule. 303 F.\xc2\xa03d 802 (CA7 2002). We granted certiorari in order to resolve the resulting Circuit split, see Spacek v. Maritime Assn., 134 F.\xc2\xa03d 283 (CA5 1998), and now affirm.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is no doubt about the centrality of ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s object of protecting employees\xe2\x80\x99 justified expectations of receiving the benefits their employers promise them.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cNothing in ERISA requires employers to establish employee benefits plans. Nor does ERISA mandate what kind of benefits employers must provide if they choose to have such a plan. ERISA does, however, seek to ensure that employees will not be left empty-handed once employers have guaranteed them certain benefits. . . . [W]hen Congress enacted ERISA, it \xe2\x80\x98wanted to . . . mak[e] sure that if a worker has been promised a defined pension benefit upon retirement\xe2\x80\x94\nand if he has fulfilled whatever conditions are required to obtain a vested benefit\xe2\x80\x94he actually will receive it.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Lockheed Corp. v. Spink, 517 U. S. 882, 887 (1996) (quoting Nachman Corp. v. Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation, 446 U. S. 359, 375 (1980) (citations omitted)).

\n

\nSee also J. Langbein & B. Wolk, Pension and Employee Benefit Law 121 (3d ed. 2000) (hereinafter Langbein & Wolk) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe central problem to which ERISA is addressed is the loss of pension benefits previously promised\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s anti-cutback rule is crucial to this object, and (with two exceptions of no concern here\xc2\xa0[Footnote 2]) provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he accrued benefit of a participant under a plan may not be decreased by an amendment of the plan \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71054(g)(1). After some initial question about whether the provision addressed early retirement benefits, see Langbein & Wolk 164, a 1984 amendment made it clear that it does. Retirement Equity Act of 1984, \xc2\xa7301(a), (2), 98 Stat. 1451. Now \xc2\xa7204(g) provides that \xe2\x80\x9ca plan amendment which has the effect of \xe2\x80\xa6 eliminating or reducing an early retirement benefit \xe2\x80\xa6 with respect to benefits attributable to service before the amendment shall be treated as reducing accrued benefits.\xe2\x80\x9d 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71054(g)(2).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Hence the question here: did the 1998 amendment to the Plan have the effect of \xe2\x80\x9celiminating or reducing an early retirement benefit\xe2\x80\x9d that was earned by service before the amendment was passed? The statute, admittedly, is not as helpful as it might be in answering this question; it does not explicitly define \xe2\x80\x9cearly retirement benefit,\xe2\x80\x9d and it rather circularly defines \xe2\x80\x9caccrued benefit\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cthe individual\xe2\x80\x99s accrued benefit determined under the plan \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71002(23)(A). Still, it certainly looks as though a benefit has suffered under the amendment here, for we agree with the Seventh Circuit that, as a matter of common sense, \xe2\x80\x9c[a] participant\xe2\x80\x99s benefits cannot be understood without reference to the conditions imposed on receiving those benefits, and an amendment placing materially greater restrictions on the receipt of the benefit \xe2\x80\x98reduces\xe2\x80\x99 the benefit just as surely as a decrease in the size of the monthly benefit payment.\xe2\x80\x9d 303 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 805. Heinz worked and accrued retirement benefits under a plan with terms allowing him to supplement retirement income by certain employment, and he was being reasonable if he relied on those terms in planning his retirement. The 1998 amendment undercut any such reliance, paying retirement income only if he accepted a substantial curtailment of his opportunity to do the kind of work he knew. We simply do not see how, in any practical sense, this change of terms could not be viewed as shrinking the value of Heinz\xe2\x80\x99s pension rights and reducing his promised benefits.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Plan\xe2\x80\x99s responses are technical ones, beginning with the suggestion that the \xe2\x80\x9cbenefit\xe2\x80\x9d that may not be devalued is actually nothing more than a \xe2\x80\x9cdefined periodic benefit the plan is legally obliged to pay,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 28, so that \xc2\xa7204(g) applies only to amendments directly altering the nominal dollar amount of a retiree\xe2\x80\x99s monthly pension payment. A retiree\xe2\x80\x99s benefit of $100 a month, say, is not reduced by a postaccrual plan amendment that suspends payments, so long as nothing affects the figure of $100 defining what he would be paid, if paid at all. Under the Plan\xe2\x80\x99s reading, \xc2\xa7204(g) would have nothing to say about an amendment that resulted even in a permanent suspension of payments. But for us to give the anti-cutback rule a reading that constricted would take textual force majeure, and certainly something closer to irresistible than the provision quoted in the Plan\xe2\x80\x99s observation that accrued benefits are ordinarily \xe2\x80\x9cexpressed in the form of an annual benefit commencing at normal retirement age,\xe2\x80\x9d 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71002(23)(A).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Plan also contends that, because \xc2\xa7204(g) only prohibits amendments that \xe2\x80\x9celiminat[e] or reduc[e] an early retirement benefit,\xe2\x80\x9d the anti-cutback rule must not apply to mere suspensions of an early retirement benefit. This argument seems to rest on a distinction between \xe2\x80\x9celiminat[e] or reduc[e]\xe2\x80\x9d on the one hand, and \xe2\x80\x9csuspend\xe2\x80\x9d on the other, but it just misses the point. No one denies that some conditions enforceable by suspending benefit payments are permissible under ERISA: conditions set before a benefit accrues can survive the anti-cutback rule, even though their sanction is a suspension of benefits. Because such conditions are elements of the benefit itself and are considered in valuing it at the moment it accrues, a later suspension of benefit payments according to the Plan\xe2\x80\x99s terms does not eliminate the benefit or reduce its value. The real question is whether a new condition may be imposed after a benefit has accrued; may the right to receive certain money on a certain date be limited by a new condition narrowing that right? In a given case, the new condition may or may not be invoked to justify an actual suspension of benefits, but at the moment the new condition is imposed, the accrued benefit becomes less valuable, irrespective of any actual suspension.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our conclusion is confirmed by a regulation of the Internal Revenue Service (IRS) that adopts just this reading of \xc2\xa7204(g). When Title I of ERISA was enacted to impose substantive legal requirements on employee pension plans (including the anti-cutback rule), Title II of ERISA amended the Internal Revenue Code to condition the eligibility of pension plans for preferential tax treatment on compliance with many of the Title I requirements. Employee Benefits Law 47, 171\xe2\x80\x93173 (S. Sacher et\xc2\xa0al., eds. 2d ed. 2000). The result was a \xe2\x80\x9ccurious duplicate structure\xe2\x80\x9d with nearly verbatim replication in the Internal Revenue Code of whole sections of text from Title I of ERISA. Langbein & Wolk 91, \xc2\xb6 ;6. The anti-cutback rule of ERISA \xc2\xa7204(g) is one such section, showing up in substantially identical form as 26 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7411(d)(6).[Footnote 3] This duplication explains the provision of the Reorganization Plan No. 4 of 1978, \xc2\xa7101, 43 Fed. Reg. 47713 (1978), 92 Stat. 3790, giving the Secretary of the Treasury the ultimate authority to interpret these overlapping anti-cutback provisions. See also Langbein & Wolk 92, \xc2\xb6 ;7 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe IRS has [regulatory] jurisdiction over \xe2\x80\xa6 benefit accrua[l] and vesting\xe2\x80\x9d). Although the pertinent regulations refer only to the Internal Revenue Code version of the anti-cutback rule, they apply with equal force to ERISA \xc2\xa7204(g). See 53 Fed. Reg. 26050, 26053 (1988) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe regulations under section 411 are also applicable to provisions of [ERISA] Title I\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The IRS has formally taken the position that the anti-cutback rule does not keep employers from specifying in advance of accrual that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he availability of a section 411(d)(6) protected benefit [is] limited to employees who satisfy certain objective conditions\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d 26 CFR \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71.411(d)\xe2\x80\x934, A\xe2\x80\x936(a)(1) (2003). Without running afoul of the rule, for example, plans may say from the outset that a single sum distribution of benefits is conditioned on the execution of a covenant not to compete. \xc2\xa71.411(d)\xe2\x80\x934,\nA\xe2\x80\x936(a)(2). And employers are perfectly free to modify the deal they are offering their employees, as long as the change goes to the terms of compensation for continued, future employment: a plan \xe2\x80\x9cmay be amended to eliminate or reduce section 411(d)(6) protected benefits with respect to benefits not yet accrued\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71.411(d)\xe2\x80\x934, at A\xe2\x80\x932(a)(1). The IRS regulations treat such conditions very differently, however, when they turn up as part of an amendment adding new conditions to the receipt of benefits already accrued. The rule in that case is categorical: \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he addition of \xe2\x80\xa6 objective conditions with respect to a section 411(d)(6) protected benefit that has already accrued violates section 411(d)(6). Also, the addition of conditions (whether or not objective) or any change to existing conditions with respect to section 411(d)(6) protected benefits that results in any further restriction violates section 411(d)(6).\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71.41(d)\xe2\x80\x934, A\xe2\x80\x937. So far as the IRS regulations are concerned, then, the anti-cutback provision flatly prohibits plans from attaching new conditions to benefits that an employee has already earned.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The IRS has, however, told two stories. The Plan points to a provision of the Internal Revenue Manual that supports its position: \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n amendment that reduces IRC 411(d)(6) protected benefits on account of [a plan\xe2\x80\x99s disqualifying employment provision] does not violate IRC 411(d)(6).\xe2\x80\x9d Internal Revenue Manual 4.72.14.3.5.3(7) (May 4, 2001), available at http//www.irs.ustreas.gov/\nirm/part4/ch49s18.html. And the United States as amicus curiae says that the IRS has routinely approved amendments to plan definitions of disqualifying employment, even when they apply retroactively to accrued benefits. But neither an unreasoned statement in the manual nor allegedly longstanding agency practice can trump a formal regulation with the procedural history necessary to take on the force of law. See generally Note, Taxpayers\xe2\x80\x99 Bill of Rights Act: Taxpayers\xe2\x80\x99 Remedy or Political Placebo?, 86 Mich. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1787, 1799\xe2\x80\x931801 (1988) (discussing legal status of the Internal Revenue Manual). Speaking in its most authoritative voice, the IRS has long since approved the interpretation of \xc2\xa7204(g) that we adopt today.[Footnote 4]

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In criticizing the Seventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s reading of \xc2\xa7204(g), the Plan and the United States rely heavily on an entirely separate section of ERISA \xc2\xa7203(a)(3)(B), 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71053(a)(3)(B). Here they claim to find specific authorization to amend suspension provisions retroactively, in terms specific enough to trump any general prohibition imposed by \xc2\xa7204(g). Section 203(a)(3)(B) provides that

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[a] right to an accrued benefit derived from employer contributions shall not be treated as forfeitable solely because the plan provides that the payment of benefits is suspended for such period as [beneficiaries like respondents are] employed . . . in the same industry, in the same trade or craft, and the same geographic area covered by the plan, as when such benefits commenced.\xe2\x80\x9d 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71053(a)(3)(B).

\n

\nThe Plan\xe2\x80\x99s arguments notwithstanding, \xc2\xa7203(a)(3)(B)\nis irrelevant to the question before us, for at least two reasons.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, as a technical matter, \xc2\xa7203(a) addresses the entirely different question of benefit forfeitures. This is a distinct concept: \xc2\xa7204(g) belongs to the section of ERISA that sets forth requirements for benefit accrual (the rate at which an employee earns benefits to put in his pension account), see 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71054, whereas \xc2\xa7203(a)(3)(b) is in the section that regulates vesting (the process by which an employee\xe2\x80\x99s already-accrued pension account becomes irrevocably his property), see 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71053. See generally Nachman Corp., 446 U. S., at 366, n. 10 (\xe2\x80\x9cSection 203(a) is a central provision in ERISA. It requires generally that a plan treat an employee\xe2\x80\x99s benefits, to the extent that they have vested by virtue of his having fulfilled age and length of service requirements no greater than those specified in \xc2\xa7203(a)(2), as not subject to forfeiture\xe2\x80\x9d). To be sure, the concepts overlap in practical effect, and a single act by a plan might raise both vesting and accrual concerns. But it would be a non sequitur to conclude that, because an amendment does not constitute a prohibited forfeiture under \xc2\xa7203, it must not be a prohibited reduction under \xc2\xa7204. Just because \xc2\xa7203(a)(3)(B) failed to forbid it would not mean that \xc2\xa7204(g) allowed it.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, read most simply and in context, \xc2\xa7203(a)(3)(B) is a statement about the terms that can be offered to plan participants up front and enforced without amounting to forfeiture, not as an authorization to adopt retroactive amendments. Section 203(a), 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71053(a), reads that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ach pension plan shall provide that an employee\xe2\x80\x99s right to his normal retirement benefit is nonforfeitable upon the attainment of normal retirement age.\xe2\x80\x9d This is\na global directive that regulates the substantive content\nof pension plans; it adds a mandatory term to all retirement packages that a company might offer. Section 203(a)(3)(B), in turn, is nothing more than an explanation of this substantive requirement. Congress wanted to allow employers to condition future benefits on a plan participant\xe2\x80\x99s agreement not to accept certain kinds of postretirement employment, see n.\xc2\xa01, supra, and it recognized that a plan provision to this effect might be seen as rendering vested benefits improperly forfeitable. Accordingly, adding \xc2\xa7203(a)(3)(B) made it clear that such suspension provisions were permissible in narrow circumstances. But critically for present purposes, \xc2\xa7203(a)(3)(B) speaks only to the permissible substantive scope of existing ERISA plans, not to the procedural permissibility of plan amendments. The fact that ERISA allows plans to include a suspension provision going to benefits not yet accrued has no logical bearing on the analysis of how ERISA treats the imposition of such a condition on (implicitly) bargained-for benefits that have accrued already.[Footnote 5] Section 203(a)(3)(B) is no help to the Plan.[Footnote 6]
