\n\t\t\t\tTANIGUCHI v. KAN PACIFIC SAIPAN, LTD., dba MARIANAS RESORT AND SPA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 10\xe2\x80\x931472.\xe2\x80\x83Argued February 21, 2012\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 21, 2012\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tTitle \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71920, as amended by the Court Interpreters Act, includes \xe2\x80\x9ccompensation of interpreters\xe2\x80\x9d among the costs that may be awarded to prevailing parties in federal-court lawsuits. \xc2\xa71920(6). In this case, the District Court awarded costs to respondent as the prevailing party in a civil action instituted by petitioner. The award included the cost of translating from Japanese to English certain documents that respondent used in preparing its defense. The Ninth Circuit affirmed, concluding that \xc2\xa71920(6) covers the cost of translating documents as well as the cost of translating live speech.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0Because the ordinary meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d is someone who translates orally from one language to another, the category \xe2\x80\x9ccompensation of interpreters\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa71920(6) does not include the cost of document translation. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x88\x9215.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0Section 1920 reflects the substance of an 1853 Act that specified for the first time what costs are allowable in federal court. That provision defines the term \xe2\x80\x9ccosts\xe2\x80\x9d as used in Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 54(d), which gives courts the discretion to award costs to prevailing parties. Crawford Fitting Co. v. J. T. Gibbons, Inc., \n\n482 U.\xc2\xa0S. 437\n. As originally configured, \xc2\xa71920 contained five categories of taxable costs, but in 1978, Congress enacted the Court Interpreters Act, which added a sixth category that includes \xe2\x80\x9ccompensation of interpreters.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71920(6). Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x88\x925.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0Because the term \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d is not defined in the Court Interpreters Act or in any other relevant statutory provision, it must be given its ordinary meaning. Asgrow Seed Co. v. Winterboer, \n\n513 U.\xc2\xa0S. 179\n. When Congress passed that Act in 1978, many dictionaries defined \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d as one who translates spoken, as opposed to written, language. Pre-1978 legal dictionaries also generally defined \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cinterpret\xe2\x80\x9d in terms of oral translation. Respondent relies almost exclusively on a version of Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third New International Dictionary that defined \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cone that translates; esp: a person who translates orally for parties conversing in different tongues.\xe2\x80\x9d Although the sense divider esp (for especially) indicates that the most common meaning of the term is one \xe2\x80\x9cwho translates orally,\xe2\x80\x9d that meaning is subsumed within the more general definition \xe2\x80\x9cone that translates.\xe2\x80\x9d That a definition is broad enough to encompass one sense of a word does not establish, however, that the word is ordinarily understood in that sense. See Mallard v. United States Dist. Court for Southern Dist. of Iowa, \n\n490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 296\n. Although all relevant dictionaries defined \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d at the time of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s enactment as including persons who translate orally, only a handful defined the word broadly enough to encompass translators of written materials. Notably, the Oxford English Dictionary, one of the most authoritative, recognized that \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d can mean one who translates writings, but it expressly designated that meaning as obsolete. Any definition of a word that is absent from many dictionaries and is deemed obsolete in others is hardly a common or ordinary meaning. Given this survey of relevant dictionaries, the ordinary meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d does not include those who translate writings. Nothing in the Court Interpreters Act or in \xc2\xa71920 hints that Congress intended to go beyond this ordinary meaning. If anything, the statutory context suggests that \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d includes only those who translate orally. See \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71827. Moreover, Congress\xe2\x80\x99 use of technical terminology reflects the distinction in relevant professional literature between interpreters, who are used for oral conversations, and translators, who are used for written communications. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x88\x9211.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0No other tool of construction compels a departure from the ordinary meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter.\xe2\x80\x9d This Court has never held that Rule 54(d) creates a presumption in favor of the broadest possible reading of the costs enumerated in \xc2\xa71920. To the contrary, the Court has made clear that the \xe2\x80\x9cdiscretion granted by Rule 54(d) is not a power to evade\xe2\x80\x9d the specific categories of costs set forth by Congress, Crawford Fitting, supra, at 442, but \xe2\x80\x9cis solely a power to decline to tax, as costs, the items enumerated in \xc2\xa71920,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion is in keeping with the narrow bounds of taxable costs, which are limited by statute and modest in scope. Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s extratextual arguments\xe2\x80\x95that documentary evidence is no less important than testimonial evidence and that some translation tasks are not entirely oral or entirely written\xe2\x80\x95are more properly directed at Congress. In any event, neither argument is so compelling that Congress must have intended to dispense with the ordinary meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa71920(6). Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x88\x9215.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Alito delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe costs that may be awarded to prevailing parties in lawsuits brought in federal court are set forth in \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71920. The Court Interpreters Act amended that statute to include \xe2\x80\x9ccompensation of interpreters.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71920(6); see also \xc2\xa77, \n\n92Stat.\n2044. The question pre- sented in this case is whether \xe2\x80\x9ccompensation of interpreters\xe2\x80\x9d covers the cost of translating documents. Because the ordinary meaning of the word \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d is a person who translates orally from one language to another, we hold that \xe2\x80\x9ccompensation of interpreters\xe2\x80\x9d is limited to the cost of oral translation and does not include the cost of document translation.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis case arises from a personal injury action brought by petitioner Kouichi Taniguchi, a professional baseball player in Japan, against respondent Kan Pacific Saipan, Ltd., the owner of a resort in the Northern Mariana Islands. Petitioner was injured when his leg broke through a wooden deck during a tour of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s resort prop- erty. Initially, petitioner said that he needed no medical attention, but two weeks later, he informed respondent that he had suffered cuts, bruises, and torn ligaments from the accident. Due to these alleged injuries, he claimed damages for medical expenses and for lost income from contracts he was unable to honor. After discovery concluded, both parties moved for summary judgment. The United States District Court for the Northern Ma- riana Islands granted respondent\xe2\x80\x99s motion on the ground that petitioner offered no evidence that respondent knew of the defective deck or otherwise failed to exercise reasonable care.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn preparing its defense, respondent paid to have various documents translated from Japanese to English. After the District Court granted summary judgment in respondent\xe2\x80\x99s favor, respondent submitted a bill for those costs. Over petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s objection, the District Court awarded the costs to respondent as \xe2\x80\x9ccompensation of interpreters\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa71920(6). Explaining that interpreter services \xe2\x80\x9ccan- not be separated into \xe2\x80\x98translation\xe2\x80\x99 and \xe2\x80\x98interpretation,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 25a, the court held that costs for document translation \xe2\x80\x9cfal[l] within the meaning of \xe2\x80\x98compensation of an interpreter,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. Finding that it was necessary for respondent to have the documents translated in order to depose petitioner, the court con- cluded that the translation services were properly taxed as costs.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit affirmed both the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s grant of summary judgment and its award of costs. The court rejected petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the cost of document translation services is not recoverable as \xe2\x80\x9ccompensation of interpreters.\xe2\x80\x9d The court explained that \xe2\x80\x9cthe word \xe2\x80\x98interpreter\xe2\x80\x99 can reasonably encompass a \xe2\x80\x98translator,\xe2\x80\x99 both according to the dictionary definition and common usage of these terms, which does not always draw precise distinctions between foreign language interpretations involving live speech versus written documents.\xe2\x80\x9d 633 F.\xc2\xa03d 1218, 1221 (2011). \xe2\x80\x9cMore importantly,\xe2\x80\x9d the court stressed, this construction of the statute \xe2\x80\x9cis more compatible with Rule 54 of the Fed- eral Rules of Civil Procedure, which includes a decided preference for the award of costs to the prevailing party.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The court thus concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthe prevailing party should be awarded costs for services required to interpret either live speech or written documents into a familiar language, so long as interpretation of the items is necessary to the litigation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1221\xe2\x80\x931222.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBecause there is a split among the Courts of Appeals on this issue,\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t we granted certiorari. 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2011).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAlthough the taxation of costs was not allowed at common law, it was the practice of federal courts in the early years to award costs in the same manner as the courts of the relevant forum State. Alyeska Pipeline Service Co. v. Wilderness Society, \n\n421 U.\xc2\xa0S. 240\n\xe2\x80\x93248 (1975). In 1793, Congress enacted a statute that authorized the awarding of certain costs to prevailing parties based on state law:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cThat there be allowed and taxed in the supreme, circuit and district courts of the United States, in favour of the parties obtaining judgments therein, such compensation for their travel and attendance, and for attornies and counsellors\xe2\x80\x99 fees .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. as are allowed in the supreme or superior courts of the respective states.\xe2\x80\x9d Act of Mar. 1, 1793, \xc2\xa74, \n\n1Stat.\n333.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tAlthough twice reenacted, this provision expired in 1799. Alyeska Pipeline, supra, at 248, n.\xc2\xa019; Crawford Fitting Co. v. J.\xc2\xa0T. Gibbons, Inc., \n\n482 U.\xc2\xa0S. 437, \n439 (1987)\n. Yet even in the absence of express legislative authorization, the practice of referring to state rules for the taxation of costs persisted. See Alyeska Pipeline, 421 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 250.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNot until 1853 did Congress enact legislation specifying the costs allowable in federal court. Id., at 251. The impetus for a uniform federal rule was largely the consequence of two developments. First, a \xe2\x80\x9cgreat diversity in practice among the courts\xe2\x80\x9d had emerged. Ibid. Second, \xe2\x80\x9closing litigants were being unfairly saddled with exorbitant fees for the victor\xe2\x80\x99s attorney.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Against this backdrop, Congress passed the 1853 Fee Act, which we have described as a \xe2\x80\x9cfar-reaching Act specifying in detail the nature and amount of the taxable items of cost in the federal courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 251\xe2\x80\x93252. The substance of this Act was transmitted through the Revised Statutes of 1874 and the Judicial Code of 1911 to the Revised Code of 1948, where it was codified, \xe2\x80\x9cwithout any apparent intent to change the controlling rules,\xe2\x80\x9d as \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71920. 421 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 255.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFederal Rule of Civil Procedure 54(d) gives courts the discretion to award costs to prevailing parties. That Rule provides in relevant part: \xe2\x80\x9cUnless a federal statute, these rules, or a court order provides otherwise, costs\xe2\x80\x94other than attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees\xe2\x80\x94should be allowed to the prevailing party.\xe2\x80\x9d Rule 54(d)(1). We have held that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa71920 defines the term \xe2\x80\x98costs\xe2\x80\x99 as used in Rule 54(d).\xe2\x80\x9d Crawford Fitting, 482 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 441. In so doing, we rejected the view that \xe2\x80\x9cthe discretion granted by Rule 54(d) is a separate source of power to tax as costs expenses not enumerated in \xc2\xa71920.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs originally configured, \xc2\xa71920 contained five categories of taxable costs: (1) \xe2\x80\x9c[f]ees of the clerk and marshal\xe2\x80\x9d; (2) \xe2\x80\x9c[f]ees of the court reporter for all or any part of the steno- graphic transcript necessarily obtained for use in the case\xe2\x80\x9d; (3) \xe2\x80\x9c[f]ees and disbursements for printing and witnesses\xe2\x80\x9d; (4) \xe2\x80\x9c[f]ees for exemplification and copies of papers necessarily obtained for use in the case\xe2\x80\x9d; and (5) \xe2\x80\x9c[d]ocket fees under section 1923 of this title.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n62Stat.\n955. In 1978, Congress enacted the Court Interpreters Act, which amended \xc2\xa71920 to add a sixth category: \xe2\x80\x9cCompensation of court appointed experts, compensation of interpreters, and salaries, fees, expenses, and costs of special interpretation services under section 1828 of this title.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71920(6); see also \xc2\xa77, \n\n92Stat.\n2044. We are concerned here with this sixth category, specifically the item of tax- able costs identified as \xe2\x80\x9ccompensation of interpreters.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo determine whether the item \xe2\x80\x9ccompensation of interpreters\xe2\x80\x9d includes costs for document translation, we must look to the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter.\xe2\x80\x9d That term is not defined in the Court Interpreters Act or in any other relevant statutory provision. When a term goes undefined in a statute, we give the term its ordinary meaning. Asgrow Seed Co. v. Winterboer, \n\n513 U.\xc2\xa0S. 179, \n187 (1995)\n. The question here is: What is the ordinary meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d?\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMany dictionaries in use when Congress enacted the Court Interpreters Act in 1978 defined \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d as one who translates spoken, as opposed to written, language. The American Heritage Dictionary, for instance, defined the term as \xe2\x80\x9c[o]ne who translates orally from one language into another.\xe2\x80\x9d American Heritage Dictionary 685 (1978). The Scribner-Bantam English Dictionary defined the related word \xe2\x80\x9cinterpret\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cto translate orally.\xe2\x80\x9d Scribner-Bantam English Dictionary 476 (1977). Similarly, the Random House Dictionary defined the intransitive form of \xe2\x80\x9cinterpret\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cto translate what is said in a foreign language.\xe2\x80\x9d Random House Dictionary of the English Language 744 (1973) (emphasis added). And, notably, the Oxford English Dictionary defined \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9c[o]ne who translates languages,\xe2\x80\x9d but then divided that definition into two senses: \xe2\x80\x9ca. [a] translator of books or writings,\xe2\x80\x9d which it designated as obsolete, and \xe2\x80\x9cb. [o]ne who translates the communications of persons speaking different languages; spec. one whose office it is to do so orally in the presence of the persons; a dragoman.\xe2\x80\x9d 5 Oxford English Dictionary 416 (1933); see also Concise Oxford Dictionary of Current English 566 (6th ed. 1976) (\xe2\x80\x9cOne who interprets; one whose office it is to translate the words of persons speaking different languages, esp. orally in their presence\xe2\x80\x9d); Chambers Twentieth Century Dictionary 686 (1973) (\xe2\x80\x9cone who translates orally for the benefit of two or more parties speaking different languages: .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a translator (obs.)\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPre-1978 legal dictionaries also generally defined the words \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cinterpret\xe2\x80\x9d in terms of oral translation. The then-current edition of Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary, for example, defined \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9c[a] person sworn at a trial to interpret the evidence of a foreigner .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to the court,\xe2\x80\x9d and it defined \xe2\x80\x9cinterpret\xe2\x80\x9d in relevant part as \xe2\x80\x9cto translate orally from one tongue to another.\xe2\x80\x9d Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 954, 953 (rev. 4th ed. 1968); see also W. Anderson, A Dictionary of Law 565 (1888) (\xe2\x80\x9cOne who translates the testimony of witnesses speaking a foreign tongue, for the benefit of the court and jury\xe2\x80\x9d); 1 B. Abbott, Dictionary of Terms and Phrases Used in American or English Jurisprudence 639 (1878) (\xe2\x80\x9cone who restates the testimony of a witness testifying in a foreign tongue, to the court and jury, in their language\xe2\x80\x9d). But see Ballentine\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 655, 654 (3d ed. 1969) (defining \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9c[o]ne who interprets, particularly one who interprets words written or spoken in a foreign language,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cinterpret\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cto translate from a foreign language\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAgainst these authorities, respondent relies almost exclusively on Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third New International Dictionary (hereinafter Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third). The version of that dictionary in print when Congress enacted the Court Interpreters Act defined \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cone that translates; esp: a person who translates orally for parties conversing in different tongues.\xe2\x80\x9d Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third 1182 (1976).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t The sense divider esp (for especially) indicates that the most common meaning of the term is one \xe2\x80\x9cwho translates orally,\xe2\x80\x9d but that meaning is subsumed within the more general definition \xe2\x80\x9cone that translates.\xe2\x80\x9d See 12,000 Words: A Supplement to Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third 15a (1986) (explaining that esp \xe2\x80\x9cis used to introduce the most common meaning included in the more general preceding definition\xe2\x80\x9d). For respondent, the general definition suf- fices to establish that the term \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d ordinarily includes persons who translate the written word. Explaining that \xe2\x80\x9cthe word \xe2\x80\x98interpreter\xe2\x80\x99 can reasonably encompass a \xe2\x80\x98translator,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the Court of Appeals reached the same conclusion. 633 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1221. We disagree.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThat a definition is broad enough to encompass one sense of a word does not establish that the word is ordinarily understood in that sense. See Mallard v. United States Dist. Court for Southern Dist. of Iowa, \n\n490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 296, \n301 (1989)\n (relying on the \xe2\x80\x9cmost common meaning\xe2\x80\x9d and the \xe2\x80\x9cordinary and natural signification\xe2\x80\x9d of the word \xe2\x80\x9crequest,\xe2\x80\x9d even though it may sometimes \xe2\x80\x9cdouble for \xe2\x80\x98demand\xe2\x80\x99 or \xe2\x80\x98command\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). The fact that the definition of \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d in Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third has a sense divider denoting the most common usage suggests that other usages, although acceptable, might not be common or ordinary. It is telling that all the dictionaries cited above defined \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d at the time of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s enactment as including persons who translate orally, but only a handful defined the word broadly enough to encompass translators of written material. See supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x937. Although the Oxford English Dictionary, one of the most authoritative on the English language, recognized that \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d can mean one who translates writings, it expressly designated that meaning as obsolete. See supra, at 6. Were the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d that respondent advocates truly common or ordinary, we would expect to see more support for that meaning. We certainly would not expect to see it designated as obsolete in the Oxford English Dictionary. Any definition of a word that is absent from many dictionaries and is deemed obsolete in others is hardly a common or ordinary meaning.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBased on our survey of the relevant dictionaries, we conclude that the ordinary or common meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d does not include those who translate writings. Instead, we find that an interpreter is normally understood as one who translates orally from one language to another. This sense of the word is far more natural. As the Seventh Circuit put it: \xe2\x80\x9cRobert Fagles made famous translations into English of the Iliad, the Odyssey, and the Aeneid, but no one would refer to him as an English-language \xe2\x80\x98interpreter\xe2\x80\x99 of these works.\xe2\x80\x9d Extra Equipamentos E Exporta\xc3\xa7\xc3\xa3o Ltda. v. Case Corp., 541 F.\xc2\xa03d 719, 727 (2008).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo be sure, the word \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d can encompass persons who translate documents, but because that is not the ordinary meaning of the word, it does not control unless the context in which the word appears indicates that it does. Nothing in the Court Interpreters Act or in \xc2\xa71920, however, even hints that Congress intended to go beyond the ordinary meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d and to embrace the broadest possible meaning that the definition of the word can bear.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIf anything, the statutory context suggests the opposite: that the word \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d applies only to those who translate orally. As previously mentioned, Congress en- acted \xc2\xa71920(6) as part of the Court Interpreters Act. The main provision of that Act is \xc2\xa72(a), codified in 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71827 and 1828. See \n\n92Stat.\n2040\xe2\x80\x932042. Particularly relevant here is \xc2\xa71827. As it now reads, that statute provides for the establishment of \xe2\x80\x9ca program to facilitate the use of certified and otherwise qualified interpreters in judicial proceedings instituted by the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71827(a). Subsection (d) directs courts to use an interpreter in any criminal or civil action instituted by the United States if a party or witness \xe2\x80\x9cspeaks only or primarily a language other than the English language\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9csuffers from a hearing impairment\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cso as to inhibit such party\xe2\x80\x99s comprehension of the proceedings or communication with counsel or the presiding judicial officer, or so as to inhibit such witness\xe2\x80\x99 comprehension of questions and the presentation of such testimony.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71827(d)(1).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t As originally enacted, subsection (k) mandated that the \xe2\x80\x9cinterpretation provided by certified interpreters .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall be in the consecutive mode except that the presiding judicial officer .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. may authorize a simultaneous or summary interpretation.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71827(k) (1976 ed., Supp. II); see also \n\n92Stat.\n2042. In its current form, subsection (k) provides that interpretation \xe2\x80\x9cshall be in the simultaneous mode for any party .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and in the consecutive mode for witnesses,\xe2\x80\x9d unless the court directs otherwise. The simultaneous, consecutive, and summary modes are all methods of oral interpretation and have nothing to do with the translation of writings.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Taken together, these provisions are a strong contextual clue that Congress was dealing only with oral translation in the Court Interpreters Act and that it intended to use the term \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d throughout the Act in its ordinary sense as someone who translates the spoken word. As we have said before, it is a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98normal rule of statutory construction\xe2\x80\x99 that \xe2\x80\x98identical words used in different parts of the same act are intended to have the same meaning.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Gustafson v. Alloyd Co., \n\n513 U.\xc2\xa0S. 561, \n570 (1995)\n (quoting Department of Revenue of Ore. v. ACF Industries, Inc., \n\n510 U.\xc2\xa0S. 332, \n342 (1994)\n).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe references to technical terminology in the Court Interpreters Act further suggest that Congress used \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d in a technical sense, and it is therefore significant that relevant professional literature draws a line between \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreters,\xe2\x80\x9d who \xe2\x80\x9care used for oral conversations,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ctranslators,\xe2\x80\x9d who \xe2\x80\x9care used for written communications.\xe2\x80\x9d Zazueta, supra n.\xc2\xa04, at 477; see also M. Frankenthaler, Skills for Bilingual Legal Personnel 67 (1982) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhile the translator deals with the written word, the interpreter is concerned with the spoken language\xe2\x80\x9d); Brislin, Introduction, in Translation: Applications and Research 1 (R. Brislin ed. 1976) (explaining that when both terms are used together, translation \xe2\x80\x9crefers to the processing [of] written input, and interpretation to the processing of oral input\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis deleted)); J. Herbert, Interpreter\xe2\x80\x99s Handbook 1 (2d ed. 1952) (\xe2\x80\x9cIn the present-day jargon of international organisations, the words translate, translations, translator are used when the immediate result of the work is a written text; and the words interpret, interpreter, interpretation when it is a speech delivered orally\xe2\x80\x9d). That Congress specified \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreters\xe2\x80\x9d but not \xe2\x80\x9ctranslators\xe2\x80\x9d is yet another signal that it intended to limit \xc2\xa71920(6) to the costs of oral, instead of written, translation.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn sum, both the ordinary and technical meanings of \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter,\xe2\x80\x9d as well as the statutory context in which the word is found, lead to the conclusion that \xc2\xa71920(6) does not apply to translators of written materials.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t7\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNo other rule of construction compels us to depart from the ordinary meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter.\xe2\x80\x9d The Court of Appeals reasoned that a broader meaning is \xe2\x80\x9cmore compat- ible with Rule 54 of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, which includes a decided preference for the award of costs to the prevailing party.\xe2\x80\x9d 633 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1221. But we have never held that Rule 54(d) creates a presumption of statutory construction in favor of the broadest possible reading of the costs enumerated in \xc2\xa71920. To the contrary, we have made clear that the \xe2\x80\x9cdiscretion granted by Rule 54(d) is not a power to evade\xe2\x80\x9d the specific categories of costs set forth by Congress. Crawford Fitting, 482 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 442. \xe2\x80\x9cRather,\xe2\x80\x9d we have said, \xe2\x80\x9cit is solely a power to decline to tax, as costs, the items enumerated in \xc2\xa71920.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Rule 54(d) thus provides no sound basis for casting aside the ordinary meaning of the various items enumerated in the costs statute, including the ordinary meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur decision is in keeping with the narrow scope of taxable costs. \xe2\x80\x9cAlthough \xe2\x80\x98costs\xe2\x80\x99 has an everyday meaning synonymous with \xe2\x80\x98expenses,\xe2\x80\x99 the concept of taxable costs under Rule 54(d) is more limited and represents those expenses, including, for example, court fees, that a court will assess against a litigant.\xe2\x80\x9d 10 C. Wright, A. Miller, & M. Kane, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa72666, pp. 202\xe2\x80\x93203 (3d ed. 1998) (hereinafter Wright & Miller). Taxable costs are limited to relatively minor, incidental expenses as is evident from \xc2\xa71920, which lists such items as clerk fees, court reporter fees, expenses for printing and witnesses, expenses for exemplification and copies, docket fees, and compensation of court-appointed experts. Indeed, \xe2\x80\x9cthe assessment of costs most often is merely a clerical matter that can be done by the court clerk.\xe2\x80\x9d Hairline Creations, Inc. v. Kefalas, 664 F.\xc2\xa02d 652, 656 (CA7 1981). Taxable costs are a fraction of the nontaxable expenses borne by litigants for attorneys, experts, consultants, and investigators. It comes as little surprise, therefore, that \xe2\x80\x9ccosts almost always amount to less than the successful litigant\xe2\x80\x99s total expenses in connection with a lawsuit.\xe2\x80\x9d 10 Wright & Miller \xc2\xa72666, at 203. Because taxable costs are limited by statute and are modest in scope, we see no compelling reason to stretch the ordinary meaning of the cost items Congress authorized in \xc2\xa71920.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs for respondent\xe2\x80\x99s extratextual arguments, they are more properly directed at Congress. Respondent contends that documentary evidence is no less important than testimonial evidence and that it would be anomalous to require the losing party to cover translation costs for spoken words but not for written words. Brief for Respondent 20. Respondent also observes that some translation tasks are not entirely oral or entirely written. Id., at 20\xe2\x80\x9324. One task, called \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98sight translation,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d involves the oral translation of a document. Id., at 21. Another task involves the written translation of speech. Ibid. And a third task, called \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98document comparison,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d involves comparing documents in the source and target language to verify that the two are identical. Id., at 21\xe2\x80\x9322. Respondent argues that a narrow definition cannot account for these variations and that a bright-line definition of \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d as someone who translates spoken and written words would avoid complication and provide a simple, administrable rule for district courts.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNeither of these arguments convinces us that Congress must have intended to dispense with the ordinary meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa71920(6). First, Congress might have distinguished between oral and written translation out of a concern that requiring losing parties to bear the potentially sizable costs of translating discovery docu- ments, as opposed to the more limited costs of oral tes- timony, could be too burdensome and possibly unfair, especially for litigants with limited means. Cf. Fleischmann Distilling Corp. v. Maier Brewing Co., \n\n386 U.\xc2\xa0S. 714, \n718 (1967)\n (noting the argument \xe2\x80\x9cthat since litigation is at best uncertain one should not be penalized for merely defending or prosecuting a lawsuit, and that the poor might be unjustly discouraged from instituting actions to vindicate their rights if the penalty for losing included the fees of their opponents\xe2\x80\x99 counsel\xe2\x80\x9d). Congress might also have concluded that a document translator is more akin to an expert or consultant retained by a party to decipher documentary evidence\xe2\x80\x94like, for instance, a forensic accountant\xe2\x80\x94than to an interpreter whose real-time oral translation services are necessary for communication between litigants, witnesses, and the court.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t8\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSecond, respondent has not shown that any of the hybrid translation/interpretation tasks to which it points actually arise with overwhelming frequency or that the problem of drawing the line between taxable and nontax- able costs in such cases will vex the trial courts. It certainly has not shown that any such problems will be more troublesome than the task of sifting through translated discovery documents to ascertain which can be taxed as necessary to the litigation. In any event, the present case does not present a hybrid situation; it involves purely written translation, which falls outside the tasks performed by an \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d as that term is ordinarily understood.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBecause the ordinary meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d is someone who translates orally from one language to another, we hold that the category \xe2\x80\x9ccompensation of interpreters\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa71920(6) does not include costs for document translation. We therefore vacate the judgment of the United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.\n\t\t\t

See Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third New International Dictionary of the English Language 1182 (1976) (here- inafter Webster\xe2\x80\x99s) (defining \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cone that translates; esp: a person who translates orally for parties conversing in different tongues\xe2\x80\x9d); Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 895 (9th ed. 2009) (defining \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d as a \xe2\x80\x9cperson who translates, esp. orally, from one language to another\xe2\x80\x9d); Ballentine\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 655 (3d ed. 1969) (defining \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9c[o]ne who interprets, particularly one who interprets words written or spoken in a foreign language\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn short, employing the word \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreters\xe2\x80\x9d to include translators of written as well as oral speech, if not \xe2\x80\x9cthe most common usage,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 8, is at least an \xe2\x80\x9cacceptable\xe2\x80\x9d usage, ibid. Moreover, the word \xe2\x80\x9cinterpret\xe2\x80\x9d is generally understood to mean \xe2\x80\x9cto explain or tell the meaning of: translate into intelligible or familiar language or terms,\xe2\x80\x9d while \xe2\x80\x9ctranslate\xe2\x80\x9d commonly means \xe2\x80\x9cto turn into one\xe2\x80\x99s own or another language.\xe2\x80\x9d Webster\xe2\x80\x99s 1182, 2429. See also Random House Dictionary of the English Language 744, 1505 (1973) (defining the transitive verb \xe2\x80\x9cinterpret\xe2\x80\x9d as, inter alia, \xe2\x80\x9cto translate,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ctranslate\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cto turn (something written or spoken) from one language into another\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNotably, several federal district court decisions refer to translators of written documents as \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreters.\xe2\x80\x9d E.g., United States v. Prado-Cervantez, No. 11\xe2\x80\x9340044\xe2\x80\x9311, 2011 WL 4691934, *3 (Kan., Oct. 6, 2011) (\xe2\x80\x9cStandby counsel should also be prepared to arrange for interpreters to interpret or translate documents when necessary for defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d); Mendoza v. Ring, No. 07\xe2\x80\x933114, 2008 WL 2959848, *2 (CD Ill., July 30, 2008) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe interpreter is also directed to translate filings by the plaintiff from Spanish to English. The original and translated versions will be docketed.\xe2\x80\x9d). So do a number of state statutes. E.g., Cal. Govt. Code Ann. \xc2\xa726806(a) (West 2008) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he clerk of the court may employ as many foreign language interpreters as may be necessary .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to translate documents in- tended for filing in any civil or criminal action .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMost federal courts of appeals confronted with the question have held that costs may be awarded under \xc2\xa71920(6) for the translation of documents necessary to, or in preparation for, litigation. Compare 633 F.\xc2\xa03d 1218, 1220\xe2\x80\x931222 (CA9 2011); BDT Prods., Inc. v. Lexmark Int\xe2\x80\x99l, Inc., 405 F.\xc2\xa03d 415, 419 (CA6 2005); Slagenweit v. Slagenweit, 63 F.\xc2\xa03d 719, 721 (CA8 1995) (per\xc2\xa0curiam); and Chore-Time Equip., Inc. v. Cumberland Corp., 713 F.\xc2\xa02d 774, 782 (CA Fed. 1983) (all holding that costs for document translation are covered by \xc2\xa71920(6)), with Extra Equipamentos E Exporta\xc3\xa7\xc3\xa3o Ltda. v. Case Corp., 541 F.\xc2\xa03d 719, 727\xe2\x80\x93728 (CA7 2008) (costs for document translation are not covered by \xc2\xa71920(6)). See also In\xc2\xa0re Puerto Rico Elec. Power Auth., 687 F.\xc2\xa02d 501, 506, 510 (CA1 1982) (recognizing that costs of document translation may be reimbursed, with- out specifying the relevant subsection of \xc2\xa71920); Studiengesellschaft Kohle mbH v. Eastman Kodak Co., 713 F.\xc2\xa02d 128, 133 (CA5 1983) (allowing document translation costs under \xc2\xa71920(4)); Quy v. Air Am., Inc., 667 F.\xc2\xa02d 1059, 1065 (CADC 1981) (allowing \xe2\x80\x9ctranslation costs\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa71920(6)).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn practice, federal trial courts have awarded document translation costs in cases spanning several decades. See, e.g., Raffold Process Corp. v. Castanea Paper Co., 25 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 593, 594 (WD Pa. 1938). Before the Court Interpreters Act added \xc2\xa71920(6) to the taxation of costs statute in 1978, district courts awarded costs for document translation under \xc2\xa71920(4), which allowed taxation of \xe2\x80\x9c[f]ees for exemplification and copies of papers,\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n28 U. S. C. \xc2\xa71920(4) (1976 ed.), or under \xc2\xa71920\xe2\x80\x99s predecessor, \n\n28 U. S. C. \xc2\xa7830 (1925 ed.). See, e.g., Bennett Chemical Co. v. Atlantic Commodities, Ltd., 24 F. R. D. 200, 204 (SDNY 1959) (\xc2\xa71920(4)); Raffold Process Corp., 25 F. Supp., at 594 (\xc2\xa7830). Pre-1978, district courts also awarded costs for oral translation of witness testimony. See, e.g., Kaiser Industries Corp. v. McLouth Steel Corp., 50 F. R. D. 5, 11 (ED Mich. 1970). Nothing in the Court Interpreters Act, a measure intended to expand access to interpretation services, indicates a design to eliminate the availability of costs awards for document translation. See S. Rep. No. 95\xe2\x80\x93569, p. 4 (1977) (hereinafter S.\xc2\xa0Rep.) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe committee\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. feels the time has come to provide by statute for the provision of and access to qualified certified interpreters, for a broader spectrum of people than the present law allows.\xe2\x80\x9d). Post-1978, rulings awarding document translation costs under \xc2\xa71920(6) indicate the courts\xe2\x80\x99 understanding both that the term \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreter\xe2\x80\x9d can readily encompass oral and written translation, and that Congress did not otherwise instruct.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t I agree that context should guide the determination whether \xc2\xa71920(b) is most sensibly read to encompass persons who translate documents. See ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939. But the context key for me is the practice of federal courts both before and after \xc2\xa71920(6)\xe2\x80\x99s enactment.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe purpose of translation, after all, is to make relevant foreign-language communication accessible to the litigants and the court. See S.\xc2\xa0Rep., at 1 (The Court Interpreters Act is intended \xe2\x80\x9cto insure that all participants in our Federal courts can meaningfully take part.\xe2\x80\x9d). Documentary evidence in a foreign language, no less than oral statements, must be translated to equip the parties to present their case clearly and the court to decide the merits intelligently. See, e.g., United States v. Mosquera, 816 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 168, 175 (EDNY 1993) (\xe2\x80\x9cFor a non-English speaking [party] to stand equal with others before the court requires translation [of relevant documents].\xe2\x80\x9d); Lockett v. Hellenic Sea Transports, Ltd., 60 F.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0D. 469, 473 (ED Pa. 1973) (\xe2\x80\x9cTo be understood by counsel for plaintiffs and defendant, as well as for use at trial, the [ship\xe2\x80\x99s] deck log had to be translated [from Greek] into the English language.\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t And it is not extraordinary that what documents say, more than what witnesses testify, may make or break a case.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tDistinguishing written from oral translation for cost-award purposes, moreover, is an endeavor all the more dubious, for, as the Court acknowledges, ante, at 13, some translation tasks do not fall neatly into one category or the other. An interpreter, for example, may be called upon to \xe2\x80\x9csight translate\xe2\x80\x9d a written document, i.e., to convey a written foreign-language document\xe2\x80\x99s content orally in English. R. Gonz\xc3\xa1lez, V. V\xc3\xa1squez, & H. Mikkelson, Fundamentals of Court Interpretation: Theory, Policy and Practice 401 (1991) (hereinafter Gonz\xc3\xa1lez). In-court sight translation, Taniguchi concedes, counts as \xe2\x80\x9cinterpretation,\xe2\x80\x9d even though it does not involve translating verbal expression. Tr. of Oral Arg. 10. Yet an interpreter\xe2\x80\x99s preparation for in-court sight translation by translating a written document in advance, Taniguchi maintains, does not count as \xe2\x80\x9cinterpretation.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. But if the interpreter then reads the prepared written translation aloud in court, that task, in Taniguchi\xe2\x80\x99s view, can be charged as \xe2\x80\x9cinterpretation,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 11, even though the reading involves no translation of foreign-language expression\xe2\x80\x94written or oral\xe2\x80\x94at all.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSimilarly hard to categorize is the common court-interpreter task of listening to a recording in a foreign language, transcribing it, then translating it into Eng- lish. See Gonz\xc3\xa1lez 439. Although this task involves oral foreign-language communication, it does not, Taniguchi contends, qualify as \xe2\x80\x9cinterpretation,\xe2\x80\x9d because it involves \xe2\x80\x9cthe luxury of multiple playbacks of the tape and the leisure to consult extrinsic linguistic sources.\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief for Petitioner 9 (internal quotation marks omitted). But sight translation\xe2\x80\x94which Taniguchi concedes may be charged as \xe2\x80\x9cinterpretation\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94may sometimes involve sim- ilarly careful linguistic analysis of a written document in advance of a court proceeding. Davis & Hewitt, Lessons in Administering Justice: What Judges Need to Know about the Requirements, Role, and Professional Responsibilities of the Court Interpreter, 1 Harv. Latino L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 121, 131 (1994).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTaniguchi warns that translation costs can be exorbitant and burdensome to police. Reply Brief 19\xe2\x80\x9322; Tr. of Oral Arg. 20\xe2\x80\x9321. The Court expresses a similar concern. Ante, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Current practice in awarding translation costs, however, has shown that district judges are up to the task of confining awards to translation services necessary to present or defeat a claim. See Eastman Kodak Co., 713 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 133 (district court should not award document translation costs \xe2\x80\x9ccarte blanche,\xe2\x80\x9d but must determine whether such costs were necessarily incurred). See also, e.g., Conn v. Zakharov, No. 1:09 CV 0760, 2010 WL 2293133, *3 (ND Ohio, June 4, 2010) (denying translation costs where prevailing party did not demonstrate the costs were necessary); Maker\xe2\x80\x99s Mark Distillery, Inc. v. Diageo North Am., Inc., No. 3:03\xe2\x80\x93CV\xe2\x80\x9393, 2010 WL 2651186, *3 (WD Ky., June 30, 2010) (same); Competitive Technologies v. Fujitsu Ltd., No. C\xe2\x80\x9302\xe2\x80\x931673, 2006 WL 6338914, *11 (ND Cal., Aug. 23, 2006) (same); Arboireau v. Adidas Salomon AG, No. CV\xe2\x80\x9301\xe2\x80\x93105, 2002 WL 31466564, *6 (Ore., June 14, 2002) (same); Oetiker v. Jurid Werke, GmbH, 104 F.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0D. 389, 393 (DC 1982) (same); Lockett, 60 F.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0D., at 473 (awarding costs for \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary\xe2\x80\x9d translations); Kaiser, 50 F.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0D., at 11\xe2\x80\x9312 (same); Bennett, 24 F.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0D., at 204 (same); Raffold Process Corp., 25 F.\xc2\xa0Supp., at 594 (same). Courts of appeals, in turn, are capable of reviewing such judgments for abuse of discretion.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn short, \xc2\xa71920(6)\xe2\x80\x99s prescription on \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreters\xe2\x80\x9d is not so clear as to leave no room for interpretation. Given the purpose served by translation and the practice prevailing in district courts, supra, at 3, there is no good reason to exclude from taxable costs payments for placing written words within the grasp of parties, jurors, and judges. I would therefore affirm the judgment of the Ninth Circuit.\n\t\t\t
