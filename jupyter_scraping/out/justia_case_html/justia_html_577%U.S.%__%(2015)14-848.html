SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

\n

MARYLAND, PETITIONER v. JAMES KULBICKI

\n

on petition for writ of certiorari to the court of appeals of maryland

\n

No. 14\xe2\x80\x93848.\xe2\x80\x83Decided October 5, 2015

\n

\n

Per Curiam.

\n

A criminal defendant \xe2\x80\x9cshall enjoy the right .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to have the Assistance of Counsel for his defence.\xe2\x80\x9d U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Amdt. 6. We have held that this right requires effective counsel in both state and federal prosecutions, even if the defendant is unable to afford counsel. Gideon v. Wainwright,\n\n372 U.\xc2\xa0S. 335,\n344 (1963)\n. Counsel is unconstitutionally ineffective if his performance is both deficient, meaning his errors are \xe2\x80\x9cso serious\xe2\x80\x9d that he no longer functions as \xe2\x80\x9ccounsel,\xe2\x80\x9d and prejudicial, meaning his errors deprive the defendant of a fair trial. Strickland v. Washington,\n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 668,\n687 (1984)\n. Applying this standard in name only, the Court of Appeals of Maryland held that James Kulbicki\xe2\x80\x99s defense attorneys were unconstitutionally ineffective. We summarily reverse.

\n

In 1993, Kulbicki shot his 22-year-old mistress in the head at pointblank range. The two had been ensnarled in a paternity suit, and the killing occurred the weekend before a scheduled hearing about unpaid child support. At Kulbicki\xe2\x80\x99s trial, commencing in 1995, Agent Ernest Peele of the FBI testified as the State\xe2\x80\x99s expert on Comparative Bullet Lead Analysis, or CBLA. In testimony of the sort CBLA experts had provided for decades, Peele testified that the composition of elements in the molten lead of a bullet fragment found in Kulbicki\xe2\x80\x99s truck matched the composition of lead in a bullet fragment removed from the victim\xe2\x80\x99s brain; a similarity of the sort one would \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98expect\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d if \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98examining two pieces of the same bullet.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 440 Md. 33, 41, 99 A.\xc2\xa03d 730, 735 (2014). He further testified that a bullet taken from Kulbicki\xe2\x80\x99s gun was not an \xe2\x80\x9cexac[t]\xe2\x80\x9d match to the bullet fragments, but was similar enough that the two bullets likely came from the same package. Id., at 42\xe2\x80\x9344, 99 A.\xc2\xa03d, at 735\xe2\x80\x93736. After considering this ballistics evidence, additional physical evidence from Kulbicki\xe2\x80\x99s truck, and witness testimony, the jury convicted Kulbicki of first-degree murder.

\n

Kulbicki then filed a petition for post-conviction relief, which lingered in state court until 2006 when Kulbicki added a claim that his defense attorneys were ineffective for failing to question the legitimacy of CBLA. By then, 11 years after his conviction, CBLA had fallen out of favor. Indeed, Kulbicki supplemented his petition once more in 2006 after the Court of Appeals of Maryland held for the first time that CBLA evidence was not generally accepted by the scientific community and was therefore inadmissible. See Clemons v. State, 392 Md. 339, 371, 896 A.\xc2\xa02d 1059, 1078 (2006).

\n

Kulbicki lost in the lower state courts and appealed to the Court of Appeals of Maryland. At that point, Kulbicki abandoned his claim of ineffective assistance with respect to the CBLA evidence, but the high court vacated Kulbicki\xe2\x80\x99s conviction on that ground alone. Kulbicki\xe2\x80\x99s counsel, according to the court, should have found a report coauthored by Agent Peele in 1991 that \xe2\x80\x9cpresaged the flaws in CBLA evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d 440 Md., at 40, 99 A.\xc2\xa03d, at 734. One of the many findings of the report was that the composition of lead in some bullets was the same as that of lead in other bullets packaged many months later in a separate box. Rather than conduct \xe2\x80\x9cfurther research to explain the existence of overlapping compositions,\xe2\x80\x9d the authors \xe2\x80\x9cspeculated\xe2\x80\x9d that coincidence (or, in one case, the likelihood that separately packaged bullets originated from the same source of lead) caused the overlap. Id., at 49, 99 A.\xc2\xa03d, at 739. The Court of Appeals opined that this lone finding should have caused the report\xe2\x80\x99s authors to doubt \xe2\x80\x9cthat bullets produced from different sources of lead would have a unique chemical composition,\xe2\x80\x9d the faulty assumption that ultimately led the court to reject CBLA evidence 15 years later. Ibid.; see Clemons, supra, 369\xe2\x80\x93370, 896 A.\xc2\xa02d, at 1077. The authors\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cfailure to fully explore the variance,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court of Appeals concluded, was \xe2\x80\x9cat odds with the scientific method.\xe2\x80\x9d 440 Md., at 50, 99 A.\xc2\xa03d, at 740.

\n

In the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 view, any good attorney should have spotted this methodological flaw. The court held that counsel\xe2\x80\x99s failure to unearth the report, to identify one of its findings as \xe2\x80\x9cat odds with the scientific method,\xe2\x80\x9d and to use this methodological flaw to cast doubt on CBLA during counsel\xe2\x80\x99s cross-examination of Peele, \xe2\x80\x9cfell short of prevailing professional norms.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 50\xe2\x80\x9353, 99 A.\xc2\xa03d, at 740\xe2\x80\x93742. Concluding that counsel\xe2\x80\x99s supposed deficiency was prejudicial, the court set aside the conviction and ordered a new trial. Id., at 56, 99 A.\xc2\xa03d, at 743\xe2\x80\x93744.

\n

We reverse. The Court of Appeals offered no support for its conclusion that Kulbicki\xe2\x80\x99s defense attorneys were constitutionally required to predict the demise of CBLA. Instead, the court indulged in the \xe2\x80\x9cnatural tendency to speculate as to whether a different trial strategy might have been more successful.\xe2\x80\x9d Lockhart v. Fretwell,\n\n506 U.\xc2\xa0S. 364,\n372 (1993)\n. To combat this tendency, we have \xe2\x80\x9cadopted the rule of contemporary assessment of counsel\xe2\x80\x99s conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Had the Court of Appeals heeded this rule, it would have \xe2\x80\x9cjudge[d] the reasonableness of counsel\xe2\x80\x99s challenged conduct . . . viewed as of the time of counsel\xe2\x80\x99s conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d Strickland, supra, at 690.

\n

At the time of Kulbicki\xe2\x80\x99s trial in 1995, the validity of CBLA was widely accepted, and courts regularly admitted CBLA evidence until 2003. See United States v. Higgs, 663 F.\xc2\xa03d 726, 738 (CA4 2011). As the Court of Appeals acknowledged, even the 1991 report itself did not question the validity of CBLA, concluding that it was a valid and useful forensic tool to match suspect to victim. 440 Md., at 51, n.\xc2\xa011, 99 A.\xc2\xa03d, at 740, n.\xc2\xa011. Counsel did not perform deficiently by dedicating their time and focus to elements of the defense that did not involve poking methodological holes in a then-uncontroversial mode of ballistics analysis.

\n

That is especially the case here, since there is no reason to believe that a diligent search would even have discovered the supposedly crucial report. The Court of Appeals offered a single citation in support of its sweeping statement that the report \xe2\x80\x9cwas available\xe2\x80\x9d to Kulbicki\xe2\x80\x99s counsel in 1995\xe2\x80\x94a Government Printing Office Web page accessed by the Court of Appeals, apparently conducting its own Internet research nearly two decades after the trial. Id., at 51, and n.\xc2\xa012, 99 A.\xc2\xa03d, at 741, and n.\xc2\xa012; see also Brief in Opposition 14. The Web page indicates that a compilation of forensic studies that included the report was \xe2\x80\x9cdistributed to various public libraries in 1994.\xe2\x80\x9d 440 Md., at 51, n.\xc2\xa012, 99 A. 3d, at 741, n.\xc2\xa012. But which ones? And in an era of card catalogues, not a worldwide web, what efforts would counsel have had to expend to find the compilation? And had they found it, would counsel really have combed through the entire compilation, and have identified the one (of many) findings in one of the reports, the disregard of which counsel would have recognized to be \xe2\x80\x9cat odds with the scientific method\xe2\x80\x9d? And then, would effective counsel really have brought to the attention of the jury a report whose conclusion was that CBLA was a valid investigative technique in cases just like Kulbicki\xe2\x80\x99s? Neither the Court of Appeals nor Kulbicki has answers. Given the uncontroversial nature of CBLA at the time of Kulbicki\xe2\x80\x99s trial, the effect of the judgment below is to demand that lawyers go \xe2\x80\x9clooking for a needle in a haystack,\xe2\x80\x9d even when they have \xe2\x80\x9creason to doubt there is any needle there.\xe2\x80\x9d Rompilla v. Beard,\n\n545 U.\xc2\xa0S. 374,\n389 (2005)\n. The Court of Appeals demanded something close to \xe2\x80\x9cperfect advocacy\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94far more than the \xe2\x80\x9creasonable competence\xe2\x80\x9d the right to counsel guarantees. Yarborough v. Gentry,\n\n540 U. S. 1,\n8 (2003)\n (per curiam).

\n

Kulbicki\xe2\x80\x99s trial counsel did not provide deficient performance when they failed to uncover the 1991 report and to use the report\xe2\x80\x99s so-called methodological flaw against Peele on cross-examination. (We need not, and so do not, decide whether the supposed error prejudiced Kulbicki.) The petition for writ of certiorari is granted, and the judgment of the Court of Appeals for Maryland is reversed.

\n

It is so ordered.
