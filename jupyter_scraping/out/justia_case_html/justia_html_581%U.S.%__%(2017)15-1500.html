Syllabus

\n

LEWIS et\xc2\xa0al. v. CLARKE

\n

certiorari to the supreme court of connecticut

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x931500.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 9, 2017\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 25, 2017

\n

Petitioners Brian and Michelle Lewis were driving on a Connecticut interstate when they were struck from behind by a vehicle driven by respondent William Clarke, a Mohegan Tribal Gaming Authority employee, who was transporting Mohegan Sun Casino patrons. The Lewises sued Clarke in his individual capacity in state court. Clarke moved to dismiss for lack of subject-matter jurisdiction, arguing that because he was an employee of the Gaming Authority\xe2\x80\x94an arm of the Mohegan Tribe entitled to sovereign immunity\xe2\x80\x94and was acting within the scope of his employment at the time of the accident, he was similarly entitled to sovereign immunity against suit. He also argued, in the alternative, that he should prevail because the Gaming Authority was bound by tribal law to indemnify him. The trial court denied Clarke\xe2\x80\x99s motion, but the Supreme Court of Connecticut reversed, holding that tribal sovereign immunity barred the suit because Clarke was acting within the scope of his employment when the accident occurred. It did not consider whether Clarke should be entitled to sovereign immunity based on the indemnification statute.

\n

Held:

\n

1.\xc2\xa0In a suit brought against a tribal employee in his individual capacity, the employee, not the tribe, is the real party in interest and the tribe\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign immunity is not implicated. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0 In the context of lawsuits against state and federal employees or entities, courts look to whether the sovereign is the real party in interest to determine whether sovereign immunity bars the suit, see Hafer v. Melo,\n\n502 U.\xc2\xa0S. 21\n. A defendant in an official-capacity action\xe2\x80\x94where the relief sought is only nominally against the official and in fact is against the official\xe2\x80\x99s office and thus the sovereign itself\xe2\x80\x94may assert sovereign immunity. Kentucky v. Graham,\n\n473 U.\xc2\xa0S. 159\n. But an officer in an individual-capacity action\xe2\x80\x94which seeks \xe2\x80\x9cto impose individual liability upon a government officer for actions taken under color of state law,\xe2\x80\x9d Hafer, 502 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 25\xe2\x80\x94may be able to assert personal immunity defenses but not sovereign immunity, id., at 30\xe2\x80\x9331. The Court does not reach Clarke\xe2\x80\x99s argument that he is entitled to the personal immunity defense of official immunity, which Clarke raised for the first time on appeal. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0Applying these general rules in the context of tribal sovereign immunity, it is apparent that they foreclose Clarke\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign immunity defense. This action arises from a tort committed by Clarke on a Connecticut interstate and is simply a suit against Clarke to recover for his personal actions. Clarke, not the Gaming Authority, is the real party in interest. The State Supreme Court extended sovereign immunity for tribal employees beyond what common-law sovereign immunity principles would recognize for either state or federal employees. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

2.\xc2\xa0An indemnification provision cannot, as a matter of law, extend sovereign immunity to individual employees who would otherwise not fall under its protective cloak. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0This conclusion follows naturally from the principles discussed above and previously applied to the different question whether a state instrumentality may invoke the State\xe2\x80\x99s immunity from suit even when the Federal Government has agreed to indemnify that instrumentality against adverse judgments, Regents of Univ. of Cal. v. Doe,\n\n519 U.\xc2\xa0S. 425\n. There, this Court held that the indemnification provision did not divest the state instrumentality of\n \nEleventh Amendment immunity, and its analysis turned on where the potential legal liability lay, not from whence the money to pay the damages award ultimately came. Here, the Connecticut courts exercise no jurisdiction over the Tribe or Gaming Authority, and their judgments will not bind the Tribe or its instrumentalities in any way. Moreover, indemnification is not a certainty, because Clarke will not be indemnified should the Gaming Authority determine that he engaged in \xe2\x80\x9cwanton, reckless, or malicious\xe2\x80\x9d activity. Mohegan Tribe Code \xc2\xa74\xe2\x80\x9352. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0Courts have extended sovereign immunity to private healthcare insurance companies under certain circumstances, but those cases rest on the proposition that the fiscal intermediaries are essentially state instrumentalities, and Clarke offers no persuasive reason to depart from precedent and treat a lawsuit against an individual employee as one against a state instrumentality. Similarly, this Court has never held that a civil rights suit under\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983 against a state officer in his individual capacity implicates the\n \nEleventh Amendment and a State\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign immunity from suit. Finally, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that indemnification provisions do not alter the real-party-in-interest analysis for sovereign immunity purposes is consistent with the practice that applies in the contexts of diversity of citizenship and joinder. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

320 Conn.706, 135 A. 3d 677, reversed and remanded.

\n

\n

BRIAN LEWIS, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v.WILLIAM CLARKE

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of connecticut

\n

[April 25, 2017]

\n

\n

Justice Sotomayor delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

Indian tribes are generally entitled to immunity from suit. This Court has considered the scope of that immu-nity in a number of circumstances. This case presents an ordinary negligence action brought against a tribal employee in state court under state law. We granted certiorari to resolve whether an Indian tribe\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign immu-nity bars individual-capacity damages actions against tribal employees for torts committed within the scope of their employment and for which the employees are indemnified by the tribe.

\n

We hold that, in a suit brought against a tribal employee in his individual capacity, the employee, not the tribe, is the real party in interest and the tribe\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign immu-nity is not implicated. That an employee was acting within the scope of his employment at the time the tort was committed is not, on its own, sufficient to bar a suit against that employee on the basis of tribal sovereign immunity. We hold further that an indemnification provision does not extend a tribe\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign immunity where it otherwise would not reach. Accordingly, we reverse and remand.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

The Mohegan Tribe of Indians of Connecticut traces its lineage back centuries. Originally part of the Lenni Lenape, the Tribe formed the independent Mohegan Tribe under the leadership of Sachem Uncas in the early 1600\xe2\x80\x99s. M. Fawcett, The Lasting of the Mohegans 7, 11\xe2\x80\x9313 (1995). In 1994, in accordance with the petition procedures established by the Bureau of Indian Affairs, the Tribe attained federal recognition.[1] See 59 Fed. Reg. 12140 (1994); Mohegan Const., Preamble and Art. II.

\n

As one means of maintaining its economic self-sufficiency, the Tribe entered into a Gaming Compact with the State of Connecticut pursuant to the Indian Gaming Regulatory Act,\n\n102Stat.\n2467,\n\n25 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72701 et seq. The compact authorizes the Tribe to conduct gaming on its land, subject to certain conditions including establishment of the Gaming Disputes Court. See 59 Fed. Reg. 65130 (approving the Tribal-State Compact Between the Mohegan Indian Tribe and the State of Connecticut (May 17, 1994)); Mohegan Const., Art. XIII, \xc2\xa72; Mohegan Tribe Code 3\xe2\x80\x93248(a) (Supp. 2016). The Mohegan Tribal Gaming Authority, an arm of the Tribe, exercises the powers of the Mohegan Tribe over tribal gaming activities. Mohegan Const., Art. XIII, \xc2\xa71; Mohegan Tribe Code \xc2\xa72\xe2\x80\x9321.

\n

Of particular relevance here, Mohegan law sets out sovereign immunity and indemnification policies applicable to disputes arising from gaming activities. The Gaming Authority has waived its sovereign immunity and consented to be sued in the Mohegan Gaming Disputes Court. Mohegan Const., Art. XIII, \xc2\xa71; Mohegan Tribe Code \xc2\xa73\xe2\x80\x93250(b). Neither the Tribe nor the Gaming Authority has consented to suit for claims arising under Connecticut state law. See Mohegan Const., Art. IX, \xc2\xa72(t); Mohegan Tribe Code \xc2\xa73\xe2\x80\x93250(g); see also Blatchford v. Native Village of Noatak,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 775,\n782 (1991)\n (observing that Indian tribes have not surrendered their immunity against suits by States). Further, Mohegan Tribe Code \xc2\xa74\xe2\x80\x9352 provides that the Gaming Authority \xe2\x80\x9cshall save harmless and indemnify its Officer or Em-ployee from financial loss and expense arising out of any claim, demand, or suit by reason of his or her alleged negligence .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. if the Officer or Employee is found to have been acting in the discharge of his or her duties or within the scope of his or her employment.\xe2\x80\x9d The Gaming Authority does not indemnify employees who engage in \xe2\x80\x9cwanton, reckless or malicious\xe2\x80\x9d activity. Mohegan Tribe Code\xc2\xa74\xe2\x80\x9352.

\n

B

\n

Petitioners Brian and Michelle Lewis were driving down Interstate 95 in Norwalk, Connecticut, when a limousine driven by respondent William Clarke hit their vehicle from behind. Clarke, a Gaming Authority employee, was transporting patrons of the Mohegan Sun Casino to their homes. For purposes of this appeal, it is undisputed that Clarke caused the accident.

\n

The Lewises filed suit against Clarke in his individual capacity in Connecticut state court, and Clarke moved to dismiss for lack of subject-matter jurisdiction on the basis of tribal sovereign immunity. See 2014 WL 5354956, *2 (Super. Ct. Conn., Sept. 10, 2014) (Cole-Chu, J.). Clarke argued that because the Gaming Authority, an arm of the Tribe, was entitled to sovereign immunity, he, an employee of the Gaming Authority acting within the scope of his employment at the time of the accident, was similarly entitled to sovereign immunity against suit. According to Clarke, denying the motion would abrogate the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign immunity.

\n

The trial court denied Clarke\xe2\x80\x99s motion to dismiss. Id., at *8. The court agreed with the Lewises that the sovereign immunity analysis should focus on the remedy sought in their complaint. To that end, the court identified Clarke, not the Gaming Authority or the Tribe, as the real party in interest because the damages remedy sought was solely against Clarke and would in no way affect the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s ability to govern itself independently. The court therefore concluded that tribal sovereign immunity was not implicated. Id., at *2\xe2\x80\x93*8. It also rejected Clarke\xe2\x80\x99s alternative argument that because the Gaming Authority was obligated to indemnify him pursuant to Mohegan Tribe Code \xc2\xa74\xe2\x80\x9352 and would end up paying the damages, he should prevail under the remedy analysis. Id., at *7. The trial court reasoned that a \xe2\x80\x9cvoluntary undertaking cannot be used to extend sovereign immunity where it did not otherwise exist.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

The Supreme Court of Connecticut reversed, holding that tribal sovereign immunity did bar the suit. 320 Conn. 706, 135 A.\xc2\xa03d 677 (2016). The court agreed with Clarke that \xe2\x80\x9cbecause he was acting within the scope of his employment for the Mohegan Tribal Gaming Authority and the Mohegan Tribal Gaming Authority is an arm of the Mohegan Tribe, tribal sovereign immunity bars the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 claims against him.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 709, 135 A.\xc2\xa03d, at 680. Of particular significance to the court was ensuring that \xe2\x80\x9cplaintiffs cannot circumvent tribal immunity by merely naming the defendant, an employee of the tribe, when the complaint concerns actions taken within the scope of his duties and the complaint does not allege, nor have the plaintiffs offered any other evidence, that he acted outside the scope of his authority.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 720, 135 A.\xc2\xa03d, at 685. To do otherwise, the court reasoned, would \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98eviscerate\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the protections of tribal immunity. Id., at 717, 135 A.\xc2\xa03d, at 684 (alterations and internal quotation marks omitted). Because the court determined that Clarke was entitled to sovereign immunity on the sole basis that he was acting within the scope of his employment when the accident occurred, id., at 720, 135 A.\xc2\xa03d, at 685\xe2\x80\x93686, it did not consider whether Clarke should be entitled to sovereign immunity on the basis of the indemnification statute.

\n

We granted certiorari to consider whether tribal sovereign immunity bars the Lewises\xe2\x80\x99 suit against Clarke, 579 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2016), and we now reverse the judgment of the Supreme Court of Connecticut.

\n

II

\n

Two issues require our resolution: (1) whether the sovereign immunity of an Indian tribe bars individual-capacity damages against tribal employees for torts committed within the scope of their employment; and (2) what role, if any, a tribe\xe2\x80\x99s decision to indemnify its employees plays in this analysis. We decide this case under the framework of our precedents regarding tribal immunity.

\n

A

\n

Our cases establish that, in the context of lawsuits against state and federal employees or entities, courts should look to whether the sovereign is the real party in interest to determine whether sovereign immunity bars the suit. See Hafer v. Melo,\n\n502 U.\xc2\xa0S. 21,\n25 (1991)\n. In making this assessment, courts may not simply rely on the characterization of the parties in the complaint, but rather must determine in the first instance whether the remedy sought is truly against the sovereign. See, e.g., Ex parte New York,\n\n256 U.\xc2\xa0S. 490\n\xe2\x80\x93502 (1921). If, for example, an action is in essence against a State even if the State is not a named party, then the State is the real party in interest and is entitled to invoke the\n \nEleventh Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s protection. For this reason, an arm or instrumentality of the State generally enjoys the same immunity as the sovereign itself. E.g., Regents of Univ. of Cal. v. Doe,\n\n519 U.\xc2\xa0S. 425\n\xe2\x80\x93430 (1997). Similarly, lawsuits brought against employees in their official capacity \xe2\x80\x9crepresent only another way of pleading an action against an entity of which an officer is an agent,\xe2\x80\x9d and they may also be barred by sovereign immunity. Kentucky v. Graham,\n\n473 U.\xc2\xa0S. 159\n\xe2\x80\x93166 (1985) (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

The distinction between individual- and official-capacity suits is paramount here. In an official-capacity claim, the relief sought is only nominally against the official and in fact is against the official\xe2\x80\x99s office and thus the sovereign itself. Will v. Michigan Dept. of State Police,\n\n491 U.\xc2\xa0S. 58,\n71 (1989)\n; Dugan v. Rank,\n\n372 U.\xc2\xa0S. 609\n\xe2\x80\x93622 (1963). This is why, when officials sued in their official capacities leave office, their successors automatically assume their role in the litigation. Hafer, 502 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 25. The real party in interest is the government entity, not the named official. See Edelman v. Jordan,\n\n415 U.\xc2\xa0S. 651\n\xe2\x80\x93665 (1974). \xe2\x80\x9cPersonal-capacity suits, on the other hand, seek to impose individual liability upon a government officer for actions taken under color of state law.\xe2\x80\x9d Hafer, 502 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 25 (emphasis added); see also id., at 27\xe2\x80\x9331 (discharged employees entitled to bring personal damages action against state auditor general); cf. Bivens v. Six Unknown Fed. Narcotics Agents,\n\n403 U.\xc2\xa0S. 388 (1971)\n. \xe2\x80\x9c[O]fficers sued in their personal capacity come to court as individuals,\xe2\x80\x9d Hafer, 502 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 27, and the real party in interest is the individual, not the sovereign.

\n

The identity of the real party in interest dictates what immunities may be available. Defendants in an official-capacity action may assert sovereign immunity. Graham, 473 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 167. An officer in an individual-capacity action, on the other hand, may be able to assert personal immunity defenses, such as, for example, absolute prosecutorial immunity in certain circumstances. Van de Kamp v. Goldstein,\n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 335\n\xe2\x80\x93344 (2009). But sovereign immunity \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not erect a barrier against suits to impose individual and personal liability.\xe2\x80\x9d Hafer, 502 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 30\xe2\x80\x9331 (internal quotation marks omitted); see Alden v. Maine,\n\n527 U.\xc2\xa0S. 706,\n757 (1996)\n.

\n

B

\n

There is no reason to depart from these general rules in the context of tribal sovereign immunity. It is apparent that these general principles foreclose Clarke\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign immunity defense in this case. This is a negligence action arising from a tort committed by Clarke on an interstate highway within the State of Connecticut. The suit is brought against a tribal employee operating a vehicle within the scope of his employment but on state lands, and the judgment will not operate against the Tribe. This is not a suit against Clarke in his official capacity. It is simply a suit against Clarke to recover for his personal actions, which \xe2\x80\x9cwill not require action by the sovereign or disturb the sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s property.\xe2\x80\x9d Larson v. Domestic and Foreign Commerce Corp.,\n\n337 U.\xc2\xa0S. 682,\n687 (1949)\n. We are cognizant of the Supreme Court of Connecticut\xe2\x80\x99s concern that plaintiffs not circumvent tribal sovereign immunity. But here, that immunity is simply not in play. Clarke, not the Gaming Authority, is the real party in interest.

\n

In ruling that Clarke was immune from this suit solely because he was acting within the scope of his employment, the court extended sovereign immunity for tribal employees beyond what common-law sovereign immunity principles would recognize for either state or federal employees. See, e.g., Graham, 473 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 167\xe2\x80\x93168. The protection offered by tribal sovereign immunity here is no broader than the protection offered by state or federal sovereign immunity.

\n

Accordingly, under established sovereign immunity principles, the Gaming Authority\xe2\x80\x99s immunity does not, in these circumstances, bar suit against Clarke.[2]

\n

III

\n

The conclusion above notwithstanding, Clarke argues that the Gaming Authority is the real party in interest here because it is required by Mohegan Tribe Code \xc2\xa74\xe2\x80\x9352 to indemnify Clarke for any adverse judgment.[3]

\n

A

\n

We have never before had occasion to decide whether an indemnification clause is sufficient to extend a sovereign immunity defense to a suit against an employee in his individual capacity. We hold that an indemnification provision cannot, as a matter of law, extend sovereign immunity to individual employees who would otherwise not fall under its protective cloak.

\n

Our holding follows naturally from the principles discussed above. Indeed, we have applied these same principles to a different question before\xe2\x80\x94whether a state instrumentality may invoke the State\xe2\x80\x99s immunity from suit even when the Federal Government has agreed to indemnify that instrumentality against adverse judgments. In Regents of Univ. of Cal., an individual brought suit against the University of California, a public university of the State of California, for breach of contract related to his employment at a laboratory operated by the university pursuant to a contract with the Federal Government. We held that the indemnification provision did not divest the state instrumentality of\n \nEleventh Amendment immunity. 519 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 426. Our analysis turned on where the potential legal liability lay, not from whence the money to pay the damages award ultimately came. Because the lawsuit bound the university, we held, the\n \nEleventh Amendment applied to the litigation even though the damages award would ultimately be paid by the federal Department of Energy. Id., at 429\xe2\x80\x93431. Our reasoning remains the same. The critical inquiry is who may be legally bound by the court\xe2\x80\x99s adverse judgment, not who will ultimately pick up the tab.[4]

\n

Here, the Connecticut courts exercise no jurisdiction over the Tribe or the Gaming Authority, and their judgments will not bind the Tribe or its instrumentalities in any way. The Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s indemnification provision does not somehow convert the suit against Clarke into a suit against the sovereign; when Clarke is sued in his individual capacity, he is held responsible only for his individual wrongdoing. Moreover, indemnification is not a certainty here. Clarke will not be indemnified by the Gaming Authority should it determine that he engaged in \xe2\x80\x9cwanton, reckless, or malicious\xe2\x80\x9d activity. Mohegan Tribe Code \xc2\xa74\xe2\x80\x9352. That determination is not necessary to the disposition of the Lewises\xe2\x80\x99 suit against Clarke in the Connecticut state courts, which is a separate legal matter.

\n

B

\n

Clarke notes that courts have extended sovereign immunity to private healthcare insurance companies under certain circumstances. See, e.g., Pani v. Empire Blue Cross Blue Shield, 152 F.\xc2\xa03d 67, 71\xe2\x80\x9372 (CA2 1998); Pine View Gardens, Inc. v. Mutual of Omaha Ins. Co., 485 F.\xc2\xa02d 1073, 1074\xe2\x80\x931075 (CADC 1973); Brief for Respondent 19, n.\xc2\xa04. But, these cases rest on the proposition that the fiscal intermediaries are essentially state instrumentalities, as the governing regulations make clear. See 42 CFR \xc2\xa7421.5(b) (2016) (providing that the Medicare Administrator \xe2\x80\x9cis the real party of interest in any litigation involving the administration of the program\xe2\x80\x9d). It is well established in our precedent that a suit against an arm or instrumentality of the State is treated as one against the State itself. See Regents of Univ. of Cal., 519 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 429. We have not before treated a lawsuit against an individual em-ployee as one against a state instrumentality, and Clarke offers no persuasive reason to do so now.

\n

Nor have we ever held that a civil rights suit under\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983 against a state officer in his individual capacity implicates the\n \nEleventh Amendment and a State\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign immunity from suit.[5] Federal appellate courts that have considered the indemnity question have rejected the argument that an indemnity statute brings the\n \nEleventh Amendment into play in \xc2\xa71983 actions. See, e.g., Stoner v. Wisconsin Dept. of Agriculture, Trade and Consumer Protection, 50 F.\xc2\xa03d 481, 482\xe2\x80\x93483 (CA7 1995); Blalock v. Schwinden, 862 F.\xc2\xa02d 1352, 1354 (CA9 1988); Duckworth v. Franzen, 780 F.\xc2\xa02d 645, 650 (CA7 1985). These cases rely on the concern that originally drove the adoption of the\n \nEleventh Amendment\xe2\x80\x94the protection of the States against involuntary liability. See Hess v. Port Authority Trans-Hudson Corporation,\n\n513 U.\xc2\xa0S. 30,\n39,\n48 (1994)\n. But States institute indemnification policies voluntarily. And so, indemnification provisions do not implicate one of the underlying rationales for state sovereign immunity\xe2\x80\x94a government\xe2\x80\x99s ability to make its own decisions about \xe2\x80\x9cthe allocation of scarce resources.\xe2\x80\x9d Alden, 527 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 751.

\n

Finally, our conclusion that indemnification provisions do not alter the real-party-in-interest analysis for purposes of sovereign immunity is consistent with the practicethat applies in the contexts of diversity of citizenship and joinder. In assessing diversity jurisdiction, courts look to the real parties to the controversy. Navarro Savings Assn. v. Lee,\n\n446 U.\xc2\xa0S. 458,\n460 (1980)\n. Applying this principle, courts below have agreed that the fact that a third party indemnifies one of the named parties to the case does not, as a general rule, influence the diversity analysis. See, e.g., Corfield v. Dallas Glen Hills LP, 355 F.\xc2\xa03d 853, 865 (CA5 2003); E.\xc2\xa0R. Squibb & Sons, Inc. v. Accident & Cas. Ins. Co., 160 F.\xc2\xa03d 925, 936\xe2\x80\x93937 (CA2 1998). They have similarly held that a party does not become a required party for joinder purposes under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 19 simply by virtue of indemnifying one of the named parties. See, e.g., Gardiner v. Virgin Islands Water & Power Auth., 145 F.\xc2\xa03d 635, 641 (CA3 1998); Rochester Methodist Hospital v. Travelers Ins. Co., 728 F.\xc2\xa02d 1006, 1016\xe2\x80\x931017 (CA8 1984).

\n

In sum, although tribal sovereign immunity is implicated when the suit is brought against individual officers in their official capacities, it is simply not present when the claim is made against those employees in their individual capacities. An indemnification statute such as the one at issue here does not alter the analysis. Clarke may not avail himself of a sovereign immunity defense.

\n

IV

\n

The judgment of the Supreme Court of Connecticut is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

We nevertheless consider the issue fairly included within the question presented, as it is a purely legal question that is an integral part of Clarke\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign immunity argument and that was both raised to and passed on by the trial court. See Mitchell v. Forsyth,\n\n472 U.\xc2\xa0S. 511,\n530 (1985)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he purely legal question on which [petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s] claim of immunity turns is appropriate for our immediate resolution notwithstanding that it was not addressed by the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).\n\t\n
\n\n4\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0Our holding in Hess v. Port Authority Trans-Hudson Corporation,\n\n513 U.\xc2\xa0S. 30 (1994)\n, is not to the contrary. There the immunity question turned on whether the Port Authority Trans-Hudson Corporation was a state agency cloaked with\n \nEleventh Amendment immunity such that any judgment \xe2\x80\x9cmust be paid out of a State\xe2\x80\x99s treasury.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 48, 51\xe2\x80\x9352 (emphasis added). Here, unlike in Hess, the damages judgment would not come from the sovereign.\n\t\n
\n\n5\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0A suit against a state officer in his official, rather than individual, capacity might implicate the\n \nEleventh Amendment. See Kentucky v. Graham,\n\n473 U.\xc2\xa0S. 159\n\xe2\x80\x93166 (1985).\n\t\n
