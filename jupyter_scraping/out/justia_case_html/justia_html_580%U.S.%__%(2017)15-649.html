SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

Syllabus

\n

CZYZEWSKI et\xc2\xa0al. v. JEVIC HOLDING CORP. et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the third circuit

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x93649.\xe2\x80\x83Argued December 7, 2016\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 22, 2017

\n

There are three possible conclusions to a Chapter 11 bankruptcy. First, debtor and creditors may negotiate a plan to govern the distribution of the estate\xe2\x80\x99s value. See, e.g., 11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71121, 1123, 1129, 1141. Second, the bankruptcy court may convert the case to Chapter 7 for liquidation of the business and distribution of its assets to creditors. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71112(a), (b), 726. Finally, the bankruptcy court may dismiss the case. \xc2\xa71112(b). A court ordering a dismissal ordinarily attempts to restore the prepetition financial status quo. \xc2\xa7349(b)(3). Yet if perfect restoration proves difficult or impossible, the court may, \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause,\xe2\x80\x9d alter the dismissal\xe2\x80\x99s normal restorative consequences, \xc2\xa7349(b)\xe2\x80\x94i.e., it may order a \xe2\x80\x9cstructured dismissal.\xe2\x80\x9d The Bankruptcy Code also establishes basic priority rules for determining the order in which the court will distribute an estate\xe2\x80\x99s assets. The Code makes clear that distributions in a Chapter 7 liquidation must follow this prescribed order. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7725, 726. Chapter 11 permits some flexibility, but a court still cannot confirm a plan that contains priority-violating distributions over the objection of an impaired creditor class. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71129(a)(7), (b)(2). The Code does not explicitly state what priority rules\xe2\x80\x94if any\xe2\x80\x94apply to the distribution of assets in a structured dismissal.

\n

Respondent Jevic Transportation filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy after being purchased in a leveraged buyout. The bankruptcy prompted two lawsuits. In the first, a group of former Jevic truckdrivers, petitioners here, was awarded a judgment against Jevic for Jevic\xe2\x80\x99s failure to provide proper notice of termination in violation of state and federal Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification (WARN) Acts. Part of that judgment counted as a priority wage claim under \xc2\xa7507(a)(4), entitling the workers to payment ahead of general unsecured claims against the Jevic estate. In the second suit, a court-authorized committee representing Jevic\xe2\x80\x99s unsecured creditors sued Sun Capital and CIT Group, respondents here, for fraudulent conveyance in connection with the leveraged buyout of Jevic. These parties negotiated a settlement agreement that called for a structured dismissal of Jevic\xe2\x80\x99s Chapter 11 bankruptcy. Under the proposed structured dismissal, petitioners would receive nothing on their WARN claims, but lower-priority general unsecured creditors would be paid. Petitioners argued that the distribution scheme accordingly violated the Code\xe2\x80\x99s priority rules by paying general unsecured claims ahead of their own. The Bankruptcy Court nevertheless approved the settlement agreement and dismissed the case, reasoning that because the proposed payouts would occur pursuant to a structured dismissal rather than an approved plan, the failure to follow ordinary priority rules did not bar approval. The District Court and the Third Circuit affirmed.

\n

Held:

\n

1.\xc2\xa0Petitioners have Article III standing. Respondents argue that petitioners have not \xe2\x80\x9csuffered an injury in fact,\xe2\x80\x9d or at least one \xe2\x80\x9clikely to be redressed by a favorable judicial decision,\xe2\x80\x9d Spokeo, Inc. v. Robins, 578 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, because petitioners would have gotten nothing even if the Bankruptcy Court had never approved the structured dismissal and will still get nothing if the structured dismissal is undone now. That argument rests upon the assumptions that (1) without a violation of ordinary priority rules, there will be no settlement, and (2) without a settlement, the fraudulent-conveyance lawsuit has no value. The record, however, indicates both that a settlement that respects ordinary priorities remains a reasonable possibility and that the fraudulent-conveyance claim could have litigation value. Therefore, as a consequence of the Bankruptcy Court\xe2\x80\x99s approval of the structured dismissal, petitioners lost a chance to obtain a settlement that respected their priorities or, if not that, the power to assert the fraudulent-conveyance claim themselves. A decision in their favor is likely to redress that loss. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

2. Bankruptcy courts may not approve structured dismissals that provide for distributions that do not follow ordinary priority rules without the consent of affected creditors. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0Given the importance of the priority system, this Court looks for an affirmative indication of intent before concluding that Congress means to make a major departure. See Whitman v. American Trucking Assns., Inc.,\n\n531 U.\xc2\xa0S. 457\n. Nothing in the statute evinces such intent. Insofar as the dismissal sections of Chapter 11 foresee any transfer of assets, they seek a restoration of the prepetition financial status quo. Read in context, \xc2\xa7349(b), which permits a bankruptcy judge, \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause, [to] orde[r] otherwise,\xe2\x80\x9d seems designed to give courts the flexibility to protect reliance interests acquired in the bankruptcy, not to make general end-of-case distributions that would be flatly impermissible in a Chapter 11 plan or Chapter 7 liquidation. Precedent does not support a contrary position. E.g., In\xc2\xa0re Iridium Operating LLC, 478 F.\xc2\xa03d 452 (CA2), distinguished. Cases in which courts have approved deviations from ordinary priority rules generally involve interim distributions serving significant Code-related objectives. That is not the case here, where, e.g., the priority-violating distribution is attached to a final disposition, does not preserve the debtor as a going concern, does not make the disfavored creditors better off, does not promote the possibility of a confirmable plan, does not help to restore the status quo ante, and does not protect reliance interests. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

(b) Congress did not authorize a \xe2\x80\x9crare case\xe2\x80\x9d exception that permits courts to disregard priority in structured dismissals for \xe2\x80\x9csufficient reasons.\xe2\x80\x9d The fact that it is difficult to give precise content to the concept of \xe2\x80\x9csufficient reasons\xe2\x80\x9d threatens to turn the court below\xe2\x80\x99s exception into a more general rule, resulting in uncertainty that has potentially serious consequences\xe2\x80\x94e.g., departure from the protections granted particular classes of creditors, changes in the bargaining power of different classes of creditors even in bankruptcies that do not end in structured dismissals, risks of collusion, and increased difficulty in achieving settlements. Courts cannot deviate from the strictures of the Code, even in \xe2\x80\x9crare cases.\xe2\x80\x9d Pp.\xc2\xa016\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

787 F.\xc2\xa03d 173, reversed and remanded.

CASIMIR CZYZEWSKI, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. JEVIC HOLDING CORP., et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the third circuit

\n

[March 22, 2017]

\n

\n

Justice Breyer delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

Bankruptcy Code Chapter 11 allows debtors and their creditors to negotiate a plan for dividing an estate\xe2\x80\x99s value. See 11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71123, 1129, 1141. But sometimes the parties cannot agree on a plan. If so, the bankruptcy court may decide to dismiss the case. \xc2\xa71112(b). The Code then ordinarily provides for what is, in effect, a restoration of the prepetition financial status quo. \xc2\xa7349(b).

\n

In the case before us, a Bankruptcy Court dismissed a Chapter 11 bankruptcy. But the court did not simply restore the prepetition status quo. Instead, the court ordered a distribution of estate assets that gave money to high-priority secured creditors and to low-priority general unsecured creditors but which skipped certain dissenting mid-priority creditors. The skipped creditors would have been entitled to payment ahead of the general unsecured creditors in a Chapter 11 plan (or in a Chapter 7 liquidation). See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7507, 725, 726, 1129. The question before us is whether a bankruptcy court has the legal power to order this priority-skipping kind of distribution scheme in connection with a Chapter 11 dismissal.

\n

In our view, a bankruptcy court does not have such a power. A distribution scheme ordered in connection with the dismissal of a Chapter 11 case cannot, without the consent of the affected parties, deviate from the basic priority rules that apply under the primary mechanisms the Code establishes for final distributions of estate value in business bankruptcies.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

1

\n

We begin with a few fundamentals: A business may file for bankruptcy under either Chapter 7 or Chapter 11. In Chapter 7, a trustee liquidates the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s assets and distributes them to creditors. See \xc2\xa7701 et\xc2\xa0seq. In Chapter 11, debtor and creditors try to negotiate a plan that will govern the distribution of valuable assets from the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s estate and often keep the business operating as a going concern. See, e.g., \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71121, 1123, 1129, 1141 (setting out the framework in which the parties negotiate).

\n

Filing for Chapter 11 bankruptcy has several relevant legal consequences. First, an estate is created comprising all property of the debtor. \xc2\xa7541(a)(1). Second, a fiduciary is installed to manage the estate in the interest of the creditors. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71106, 1107(a). This fiduciary, often the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s existing management team, acts as \xe2\x80\x9cdebtor in possession.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71101(1), 1104. It may operate the business, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7363(c)(1), 1108, and perform certain bankruptcy-related functions, such as seeking to recover for the estate preferential or fraudulent transfers made to other persons, \xc2\xa7547 (transfers made before bankruptcy that unfairly preferred particular creditors); \xc2\xa7548 (fraudulent transfers, including transfers made before bankruptcy for which the debtor did not receive fair value). Third, an \xe2\x80\x9cautomatic stay\xe2\x80\x9d of all collection proceedings against the debtor takes effect. \xc2\xa7362(a).

\n

It is important to keep in mind that Chapter 11 foresees three possible outcomes. The first is a bankruptcy-court-confirmed plan. Such a plan may keep the business operating but, at the same time, help creditors by providing for payments, perhaps over time. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71123, 1129, 1141. The second possible outcome is conversion of the case to a Chapter 7 proceeding for liquidation of the business and a distribution of its remaining assets. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71112(a), (b), 726. That conversion in effect confesses an inability to find a plan. The third possible outcome is dismissal of the Chapter 11 case. \xc2\xa71112(b). A dismissal typically \xe2\x80\x9crevests the property of the estate in the entity in which such property was vested immediately before the commencement of the case\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94in other words, it aims to return to the prepetition financial status quo. \xc2\xa7349(b)(3).

\n

Nonetheless, recognizing that conditions may have changed in ways that make a perfect restoration of the status quo difficult or impossible, the Code permits the bankruptcy court, \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause,\xe2\x80\x9d to alter a Chapter 11 dismissal\xe2\x80\x99s ordinary restorative consequences. \xc2\xa7349(b). A dismissal that does so (or which has other special conditions attached) is often referred to as a \xe2\x80\x9cstructured dismissal,\xe2\x80\x9d defined by the American Bankruptcy Institute as a

\n

\xe2\x80\x9chybrid dismissal and confirmation order .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. typically dismisses the case while, among other things, approving certain distributions to creditors, granting certain third-party releases, enjoining certain conduct by creditors, and not necessarily vacating orders or unwinding transactions undertaken during the case.\xe2\x80\x9d American Bankruptcy Institute Commission To Study the Reform of Chapter 11, 2012\xe2\x80\x932014 Final Report and Recommendations 270 (2014).

\n

Although the Code does not expressly mention structured dismissals, they \xe2\x80\x9cappear to be increasingly common.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid., n.\xc2\xa0973.

\n

2

\n

The Code also sets forth a basic system of priority, which ordinarily determines the order in which the bankruptcy court will distribute assets of the estate. Secured creditors are highest on the priority list, for they must receive the proceeds of the collateral that secures their debts.\n\n11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7725. Special classes of creditors, such as those who hold certain claims for taxes or wages, come next in a listed order. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7507, 726(a)(1). Then come low-priority creditors, including general unsecured creditors. \xc2\xa7726(a)(2). The Code places equity holders at the bottom of the priority list. They receive nothing until all previ-ously listed creditors have been paid in full. \xc2\xa7726(a)(6).

\n

The Code makes clear that distributions of assets in a Chapter 7 liquidation must follow this prescribed order. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7725, 726. It provides somewhat more flexibility for distributions pursuant to Chapter 11 plans, which may impose a different ordering with the consent of the af-fected parties. But a bankruptcy court cannot confirm a plan that contains priority-violating distributions over the objection of an impaired creditor class. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71129(a)(7), 1129(b)(2).

\n

The question here concerns the interplay between the Code\xe2\x80\x99s priority rules and a Chapter 11 dismissal. Here, the Bankruptcy Court neither liquidated the debtor under Chapter 7 nor confirmed a Chapter 11 plan. But the court, instead of reverting to the prebankruptcy status quo, ordered a distribution of the estate assets to creditors by attaching conditions to the dismissal (i.e., it ordered a structured dismissal). The Code does not explicitly state what priority rules\xe2\x80\x94if any\xe2\x80\x94apply to a distribution in these circumstances. May a court consequently provide for distributions that deviate from the ordinary priority rules that would apply to a Chapter 7 liquidation or a Chapter 11 plan? Can it approve conditions that give estate assets to members of a lower priority class while skipping objecting members of a higher priority class?

\n

B

\n

In 2006, Sun Capital Partners, a private equity firm, acquired Jevic Transportation Corporation with money borrowed from CIT Group in a \xe2\x80\x9cleveraged buyout.\xe2\x80\x9d In a leveraged buyout, the buyer (B) typically borrows from a third party (T) a large share of the funds needed to purchase a company (C). B then pays the money to C\xe2\x80\x99s shareholders. Having bought the stock, B owns C. B then pledges C\xe2\x80\x99s assets to T so that T will have security for its loan. Thus, if the selling price for C is $50 million, B might use $10 million of its own money, borrow $40 million from T, pay $50 million to C\xe2\x80\x99s shareholders, and then pledge C assets worth $40 million (or more) to T as secu-rity for T\xe2\x80\x99s $40 million loan. If B manages C well, it might make enough money to pay T back the $40 million and earn a handsome profit on its own $10 million investment. But, if the deal sours and C descends into bankruptcy, beware of what might happen: Instead of C\xe2\x80\x99s $40 million in assets being distributed to its existing creditors, the money will go to T to pay back T\xe2\x80\x99s loan\xe2\x80\x94the loan that allowed B to buy C. (T will receive what remains of C\xe2\x80\x99s assets because T is now a secured creditor, putting it at the top of the priority list). Since C\xe2\x80\x99s shareholders receive money while C\xe2\x80\x99s creditors lose their claim to C\xe2\x80\x99s remaining assets, unsuccessful leveraged buyouts often lead to fraudulent conveyance suits alleging that the purchaser (B) transferred the company\xe2\x80\x99s assets without receiving fair value in return. See Lipson & Vandermeuse, Stern, Seriously: The Article I Judicial Power, Fraudulent Transfers, and Leveraged Buyouts, 2013 Wis. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1161, 1220\xe2\x80\x931221.

\n

This is precisely what happened here. Just two years after Sun\xe2\x80\x99s buyout, Jevic (C in our leveraged buyout example) filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy. At the time of filing, it owed $53 million to senior secured creditors Sun and CIT (B and T in our example), and over $20 million to tax and general unsecured creditors.

\n

The circumstances surrounding Jevic\xe2\x80\x99s bankruptcy led to two lawsuits. First, petitioners, a group of former Jevic truckdrivers, filed suit in bankruptcy court against Jevic and Sun. Petitioners pointed out that, just before entering bankruptcy, Jevic had halted almost all its operations and had told petitioners that they would be fired. Petitioners claimed that Jevic and Sun had thereby violated state and federal Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification (WARN) Acts\xe2\x80\x94laws that require a company to give workers at least 60 days\xe2\x80\x99 notice before their termination. See\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72102; N. J. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa734:21\xe2\x80\x932 (West 2011). The Bankruptcy Court granted summary judgment for petitioners against Jevic, leaving them (and this is the point to remember) with a judgment that petitioners say is worth $12.4 million. See In\xc2\xa0re Jevic Holding Corp., 496 B.R. 151 (Bkrtcy. Ct. Del. 2013). Some $8.3 million of that judgment counts as a priority wage claim under\n\n11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7507(a)(4), and is therefore entitled to payment ahead of general unsecured claims against the Jevicestate.

\n

Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 WARN suit against Sun continued throughout most of the litigation now before us. But eventually Sun prevailed on the ground that Sun was not the workers\xe2\x80\x99 employer at the relevant times. See In\xc2\xa0re Jevic Holding Corp., 656 Fed. Appx. 617 (CA3 2016).

\n

Second, the Bankruptcy Court authorized a committee representing Jevic\xe2\x80\x99s unsecured creditors to sue Sun and CIT. The Bankruptcy Court and the parties were aware that any proceeds from such a suit would belong not to the unsecured creditors, but to the bankruptcy estate. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7541(a)(1), (6); Official Comm. of Unsecured Creditors of Cybergenics Corp. v. Chinery, 330 F.3d 548, 552\xe2\x80\x93553 (CA3 2003) (en banc) (holding that a creditor\xe2\x80\x99s committee can bring a derivative action on behalf of the estate). The committee alleged that Sun and CIT, in the course of their leveraged buyout, had \xe2\x80\x9chastened Jevic\xe2\x80\x99s bankruptcy by saddling it with debts that it couldn\xe2\x80\x99t service.\xe2\x80\x9d In\xc2\xa0re Jevic Holding Corp., 787 F.3d 173, 176 (CA3 2015). In 2011, the Bankruptcy Court held that the committee had adequately pleaded claims of preferential transfer under \xc2\xa7547 and of fraudulent transfer under \xc2\xa7548. In\xc2\xa0re Jevic Holding Corp., 2011 WL 4345204 (Bkrtcy. Ct. Del., Sept. 15, 2011).

\n

Sun, CIT, Jevic, and the committee then tried to negotiate a settlement of this \xe2\x80\x9cfraudulent-conveyance\xe2\x80\x9d lawsuit. By that point, the depleted Jevic estate\xe2\x80\x99s only remaining assets were the fraudulent-conveyance claim itself and $1.7 million in cash, which was subject to a lien held by Sun.

\n

The parties reached a settlement agreement. It pro-vided (1) that the Bankruptcy Court would dismiss the fraudulent-conveyance action with prejudice; (2) that CIT would deposit $2 million into an account earmarked to pay the committee\xe2\x80\x99s legal fees and administrative expenses; (3) that Sun would assign its lien on Jevic\xe2\x80\x99s remaining $1.7 million to a trust, which would pay taxes and administrative expenses and distribute the remainder on a pro\xc2\xa0rata basis to the low-priority general unsecured creditors, but which would not distribute anything to petitioners (who, by virtue of their WARN judgment, held an $8.3 million mid-level-priority wage claim against the estate); and (4) that Jevic\xe2\x80\x99s Chapter 11 bankruptcy would be dismissed.

\n

Apparently Sun insisted on a distribution that would skip petitioners because petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 WARN suit against Sun was still pending and Sun did not want to help finance that litigation. See 787 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 177\xe2\x80\x93178, n.\xc2\xa04 (Sun\xe2\x80\x99s counsel acknowledging before the Bankruptcy Court that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Sun probably does care where the money goes because you can take judicial notice that there\xe2\x80\x99s a pending WARN action against Sun by the WARN plaintiffs. And if the money goes to the WARN plaintiffs, then you\xe2\x80\x99re funding someone who is suing you who otherwise doesn\xe2\x80\x99t have funds and is doing it on a contingent fee basis\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). The essential point is that, regardless of the reason, the proposed settlement called for a structured dismissal that provided for distributions that did not follow ordinary priority rules.

\n

Sun, CIT, Jevic, and the committee asked the Bankruptcy Court to approve the settlement and dismiss the case. Petitioners and the U.\xc2\xa0S. Trustee objected, arguing that the settlement\xe2\x80\x99s distribution plan violated the Code\xe2\x80\x99s priority scheme because it skipped petitioners\xe2\x80\x94who, by virtue of their WARN judgment, had mid-level priority claims against estate assets\xe2\x80\x94and distributed estate money to low-priority general unsecured creditors.

\n

The Bankruptcy Court agreed with petitioners that the settlement\xe2\x80\x99s distribution scheme failed to follow ordinary priority rules. App. to Pet. for Cert. 58a. But it held that this did not bar approval. Ibid. That, in the Bankruptcy Court\xe2\x80\x99s view, was because the proposed payouts would occur pursuant to a structured dismissal of a Chapter 11 petition rather than an approval of a Chapter 11 plan. Ibid. The court accordingly decided to grant the motion in light of the \xe2\x80\x9cdire circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d facing the estate and its creditors. Id., at 57a. Specifically, the court predicted that without the settlement and dismissal, there was \xe2\x80\x9cno realistic prospect\xe2\x80\x9d of a meaningful distribution for anyone other than the secured creditors. Id., at 58a. A confirm-able Chapter 11 plan was unattainable. And there would be no funds to operate, investigate, or litigate were the case converted to a proceeding in Chapter 7. Ibid.

\n

The District Court affirmed the Bankruptcy Court. It recognized that the settlement distribution violated ordinary priority rules. But those rules, it wrote, were \xe2\x80\x9cnot a bar to the approval of the settlement as [the settlement] is not a reorganization plan.\xe2\x80\x9d In\xc2\xa0re Jevic Holding Corp., 2014 WL 268613, *3 (D Del., Jan. 24, 2014).

\n

The Third Circuit affirmed the District Court by a vote of 2 to 1. 787 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 175; id., at 186 (Scirica, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part). The majority held that structured dismissals need not always respect prior-ity. Congress, the court explained, had only \xe2\x80\x9ccodified the absolute priority rule .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in the specific context of plan confirmation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 183. As a result, courts could, \xe2\x80\x9cin rare instances like this one, approve structured dismissals that do not strictly adhere to the Bankruptcy Code\xe2\x80\x99s priority scheme.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 180.

\n

Petitioners (the workers with the WARN judgment) sought certiorari. We granted their petition.

\n

II

\n

Respondents initially argue that petitioners lack standing because they have suffered no injury, or at least no injury that will be remedied by a decision in their favor. See Spokeo, Inc. v. Robins, 578 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2016) (slip op., at 6) (explaining that, for Article III standing, a plaintiff must have \xe2\x80\x9c(1) suffered an injury in fact, (2) that is fairly traceable to the challenged conduct of the defendant, and (3) that is likely to be redressed by a favorable judicial decision\xe2\x80\x9d). Respondents concede that the structured dismissal approved by the Bankruptcy Court contained distribution conditions that skipped over petitioners, ensur-ing that petitioners received nothing on their multimillion-dollar WARN claim against the Jevic estate. But respondents still assert that petitioners suffered no loss.

\n

The reason, respondents say, is that petitioners would have gotten nothing even if the Bankruptcy Court had never approved the structured dismissal in the first place, and will still get nothing if the structured dismissal is undone now. Reversal will eliminate the settlement of the committee\xe2\x80\x99s fraudulent-conveyance lawsuit, which was conditioned on the Bankruptcy Court\xe2\x80\x99s approval of the priority-violating structured dismissal. If the Bankruptcy Court cannot approve that dismissal, respondents contend, Sun and CIT will no longer agree to settle. Nor will petitioners ever be able to obtain a litigation recovery. Hence there will be no lawsuit money to distribute. And in the absence of lawsuit money, Jevic\xe2\x80\x99s assets amount to about $1.7 million, all pledged to Sun, leaving nothing for anyone else, let alone petitioners. Thus, even if petitioners are right that the structured dismissal was impermissible, it cost them nothing. And a judicial decision in their favor will gain them nothing. No loss. No redress.

\n

This argument, however, rests upon respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims (1) that, without a violation of ordinary priority rules, there will be no settlement, and (2) that, without a settlement, the fraudulent-conveyance lawsuit has no value. In our view, the record does not support either of these propositions.

\n

As to the first, the record indicates that a settlement that respects ordinary priorities remains a reasonable possibility. It makes clear (as counsel made clear before our Court, see Tr. of Oral Arg. 58) that Sun insisted upon a settlement that gave petitioners nothing only because it did not want to help fund petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 WARN lawsuit against it. See 787 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 177\xe2\x80\x93178, n.\xc2\xa04. But, Sun has now won that lawsuit. See 656 Fed. Appx. 617. If Sun\xe2\x80\x99s given reason for opposing distributions to petitioners has disappeared, why would Sun not settle while permitting some of the settlement money to go to petitioners?

\n

As to the second, the record indicates that the fraudulent-conveyance claim could have litigation value. CIT and Sun, after all, settled the lawsuit for $3.7 million, which would make little sense if the action truly had no chance of success. The Bankruptcy Court could convert the case to Chapter 7, allowing a Chapter 7 trustee to pursue the suit against Sun and CIT. Or the court could simply dismiss the Chapter 11 bankruptcy, thereby allowing petitioners to assert the fraudulent-conveyance claim themselves. Given these possibilities, there is no reason to believe that the claim could not be pursued with counsel obtained on a contingency basis. Of course, the lawsuit\xe2\x80\x94like any lawsuit\xe2\x80\x94might prove fruitless, but the mere possibility of failure does not eliminate the value of the claim or petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 injury in being unable to bring it.

\n

Consequently, the Bankruptcy Court\xe2\x80\x99s approval of the structured dismissal cost petitioners something. They lost a chance to obtain a settlement that respected their prior-ity. Or, if not that, they lost the power to bring their own lawsuit on a claim that had a settlement value of $3.7 million. For standing purposes, a loss of even a small amount of money is ordinarily an \xe2\x80\x9cinjury.\xe2\x80\x9d See, e.g., McGowan v. Maryland,\n\n366 U.S. 420\n\xe2\x80\x93431 (1961) (finding that appellants fined $5 plus costs had standing to assert an Establishment Clause challenge). And the ruling before us could well have cost petitioners considerably more. See Clinton v. City of New York,\n\n524 U.S. 417\n\xe2\x80\x93431 (1998) (imposition of a \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial contingent liability\xe2\x80\x9d qualifies as an injury). A decision in petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 favor is likely to redress that loss. We accordingly conclude that petitioners have standing.

\n

III

\n

We turn to the basic question presented: Can a bankruptcy court approve a structured dismissal that provides for distributions that do not follow ordinary priority rules without the affected creditors\xe2\x80\x99 consent? Our simple answer to this complicated question is \xe2\x80\x9cno.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

The Code\xe2\x80\x99s priority system constitutes a basic underpinning of business bankruptcy law. Distributions of estate assets at the termination of a business bankruptcy normally take place through a Chapter 7 liquidation or a Chapter 11 plan, and both are governed by priority. In Chapter 7 liquidations, priority is an absolute command\xe2\x80\x94lower priority creditors cannot receive anything until higher priority creditors have been paid in full. See 11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7725, 726. Chapter 11 plans provide somewhat more flexibility, but a priority-violating plan still cannot be confirmed over the objection of an impaired class of creditors. See \xc2\xa71129(b).

\n

The priority system applicable to those distributions has long been considered fundamental to the Bankruptcy Code\xe2\x80\x99s operation. See H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 103\xe2\x80\x93835, p. 33 (1994) (explaining that the Code is \xe2\x80\x9cdesigned to enforce a distribution of the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s assets in an orderly manner .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in accordance with established principles rather than on the basis of the inside influence or economic leverage of a particular creditor\xe2\x80\x9d); Roe & Tung, Breaking Bankruptcy Priority: How Rent-Seeking Upends The Creditors\xe2\x80\x99 Bargain, 99 Va. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1235, 1243, 1236 (2013) (arguing that the first principle of bankruptcy is that \xe2\x80\x9cdistribution conforms to predetermined statutory and contractual priorities,\xe2\x80\x9d and that priority is, \xe2\x80\x9cquite appropriately, bankruptcy\xe2\x80\x99s most important and famous rule\xe2\x80\x9d); Markell, Owners, Auctions, and Absolute Priority in Bankruptcy Reorganizations, 44 Stan. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 69, 123 (1991) (stating that a fixed priority scheme is recognized as \xe2\x80\x9cthe cornerstone of reorganization practice and theory\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

The importance of the priority system leads us to expect more than simple statutory silence if, and when, Congress were to intend a major departure. See Whitman v. American Trucking Assns., Inc.,\n\n531 U.S. 457,\n468 (2001)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. does not, one might say, hide elephants in mouseholes\xe2\x80\x9d). Put somewhat more directly, we would expect to see some affirmative indication of intent if Congress actually meant to make structured dismissals a backdoor means to achieve the exact kind of nonconsen-sual priority-violating final distributions that the Code prohibits in Chapter 7 liquidations and Chapter 11 plans.

\n

We can find nothing in the statute that evinces this intent. The Code gives a bankruptcy court the power to \xe2\x80\x9cdismiss\xe2\x80\x9d a Chapter 11 case. \xc2\xa71112(b). But the word \xe2\x80\x9cdismiss\xe2\x80\x9d itself says nothing about the power to make nonconsensual priority-violating distributions of estate value. Neither the word \xe2\x80\x9cstructured,\xe2\x80\x9d nor the word \xe2\x80\x9cconditions,\xe2\x80\x9d nor anything else about distributing estate value to creditors pursuant to a dismissal appears in any relevant part of the Code.

\n

Insofar as the dismissal sections of Chapter 11 foresee any transfer of assets, they seek a restoration of the pre-petition financial status quo. See \xc2\xa7349(b)(1) (dismissal ordinarily reinstates a variety of avoided transfers and voided liens); \xc2\xa7349(b)(2) (dismissal ordinarily vacates certain types of bankruptcy orders); \xc2\xa7349(b)(3) (dismissal ordinarily \xe2\x80\x9crevests the property of the estate in the entity in which such property was vested immediately before the commencement of the case\xe2\x80\x9d); see also H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 95\xe2\x80\x93595, p. 338 (1977) (dismissal\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cbasic purpose .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is to undo the bankruptcy case, as far as practicable, and to restore all property rights to the position in which they were found at the commencement of the case\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Section 349(b), we concede, also says that a bankruptcy judge may, \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause, orde[r] otherwise.\xe2\x80\x9d But, read in context, this provision appears designed to give courts the flexibility to \xe2\x80\x9cmake the appropriate orders to protect rights acquired in reliance on the bankruptcy case.\xe2\x80\x9d H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 95\xe2\x80\x93595, at 338; cf., e.g., Wiese v. Community Bank of Central Wis., 552 F.3d 584, 590 (CA7 2009) (upholding, under \xc2\xa7349(b), a Bankruptcy Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision not to reinstate a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s claim against a bank that gave up a lien in reliance on the claim being released in the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s reorganization plan). Nothing else in the Code authorizes a court ordering a dismissal to make general end-of-case distributions of estate assets to creditors of the kind that normally take place in a Chapter 7 liquidation or Chapter 11 plan\xe2\x80\x94let alone final distributions that do not help to restore the status quo ante or protect reliance interests acquired in the bankruptcy, and that would be flatly impermissible in a Chapter 7 liquidation or a Chapter 11 plan because they violate priority without the impaired creditors\xe2\x80\x99 consent. That being so, the word \xe2\x80\x9ccause\xe2\x80\x9d is too weak a reed upon which to rest so weighty a power. See United Sav. Assn. of Tex. v. Timbers of Inwood Forest Associates, Ltd.,\n\n484 U.S. 365,\n371 (1988)\n (noting that \xe2\x80\x9c[s]tatutory construction .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is a holistic endeavor\xe2\x80\x9d and that a court should select a \xe2\x80\x9cmeanin[g that] produces a substantive effect that is compatible with the rest of the law\xe2\x80\x9d); Kelly v. Robinson,\n\n479 U.S. 36,\n43 (1986)\n (in interpreting a statute, a court \xe2\x80\x9cmust not be guided by a single sentence or member of a sentence, but look to the provisions of the whole law, and to its object and policy\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)); cf. In\xc2\xa0re Sadler, 935 F.2d 918, 921 (CA7 1991) (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Cause\xe2\x80\x99 under \xc2\xa7349(b) means an acceptable reason. Desire to make an end run around a statute is not an adequate reason\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

We have found no contrary precedent, either from this Court, or, for that matter, from lower court decisions reflecting common bankruptcy practice. The Third Circuit referred briefly to In\xc2\xa0re Buffet Partners, L. P., 2014 WL 3735804 (Bkrtcy. Ct. ND Tex., July 28, 2014). The court in that case approved a structured dismissal. (We express no view about the legality of structured dismissals in general.) But at the same time it pointed out \xe2\x80\x9cthat not one party with an economic stake in the case has objected to the dismissal in this manner.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at *4.

\n

The Third Circuit also relied upon In\xc2\xa0re Iridium Operating LLC, 478 F.3d 452 (CA2 2007). But Iridium did not involve a structured dismissal. It addressed an interim distribution of settlement proceeds to fund a litigation trust that would press claims on the estate\xe2\x80\x99s behalf. See id., at 459\xe2\x80\x93460. The Iridium court observed that, when evaluating this type of preplan settlement, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t is difficult to employ the rule of priorities\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9cthe nature and extent of the Estate and the claims against it are not yet fully resolved.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 464 (emphasis added). The decision does not state or suggest that the Code authorizes nonconsensual departures from ordinary priority rules in the context of a dismissal\xe2\x80\x94which is a final distribution of estate value\xe2\x80\x94and in the absence of any further unresolved bankruptcy issues.

\n

We recognize that Iridium is not the only case in which a court has approved interim distributions that violate ordinary priority rules. But in such instances one can generally find significant Code-related objectives that the priority-violating distributions serve. Courts, for example, have approved \xe2\x80\x9cfirst-day\xe2\x80\x9d wage orders that allow payment of employees\xe2\x80\x99 prepetition wages, \xe2\x80\x9ccritical vendor\xe2\x80\x9d orders that allow payment of essential suppliers\xe2\x80\x99 prepetition invoices, and \xe2\x80\x9croll-ups\xe2\x80\x9d that allow lenders who continue financing the debtor to be paid first on their prepetition claims. See Cybergenics, 330 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 574, n.\xc2\xa08; D. Baird, Elements of Bankruptcy 232\xe2\x80\x93234 (6th ed. 2014); Roe, 99 Va. L.\xc2\xa0Rev., at 1250\xe2\x80\x931264. In doing so, these courts have usually found that the distributions at issue would \xe2\x80\x9cenable a successful reorganization and make even the disfavored creditors better off.\xe2\x80\x9d In\xc2\xa0re Kmart Corp., 359 F.3d 866, 872 (CA7 2004) (discussing the justifications for critical-vendor orders); see also Toibb v. Radloff,\n\n501 U.S. 157\n\xe2\x80\x93164 (1991) (recognizing \xe2\x80\x9cpermitting business debtors to reorganize and restructure their debts in order to revive the debtors\xe2\x80\x99 businesses\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cmaximizing the value of the bankruptcy estate\xe2\x80\x9d as purposes of the Code). By way of contrast, in a structured dismissal like the one ordered below, the priority-violating distribution is attached to a final disposition; it does not preserve the debtor as a going concern; it does not make the disfavored creditors better off; it does not promote the possibility of a confirmable plan; it does not help to restore the status quo ante; and it does not protect reliance interests. In short, we cannot find in the violation of ordinary priority rules that occurred here any significant offsetting bankruptcy-related justification.

\n

Rather, the distributions at issue here more closely resemble proposed transactions that lower courts have refused to allow on the ground that they circumvent the Code\xe2\x80\x99s procedural safeguards. See, e.g., In\xc2\xa0re Braniff Airways, Inc., 700 F.2d 935, 940 (CA5 1983) (prohibiting an attempt to \xe2\x80\x9cshort circuit the requirements of Chapter 11 for confirmation of a reorganization plan by establishing the terms of the plan sub\xc2\xa0rosa in connection with a sale of assets\xe2\x80\x9d); In\xc2\xa0re Lionel Corp., 722 F.2d 1063, 1069 (CA2 1983) (reversing a Bankruptcy Court\xe2\x80\x99s approval of an asset sale after holding that \xc2\xa7363 does not \xe2\x80\x9cgran[t] the bankruptcy judge carte blanche\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cswallo[w] up Chapter 11\xe2\x80\x99s safeguards\xe2\x80\x9d); In\xc2\xa0re Biolitec, Inc., 528 B.R. 261, 269 (Bkrtcy. Ct. NJ 2014) (rejecting a structured dismissal because it \xe2\x80\x9cseeks to alter parties\xe2\x80\x99 rights without their consent and lacks many of the Code\xe2\x80\x99s most important safeguards\xe2\x80\x9d); cf. In\xc2\xa0re Chrysler LLC, 576 F.3d 108, 118 (CA2 2009) (approving a \xc2\xa7363 asset sale because the bankruptcy court demonstrated \xe2\x80\x9cproper solicitude for the priority between creditors and deemed it essential that the [s]ale in no way upset that priority\xe2\x80\x9d), vacated as moot, 592 F.3d 370 (CA2 2010) (per curiam).

\n

IV

\n

We recognize that the Third Circuit did not approve nonconsensual priority-violating structured dismissals in general. To the contrary, the court held that they were permissible only in those \xe2\x80\x9crare case[s]\xe2\x80\x9d in which courts could find \xe2\x80\x9csufficient reasons\xe2\x80\x9d to disregard priority. 787 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 175, 186. Despite the \xe2\x80\x9crare case\xe2\x80\x9d limitation, we still cannot agree.

\n

For one thing, it is difficult to give precise content to the concept \xe2\x80\x9csufficient reasons.\xe2\x80\x9d That fact threatens to turn a \xe2\x80\x9crare case\xe2\x80\x9d exception into a more general rule. Consider the present case. The Bankruptcy Court feared that (1) without the worker-skipping distribution, there would be no settlement, (2) without a settlement, all the unsecured creditors would receive nothing, and consequently (3) its distributions would make some creditors (high- and low-priority creditors) better off without making other (mid-priority) creditors worse off (for they would receive nothing regardless). But, as we have pointed out, the record provides equivocal support for the first two propositions. See supra, at 9\xe2\x80\x9311. And, one can readily imagine other cases that turn on comparably dubious predictions. The result is uncertainty. And uncertainty will lead to similar claims being made in many, not just a few, cases. See Rudzik, A Priority Is a Priority Is a Priority\xe2\x80\x94Except When It Isn\xe2\x80\x99t, 34 Am. Bankr. Inst. J. 16, 79 (2015) (\xe2\x80\x9c[O]nce the floodgates are opened, debtors and favored creditors can be expected to make every case that \xe2\x80\x98rare case\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

The consequences are potentially serious. They include departure from the protections Congress granted particular classes of creditors. See, e.g., United States v. Embassy Restaurant, Inc.,\n\n359 U.S. 29,\n32 (1959)\n (Congress established employee wage priority \xe2\x80\x9cto alleviate in some degree the hardship that unemployment usually brings to workers and their families\xe2\x80\x9d when an employer files for bankruptcy); H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 95\xe2\x80\x93595, at 187 (explaining the importance of ensuring that employees do not \xe2\x80\x9cabandon a failing business for fear of not being paid\xe2\x80\x9d). They include changes in the bargaining power of different classes of creditors even in bankruptcies that do not end in structured dismissals. See Warren, A Theory of Absolute Priority, 1991 Ann. Survey Am. L. 9, 30. They include risks of collusion, i.e., senior secured creditors and general unsecured creditors teaming up to squeeze out priority unsecured creditors. See Bank of America Nat. Trust and Sav. Assn. v. 203 North LaSalle Street Partnership,\n\n526 U.S. 434,\n444 (1999)\n (discussing how the absolute priority rule was developed in response to \xe2\x80\x9cconcern with \xe2\x80\x98the ability of a few insiders, whether representatives of management or major creditors, to use the reorganization process to gain an unfair advantage\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting H.\xc2\xa0R. Doc. No. 93\xe2\x80\x93137, pt. I, p. 255 (1973))). And they include making settlement more difficult to achieve. See Landes & Posner, Legal Precedent: A Theoretical and Empirical Analysis, 19 J.\xc2\xa0Law & Econ. 249, 271 (1976) (arguing that \xe2\x80\x9cthe ratio of lawsuits to settlements is mainly a function of the amount of uncertainty, which leads to divergent estimates by the parties of the probable outcome\xe2\x80\x9d); see also RadLAX Gateway Hotel, LLC v. Amalgamated Bank,\n\n566 U.S. 639,\n649 (2012)\n (noting the importance of clarity and predictability in light of the fact that the \xe2\x80\x9cBankruptcy Code standardizes an expansive (and sometimes unruly) area of law\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

For these reasons, as well as those set forth in Part III, we conclude that Congress did not authorize a \xe2\x80\x9crare case\xe2\x80\x9d exception. We cannot \xe2\x80\x9calter the balance struck by the statute,\xe2\x80\x9d Law v. Siegel, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 11), not even in \xe2\x80\x9crare cases.\xe2\x80\x9d Cf. Norwest Bank Worthington v. Ahlers,\n\n485 U.S. 197,\n207 (1988)\n (explaining that courts cannot deviate from the procedures \xe2\x80\x9cspecified by the Code,\xe2\x80\x9d even when they sincerely \xe2\x80\x9cbeliev[e] that .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. creditors would be better off\xe2\x80\x9d). The judgment of the Court of Appeals is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

\n

It is so ordered.

CASIMIR CZYZEWSKI, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. JEVIC HOLDING CORP., et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

Justice Thomas, with whom Justice Alito joins, dissenting.

\n

Today, the Court answers a novel and important question of bankruptcy law. Unfortunately, it does so without the benefit of any reasoned opinions on the dispositive issue from the courts of appeals (apart from the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 opinion in this case) and with briefing on that issue from only one of the parties. That is because, having persuaded us to grant certiorari on one question, petitioners chose to argue a different question on the merits. In light of that switch, I would dismiss the writ of certiorari as improvidently granted.

\n

We granted certiorari to decide \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hether a bankruptcy court may authorize the distribution of settlement proceeds in a manner that violates the statutory priority scheme.\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Cert. i. According to petitioners, the decision below \xe2\x80\x9cdeepened an existing .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. split\xe2\x80\x9d among the Courts of Appeals on this question. Id., at 8; see id., at 15\xe2\x80\x9316 (citing In\xc2\xa0re AWECO, Inc., 725 F.\xc2\xa02d 293, 298 (CA5 1984), and In\xc2\xa0re Iridium Operating LLC, 478 F.\xc2\xa03d 452, 464 (CA2 2007)). After we granted certiorari, however, petitioners recast the question presented to ask \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hether a Chapter 11 case may be terminated by a \xe2\x80\x98structured dismissal\xe2\x80\x99 that distributes estate property in violation of the Bankruptcy Code\xe2\x80\x99s priority scheme.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners i. Although both questions involve priority-skipping distributions of estate assets, the recast question is narrower\xe2\x80\x94and different\xe2\x80\x94than the one on which we granted certiorari. It is also not the subject of a circuit conflict.

\n

I think it is unwise for the Court to decide the reformulated question today, for two reasons. First, it is a \xe2\x80\x9cnovel question of bankruptcy law\xe2\x80\x9d arising in the rapidly developing field of structured dismissals. In\xc2\xa0re Jevic Holding Corp., 787 F.\xc2\xa03d 173, 175 (CA3 2015). Experience shows that we would greatly benefit from the views of additional courts of appeals on this question. We also would have benefited from full, adversarial briefing. In reliance on this Court\xe2\x80\x99s Rules prohibiting parties from changing the substance of the question presented, see Rule 24.1(a); see also Rule 14.1(a), respondents declined to brief the question that the majority now decides, see Brief for Respondents 52. Second, deciding this question may invite future petitioners to seek review of a circuit conflict only then to change the question to one that seems more favorable. \xe2\x80\x9cI would not reward such bait-and-switch tactics.\xe2\x80\x9d City and County of San Francisco v. Sheehan, 575 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ 2015) (Scalia, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part) (slip op., at 3); see also Visa, Inc. v. Osborn, post, p. ___.

\n

Accordingly, I would dismiss the writ as improvidently granted. I respectfully dissent.
