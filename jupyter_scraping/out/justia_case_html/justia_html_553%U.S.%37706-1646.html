UNITED STATES v. RODRIQUEZ

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 06\xe2\x80\x931646.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 15, 2008\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 19, 2008

\n

Upon respondent\xe2\x80\x99s federal conviction for possession of a firearm by a convicted felon, 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7922(g)(1), he had three prior Washington state convictions for delivery of a controlled substance. At the time of those convictions, Washington law specified a maximum 5-year prison term for the first such offense. A recidivist provision, however, set a 10-year ceiling for a second or subsequent offense, and the state court had sentenced respondent to concurrent 48-month sentences on each count. The Government contended in the federal felon-in-possession case that respondent should be sentenced under the Armed Career Criminal Act (ACCA), \xc2\xa7924(e), which sets a 15-year minimum sentence \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n the case of a person who violates [\xc2\xa7922(g)] and has three previous convictions \xe2\x80\xa6 for a \xe2\x80\xa6 serious drug offense,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7924(e)(1). Because a state drug-trafficking conviction qualifies as \xe2\x80\x9ca serious drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d if \xe2\x80\x9ca maximum term of imprisonment of ten years or more is prescribed by law\xe2\x80\x9d for the \xe2\x80\x9coffense,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7924(e)(2)(A)(ii), and the maximum term on at least two of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s Washington crimes was 10 years under the state recidivist provision, the Government argued that these convictions had to be counted under ACCA. The District Court disagreed, holding that the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term of imprisonment\xe2\x80\x9d for \xc2\xa7924(e)(2)(A)(ii) purposes is determined without reference to recidivist enhancements. The Ninth Circuit affirmed.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term of imprisonment \xe2\x80\xa6 prescribed by law\xe2\x80\x9d for the state drug convictions at issue was the 10-year maximum set by the applicable state recidivist provision. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0This reading is compelled by a straightforward application of \xc2\xa7924(e)(2)(A)(ii)\xe2\x80\x99s three key terms: \xe2\x80\x9coffense,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9claw,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term.\xe2\x80\x9d The \xe2\x80\x9coffense\xe2\x80\x9d was the crime charged in each of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s drug-delivery cases. And because the relevant \xe2\x80\x9claw\xe2\x80\x9d is the state statutes prescribing 5- and 10-year prison terms, the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term\xe2\x80\x9d prescribed for at least two of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s state drug offenses was 10 years. The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s holding that the maximum term was 5 years contorts ACCA\xe2\x80\x99s plain terms. Although the state court sentenced respondent to 48 months, there is no dispute that state law permitted a sentence of up to 10 years. The Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation is also inconsistent with how the concept \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term of imprisonment\xe2\x80\x9d is customarily understood by participants in the criminal justice process. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s textual argument\xe2\x80\x94that because \xe2\x80\x9coffense\xe2\x80\x9d generally describes a crime\xe2\x80\x99s elements, while prior convictions required for recidivist enhancements are not typically elements, such convictions are not part of the ACCA \xe2\x80\x9coffense,\xe2\x80\x9d and the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term\xe2\x80\x9d for the convictions at issue was the 5-year ceiling for simply committing the drug offense elements\xe2\x80\x94is not faithful to the statutory text, which refers to the maximum 10-year term prescribed by Washington law for each of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s two relevant offenses. Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cmanifest purpose\xe2\x80\x9d argument\xe2\x80\x94that because ACCA uses the maximum state-law penalty as shorthand for conduct sufficiently serious to trigger the mandatory penalty, while an offense\xe2\x80\x99s seriousness is typically gauged by the nature of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conduct, the offense\xe2\x80\x99s elements, and the crime\xe2\x80\x99s impact, a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s recidivist status has no connection to whether his offense was serious\xe2\x80\x94rests on the erroneous proposition that a prior record has no bearing on an offense\xe2\x80\x99s seriousness. Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s understanding of recidivism statutes has been has squarely rejected. See, e.g., Nichols v. United States, 511 U. S. 738, 747. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s ACCA interpretation produces a perverse bootstrapping whereby a defendant is punished under federal law for being treated as a recidivist under state law is rejected. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s reading is bolstered by the fact that ACCA is itself a recidivist statute, so that Congress must have understood that the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum penalty prescribed by [state] law\xe2\x80\x9d could be increased by state recidivism provisions. Contrary to respondent\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion, United States v. LaBonte, 520 U. S. 751\xe2\x80\x94in which the Court held that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term authorized\xe2\x80\x9d in 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7994(h) \xe2\x80\x9crefers to all applicable statutes,\xe2\x80\x9d including recidivist enhancements\xe2\x80\x94supports the Court\xe2\x80\x99s ACCA interpretation. Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on Taylor v. United States, 495 U. S. 575, is also misplaced: There is no connection between the issue there (the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cburglary\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa7924(e)(2)(B)(ii)) and the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term of imprisonment \xe2\x80\xa6 prescribed by law\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa7924(e)(2)(A)(ii). Respondent argues unpersuasively that, under today\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation, offenses that are not really serious will be included as \xe2\x80\x9cserious drug offense[s]\xe2\x80\x9d because of recidivist enhancements. Since Congress presumably thought that state lawmakers must consider a crime \xe2\x80\x9cserious\xe2\x80\x9d when they provide a 10-year sentence for it, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding poses no risk that a drug-trafficking offense will be treated as \xe2\x80\x9cserious\xe2\x80\x9d without satisfying the standard Congress prescribed. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(d)\xc2\xa0Also rejected is respondent\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding will often require federal courts to engage in difficult inquiries regarding novel state-law questions and complex factual determinations about long-past state-court proceedings. Respondent greatly exaggerates the difficulties because (1) receipt of a recidivist enhancement will necessarily be evident from the sentence\xe2\x80\x99s length in some cases; (2) the conviction judgment will sometimes list the maximum possible sentence even where the sentence actually imposed did not exceed the top sentence allowed without recidivist enhancement; (3) some jurisdictions require the prosecution to submit a publicly available charging document to obtain a recidivist enhancement; (4) a plea colloquy will often include a statement by the trial judge regarding the maximum penalty; and (5) where the records do not show that the defendant faced a recidivist enhancement, the Government may well be precluded from establishing that a conviction was for a qualifying offense. Merely because future cases might present difficulties cannot justify disregarding ACCA\xe2\x80\x99s clear meaning. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(e)\xc2\xa0Also unavailing is respondent\xe2\x80\x99s argument that if recidivist enhancements can increase the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term\xe2\x80\x9d under ACCA, then mandatory guidelines systems capping sentences can decrease the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term,\xe2\x80\x9d whereas Congress cannot have wanted to make the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term\xe2\x80\x9d dependent on the complexities of state sentencing guidelines. The phrase \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term of imprisonment \xe2\x80\xa6 prescribed by law\xe2\x80\x9d for the \xe2\x80\x9coffense\xe2\x80\x9d could not have been meant to apply to the top sentence in a guidelines range because (1) such a sentence is generally not really the maximum because guidelines systems typically allow a sentencing judge to impose a sentence that exceeds the top of the guidelines range under appropriate circumstances; and (2) in all of the many statutes predating ACCA and the federal Sentencing Reform Act of 1984 that used the concept of the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum\xe2\x80\x9d term prescribed by law, the concept necessarily referred to the maximum term prescribed by the relevant criminal statute, not the top of a sentencing guideline range. United States v. R.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0C., 503 U. S. 291, 295, n.\xc2\xa01, 299, distinguished. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

464 F.\xc2\xa03d 1072, reversed and remanded.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Alito delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under the Armed Career Criminal Act (ACCA), 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7924(e)(2)(A)(ii), a state drug-trafficking conviction qualifies as \xe2\x80\x9ca serious drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d if \xe2\x80\x9ca maximum term of imprisonment of ten years or more is prescribed by law\xe2\x80\x9d for the \xe2\x80\x9coffense.\xe2\x80\x9d The Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit held that \xe2\x80\x9cthe maximum term of imprisonment . . . prescribed by law\xe2\x80\x9d must be determined without taking recidivist enhancements into account. 464 F.\xc2\xa03d 1072, 1082 (2006). We reverse.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At issue in this case is respondent\xe2\x80\x99s sentence on his 2004 conviction in the United States District Court for the Eastern District of Washington for possession of a firearm by a convicted felon, in violation of 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7922(g)(1). Respondent had two prior state convictions in California for residential burglary and three state convictions in Washington for delivery of a controlled substance, in violation of Wash. Rev. Code \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa769.50.401(a)(1)(ii)\xe2\x80\x93(iv) (1994).[Footnote 1] Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s three Washington drug convictions occurred on the same day but were based on deliveries that took place on three separate dates. Sentencing Order No. CR\xe2\x80\x9303\xe2\x80\x93142\xe2\x80\x93RHW (ED Wash., Sept. 3, 2004), p. 5, App. 245, 250 (hereinafter Sentencing Order). At the time of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s drug offenses, the Washington statute that respondent was convicted of violating stated that, upon conviction, a defendant could be \xe2\x80\x9cimprisoned for not more than five years,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa769.50.401(a)(1)(ii)\xe2\x80\x93(iv), but another provision specified that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny person convicted of a second or subsequent offense\xe2\x80\x9d could \xe2\x80\x9cbe imprisoned for a term up to twice the term otherwise authorized,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa769.50.408(a). Thus, by virtue of this latter, recidivist, provision respondent faced a maximum penalty of imprisonment for 10 years. The judgment of conviction for each of the drug-delivery charges listed the maximum term of imprisonment for the offense as \xe2\x80\x9cten years,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 16, 42, 93, but the state court sentenced respondent to concurrent sentences of 48 months\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment on each count. Id., at 21, 47, 98.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the federal felon-in-possession case, the Government asked the District Court to sentence respondent under ACCA, which sets a 15-year minimum sentence \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n the case of a person who violates section 922(g) of [Title 18] and has three previous convictions . . . for a violent felony or a serious drug offense, or both, committed on occasions different from one another \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7924(e)(1) (2000 ed., Supp. V). The Government argued that respondent\xe2\x80\x99s two prior California burglary convictions were for \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98violent felonies.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Cert. 4. See \xc2\xa7924(e)(2)(B)(ii) (2000 ed.) (listing \xe2\x80\x9cburglary\xe2\x80\x9d as a \xe2\x80\x9cviolent felony\xe2\x80\x9d). The District Court agreed, and that ruling is not at issue here.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government also argued that at least two of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s Washington drug convictions were for \xe2\x80\x9cserious drug offense[s].\xe2\x80\x9d Under ACCA, a \xe2\x80\x9cserious drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d includes:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9can offense under State law, involving manufacturing, distributing, or possessing with intent to distribute, a controlled substance (as defined in section 102 of the Controlled Substances Act (21 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. 802)), for which a maximum term of imprisonment of ten years or more is prescribed by law.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7924(e)(2)(A) (emphasis added).

\n

\nBecause the maximum term that respondent faced on at least two of the Washington charges was 10 years, the Government contended that these convictions had to be counted under ACCA. The District Court disagreed, holding that respondent\xe2\x80\x99s drug-trafficking convictions were not convictions for \xe2\x80\x9cserious drug offense[s]\xe2\x80\x9d under ACCA because the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term of imprisonment\xe2\x80\x9d for the purposes of \xc2\xa7924(e)(2)(A)(ii) is determined without reference to recidivist enhancements. Sentencing Order, at 9, App. 254.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit, applying its prior precedent in United States v. Corona-Sanchez, 291 F.\xc2\xa03d 1201 (2002) (en banc), affirmed. 464 F.\xc2\xa03d 1072. The Court recognized that its decision conflicted with the Seventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision in United States v. Henton, 374 F.\xc2\xa03d 467, 469\xe2\x80\x93470, cert. denied, 543 U. S. 967 (2004), and was \xe2\x80\x9cin tension\xe2\x80\x9d with decisions of the Fourth and Fifth Circuits. 464 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1082, n.\xc2\xa06; see Mutascu v. Gonzales, 444 F.\xc2\xa03d 710, 712 (CA5 2006) (per curiam); United States v. Williams, 326 F. 3d 535, 539 (CA4 2003). We granted the Government\xe2\x80\x99s petition for a writ of certiorari, 551 U. S. ___ (2007).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The question that we must decide is whether the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term of imprisonment prescribed by law\xe2\x80\x9d in this case is, as respondent maintains and the Ninth Circuit held, the 5-year ceiling for first offenses or, as the Government contends, the 10-year ceiling for second or subsequent offenses. See Wash. Rev. Code \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa769.50.401(a)\n(ii)\xe2\x80\x93(iv), 69.50.408(a).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government\xe2\x80\x99s reading is compelled by the language of ACCA. For present purposes, there are three key statutory terms: \xe2\x80\x9coffense,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9claw,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term.\xe2\x80\x9d The \xe2\x80\x9coffense\xe2\x80\x9d in each of the drug-delivery cases was a violation of \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa769.50.401(a)(ii)\xe2\x80\x93(iv). The relevant \xe2\x80\x9claw\xe2\x80\x9d is set out in both that provision, which prescribes a \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term\xe2\x80\x9d of five years for a first \xe2\x80\x9coffense,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xc2\xa769.50.408(a), which prescribes a \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term\xe2\x80\x9d of 10 years for a second or subsequent \xe2\x80\x9coffense.\xe2\x80\x9d Thus, in this case, the maximum term prescribed by Washington law for at least two of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s state drug offenses was 10 years.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s holding that the maximum term was five years contorts ACCA\xe2\x80\x99s plain terms. Although the Washington state court sentenced respondent to 48 months\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment, there is no dispute that \xc2\xa769.50.408(a) permitted a sentence of up to 10 years. On the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s reading of ACCA, even if respondent had been sentenced to, say, six years\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment, \xe2\x80\x9cthe maximum term of imprisonment\xe2\x80\x9d prescribed by law still would have been five years. It is hard to accept the proposition that a defendant may lawfully be sentenced to a term of imprisonment that exceeds the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term of imprisonment . . . prescribed by law,\xe2\x80\x9d but that is where the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s reading of the statute leads.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation is also inconsistent with the way in which the concept of the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term of imprisonment\xe2\x80\x9d is customarily understood by participants in the criminal justice process. Suppose that a defendant who indisputably had more than three prior convictions for \xe2\x80\x9cviolent felon[ies]\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cserious drug offense[s]\xe2\x80\x9d was charged in federal court with violating the felon-in-possession statute. Under ACCA, this defendant would face a sentence of \xe2\x80\x9cnot less than 15 years.\xe2\x80\x9d 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7924(e)(1) (2000 ed., Supp. V). Suppose that the defendant asked his or her attorney, \xe2\x80\x9cWhat\xe2\x80\x99s the maximum term I face for the new offense?\xe2\x80\x9d An attorney aware of ACCA would surely not respond, \xe2\x80\x9c10 years,\xe2\x80\x9d even though 10 years is the maximum sentence without the ACCA enhancement. See \xc2\xa7924(a)(2) (2000 ed.).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Suppose that the defendant then pleaded guilty to the felon-in-possession charge. Under Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 11(b)(1)(H), the trial judge would be required to advise the defendant of the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum possible penalty.\xe2\x80\x9d If the judge told the defendant that the maximum possible sentence was 10 years and then imposed a sentence of 15 years based on ACCA, the defendant would have been sorely misled and would have a ground for moving to withdraw the plea. See United States v. Gonzalez, 420 F.\xc2\xa03d 111, 132 (CA2 2005); United States v. Harrington, 354 F.\xc2\xa03d 178, 185\xe2\x80\x93186 (CA2 2004). In sum, a straightforward application of the language of ACCA leads to the conclusion that the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term of imprisonment prescribed by law\xe2\x80\x9d in this case was 10 years.

\n

III

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In an effort to defend the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision, respondent offers both a textual argument and a related argument based on the \xe2\x80\x9cmanifest purpose\xe2\x80\x9d of ACCA. Brief for Respondent 8.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s textual argument is as follows. The term \xe2\x80\x9coffense\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cgenerally is understood to describe the elements constituting the crime.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 10. Because prior convictions required for recidivist enhancements are not typically offense elements, they should not be considered part of the \xe2\x80\x9coffense\xe2\x80\x9d under ACCA. Thus, the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term of imprisonment prescribed by law\xe2\x80\x9d for the drug convictions at issue was the maximum term prescribed for simply committing the elements of the drug offense and was therefore five years. Id., at 10\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s argument is not faithful to the statutory text. Respondent reads ACCA as referring to \xe2\x80\x9cthe maximum term of imprisonment prescribed by law\xe2\x80\x9d for a defendant with no prior convictions that trigger a recidivist enhancement, but that is not what ACCA says. ACCA instead refers to \xe2\x80\x9cthe maximum term of imprisonment prescribed by law\xe2\x80\x9d for \xe2\x80\x9can offense,\xe2\x80\x9d and, as previously explained, in this case, the maximum term prescribed by Washington law for each of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s two relevant offenses was 10 years.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s argument based on ACCA\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cmanifest purpose\xe2\x80\x9d must also be rejected. Respondent argues that ACCA uses \xe2\x80\x9cthe maximum penalty specified for the offense by state law as a short-hand means of identifying conduct deemed sufficiently \xe2\x80\x98serious\xe2\x80\x99 to trigger [the] mandatory penalty.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 9. According to respondent, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he nature of [a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s] conduct, the elements of the offense, and the impact of the crime . . . are the characteristics that typically are used to gauge the \xe2\x80\x98seriousness\xe2\x80\x99 of an offense,\xe2\x80\x9d and a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cstatus as a recidivist has no connection to whether the offense committed by the defendant was a \xe2\x80\x98serious\xe2\x80\x99 one.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 11.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This argument rests on the erroneous proposition that a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s prior record of convictions has no bearing on the seriousness of an offense. On the contrary, however, an offense committed by a repeat offender is often thought to reflect greater culpability and thus to merit greater punishment. Similarly, a second or subsequent offense is often regarded as more serious because it portends greater future danger and therefore warrants an increased sentence for purposes of deterrence and incapacitation. See Witte v. United States, 515 U. S. 389, 403 (1995); Spencer v. Texas, 385 U. S. 554, 570 (1967) (Warren, C.\xc2\xa0J., dissenting in two judgments and concurring in one).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If respondent were correct that a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s record of prior convictions has no bearing on the seriousness of an offense, then it would follow that any increased punishment imposed under a recidivist provision would not be based on the offense of conviction but on something else\xe2\x80\x94presumably the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s prior crimes or the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cstatus as a recidivist,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 11. But we have squarely rejected this understanding of recidivism statutes. In Nichols v. United States, 511 U. S. 738 (1994), we explained that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[t]his Court consistently has sustained repeat-offender laws as penalizing only the last offense committed by the defendant.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 747 (quoting Baldasar v. Illinois, 446 U. S. 222, 232 (1980) (Powell, J., dissenting)). When a defendant is given a higher sentence under a recidivism statute\xe2\x80\x94or for that matter, when a sentencing judge, under a guidelines regime or a discretionary sentencing system, increases a sentence based on the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s criminal history\xe2\x80\x94100% of the punishment is for the offense of conviction. None is for the prior convictions or the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cstatus as a recidivist.\xe2\x80\x9d The sentence \xe2\x80\x9cis a stiffened penalty for the latest crime, which is considered to be an aggravated offense because [it is] a repetitive one.\xe2\x80\x9d Gryger v. Burke, 334 U. S. 728, 732 (1948).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent argues that our interpretation of ACCA produces \xe2\x80\x9ca sort of perverse bootstrapping\xe2\x80\x9d under which a defendant is \xe2\x80\x9cpunished under federal law for being treated as a recidivist under state law,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 20 (emphasis deleted), but the fact that ACCA is itself a recidivist statute bolsters our reading. Since ACCA is a recidivist statute, Congress must have had such provisions in mind and must have understood that the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum penalty prescribed by [state] law\xe2\x80\x9d in some cases would be increased by state recidivism provisions.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Contrary to respondent\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion, United States v. LaBonte, 520 U. S. 751 (1997), supports our interpretation of ACCA. The statute at issue in LaBonte, a provision of the Sentencing Reform Act of 1984, as amended, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7994(h), directed the United States Sentencing Commission to \xe2\x80\x9cassure\xe2\x80\x9d that the Sentencing Guidelines specify a prison sentence \xe2\x80\x9cat or near the maximum term authorized for categories of\xe2\x80\x9d adult offenders who commit their third felony drug offense or violent crime. We held that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term authorized\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9crefers to all applicable statutes,\xe2\x80\x9d including recidivist enhancements. 520 U. S., at 758, n.\xc2\xa04.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent claims that LaBonte supports his position because ACCA, unlike 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7994(h), does not refer to \xe2\x80\x9ccategories of\xe2\x80\x9d offenders. Respondent suggests that Congress\xe2\x80\x99 failure to include such language in ACCA means that Congress intended to refer to a \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term\xe2\x80\x9d that does not depend on whether a defendant falls into the first-time-offender or recidivist \xe2\x80\x9ccategory.\xe2\x80\x9d Respondent does not explain how 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7924(e)(2)(A) could have easily been reworded to mirror 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7994(h). But in any event, the language used in ACCA, for the reasons explained above, is more than clear enough.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent argues that the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision is supported by the so-called \xe2\x80\x9ccategorical\xe2\x80\x9d approach that we used in Taylor v. United States, 495 U. S. 575 (1990), in determining which offenses qualify as \xe2\x80\x9cviolent felon[ies]\xe2\x80\x9d under 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7924(e)(2)(B)(ii). Section 924(e)(2)(B)(ii) provides that four enumerated crimes\xe2\x80\x94burglary, arson, extortion, and offenses involving the use of explosives\xe2\x80\x94are \xe2\x80\x9cviolent felon[ies]\xe2\x80\x9d for ACCA purposes. In Taylor, we held that Congress intended for these crimes to have a \xe2\x80\x9cuniform definition\xe2\x80\x9d that was \xe2\x80\x9cindependent of the labels employed by the various States\xe2\x80\x99 criminal codes.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 592. According to respondent, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he categorical approach rests on the congressional intent\xe2\x80\x94reflected in the statutory language\xe2\x80\x94to focus the ACCA inquiry on the offense of conviction, rather than on collateral matters unrelated to the definition of the crime.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 12.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We see no connection, however, between the issue in Taylor (the meaning of the term \xe2\x80\x9cburglary\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa7924(e)(2)(B)(ii)) and the issue here (the meaning of the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term of imprisonment . . . prescribed by law\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa7924(e)(2)(A)(ii)). Taylor held that the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cburglary\xe2\x80\x9d for purposes of ACCA does not depend on the label attached by the law of a particular State, 495 U. S., at 600\xe2\x80\x93601, but the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum penalty prescribed by law\xe2\x80\x9d for a state offense necessarily depends on state law.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For a similar reason, we reject respondent\xe2\x80\x99s argument that, under our interpretation, offenses that are not really serious will be included as \xe2\x80\x9cserious drug offense[s]\xe2\x80\x9d because of recidivist enhancements. In \xc2\xa7924(e)(2)(A)(ii), Congress chose to rely on the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term of imprisonment . . . prescribed\xe2\x80\x9d by state law as the measure of the seriousness of state offenses involving the manufacture, distribution, or possession of illegal drugs. Congress presumably thought\xe2\x80\x94not without reason\xe2\x80\x94that if state lawmakers provide that a crime is punishable by 10 years\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment, the lawmakers must regard the crime as \xe2\x80\x9cserious,\xe2\x80\x9d and Congress chose to defer to the state lawmakers\xe2\x80\x99 judgment. Therefore, our interpretation poses no risk that a drug-trafficking offense will be treated as a \xe2\x80\x9cserious\xe2\x80\x9d without satisfying the standard that Congress prescribed.[Footnote 2]

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent argues that it will often be difficult to determine whether a defendant faced the possibility of a recidivist enhancement in connection with a past state drug conviction and that therefore our interpretation of ACCA will require the federal courts to \xe2\x80\x9cengage in difficult inquiries regarding novel questions of state law and complex factual determinations about long-past proceedings in state courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 21. Respondent greatly exaggerates the problems to which he refers.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, in some cases, a defendant will have received a recidivist enhancement, and this will necessarily be evident from the length of the sentence imposed. Second, as the present case illustrates, see App. 16, 42, 93, the judgment of conviction will sometimes list the maximum possible sentence even where the sentence that was imposed did not exceed the top sentence allowed without any recidivist enhancement. Third, as respondent himself notes, some jurisdictions require that the prosecution submit a formal charging document in order to obtain a recidivist enhancement. See Brief for Respondent 33. Such documents fall within the limited list of generally available documents that courts already consult for the purpose of determining if a past conviction qualifies as an ACCA predicate. See Shepard v. United States, 544 U. S. 13, 20 (2005). Fourth, in those cases in which the defendant pleaded guilty to the state drug charges, the plea colloquy will very often include a statement by the trial judge regarding the maximum penalty. This is mandated by Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 11(b)(1)(H), and many States have similar requirements.[Footnote 3] Finally, in those cases in which the records that may properly be consulted do not show that the defendant faced the possibility of a recidivist enhancement, it may well be that the Government will be precluded from establishing that a conviction was for a qualifying offense. The mere possibility that some future cases might present difficulties cannot justify a reading of ACCA that disregards the clear meaning of the statutory language.

\n

D

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s last argument is that if recidivist enhancements can increase the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term\xe2\x80\x9d of imprisonment under ACCA, it must follow that mandatory guidelines systems that cap sentences can decrease the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term\xe2\x80\x9d of imprisonment. Brief for Respondent 38. In each situation, respondent argues, the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term\xe2\x80\x9d of imprisonment is the term to which the state court could actually have sentenced the defendant. Respondent concedes that he has waived this argument with respect to his own specific state-court convictions. See Brief in Opposition 15, n.\xc2\xa07. He argues, however, that Congress cannot have wanted to make the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term\xe2\x80\x9d of imprisonment for ACCA purposes dependent on the complexities of state sentencing guidelines. We conclude, however, that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term of imprisonment . . . prescribed by law\xe2\x80\x9d for the \xe2\x80\x9coffense\xe2\x80\x9d was not meant to apply to the top sentence in a guidelines range.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, the top sentence in a guidelines range is generally not really the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term . . . prescribed by law\xe2\x80\x9d for the \xe2\x80\x9coffense\xe2\x80\x9d because guidelines systems typically allow a sentencing judge to impose a sentence that exceeds the top of the guidelines range under appropriate circumstances. The United States Sentencing Guidelines, for example, permit \xe2\x80\x9cupward departures,\xe2\x80\x9d see United States Sentencing Commission, Guidelines Manual \xc2\xa75K2.0 (Nov. 2007), and essentially the same characteristic was shared by all of the mandatory guidelines system in existence at the time of the enactment of the ACCA provision at issue in this case.[Footnote 4] (Following this pattern, Washington law likewise provided at the time of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s state convictions that a sentencing judge could \xe2\x80\x9cimpose a sentence outside the standard sentence range\xe2\x80\x9d upon a finding \xe2\x80\x9cthat there [were] substantial and compelling reasons justifying an exceptional sentence.\xe2\x80\x9d Wash. Rev. Code \xc2\xa79.94A.120(2) (1994).[Footnote 5])

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, the concept of the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum\xe2\x80\x9d term of imprisonment or sentence prescribed by law was used in many statutes that predated the enactment of ACCA and the federal Sentencing Reform Act of 1984, Pub. L. 98\xe2\x80\x93473, \xc2\xa7211, 98 Stat. 1987, and in all those statutes the concept necessarily referred to the maximum term prescribed by the relevant criminal statute, not the top of a sentencing guideline range. See, e.g., 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73 (1982 ed.) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]n accessory after the fact shall be imprisoned not more than one-half the maximum term of imprisonment \xe2\x80\xa6 for the punishment of the principal\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa73575(b) (allowing for an increased sentence for dangerous special offenders \xe2\x80\x9cnot disproportionate in severity to the maximum term otherwise authorized by law for\xe2\x80\x9d the underlying felony); see also \xc2\xa7371 (the punishment for conspiracy to commit a misdemeanor \xe2\x80\x9cshall not exceed the maximum punishment provided for such misdemeanor\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa73651 (allowing for confinement and suspension of sentence upon conviction of an offense not punishable by death or life imprisonment \xe2\x80\x9cif the maximum sentence for such offense is more than six months\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa73653 (referring to the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum probation period\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is instructive that, even in the Sentencing Reform Act, the concept of the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term of imprisonment\xe2\x80\x9d prescribed for an offense was used in this sense. See \xc2\xa7212, 98 Stat. 1991\xe2\x80\x931992 (new 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73559 classifying offenses based on \xe2\x80\x9cthe maximum term of imprisonment authorized \xe2\x80\xa6 by the statute describing the offense\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa7235(b)(1)(F), 98 Stat. 2032 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe maximum term of imprisonment in effect on the effective date [of the Sentencing Reform Act]\xe2\x80\x9d remains in effect for five years after the effective date \xe2\x80\x9cfor an offense committed before the effective date\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa71003(a), id., at 2138 (solicitation to commit a crime of violence punishable by \xe2\x80\x9cone-half the maximum term of imprisonment . . . prescribed for the punishment of the crime solicited\xe2\x80\x9d). In light of this established pattern and the relative newness of sentencing guidelines systems when the ACCA provision at issue here was added, we conclude that Congress meant for the concept of the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term of imprisonment\xe2\x80\x9d prescribed by law for an \xe2\x80\x9coffense\xe2\x80\x9d to have same meaning in ACCA.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our decision in United States v. R. L. C., 503 U. S. 291 (1992), is not to the contrary. The statutory provision there, 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa75037(c) (2000 ed.), set out the term of official detention for a juvenile found to be a delinquent. This provision was amended by the Sentencing Reform Act, see \xc2\xa7214, 98 Stat. 2013, and then amended again two years later, see \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa721(a)(2)\xe2\x80\x93(4), 100 Stat. 3596. As thus amended, the provision did not refer to the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term of imprisonment\xe2\x80\x9d prescribed for an \xe2\x80\x9coffense.\xe2\x80\x9d Rather, the provision focused on the particular juvenile being sentenced. It provided that, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98in the case of a juvenile who is less than eighteen years old,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d official detention could not extend beyond the earlier of two dates: the juvenile\xe2\x80\x99s 21st birthday or \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the maximum term of imprisonment that would be authorized if the juvenile had been tried and convicted as an adult.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. R.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0C., supra, at 295\xe2\x80\x93296, n.\xc2\xa01 (quoting 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa75037(c)). Because this provision clearly focuses on the circumstances of the particular juvenile and not on the offense, 503 U. S., at 299, it is not analogous to the ACCA provision that is before us in this case.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For these reasons, we hold that the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term of imprisonment \xe2\x80\xa6 prescribed by law\xe2\x80\x9d for the state drug convictions at issue in this case was the 10-year maximum set by the applicable recidivist provision. Accordingly, we reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeals and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court chooses one reading of the Armed Career Criminal Act (ACCA), 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7924(e) (2000 ed. and Supp. V), over another that would make at least as much sense of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s ambiguous text and would follow the counsel of a tradition of lenity in construing perplexing criminal laws. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s choice, moreover, promises hard times for the trial courts that will have to make the complex sentencing calculations this decision demands. I respectfully dissent.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The ACCA mandates a 15-year minimum sentence for anyone convicted of violating \xc2\xa7922(g) (2000 ed.) who \xe2\x80\x9chas three previous convictions [for] a serious drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d among his prior crimes. \xc2\xa7924(e)(1) (2000 ed., Supp. V). Section 924(e)(2)(A) (2000 ed.) defines \xe2\x80\x9cserious drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d as an offense under state or federal drug laws, \xe2\x80\x9cfor which a maximum term of imprisonment of ten years or more is prescribed by law.\xe2\x80\x9d This limitation leaves open the question whether a given conviction qualifies as \xe2\x80\x9cserious\xe2\x80\x9d by reference to the penalty for the acts making up the basic offense, regardless of who commits it, or whether account must also be taken of further facts (such as an offender\xe2\x80\x99s criminal record that qualified him for an enhanced penalty at the time of that earlier conviction). If the first alternative is the reading Congress intended, a sentencing judge needs to look only to the penalty specified for the basic offense committed by a first-time\noffender. But if the second is the intended one, a judge may have to consider sentencing variations (for using a gun, say, or for repeating the offense) set out in other provisions.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It all turns on the meaning of the word \xe2\x80\x9coffense,\xe2\x80\x9d to which the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum term\xe2\x80\x9d is tied. One can naturally read \xe2\x80\x9can offense\xe2\x80\x9d at a general level as synonymous with \xe2\x80\x9ca crime,\xe2\x80\x9d which would tend to rule out reference to maximums adjusted for other facts; we do not usually speak of a crime of \xe2\x80\x9cburglary while having a criminal record and while out on bail.\xe2\x80\x9d Those details would come up only if we were speaking about a specific instance, described as a burglary \xe2\x80\x9ccommitted by someone with a record while out on bail,\xe2\x80\x9d in which case the other facts may \xe2\x80\x9cenhance\xe2\x80\x9d his sentence beyond what would have been the maximum term for burglary. The trouble is that \xe2\x80\x9coffense\xe2\x80\x9d could easily refer to a specific occurrence, too; looking at it that way would make it less jarring to suggest that the circumstances around an event that authorize higher penalty ranges (such as the use of a gun) or the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s history (like a prior conviction) ought to count in identifying the maximum penalty for the offense committed on the given day, at the given place, by the particular offender, in a given way. Either reading seems to offer a plausible take on the \xe2\x80\x9coffense\xe2\x80\x9d for which the ACCA court will have to identify or calculate \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum\xe2\x80\x9d penalties, under state law.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We get no help from imagining the circumstances in which a sentencing court would ask which reading to adopt. The choice of answer would be easy if the question arose in the mind of a lawyer whose client is thinking about a guilty plea and asks what maximum term he faces. See ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x935. His lawyer knows that he means the maximum term for him in his case. When a repeat offender wants to know, counsel understands that the penalty prescribed for the basic crime without the recidivist add-on is not the baseline for comparison that may make or break the potential plea agreement. And if the repeat offender faces a further statutory enhancement for carrying a gun during the offense, or for being out on bail, his lawyer would not tell him the maximum term for repeat offenders without guns or bail restrictions. By the same token, if the offender faced (as Rodriquez did) a lower sentence ceiling than what the statute says, by grace of mandatory sentencing guidelines, his lawyer would know enough to tell him that his maximum was capped in this way.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When the issue comes up not in a particular client\xe2\x80\x99s questions about his own prospects, however, but in a trial judge\xe2\x80\x99s mind wondering about the meaning of the general statute, context gives no ready answer. Nor does it break the tie to say, as the Court does, that taking \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum\xe2\x80\x9d to refer to the basic offense would mean that a recidivist with add-ons could be sentenced above the ACCA \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum,\xe2\x80\x9d see ante, at 4 (\xe2\x80\x9ceven if respondent had been sentenced to, say, six years\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment, \xe2\x80\x98the maximum term of imprisonment\xe2\x80\x99 prescribed by law still would have been five years\xe2\x80\x9d). That description, after all, might be just a verbal quirk showing the statutory design in proper working order: if Congress meant an offense to be viewed generically and apart from offender characteristics, a gap between the maximum for ACCA purposes, and a heavier, actual sentence accounting for a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s history is to be expected.[Footnote 1]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The text does not point to any likelier interpretive choices, and as between these alternatives, it is simply ambiguous.[Footnote 2] Because I do not believe its ambiguity is fairly resolved in the Government\xe2\x80\x99s favor, I would affirm.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0None of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s three principal points or ripostes solves the puzzle. To begin with, there is something arbitrary about trying to resolve the ambiguity by rejecting the maximum-for-basic-offense option while declining to consider an entire class of offender-based sentencing adjustments. If offender characteristics are going to count in identifying the relevant maximum penalty, it would seem to follow that in jurisdictions with mandatory sentencing guidelines, the maximum \xe2\x80\x9cprescribed by law\xe2\x80\x9d would be what the guidelines determine. The original Federal Guidelines, and the mandatory state guidelines I am aware of, were established under statutory authority that invests a guideline with the same legal status as a customary penalty provision. Cf. United States v. R. L. C., 503 U. S. 291, 297 (1992) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe answer to any suggestion that the statutory character of a specific penalty provi-\nsion gives it primacy over administrative sentencing guidelines is that the mandate to apply the Guidelines is itself statutory\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court tries to deflect the implication of its position by denying that state sentencing guidelines really do set maximum penalties, since typically they allow a judge to depart from them, up or down, when specified conditions are met. See ante, at 12. But while this is true, the objection stands. However a particular mandatory guideline scheme works, it sets a maximum somewhere; if it includes conditions affecting what would otherwise be a guideline maximum, the top of the range as affected should be the relevant maximum on the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reading of the statute. Indeed, the factual conditions involved are usually offender characteristics, and if the ACCA is going to count them under offense-defining statutes or freestanding recidivism laws, those same facts ought to count under a guideline rule (whether setting, or authorizing a departure from, a particular limit). There is no practical difference whether maximums are adjusted by a statute, a statutorily mandated guideline, or a guideline-specified departure; wherever a \xe2\x80\x9cprescri[ption] by law\xe2\x80\x9d resides, it ought to be honored by the ACCA court.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If we were to follow the Court\xe2\x80\x99s lights, then, I think we would have to accept the complication that guidelines schemes present, and face the difficulty of calculating enhanced maximums in guidelines jurisdictions.[Footnote 3] What we cannot do is to resolve statutory ambiguity by looking to the sentencing range for an imaginary offender who meets statutory conditions for altering the basic sentence, but is artificially stripped of any characteristic that triggers a guideline rule also \xe2\x80\x9cprescribed by law.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The more fundamental objection, though, goes to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s basic conclusion that it makes the better sense to read the ACCA as resting the federal treatment of recidivists on the maximum sentence authorized by state recidivist schemes, in cases where state law must be considered. The Court says it would have been natural for Congress to think in terms of state judgments about repeat criminals when thinking about what to do at the national level, and the Court is quite possibly right about this; the fact that the federal penalty may turn on a state felony classification at all shows that Congress was thinking about state law. But the chances are at least equally good that the Court is wrong; it is odd to think that Congress would have piggybacked the federal system on state repeat-offender schemes, given the extraordinary and irreconcilable variations among state policies on the subject.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For one thing, the States\xe2\x80\x99 recidivism schemes vary in their methods for augmenting sentences. Iowa\xe2\x80\x99s law, for example, subjects repeat drug offenders to triple penalties, Iowa Code \xc2\xa7124.411(1) (2005); but in Wisconsin a repeat drug distributor will see his maximum term increased by\na fixed number of years, whatever the starting point,\nsee, e.g., Wis. Stat. \xc2\xa7961.48(1)(b) (2003\xe2\x80\x932004) (4-year in-\ncrease for Class H felony such as selling 1 kilogram of marijuana).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0More striking than differing structures, though, are the vast disparities in severity from State to State: under Massachusetts drug laws, a third conviction for selling a small amount of marijuana carries a maximum of 2.5 years. Mass. Gen. Laws Ann., ch. 94C, \xc2\xa732C(b) (West 2006). In Delaware, a third conviction means a mandatory sentence of life in prison without parole. See Del. Code Ann., Tit. 11, \xc2\xa74214(b) (2007) (third-felony penalty of life without parole for violations of non-narcotic controlled substances law, Tit. 16, \xc2\xa74752 (2004)). That Congress might have chosen to defer to state-law judgments about \xe2\x80\x9cseriousness\xe2\x80\x9d that vary so widely for the same conduct is at least open to doubt. And that doubt only gets worse when we notice that even where two States have similar maximum penalties for a base-level offense, their recidivist enhancements may lead the same conduct to trigger the ACCA sanction in one State but not the other: on the Court\xe2\x80\x99s view, an offender\xe2\x80\x99s second conviction for selling, say, just over two pounds of marijuana will qualify as an ACCA predicate crime if the conviction occurred in Arizona (maximum of 13 years), Iowa (15 years), Utah (15 years), and the District of Columbia (10 years), for example;[Footnote 4] but it will fall short of the mark in California (8 years), Michigan (8 years), and New York (8 years).[Footnote 5] Yet in each of these States, the base-level offense has a maximum term falling within a much narrower range (between 3.5 and 5.5 years).[Footnote 6] With this backdrop of state law, the Government can hardly be heard to say that there would be something \xe2\x80\x9cincongruous\xe2\x80\x9d about a federal law targeting offenses flagged by the penalties assigned only to bare conduct, without regard to recidivism or other offender facts. Brief for United States 17.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor does it show what the ACCA means by \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9coffense\xe2\x80\x9d when the Court points to language from our prior cases saying that enhanced recidivist penalties are not to be viewed as retroactive punishment for past crimes, for purposes of double-jeopardy and right-to-counsel enquiries. See ante, at 7 (citing Nichols v. United States, 511 U. S. 738, 747 (1994), and Gryger v. Burke, 334 U. S. 728, 732 (1948)). The quotations show that a separate offense is identified by an enhanced penalty, the Court says, because from them we can draw the conclusion that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen a defendant is given a higher sentence under a recidivism statute,\xe2\x80\x9d nonetheless \xe2\x80\x9c100% of the punishment is for the offense of conviction,\xe2\x80\x9d leaving nothing to be attributed to \xe2\x80\x9cprior convictions or the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x98status as a recidivist,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 7.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Still, the fact is that state-law maximums for repeat offenders sometimes bear hardly any relation to the gravity of the triggering offense, as \xe2\x80\x9cthree-strikes\xe2\x80\x9d laws (not to mention the Delaware example, above) often show. See, e.g., Ill. Comp. Stat., ch. 720, \xc2\xa75/33B\xe2\x80\x931 (2004) (mandatory life sentence for third \xe2\x80\x9cClass X\xe2\x80\x9d felony, such as dealing heroin, without regard to the specific penalty gradation for the latest Class X felony or to any similarity with prior offenses); W.\xc2\xa0Va. Code Ann. \xc2\xa761\xe2\x80\x9311\xe2\x80\x9318(c) (2005) (if offender was \xe2\x80\x9ctwice before convicted in the United States of a crime punishable by confinement in a penitentiary,\xe2\x80\x9d third such conviction incurs a mandatory life sentence). Cf. Ewing v. California, 538 U. S. 11, 30, n. 2 (2003) (plurality opinion) (the \xe2\x80\x9cCalifornia Legislature therefore made a deliberate policy decision \xe2\x80\xa6 that the gravity of the new felony should not be a determinative factor in triggering the application of the Three Strikes Law\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). And there is no denying that the fact of prior convictions (or a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s recidivist status) is necessary for the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98stiffened penalty\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to be imposed for \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the latest crime,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 7, the necessary fact being specific to the offender, and falling outside the definition of the offense. This is, after all, what it means to apply an \xe2\x80\x9cenhancement.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The upshot is that it may have been natural for Congress to think of state recidivism schemes, but it may well not have been. If there is anything strange about ignoring enhanced penalties, there is something at least as strange about a federal recidivist statute that piles enhancement on enhancement, magnifying the severity of state laws severe to begin with.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Whatever may be the plausibility of the offender-based reading of the statute as the Court describes it, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s description avoids a source of serious doubt by glossing over the practical problems its take on the statute portends. The Court is unmoved by the argument that Congress probably did not expect federal courts applying the ACCA to master the countless complications of state sentencing schemes; because all jurisdictions provide for enhanced sentencing some way or another, the Court thinks there is nothing threatening in the subject, which it tries to simplify by offering a few practical pointers. It notes that there will be cases with a qualifying enhancement \xe2\x80\x9cevident from the length of the sentence imposed\xe2\x80\x9d by the state court; sometimes, it says, a court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cjudgment of conviction will \xe2\x80\xa6 list the maximum possible sentence\xe2\x80\x9d; or the state prosecutor will have \xe2\x80\x9csubmit[ted] a formal charging document in order to obtain a recidivist enhancement.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10. And in cases involving pleas, the Court notes, \xe2\x80\x9cthe plea colloquy will very often include a statement by the trial judge regarding the maximum penalty.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Even when there are no pointers to help, says the Court, and \xe2\x80\x9cthe records that may properly be consulted\xe2\x80\x9d yield no clear answer, the worst that can happen will be the Government\xe2\x80\x99s inability to show that a prior conviction qualifies. Ante, at 11.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But it is not that easy, and the Court\xe2\x80\x99s pointers are not much comfort. To start with, even where a \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum\xe2\x80\x9d sentence is mentioned in state records, how will the ACCA court be supposed to know that the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum\xe2\x80\x9d written down there is what the Court today holds that \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum\xe2\x80\x9d means? A State\xe2\x80\x99s number below 10 years may refer to the base-level offense, or it may be the reduced maximum required by mandatory guidelines; and a number over 10 years may be the product of other enhancements (as for weapons use or being out on bail at the time of commission). Having to enquire into just what imposed sentences or what trial documents really mean would seem to leave plenty of sorting out for the federal courts to do (or at least, for federal prosecutors, if they end up with the job).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Another example: State laws are not written to coordinate with the ACCA, and if a State\xe2\x80\x99s specific repeat drug-offender provisions, say, are supposed to be read together with its general habitual-offender statutes, the resulting \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum\xe2\x80\x9d may not be the Court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum.\xe2\x80\x9d Indeed, a federal court may have to figure out just how those state statutes may be read together to avoid conflict between them, when the way to avoid conflict is not clear cut even for the state courts, see, e.g., Goldberg v. State, 282 Ga. 542, 651 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 667 (2007) (general recidivist statute trumps more specific one; overruling same court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Mikell v. State, 270 Ga. 467, 510 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 523 (1999)); State v. Keith, 102 N.\xc2\xa0M. 462, 697 P.\xc2\xa02d 145 (App. 1985) (specific trumps general). Cf. Clines v. State, 912 So.\xc2\xa02d 550 (Fla. 2005) (relying on rule of lenity to resolve whether multiple recidivist categories in same habitual-offender law could apply to a single sentence).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0And there is more: as Rodriquez reminds us, just deciding what counts as a \xe2\x80\x9cprior\xe2\x80\x9d offense under state law is not always an easy thing. See People v. Wiley, 9 Cal. 4th 580, 583, 889 P.\xc2\xa02d 541, 542 (1995) (noting difficulty of applying requirement that \xe2\x80\x9cprior\xe2\x80\x9d charges have been \xe2\x80\x9cbrought and tried separately,\xe2\x80\x9d where defendant had been convicted in trials occurring one day apart and sentenced at the same court session; in the end, drawing the needed inference from docket numbers revealed on documents requested from the municipal trial court); id., at 595, 889 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 550 (Werdegar, J., dissenting) (protesting the court\xe2\x80\x99s solicitation and use of extra-record documents). Nor would that sort of enquiry get any easier, or be more likely to benefit from well-settled state law, when a given State\xe2\x80\x99s law takes account of prior offenses in other States, see Timothy v. State, 90 P.\xc2\xa03d 177 (Alaska App. 2004) (holding Oklahoma burglary not to be analogous to one in Alaska, for purposes of Alaska\xe2\x80\x99s recidivism enhancements, thus overruling its own 2-year-old decision, Butts v. State, 53 P.\xc2\xa03d 609 (2002)); or, to take a specific example, when what qualifies a prior offense under one State\xe2\x80\x99s recidivism scheme is the length of the sentence authorized by another State\xe2\x80\x99s law (raising the question whether that first State would see recidivist enhancements the same way the Court does today). See, e.g., N.\xc2\xa0J. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa72C:44\xe2\x80\x934(c) (West 2005) (\xe2\x80\x9cA conviction in another jurisdiction shall constitute a prior conviction of a crime if a sentence of imprisonment in excess of 6 months was authorized under the law of the other jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d); N.\xc2\xa0M. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x9318\xe2\x80\x9317(D)(2)(b) (2007 Supp.) (defining \xe2\x80\x9cprior felony conviction\xe2\x80\x9d as, inter alia, a felony \xe2\x80\x9cpunishable [by] a maximum term of imprisonment of more than one year\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A still thornier problem is how federal courts are supposed to treat a State\xe2\x80\x99s procedural safeguards for using prior convictions at sentencing. Saying that congressional deference to the States\xe2\x80\x99 judgments about the severity of crimes also extends to their judgments about recidivism raises, but does not answer, the question whether such deference goes only as far as the state courts themselves could go in raising penalties. (The Court\xe2\x80\x99s disregard of mandatory sentencing guidelines would seem to suggest that the answer is no.) In those States that require notice before the prosecutor can seek a recidivism enhancement, for example, how will a federal court decide whether the ACCA counts a prior conviction that would have qualified for recidivism enhancement if the state prosecutor had not failed to give timely notice? See, e.g., Commonwealth v. Fernandes, 430 Mass. 517, 522, 722 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 406, 409 (1999) (noting longstanding rule that the indictment must give notice of prior convictions \xe2\x80\x9cthat may subject the defendant to enhanced punishment\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I could go on, but this is enough to show that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation promises that ACCA courts will face highly complicated enquiries into every State\xe2\x80\x99s or Territory\xe2\x80\x99s collection of ancillary sentencing laws. That is an unconvincing answer to the ambiguity.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the end of the day, a plainly superior reading may well be elusive; one favoring the Government certainly is. It does not defy common English or common sense, after all, to look at a statute with one penalty range for the basic crime and a higher one for a repeat offender and say that the former sets the maximum penalty for the \xe2\x80\x9coffense\xe2\x80\x9d; but neither is it foolish to see the \xe2\x80\x9coffense\xe2\x80\x9d as defined by its penalty, however that is computed. What I have said so far suggests that I think the basic-crime view of \xe2\x80\x9coffense\xe2\x80\x9d is the better one, but I will concede that the competing positions are pretty close to evenly matched. And on that assumption, there is a ready tie-breaker.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The interpretation adopted by both the District Court and the Court of Appeals is the one counseled by the rule of lenity, which applies where (as here) we have \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0seiz[ed] every thing from which aid can be derived,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d but are \xe2\x80\x9cleft with an ambiguous statute,\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Bass, 404 U. S. 336, 347 (1971) (quoting United States v. Fisher, 2 Cranch 358, 386 (1805) (opinion of the Court by Marshall, C.\xc2\xa0J.)). The rule is grounded in \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the instinctive distaste against men languishing in prison unless the lawmaker has clearly said they should,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Bass, supra, at 348 (quoting H. Friendly, Benchmarks 209 (1967)), and we have used it to resolve questions both about metes and bounds of criminal conduct and about the severity of sentencing. See Bifulco v. United States, 447 U. S. 381, 387 (1980) (collecting cases). \xe2\x80\x9cThis policy of lenity means that the Court will not interpret a federal criminal statute so as to increase the penalty that it places on an individual when such an interpretation can be based on no more than a guess as to what Congress intended.\xe2\x80\x9d Ladner v. United States, 358 U. S. 169, 178 (1958).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This is why lenity should control here. Even recognizing the best that can be said for the Government\xe2\x80\x99s side, its position rests on debatable guesswork to send a man to prison for 180 months, as against 92 months on the basic-crime view. And the District Courts will be imposing higher sentences more than doubling the length of the alternative in a good many other cases, as well.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The \xe2\x80\x9cfair warning\xe2\x80\x9d that motivates the lenity rule, McBoyle v. United States, 283 U. S. 25, 27 (1931) (opinion of the Court by Holmes, J.), may sometimes be a benign fiction, see R. L. C., 503 U. S., at 309 (Scalia, J., concurring), but there is only one reading of this statute with any realistic chance of giving fair notice of how the ACCA will apply, and that is the reading the District Court and the Court of Appeals each chose. Their choice should be ours, too.
