NELSON v. CAMPBELL, COMMISSIONER, ALABAMA DEPARTMENT OF CORRECTIONS, et al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

No. 03\xe2\x80\x936821. Argued March 29, 2004\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 24, 2004

\n

Three days before his scheduled execution by lethal injection, petitioner filed a 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983 action against respondent Alabama prison officials, alleging that the use of a \xe2\x80\x9ccut-down\xe2\x80\x9d procedure requiring an incision into his arm or leg to access his severely compromised veins constituted cruel and unusual punishment and deliberate indifference to his medical needs in violation of the Eighth Amendment. Petitioner, who had already filed an unsuccessful federal habeas application, sought a permanent injunction against the cut-down\xe2\x80\x99s use, a temporary stay of execution so the District Court could consider his claim\xe2\x80\x99s merits, and orders requiring respondents to furnish a copy of the protocol on the medical procedures for venous access and directing them to promulgate a venous access protocol that comports with contemporary standards. Respondents moved to dismiss the complaint for want of jurisdiction on the grounds that the \xc2\xa71983 claim and stay request were the equivalent of a second or successive habeas application subject to 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72244(b)\xe2\x80\x99s gatekeeping requirements. Agreeing, the District Court dismissed the complaint because petitioner had not obtained authorization to file such an application. In affirming, the Eleventh Circuit held that method-of-execution challenges necessarily sound in habeas, and that it would have denied a habeas authorization request.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Section 1983 is an appropriate vehicle for petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s Eighth Amendment claim seeking a temporary stay and permanent injunctive relief. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Section 1983 must yield to the federal habeas statute where an inmate seeks injunctive relief challenging the fact of his conviction or the duration of his sentence. Such claims fall within the core of habeas. By contrast, constitutional claims challenging confinement conditions fall outside of that core and may be brought under \xc2\xa71983 in the first instance. The Court need not reach here the difficult question of how method-of-execution claims should be classified generally. Respondents have conceded that \xc2\xa71983 would be the appropriate vehicle for an inmate who is not facing execution to bring a \xe2\x80\x9cdeliberate indifference\xe2\x80\x9d challenge to the cut-down procedure\xe2\x80\x99s constitutionality if used to gain venous access for medical treatment. There is no reason on the complaint\xe2\x80\x99s face to treat petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claim differently solely because he has been condemned to die. Respondents claim that because the cut-down is part of the execution procedure, petitioner is actually challenging the fact of his execution. However, that venous access is a necessary prerequisite to execution does not imply that a particular means of gaining such access is likewise necessary. Petitioner has argued throughout the proceedings that the cut-down and the warden\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to provide reliable information on the cut-down protocol are wholly unnecessary to gaining venous access. If, after an evidentiary hearing, the District Court finds the cut-down necessary, it will need to address the broader method-of-execution question left open here. The instant holding is consistent with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s approach to civil rights damages actions, which also fall at the margins of habeas. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0If a permanent injunction request does not sound in habeas, it follows that the lesser-included request for a temporary stay (or preliminary injunction) does not either. Here, a fair reading of the complaint leaves no doubt that petitioner sought to enjoin the cut-down, not his execution by lethal injection. However, his stay request asked to stay his execution, seemingly without regard to whether the State did or did not resort to the cut-down. The execution warrant has now expired. If the State reschedules the execution while this case is pending on remand and petitioner seeks another similarly broad stay, the District Court will need to address the question whether a request to enjoin the execution, rather than merely to enjoin an allegedly unnecessary precursor medical procedure, properly sounds in habeas. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Respondents are incorrect that a reversal here would open the floodgates to all manner of method-of-execution challenges and last-minute stay requests. Because this Court does not here resolve the question of how to treat method-of-execution claims generally, the instant holding is extremely limited. Moreover, merely stating a cognizable \xc2\xa71983 claim does not warrant a stay as a matter of right. A court may consider a stay application\xe2\x80\x99s last-minute nature in deciding whether to grant such equitable relief. And the ability to bring a \xc2\xa71983 claim does not free inmates from the substantive or procedural limitations of the Prison Litigation Reform Act of 1995. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

347 F.\xc2\xa03d 910, reversed and remanded.

DAVID L. NELSON, PETITIONER v. DONAL CAMPBELL,\nCOMMISSIONER, ALABAMA DEPARTMENT\nOF CORRECTIONS, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

[May 24, 2004]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice O\xe2\x80\x99Connor delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Three days before his scheduled execution by lethal injection, petitioner David Nelson filed a civil rights action in District Court, pursuant to 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983, alleging that the use of a \xe2\x80\x9ccut-down\xe2\x80\x9d procedure to access his veins would violate the Eighth Amendment. Petitioner, who had already filed one unsuccessful federal habeas application, sought a stay of execution so that the District Court could consider the merits of his constitutional claim. The question before us is whether \xc2\xa71983 is an appropriate vehicle for petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s Eighth Amendment claim seeking a temporary stay and permanent injunctive relief. We answer that question in the affirmative, reverse the contrary judgment of the Eleventh Circuit, and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because the District Court dismissed the suit at the pleading stage, we assume the allegations in petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s complaint to be true. Petitioner was found guilty by a jury in 1979 of capital murder and sentenced to death. Following two resentencings, the Eleventh Circuit, on June 3, 2002, affirmed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s denial of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s first federal habeas petition challenging the most recent death sentence. Nelson v. Alabama, 292 F.\xc2\xa03d 1291. Up until and at the time of that disposition, Alabama employed electrocution as its sole method of execution. On July 1, 2002, Alabama changed to lethal injection, though it still allowed inmates to opt for electrocution upon written notification within 30 days of the Alabama Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s entry of judgment or July 1, 2002, whichever is later. Ala. Code \xc2\xa715\xe2\x80\x9318\xe2\x80\x9382.1 (Lexis Supp. 2003). Because he failed to make a timely request, petitioner waived his option to be executed by electrocution.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court denied petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s request for certiorari review of the Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision on March 24, 2003. Nelson v. Alabama, 538 U. S. 926. Two weeks later, the Alabama Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s office moved the Alabama Supreme Court to set an execution date. App. 81. Petitioner responded by letter that he \xe2\x80\x9cha[d] no plans to contest [the] motion,\xe2\x80\x9d agreeing \xe2\x80\x9cthat an execution date should be set promptly by the court in the immediate future.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 89. Hearing no objection, the Alabama Supreme Court, on September 3, 2003, set petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s execution for October 9, 2003.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Due to years of drug abuse, petitioner has severely compromised peripheral veins, which are inaccessible by standard techniques for gaining intravenous access, such as a needle. Id., at 7. In August 2003, counsel for petitioner contacted Grantt Culliver, warden of Holman Correctional Facility where the execution was to take place, to discuss how petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s medical condition might impact the lethal injection procedure. Counsel specifically requested a copy of the State\xe2\x80\x99s written protocol for gaining venous access prior to execution, and asked that a privately retained or prison physician consult with petitioner about the procedure. Id., at 8\xe2\x80\x939, 25\xe2\x80\x9326. The warden advised counsel that the State had such a protocol, but stated that he could not provide it to her. He nevertheless assured counsel that \xe2\x80\x9cmedical personnel\xe2\x80\x9d would be present during the execution and that a prison physician would evaluate and speak with petitioner upon his arrival at Holman Correctional Facility. Id., at 8, 26.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner was transferred to Holman shortly after the Alabama Supreme Court set the execution date. Warden Culliver and a prison nurse met with and examined petitioner on September 10, 2003. Id., at 9\xe2\x80\x9310. Upon confirming that petitioner had compromised veins, Warden Culliver informed petitioner that prison personnel would cut a 0.5-inch incision in petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s arm and catheterize a vein 24 hours before the scheduled execution. Id., at 11. At a second meeting on Friday, October 3, 2003, the warden dramatically altered the prognosis: prison personnel would now make a 2-inch incision in petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s arm or leg; the procedure would take place one hour before the scheduled execution; and only local anesthesia would be used. Id., at 12. There was no assurance that a physician would perform or even be present for the procedure. Counsel immediately contacted the Alabama Department of Corrections Legal Department requesting a copy of the State\xe2\x80\x99s execution protocol. Id., at 13, 27. The legal department denied counsel\xe2\x80\x99s request. Id., at 28.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The following Monday, three days before his scheduled execution, petitioner filed the present \xc2\xa71983 action alleging that the so-called \xe2\x80\x9ccut-down\xe2\x80\x9d procedure constituted cruel and unusual punishment and deliberate indifference to his serious medical needs in violation of the Eighth Amendment. Id., at 21 (complaint), 102 (amended complaint). Petitioner sought: a permanent injunction against use of the cut-down; a temporary stay of execution to allow the District Court to consider the merits of his claim; an order requiring respondents to furnish a copy of the protocol setting forth the medical procedures to be used to gain venous access; and an order directing respondents, in consultation with medical experts, to promulgate a venous access protocol that comports with contemporary standards of medical care. Id., at 22. Appended to the complaint was an affidavit from Dr. Mark Heath, a board certified anesthesiologist and assistant professor at Columbia University College of Physicians and Surgeons, attesting that the cut-down is a dangerous and antiquated medical procedure to be performed only by a trained physician in a clinical environment with the patient under deep sedation. In light of safer and less-invasive contemporary means of venous access, Dr. Heath concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthere is no comprehensible reason for the State of Alabama to be planning to employ the cut-down procedure to obtain intravenous access, unless there exists an intent to render the procedure more painful and risky than it otherwise needs to be.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 37.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents moved to dismiss the complaint for want of jurisdiction on the grounds that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s \xc2\xa71983 claim and accompanying stay request were the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98functional equivalent\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of a second or successive habeas application subject to the gatekeeping provisions of 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72244(b). App. 82. The District Court agreed and, because petitioner had not obtained authorization to file a second or successive application as required by \xc2\xa72244(b)(3), dismissed the complaint. A divided panel of the Eleventh Circuit affirmed. Relying on Fugate v. Department of Corrections, 301 F.\xc2\xa03d 1287 (CA11 2002), in which the Eleventh Circuit had held that \xc2\xa71983 claims challenging the method of execution necessarily sound in habeas, the majority held that petitioner should have sought authorization to file a second or successive habeas application. 347 F.\xc2\xa03d 910, 912 (2003). The majority also concluded that, even were it to construe petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s appeal as a request for such authorization, it would nevertheless deny the request because petitioner could not show that, but for the purported Eighth Amendment violation, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98no reasonable factfinder would have found [him] guilty of the underlying offense.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72244(b)(2)(B)(ii)). Thus, the Eleventh Circuit held that petitioner was without recourse to challenge the constitutionality of the cut-down procedure in Federal District Court. We granted certiorari, 540 U. S. 1046 (2003), and now reverse.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 1983 authorizes a \xe2\x80\x9csuit in equity, or other proper proceeding for redress\xe2\x80\x9d against any person who, under color of state law, \xe2\x80\x9csubjects, or causes to be subjected, any citizen of the United States . . . to the deprivation of any rights, privileges, or immunities secured by the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s complaint states such a claim. Despite its literal applicability, however, \xc2\xa71983 must yield to the more specific federal habeas statute, with its attendant procedural and exhaustion requirements, where an inmate seeks injunctive relief challenging the fact of his conviction or the duration of his sentence. See Preiser v. Rodriguez, 411 U. S. 475, 489 (1973). Such claims fall within the \xe2\x80\x9ccore\xe2\x80\x9d of habeas corpus and are thus not cognizable when brought pursuant to \xc2\xa71983. Ibid. By contrast, constitutional claims that merely challenge the conditions of a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s confinement, whether the inmate seeks monetary or injunctive relief, fall outside of that core and may be brought pursuant to \xc2\xa71983 in the first instance. See Muhammad v. Close, 540 U. S. _____, _____ (2004) (per curiam) (slip op., at 1\xe2\x80\x932); Preiser, supra, at 498\xe2\x80\x93499.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have not yet had occasion to consider whether civil rights suits seeking to enjoin the use of a particular method of execution\xe2\x80\x94e.g., lethal injection or electrocution\xe2\x80\x94fall within the core of federal habeas corpus or, rather, whether they are properly viewed as challenges to the conditions of a condemned inmate\xe2\x80\x99s death sentence. Neither the \xe2\x80\x9cconditions\xe2\x80\x9d nor the \xe2\x80\x9cfact or duration\xe2\x80\x9d label is particularly apt. A suit seeking to enjoin a particular means of effectuating a sentence of death does not directly call into question the \xe2\x80\x9cfact\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cvalidity\xe2\x80\x9d of the sentence itself\xe2\x80\x94by simply altering its method of execution, the State can go forward with the sentence. Cf. Weaver v. Graham, 450 U. S. 24, 32\xe2\x80\x9333, n.\xc2\xa017 (1981) (no ex\xc2\xa0post facto violation to change method of execution to more humane method). On the other hand, imposition of the death penalty presupposes a means of carrying it out. In a State such as Alabama, where the legislature has established lethal injection as the preferred method of execution, see Ala. Code \xc2\xa715\xe2\x80\x9318\xe2\x80\x9382 (Lexis Supp. 2003) (lethal injection as default method), a constitutional challenge seeking to permanently enjoin the use of lethal injection may amount to a challenge to the fact of the sentence itself. A finding of unconstitutionality would require statutory amendment or variance, imposing significant costs on the State and the administration of its penal system. And while it makes little sense to talk of the \xe2\x80\x9cduration\xe2\x80\x9d of a death sentence, a State retains a significant interest in meting out a sentence of death in a timely fashion. See Calderon v. Thompson, 523 U. S. 538, 556\xe2\x80\x93557 (1998); In\xc2\xa0re Blodgett, 502 U. S. 236, 238 (1992) (per curiam); McCleskey v. Zant, 499 U. S. 467, 491 (1991) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he power of a State to pass laws means little if the State cannot enforce them\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We need not reach here the difficult question of how to categorize method-of-execution claims generally. Respondents at oral argument conceded that \xc2\xa71983 would be an appropriate vehicle for an inmate who is not facing execution to bring a \xe2\x80\x9cdeliberate indifference\xe2\x80\x9d challenge to the constitutionality of the cut-down procedure if used to gain venous access for purposes of providing medical treatment. Tr. of Oral Arg. 40 (\xe2\x80\x9cI don\xe2\x80\x99t disagree . . . that a cut-down occurring for purposes of venous access, wholly divorced from an execution, is indeed a valid conditions of confinement claim\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Estelle v. Gamble, 429 U. S. 97, 104 (1976) (\xe2\x80\x9cWe therefore conclude that deliberate indifference to serious medical needs of prisoners constitutes the \xe2\x80\x98unnecessary and wanton infliction of pain\xe2\x80\x99 proscribed by the Eighth Amendment\xe2\x80\x9d (citation omitted)). We see no reason on the face of the complaint to treat petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claim differently solely because he has been condemned to die.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents counter that, because the cut-down is part of the execution procedure, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s challenge is, in fact, a challenge to the fact of his execution. They offer the following argument: A challenge to the use of lethal injection as a method of execution sounds in habeas; venous access is a necessary prerequisite to, and thus an indispensable part of, any lethal injection procedure; therefore, a challenge to the State\xe2\x80\x99s means of achieving venous access must be brought in a federal habeas application. Even were we to accept as given respondents\xe2\x80\x99 premise that a challenge to lethal injection sounds in habeas, the conclusion does not follow. That venous access is a necessary prerequisite does not imply that a particular means of gaining such access is likewise necessary. Indeed, the gravamen of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s entire claim is that use of the cut-down would be gratuitous. Merely labeling something as part of an execution procedure is insufficient to insulate it from a \xc2\xa71983 attack.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If as a legal matter the cut-down were a statutorily mandated part of the lethal injection protocol, or if as a factual matter petitioner were unable or unwilling to concede acceptable alternatives for gaining venous access, respondents might have a stronger argument that success on the merits, coupled with injunctive relief, would call into question the death sentence itself. But petitioner has been careful throughout these proceedings, in his complaint and at oral argument, to assert that the cut-down, as well as the warden\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to provide reliable information regarding the cut-down protocol, are wholly unnecessary to gaining venous access. Petitioner has alleged alternatives that, if they had been used, would have allowed the State to proceed with the execution as scheduled. App. 17 (complaint) (proffering as \xe2\x80\x9cless invasive, less painful, faster, cheaper, and safer\xe2\x80\x9d the alternative procedure of \xe2\x80\x9cpercutaneous central line placement\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 37\xe2\x80\x9338 (affidavit of Dr. Mark Heath) (describing relative merits of the cut-down and percutaneous central line placement). No Alabama statute requires use of the cut-down, see Ala. Code \xc2\xa715\xe2\x80\x9318\xe2\x80\x9382 (Lexis Supp. 2003) (saying only that method of execution is lethal injection), and respondents have offered no duly-promulgated regulations to the contrary.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If on remand and after an evidentiary hearing the District Court concludes that use of the cut-down procedure as described in the complaint is necessary for administering the lethal injection, the District Court will need to address the broader question, left open here, of how to treat method-of-execution claims generally. An evidentiary hearing will in all likelihood be unnecessary, however, as the State now seems willing to implement petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s proposed alternatives. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 45\xe2\x80\x9346 (\xe2\x80\x9cI think there is no disagreement here that percutaneous central line placement is the preferred method and will,\nin fact, be used, a cut-down to be used only if actually necessary\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We note that our holding here is consistent with our approach to civil rights damages actions, which, like method-of-execution challenges, fall at the margins of habeas. Although damages are not an available habeas remedy, we have previously concluded that a \xc2\xa71983 suit for damages that would \xe2\x80\x9cnecessarily imply\xe2\x80\x9d the invalidity of the fact of an inmate\xe2\x80\x99s conviction, or \xe2\x80\x9cnecessarily imply\xe2\x80\x9d the invalidity of the length of an inmate\xe2\x80\x99s sentence, is not cognizable under \xc2\xa71983 unless and until the inmate obtains favorable termination of a state, or federal habeas, challenge to his conviction or sentence. Heck v. Humphrey, 512 U. S. 477, 487 (1994); Edwards v. Balisok, 520 U. S. 641, 648 (1997). This \xe2\x80\x9cfavorable termination\xe2\x80\x9d requirement is necessary to prevent inmates from doing indirectly through damages actions what they could not do directly by seeking injunctive relief\xe2\x80\x94challenge the fact or duration of their confinement without complying with the procedural limitations of the federal habeas statute. Muhammad, 540 U. S., at _____ (slip op., at 2). Even so, we were careful in Heck to stress the importance of the term \xe2\x80\x9cnecessarily.\xe2\x80\x9d For instance, we acknowledged that an inmate could bring a challenge to the lawfulness of a search pursuant to \xc2\xa71983 in the first instance, even if the search revealed evidence used to convict the inmate at trial, because success on the merits would not \xe2\x80\x9cnecessarily imply that the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s conviction was unlawful.\xe2\x80\x9d 512 U. S., at 487, n.\xc2\xa07 (noting doctrines such as inevitable discovery, independent source, and harmless error). To hold otherwise would have cut off potentially valid damages actions as to which a plaintiff might never obtain favorable termination\xe2\x80\x94suits that could otherwise have gone forward had the plaintiff not been convicted. In the present context, focusing attention on whether petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s challenge to the cut-down procedure would necessarily prevent Alabama from carrying out its execution both protects against the use of \xc2\xa71983 to circumvent any limits imposed by the habeas statute and minimizes the extent to which the fact of a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s imminent execution will require differential treatment of his otherwise cognizable \xc2\xa71983 claims.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There remains the question whether petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s request for a temporary stay of execution, subsequently recharacterized by petitioner as a request for a preliminary injunction, App. 49, transformed his conditions of confinement claim into a challenge to the validity of his death sentence. Normally, it would not. If a request for a permanent injunction does not sound in habeas, it follows that the lesser-included request for a temporary stay (or preliminary injunction) does not either.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is a complication in the present case, however. In his prayer for relief, petitioner asked the District Court, among other things, to \xe2\x80\x9c[e]nter an order granting injunctive relief and staying [petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s] execution, which is currently scheduled for October 9, 2003.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 22. Though he did not specify what permanent injunctive relief he was seeking, a fair reading of the complaint leaves no doubt that petitioner was asking only to enjoin the State\xe2\x80\x99s use of the cut-down, not his execution by lethal injection. The same cannot be said of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s stay request. There, he explicitly requested that the District Court stay his execution, seemingly without regard to whether the State did or did not resort to the cut-down. This observation is potentially significant given the fact that the State has maintained, from the outset of this litigation, that it would attempt other methods of venous access prior to engaging in the cut-down. See id., at 51\xe2\x80\x9352; id., at 93\xe2\x80\x9394 (affidavit of Warden Culliver). By asking for broader relief than necessary, petitioner undermines his assertions that: (1) his \xc2\xa71983 suit is not a tactic for delay, and (2) he is not challenging the fact of his execution, but merely a dispensable preliminary procedure.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Whatever problem this failing might have caused before this Court entered a stay, the execution warrant has now expired. If the State reschedules the execution while this case is pending on remand and petitioner seeks another similarly broad stay, the District Court will need to address the question whether a request to enjoin the execution, rather than merely to enjoin an allegedly unnecessary precursor medical procedure, properly sounds in habeas. See also 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73626(a)(2) (\xe2\x80\x9cPreliminary injunctive relief [in prison conditions cases] must be narrowly drawn, extend no further than necessary to correct the harm the court finds requires preliminary relief, and be the least intrusive means necessary to correct that harm\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

C

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents argue that a decision to reverse the judgment of the Eleventh Circuit would open the floodgates to all manner of method-of-execution challenges, as well as last minute stay requests. But, because we do not here resolve the question of how to treat method-of-execution claims generally, our holding is extremely limited.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, as our previous decision in Gomez v. United States Dist. Court for Northern Dist. of Cal., 503 U. S. 653 (1992) (per curiam), makes clear, the mere fact that an inmate states a cognizable \xc2\xa71983 claim does not warrant the entry of a stay as a matter of right. Gomez came to us on a motion by the State to vacate a stay entered by an en banc panel of the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit that would have allowed the District Court time to consider the merits of a condemned inmate\xe2\x80\x99s last-minute \xc2\xa71983 action challenging the constitutionality of California\xe2\x80\x99s use of the gas chamber. We left open the question whether the inmate\xe2\x80\x99s claim was cognizable under \xc2\xa71983, but vacated the stay nonetheless. The inmate, Robert Alton Harris, who had already filed four unsuccessful federal habeas applications, waited until the 11th hour to file his challenge despite the fact that California\xe2\x80\x99s method of execution had been in place for years: \xe2\x80\x9cThis claim could have been brought more than a decade ago. There is no good reason for this abusive delay, which has been compounded by last-minute attempts to manipulate the judicial process. A court may consider the last-minute nature of an application to stay execution in deciding whether to grant equitable relief.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 654.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A stay is an equitable remedy, and \xe2\x80\x9c[e]quity must take into consideration the State\xe2\x80\x99s strong interest in proceeding with its judgment and . . . attempt[s] at manipulation.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Thus, before granting a stay, a district court must consider not only the likelihood of success on the merits and the relative harms to the parties, but also the extent to which the inmate has delayed unnecessarily in bringing the claim. Given the State\xe2\x80\x99s significant interest in enforcing its criminal judgments, see Blodgett, 502 U. S., at 239; McCleskey, 499 U. S., at 491, there is a strong equitable presumption against the grant of a stay where a claim could have been brought at such a time as to allow consideration of the merits without requiring entry of a stay.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, the ability to bring a \xc2\xa71983 claim, rather than a habeas application, does not entirely free inmates from substantive or procedural limitations. The Prison Litigation Reform Act of 1995 (Act) imposes limits on the scope and duration of preliminary and permanent injunctive relief, including a requirement that, before issuing such relief, \xe2\x80\x9c[a] court shall give substantial weight to any adverse impact on \xe2\x80\xa6 the operation of a criminal justice system caused by the relief.\xe2\x80\x9d 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73626(a)(1); accord, \xc2\xa73626(a)(2). It requires that inmates exhaust available state administrative remedies before bringing a \xc2\xa71983 action challenging the conditions of their confinement. 110 Stat. 1321\xe2\x80\x9371, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71997e(a) (\xe2\x80\x9cNo action shall be brought with respect to prison conditions under section 1983 of this title, or any other Federal law, by a prisoner confined in any jail, prison, or other correctional facility until such administrative remedies as are available are exhausted\xe2\x80\x9d). The Act mandates that a district court \xe2\x80\x9cshall,\xe2\x80\x9d on its own motion, dismiss \xe2\x80\x9cany action brought with respect to prison conditions under section 1983 of this title \xe2\x80\xa6 if the court is satisfied that the action is frivolous, malicious, fails to state a claim upon which relief can be granted, or seeks monetary relief from a defendant who is immune from relief.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71997e(c)(1). Indeed, if the claim is frivolous on its face, a district court may dismiss the suit before the plaintiff has exhausted his state remedies. \xc2\xa71997e(c)(2).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For the reasons stated herein, the judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

It is so ordered.
