\nBURGESS V. UNITED STATES
\n

\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

BURGESS v. UNITED STATES

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fourth circuit

\n

No. 06\xe2\x80\x9311429.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 24, 2008\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 16, 2008

\n

The Controlled Substances Act (CSA) doubles the mandatory minimum sentence for certain federal drug crimes if the defendant was previously convicted of a \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense.\xe2\x80\x9d 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(A). Section 802(13) defines the unadorned term \xe2\x80\x9cfelony\xe2\x80\x9d to mean any \xe2\x80\x9coffense classified by applicable Federal or State law as a felony,\xe2\x80\x9d while \xc2\xa7802(44) defines the compound term \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9cmea[n] an offense [involving specified drugs] that is punishable by imprisonment for more than one year under any law of the United States or of a State or foreign country.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner Burgess pleaded guilty in federal court to conspiracy to possess with intent to distribute 50 grams or more of cocaine base, an offense that ordinarily carries a 10-year mandatory minimum sentence. Burgess had a prior South Carolina cocaine possession conviction, which carried a maximum sentence of two years but was classified as a misdemeanor under state law. The Federal Government argued that Burgess\xe2\x80\x99 minimum federal sentence should be enhanced to 20 years under \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(A) because his South Carolina conviction was punishable by more than one year\xe2\x80\x99s imprisonment. Burgess countered that because \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d incorporates the term \xe2\x80\x9cfelony,\xe2\x80\x9d a word separately defined in \xc2\xa7802(13), a prior drug offense does not warrant an enhanced \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(A) sentence unless it is both (1) classified as a felony under the law of the punishing jurisdiction, per \xc2\xa7802(13); and (2) punishable by more than one year\xe2\x80\x99s imprisonment, per \xc2\xa7802(44). Rejecting that argument, the District Court ruled that \xc2\xa7802(44) alone controls the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(A). The Fourth Circuit affirmed.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Because the term \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(A) is defined exclusively by \xc2\xa7802(44) and does not incorporate \xc2\xa7802(13)\xe2\x80\x99s definition of \xe2\x80\x9cfelony,\xe2\x80\x9d a state drug offense punishable by more than one year qualifies as a \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense,\xe2\x80\x9d even if state law classifies the offense as a misdemeanor. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The CSA\xe2\x80\x99s language and structure indicate that Congress used \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d as a term of art defined by \xc2\xa7802(44) without reference to \xc2\xa7802(13). First, a definition such as \xc2\xa7802(44)\xe2\x80\x99s that declares what a term \xe2\x80\x9cmeans\xe2\x80\x9d generally excludes any meaning that is not stated. E.g., Colautti v. Franklin, 439 U. S. 379, 392\xe2\x80\x93393, n.\xc2\xa010. Second, because \xe2\x80\x9cfelony\xe2\x80\x9d is commonly defined to mean a crime punishable by imprisonment for more than one year, see, e.g., 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73559(a), \xc2\xa7802(44)\xe2\x80\x99s definition of \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9can offense \xe2\x80\xa6 punishable by imprisonment for more than one year\xe2\x80\x9d leaves no blank for \xc2\xa7802(13) to fill. Third, if Congress wanted \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d to incorporate \xc2\xa7802(13)\xe2\x80\x99s definition of \xe2\x80\x9cfelony,\xe2\x80\x9d it easily could have written \xc2\xa7802(44) to state: \xe2\x80\x9cThe term \xe2\x80\x98felony drug offense\xe2\x80\x99 means a felony that is punishable by imprisonment for more than one year \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Fourth, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reading avoids anomalies that would arise if both \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7802(13) and 802(44) governed application of \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(A)\xe2\x80\x99s sentencing enhancement. Section 802(13) includes only federal and state offenses and would exclude enhancement based on a foreign offense, notwithstanding the express inclusion of foreign offenses in \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(A). Furthermore, Burgess\xe2\x80\x99 compound definition of \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d leaves unanswered the appropriate classification of drug convictions in state and foreign jurisdictions that do not label offenses as felonies or misdemeanors. Finally, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reading of \xc2\xa7802(44) hardly renders \xc2\xa7802(13) extraneous; the latter section serves to define \xe2\x80\x9cfelony\xe2\x80\x9d for the many CSA provisions using that unadorned term. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The CSA\xe2\x80\x99s drafting history reinforces the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reading. In 1988, Congress first defined \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d as, inter alia, \xe2\x80\x9can offense that is a felony under \xe2\x80\xa6 any law of a State\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added), but, in 1994, it amended the statutory definition to its present form. By recognizing \xc2\xa7802(44) as the exclusive definition of \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reading serves an evident purpose of the 1994 revision: to eliminate disparities resulting from divergent state classifications of offenses by adopting a uniform federal standard based on the authorized term of imprisonment. By contrast, Burgess\xe2\x80\x99 reading of the 1994 alteration as merely adding a length-of-imprisonment requirement to a definition already requiring designation of an offense as a felony by the punishing jurisdiction would attribute to the amendment little practical effect and encounters formidable impediments: the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text and history. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Burgess\xe2\x80\x99 argument that the rule of lenity should be applied in determining whether \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d incorporates \xc2\xa7802(13)\xe2\x80\x99s definition of \xe2\x80\x9cfelony\xe2\x80\x9d is rejected. The touchstone of the rule of lenity is statutory ambiguity. E.g., Bifulco v. United States, 447 U. S. 381, 387. Because Congress expressly defined \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d in a manner that is coherent, complete, and by all signs exclusive, there is no ambiguity for the rule of lenity to resolve here. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

478 F.\xc2\xa03d 658, affirmed.

\n

KEITH LAVON BURGESS, PETITIONER v. UNITED\nSTATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fourth circuit

\n

[April 16, 2008]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For certain federal drug offenses, the Controlled Substances Act mandates a minimum sentence of imprisonment for 10 years. 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(A). That minimum doubles to 20 years for defendants previously convicted of a \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The question in this case is whether a state drug offense classified as a misdemeanor, but punishable by more than one year\xe2\x80\x99s imprisonment, is a \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d as that term is used in \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(A).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Two statutory definitions figure in our decision. Section 802(13) defines the unadorned term \xe2\x80\x9cfelony\xe2\x80\x9d to mean any \xe2\x80\x9coffense classified by applicable Federal or State law as a felony.\xe2\x80\x9d Section 802(44) defines the compound term \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d to mean an offense involving specified drugs that is \xe2\x80\x9cpunishable by imprisonment for more than one year under any law of the United States or of a State or foreign country.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The term \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d contained in \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(A)\xe2\x80\x99s provision for a 20-year minimum sentence, we hold, is defined exclusively by \xc2\xa7802(44) and does not incorporate \xc2\xa7802(13)\xe2\x80\x99s definition of \xe2\x80\x9cfelony.\xe2\x80\x9d A state drug offense punishable by more than one year therefore qualifies as a \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense,\xe2\x80\x9d even if state law classifies the offense as a misdemeanor.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner Keith Lavon Burgess pleaded guilty in the United States District Court for the District of South Carolina to conspiracy to possess with intent to distribute 50 grams or more of cocaine base in violation of 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7841(a) and 846.[Footnote 1] A violation of \xc2\xa7841(a) involving that quantity of cocaine base ordinarily carries a mandatory minimum sentence of 10 years. \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(A). The minimum sentence increases to 20 years, however, if the crime follows a prior conviction for a \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Burgess had previously been convicted of possessing cocaine in violation of S.\xc2\xa0C. Code Ann. \xc2\xa744\xe2\x80\x9353\xe2\x80\x93370(c) and (d)(1) (2002 and Supp. 2007). Although that offense carried a maximum sentence of two years\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment, South Carolina classified it as a misdemeanor. \xc2\xa744\xe2\x80\x9353\xe2\x80\x93370(d)(1). Burgess\xe2\x80\x99 prior South Carolina conviction, the Government urged, raised the minimum sentence for his federal conviction to 20 years. The enhancement was mandatory, the Government maintained, because Congress defined \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d to include state cocaine offenses \xe2\x80\x9cpunishable by imprisonment for more than one year.\xe2\x80\x9d 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7802(44).[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Burgess contested the enhancement of his federal sentence. The term \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense,\xe2\x80\x9d he argued, incorporates the term \xe2\x80\x9cfelony,\xe2\x80\x9d a word separately defined in \xc2\xa7802(13) to mean \xe2\x80\x9cany Federal or State offense classified by applicable Federal or State law as a felony.\xe2\x80\x9d A prior drug offense does not rank as a \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense,\xe2\x80\x9d he contended, unless it is (1) classified as a felony under the law of the punishing jurisdiction, per \xc2\xa7802(13); and (2) punishable by more than one year\xe2\x80\x99s imprisonment, per \xc2\xa7802(44).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Rejecting Burgess\xe2\x80\x99 argument, the District Court ruled that \xc2\xa7802(44) alone controls the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d as that term is used in \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(A). Although the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling subjected Burgess to a 20-year minimum sentence, the Government moved for a downward departure based on Burgess\xe2\x80\x99 substantial assistance in another prosecution. See 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73553(e) (2000 ed., Supp. V). The court granted the motion and sentenced Burgess to 156 months\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment followed by ten years\xe2\x80\x99 supervised release.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The United States Court of Appeals for the Fourth Circuit affirmed. The \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98commonsense way to interpret \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense,\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d that court said, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98is by reference to the definition in \xc2\xa7802(44).\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 478 F.\xc2\xa03d 658, 662 (2007) (quoting United States v. Roberson, 459 F.\xc2\xa03d 39, 52 (CA1 2006)). The Fourth Circuit found nothing in the \xe2\x80\x9cplain language or statutory scheme \xe2\x80\xa6 to indicate that Congress intended \xe2\x80\x98felony drug offense\xe2\x80\x99 also to incorporate the definition [of \xe2\x80\x98felony\xe2\x80\x99] in \xc2\xa7802(13).\xe2\x80\x9d 478 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 662.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Burgess, proceeding pro se, petitioned for a writ of certiorari. We granted the writ, 552 U. S. ___ (2007), to resolve a split among the Circuits on the question Burgess presents: Does a drug crime classified as a misdemeanor by state law, but punishable by more than one year\xe2\x80\x99s imprisonment, rank as a \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d under 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(A)? Compare 478 F.\xc2\xa03d 658 (case below), and Roberson, 459 F.\xc2\xa03d 39 (\xc2\xa7802(44) provides exclusive definition of \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d), with United States v. West, 393 F.\xc2\xa03d 1302 (CADC 2005) (both \xc2\xa7802(13) and \xc2\xa7802(44) limit meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Controlled Substances Act (CSA), 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7801 et seq., contains two definitions central to the dispute before us; they bear repetition in full. Section 802(13) provides:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe term \xe2\x80\x98felony\xe2\x80\x99 means any Federal or State offense classified by applicable Federal or State law as a felony.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Section 802(44) states:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe term \xe2\x80\x98felony drug offense\xe2\x80\x99 means an offense that is punishable by imprisonment for more than one year under any law of the United States or of a State or foreign country that prohibits or restricts conduct relating to narcotic drugs, marihuana, anabolic steroids, or depressant or stimulant substances.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Burgess argues here, as he did below, that \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense,\xe2\x80\x9d as used in \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(A), should be construed to incorporate both the definition of \xe2\x80\x9cfelony\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa7802(13) and the definition of \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa7802(44). Under his reading, the \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(A) enhancement is triggered only when the prior conviction is both \xe2\x80\x9cclassified by applicable Federal or State law as a felony,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7802(13), and \xe2\x80\x9cpunishable by imprisonment for more than one year,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7802(44).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government, in contrast, reads \xc2\xa7802(44) to provide the exclusive definition of \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense.\xe2\x80\x9d Under the Government\xe2\x80\x99s reading, all defendants whose prior drug crimes were punishable by more than one year in prison would be subject to the \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(A) enhancement, regardless of the punishing jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x99s classification of the offense.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government\xe2\x80\x99s reading, we are convinced, correctly interprets the statutory text and context. Section 802(44) defines the precise phrase used in \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(A)\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense.\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cStatutory definitions control the meaning of statutory words \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0in the usual case.\xe2\x80\x9d Lawson v. Suwannee Fruit & S. S. Co., 336 U. S. 198, 201 (1949). See also Stenberg v. Carhart, 530 U. S. 914, 942 (2000) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhen a statute includes an explicit definition, we must follow that definition \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d); 2A N. Singer & J. Singer, Statutes and Statutory Construction \xc2\xa747:7, pp. 298\xe2\x80\x93299, and nn.\xc2\xa02\xe2\x80\x933 (7th ed. 2007) (hereinafter Singer).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The CSA, to be sure, also defines the term \xe2\x80\x9cfelony.\xe2\x80\x9d The language and structure of the statute, however, indicate that Congress used the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d as a term of art defined by \xc2\xa7802(44) without reference to \xc2\xa7802(13). First, Congress stated that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he term \xe2\x80\x98felony drug offense\xe2\x80\x99 means an offense that is punishable by imprisonment for more than one year.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7802(44) (emphasis added). \xe2\x80\x9cAs a rule, [a] definition which declares what a term \xe2\x80\x98means\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\xa6 excludes any meaning that is not stated.\xe2\x80\x9d Colautti v. Franklin, 439 U. S. 379, 392\xe2\x80\x93393, n.\xc2\xa010 (1979) (some internal quotation marks omitted). See also Groman v. Commissioner, 302 U. S. 82, 86 (1937); 2A Singer \xc2\xa747:7, p. 306, and n.\xc2\xa020.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, the term \xe2\x80\x9cfelony\xe2\x80\x9d is commonly defined to mean a crime punishable by imprisonment for more than one year. See, e.g., 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73559(a) (classifying crimes with a maximum term of more than one year as felonies); Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 651 (8th ed. 2004) (defining \xe2\x80\x9cfelony\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9c[a] serious crime usu[ally] punishable by imprisonment for more than one year or by death\xe2\x80\x9d). Section 802(44)\xe2\x80\x99s definition of \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9can offense \xe2\x80\xa6 punishable by imprisonment for more than one year,\xe2\x80\x9d in short, leaves no blank to be filled by \xc2\xa7802(13) or any other definition of \xe2\x80\x9cfelony.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Third, if Congress wanted \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d to incorporate the definition of \xe2\x80\x9cfelony\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa7802(13), it easily could have written \xc2\xa7802(44) to state: \xe2\x80\x9cThe term \xe2\x80\x98felony drug offense\xe2\x80\x99 means a felony that is punishable by imprisonment for more than one year \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d See Roberson, 459 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 52. Congress has often used that drafting technique\xe2\x80\x94i.e., repeating a discretely defined word\xe2\x80\x94when it intends to incorporate the definition of a particular word into the definition of a compound expression. See, e.g., 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71672(a)\xe2\x80\x93(b) (defining \xe2\x80\x9cearnings\xe2\x80\x9d and then defining \xe2\x80\x9cdisposable earnings\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cthat part of the earnings\xe2\x80\x9d meeting certain criteria); 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71956(c)(3)\xe2\x80\x93(4) (defining \xe2\x80\x9ctransaction\xe2\x80\x9d and then defining \xe2\x80\x9cfinancial transaction\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9ca transaction which\xe2\x80\x9d meets other criteria); \xc2\xa71961(1), (5) (2000 ed. and Supp. V) (defining \xe2\x80\x9cracketeering activity\xe2\x80\x9d and then defining \xe2\x80\x9cpattern of racketeering activity\xe2\x80\x9d to require \xe2\x80\x9cat least two acts of racketeering activity\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Fourth, our reading avoids anomalies that would arise if both 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7802(13) and \xc2\xa7802(44) governed application of the sentencing enhancement in \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(A). Notably, \xc2\xa7802(44) includes foreign offenses punishable by more than one year, while \xc2\xa7802(13) includes only federal and state offenses. Incorporation of \xc2\xa7802(13) into \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(A) would exclude enhancement based on a foreign offense, notwithstanding the express inclusion of foreign offenses in \xc2\xa7802(44)\xe2\x80\x99s definition of \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense.\xe2\x80\x9d Furthermore, some States and many foreign jurisdictions do not label offenses as felonies or misdemeanors. See N.\xc2\xa0J. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa72C:1\xe2\x80\x934 (West 2005); Me. Rev. Stat. Ann., Tit. 17\xe2\x80\x93A, \xc2\xa71252 (Supp. 2007); Brief for United States 35. Burgess\xe2\x80\x99 compound definition of \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d leaves unanswered the appropriate classification of drug convictions in those jurisdictions. See, e.g., United States v. Brown, 937 F.\xc2\xa02d 68, 70 (CA2 1991) (relying on New Jersey common law to determine that the State classifies offenses punishable by more than one year as felonies). No such uncertainty arises under the precise definition Congress provided in \xc2\xa7802(44).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, reading \xc2\xa7802(44) as the exclusive definition of \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d hardly renders \xc2\xa7802(13) extraneous. Section 802(13) serves to define \xe2\x80\x9cfelony\xe2\x80\x9d for many CSA provisions using that unadorned term. See, e.g., \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7824(a)(2) (revocation of license to manufacture controlled substances upon conviction of a felony), 843(b) (use of a communication facility to commit a felony), 843(d)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(2) (sentencing enhancements), 843(e) (prohibition on engaging in transactions involving listed chemicals upon conviction of a felony involving those chemicals), 848(c)(1) (definition of \xe2\x80\x9ccontinuing criminal enterprise\xe2\x80\x9d), 848(e)(1)(B) (mandatory minimum term for killing a law enforcement officer to avoid prosecution for a felony), 853(d) (rebuttable presumption that property acquired during commission of certain felonies is subject to criminal forfeiture), 878(a)(3) (authority to make warrantless arrest where there is probable cause to believe a felony has been committed).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The drafting history of the CSA reinforces our reading of \xc2\xa7802(44) as the exclusive definition of \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense.\xe2\x80\x9d In 1988, Congress first used the term \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d to describe the type of prior conviction that would trigger a 20-year mandatory minimum sentence under \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(A). See National Narcotics Leadership Act, Pub. L. 100\xe2\x80\x93690, \xc2\xa76452(a), 102 Stat. 4371. The 1988 definition of the term was placed within \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(A) itself; the definition covered \xe2\x80\x9can offense that is a felony under any \xe2\x80\xa6 Federal law \xe2\x80\xa6 or \xe2\x80\xa6 any law of a State or a foreign country\xe2\x80\x9d prohibiting or restricting conduct relating to certain types of drugs. \xc2\xa76452(a)(2), ibid.[Footnote 4] But in 1994, Congress amended the definition, replacing \xe2\x80\x9can offense that is a felony under \xe2\x80\xa6 any law of a State,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. (emphasis added), with \xe2\x80\x9can offense that is punishable by imprisonment for more than one year under any law \xe2\x80\xa6 of a State,\xe2\x80\x9d Violent Crime Control and Law Enforcement Act, Pub. L. 103\xe2\x80\x93322, \xc2\xa790105(c)\xe2\x80\x93(d), 108 Stat. 1988 (emphasis added). In lieu of incorporation within \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(A), the new definition was placed in a discrete \xc2\xa7802 definition section. Ibid.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This alteration lends considerable support to our reading of the statute. Before 1994, the definition of \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d depended on the vagaries of state-law classifications of offenses as felonies or misdemeanors. The 1994 amendments replaced that definition with a uniform federal standard based on the authorized length of imprisonment. By recognizing \xc2\xa7802(44) as the exclusive definition of \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense,\xe2\x80\x9d our reading serves an evident purpose of the 1994 revision: to bring a measure of uniformity to the application of \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(A) by eliminating disparities based on divergent state classifications of offenses.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0By contrast, Burgess reads the 1994 alteration as merely adding a length-of-imprisonment requirement to a definition that already required\xe2\x80\x94and, he contends, continues to require\xe2\x80\x94designation of an offense as a felony by the punishing jurisdiction. That view, however, is difficult to square with Congress\xe2\x80\x99 deletion of the word \xe2\x80\x9cfelony\xe2\x80\x9d and substitution of the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cpunishable by imprisonment for more than one year.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If Burgess were correct, moreover, the sole effect of the 1994 change would have been to exclude from the compass of \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(A) the few drug offenses classified as felonies under the law of the punishing jurisdiction but subject to a sentence of one year or less. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 6\xe2\x80\x938.[Footnote 5] See also Brief for Petitioner 15 (purpose of 1994 alteration was to eliminate enhancement for \xe2\x80\x9ctruly minor offenses\xe2\x80\x9d nonetheless classified as felonies). Burgess concedes that under his reading of the statute \xe2\x80\x9cthe language that Congress added [in 1994] has very little practical effect,\xe2\x80\x9d but defends his interpretation on the ground that Congress labeled the changes \xe2\x80\x9cconforming amendments.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 8. See also 108 Stat. 1987; Brief for Petitioner 12.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Burgess places more weight on the \xe2\x80\x9cConforming Amendments\xe2\x80\x9d caption than it can bear. Congress did not disavow any intent to make substantive changes; rather, the amendments were \xe2\x80\x9cconforming\xe2\x80\x9d because they harmonized sentencing provisions in the CSA and the Controlled Substances Import and Export Act, 84 Stat. 1285, 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7951 et seq. Treating the amendments as nonsubstantive would be inconsistent with their text, not to mention Burgess\xe2\x80\x99 own view that \xc2\xa7802(44) added a new length-of-imprisonment requirement to the definition of \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, the 1994 alteration replaced a patchwork of state and foreign classifications with a uniform federal standard based on the authorized term of imprisonment. Burgess\xe2\x80\x99 argument that Congress added something\xe2\x80\x94the definition now in \xc2\xa7802(44)\xe2\x80\x94but subtracted nothing encounters formidable impediments: the text and history of the statute.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Burgess urges us to apply the rule of lenity in determining whether the term \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d incorporates \xc2\xa7802(13)\xe2\x80\x99s definition of \xe2\x80\x9cfelony.\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he touchstone of the rule of lenity is statutory ambiguity.\xe2\x80\x9d Bifulco v. United States, 447 U. S. 381, 387 (1980) (internal quotation marks omitted). \xe2\x80\x9cThe rule comes into operation at the end of the process of construing what Congress has expressed,\xe2\x80\x9d Callanan v. United States, 364 U. S. 587, 596 (1961), and \xe2\x80\x9capplies only when, after consulting traditional canons of statutory construction, we are left with an ambiguous statute,\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Shabani, 513 U. S. 10, 17 (1994). Here, Congress expressly defined the term \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense.\xe2\x80\x9d The definition is coherent, complete, and by all signs exclusive. Accordingly, there is no ambiguity for the rule of lenity to resolve.

Burgess\xe2\x80\x99 fourth example is also inapposite. The definition of \xe2\x80\x9cdebtor\xe2\x80\x99s principal residence\xe2\x80\x9d in the Bankruptcy Code, he notes, does not repeat the word \xe2\x80\x9cdebtor,\xe2\x80\x9d itself a discretely defined term. See 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7101(13), (13A) (2000 ed., Supp. V). Section 101(13A) states: \xe2\x80\x9cThe term \xe2\x80\x98debtor\xe2\x80\x99s principal residence\xe2\x80\x99\xe2\x80\x94(A) means a residential structure, including incidental property, without regard to whether that structure is attached to real property; and (B) includes an individual condominium or cooperative unit, a mobile or manufactured home, or trailer.\xe2\x80\x9d That definition, unlike 21 U. \n

\n\nAt most, therefore, Burgess\xe2\x80\x99 fourth example illustrates the importance of considering context in applying canons of statutory construction. There may well be other examples lurking in the United States Code of nested terms that draw their meaning from two different statutory provisions without repeating one term in the definition of the other. But \xe2\x80\x9cfelony drug offense\xe2\x80\x9d is not among them.
