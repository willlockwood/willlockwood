\n\t\t\t\tFEDERAL AVIATION ADMINISTRATION et\xc2\xa0al. v. COOPER\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 10\xe2\x80\x931024.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 30, 2011\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 28, 2012\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tRespondent Cooper, a licensed pilot, failed to disclose his human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) diagnosis to the Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) at a time when the agency did not issue medical certificates, which are required to operate an aircraft, to persons with HIV. Subsequently, respondent applied to the Social Security Administration (SSA) and received long-term disability benefits on the basis of his HIV status. Thereafter, he renewed his certificate with the FAA on several occasions, each time intentionally withholding information about his condition. The Department of Transportation (DOT), the FAA\xe2\x80\x99s parent agency, launched a joint criminal investigation with the SSA to identify medically unfit individuals who had obtained FAA certifications. The DOT provided the SSA with the names of licensed pilots, and the SSA, in turn, provided the DOT with a spreadsheet containing information on those pilots who had also received disability benefits. Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s name appeared on the spreadsheet, and an investigation led to his admission that he had intentionally withheld information about his HIV status from the FAA. His pilot certificate was revoked, and he was indicted for making false statements to a Government agency. He pleaded guilty and was fined and sentenced to probation. He then filed suit, alleging that the FAA, DOT, and SSA violated the Privacy Act of 1974, which contains a detailed set of requirements for the management of records held by Executive Branch agencies. The Act allows an aggrieved individual to sue for \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages,\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7552a(g)(4)(A), if the Government intentionally or willfully violates the Act\xe2\x80\x99s requirements in such a way as to adversely affect the individual. Specifically, respondent claimed that the unlawful disclosure to the DOT of his confidential medical information had caused him mental and emotional distress. The District Court concluded that the Government had violated the Act. But, finding the term \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d ambiguous, the court relied on the sovereign immunity canon, which provides that sovereign immunity waivers must be strictly construed in the Government\xe2\x80\x99s favor, to hold that the Act does not authorize the recovery of nonpecuniary damages. Reversing the District Court, the Ninth Circuit concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d in the Act is not ambiguous and includes damages for mental and emotional distress.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0The Privacy Act does not unequivocally authorize damages for mental or emotional distress and therefore does not waive the Government\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign immunity from liability for such harms. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9319.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0A waiver of sovereign immunity must be unequivocally expressed in statutory text, see e.g., Lane v. Pe\xc3\xb1a, \n\n518 U.\xc2\xa0S. 187\n, and any ambiguities are to be construed in favor of immunity, United States v. Williams, \n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 527\n. Ambiguity exists if there is a plausible interpretation of the statute that would not allow money damages against the Government. United States v. Nordic Village, Inc., \n\n503 U.\xc2\xa0S. 30\n. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x936.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0The term \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d in the Privacy Act is a legal term of art, and Congress, when it employs a term of art, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98presumably knows and adopts the cluster of ideas that were attached to each borrowed word in the body of learning from which it was taken,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Molzof v. United States, \n\n502 U.\xc2\xa0S. 301\n. Even as a legal term, the precise meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d is far from clear. Although the term is sometimes understood to include nonpecuniary harm, it has also been used or construed more narrowly to cover damages for only pecuniary harm. Because of the term\xe2\x80\x99s chameleon-like quality, it must be considered in the particular context in which it appears. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x939.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0The Privacy Act serves interests similar to those protected by defamation and privacy torts. Its remedial provision, under which plaintiffs can recover a minimum award of $1,000 if they first prove at least some \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cparallels\xe2\x80\x9d the common-law torts of libel per quod and slander, under which plaintiffs can recover \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral damages\xe2\x80\x9d if they first prove \xe2\x80\x9cspecial damages.\xe2\x80\x9d Doe v. Chao, \n\n540 U.\xc2\xa0S. 614\n. \xe2\x80\x9cSpecial damages\xe2\x80\x9d are limited to actual pecuniary loss, which must be specially pleaded and proved. \xe2\x80\x9cGeneral damages\xe2\x80\x9d cover nonpecuniary loss and need not be pleaded or proved. This parallel suggests the possibility that Congress intended the term \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d to mean \xe2\x80\x9cspecial damages,\xe2\x80\x9d thus barring Privacy Act victims from any recovery unless they can first show some actual pecuniary harm. That Congress would choose \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d instead of \xe2\x80\x9cspecial damages\xe2\x80\x9d is not without precedent, as the terms have occasionally been used interchangeably. Furthermore, any doubt about the plausibility of construing \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d as special damages in the Privacy Act is put to rest by Congress\xe2\x80\x99 deliberate refusal to allow recovery for \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral damages.\xe2\x80\x9d In common-law defamation and privacy cases, special damages is the only category of compensatory damages other than general damages. Because Congress declined to authorize general damages, it is reasonable to infer that Congress intended the term \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d in the Act to mean special dam-ages for proven pecuniary loss. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9314.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(d)\xc2\xa0Although the contrary reading of the Privacy Act accepted by the Ninth Circuit and advanced by respondent is not inconceivable, it is plausible to read the Act as authorizing only damages for economic loss. Because Congress did not speak unequivocally, the Court adopts an interpretation of \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d limited to proven pecuniary harm. To do otherwise would expand the scope of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 sovereign immunity waiver beyond what the statutory text clearly requires. P.\xc2\xa014.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(e)\xc2\xa0Respondent raises several counterarguments:\xc2\xa0(1) common-law cases often define \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d to mean all compensatory damages; (2) the elimination of \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral damages\xe2\x80\x9d from the Privacy Act means that there can be no recovery for presumed damages, but plaintiffs can still recover for proven mental and emotional distress; (3) because some courts have construed \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d in similar statutes to include mental and emotional distress, Congress must have intended \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d in the Act to include mental and emotional distress as well; and (4) precluding nonpecuniary damages would lead to absurd results, thereby frustrating the Act\xe2\x80\x99s remedial purpose. None of these arguments overcomes the sovereign immu-nity canon. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9319.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t622 F.\xc2\xa03d 1016, reversed and remanded.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Alito delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Privacy Act of 1974, codified in part at \n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7552a, contains a comprehensive and detailed set of requirements for the management of confidential records held by Executive Branch agencies. If an agency fails to comply with those requirements \xe2\x80\x9cin such a way as to have an adverse effect on an individual,\xe2\x80\x9d the Act authorizes the individual to bring a civil action against the agency. \xc2\xa7552a(g)(1)(D). For violations found to be \xe2\x80\x9cintentional or willful,\xe2\x80\x9d the United States is liable for \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7552a(g)(4)(A). In this case, we must decide whether the term \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages,\xe2\x80\x9d as used in the Privacy Act, includes damages for mental or emotional distress. We hold that it does not.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) requires pilots to obtain a pilot certificate and medical certificate as a precondition for operating an aircraft. 14 CFR \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa761.3(a), (c) (2011). Pilots must periodically renew their medical certificates to ensure compliance with FAA medical standards. See \xc2\xa761.23(d). When applying for renewal, pilots must disclose any illnesses, disabilities, or surgeries they have had, and they must identify any medications they are taking. See 14 CFR pt. 67.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondent Stanmore Cooper has been a private pilot since 1964. In 1985, he was diagnosed with a human im- munodeficiency virus (HIV) infection and began taking antiretroviral medication. At that time, the FAA did not issue medical certificates to persons with respondent\xe2\x80\x99s condition. Knowing that he would not qualify for renewal of his medical certificate, respondent initially grounded himself and chose not to apply. In 1994, however, he ap- plied for and received a medical certificate, but he did so without disclosing his HIV status or his medication. He renewed his certificate in 1998, 2000, 2002, and 2004, each time intentionally withholding information about his condition.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhen respondent\xe2\x80\x99s health deteriorated in 1995, he applied for long-term disability benefits under Title II of the Social Security Act, \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7401 et\xc2\xa0seq. To substantiate his claim, he disclosed his HIV status to the Social Security Administration (SSA), which awarded him benefits for the year from August 1995 to August 1996.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 2002, the Department of Transportation (DOT), the FAA\xe2\x80\x99s parent agency, launched a joint criminal investigation with the SSA, known as \xe2\x80\x9cOperation Safe Pilot,\xe2\x80\x9d to identify medically unfit individuals who had obtained FAA certifications to fly. The DOT gave the SSA a list of names and other identifying information of 45,000 licensed pilots in northern California. The SSA then compared the list with its own records of benefit recipients and compiled a spreadsheet, which it gave to the DOT.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe spreadsheet revealed that respondent had a current medical certificate but had also received disability benefits. After reviewing respondent\xe2\x80\x99s FAA medical file and his SSA disability file, FAA flight surgeons determined in 2005 that the FAA would not have issued a medical cer- tificate to respondent had it known his true medical condition.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhen investigators confronted respondent with what had been discovered, he admitted that he had intention- ally withheld from the FAA information about his HIV status and other relevant medical information. Because of these fraudulent omissions, the FAA revoked respondent\xe2\x80\x99s pilot certificate, and he was indicted on three counts of making false statements to a Government agency, in violation of \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71001. Respondent ultimately pleaded guilty to one count of making and delivering a false official writing, in violation of \xc2\xa71018. He was sentenced to two years of probation and fined $1,000.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tClaiming that the FAA, DOT, and SSA (hereinafter Government) violated the Privacy Act by sharing his records with one another, respondent filed suit in the United States District Court for the Northern District of California. He alleged that the unlawful disclosure to the DOT of his confidential medical information, including his HIV status, had caused him \xe2\x80\x9chumiliation, embarrassment, mental anguish, fear of social ostracism, and other severe emotional distress.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 120a. Notably, he did not allege any pecuniary or economic loss.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe District Court granted summary judgment against respondent. 816 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 778, 781 (2008). The court concluded that the Government had violated the Privacy Act and that there was a triable issue of fact as to whether the violation was intentional or willful.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t But the court held that respondent could not recover damages because he alleged only mental and emotional harm, not economic loss. Finding that the term \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cfacially ambiguous,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 791, and relying on the sovereign immunity canon, which provides that waivers of sovereign immunity must be strictly construed in favor of the Government, the court concluded that the Act does not authorize the recovery of damages from the Government for nonpecuniary mental or emotional harm.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit reversed and remanded. 622 F.\xc2\xa03d 1016, 1024 (2010). The court acknowledged that the term \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d is a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98chameleon\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in that \xe2\x80\x9cits meaning changes with the specific statute in which it is found.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1029. But the court nevertheless held that, as used in the Privacy Act, the term includes damages for mental and emotional distress. Looking to what it described as \xe2\x80\x9c[i]ntrinsic\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c[e]xtrinsic\xe2\x80\x9d sources, id., at 1028, 1031, the court concluded that the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d in the Privacy Act is not ambiguous and that \xe2\x80\x9ca construction that limits recovery to pecuniary loss\xe2\x80\x9d is not \xe2\x80\x9cplausible,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 1034.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government petitioned for rehearing or rehearing en banc, but a divided court denied the petition. Id., at 1019. The Government then petitioned for certiorari, and we granted review. 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2011).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBecause respondent seeks to recover monetary compensation from the Government for mental and emotional harm, we must decide whether the civil remedies provision of the Privacy Act waives the Government\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign immunity with respect to such a recovery.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe have said on many occasions that a waiver of sovereign immunity must be \xe2\x80\x9cunequivocally expressed\xe2\x80\x9d in statutory text. See, e.g., Lane v. Pe\xc3\xb1a, \n\n518 U.\xc2\xa0S. 187, \n192 (1996)\n; United States v. Nordic Village, Inc., \n\n503 U.\xc2\xa0S. 30, \n33 (1992)\n; Irwin v. Department of Veterans Affairs, \n\n498 U.\xc2\xa0S. 89, \n95 (1990)\n. Legislative history cannot supply a waiver that is not clearly evident from the language of the statute. Lane, supra, at 192. Any ambiguities in the statutory language are to be construed in favor of immu- nity, United States v. Williams, \n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 527, \n531 (1995)\n, so that the Government\xe2\x80\x99s consent to be sued is never en- larged beyond what a fair reading of the text requires, Ruckelshaus v. Sierra Club, \n\n463 U.\xc2\xa0S. 680\n\xe2\x80\x93686 (1983) (citing Eastern Transp. Co. v. United States, \n\n272 U.\xc2\xa0S. 675, \n686 (1927)\n). Ambiguity exists if there is a plausible interpretation of the statute that would not authorize money damages against the Government. Nordic Village, supra, at 34, 37.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe question that confronts us here is not whether Congress has consented to be sued for damages under the Privacy Act. That much is clear from the statute, which expressly authorizes recovery from the Government for \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages.\xe2\x80\x9d Rather, the question at issue concerns the scope of that waiver. For the same reason that we refuse to enforce a waiver that is not unambiguously expressed in the statute, we also construe any ambiguities in the scope of a waiver in favor of the sovereign. Lane, supra, at 192.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAlthough this canon of interpretation requires an unmistakable statutory expression of congressional intent to waive the Government\xe2\x80\x99s immunity, Congress need not state its intent in any particular way. We have never required that Congress use magic words. To the contrary, we have observed that the sovereign immunity canon \xe2\x80\x9cis a tool for interpreting the law\xe2\x80\x9d and that it does not \xe2\x80\x9cdisplac[e] the other traditional tools of statutory construction.\xe2\x80\x9d Richlin Security Service Co. v. Chertoff, \n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 571, \n589 (2008)\n. What we thus require is that the scope of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 waiver be clearly discernable from the statutory text in light of traditional interpretive tools. If it is not, then we take the interpretation most favorable to the Government.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe civil remedies provision of the Privacy Act provides that, for any \xe2\x80\x9cintentional or willful\xe2\x80\x9d refusal or failure to comply with the Act, the United States shall be liable for \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages sustained by the individual as a result of the refusal or failure, but in no case shall a person entitled to recovery receive less than the sum of $1,000.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7552a(g)(4)(A). Because Congress did not define \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages,\xe2\x80\x9d respondent urges us to rely on the ordinary meaning of the word \xe2\x80\x9cactual\xe2\x80\x9d as it is defined in standard general-purpose dictionaries. But as the Court of Appeals explained, \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d is a legal term of art, 622 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1028, and it is a \xe2\x80\x9ccardinal rule of statutory construction\xe2\x80\x9d that, when Congress employs a term of art, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98it presumably knows and adopts the cluster of ideas that were attached to each borrowed word in the body of learning from which it was taken,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Molzof v. United States, \n\n502 U.\xc2\xa0S. 301, \n307 (1992)\n (quoting Morissette v. United States, \n\n342 U.\xc2\xa0S. 246, \n263 (1952)\n).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEven as a legal term, however, the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d is far from clear. The latest edition of Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary available when Congress enacted the Privacy Act defined \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9c[r]eal, substantial and just damages, or the amount awarded to a complainant in compensation for his actual and real loss or injury, as opposed on the one hand to \xe2\x80\x98nominal\xe2\x80\x99 damages, and on the other to \xe2\x80\x98exemplary\xe2\x80\x99 or \xe2\x80\x98punitive\xe2\x80\x99 damages.\xe2\x80\x9d Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 467 (rev. 4th ed. 1968). But this general (and notably circular) definition is of little value here because, as the Court of Appeals accurately observed, the precise meaning of the term \xe2\x80\x9cchanges with the specific statute in which it is found.\xe2\x80\x9d 622 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1029.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe term is sometimes understood to include nonpecuniary harm. Take, for instance, some courts\xe2\x80\x99 interpretations of the Fair Housing Act (FHA), \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73613(c), and the Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA), 15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71681n, 1681o. A number of courts have construed \xe2\x80\x9cactual\xe2\x80\x9d damages in the remedial provisions of both statutes to include compensation for mental and emotional distress. See, e.g., Seaton v. Sky Realty Co., 491 F.\xc2\xa02d 634, 636\xe2\x80\x93638 (CA7 1974) (authorizing compensatory damages under the FHA, \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73612, the predecessor to \xc2\xa73613, for humiliation); Steele v. Title Realty Co., 478 F.\xc2\xa02d 380, 384 (CA10 1973) (stating that damages under the FHA \xe2\x80\x9care not limited to out-of-pocket losses but may include an award for emotional distress and humiliation\xe2\x80\x9d); Thompson v. San Antonio Retail Merchants Assn., 682 F.\xc2\xa02d 509, 513\xe2\x80\x93514 (CA5 1982) (per\xc2\xa0curiam) (explaining that, \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ven when there are no out-of-pocket expenses, humiliation and mental distress do constitute recoverable elements of damage\xe2\x80\x9d under the FCRA); Millstone v. O\xe2\x80\x99Hanlon Reports, Inc., 528 F.\xc2\xa02d 829, 834\xe2\x80\x93835 (CA8 1976) (approving an award of damages under the FCRA for \xe2\x80\x9closs of sleep, nervousness, frustration and mental anguish\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn other contexts, however, the term has been used or construed more narrowly to authorize damages for only pecuniary harm. In the wrongful-death provision of the Federal Tort Claims Act (FTCA), for example, Congress authorized \xe2\x80\x9cactual or compensatory damages, measured by the pecuniary injuries resulting from such death.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72674, \xc2\xb62. At least one court has defined \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d in the Copyright Act of 1909, \n\n17 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7101(b) (1970 ed.), as \xe2\x80\x9cthe extent to which the market value of a copyrighted work has been injured or destroyed by an infringement.\xe2\x80\x9d Frank Music Corp. v. Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer, Inc., 772 F.\xc2\xa02d 505, 512 (CA9 1985); see also Mackie v. Rieser, 296 F.\xc2\xa03d 909, 917 (CA9 2002) (holding that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98hurt feelings\xe2\x80\x99 over the nature of the infringement\xe2\x80\x9d have no place in the actual damages calculus). And some courts have construed \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d in the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, \n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778bb(a), to mean \xe2\x80\x9csome form of economic loss.\xe2\x80\x9d Ryan v. Foster & Marshall, Inc., 556 F.\xc2\xa02d 460, 464 (CA9 1977); see also Osofsky v. Zipf, 645 F.\xc2\xa02d 107, 111 (CA2 1981) (stating that the purpose of \xc2\xa778bb(a) \xe2\x80\x9cis to compensate civil plaintiffs for economic loss suffered as a result of wrongs committed in violation of the 1934 Act\xe2\x80\x9d); Herpich v. Wallace, 430 F.\xc2\xa02d 792, 810 (CA5 1970) (noting that the \xe2\x80\x9cgist\xe2\x80\x9d of an action for damages under the Act is \xe2\x80\x9ceconomic injury\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBecause the term \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d has this chameleon-like quality, we cannot rely on any all-purpose definition but must consider the particular context in which the term appears.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tC\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Privacy Act directs agencies to establish safeguards to protect individuals against the disclosure of confiden- tial records \xe2\x80\x9cwhich could result in substantial harm, embarrassment, inconvenience, or unfairness to any indi- vidual on whom information is maintained.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7552a(e)(10); see also \xc2\xa72(b), \n\n88Stat.\n1896 (stating that the \xe2\x80\x9cpurpose of this Act is to provide certain safeguards for an individual against an invasion of personal privacy\xe2\x80\x9d). Because the Act serves interests similar to those protected by defamation and privacy torts, there is good reason to infer that Congress relied upon those torts in drafting the Act.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn Doe v. Chao, \n\n540 U.\xc2\xa0S. 614 (2004)\n, we held that the Privacy Act\xe2\x80\x99s remedial provision authorizes plaintiffs to recover a guaranteed minimum award of $1,000 for violations of the Act, but only if they prove at least some \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 620, 627; see \xc2\xa7552a(g)(4)(A). Although we did not address the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 622, n.\xc2\xa05, 627, n.\xc2\xa012, we observed that the provision \xe2\x80\x9cparallels\xe2\x80\x9d the remedial scheme for the common-law torts of libel per quod and slander, under which plaintiffs can recover \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral damages,\xe2\x80\x9d but only if they prove \xe2\x80\x9cspecial harm\xe2\x80\x9d (also known as \xe2\x80\x9cspecial damages\xe2\x80\x9d), id., at 625; see also 3 Restatement of Torts \xc2\xa7575, Comments a and b (1938) (hereinafter Restatement); D. Dobbs, Law of Remedies \xc2\xa77.2, pp. 511\xe2\x80\x93513 (1973) (hereinafter Dobbs).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t \xe2\x80\x9cSpecial damages\xe2\x80\x9d are limited to actual pecuniary loss, which must be specially pleaded and proved. 1 D. Haggard, Cooley on Torts \xc2\xa7164, p. 580 (4th ed. 1932) (hereinafter Cooley).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t \xe2\x80\x9cGeneral damages,\xe2\x80\x9d on the other hand, cover \xe2\x80\x9closs of reputation, shame, mortification, injury to the feelings and the like and need not be alleged in detail and require no proof.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., \xc2\xa7164, at 579.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t7\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis parallel between the Privacy Act and the common-law torts of libel per quod and slander suggests the possibility that Congress intended the term \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d in the Act to mean special damages. The basic idea is that Privacy Act victims, like victims of libel per quod or slander, are barred from any recovery unless they can first show actual\xe2\x80\x94that is, pecuniary or material\xe2\x80\x94harm. Upon showing some pecuniary harm, no matter how slight, they can recover the statutory minimum of $1,000, presumably for any unproven harm. That Congress would choose to use the term \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d instead of \xe2\x80\x9cspecial damages\xe2\x80\x9d was not without precedent. The terms had occasionally been used interchangeably. See, e.g., Wetzel v. Gulf Oil Corp., 455 F.\xc2\xa02d 857, 862 (CA9 1972) (holding that plaintiff could not establish libel per quod because he \xe2\x80\x9cdid not introduce any valid and sufficient evidence of actual damage\xe2\x80\x9d); Electric Furnace Corp. v. Deering Milliken Research Corp., 325 F.\xc2\xa02d 761, 765 (CA6 1963) (stating that \xe2\x80\x9clibel per quod standing alone without proof of actual damages .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. will not support a verdict for the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x9d); M\xc2\xa0&\xc2\xa0S Furniture Sales Co. v. Edward J. De Bartolo Corp., 249 Md. 540, 544, 241 A.\xc2\xa02d 126, 128 (1968) (\xe2\x80\x9cIn the case of words or conduct actionable only per quod, the injurious effect must be established by allegations and proof of special damage and in such cases it is not only necessary to plead and show that the words or actions were defamatory, but it must also appear that such words or conduct caused actual damage\xe2\x80\x9d); Clementson v. Minnesota Tribune Co., 45 Minn. 303, 47 N.\xc2\xa0W. 781 (1891) (distinguishing \xe2\x80\x9cactual, or, as they are sometimes termed, \xe2\x80\x98special,\xe2\x80\x99 damages\xe2\x80\x9d from \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral damages\xe2\x80\x94that is, damages not pecuniary in their nature\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t8\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAny doubt about the plausibility of construing \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d in the Privacy Act synonymously with \xe2\x80\x9cspecial damages\xe2\x80\x9d is put to rest by Congress\xe2\x80\x99 refusal to authorize \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral damages.\xe2\x80\x9d In an uncodified section of the Act, Congress established the Privacy Protection Study Commission to consider, among other things, \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the Federal Government should be liable for general dam- ages.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa75(c)(2)(B)(iii), \n\n88Stat.\n1907, note following \n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7552a, p. 712. As we explained in Doe, \xe2\x80\x9cCongress left the question of general damages .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. for another day.\xe2\x80\x9d 540 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 622. Although the Commission later recom- mended that general damages be allowed, ibid., n.\xc2\xa04, Congress never amended the Act to include them. For that reason, we held that it was \xe2\x80\x9cbeyond serious doubt\xe2\x80\x9d that general damages are not available for violations of the Privacy Act. Id., at 622.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBy authorizing recovery for \xe2\x80\x9cactual\xe2\x80\x9d but not for \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral\xe2\x80\x9d damages, Congress made clear that it viewed those terms as mutually exclusive. In actions for defamation and related dignitary torts, two categories of compensa- tory damages are recoverable: general damages and special damages. Cooley \xc2\xa7164, at 579; see also 4 Restatement \xc2\xa7867, Comment d (1939) (noting that damages for interference with privacy \xe2\x80\x9ccan be awarded in the same way in which general damages are given for defamation\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t9\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Because Congress declined to authorize \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral damages,\xe2\x80\x9d we think it likely that Congress intended \xe2\x80\x9cactual dam- ages\xe2\x80\x9d in the Privacy Act to mean special damages for proven pecuniary loss.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNot surprisingly, this interpretation was accepted by the Privacy Protection Study Commission, an expert body authorized by Congress and highly sensitive to the Act\xe2\x80\x99s goals. The Commission understood \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d in the Act to be \xe2\x80\x9ca synonym for special damages as that term is used in defamation cases.\xe2\x80\x9d Personal Privacy in an Information Society: The Report of the Privacy Protection Study Commission 530 (July 1977); see also ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9cThe legislative history and language of the Act suggest that Congress meant to restrict recovery to specific pecuniary losses until the Commission could weigh the propriety of extending the standard of recovery\xe2\x80\x9d). Although we are not bound in any way by the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s report, we think it confirms the reasonableness of interpreting \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d in the unique context of the Privacy Act as the equivalent of special damages.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tD\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe do not claim that the contrary reading of the statute accepted by the Court of Appeals and advanced now by respondent is inconceivable. But because the Privacy Act waives the Federal Government\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign immunity, the question we must answer is whether it is plausible to read the statute, as the Government does, to authorize only damages for economic loss. Nordic Village, 503 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 34, 37. When waiving the Government\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign immunity, Congress must speak unequivocally. Lane, 518 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 192. Here, we conclude that it did not. As a consequence, we adopt an interpretation of \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d limited to proven pecuniary or economic harm. To do otherwise would expand the scope of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 sovereign immunity waiver beyond what the statutory text clearly requires.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNone of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s contrary arguments suffices to overcome the sovereign immunity canon.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondent notes that the term \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d has often been defined broadly in common-law cases, and in our own, to include all compensatory damages. See Brief for Respondent 18\xe2\x80\x9325. For example, in Birdsall v. Coolidge, \n\n93 U.\xc2\xa0S. 64 (1876)\n, a patent infringement case, we observed that \xe2\x80\x9c[c]ompensatory damages and actual damages mean the same thing.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. And in Gertz v. Robert Welch, Inc., \n\n418 U.\xc2\xa0S. 323 (1974)\n, we wrote that actual injury in the defamation context \xe2\x80\x9cis not limited to out-of-pocket loss\xe2\x80\x9d and that it customarily includes \xe2\x80\x9cimpairment of reputation and standing in the community, personal humiliation, and mental anguish and suffering.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 350.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThese cases and others cited by respondent stand for the unremarkable point that the term \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d can include nonpecuniary loss. But this generic meaning does not establish with the requisite clarity that the Privacy Act, with its distinctive features, authorizes damages for mental and emotional distress. As we already explained, the term \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d takes on different meanings in different contexts.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondent\xe2\x80\x99s stronger argument is that the exclusion of \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral damages\xe2\x80\x9d from the statute simply means that there can be no recovery for presumed damages. Privacy Act victims can still recover for mental and emotional distress, says respondent, so long as it is proved. See Brief for Respondent 54\xe2\x80\x9356.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t10\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis argument is flawed because it suggests that proven mental and emotional distress does not count as general damages. The term \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral damages\xe2\x80\x9d is not limited to compensation for unproven injuries; it includes compensation for proven injuries as well. See 3 Restatement \xc2\xa7621, Comment a (noting that general damages compensate for \xe2\x80\x9charm which .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is proved, or, in the absence of proof, is assumed to have caused to [the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s] reputation\xe2\x80\x9d). To be sure, specific proof of emotional harm is not required to recover general damages for dignitary torts. Dobbs \xc2\xa77.3, at 529. But it does not follow that general damages cannot be recovered for emotional harm that is actually proved.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAside from the fact that general damages need not be proved, what distinguishes those damages, whether proved or not, from the only other category of compensa- tory damages available in the relevant common-law suits is the type of harm. In defamation and privacy cases, \xe2\x80\x9cthe affront to the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s dignity and the emotional harm done\xe2\x80\x9d are \xe2\x80\x9ccalled general damages, to distinguish them from proof of actual economic harm,\xe2\x80\x9d which is called \xe2\x80\x9cspecial damages.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., \xc2\xa73.2, at 139; see also supra, at 10, 12\xe2\x80\x9313, and nn.\xc2\xa06, 7, 9. Therefore, the converse of general damages is special damages, not all proven damages, as respondent would have it. Because Congress removed \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral damages\xe2\x80\x9d from the Act\xe2\x80\x99s remedial provision, it is reasonable to infer that Congress foreclosed recovery for nonpecuniary harm, even if such harm can be proved, and instead waived the Government\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign immunity only with respect to harm compensable as special damages.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tC\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tLooking beyond the Privacy Act\xe2\x80\x99s text, respondent points to the use of the term \xe2\x80\x9cactual\xe2\x80\x9d damages in the remedial provisions of the FHA, \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73613(c), and the FCRA, 15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71681n, 1681o. As previously mentioned, courts have held that \xe2\x80\x9cactual\xe2\x80\x9d damages within the meaning of these statutes include compensation for mental and emotional distress. Supra, at 7. Citing the rule of construction that Congress intends the same language in similar statutes to have the same meaning, see Northcross v. Board of Ed. of Memphis City Schools, \n\n412 U.\xc2\xa0S. 427, \n428 (1973)\n (per\xc2\xa0curiam), respondent argues that the Privacy Act should also be interpreted as authorizing damages for mental and emotional distress. See Brief for Respondent 25\xe2\x80\x9332.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAssuming for the sake of argument that these lower court decisions are correct, they provide only weak support for respondent\xe2\x80\x99s argument here. Since the term \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d can mean different things in different contexts, statutes other than the Privacy Act provide only limited interpretive aid, and that is especially true here. Neither the FHA nor the FCRA contains text that precisely mirrors the Privacy Act.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t11\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t In neither of those statutes did Congress specifically decline to authorize recovery for general damages as it did in the Privacy Act. Supra, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313. And most importantly, none of the lower court cases interpreting the statutes, which respondent has cited, see Brief for Respondent 29\xe2\x80\x9331, involves the sovereign immunity canon.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondent also points to the FTCA, but the FTCA\xe2\x80\x99s general liability provision does not even use the term \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages.\xe2\x80\x9d It instead provides that the \xe2\x80\x9cUnited States shall be liable\xe2\x80\x9d for certain tort claims \xe2\x80\x9cin the same manner and to the same extent as a private individual\xe2\x80\x9d under relevant state law. \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72674, \xc2\xb61. For that reason alone, the FTCA\xe2\x80\x99s general liability provision is not a reliable source for interpreting the term \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d in the Privacy Act. Nor does the FTCA\xe2\x80\x99s wrongful- death provision\xe2\x80\x94which authorizes \xe2\x80\x9cactual or compensa- tory damages, measured by the pecuniary injuries resulting from such death,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72674, \xc2\xb62\xe2\x80\x94prove that Congress understood the term \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d in the Privacy Act to include nonpecuniary mental and emotional harm. To the contrary, it proves that actual damages can be understood to entail only pecuniary harm depending on the context. Because the FTCA, like the FHA and FCRA, does not share the same text or design as the Privacy Act, it is not a fitting analog for construing the Act.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tD\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, respondent argues that excluding damages for mental and emotional harm would lead to absurd results. Persons suffering relatively minor pecuniary loss would be entitled to recover $1,000, while others suffering only severe and debilitating mental or emotional distress would get nothing. See Brief for Respondent 33\xe2\x80\x9335.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress enacted the Privacy Act of 1974 for the stated purpose of safeguarding individual privacy against Government invasion. To that end, the Act provides a civil remedy entitling individuals adversely affected by certain agency misconduct to recover \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d sustained as a result of the unlawful action.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tToday the Court holds that \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d is limited to pecuniary loss. Consequently, individuals can no longer recover what our precedents and common sense understand to be the primary, and often only, damages sustained as a result of an invasion of privacy, namely mental or emotional distress. That result is at odds with the text, structure, and drafting history of the Act. And it cripples the Act\xe2\x80\x99s core purpose of redressing and deterring violations of privacy interests. I respectfully dissent.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority concludes that \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d in the civil-remedies provision of the Privacy Act allows recovery for pecuniary loss alone. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe canon simply cannot bear the weight the majority ascribes it. \xe2\x80\x9cThe sovereign immunity canon is just that\xe2\x80\x94a canon of construction. It is a tool for interpreting the law, and we have never held that it displaces the other traditional tools of statutory construction.\xe2\x80\x9d Richlin Security Service Co. v. Chertoff, \n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 571, \n589 (2008)\n (opinion of Alito, J.). Here, traditional tools of statutory construction\xe2\x80\x94the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text, structure, drafting history, and purpose\xe2\x80\x94provide a clear answer: The term \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d permits recovery for all injuries established by competent evidence in the record, whether pecuniary or nonpecuniary, and so encompasses damages for mental and emotional distress. There is no need to seek refuge in a canon of construction, see id., at 589\xe2\x80\x93590 (declining to rely on canon as there is \xe2\x80\x9cno ambiguity left for us to construe\xe2\x80\x9d after application of \xe2\x80\x9ctraditional tools of statutory interpretation and considerations of stare decisis\xe2\x80\x9d), much less one that has been used so haphazardly in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s history, see United States v. Each cited authority involves either a statute in which Congress expressly directed that compensation be measured in strictly economic terms, or else a statute (e.g., the Copyright Act of 1909) in which economic loss is the natural and probable consequence of a violation of the defined legal interest.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Neither factor is present here. Notably absent from the Privacy Act is any provision so much as hinting that \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d should be limited to economic loss. And while \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98 \xe2\x80\x9churt feelings\xe2\x80\x9d over the nature of the [copyright] infringement\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d may \xe2\x80\x9chave no place in the actual damages calculus\xe2\x80\x9d under the Copyright Act of 1909, ante, at 8 (quoting in parenthetical Mackie v. Rieser, 296 F.\xc2\xa03d 909, 917 (CA9 2002)), the majority provides no basis for concluding that \xe2\x80\x9churt feelings\xe2\x80\x9d are equally invalid in an Act concerned with safeguarding individual privacy. Thus, while context is no doubt relevant, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s cited authority does little to help its cause in the stated context of this statute.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIndeed, the relevant statutory context\xe2\x80\x94the substantive provisions whose breach may trigger suit under the civil-remedies provision\xe2\x80\x94only reinforces the ordinary meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress established substantive duties in the Act that are expressly designed to prevent agency conduct resulting in intangible harms to the individual. The Act requires agencies to \xe2\x80\x9cestablish appropriate administrative, technical, and physical safeguards\xe2\x80\x9d to ensure against security breaches that could result in \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial harm, embarrassment, inconvenience, or unfairness to any individual.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7552a(e)(10). It also requires agencies to \xe2\x80\x9cmaintain all records\xe2\x80\x9d used in making a determination about an individual in a manner that is \xe2\x80\x9creasonably necessary to assure fairness to the individual in the determination.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7552a(e)(5). Thus an agency violates the terms of the Act if it fails, e.g., to maintain safeguards protecting against \xe2\x80\x9cembarrassment\xe2\x80\x9d; there is no additional requirement that the pocketbook be implicated. An agency\xe2\x80\x99s intentional or willful violation of those duties triggers liability for \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa7552a(g)(4) in the event of an adverse impact. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7552a(g)(1)(C)\xe2\x80\x93(D), (g)(4).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAdopting a reading of \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d that permits recovery for pecuniary loss alone creates a disconnect between the Act\xe2\x80\x99s substantive and remedial provisions. It allows a swath of Government violations to go unremedied: A federal agency could intentionally or willfully forgo establishing safeguards to protect against embarrassment and no successful private action could be taken against it for the harm Congress identified. Only an interpretation of \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d that permits recovery for nonpecuniary harms harmonizes the Act\xe2\x80\x99s substantive and remedial provisions. Robinson v. Shell Oil Co., \n\n519 U.\xc2\xa0S. 337, \n341 (1997)\n (statutory interpretation must consider \xe2\x80\x9cthe broader context of the statute as a whole\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority draws a different conclusion from the substantive provisions of the Privacy Act. It (correctly) in- fers from them that the Act \xe2\x80\x9cserves interests similar to those protected by defamation and privacy torts.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9. It then points to our observation in Doe v. Chao, \n\n540 U.\xc2\xa0S. 614, \n625 (2004)\n, that the Act\xe2\x80\x99s civil-remedies provision \xe2\x80\x9cparallels\xe2\x80\x9d the remedial scheme for the common-law torts of defamation per quod, which permitted recovery of \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral damages\xe2\x80\x9d (i.e., presumed damages) only if a plaintiff first establishes \xe2\x80\x9cspecial damages\xe2\x80\x9d (i.e., monetary loss).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Ante, at 10. That \xe2\x80\x9cparallel,\xe2\x80\x9d the majority concludes, \xe2\x80\x9csuggests the possibility that Congress intended the term \xe2\x80\x98actual damages\xe2\x80\x99 in the Act to mean special damages.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority reads too much into Doe. At issue in that case was the question whether the Act\xe2\x80\x99s civil-suit provision authorized recovery of a guaranteed minimum award of $1,000 absent proof of some \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages.\xe2\x80\x9d The Court answered in the negative, and in the course of doing so replied to petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s argument that there was \xe2\x80\x9csomething peculiar in offering some guaranteed damages .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. only to those plaintiffs who can demonstrate actual damages.\xe2\x80\x9d 540 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 625. Although the Court cited the Act\xe2\x80\x99s parallels to defamation per quod actions in noting that nothing was \xe2\x80\x9cpeculiar\xe2\x80\x9d about the Act\xe2\x80\x99s remedial scheme, Doe did not take the further step of deciding that \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d means economic loss alone. Indeed, it expressly reserved that question. Id., at 627, n.\xc2\xa012.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority, moreover, is wrong to conclude that the Act\xe2\x80\x99s parallels with defamation per quod actions suggest Congress intended \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d to mean \xe2\x80\x9cspecial damages.\xe2\x80\x9d Quite the opposite. The fact that Congress \xe2\x80\x9cwould probably have known about\xe2\x80\x9d defamation per quod actions, id., at 625, makes it all the more significant that Congress did not write \xe2\x80\x9cspecial damages\xe2\x80\x9d in the civil-remedies provision. This Court is typically not in the business of substituting words we think Congress in- tended to use for words Congress in fact used. Yet that is precisely what the majority does when it rewrites \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d to mean \xe2\x80\x9cspecial damages.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t In sum, the statutory context, and in particular the Act\xe2\x80\x99s substantive provisions, confirms the ordinary meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages.\xe2\x80\x9d Although the Act shares parallels with common-law defamation torts, such analogies do not warrant a reading of the phrase that is at odds with the statute\xe2\x80\x99s plain text.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t7\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tC\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAn uncodified provision of the Act, tied to the Act\xe2\x80\x99s drafting history, also reinforces the ordinary meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages.\xe2\x80\x9d As the majority notes, prior to reconciliation, the Senate and House bills contained civil-remedies provisions that were different in a critical respect: The Senate bill allowed for the recovery of \xe2\x80\x9cactual and general damages,\xe2\x80\x9d whereas the House bill allowed for the recovery of \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d alone.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t8\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t In the reconciliation process, the provision for \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral damages\xe2\x80\x9d was dropped and an uncodified section of the Act was amended to require the newly established Privacy Protection Study Commission to consider, among its other jobs, \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the Federal Government should be liable for general damages incurred by an individual as the result of a willful or intentional violation of the provisions of sections 552a(g)(1)(C) or (D).\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa75(c)(2)(B)(iii), \n\n88Stat.\n1907; see also Doe, 540 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 622.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs the Court explained in Doe, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he deletion of \xe2\x80\x98general damages\xe2\x80\x99 from the bill is fairly seen .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. as a deliberate elimination of any possibility of imputing harm and awarding presumed damages.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 623; see also id., at 622, n.\xc2\xa05 (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress explicitly rejected the proposal to make presumed damages available for Privacy Act violations\xe2\x80\x9d). The elimination of presumed damages from the bill can only reasonably imply that what Congress left behind\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94comprised damages that are not presumed, i.e., damages proven by competent evidence in the record. See Gertz v. Robert Welch, Inc., \n\n418 U.\xc2\xa0S. 323\n\xe2\x80\x93350 (1974) (distinguishing in defamation context between presumed damages and damages for actual injuries sustained by competent evidence in the record, which include \xe2\x80\x9cimpairment of reputation and standing in the community, personal humiliation, and mental anguish and suffering\xe2\x80\x9d); Carey v. Piphus, \n\n435 U.\xc2\xa0S. 247\n\xe2\x80\x93264 (1978) (distinguishing between presumed damages and proven damages for mental and emotional distress).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRather than view the deletion of general damages (presumed damages) as leaving the converse (proven damages), the majority supposes that the deletion leaves only a subset of proven damages\xe2\x80\x94those of an economic nature, i.e., \xe2\x80\x9cspecial damages.\xe2\x80\x9d Once again, however, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s insistence that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress intended \xe2\x80\x98actual damages\xe2\x80\x99 in the Privacy Act to mean special damages for proven pecuniary loss,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 13, finds no basis in the statutory text, see supra, at 8. And its response to the conclusion that Congress retained recovery for proven damages when it eliminated presumed damages is singularly unsatisfying. The majority declares such a conclusion \xe2\x80\x9cflawed\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral damages\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cincludes compensation for proven injuries as well,\xe2\x80\x9d so that \xe2\x80\x9cwhat distinguishes [general] damages, whether proved or not, from the only other category of compensatory damages available in the relevant common-law suits is the type of harm\xe2\x80\x9d the term encompasses\xe2\x80\x94which the majority takes to be emotional harm alone. Ante, at 15\xe2\x80\x9316. That assertion is defective on two scores. First, a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s ability to present proof of injury in a defamation per\xc2\xa0se action (and to recover for such proven injury) does not alter the definition of \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral damages,\xe2\x80\x9d which we already explained in Doe means \xe2\x80\x9cpresumed damages.\xe2\x80\x9d 540 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 621; see also id., at 623; n.\xc2\xa05, supra. Second, \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral damages\xe2\x80\x9d is not limited to a \xe2\x80\x9ctype\xe2\x80\x9d of harm. The majority\xe2\x80\x99s contrary assertion that the term permits recovery only for emotional \xe2\x80\x9ctypes\xe2\x80\x9d of harm overlooks the fact that \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral damages are partly based on the belief that the plaintiff will suffer unprovable pecuniary losses.\xe2\x80\x9d Dobbs \xc2\xa77.2, at 514 (emphasis added). It thus was established at common law that in a defamation per\xc2\xa0se action, \xe2\x80\x9cthe plaintiff is usually free to prove whatever actual pecuniary loss he can,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cthe jury may be permitted to view the actual pecuniary loss proven as the tip of the iceberg, assume that there is still more un-proven, and award damage accordingly.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAt its core, the majority opinion relies on the following syllogism: The common law employed two terms of art in defamation actions. Because Congress excluded recovery for \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral damages,\xe2\x80\x9d it must have meant to retain recovery only for \xe2\x80\x9cspecial damages.\xe2\x80\x9d That syllogism, of course, ignores that there is another category of damages. It is the very category Congress used in the text of the Privacy Act: \xe2\x80\x9cActual damages.\xe2\x80\x9d However much Congress may have drawn \xe2\x80\x9cparallels,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 10, between the Act and the common-law tort of defamation, the fact remains that Congress expressly choose not to use the words \xe2\x80\x9cspecial damages.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t9\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI turn finally to the statute\xe2\x80\x99s purpose, for \xe2\x80\x9c[a]s in all cases of statutory interpretation, our task is to interpret the words of th[e] statut[e] in light of the purposes Congress sought to serve.\xe2\x80\x9d Chapman v. Houston Welfare Rights Organization, \n\n441 U.\xc2\xa0S. 600, \n608 (1979)\n; see also Dolan v. Postal Service, \n\n546 U.\xc2\xa0S. 481, \n486 (2006)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cInterpretation of a word or phrase depends upon reading the whole statutory text, considering the purpose and context of the statute, and consulting any precedents or authorities that inform the analysis\xe2\x80\x9d). The purposes of the Privacy Act could not be more explicit, and they are consistent with interpreting \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d according to its ordinary meaning.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cThe historical context of the Act is important to an understanding of its remedial purposes. In 1974, Congress was concerned with curbing the illegal surveillance and investigation of individuals by federal agencies that had been exposed during the Watergate scandal.\xe2\x80\x9d Dept. of Justice, Office of Privacy and Civil Liberties, Overview of the Privacy Act\xc2\xa04 (2010). In particular, Congress recognized that \xe2\x80\x9cthe increasing use of computers and sophisticated information technology .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. has greatly magnified the harm to individual privacy that can occur from any collection, maintenance, use, or dissemination of personal information.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72(a), \n\n88Stat.\n1896. Identifying the right to privacy as \xe2\x80\x9ca personal and fundamental right,\xe2\x80\x9d Congress found it \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary and proper\xe2\x80\x9d to enact the Privacy Act \xe2\x80\x9cin order to protect the privacy of individuals identified in information systems maintained by Federal agencies.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress explained that the \xe2\x80\x9cpurpose of this Act is to provide certain safeguards for an individual against an invasion of personal privacy by requiring Federal agencies, except as otherwise provided by law, to,\xe2\x80\x9d inter alia, \xe2\x80\x9cbe subject to civil suit for any damages which occur as a result of willful or intentional action which violates any individual\xe2\x80\x99s rights under this Act.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72(b)(6), ibid. (emphasis added). That statement is an explicit reference to suits brought under \xc2\xa7552a(g)(4); no other provision speaks to a civil suit based on \xe2\x80\x9cwillful or intentional\xe2\x80\x9d agency misconduct. It signals unmistakably congressional recognition that the civil-remedies provision is integral to realizing the Act\xe2\x80\x99s purposes.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tReading \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d to permit recovery for any injury established by competent evidence in the record\xe2\x80\x94pecuniary or not\xe2\x80\x94best effectuates the statute\xe2\x80\x99s basic purpose. Although some privacy invasions no doubt result in economic loss, we have recognized time and again that the primary form of injuries is nonpecuniary, and includes mental distress and personal humiliation. See Time, Inc. v. Hill, \n\n385 U.\xc2\xa0S. 374, \n385, n. \n9 (1967)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cIn the \xe2\x80\x98right of privacy\xe2\x80\x99 cases the primary damage is the mental distress\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Gertz, 418 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 350 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]ctual injury\xe2\x80\x9d in defamatory falsehood cases \xe2\x80\x9cis not limited to out-of-pocket loss. Indeed, the more customary types of actual harm inflicted by defamatory falsehood include impairment of reputation and standing in the community, personal humiliation, and mental anguish and suffering\xe2\x80\x9d). Accord, 2 Dobbs \xc2\xa77.1.(1), at 259 (2d ed. 1993) (privacy is a dignitary interest, and \xe2\x80\x9cin a great many of the cases\xe2\x80\x9d in which the interest is invaded \xe2\x80\x9cthe only harm is the affront to the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s dignity as a human being, the damage to his self-image, and the resulting mental distress\xe2\x80\x9d). That accords with common sense.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn interpreting the civil-remedies provision, we must not forget Congress enacted the Privacy Act to protect pri- vacy. The majority\xe2\x80\x99s reading of \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d renders the remedial provision impotent in the face of concededly unlawful agency action whenever the injury is solely nonpecuniary. That result is patently at odds with Congress\xe2\x80\x99 stated purpose. The majority, however, does not grapple with the ramifications of its opinion. It acknowledges the suggestion that its holding leads to absurd results as it allows individuals suffering relatively minor pecuniary losses to recover $1,000 while others suffering severe mental anguish to recover nothing. But it concludes that \xe2\x80\x9cthere is nothing absurd about a scheme that limits the Government\xe2\x80\x99s Privacy Act liability to harm that can be substantiated by proof of tangible economic loss.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 18. Perhaps; it is certainly within Congress\xe2\x80\x99 prerogative to enact the statute the majority envisions, namely one that seeks to safeguard against invasions of privacy without remedying the primary harm that results from invasions of privacy. The problem for the majority is that one looks in vain for any indication in the text of the statute before us that Congress intended such a result. Nowhere in the Privacy Act does Congress so much as hint that it views a $5 hit to the pocketbook as more worthy of remedy than debilitating mental distress, and the majority\xe2\x80\x99s contrary assumption discounts the gravity of emotional harm caused by an invasion of the personal integrity that privacy protects.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAfter today, no matter how debilitating and substantial the resulting mental anguish, an individual harmed by a federal agency\xe2\x80\x99s intentional or willful violation of the Privacy Act will be left without a remedy unless he or she is able to prove pecuniary harm. That is not the result Congress intended when it enacted an Act with the express purpose of safeguarding individual privacy against Government invasion. And it is not a result remotely suggested by anything in the text, structure, or history of the Act. For those reasons, I respectfully dissent.\n\t\t\t
