KANSAS v. VENTRIS

\n

certiorari to the supreme court of kansas

\n

No. 07\xe2\x80\x931356.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 21, 2009\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 29, 2009

\n

Respondent Donnie Ray Ventris and Rhonda Theel were charged with murder and other crimes. Prior to trial, an informant planted in Ventris\xe2\x80\x99s cell heard him admit to shooting and robbing the victim, but Ventris testified at trial that Theel committed the crimes. When the State sought to call the informant to testify to his contradictory statement, Ventris objected. The State conceded that Ventris\xe2\x80\x99s Sixth Amendment right to counsel had likely been violated, but argued that the statement was admissible for impeachment purposes. The trial court allowed the testimony. The jury convicted Ventris of aggravated burglary and aggravated robbery. Reversing, the Kansas Supreme Court held that the informant\xe2\x80\x99s statements were not admissible for any reason, including impeachment.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Ventris\xe2\x80\x99s statement to the informant, concededly elicited in violation of the Sixth Amendment, was admissible to impeach his inconsistent testimony at trial. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Whether a confession that was not admissible in the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s case in chief nonetheless can be admitted for impeachment purposes depends on the nature of the constitutional guarantee violated. The Fifth Amendment guarantee against compelled self-incrimination is violated by introducing a coerced confession at trial, whether by way of impeachment or otherwise. New Jersey v. Portash, 440 U. S. 450, 458\xe2\x80\x93459. But for the Fourth Amendment guarantee against unreasonable searches or seizures, where exclusion comes by way of deterrent sanction rather than to avoid violation of the substantive guarantee, admissibility is determined by an exclusionary-rule balancing test. See Walder v. United States, 347 U. S. 62, 65. The same is true for violations of the Fifth and Sixth Amendment prophylactic rules forbidding certain pretrial police conduct. See, e.g., Harris v. New York, 401 U. S. 222, 225\xe2\x80\x93226. The core of the Sixth Amendment right to counsel is a trial right, but the right covers pretrial interrogations to ensure that police manipulation does not deprive the defendant of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98effective representation by counsel at the only stage when legal aid and advice would help him.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Massiah v. United States, 377 U. S. 201, 204. This right to be free of uncounseled interrogation is infringed at the time of the interrogation, not when it is admitted into evidence. It is that deprivation that demands the remedy of exclusion from the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s case in chief. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The interests safeguarded by excluding tainted evidence for impeachment purposes are \xe2\x80\x9coutweighed by the need to prevent perjury and to assure the integrity of the trial process.\xe2\x80\x9d Stone v. Powell, 428 U. S. 465, 488. Once the defendant testifies inconsistently, denying the prosecution \xe2\x80\x9cthe traditional truth-testing devices of the adversary process,\xe2\x80\x9d Harris, supra, at 225, is a high price to pay for vindicating the right to counsel at the prior stage. On the other hand, preventing impeachment use of statements taken in violation of Massiah would add little appreciable deterrence for officers, who have an incentive to comply with the Constitution, since statements lawfully obtained can be used for all purposes, not simply impeachment. In every other context, this Court has held that tainted evidence is admissible for impeachment. See, e.g., Oregon v. Hass, 420 U. S. 714, 723. No distinction here alters that balance. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

285 Kan. 595, 176 P.\xc2\xa03d 920, reversed and remanded.

\nOPINION OF THE COURT
\nKANSAS V. VENTRIS
\n556 U. S. ____ (2009)
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 07-1356
\n
\n

KANSAS, PETITIONER v. DONNIE RAY VENTRIS

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of\nkansas

\n

[April 29, 2009]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We address in this case the question whether a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s incriminating statement to a jailhouse informant, concededly elicited in violation of Sixth Amendment strictures, is admissible at trial to impeach the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conflicting statement.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the early hours of January 7, 2004, after two days of no sleep and some drug use, Rhonda Theel and respondent Donnie Ray Ventris reached an ill-conceived agreement to confront Ernest Hicks in his home. The couple testified that the aim of the visit was simply to investigate rumors that Hicks abused children, but the couple may have been inspired by the potential for financial gain: Theel had recently learned that Hicks carried large amounts of cash.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The encounter did not end well. One or both of the pair shot and killed Hicks with shots from a .38-caliber revolver, and the companions drove off in Hicks\xe2\x80\x99s truck with approximately $300 of his money and his cell phone. On receiving a tip from two friends of the couple who had helped transport them to Hicks\xe2\x80\x99s home, officers arrested Ventris and Theel and charged them with various crimes, chief among them murder and aggravated robbery. The State dropped the murder charge against Theel in exchange for her guilty plea to the robbery charge and her testimony identifying Ventris as the shooter.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Prior to trial, officers planted an informant in Ventris\xe2\x80\x99s holding cell, instructing him to \xe2\x80\x9ckeep [his] ear open and listen\xe2\x80\x9d for incriminating statements. App. 146. According to the informant, in response to his statement that Ventris appeared to have \xe2\x80\x9csomething more serious weighing in on his mind,\xe2\x80\x9d Ventris divulged that \xe2\x80\x9c[h]e\xe2\x80\x99d shot this man in his head and in his chest\xe2\x80\x9d and taken \xe2\x80\x9chis keys, his wallet, about $350.00, and \xe2\x80\xa6 a vehicle.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 154, 150.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At trial, Ventris took the stand and blamed the robbery and shooting entirely on Theel. The government sought to call the informant, to testify to Ventris\xe2\x80\x99s prior contradictory statement; Ventris objected. The State conceded that there was \xe2\x80\x9cprobably a violation\xe2\x80\x9d of Ventris\xe2\x80\x99s Sixth Amendment right to counsel but nonetheless argued that the statement was admissible for impeachment purposes because the violation \xe2\x80\x9cdoesn\xe2\x80\x99t give the Defendant \xe2\x80\xa6 a license to just get on the stand and lie.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 143. The trial court agreed and allowed the informant\xe2\x80\x99s testimony, but instructed the jury to \xe2\x80\x9cconsider with caution\xe2\x80\x9d all testimony given in exchange for benefits from the State. Id., at 30. The jury ultimately acquitted Ventris of felony murder and misdemeanor theft but returned a guilty verdict on the aggravated burglary and aggravated robbery counts.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Kansas Supreme Court reversed the conviction, holding that \xe2\x80\x9c[o]nce a criminal prosecution has commenced, the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s statements made to an undercover informant surreptitiously acting as an agent for the State are not admissible at trial for any reason, including the impeachment of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s testimony.\xe2\x80\x9d 285 Kan. 595, 606, 176 P.\xc2\xa03d 920, 928 (2008). Chief Justice McFarland dissented, id., at 611, 176 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 930. We granted the State\xe2\x80\x99s petition for certiorari, 554 U. S. ___ (2008).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Sixth Amendment, applied to the States through the Fourteenth Amendment, guarantees that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n all criminal prosecutions, the accused shall \xe2\x80\xa6 have the Assistance of Counsel for his defence.\xe2\x80\x9d The core of this right has historically been, and remains today, \xe2\x80\x9cthe opportunity for a defendant to consult with an attorney and to have him investigate the case and prepare a defense for trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Michigan v. Harvey, 494 U. S. 344, 348 (1990). We have held, however, that the right extends to having counsel present at various pretrial \xe2\x80\x9ccritical\xe2\x80\x9d interactions between the defendant and the State, United States v. Wade, 388 U. S. 218, 224 (1967), including the deliberate elicitation by law enforcement officers (and their agents) of statements pertaining to the charge, Massiah v. United States, 377 U. S. 201, 206 (1964). The State has conceded throughout these proceedings that Ventris\xe2\x80\x99s confession was taken in violation of Massiah\xe2\x80\x99s dictates and was therefore not admissible in the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s case in chief. Without affirming that this concession was necessary, see Kuhlmann v. Wilson, 477 U. S. 436, 459\xe2\x80\x93460 (1986), we accept it as the law of the case. The only question we answer today is whether the State must bear the additional consequence of inability to counter Ventris\xe2\x80\x99s contradictory testimony by placing the informant on the stand.

\n

A

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Whether otherwise excluded evidence can be admitted for purposes of impeachment depends upon the nature of the constitutional guarantee that is violated. Sometimes that explicitly mandates exclusion from trial, and sometimes it does not. The Fifth Amendment guarantees that no person shall be compelled to give evidence against himself, and so is violated whenever a truly coerced confession is introduced at trial, whether by way of impeachment or otherwise. New Jersey v. Portash, 440 U. S. 450, 458\xe2\x80\x93459 (1979). The Fourth Amendment, on the other hand, guarantees that no person shall be subjected to unreasonable searches or seizures, and says nothing about excluding their fruits from evidence; exclusion comes by way of deterrent sanction rather than to avoid violation of the substantive guarantee. Inadmissibility has not been automatic, therefore, but we have instead applied an exclusionary-rule balancing test. See Walder v. United States, 347 U. S. 62, 65 (1954). The same is true for violations of the Fifth and Sixth Amendment prophylactic rules forbidding certain pretrial police conduct. See Harris v. New York, 401 U. S. 222, 225\xe2\x80\x93226 (1971); Harvey, supra, at 348\xe2\x80\x93350.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent argues that the Sixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s right to counsel is a \xe2\x80\x9cright an accused is to enjoy a[t] trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 11. The core of the right to counsel is indeed a trial right, ensuring that the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s case is subjected to \xe2\x80\x9cthe crucible of meaningful adversarial testing.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Cronic, 466 U. S. 648, 656 (1984). See also Powell v. Alabama, 287 U. S. 45, 57\xe2\x80\x9358 (1932). But our opinions under the Sixth Amendment, as under the Fifth, have held that the right covers pretrial interrogations to ensure that police manipulation does not render counsel entirely impotent\xe2\x80\x94depriving the defendant of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98effective representation by counsel at the only stage when legal aid and advice would help him.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Massiah, supra, at 204 (quoting Spano v. New York, 360 U. S. 315, 326 (1959) (Douglas, J., concurring)). See also Miranda v. Arizona, 384 U. S. 436, 468\xe2\x80\x93469 (1966).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our opinion in Massiah, to be sure, was equivocal on what precisely constituted the violation. It quoted various authorities indicating that the violation occurred at the moment of the postindictment interrogation because such questioning \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98contravenes the basic dictates of fairness in the conduct of criminal causes.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 377 U. S., at 205 (quoting People v. Waterman, 9 N.\xc2\xa0Y. 2d 561, 565, 175 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 445, 448 (1961)). But the opinion later suggested that the violation occurred only when the improperly obtained evidence was \xe2\x80\x9cused against [the defendant] at his trial.\xe2\x80\x9d 377 U. S., at 206\xe2\x80\x93207. That question was irrelevant to the decision in Massiah in any event. Now that we are confronted with the question, we conclude that the Massiah right is a right to be free of uncounseled interrogation, and is infringed at the time of the interrogation. That, we think, is when the \xe2\x80\x9cAssistance of Counsel\xe2\x80\x9d is denied.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is illogical to say that the right is not violated until trial counsel\xe2\x80\x99s task of opposing conviction has been undermined by the statement\xe2\x80\x99s admission into evidence. A defendant is not denied counsel merely because the prosecution has been permitted to introduce evidence of guilt\xe2\x80\x94even evidence so overwhelming that the attorney\xe2\x80\x99s job of gaining an acquittal is rendered impossible. In such circumstances the accused continues to enjoy the assistance of counsel; the assistance is simply not worth much. The assistance of counsel has been denied, however, at the prior critical stage which produced the inculpatory evidence. Our cases acknowledge that reality in holding that the stringency of the warnings necessary for a waiver of the assistance of counsel varies according to \xe2\x80\x9cthe usefulness of counsel to the accused at the particular [pretrial] proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d Patterson v. Illinois, 487 U. S. 285, 298 (1988). It is that deprivation which demands a remedy.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The United States insists that \xe2\x80\x9cpost-charge deliberate elicitation of statements without the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s counsel or a valid waiver of counsel is not intrinsically unlawful.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 17, n.\xc2\xa04. That is true when the questioning is unrelated to charged crimes\xe2\x80\x94the Sixth Amendment right is \xe2\x80\x9coffense specific,\xe2\x80\x9d McNeil v. Wisconsin, 501 U. S. 171, 175 (1991). We have never said, however, that officers may badger counseled defendants about charged crimes so long as they do not use information they gain. The constitutional violation occurs when the uncounseled interrogation is conducted.

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case does not involve, therefore, the prevention of a constitutional violation, but rather the scope of the remedy for a violation that has already occurred. Our precedents make clear that the game of excluding tainted evidence for impeachment purposes is not worth the candle. The interests safeguarded by such exclusion are \xe2\x80\x9coutweighed by the need to prevent perjury and to assure the integrity of the trial process.\xe2\x80\x9d Stone v. Powell, 428 U. S. 465, 488 (1976). \xe2\x80\x9cIt is one thing to say that the Government cannot make an affirmative use of evidence unlawfully obtained. It is quite another to say that the defendant can \xe2\x80\xa6 provide himself with a shield against contradiction of his untruths.\xe2\x80\x9d Walder, supra, at 65. Once the defendant testifies in a way that contradicts prior statements, denying the prosecution use of \xe2\x80\x9cthe traditional truth-testing devices of the adversary process,\xe2\x80\x9d Harris, supra, at 225, is a high price to pay for vindication of the right to counsel at the prior stage.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On the other side of the scale, preventing impeachment use of statements taken in violation of Massiah would add little appreciable deterrence. Officers have significant incentive to ensure that they and their informants comply with the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s demands, since statements lawfully obtained can be used for all purposes rather than simply for impeachment. And the ex ante probability that evidence gained in violation of Massiah would be of use for impeachment is exceedingly small. An investigator would have to anticipate both that the defendant would choose to testify at trial (an unusual occurrence to begin with) and that he would testify inconsistently despite the admissibility of his prior statement for impeachment. Not likely to happen\xe2\x80\x94or at least not likely enough to risk squandering the opportunity of using a properly obtained statement for the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s case in chief.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In any event, even if \xe2\x80\x9cthe officer may be said to have little to lose and perhaps something to gain by way of possibly uncovering impeachment material,\xe2\x80\x9d we have multiple times rejected the argument that this \xe2\x80\x9cspeculative possibility\xe2\x80\x9d can trump the costs of allowing perjurious statements to go unchallenged. Oregon v. Hass, 420 U. S. 714, 723 (1975). We have held in every other context that tainted evidence\xe2\x80\x94evidence whose very introduction does not constitute the constitutional violation, but whose obtaining was constitutionally invalid\xe2\x80\x94is admissible for impeachment. See ibid.; Walder, 347 U. S., at 65; Harris, 401 U. S., at 226; Harvey, 494 U. S., at 348. We see no distinction that would alter the balance here.*

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We hold that the informant\xe2\x80\x99s testimony, concededly elicited in violation of the Sixth Amendment, was admissible to challenge Ventris\xe2\x80\x99s inconsistent testimony at trial. The judgment of the Kansas Supreme Court is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n

*\xc2\xa0Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s amicus insists that jailhouse snitches are so inherently unreliable that this Court should craft a broader exclusionary rule for uncorroborated statements obtained by that means. Brief for National Association of Criminal Defense Lawyers 25\xe2\x80\x9326. Our legal system, however, is built on the premise that it is the province of the jury to weigh the credibility of competing witnesses, and we have long purported to avoid \xe2\x80\x9cestablish[ing] this Court as a rule-making organ for the promulgation of state rules of criminal procedure.\xe2\x80\x9d Spencer v. Texas, 385 U. S. 554, 564 (1967). It would be especially inappropriate to fabricate such a rule in this case, where it appears the jury took to heart the trial judge\xe2\x80\x99s cautionary instruction on the unreliability of rewarded informant testimony by acquitting Ventris of felony murder.

\nSTEVENS, J., DISSENTING

KANSAS, PETITIONER v. DONNIE RAY VENTRIS

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of\nkansas

\n

[April 29, 2009]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens, with whom Justice Ginsburg joins, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Michigan v. Harvey, 494 U. S. 344 (1990), the Court held that a statement obtained from a defendant in violation of the Sixth Amendment could be used to impeach his testimony at trial. As I explained in a dissent joined by three other Members of the Court, that holding eroded the principle that \xe2\x80\x9cthose who are entrusted with the power of government have the same duty to respect and obey the law as the ordinary citizen.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 369. It was my view then, as it is now, that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Sixth Amendment is violated when the fruits of the State\xe2\x80\x99s impermissible encounter with the represented defendant are used for impeachment just as it is when the fruits are used in the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s case in chief.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 355.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this case, the State has conceded that it violated the Sixth Amendment as interpreted in Massiah v. United States, 377 U. S. 201, 206 (1964), when it used a jailhouse informant to elicit a statement from the defendant. No Miranda warnings were given to the defendant,[Footnote 1] nor was he otherwise alerted to the fact that he was speaking to a state agent. Even though the jury apparently did not credit the informant\xe2\x80\x99s testimony, the Kansas Supreme Court correctly concluded that the prosecution should not be allowed to exploit its pretrial constitutional violation during the trial itself. The Kansas Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment should be affirmed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court\xe2\x80\x99s contrary holding relies on the view that a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s pretrial right to counsel is merely \xe2\x80\x9cprophylactic\xe2\x80\x9d in nature. See ante, at 4. The majority argues that any violation of this prophylactic right occurs solely at the time the State subjects a counseled defendant to an uncounseled interrogation, not when the fruits of the encounter are used against the defendant at trial. Ante, at 5. This reasoning is deeply flawed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The pretrial right to counsel is not ancillary to, or of lesser importance than, the right to rely on counsel at trial. The Sixth Amendment grants the right to counsel \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n all criminal prosecutions,\xe2\x80\x9d and we have long recognized that the right applies in periods before trial commences, see United States v. Wade, 388 U. S. 218, 224 (1967). We have never endorsed the notion that the pretrial right to counsel stands at the periphery of the Sixth Amendment. To the contrary, we have explained that the pretrial period is \xe2\x80\x9cperhaps the most critical period of the proceedings\xe2\x80\x9d during which a defendant \xe2\x80\x9crequires the guiding hand of counsel.\xe2\x80\x9d Powell v. Alabama, 287 U. S. 45, 57, 69 (1932); see Maine v. Moulton, 474 U. S. 159, 176 (1985) (recognizing the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cright to rely on counsel as a \xe2\x80\x98medium\xe2\x80\x99 between him and the State\xe2\x80\x9d in all critical stages of prosecution). Placing the prophylactic label on a core Sixth Amendment right mischaracterizes the sweep of the constitutional guarantee.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Treating the State\xe2\x80\x99s actions in this case as a violation of a prophylactic right, the Court concludes that introducing the illegally obtained evidence at trial does not itself violate the Constitution. I strongly disagree. While the constitutional breach began at the time of interrogation, the State\xe2\x80\x99s use of that evidence at trial compounded the violation. The logic that compels the exclusion of the evidence during the State\xe2\x80\x99s case in chief extends to any attempt by the State to rely on the evidence, even for impeachment. The use of ill-gotten evidence during any phase of criminal prosecution does damage to the adversarial process\xe2\x80\x94the fairness of which the Sixth Amendment was designed to protect. See Strickland v. Washington, 466 U. S. 668, 685 (1984); see also Adams v. United States ex rel. McCann, 317 U. S. 269, 276 (1942) (\xe2\x80\x9c[The] procedural devices rooted in experience were written into the Bill of Rights not as abstract rubrics in an elegant code but in order to assure fairness and justice before any person could be deprived of \xe2\x80\x98life, liberty, or property\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When counsel is excluded from a critical pretrial interaction between the defendant and the State, she may be unable to effectively counter the potentially devastating, and potentially false,[Footnote 2] evidence subsequently introduced at trial. Inexplicably, today\xe2\x80\x99s Court refuses to recognize that this is a constitutional harm.[Footnote 3] Yet in Massiah, the Court forcefully explained that a defendant is \xe2\x80\x9cdenied the basic protections of the [Sixth Amendment] guarantee when there [is] used against him at his trial evidence of his own incriminating words\xe2\x80\x9d that were \xe2\x80\x9cdeliberately elicited from him after he had been indicted and in the absence of counsel.\xe2\x80\x9d 377 U. S., at 206. Sadly, the majority has retreated from this robust understanding of the right to counsel.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision is lamentable not only because of its flawed underpinnings, but also because it is another occasion in which the Court has privileged the prosecution at the expense of the Constitution. Permitting the State to cut corners in criminal proceedings taxes the legitimacy of the entire criminal process. \xe2\x80\x9cThe State\xe2\x80\x99s interest in truthseeking is congruent with the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s interest in representation by counsel, for it is an elementary premise of our system of criminal justice \xe2\x80\x98that partisan advocacy on both sides of a case will best promote the ultimate objective that the guilty be convicted and the innocent go free.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Harvey, 494 U. S., at 357 (Stevens, J., dissenting) (quoting United States v. Cronic, 466 U. S. 648, 655 (1984)). Although the Court may not be concerned with the use of ill-gotten evidence in derogation of the right to counsel, I remain convinced that such shabby tactics are intolerable in all cases. I respectfully dissent.
