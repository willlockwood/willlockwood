SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

\n

RANDY WHITE, WARDEN v. ROGER L. WHEELER

\n

on petition for writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

No. 14\xe2\x80\x931372.\xe2\x80\x83Decided December 14, 2015

\n

\n

Per Curiam.

\n

A death sentence imposed by a Kentucky trial court and affirmed by the Kentucky Supreme Court has been overturned, on habeas corpus review, by the Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit. During the jury selection process, the state trial court excused a juror after concluding he could not give sufficient assurance of neutrality or impartiality in considering whether the death penalty should be imposed. The Court of Appeals, despite the substantial deference it must accord to state-court rulings in federal habeas proceedings, determined that excusing the juror in the circumstances of this case violated the Sixth and\n \nFourteenth Amendments. That ruling contravenes controlling precedents from this Court, and it is now necessary to reverse the Court of Appeals by this summary disposition.

\n

Warden Randy White is the petitioner here, and the convicted prisoner, Roger Wheeler, is the respondent.

\n

In October 1997, police in Louisville, Kentucky, found the bodies of Nigel Malone and Nairobi Warfield in the apartment the couple shared. Malone had been stabbed nine times. Warfield had been strangled to death and a pair of scissors stuck out from her neck. She was pregnant. DNA taken from blood at the crime scene matched respondent\xe2\x80\x99s. Respondent was charged with the murders.

\n

During voir dire, Juror 638 gave equivocal and inconsistent answers when questioned about whether he could consider voting to impose the death penalty. In response to the judge\xe2\x80\x99s questions about his personal beliefs on the death penalty, Juror 638 said, \xe2\x80\x9cI\xe2\x80\x99m not sure that I have formed an opinion one way or the other. I believe there are arguments on both sides of the\xe2\x80\x94of it.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 126a. When asked by the prosecution about his ability to consider all available penalties, Juror 638 noted he had \xe2\x80\x9cnever been confronted with that situation in a, in a real-life sense of having to make that kind of determination.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 131a. \xe2\x80\x9cSo it\xe2\x80\x99s difficult for me,\xe2\x80\x9d he explained, \xe2\x80\x9cto judge how I would I guess act, uh.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The prosecution sought to clarify Juror 638\xe2\x80\x99s answer, asking if the juror meant he was \xe2\x80\x9cnot absolutely certain whether [he] could realistically consider\xe2\x80\x9d the death penalty. Id., at 132a. Juror 638 replied, \xe2\x80\x9cI think that would be the most accurate way I could answer your question.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. During defense counsel\xe2\x80\x99s examination, Juror 638 described himself as \xe2\x80\x9ca bit more contemplative on the issue of taking a life and, uh, whether or not we have the right to take that life.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 133a. Later, however, he expressed his belief that he could consider all the penalty options. Id., at 134a.

\n

The prosecution moved to strike Juror 638 for cause based on his inconsistent replies, as illustrated by his statement that he was not absolutely certain he could realistically consider the death penalty. The defense opposed the motion, arguing that Juror 638\xe2\x80\x99s answers indicated his ability to consider all the penalty options, despite having some reservations about the death penalty. The judge said that when she was done questioning Juror 638, she wrote in her notes that the juror \xe2\x80\x9c \xe2\x80\x98could consider [the] entire range\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9d of penalties. Id., at 138a. She further stated that she did not \xe2\x80\x9csee him as problematic\xe2\x80\x9d at the end of her examination. Ibid. But she also noted that she did not \xe2\x80\x9chear him say that he couldn\xe2\x80\x99t realistically consider the death penalty,\xe2\x80\x9d and reserved ruling on the motion until she could review Juror 638\xe2\x80\x99s testimony. Ibid. The next day, after reviewing the relevant testimony, the judge struck Juror 638 for cause. When she announced her decision to excuse the juror, the trial judge stated, \xe2\x80\x9cAnd when I went back and reviewed [the juror\xe2\x80\x99s] entire testimony, [the prosecution] concluded with saying, \xe2\x80\x98Would it be accurate to say that you couldn\xe2\x80\x99t, couldn\xe2\x80\x99t consider the entire range?\xe2\x80\x99 And his response is\xe2\x80\x94I think was, \xe2\x80\x98I think that would be pretty accurate.\xe2\x80\x99 So, I\xe2\x80\x99m going to sustain that one, too.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 139a\xe2\x80\x93140a.

\n

The case proceeded to trial. Respondent was convicted of both murders and sentenced to death. The Kentucky Supreme Court affirmed the convictions and the sentence. Wheeler v. Commonwealth, 121 S. W. 3d 173, 189 (2003). In considering respondent\xe2\x80\x99s challenges to the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s excusal of certain jurors for cause, the Kentucky Supreme Court held that the trial judge \xe2\x80\x9cappropriately struck for cause those jurors that could not impose the death pen-alty. . . . There was no error and the rights of the defendant to a fair trial by a fair and impartial jury . . . under both the federal and state constitutions were not violated.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 179.

\n

After exhausting available state postconviction procedures, respondent sought a writ of habeas corpus under\n\n28 U. S. C. \xc2\xa72254 from the United States District Court for the Western District of Kentucky. He asserted, inter alia, that the Kentucky trial court erred in striking Juror 638 during voir dire on the ground that the juror could not give assurances that he could consider the death penalty as a sentencing option. The District Court dismissed the petition; but a divided panel of the Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit reversed, granting habeas relief as to respondent\xe2\x80\x99s sentence. Wheeler v. Simpson, 779 F. 3d 366, 379 (2015). While acknowledging the deferential standard required on federal habeas review of a state conviction, the Court of Appeals held that allowing the exclusion of Juror 638 was an unreasonable application of Witherspoon v. Illinois,\n\n391 U. S. 510 (1968)\n, Wainwright v. Witt,\n\n469 U. S. 412 (1985)\n, and their progeny. 779 F. 3d, at 372\xe2\x80\x93374.

\n

Under the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA), habeas relief is authorized if the state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision \xe2\x80\x9cwas contrary to, or involved an unreasonable application of, clearly established Federal law, as determined by the Supreme Court of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n28 U. S. C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1). This Court, time and again, has instructed that AEDPA, by setting forth necessary predicates before state-court judgments may be set aside, \xe2\x80\x9cerects a formidable barrier to federal habeas relief for prisoners whose claims have been adjudicated in state court.\xe2\x80\x9d Burt v. Titlow, 571 U. S. ___, ___ (2013) (slip op., at 6). Under \xc2\xa72254(d)(1), \xe2\x80\x9c \xe2\x80\x98a state prisoner must show that the state court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling on the claim being presented in federal court was so lacking in justification that there was an error well understood and comprehended in existing law beyond any possibility for fairminded disagreement.\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9d White v. Woodall, 572 U. S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 4) (quoting Harrington v. Richter,\n\n562 U. S. 86,\n103 (2011)\n).

\n

The Court of Appeals was required to apply this deferential standard to the state court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s juror exclusion claim. In Witherspoon, this Court set forth the rule for juror disqualification in capital cases. Witherspoon recognized that the\n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s guarantee of an impartial jury confers on capital defendants the right to a jury not \xe2\x80\x9cuncommonly willing to condemn a man to die.\xe2\x80\x9d 391 U. S., at 521. But the Court with equal clarity has acknowledged the State\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cstrong interest in having jurors who are able to apply capital punishment within the framework state law prescribes.\xe2\x80\x9d Uttecht v. Brown,\n\n551 U. S. 1,\n9 (2007)\n. To ensure the proper balance between these two interests, only \xe2\x80\x9ca juror who is substantially impaired in his or her ability to impose the death penalty under the state-law framework can be excused for cause.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. As the Court explained in Witt, a juror may be excused for cause \xe2\x80\x9cwhere the trial judge is left with the definite impression that a prospective juror would be unable to faithfully and impartially apply the law.\xe2\x80\x9d 469 U. S., at 425\xe2\x80\x93426.

\n

Reviewing courts owe deference to a trial court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling on whether to strike a particular juror \xe2\x80\x9cregardless of whether the trial court engages in explicit analysis regarding substantial impairment; even the granting of a motion to excuse for cause constitutes an implicit finding of bias.\xe2\x80\x9d Uttecht, 551 U. S., at 7. A trial court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cfinding may be upheld even in the absence of clear statements from the juror that he or she is impaired . . . .\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. And where, as here, the federal courts review a state-court ruling under the constraints imposed by AEDPA, the federal court must accord an additional and \xe2\x80\x9cindependent, high standard\xe2\x80\x9d of deference. Id., at 10. As a result, federal habeas reviewof a Witherspoon-Witt claim\xe2\x80\x94much like federal habeas review of an ineffective-assistance-of-counsel claim\xe2\x80\x94must be \xe2\x80\x9c \xe2\x80\x98 \xe2\x80\x9cdoubly deferential.\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9d Burt, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 1) (quoting Cullen v. Pinholster,\n\n563 U. S. 170,\n190 (2011)\n).

\n

The Court of Appeals held that the Kentucky Supreme Court unreasonably applied Witherspoon, Witt, and their progeny when it determined that removing Juror 638 for cause was constitutional. 779 F. 3d, at 372\xe2\x80\x93374. The Court of Appeals determined Juror 638 \xe2\x80\x9cunderstood the decisions he would face and engaged with them in a thoughtful, honest, and conscientious manner.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 373. In the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 estimation, the trial judge concluded the juror was not qualified only by \xe2\x80\x9cmisapprehending a single question and answer exchange\xe2\x80\x9d between Juror 638 and the prosecution, id., at 374\xe2\x80\x94the exchange in which Juror 638 stated he was not absolutely certain he could realistically consider the death penalty, id., at 372. According to the Court of Appeals, Juror 638 \xe2\x80\x9cagreed he did not know to an absolute certainty whether he could realistically consider the death penalty, but the court proceeded as if he knew he could not.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The Court of Appeals further determined that if the trial judge, when reviewing Juror 638\xe2\x80\x99s examination, had \xe2\x80\x9cproperly processed that exchange\xe2\x80\x9d between Juror 638 and the prosecution, Juror 638 would not have been excused. Id., at 374.

\n

Both the analysis and the conclusion in the decision under review were incorrect. While the Court of Appeals acknowledged that deference was required under AEDPA, it failed to ask the critical question: Was the Kentucky Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to affirm the excusal of Juror 638 for cause \xe2\x80\x9c \xe2\x80\x98so lacking in justification that there was an error well understood and comprehended in existing law beyond any possibility for fairminded disagreement\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9d? Woodall, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 4) (quoting Harrington, supra, at 103).

\n

The Court of Appeals did not properly apply the deference it was required to accord the state-court ruling. A fairminded jurist could readily conclude that the trial judge\xe2\x80\x99s exchange with Juror 638 reflected a \xe2\x80\x9cdiligent and thoughtful voir dire\xe2\x80\x9d; that she considered with care the juror\xe2\x80\x99s testimony; and that she was fair in the exercise of her \xe2\x80\x9cbroad discretion\xe2\x80\x9d in determining whether the juror was qualified to serve in this capital case. Uttecht, 551 U. S., at 20. Juror 638\xe2\x80\x99s answers during voir dire were at least ambiguous as to whether he would be able to give appropriate consideration to imposing the death penalty. And as this Court made clear in Uttecht, \xe2\x80\x9cwhen there is ambiguity in the prospective juror\xe2\x80\x99s statements,\xe2\x80\x9d the trial court is \xe2\x80\x9c \xe2\x80\x98entitled to resolve it in favor of the State.\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 7 (quoting Witt, supra, at 434).

\n

The Court of Appeals erred in its assessment of the trial judge\xe2\x80\x99s reformulation of an important part of Juror 638\xe2\x80\x99s questioning. 779 F. 3d, at 372. When excusing the juror the day after the voir dire, the trial judge said that the prosecution had asked whether the juror \xe2\x80\x9ccouldn\xe2\x80\x99t con-sider the entire range\xe2\x80\x9d of penalties. App. to Pet. for Cert. 139a. The prosecution in fact asked if the juror was \xe2\x80\x9cnot absolutely certain whether [he] could realistically con-sider\xe2\x80\x9d the entire range of penalties. Id., at 132a. The juror\xe2\x80\x99s confirmation that he was \xe2\x80\x9cnot absolutely certain whether [he] could realistically consider\xe2\x80\x9d the death penalty, ibid., was a reasonable basis for the trial judge to conclude that the juror was unable to give that penalty fair consideration. The trial judge\xe2\x80\x99s decision to excuse Juror 638 did not violate clearly established federal law by concluding that Juror 638 was not qualified to serve as a member ofthis capital jury. See Witt, supra, at 424\xe2\x80\x93426. And similarly, the Kentucky Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling that therewas no error is not beyond any possibility for fairminded disagreement.

\n

The Court of Appeals noted that the deference toward trial courts recognized in Uttecht \xe2\x80\x9cwas largely premised on the trial judge\xe2\x80\x99s ability to \xe2\x80\x98observe the demeanor of\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9d the juror. 779 F. 3d, at 373 (quoting 551 U. S., at 17). It concluded that deference to the trial court here supported habeas relief, because the trial judge\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cinitial assessment of [the juror\xe2\x80\x99s] answers and demeanor\xe2\x80\x9d did not lead her to immediately strike Juror 638 for cause. 779 F. 3d, at 373\xe2\x80\x93374.

\n

The Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 conclusion conflicts with the meaning and holding of Uttecht and with a common-sense understanding of the jury selection process. Nothing in Uttecht limits the trial court to evaluating demeanor alone and not the substance of a juror\xe2\x80\x99s response. And the implicit suggestion that a trial judge is entitled to less deference for having deliberated after her initial ruling is wrong. In the ordinary case the conclusion should be quite the opposite. It is true that a trial court\xe2\x80\x99s contemporaneous assessment of a juror\xe2\x80\x99s demeanor, and its bearing on how to interpret or understand the juror\xe2\x80\x99s responses, are entitled to substantial deference; but a trial court ruling is likewise entitled to deference when made after a careful review of a formal transcript or recording. If the trial judge chooses to reflect and deliberate further, as this trial judge did after the proceedings recessed for the day, that is not to be faulted; it is to be commended.

\n

This is not a case where \xe2\x80\x9cthe record discloses no basis for a finding of substantial impairment.\xe2\x80\x9d Uttecht, supra, at 20. The two federal judges in the majority below might have reached a different conclusion had they been presiding over this voir dire. But simple disagreement does not overcome the two layers of deference owed by a federal habeas court in this context.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

The Kentucky Supreme Court was not unreasonable in its application of clearly established federal law when it concluded that the exclusion of Juror 638 did not violate the\n \nSixth Amendment. Given this conclusion, there is no need to consider petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s further contention that, if there were an error by the trial court in excluding the juror, it should be subject to harmless-error analysis. And this Court does not review the other rulings of the Court of Appeals that are not addressed in this opinion.

\n

As a final matter, this Court again advises the Court of Appeals that the provisions of AEDPA apply with full force even when reviewing a conviction and sentence imposing the death penalty. See, e.g., Parker v. Matthews, 567 U. S. ___ (2012) (per curiam); Bobby v. Dixon, 565 U. S. ___ (2011) (per curiam); Bobby v. Mitts,\n\n563 U. S. 395 (2011)\n (per curiam); Bobby v. Van Hook,\n\n558 U. S. 4 (2009)\n (per curiam).

\n

The petition for certiorari and respondent\xe2\x80\x99s motion to proceed in forma pauperis are granted. The judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.
