\nSYLLABUS
\nOCTOBER TERM, 2007
\nROWE V. NEW HAMPSHIRE MOTOR TRANSP. ASSN.
\n
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\n
\n

ROWE, ATTORNEY GENERAL OF MAINE v. NEW HAMPSHIRE MOTOR TRANSPORT ASSOCIATION et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the first circuit

\n

No. 06\xe2\x80\x93457.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 28, 2007\xe2\x80\x94Decided February 20, 2008

\n

Although a provision of the Federal Aviation Administration Authorization Act of 1994 forbids States to \xe2\x80\x9cenact or enforce a law \xe2\x80\xa6 related to a price, route, or service of any motor carrier,\xe2\x80\x9d 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa714501(c)(1), see also \xc2\xa741713(b)(4)(a), Maine adopted a law which, inter alia, (1) specifies that a state-licensed tobacco shipper must utilize a delivery company that provides a recipient-verification service that confirms the buyer is of legal age, and (2) adds, in prohibiting unlicensed tobacco shipments into the State, that a person is deemed to know that a package contains tobacco if it is marked as originating from a Maine-licensed tobacco retailer or if it is received from someone whose name appears on an official list of un-licensed tobacco retailers distributed to package-delivery companies. In respondent carrier associations\xe2\x80\x99 suit, the District Court and the First Circuit agreed with respondents that Maine\xe2\x80\x99s recipient-verification and deemed-to-know provisions were pre-empted by federal law.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Federal law pre-empts the two state-law provisions at issue. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0In interpreting the 1994 federal Act, the Court follows Morales v. Trans World Airlines, Inc., 504 U. S. 374, 378, in which it interpreted similar language in the pre-emption provision of the Airline Deregulation Act of 1978. Voiding state enforcement of consumer fraud statutes against deceptive airline-fare advertisements, Morales determined, inter alia, that the federal Act pre-empted state actions having a \xe2\x80\x9cconnection with\xe2\x80\x9d carrier \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0rates, routes, or services,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 384; that pre-emption may occur even if a state law has only an indirect effect on rates, routes, or services, id., at 386; and that pre-emption occurs at least where state laws have a \xe2\x80\x9csignificant impact\xe2\x80\x9d related to Congress\xe2\x80\x99 deregulatory and pre-emption-related objectives, id., at 390. The Court also emphasized that the airline Act\xe2\x80\x99s overarching goal of helping assure that transportation rates, routes, and services reflects maximum reliance on competitive market forces, id., at 378, and stated that federal law might not pre-empt state laws affecting fares only tenuously, remotely, or peripherally, but did not say where, or how, it would draw the line on \xe2\x80\x9cborderline\xe2\x80\x9d questions, id., at 390. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0In light of Morales, the Maine laws at issue are pre-empted. In regulating delivery service procedures, the recipient-verification provision focuses on trucking and similar services, thereby creating a direct \xe2\x80\x9cconnection with\xe2\x80\x9d motor carrier services. See 504 U. S., at 384. It also has a \xe2\x80\x9csignificant\xe2\x80\x9d and adverse \xe2\x80\x9cimpact\xe2\x80\x9d in respect to the federal Act\xe2\x80\x99s ability to achieve its pre-emption-related objectives, id., at 390, because it requires carriers to offer a system of services that the market does not now provide (and which the carriers would prefer not to offer). Even were that not so, the law would freeze into place services that carriers might prefer to discontinue in the future, thereby producing the very effect the federal law sought to avoid, i.e., a State\xe2\x80\x99s direct substitution of its own governmental commands for \xe2\x80\x9ccompetitive market forces\xe2\x80\x9d in determining (to a significant degree) the services that motor carriers will provide. Id., at 378. Maine\xe2\x80\x99s deemed-to-know provision applies yet more directly to motor carrier services by creating a conclusive presumption of carrier knowledge that a shipment contains tobacco in the specified circumstances. That presumption means that the law imposes civil liability upon the carrier, not simply for its knowing transport of (unlicensed) tobacco, but for the carrier\xe2\x80\x99s failure sufficiently to examine every package. The provision thus requires the carrier to check each shipment for certain markings and to compare it against the list of proscribed shippers, thereby directly regulating a significant aspect of the motor carrier\xe2\x80\x99s package pick-up and delivery service and creating the kind of state-mandated regulation that the federal Act pre-empts. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Maine\xe2\x80\x99s primary arguments for an exception from pre-emption\xe2\x80\x94that its laws help prevent minors from obtaining cigarettes and thereby protect its citizens\xe2\x80\x99 public health\xe2\x80\x94are unavailing. The federal law does not create a public health exception, but, to the contrary, explicitly lists a set of exceptions that do not include public health. See, e.g., \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa714501(c)(2) to (c)(3). Nor does its legislative history mention specific state enforcement methods or suggest that Congress made a firm judgment about, or even focused upon, the issue here. Maine\xe2\x80\x99s inability to find significant support for such an exception is not surprising, given the number of States through which carriers travel, the number of products carried, the variety of potential adverse public health effects, the many different kinds of regulatory rules potentially available, and the difficulty of finding a legal criterion for separating permissible from impermissible public-health-oriented regulations. Although federal law does not generally pre-empt state public health regulation, the state laws at issue are not general, their impact on carrier rates, routes, or services is significant, and their connection with trucking is not tenuous, remote, or peripheral: They aim directly at the carriage of goods, a commercial field where carriage by commercial motor vehicles plays a major role. From the perspective of pre-emption, this case is no more \xe2\x80\x9cborderline\xe2\x80\x9d than was Morales. Maine\xe2\x80\x99s argument that to set aside its regulations will seriously harm its efforts to prevent minors from obtaining cigarettes is unpersuasive in light of other legislative alternatives available to the State. Regardless, given Morales\xe2\x80\x99 holding that federal law pre-empts state consumer-protection laws, federal law must also pre-empt Maine\xe2\x80\x99s efforts directly to regulate carrier services. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9311.

448 F.\xc2\xa03d 66, affirmed.

G. STEVEN ROWE, ATTORNEY GENERAL OF MAINE,\nPETITIONER v. NEW HAMPSHIRE MOTOR\nTRANSPORT ASSOCIATION et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Breyer delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We here consider whether a federal statute that prohibits States from enacting any law \xe2\x80\x9crelated to\xe2\x80\x9d a motor carrier \xe2\x80\x9cprice, route, or service\xe2\x80\x9d pre-empts two provisions of a Maine tobacco law, which regulate the delivery of tobacco to customers within the State. 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa714501(c)(1), 41713(b)(4)(A); see Me. Rev. Stat. Ann., Tit. 22, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71555\xe2\x80\x93C(3)(C), 1555\xe2\x80\x93D (second sentence) (2004). We hold that the federal law pre-empts both provisions.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1978, Congress \xe2\x80\x9cdetermin[ed] that \xe2\x80\x98maximum reliance on competitive market forces\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d would favor lower airline fares and better airline service, and it enacted the Airline Deregulation Act. Morales v. Trans World Airlines, Inc., 504 U. S. 374, 378 (1992) (quoting 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. App. \xc2\xa71302(a)(4) (1988 ed.)); see 92 Stat. 1705. In order to \xe2\x80\x9censure that the States would not undo federal deregulation with regulation of their own,\xe2\x80\x9d that Act \xe2\x80\x9cincluded a pre-emption provision\xe2\x80\x9d that said \xe2\x80\x9cno State \xe2\x80\xa6 shall enact or enforce any law \xe2\x80\xa6 relating to rates, routes, or services of any air carrier.\xe2\x80\x9d Morales, supra, at 378; 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. App. \xc2\xa71305(a)(1) (1988 ed.).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1980, Congress deregulated trucking. See Motor Carrier Act of 1980, 94 Stat. 793. And a little over a decade later, in 1994, Congress similarly sought to pre-empt state trucking regulation. See Federal Aviation Administration Authorization Act of 1994, 108 Stat. 1605\xe2\x80\x931606; see also ICC Termination Act of 1995, 109 Stat. 899. In doing so, it borrowed language from the Airline Deregulation Act of 1978 and wrote into its 1994 law language that says: \xe2\x80\x9c[A] State . . . may not enact or enforce a law . . . related to a price, route, or service of any motor carrier . . . with respect to the transportation of property.\xe2\x80\x9d 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa714501(c)(1); see also \xc2\xa741713(b)(4)(A) (similar provision for combined motor-air carriers).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The State of Maine subsequently adopted An Act To Regulate the Delivery and Sales of Tobacco Products and To Prevent the Sale of Tobacco Products to Minors, 2003 Me. Acts p. 1089, two sections of which are relevant here. The first section forbids anyone other than a Maine-licensed tobacco retailer to accept an order for delivery of tobacco. Me. Rev. Stat. Ann., Tit. 22, \xc2\xa71555\xe2\x80\x93C(1). It then adds that, when a licensed retailer accepts an order and ships tobacco, the retailer must \xe2\x80\x9cutilize a delivery service\xe2\x80\x9d that provides a special kind of recipient-verification service. \xc2\xa71555\xe2\x80\x93C(3)(C). The delivery service must make certain that (1) the person who bought the tobacco is the person to whom the package is addressed; (2) the person to whom the package is addressed is of legal age to purchase tobacco; (3) the person to whom the package is addressed has himself or herself signed for the package; and (4) the person to whom the package is addressed, if under the age of 27, has produced a valid government-issued photo identification with proof of age. Ibid. Violations are punishable by civil penalties. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71555\xe2\x80\x93C(3)(E) to C(3)(F) (first offense up to $1,500; subsequent offenses up to $5,000).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The second section forbids any person \xe2\x80\x9cknowingly\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9ctransport\xe2\x80\x9d a \xe2\x80\x9ctobacco product\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9ca person\xe2\x80\x9d in Maine unless either the sender or the receiver has a Maine license. \xc2\xa71555\xe2\x80\x93D. It then adds that a \xe2\x80\x9cperson is deemed to know that a package contains a tobacco product\xe2\x80\x9d (1) if the package is marked as containing tobacco and displays the name and license number of a Maine-licensed tobacco retailer; or (2) if the person receives the package from someone whose name appears on a list of un-licensed tobacco retailers that Maine\xe2\x80\x99s Attorney General distributes to various package-delivery companies. Ibid. (emphasis added); see also \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71555\xe2\x80\x93C(3)(B), 1555\xe2\x80\x93D(1). Violations are again punishable by civil penalties. \xc2\xa71555\xe2\x80\x93D(2) (up to $1,500 per violation against violator and/or violator\xe2\x80\x99s employer).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents, several transport carrier associations, brought this lawsuit in federal court, claiming that federal law pre-empts several sections of Maine\xe2\x80\x99s statute. The District Court held (among other things) that federal law pre-empts the portions of the two sections we have described, namely the \xe2\x80\x9crecipient-verification\xe2\x80\x9d provision (\xc2\xa71555\xe2\x80\x93C(3)(C)) and the \xe2\x80\x9cdeemed to know\xe2\x80\x9d provision (the second sentence of \xc2\xa71555\xe2\x80\x93D). See 377 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 197, 220 (Me. 2005). On appeal, the Court of Appeals for the First Circuit agreed that federal law pre-empted the two provisions. 448 F.\xc2\xa03d 66, 82 (2006). We granted certiorari to review these determinations. 551 U. S. ___ (2007).

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Morales, this Court interpreted the pre-emption provision in the Airline Deregulation Act of 1978. See 504 U. S., at 378. And we follow Morales in interpreting similar language in the 1994 Act before us here. We have said that \xe2\x80\x9cwhen judicial interpretations have settled the meaning of an existing statutory provision, repetition of the same language in a new statute indicates, as a general matter, the intent to incorporate its judicial interpretations as well.\xe2\x80\x9d Merrill Lynch, Pierce, Fenner & Smith Inc. v. Dabit, 547 U. S. 71, 85 (2006) (internal quotation marks and alterations omitted). Here, the Congress that wrote the language before us copied the language of the air-carrier pre-emption provision of the Airline Deregulation Act of 1978. Compare 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa714501(c)(1), 41713(b)(4)(A), with 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. App. \xc2\xa71305(a)(1) (1988 ed.); see also H.\xc2\xa0R. Conf. Rep. No. 103\xe2\x80\x93677, pp. 82\xe2\x80\x9383, 85 (1994) (hereinafter H.\xc2\xa0R. Conf. Rep.). And it did so fully aware of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of that language as set forth in Morales. See H.\xc2\xa0R. Conf. Rep., at 83 (motor carriers will enjoy \xe2\x80\x9cthe identical intrastate preemption of prices, routes and services as that originally contained in\xe2\x80\x9d the Airline Deregulation Act); ibid. (expressing agreement with \xe2\x80\x9cthe broad preemption interpretation adopted by the United States Supreme Court in Morales\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 85.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Morales, the Court determined: (1) that \xe2\x80\x9c[s]tate enforcement actions having a connection with, or reference to\xe2\x80\x9d carrier \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98rates, routes, or services\xe2\x80\x99 are pre-empted,\xe2\x80\x9d 504 U. S., at 384 (emphasis added); (2) that such pre-emption may occur even if a state law\xe2\x80\x99s effect on rates, routes or services \xe2\x80\x9cis only indirect,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 386 (internal quotation marks omitted); (3) that, in respect to pre-emption, it makes no difference whether a state law is \xe2\x80\x9cconsistent\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cinconsistent\xe2\x80\x9d with federal regulation, id., at 386\xe2\x80\x93387 (emphasis deleted); and (4) that pre-emption occurs at least where state laws have a \xe2\x80\x9csignificant impact\xe2\x80\x9d related to Congress\xe2\x80\x99 deregulatory and pre-emption-related objectives, id., at 390. The Court described Congress\xe2\x80\x99 overarching goal as helping assure transportation rates, routes, and services that reflect \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum reliance on competitive market forces,\xe2\x80\x9d thereby stimulating \xe2\x80\x9cefficiency, innovation, and low prices,\xe2\x80\x9d as well as \xe2\x80\x9cvariety\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cquality.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 378 (internal quotation marks omitted). Morales held that, given these principles, federal law pre-empts States from enforcing their consumer-fraud statutes against deceptive airline-fare advertisements. Id., at 391. See American Airlines, Inc. v. Wolens, 513 U. S. 219, 226\xe2\x80\x93228 (1995) (federal law pre-empts application of a State\xe2\x80\x99s general consumer-protection statute to an airline\xe2\x80\x99s frequent flyer program).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, Morales said that federal law might not pre-empt state laws that affect fares in only a \xe2\x80\x9ctenuous, remote, or peripheral \xe2\x80\xa6 manner,\xe2\x80\x9d such as state laws forbidding gambling. 504 U. S., at 390 (internal quotation marks omitted). But the Court did not say where, or how, \xe2\x80\x9cit would be appropriate to draw the line,\xe2\x80\x9d for the state law before it did not \xe2\x80\x9cpresent a borderline question.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted); see also Wolens, supra, at 226.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In light of Morales, we find that federal law pre-empts the Maine laws at issue here. Section 1555\xe2\x80\x93C(3)(C) of the Maine statute forbids licensed tobacco retailers to employ a \xe2\x80\x9cdelivery service\xe2\x80\x9d unless that service follows particular delivery procedures. Me. Rev. Stat. Ann., Tit. 22, \xc2\xa71555\xe2\x80\x93C(3)(C). In doing so, it focuses on trucking and other motor carrier services (which make up a substantial portion of all \xe2\x80\x9cdelivery services,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71551(1\xe2\x80\x93C)), thereby creating a direct \xe2\x80\x9cconnection with\xe2\x80\x9d motor carrier services. See Morales, 504 U. S., at 384.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the same time, the provision has a \xe2\x80\x9csignificant\xe2\x80\x9d and adverse \xe2\x80\x9cimpact\xe2\x80\x9d in respect to the federal Act\xe2\x80\x99s ability to achieve its pre-emption-related objectives. Id., at 390. The Solicitor General and the carrier associations claim (and Maine does not deny) that the law will require carriers to offer a system of services that the market does not now provide (and which the carriers would prefer not to offer). And even were that not so, the law would freeze into place services that carriers might prefer to discontinue in the future. The Maine law thereby produces the very effect that the federal law sought to avoid, namely, a State\xe2\x80\x99s direct substitution of its own governmental commands for \xe2\x80\x9ccompetitive market forces\xe2\x80\x9d in determining (to a significant degree) the services that motor carriers will provide. Id., at 378 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We concede that the regulation here is less \xe2\x80\x9cdirect\xe2\x80\x9d than it might be, for it tells shippers what to choose rather than carriers what to do. Nonetheless, the effect of the regulation is that carriers will have to offer tobacco delivery services that differ significantly from those that, in the absence of the regulation, the market might dictate. And that being so, \xe2\x80\x9ctreating sales restrictions and purchase restrictions differently for pre-emption purposes would make no sense.\xe2\x80\x9d Engine Mfrs. Assn. v. South Coast Air Quality Management Dist., 541 U. S. 246, 255 (2004). If federal law pre-empts state efforts to regulate, and consequently to affect, the advertising about carrier rates and services at issue in Morales, it must pre-empt Maine\xe2\x80\x99s efforts to regulate carrier delivery services themselves.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 1555\xe2\x80\x93D\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cdeemed to know\xe2\x80\x9d provision applies yet more directly to motor carrier services. The provision creates a conclusive presumption of carrier knowledge that a shipment contains tobacco when it is marked as originating from a Maine-licensed tobacco retailer or is sent by anyone Maine has specifically identified as an unlicensed tobacco retailer. That presumption means that the Maine law imposes civil liability upon the carrier, not simply for its knowing transport of (unlicensed) tobacco, but for the carrier\xe2\x80\x99s failure sufficiently to examine every package. The provision thus requires the carrier to check each shipment for certain markings and to compare it against the Maine attorney general\xe2\x80\x99s list of proscribed shippers. And it thereby directly regulates a significant aspect of the motor carrier\xe2\x80\x99s package pick-up and delivery service. In this way it creates the kind of state-mandated regulation that the federal Act pre-empts.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Maine replies that the regulation will impose no significant additional costs upon carriers. But even were that so (and the carriers deny it), Maine\xe2\x80\x99s reply is off the mark. As with the recipient-verification provision, the \xe2\x80\x9cdeemed to know\xe2\x80\x9d provision would freeze in place and immunize from competition a service-related system that carriers do not (or in the future might not) wish to provide. Supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x936. To allow Maine to insist that the carriers provide a special checking system would allow other States to do the same. And to interpret the federal law to permit these, and similar, state requirements could easily lead to a patchwork of state service-determining laws, rules, and regulations. That state regulatory patchwork is inconsistent with Congress\xe2\x80\x99 major legislative effort to leave such decisions, where federally unregulated, to the competitive marketplace. See H.\xc2\xa0R. Conf. Rep., at 87. If federal law pre-empts state regulation of the details of an air carrier\xe2\x80\x99s frequent flyer program, a program that primarily promotes carriage, see Wolens, supra, at 226\xe2\x80\x93228, it must pre-empt state regulation of the essential details of a motor carrier\xe2\x80\x99s system for picking-up, sorting, and carrying goods\xe2\x80\x94essential details of the carriage itself.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Maine\xe2\x80\x99s primary arguments focus upon the reason why it has enacted the provisions in question. Maine argues for an exception from pre-emption on the ground that its laws help it prevent minors from obtaining cigarettes. In Maine\xe2\x80\x99s view, federal law does not pre-empt a State\xe2\x80\x99s efforts to protect its citizens\xe2\x80\x99 public health, particularly when those laws regulate so dangerous an activity as underage smoking.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Despite the importance of the public health objective, we cannot agree with Maine that the federal law creates an exception on that basis, exempting state laws that it would otherwise pre-empt. The Act says nothing about a public health exception. To the contrary, it explicitly lists a set of exceptions (governing motor vehicle safety, certain local route controls, and the like), but the list says nothing about public health. See 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa714501(c)(2) to (c)(3); see also \xc2\xa741713(b)(4)(B). Maine suggests that the provision\xe2\x80\x99s history indicates that Congress\xe2\x80\x99 primary concern was not with the sort of law it has enacted, but instead with state \xe2\x80\x9ceconomic\xe2\x80\x9d regulation. See, e.g., H.\xc2\xa0R. Conf. Rep., at 88; see also Columbus v. Ours Garage & Wrecker Service, Inc., 536 U. S. 424, 440 (2002). But it is frequently difficult to distinguish between a State\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ceconomic\xe2\x80\x9d-related and \xe2\x80\x9chealth\xe2\x80\x9d-related motivations, see infra, at 9, and, indeed, the parties vigorously dispute Maine\xe2\x80\x99s actual motivation for the laws at issue here. Consequently, it is not surprising that Congress declined to insert the term \xe2\x80\x9ceconomic\xe2\x80\x9d into the operative language now before us, despite having at one time considered doing so. See S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 95\xe2\x80\x93631, p. 171 (1978) (reprinting Senate bill).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Maine\xe2\x80\x99s argument for an implied \xe2\x80\x9cpublic health\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ctobacco\xe2\x80\x9d exception to federal pre-emption rests largely upon (1) legislative history containing a list of nine States, with laws resembling Maine\xe2\x80\x99s, that Congress thought did not regulate \xe2\x80\x9cintrastate prices, routes and services of motor carriers,\xe2\x80\x9d see H.\xc2\xa0R. Conf. Rep., at 86; and (2) the Synar Amendment, a law that denies States federal funds unless they forbid sales of tobacco to minors, see 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7300x\xe2\x80\x9326(a)(1), (b)(1). The legislative history, however, does not suggest Congress made a firm judgment about, or even focused upon, the issue now before us. And the Synar Amendment nowhere mentions the particular state enforcement method here at issue; indeed, it does not mention specific state enforcement methods at all.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Maine\xe2\x80\x99s inability to find significant support for some kind of \xe2\x80\x9cpublic health\xe2\x80\x9d exception is not surprising. \xe2\x80\x9cPublic health\xe2\x80\x9d does not define itself. Many products create \xe2\x80\x9cpublic health\xe2\x80\x9d risks of differing kind and degree. To accept Maine\xe2\x80\x99s justification in respect to a rule regulating services would legitimate rules regulating routes or rates for similar public health reasons. And to allow Maine directly to regulate carrier services would permit other States to do the same. Given the number of States through which carriers travel, the number of products, the variety of potential adverse public health effects, the many different kinds of regulatory rules potentially available, and the difficulty of finding a legal criterion for separating permissible from impermissible public-health-oriented regulations, Congress is unlikely to have intended an implicit general \xe2\x80\x9cpublic health\xe2\x80\x9d exception broad enough to cover even the shipments at issue here.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This is not to say that this federal law generally pre-empts state public health regulation: for instance, state regulation that broadly prohibits certain forms of conduct and affects, say, truckdrivers, only in their capacity as members of the public (e.g., a prohibition on smoking in certain public places). We have said that federal law does not pre-empt state laws that affect rates, routes, or services in \xe2\x80\x9ctoo tenuous, remote, or peripheral a manner.\xe2\x80\x9d Morales, 504 U. S., at 390 (internal quotation marks omitted). And we have written that the state laws whose \xe2\x80\x9ceffect\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cforbidden\xe2\x80\x9d under federal law are those with a \xe2\x80\x9csignificant impact\xe2\x80\x9d on carrier rates, routes, or services. Id., at 388, 390 (emphasis added).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this case, the state law is not general, it does not affect truckers solely in their capacity as members of the general public, the impact is significant, and the connection with trucking is not tenuous, remote, or peripheral. The state statutes aim directly at the carriage of goods, a commercial field where carriage by commercial motor vehicles plays a major role. The state statutes require motor carrier operators to perform certain services, thereby limiting their ability to provide incompatible alternative services; and they do so simply because the State seeks to enlist the motor carrier operators as allies in its enforcement efforts. Given these circumstances, from the perspective of pre-emption, this case is no more \xe2\x80\x9cborderline\xe2\x80\x9d than was Morales. Id., at 390 (internal quotation marks omitted); see also Wolens, 513 U. S., at 226.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Maine adds that it possesses legal authority to prevent any tobacco shipments from entering into or moving within the State, and that the broader authority must encompass the narrower authority to regulate the manner of tobacco shipments. But even assuming purely for argument\xe2\x80\x99s sake that Maine possesses the broader authority, its conclusion does not follow. To accept that conclusion would permit Maine to regulate carrier routes, carrier rates, and carrier services, all on the ground that such regulation would not restrict carriage of the goods as seriously as would a total ban on shipments. And it consequently would severely undermine the effectiveness of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 pre-emptive provision. Indeed, it would create the very exception that we have just rejected, extending that exception to all other products a State might ban. We have explained why we do not believe Congress intended that result. Supra, at 7\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, Maine says that to set aside its regulations will seriously harm its efforts to prevent cigarettes from falling into the hands of minors. The Solicitor General denies that this is so. He suggests that Maine, like other States, can prohibit all persons from providing tobacco products to minors (as it already has, see Me. Rev. Stat. Ann., Tit. 22, \xc2\xa71555\xe2\x80\x93B(2) (Supp. 2007)); that it can ban all non-face-to-face sales of tobacco; that it might pass other laws of general (non-carrier-specific) applicability; and that it can, if necessary, seek appropriate federal regulation (see, e.g., H.\xc2\xa0R. 4081, 110th Cong., 1st Sess. (2007) (proposed bill regulating tobacco shipment); H.\xc2\xa0R. 4128, 110th Cong., 1st Sess., \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71411\xe2\x80\x931416, pp. 577\xe2\x80\x93583 (2007) (proposed bill providing criminal penalties for trafficking in contraband tobacco)). Regardless, given Morales, where the Court held that federal law pre-empts state consumer-protection laws, we find that federal law must also pre-empt Maine\xe2\x80\x99s efforts directly to regulate carrier services.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For these reasons, the judgment of the Court of Appeals is affirmed.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision declares key portions of Maine\xe2\x80\x99s Tobacco Delivery Law incompatible with the Federal Aviation Administration Authorization Act of 1994 (FAAAA). The breadth of FAAAA\xe2\x80\x99s preemption language, 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa714501(c)(1) and 41713(b)(4)(A), coupled with our decisions closely in point, Morales v. Trans World Airlines, Inc., 504 U. S. 374 (1992), and American Airlines, Inc. v. Wolens, 513 U. S. 219 (1995), impel that conclusion. I write separately to emphasize the large regulatory gap left by an application of the FAAAA perhaps overlooked by Congress, and the urgent need for the National Legislature to fill that gap.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Tobacco use by children and adolescents, we have recognized, may be \xe2\x80\x9cthe single most significant threat to public health in the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d FDA v. Brown & Williamson Tobacco Corp., 529 U. S. 120, 161 (2000). But no comprehensive federal law currently exists to prevent tobacco sellers from exploiting the underage market. Instead, Congress has encouraged state efforts. Congress has done so by providing funding incentives for the States to pass legislation making it unlawful to \xe2\x80\x9csell or distribute any [tobacco] product to any individual under the age of 18.\xe2\x80\x9d Synar Amendment, 106 Stat. 394, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7300x\xe2\x80\x9326(a)(1). See Lorillard Tobacco Co. v. Reilly, 533 U. S. 525, 552, 571 (2001).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0State measures to prevent youth access to tobacco, however, are increasingly thwarted by the ease with which tobacco products can be purchased through the Internet. \xe2\x80\x9cAs cyberspace acts as a risk-free zone where minors can anonymously purchase tobacco, unrestricted online tobacco sales create a major barrier to comprehensive youth tobacco control.\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0 Brief for Tobacco Control Legal Consortium et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 10 (footnote omitted). See also Brief for California et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 9 (\xe2\x80\x9cIllegal Internet tobacco sales have reached epidemic proportions.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Maine and its amici maintain that, to guard against delivery of tobacco products to children, \xe2\x80\x9cthe same sort of age verification safeguards [must be] used when tobacco is handed over-the-doorstep as \xe2\x80\xa6 when it is handed over-the-counter.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 8; Brief for California et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 11; Brief for Tobacco Control Legal Consortium et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 11\xe2\x80\x9312; cf. Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 16. The FAAAA\xe2\x80\x99s broad preemption provisions, the Court holds, bar States from adopting this sensible enforcement strategy. While I join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, I doubt that the drafters of the FAAAA, a statute designed to deregulate the carriage of goods, anticipated the measure\xe2\x80\x99s facilitation of minors\xe2\x80\x99 access to tobacco. Now alerted to the problem, Congress has the capacity to act with care and dispatch to provide an effective solution.
