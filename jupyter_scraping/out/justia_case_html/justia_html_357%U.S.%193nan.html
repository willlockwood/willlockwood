Decided June 16, 1958

Syllabus\n

\n

Respondents, natives of China, came to the United States between 1949 and 1954, seeking admission. All were paroled in the United States but have been ordered excluded. They applied for stays of deportation under \xc2\xa7 243(h) of the Immigration and Nationality Act, on the ground that their deportation to China would subject them.to physical persecution at the hands of the existing government. The stays were denied, and they sued for judgments declaring their nondeportability to China, directing consideration of their claims under \xc2\xa7243(h), and restraining the Attorney General from deporting them.

\n

Held:

\n

1. Their release on parole did not alter their status as excluded aliens; they were not "within the United States," within the meaning of \xc2\xa7 243(h); and thus they were not eligible for the benefits of that section. Len May Ma v. Barber, ante, p. 357 U. S. 185. P. 357 U. S. 194.

\n

2. Deportation authority under the two exclusion sections, \xc2\xa7 237 of the Immigration and Nationality Act and \xc2\xa7 18 of the Immigration Act of 1917, is not confined to situations where deportation is immediate. Pp. 357 U. S. 194-196.

\n

3. Regardless of which of the two exclusion sections, \xc2\xa7 237(a) of the 1952 Act or \xc2\xa7 18 of the 1917 Act, provides the basis for respondents\' deportation, their applications for stays were all filed subsequent to the 1952 Act, and must be determined by that Act. P. 357 U. S. 196.

\n

MR. JUSTICE CLARK delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

This is a companion case to Leng May Ma v. Barber, ante, p. 357 U. S. 185. The five respondents are natives of China who came to the United States seeking admission between 1949 and 1954, four of them arriving before the effective date of the Immigration and Nationality Act. Like petitioner in Leng May Ma, all were paroled into the United States, and all have been ordered excluded. They applied for stays of deportation under \xc2\xa7 243(h) of the Immigration and Nationality Act, [Footnote 1] and, upon refusal, filed complaints in the District Court seeking judgments declaring their nondeportability to China, directing consideration of their claims under \xc2\xa7 243(h), and restraining the Attorney General from deporting them. The complaints were dismissed by the District Court, but the Court of Appeals held that excluded aliens on parole are "within the United States" for purposes of \xc2\xa7 243(h), 101 U.S.App.D.C. 229, 248 F.2d 89. Because of the conflict with the Ninth Circuit\'s decision in Leng May Ma, we granted certiorari. 355 U.S. 861 (1957). We have concluded that respondents, like petitioner in Leng May Ma, are ineligible for stays of deportation under \xc2\xa7 243(h). However, because of the importance of this problem in the administration of the immigration laws, we deem it appropriate to deal specifically with a contention not directly asserted by petitioner in Leng May Ma.

\n

The deportation of excluded aliens under the Immigration and Nationality Act is authorized in \xc2\xa7 237(a) of Chapter 4, wherein it is provided that an alien excluded

Page 357 U. S. 195

under the Act "shall be immediately deported to the country whence he came. . . ." 66 Stat. 201, 8 U.S.C. \xc2\xa7 1227(a). A similar provision existed in the immediate predecessor to \xc2\xa7 237(a), which was \xc2\xa7 18 of the Immigration Act of 1917. [Footnote 2] Deportation in expulsion proceedings is separately provided for under the present Act in \xc2\xa7 243 of Chapter 5, subsection (h) of which, of course, contains the authority which respondents seek to invoke in this case. 66 Stat. 212, 8 U.S.C. \xc2\xa7 1253. Like authority existed in the immediate predecessor of \xc2\xa7 243, which was \xc2\xa7 20 of the Immigration Act of 1917, 39 Stat. 890, as amended by \xc2\xa7 23 of the Internal Security Act of 1950, 64 Stat. 1010.

\n

Respondents assert, however, that neither \xc2\xa7 237(a) nor its predecessor, \xc2\xa7 18 of the 1917 Act, is the basis for their deportation, since they were not "immediately" deported, as required in the sections. Hence, they argue that deportation must rest upon \xc2\xa7 243 of the present Act, as to the respondent who arrived after the Immigration and Nationality Act, and its predecessor, \xc2\xa7 20 of the 1917 Act, as to the four who arrived prior to the present Act.

\n

We will assume, for purpose of analysis, that four of the five respondents are, as they claim, deportable only under prior Acts by virtue of their early arrival. However, under neither of the exclusion sections, i.e., \xc2\xa7 237(a) of the present Act or \xc2\xa7 18 of the 1917 Act, is the deportation authority confined, as respondents contend, to those situations where deportation is immediate. Neither section, when read in its entirety and in context, fairly suggests any such limitation. Nor are there reasons of policy to compel such a result. As the desire to remain increases, those knocking on our doors quite naturally become more litigious, and contested departures

Page 357 U. S. 196

often involve long delays. We doubt that the Congress intended the mere fact of delay to improve an alien\'s status from that of one seeking admission to that of one legally considered within the United States. We conclude that there is ample basis under \xc2\xa7 237(a) and \xc2\xa7 18 of the 1917 Act to deport respondents; we need not draw upon the provisions in \xc2\xa7 243 of the present Act or \xc2\xa7 20 of the 1917 Act.

\n

Regardless of which of the two exclusion cections, \xc2\xa7 237(a) of the 1952 Act or \xc2\xa7 18 of the 1917 Act, provides the basis for respondents\' deportation, the applications for stays were all filed subsequent to the 1952 Act, and hence must be determined by that Act. For reasons explained in Leng May Ma, \xc2\xa7 243(h) is unavailable to excluded aliens, and the fact of parole creates no variance from this principle.

\n

Reversed.

\n

THE CHIEF JUSTICE, MR. JUSTICE BLACK, MR. JUSTICE DOUGLAS, and MR. JUSTICE BRENNAN dissent for the reasons stated in the dissenting opinion in Leng May Ma v. Barber, ante, p. 357 U. S. 190.
