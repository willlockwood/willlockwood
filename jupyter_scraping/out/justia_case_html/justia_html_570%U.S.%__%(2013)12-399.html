\n\t\t\t\tADOPTIVE COUPLE v. BABY GIRL, a minor child under the age of fourteen years, et\xc2\xa0al.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the supreme court of south carolina\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 12\xe2\x80\x93399.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 16, 2013\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 25, 2013\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe Indian Child Welfare Act of 1978 (ICWA), which establishes federal standards for state-court child custody proceedings involving Indian children, was enacted to address \xe2\x80\x9cthe consequences .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of abusive child welfare practices that [separated] Indian children from their families and tribes through adoption or foster care placement, usually in non-Indian homes,\xe2\x80\x9d Mississippi Band of Choctaw Indians v. Holyfield, \n\n490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 30\n. As relevant here, the ICWA bars involuntary termination of a parent\xe2\x80\x99s rights in the absence of a heightened showing that serious harm to the Indian child is likely to result from the parent\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccontinued custody\xe2\x80\x9d of the child, \n\n25 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71912(f); conditions involuntary termination of parental rights with respect to an Indian child on a showing that remedial efforts have been made to prevent the \xe2\x80\x9cbreakup of the Indian family,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71912(d); and provides placement preferences for the adoption of Indian children to members of the child\xe2\x80\x99s extended family, other members of the Indian child\xe2\x80\x99s tribe, and other Indian families, \xc2\xa71915(a).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhile Birth Mother was pregnant with Biological Father\xe2\x80\x99s child, their relationship ended and Biological Father (a member of the Cherokee Nation) agreed to relinquish his parental rights. Birth Mother put Baby Girl up for adoption through a private adoption agency and selected Adoptive Couple, non-Indians living in South Carolina. For the duration of the pregnancy and the first four months after Baby Girl\xe2\x80\x99s birth, Biological Father provided no financial assistance to Birth Mother or Baby Girl. About four months after Baby Girl\xe2\x80\x99s birth, Adoptive Couple served Biological Father with notice of the pending adoption. In the adoption proceedings, Biological Father sought custody and stated that he did not consent to the adoption. Following a trial, which took place when Baby Girl was two years old, the South Carolina Family Court denied Adoptive Couple\xe2\x80\x99s adoption petition and awarded custody to Biological Father. At the age of 27 months, Baby Girl was handed over to Biological Father, whom she had never met. The State Supreme Court affirmed, concluding that the ICWA applied because the child custody proceeding related to an Indian child; that Biological Father was a \xe2\x80\x9cparent\xe2\x80\x9d under the ICWA; that \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71912(d) and (f) barred the termination of his parental rights; and that had his rights been terminated, \xc2\xa71915(a)\xe2\x80\x99s adoptionplacement preferences would have applied. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld: \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t1.\xc2\xa0Assuming for the sake of argument that Biological Father is a \xe2\x80\x9cparent\xe2\x80\x9d under the ICWA, neither \xc2\xa71912(f) nor \xc2\xa71912(d) bars the termination of his parental rights. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9314.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0Section 1912(f) conditions the involuntary termination of parental rights on a heightened showing regarding the merits of the parent\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccontinued custody of the child.\xe2\x80\x9d The adjective \xe2\x80\x9ccontinued\xe2\x80\x9d plainly refers to a pre-existing state under ordinary dictionary definitions. The phrase \xe2\x80\x9ccontinued custody\xe2\x80\x9d thus refers to custody that a parent already has (or at least had at some point in the past). As a result, \xc2\xa71912(f) does not apply where the Indian parent never had custody of the Indian child. This reading comports with the statutory text, which demonstrates that the ICWA was designed primarily to counteract the unwarranted removal of Indian children from Indian families. See \xc2\xa71901(4). But the ICWA\xe2\x80\x99s primary goal is not implicated when an Indian child\xe2\x80\x99s adoption is voluntarily and lawfully initiated by a non-Indian parent with sole custodial rights. Nonbinding guidelines issued by the Bureau of Indian Affairs (BIA) demonstrate that the BIA envisioned that \xc2\xa71912(f)\xe2\x80\x99s standard would apply only to termination of a custodial parent\xe2\x80\x99s rights. Under this reading, Biological Father should not have been able to invoke \xc2\xa71912(f) in this case because he had never had legal or physical custody of Baby Girl as of the time of the adoption proceedings. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9311.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0Section \xc2\xa71912(d) conditions an involuntary termination of parental rights with respect to an Indian child on a showing \xe2\x80\x9cthat active efforts have been made to provide remedial services .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. designed to prevent the breakup of the Indian family and that these efforts have proved unsuccessful.\xe2\x80\x9d Consistent with this text, \xc2\xa71912(d) applies only when an Indian family\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cbreakup\xe2\x80\x9d would be precipitated by terminating parental rights. The term \xe2\x80\x9cbreakup\xe2\x80\x9d refers in this context to \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he discontinuance of a relationship,\xe2\x80\x9d American Heritage Dictionary 235 (3d ed. 1992), or \xe2\x80\x9can ending as an effective entity,\xe2\x80\x9d Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third New International Dictionary 273 (1961). But when an Indian parent abandons an Indian child prior to birth and that child has never been in the Indian parent\xe2\x80\x99s legal or physical custody, there is no \xe2\x80\x9crelationship\xe2\x80\x9d to be \xe2\x80\x9cdiscontinu[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d and no \xe2\x80\x9ceffective entity\xe2\x80\x9d to be \xe2\x80\x9cend[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d by terminating the Indian parent\xe2\x80\x99s rights. In such a situation, the \xe2\x80\x9cbreakup of the Indian family\xe2\x80\x9d has long since occurred, and \xc2\xa71912(d) is inapplicable. This interpretation is consistent with the explicit congressional purpose of setting certain \xe2\x80\x9cstandards for the removal of Indian children from their families,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71902, and with BIA Guidelines. Section 1912(d)\xe2\x80\x99s proximity to \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71912(e) and (f), which both condition the outcome of proceedings on the merits of an Indian child\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccontinued custody\xe2\x80\x9d with his parent, strongly suggests that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cbreakup of the Indian family\xe2\x80\x9d should be read in harmony with the \xe2\x80\x9ccontinued custody\xe2\x80\x9d requirement. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9314.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t2.\xc2\xa0Section 1915(a)\xe2\x80\x99s adoption-placement preferences are inapplicable in cases where no alternative party has formally sought to adopt the child. No party other than Adoptive Couple sought to adopt Baby Girl in the Family Court or the South Carolina Supreme Court. Biological Father is not covered by \xc2\xa71915(a) because he did not seek to adopt Baby Girl; instead, he argued that his parental rights should not be terminated in the first place. And custody was never sought by Baby Girl\xe2\x80\x99s paternal grandparents, other members of the Cherokee Nation, or other Indian families. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9316.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\ton writ of certiorari to the supreme court of south carolina\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Alito delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis case is about a little girl (Baby Girl) who is classified as an Indian because she is 1.2% (3/256) Cherokee. Because Baby Girl is classified in this way, the South Carolina Supreme Court held that certain provisions of the federal Indian Child Welfare Act of 1978 required her to be taken, at the age of 27 months, from the only parents she had ever known and handed over to her biological father, who had attempted to relinquish his parental rights and who had no prior contact with the child. The provisions of the federal statute at issue here do not demand this result.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tContrary to the State Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling, we hold that \n\n25 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71912(f)\xe2\x80\x94which bars involuntary termination of a parent\xe2\x80\x99s rights in the absence of a heightened showing that serious harm to the Indian child is likely to result from the parent\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccontinued custody\xe2\x80\x9d of the child\xe2\x80\x94does not apply when, as here, the relevant parent never had custody of the child. We further hold that \xc2\xa71912(d)\xe2\x80\x94which conditions involuntary termination of parental rights with respect to an Indian child on a showing that remedial efforts have been made to prevent the \xe2\x80\x9cbreakup of the Indian family\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94is inapplicable when, as here, the parent abandoned the Indian child before birth and never had custody of the child. Finally, we clarify that \xc2\xa71915(a), which provides placement preferences for the adoption of Indian children, does not bar a non-Indian family like Adoptive Couple from adopting an Indian child when no other eligible candidates have sought to adopt the child. We accordingly reverse the South Carolina Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment and remand for further proceedings.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cThe Indian Child Welfare Act of 1978 (ICWA), \n\n92Stat.\n3069,\n25 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71901\xe2\x80\x931963, was the product of rising concern in the mid-1970\xe2\x80\x99s over the consequences to In- dian children, Indian families, and Indian tribes of abusive child welfare practices that resulted in the separation of large numbers of Indian children from their families and tribes through adoption or foster care placement, usually in non-Indian homes.\xe2\x80\x9d Mississippi Band of Choctaw Indians v. Holyfield, \n\n490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 30, \n32 (1989)\n. Congress found that \xe2\x80\x9can alarmingly high percentage of Indian families [were being] broken up by the removal, often unwarranted, of their children from them by nontribal public and private agencies.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71901(4). This \xe2\x80\x9cwholesale removal of Indian children from their homes\xe2\x80\x9d prompted Congress to enact the ICWA, which establishes federal standards that govern state-court child custody proceedings involving Indian children. Id., at 32, 36 (internal quotation marks omitted); see also \xc2\xa71902 (declaring that the ICWA es- tablishes \xe2\x80\x9cminimum Federal standards for the removal of Indian children from their families\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThree provisions of the ICWA are especially relevant to this case. First, \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny party seeking\xe2\x80\x9d an involuntary termination of parental rights to an Indian child under state law must demonstrate that \xe2\x80\x9cactive efforts have been made to provide remedial services and rehabilitative programs designed to prevent the breakup of the Indian family and that these efforts have proved unsuccessful.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71912(d). Second, a state court may not involuntarily terminate parental rights to an Indian child \xe2\x80\x9cin the absence of a determination, supported by evidence beyond a reasonable doubt, including testimony of qualified expert witnesses, that the continued custody of the child by the parent or Indian custodian is likely to result in serious emotional or physical damage to the child.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71912(f). Third, with respect to adoptive placements for an Indian child under state law, \xe2\x80\x9ca preference shall be given, in the absence of good cause to the contrary, to a placement with (1) a member of the child\xe2\x80\x99s extended family; (2) other members of the Indian child\xe2\x80\x99s tribe; or (3) other Indian families.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71915(a).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn this case, Birth Mother (who is predominantly Hispanic) and Biological Father (who is a member of the Cherokee Nation) became engaged in December 2008. One month later, Birth Mother informed Biological Father, who lived about four hours away, that she was pregnant. After learning of the pregnancy, Biological Father asked Birth Mother to move up the date of the wedding. He also refused to provide any financial support until after the two had married. The couple\xe2\x80\x99s relationship deteriorated, and Birth Mother broke off the engagement in May 2009. In June, Birth Mother sent Biological Father a text message asking if he would rather pay child support or re- linquish his parental rights. Biological Father responded via text message that he relinquished his rights.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBirth Mother then decided to put Baby Girl up for adoption. Because Birth Mother believed that Biological Father had Cherokee Indian heritage, her attorney contacted the Cherokee Nation to determine whether Biological Father was formally enrolled. The inquiry letter misspelled Biological Father\xe2\x80\x99s first name and incorrectly stated his birthday, and the Cherokee Nation responded that, based on the information provided, it could not verify Biological Father\xe2\x80\x99s membership in the tribal records.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWorking through a private adoption agency, Birth Mother selected Adoptive Couple, non-Indians living in South Carolina, to adopt Baby Girl. Adoptive Couple supported Birth Mother both emotionally and financially throughout her pregnancy. Adoptive Couple was present at Baby Girl\xe2\x80\x99s birth in Oklahoma on September 15, 2009, and Adoptive Father even cut the umbilical cord. The next morning, Birth Mother signed forms relinquishing her parental rights and consenting to the adoption. Adoptive Couple initiated adoption proceedings in South Carolina a few days later, and returned there with Baby Girl. After returning to South Carolina, Adoptive Couple allowed Birth Mother to visit and communicate with Baby Girl.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is undisputed that, for the duration of the pregnancy and the first four months after Baby Girl\xe2\x80\x99s birth, Biological Father provided no financial assistance to Birth Mother or Baby Girl, even though he had the ability to do so. In- deed, Biological Father \xe2\x80\x9cmade no meaningful attempts to assume his responsibility of parenthood\xe2\x80\x9d during this period. App. to Pet. for Cert. 122a (Sealed; internal quotation marks omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tApproximately four months after Baby Girl\xe2\x80\x99s birth, Adoptive Couple served Biological Father with notice of the pending adoption. (This was the first notification that they had provided to Biological Father regarding the adoption proceeding.) Biological Father signed papers stating that he accepted service and that he was \xe2\x80\x9cnot contesting the adoption.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 37. But Biological Father later testified that, at the time he signed the papers, he thought that he was relinquishing his rights to Birth Mother, not to Adoptive Couple.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBiological Father contacted a lawyer the day after signing the papers, and subsequently requested a stay of the adoption proceedings.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t In the adoption proceedings, Biological Father sought custody and stated that he did not consent to Baby Girl\xe2\x80\x99s adoption. Moreover, Biological Father took a paternity test, which verified that he was Baby Girl\xe2\x80\x99s biological father.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA trial took place in the South Carolina Family Court in September 2011, by which time Baby Girl was two years old. 398 S.\xc2\xa0C. 625, 634\xe2\x80\x93635, 731 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 550, 555\xe2\x80\x93556 (2012). The Family Court concluded that Adoptive Couple had not carried the heightened burden under \xc2\xa71912(f) of proving that Baby Girl would suffer serious emotional or physical damage if Biological Father had custody. See id., at 648\xe2\x80\x93651, 731 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 562\xe2\x80\x93564. The Family Court therefore denied Adoptive Couple\xe2\x80\x99s petition for adoption and awarded custody to Biological Father. Id., at 629, 636, 731 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 552, 556. On December 31, 2011, at the age of 27 months, Baby Girl was handed over to Biological Father, whom she had never met.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe South Carolina Supreme Court affirmed the Family Court\xe2\x80\x99s denial of the adoption and the award of custody to Biological Father. Id., at 629, 731 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 552. The State Supreme Court first determined that the ICWA applied because the case involved a child custody proceeding relating to an Indian child. Id., at 637, 643, n.\xc2\xa018, 731 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 556, 560, n.\xc2\xa018. It also concluded that Biological Father fell within the ICWA\xe2\x80\x99s definition of a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98parent.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 644, 731 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 560. The court then held that two separate provisions of the ICWA barred the termination of Biological Father\xe2\x80\x99s parental rights. First, the court held that Adoptive Couple had not shown that \xe2\x80\x9cactive efforts ha[d] been made to provide remedial services and rehabilitative programs designed to prevent the breakup of the Indian family.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71912(d); see also id., at 647\xe2\x80\x93648, 731 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 562. Second, the court concluded that Adoptive Couple had not shown that Biological Father\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccustody of Baby Girl would result in serious emotional or physical harm to her beyond a reasonable doubt.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 648\xe2\x80\x93649, 731 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 562\xe2\x80\x93563 (citing \xc2\xa71912(f)). Finally, the court stated that, even if it had decided to terminate Biological Father\xe2\x80\x99s parental rights, \xc2\xa71915(a)\xe2\x80\x99s adoption- placement preferences would have applied. Id., at 655\xe2\x80\x93657, 731 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 566\xe2\x80\x93567. We granted certiorari. 568 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2013).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is undisputed that, had Baby Girl not been 3/256 Cherokee, Biological Father would have had no right to object to her adoption under South Carolina law. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 49; 398 S.\xc2\xa0C., at 644, n.\xc2\xa019, 731 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 560, n.\xc2\xa019 (\xe2\x80\x9cUnder state law, [Biological] Father\xe2\x80\x99s con- sent to the adoption would not have been required\xe2\x80\x9d). The South Carolina Supreme Court held, however, that Biological Father is a \xe2\x80\x9cparent\xe2\x80\x9d under the ICWA and that two statutory provisions\xe2\x80\x94namely, \xc2\xa71912(f) and \xc2\xa71912(d)\xe2\x80\x94bar the termination of his parental rights. In this Court, Adoptive Couple contends that Biological Father is not a \xe2\x80\x9cparent\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xc2\xa71912(f) and \xc2\xa71912(d) are inapplicable. We need not\xe2\x80\x94and therefore do not\xe2\x80\x94decide whether Biological Father is a \xe2\x80\x9cparent.\xe2\x80\x9d See \xc2\xa71903(9) (defining \xe2\x80\x9cparent\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Rather, assuming for the sake of argument that he is a \xe2\x80\x9cparent,\xe2\x80\x9d we hold that neither \xc2\xa71912(f) nor \xc2\xa71912(d) bars the termination of his parental rights.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSection 1912(f) addresses the involuntary termination of parental rights with respect to an Indian child. Specifically, \xc2\xa71912(f) provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o termination of parental rights may be ordered in such proceeding in the absence of a determination, supported by evidence beyond a reasonable doubt, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that the continued custody of the child by the parent or Indian custodian is likely to result in serious emotional or physical damage to the child.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.) The South Carolina Supreme Court held that Adoptive Couple failed to satisfy \xc2\xa71912(f) because they did not make a heightened showing that Biological Father\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cprospective legal and physical custody\xe2\x80\x9d would likely result in serious damage to the child. 398 S.\xc2\xa0C., at 651, 731 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 564 (emphasis added). That holding was error.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSection 1912(f) conditions the involuntary termination of parental rights on a showing regarding the merits of \xe2\x80\x9ccontinued custody of the child by the parent.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.) The adjective \xe2\x80\x9ccontinued\xe2\x80\x9d plainly refers to a pre-existing state. As Justice Sotomayor concedes, post, at 11 (dissenting opinion) (hereinafter the dissent), \xe2\x80\x9ccontinued\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9c[c]arried on or kept up without cessation\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9c[e]xtended in space without interruption or breach of conne[ct]ion.\xe2\x80\x9d Compact Edition of the Oxford English Dictionary 909 (1981 reprint of 1971 ed.) (Compact OED); see also American Heritage Dictionary 288 (1981) (defining \xe2\x80\x9ccontinue\xe2\x80\x9d in the following manner: \xe2\x80\x9c1. To go on with a particular action or in a particular condition; persist.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0 3. To remain in the same state, capacity, or place\xe2\x80\x9d); Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third New International Dictionary 493 (1961) (Webster\xe2\x80\x99s) (defining \xe2\x80\x9ccontinued\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cstretching out in time or space esp. without interruption\xe2\x80\x9d); Aguilar v. FDIC, 63 F.\xc2\xa03d 1059, 1062 (CA11 1995) (per curiam) (suggesting that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9ccontinue an action\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9cgo on with .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. an action\xe2\x80\x9d that is \xe2\x80\x9cpreexisting\xe2\x80\x9d). The term \xe2\x80\x9ccontinued\xe2\x80\x9d also can mean \xe2\x80\x9cresumed after interruption.\xe2\x80\x9d Webster\xe2\x80\x99s 493; see American Heritage Dictionary 288. The phrase \xe2\x80\x9ccontinued custody\xe2\x80\x9d therefore refers to custody that a parent already has (or at least had at some point in the past). As a result, \xc2\xa71912(f) does not apply in cases where the Indian parent never had custody of the Indian child.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBiological Father\xe2\x80\x99s contrary reading of \xc2\xa71912(f) is nonsensical. Pointing to the provision\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o termination of parental rights may be ordered .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in the absence of a determination\xe2\x80\x9d relating to \xe2\x80\x9cthe continued custody of the child by the parent,\xe2\x80\x9d Biological Father contends that if a determination relating to \xe2\x80\x9ccontinued custody\xe2\x80\x9d is inapposite in cases where there is no \xe2\x80\x9ccustody,\xe2\x80\x9d the statutory text prohibits termination. See Brief for Respondent Birth Father 39. But it would be absurd to think that Congress enacted a provision that permits termination of a custodial parent\xe2\x80\x99s rights, while simultaneously prohibiting termination of a noncustodial parent\xe2\x80\x99s rights. If the statute draws any distinction between custodial and noncustodial parents, that distinction surely does not provide greater protection for noncustodial parents.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur reading of \xc2\xa71912(f) comports with the statutory text demonstrating that the primary mischief the ICWA was designed to counteract was the unwarranted removal of Indian children from Indian families due to the cultural insensitivity and biases of social workers and state courts. The statutory text expressly highlights the primary problem that the statute was intended to solve: \xe2\x80\x9can alarmingly high percentage of Indian families [were being] broken up by the removal, often unwarranted, of their children from them by nontribal public and private agencies.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71901(4) (emphasis added); see also \xc2\xa71902 (explaining that the ICWA establishes \xe2\x80\x9cminimum Federal standards for the removal of Indian children from their families\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); Holyfield, 490 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 32\xe2\x80\x9334. And if the legislative history of the ICWA is thought to be relevant, it further underscores that the Act was primarily intended to stem the unwarranted removal of Indian children from intact Indian families. See, e.g., H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 95\xe2\x80\x931386, p.\xc2\xa08 (1978) (explaining that, as relevant here, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he purpose of [the ICWA] is to protect the best interests of Indian children and to promote the stability and security of Indian tribes and families by establishing minimum Federal standards for the removal of Indian children from their families and the placement of such children in foster or adoptive homes\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); id., at 9 (decrying the \xe2\x80\x9cwholesale separation of Indian children\xe2\x80\x9d from their Indian families); id., at 22 (discussing \xe2\x80\x9cthe removal\xe2\x80\x9d of Indian children from their parents pursuant to \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71912(e) and (f)). In sum, when, as here, the adoption of an Indian child is voluntarily and lawfully initiated by a non-Indian parent with sole custodial rights, the ICWA\xe2\x80\x99s primary goal of preventing the unwarranted removal of Indian children and the dissolution of Indian families is not implicated.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe dissent fails to dispute that nonbinding guidelines issued by the Bureau of Indian Affairs (BIA) shortly after the ICWA\xe2\x80\x99s enactment demonstrate that the BIA envisioned that \xc2\xa71912(f)\xe2\x80\x99s standard would apply only to termination of a custodial parent\xe2\x80\x99s rights. Specifically, the BIA stated that, under \xc2\xa71912(f), \xe2\x80\x9c[a] child may not be removed simply because there is someone else willing to raise the child who is likely to do a better job\xe2\x80\x9d; instead, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t must be shown that .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. it is dangerous for the child to remain with his or her present custodians.\xe2\x80\x9d Guidelines for State Courts; Indian Child Custody Proceedings, 44 Fed. Reg. 67593 (1979) (emphasis added) (hereinafter Guidelines). Indeed, the Guidelines recognized that \xc2\xa71912(f) applies only when there is pre-existing custody to evaluate. See ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he issue on which qualified expert testimony is required is the question of whether or not serious damage to the child is likely to occur if the child is not removed\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUnder our reading of \xc2\xa71912(f), Biological Father should not have been able to invoke \xc2\xa71912(f) in this case, because he had never had legal or physical custody of Baby Girl as of the time of the adoption proceedings. As an initial matter, it is undisputed that Biological Father never had physical custody of Baby Girl. And as a matter of both South Carolina and Oklahoma law, Biological Father never had legal custody either. See S.\xc2\xa0C. Code Ann. \xc2\xa763\xe2\x80\x9317\xe2\x80\x9320(B) (2010) (\xe2\x80\x9cUnless the court orders otherwise, the custody of an illegitimate child is solely in the natural mother unless the mother has relinquished her rights to the child\xe2\x80\x9d); Okla. Stat., Tit. 10, \xc2\xa77800 (West Cum. Supp. 2013) (\xe2\x80\x9cExcept as otherwise provided by law, the mother of a child born out of wedlock has custody of the child until determined otherwise by a court of competent jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t7\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn sum, the South Carolina Supreme Court erred in finding that \xc2\xa71912(f) barred termination of Biological Father\xe2\x80\x99s parental rights.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSection 1912(d) provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny party\xe2\x80\x9d seeking to terminate parental rights to an Indian child under state law \xe2\x80\x9cshall satisfy the court that active efforts have been made to provide remedial services and rehabilitative programs designed to prevent the breakup of the Indian family and that these efforts have proved unsuccessful.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.) The South Carolina Supreme Court found that Biological Father\xe2\x80\x99s parental rights could not be terminated because Adoptive Couple had not demonstrated that Biological Father had been provided remedial services in accordance with \xc2\xa71912(d). 398 S.\xc2\xa0C., at 647\xe2\x80\x93648, 731 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 562. We disagree.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tConsistent with the statutory text, we hold that \xc2\xa71912(d) applies only in cases where an Indian family\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cbreakup\xe2\x80\x9d would be precipitated by the termination of the parent\xe2\x80\x99s rights. The term \xe2\x80\x9cbreakup\xe2\x80\x9d refers in this context to \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he discontinuance of a relationship,\xe2\x80\x9d American Heritage Dictionary 235 (3d ed. 1992), or \xe2\x80\x9can ending as an effective entity,\xe2\x80\x9d Webster\xe2\x80\x99s 273 (defining \xe2\x80\x9cbreakup\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9ca disruption or dissolution into component parts: an ending as an effective entity\xe2\x80\x9d). See also Compact OED 1076 (defining \xe2\x80\x9cbreak-up\xe2\x80\x9d as, inter alia, a \xe2\x80\x9cdisruption, separation into parts, disintegration\xe2\x80\x9d). But when an Indian parent abandons an Indian child prior to birth and that child has never been in the Indian parent\xe2\x80\x99s legal or physical custody, there is no \xe2\x80\x9crelationship\xe2\x80\x9d that would be \xe2\x80\x9cdiscontinu[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94and no \xe2\x80\x9ceffective entity\xe2\x80\x9d that would be \xe2\x80\x9cend[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94by the termination of the Indian par- ent\xe2\x80\x99s rights. In such a situation, the \xe2\x80\x9cbreakup of the Indian family\xe2\x80\x9d has long since occurred, and \xc2\xa71912(d) is inapplicable.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur interpretation of \xc2\xa71912(d) is, like our interpretation of \xc2\xa71912(f), consistent with the explicit congressional purpose of providing certain \xe2\x80\x9cstandards for the removal of Indian children from their families.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71902 (emphasis added); see also, e.g., \xc2\xa71901(4); Holyfield, 490 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 32\xe2\x80\x9334. In addition, the BIA\xe2\x80\x99s Guidelines confirm that remedial services under \xc2\xa71912(d) are intended \xe2\x80\x9cto alleviate the need to remove the Indian child from his or her parents or Indian custodians,\xe2\x80\x9d not to facilitate a transfer of the child to an Indian parent. See 44 Fed. Reg., at 67592 (emphasis added).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur interpretation of \xc2\xa71912(d) is also confirmed by the provision\xe2\x80\x99s placement next to \xc2\xa71912(e) and \xc2\xa71912(f), both of which condition the outcome of proceedings on the merits of an Indian child\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccontinued custody\xe2\x80\x9d with his parent. That these three provisions appear adjacent to each other strongly suggests that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cbreakup of the Indian family\xe2\x80\x9d should be read in harmony with the \xe2\x80\x9ccontinued custody\xe2\x80\x9d requirement. See United Sav. Assn. of Tex. v. Timbers of Inwood Forest Associates, Ltd., \n\n484 U.\xc2\xa0S. 365, \n371 (1988)\n (explaining that statutory construction \xe2\x80\x9cis a holistic endeavor\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] provision that may seem ambiguous in isolation is often clarified by the remainder of the statutory scheme\xe2\x80\x9d). None of these three provisions creates parental rights for unwed fathers where no such rights would otherwise exist. Instead, Indian parents who are already part of an \xe2\x80\x9cIndian family\xe2\x80\x9d are provided with access to \xe2\x80\x9cremedial services and rehabilitative programs\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa71912(d) so that their \xe2\x80\x9ccustody\xe2\x80\x9d might be \xe2\x80\x9ccontinued\xe2\x80\x9d in a way that avoids foster-care placement under \xc2\xa71912(e) or termination of parental rights under \xc2\xa71912(f). In other words, the provision of \xe2\x80\x9cremedial services and rehabilitative programs\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa71912(d) supports the \xe2\x80\x9ccontinued custody\xe2\x80\x9d that is protected by \xc2\xa71912(e) and \xc2\xa71912(f).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t8\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSection 1912(d) is a sensible requirement when applied to state social workers who might otherwise be too quick to remove Indian children from their Indian families. It would, however, be unusual to apply \xc2\xa71912(d) in the context of an Indian parent who abandoned a child prior to birth and who never had custody of the child. The decision below illustrates this point. The South Carolina Supreme Court held that \xc2\xa71912(d) mandated measures such as \xe2\x80\x9cattempting to stimulate [Biological] Father\xe2\x80\x99s desire to be a parent.\xe2\x80\x9d 398 S.\xc2\xa0C., at 647, 731 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 562. But if prospective adoptive parents were required to engage in the bizarre undertaking of \xe2\x80\x9cstimulat[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d a biological father\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cdesire to be a parent,\xe2\x80\x9d it would surely dissuade some of them from seeking to adopt Indian children.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t9\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t And this would, in turn, unnecessarily place vulnerable Indian children at a unique disadvantage in finding a permanent and loving home, even in cases where neither an Indian parent nor the relevant tribe objects to the adoption.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t10\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn sum, the South Carolina Supreme Court erred in finding that \xc2\xa71912(d) barred termination of Biological Father\xe2\x80\x99s parental rights.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn the decision below, the South Carolina Supreme Court suggested that if it had terminated Biological Father\xe2\x80\x99s rights, then \xc2\xa71915(a)\xe2\x80\x99s preferences for the adoptive placement of an Indian child would have been applicable. 398 S.\xc2\xa0C., at 655\xe2\x80\x93657, 731 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 566\xe2\x80\x93567. In so doing, however, the court failed to recognize a critical lim- itation on the scope of \xc2\xa71915(a).\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSection 1915(a) provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n any adoptive placement of an Indian child under State law, a preference shall be given, in the absence of good cause to the contrary, to a placement with (1) a member of the child\xe2\x80\x99s extended family; (2) other members of the Indian child\xe2\x80\x99s tribe; or (3) other Indian families.\xe2\x80\x9d Contrary to the South Carolina Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion, \xc2\xa71915(a)\xe2\x80\x99s preferences are inapplicable in cases where no alternative party has formally sought to adopt the child. This is because there simply is no \xe2\x80\x9cpreference\xe2\x80\x9d to apply if no alternative party that is eligible to be preferred under \xc2\xa71915(a) has come forward.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn this case, Adoptive Couple was the only party that sought to adopt Baby Girl in the Family Court or the South Carolina Supreme Court. Nor did other members of the Cherokee Nation or \xe2\x80\x9cother Indian families\xe2\x80\x9d seek to adopt Baby Girl, even though the Cherokee Nation had notice of\xe2\x80\x94and intervened in\xe2\x80\x94the adoption proceedings. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Indian Child Welfare Act was enacted to help preserve the cultural identity and heritage of Indian tribes, but under the State Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s reading, the Act would put certain vulnerable children at a great disadvantage solely because an ancestor\xe2\x80\x94even a remote one\xe2\x80\x94was an Indian. As the State Supreme Court read \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71912(d) and (f), a biological Indian father could abandon his child in utero and refuse any support for the birth mother\xe2\x80\x94perhaps contributing to the mother\xe2\x80\x99s decision to put the child up for adoption\xe2\x80\x94and then could play his ICWA trump card at the eleventh hour to override the mother\xe2\x80\x99s decision and the child\xe2\x80\x99s best interests. If this were possible, many prospective adoptive parents would surely pause before adopting any child who might possibly qualify as an Indian under the ICWA. Such an interpretation would raise equal protection concerns, but the plain text of \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71912(f) and (d) makes clear that neither provision applies in the present context. Nor do \xc2\xa71915(a)\xe2\x80\x99s rebuttable adoption preferences apply when no alternative party has formally sought to adopt the child. We therefore reverse the judgment of the South Carolina Supreme Court and remand the case for further proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.\n\t\t\t

Because we conclude that these provisions are inapplicable for other reasons, however, we need not decide whether Biological Father is a \xe2\x80\x9cparent.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\n
\n\n5\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0With a torrent of words, the dissent attempts to obscure the fact that its interpretation simply cannot be squared with the statutory text. A biological father\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccontinued custody\xe2\x80\x9d of a child cannot be assessed if the father never had custody at all, and the use of a different phrase\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9ctermination of parental rights\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94cannot change that. In addition, the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on subsection headings, post, at 9, overlooks the fact that those headings were not actually enacted by Congress. See \n\xe2\x80\x933072.\n\t\n
\n\n6\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0The dissent criticizes us for allegedly concluding that a biological father qualifies for \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive\xe2\x80\x9d statutory protections \xe2\x80\x9conly when [he] has physical or state-recognized legal custody.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 2, 6\xe2\x80\x937. But the dissent undercuts its own point when it states that \xe2\x80\x9cnumerous\xe2\x80\x9d ICWA provisions not at issue here afford \xe2\x80\x9cmeaningful\xe2\x80\x9d protections to biological fathers regardless of whether they ever had custody. Post, at 4\xe2\x80\x937, and nn.\xc2\xa01, 2.\n\t\n
\n\n7\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0In an effort to rebut our supposed conclusion that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress could not possibly have intended\xe2\x80\x9d to require legal termination of Biological Father\xe2\x80\x99s rights with respect to Baby Girl, the dissent asserts that a minority of States afford (or used to afford) protection to similarly situated biological fathers. See post, at 17\xe2\x80\x9318, and n.\xc2\xa012 (emphasis added). This is entirely beside the point, because we merely conclude that, based on the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text and structure, Congress did not extend the heightened protections of \xc2\xa71912(d) and \xc2\xa71912(f) to all biological fathers. The fact that state laws may provide certain protections to biological fathers who have abandoned their children and who have never had custody of their children in no way undermines our analysis of these two federal statutory provisions.\n\t\n
\n\n8\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0The dissent claims that our reasoning \xe2\x80\x9cnecessarily extends to all Indian parents who have never had custody of their children,\xe2\x80\x9d even if those parents have visitation rights. Post, at 2\xe2\x80\x933, 13\xe2\x80\x9314. As an initial matter, the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s concern about the effect of our decision on individuals with visitation rights will be implicated, at most, in a relatively small class of cases. For example, our interpretation of \xc2\xa71912(d) would implicate the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s concern only in the case of a parent who abandoned his or her child prior to birth and never had physical or legal custody, but did have some sort of visitation rights. Moreover, in cases where this concern is implicated, such parents might receive \xe2\x80\x9ccomparable\xe2\x80\x9d protections under state law. See post, at 15. And in any event, it is the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation that would have far-reaching consequences: Under the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s reading, any biological parent\xe2\x80\x94even a sperm donor\xe2\x80\x94would enjoy the heightened protections of \xc2\xa71912(d) and\xc2\xa71912(f), even if he abandoned the mother and the child immediately after conception. Post, at 14, n.\xc2\xa08.\n\t\n
\n\n9\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0Biological Father and the Solicitor General argue that a tribeor state agency could provide the requisite remedial services under \xc2\xa71912(d). Brief for Respondent Birth Father 43; Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 22. But what if they don\xe2\x80\x99t? And if they don\xe2\x80\x99t, would the adoptive parents have to undertake the task?\n\t\n
\n\n10\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0The dissent repeatedly mischaracterizes our opinion. As our detailed discussion of the terms of the ICWA makes clear, our decisionis not based on a \xe2\x80\x9c[p]olicy disagreement with Congress\xe2\x80\x99 judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 2; see also post, at 8, 21.\n\t\n
\n\n11\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0Section 1915(c) also provides that, in the case of an adoptive placement under \xc2\xa71915(a), \xe2\x80\x9cif the Indian child\xe2\x80\x99s tribe shall establish a different order of preference by resolution, the agency or court effecting the placement shall follow such order so long as the placement is the least restrictive setting appropriate to the particular needs of the child, as provided in [\xc2\xa71915(b)].\xe2\x80\x9d Although we need not decide the issuehere, it may be the case that an Indian child\xe2\x80\x99s tribe could alter \xc2\xa71915\xe2\x80\x99s preferences in a way that includes a biological father whose rights were terminated, but who has now reformed. See \xc2\xa71915(c). If a tribe were to take such an approach, however, the court would still have the power to determine whether \xe2\x80\x9cgood cause\xe2\x80\x9d exists to disregard the tribe\xe2\x80\x99s order of preference. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71915(a), (c); In\xc2\xa0re Adoption of T. R. M., 525 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 298, 313 (Ind. 1988).\n\t\n
\n\n12\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0To be sure, an employee of the Cherokee Nation testified that the Cherokee Nation certifies families to be adoptive parents and that there are approximately 100 such families \xe2\x80\x9cthat are ready to take children that want to be adopted.\xe2\x80\x9d Record 446. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in full but write separately to explain why constitutional avoidance compels this out-come. Each party in this case has put forward a plausible interpretation of the relevant sections of the Indian Child Welfare Act (ICWA). However, the interpretations offered by respondent Birth Father and the United States raise significant constitutional problems as applied to this case. Because the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision avoids those problems, I concur in its interpretation.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis case arises out of a contested state-court adoption proceeding. Adoption proceedings are adjudicated in state family courts across the country every day, and \xe2\x80\x9cdomestic relations\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9can area that has long been regarded as a virtually exclusive province of the States.\xe2\x80\x9d Sosna v. Iowa, \n\n419 U.\xc2\xa0S. 393, \n404 (1975)\n. Indeed, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he whole subject of the domestic relations of husband and wife, parent and child, belongs to the laws of the States and not to the laws of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d In re Burrus, \n\n136 U.\xc2\xa0S. 586\n\xe2\x80\x93594 (1890). Nevertheless, when Adoptive Couple filed a petition in South Carolina Family Court to finalize their adoption of Baby Girl, Birth Father, who had relinquished his parental rights via a text message to Birth Mother, claimed a federal right under the ICWA to block the adoption and to obtain custody.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe ICWA establishes \xe2\x80\x9cfederal standards that govern state-court child custody proceedings involving Indian children.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 2. The ICWA defines \xe2\x80\x9cIndian child\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cany unmarried person who is under age eighteen and is either (a) a member of an Indian tribe or (b) is eligible for membership in an Indian tribe and is the biological child of a member of an Indian tribe.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n25 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71903(4). As relevant, the ICWA defines \xe2\x80\x9cchild custody proceeding,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71903(1), to include \xe2\x80\x9cadoptive placement,\xe2\x80\x9d which means \xe2\x80\x9cthe permanent placement of an Indian child for adoption, including any action resulting in a final decree of adoption,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71903(1)(iv), and \xe2\x80\x9ctermination of parental rights,\xe2\x80\x9d which means \xe2\x80\x9cany action resulting in the termination of the parent-child relationship,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71903(1)(ii).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe ICWA restricts a state court\xe2\x80\x99s ability to terminate the parental rights of an Indian parent in two relevant ways. Section 1912(f) prohibits a state court from involuntarily terminating parental rights \xe2\x80\x9cin the absence of a determination, supported by evidence beyond a reasonable doubt, including testimony of qualified expert witnesses, that the continued custody of the child by the parent or Indian custodian is likely to result in serious emotional or physical damage to the child.\xe2\x80\x9d Section 1912(d) prohibits a state court from terminating parental rights until the court is satisfied \xe2\x80\x9cthat active efforts have been made to provide remedial services and rehabilitative programs designed to prevent the breakup of the Indian family and that these efforts have proved unsuccessful.\xe2\x80\x9d A third provision creates specific placement preferences for the adoption of Indian children, which favor placement with Indians over other adoptive families. \xc2\xa71915(a). Operating together, these requirements often lead to different outcomes than would result under state law. That is precisely what happened here. See ante, at 6 (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is undisputed that, had Baby Girl not been 3/256 Cherokee, Biological Father would have had no right to object to her adoption under South Carolina law\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe ICWA recognizes States\xe2\x80\x99 inherent \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction over Indian child custody proceedings,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71901(5), but asserts that federal regulation is necessary because States \xe2\x80\x9chave often failed to recognize the essential tribal relations of Indian people and the cultural and social standards pre- ailing in Indian communities and families,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. However, Congress may regulate areas of traditional state concern only if the Constitution grants it such power. Admt. 10 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe powers not delegated to the United States by the Constitution, nor prohibited by it to the States, are reserved to the States respectively, or to the people\xe2\x80\x9d). The threshold question, then, is whether the Constitution grants Congress power to override state custody law whenever an Indian is involved.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe ICWA asserts that the Indian Commerce Clause, Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa78, cl.\xc2\xa03, and \xe2\x80\x9cother constitutional authority\xe2\x80\x9d provides Congress with \xe2\x80\x9cplenary power over Indian affairs.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71901(1). The reference to \xe2\x80\x9cother constitutional authority\xe2\x80\x9d is not illuminating, and I am aware of no other enumer-ated power that could even arguably support Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intrusion into this area of traditional state authority. See Fletcher, The Supreme Court and Federal Indian Policy, 85 Neb. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 121, 137 (2006) (\xe2\x80\x9cAs a matter of federal constitutional law, the Indian Commerce Clause grants Congress the only explicit constitutional authority to deal with Indian tribes\xe2\x80\x9d); Natelson, The Original Understanding of the Indian Commerce Clause, 85 Denver U. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 201, 210 (2007) (hereinafter Natelson) (evaluating, and rejecting, other potential sources of authority supporting congressional power over Indians). The assertion of plenary authority must, therefore, stand or fall on Congress\xe2\x80\x99 power under the Indian Commerce Clause. Although this Court has said that the \xe2\x80\x9ccentral function of the Indian Commerce Clause is to provide Congress with plenary power to legislate in the field of Indian affairs,\xe2\x80\x9d Cotton Petroleum Corp. v. New Mexico, \n\n490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 163, \n192 (1989)\n, neither the text nor the original understanding of the Clause supports Congress\xe2\x80\x99 claim to such \xe2\x80\x9cplenary\xe2\x80\x9d power.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Indian Commerce Clause gives Congress authority \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o regulate Commerce .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. with the Indian tribes.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa78, cl. 3 (emphasis added). \xe2\x80\x9cAt the time the original Constitution was ratified, \xe2\x80\x98commerce\xe2\x80\x99 consisted of selling, buying, and bartering, as well as transporting for these purposes.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Lopez, \n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 549, \n585 (1995)\n (Thomas, J., concurring). See also 1 S. Johnson, A Dictionary of the English Language 361 (4th rev. ed. 1773) (reprint 1978) (defining commerce as \xe2\x80\x9cIntercourse; exchange of one thing for another; interchange of any thing; trade; traffick\xe2\x80\x9d). \xe2\x80\x9c[W]hen Federalists and Anti-Federalists discussed the Commerce Clause during the ratification period, they often used trade (in its selling/bartering sense) and commerce interchangeably.\xe2\x80\x9d Lopez, supra, at 586 (Thomas, J., concurring). The term \xe2\x80\x9ccommerce\xe2\x80\x9d did not include economic activity such as \xe2\x80\x9cmanufacturing and agriculture,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., let alone noneconomic activity such as adoption of children.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFurthermore, the term \xe2\x80\x9ccommerce with Indian tribes\xe2\x80\x9d was invariably used during the time of the founding to mean \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98trade with Indians.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d See, e.g., Natelson, 215\xe2\x80\x93216, and n.\xc2\xa097 (citing 18th-century sources); Report of Committee on Indian Affairs (Feb 20, 1787), in 32 Journals of the Continental Congress 1774\xe2\x80\x931789, pp. 66, 68 (R. Hill ed. 1936) (hereinafter J. Cont\xe2\x80\x99l Cong.) (using the phrase \xe2\x80\x9ccommerce with the Indians\xe2\x80\x9d to mean trade with the Indians). And regulation of Indian commerce generally referred to legal structures governing \xe2\x80\x9cthe conduct of the merchants engaged in the Indian trade, the nature of the goods they sold, the prices charged, and similar matters.\xe2\x80\x9d Natelson 216, and n. 99.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Indian Commerce Clause contains an additional textual limitation relevant to this case: Congress is given the power to regulate Commerce \xe2\x80\x9cwith the Indian tribes.\xe2\x80\x9d The Clause does not give Congress the power to regulate commerce with all Indian persons any more than the Foreign Commerce Clause gives Congress the power to regulate commerce with all foreign nationals traveling within the United States. A straightforward reading of the text, thus, confirms that Congress may only regulate commercial interactions\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9ccommerce\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94taking place with established Indian communities\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9ctribes.\xe2\x80\x9d That power is far from \xe2\x80\x9cplenary.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress\xe2\x80\x99 assertion of \xe2\x80\x9cplenary power\xe2\x80\x9d over Indian affairs is also inconsistent with the history of the Indian Commerce Clause. At the time of the founding, the Clause was understood to reserve to the States general police powers with respect to Indians who were citizens of the several States. The Clause instead conferred on Congress the much narrower power to regulate trade with Indian tribes\xe2\x80\x94that is, Indians who had not been incorporated into the body-politic of any State.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBefore the Revolution, most Colonies adopted their own regulations governing Indian trade. See Natelson 219, and n. 121 (citing colonial laws). Such regulations were necessary because colonial traders all too often abused their Indian trading partners, through fraud, exorbitant prices, extortion, and physical invasion of Indian territory, among other things. See 1 F. Prucha, The Great Father 18\xe2\x80\x9320 (1984) (hereinafter Prucha); Natelson 220, and n. 122. These abuses sometimes provoked violent Indian retaliation. See Prucha 20. To mitigate these conflicts, most Colonies extensively regulated traders engaged in commerce with Indian tribes. See e.g., Ordinance to Regulate Indian Affairs, Statutes of South Carolina (Aug. 31, 1751), in 16 Early American Indian Documents: Treaties and Laws, 1607\xe2\x80\x931789, pp. 331\xe2\x80\x93334 (A. Vaughan and D. Rosen eds. 1998).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Over time, commercial regulation at the colonial level proved largely ineffective, in part because \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here was no uniformity among the colonies, no two sets of like regulations.\xe2\x80\x9d Prucha 21.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRecognizing the need for uniform regulation of trade with the Indians, Benjamin Franklin proposed his own \xe2\x80\x9carticles of confederation\xe2\x80\x9d to the Continental Congress on July 21, 1775, which reflected his view that central control over Indian affairs should predominate over local control. 2 J. Cont\xe2\x80\x99l Cong. 195\xe2\x80\x93199 (W. Ford ed. 1905). Franklin\xe2\x80\x99s proposal was not enacted, but in November 1775, Congress empowered a committee to draft regulations for the Indian trade. 3 id., at 364, 366. On July 12, 1776, the committee submitted a draft of the Articles of Confederation to Congress, which incorporated many of Franklin\xe2\x80\x99s proposals. 5 id., at 545, 546, n.\xc2\xa01. The draft prohibited States from waging offensive war against the Indians without congressional authorization and granted Congress the exclusive power to acquire land from the Indians out-side state boundaries, once those boundaries had been es-tablished. Id., at 549. This version also gave Congress \xe2\x80\x9cthe sole and exclusive Right and Power of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. Regulating the Trade, and managing all Affairs with the Indians.\xe2\x80\x9d Id. at 550.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOn August 20, 1776, the Committee of the Whole presented to Congress a revised draft, which provided Congress with \xe2\x80\x9cthe sole and exclusive right and power of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. regulating the trade, and managing all affairs with the Indians.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 672, 681\xe2\x80\x93682. Some delegates feared that the Articles gave Congress excessive power to in-terfere with States\xe2\x80\x99 jurisdiction over affairs with Indians residing within state boundaries. After further deliberation, the final result was a clause that included a broad grant of congressional authority with two significant exceptions: \xe2\x80\x9cThe United States in Congress assembled shall also have the sole and exclusive right and power of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. regulating the trade and managing all affairs with the Indians, not members of any of the States, provided that the legislative right of any State within its own limits be not infringed or violated.\xe2\x80\x9d Articles of Confederation, Art. IX, cl. 4. As a result, Congress retained exclusive jurisdiction over Indian affairs outside the borders of the States; the States retained exclusive jurisdiction over relations with Member-Indians;\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t and Congress and the States \xe2\x80\x9cex-ercise[d] concurrent jurisdiction over transactions with tribal Indians within state boundaries, but congressional decisions would have to be in compliance with local law.\xe2\x80\x9d Natelson 230. The drafting of the Articles of Confederation reveals the delegates\xe2\x80\x99 concern with protecting the power of the States to regulate Indian persons who were politically incorporated into the States. This concern for state power reemerged during the drafting of the Constitution.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe drafting history of the Constitutional Convention also supports a limited construction of the Indian Commerce Clause. On July 24, 1787, the convention elected a drafting committee\xe2\x80\x94the Committee of Detail\xe2\x80\x94and charged it to \xe2\x80\x9creport a Constitution conformable to the Res-olutions passed by the Convention.\xe2\x80\x9d 2 Records of the Federal Convention of 1787, p.106 (M. Farrand rev. 1966) (J. Madison). During the Committee\xe2\x80\x99s deliberations, John Rutledge, the chairman, suggested incorporating an In-dian affairs power into the Constitution. Id., at 137, n.\xc2\xa06, 143. The first draft reported back to the convention, however, provided Congress with authority \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o regulate commerce with foreign nations, and among the several States,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 181 (Madison) (Aug. 6, 1787), but did not include any specific Indian affairs clause. On August 18, James Madison proposed that the Federal Government be granted several additional powers, including the power \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o regulate affairs with the Indians as well within as without the limits of the U. States.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 324 (J. Madison) (emphasis added). On August 22, Rutledge delivered the Committee of Detail\xe2\x80\x99s second report, which modified Madison\xe2\x80\x99s proposed clause. The Committee proposed to add to Congress\xe2\x80\x99 power \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o regulate commerce with foreign nations, and among the several States\xe2\x80\x9d the words, \xe2\x80\x9cand with Indians, within the Limits of any State, not subject to the laws thereof.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 366\xe2\x80\x93367 (Journal). The Committee\xe2\x80\x99s version, which echoed the Articles of Confederation, was far narrower than Madison\xe2\x80\x99s proposal. On August 31, the revised draft was submitted to a Committee of Eleven for further action. Id., at 473 (Journal), 481 (J. Madison). That Committee recommended adding to the Commerce Clause the phrase, \xe2\x80\x9cand with the Indian tribes,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 493, which the Convention ultimately adopted.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is, thus, clear that the Framers of the Constitution were alert to the difference between the power to regulate trade with the Indians and the power to regulate all In-dian affairs. By limiting Congress\xe2\x80\x99 power to the former, the Framers declined to grant Congress the same broad powers over Indian affairs conferred by the Articles of Confederation. See Prakash, Against Tribal Fungibility, 89 Cornell L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1069, 1090 (2004).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tDuring the ratification debates, opposition to the Indian Commerce Clause was nearly nonexistent. See Natelson 248 (noting that Robert Yates, a New York Anti-Federalist was \xe2\x80\x9calmost the only writer who objected to any part [of\xc2\xa0] of the Commerce Clause\xe2\x80\x94a clear indication that its scope was understood to be fairly narrow\xe2\x80\x9d (footnote omitted)). Given the Anti-Federalists\xe2\x80\x99 vehement opposition to the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s other grants of power to the Federal Government, this silence is revealing. The ratifiers almost certainly understood the Clause to confer a relatively modest power on Congress\xe2\x80\x94namely, the power to regulate trade with Indian tribes living beyond state borders. And this feature of the Constitution was welcomed by Federalists and Anti-Federalists alike due to the considerable interest in expanding trade with such Indian tribes. See, e.g., The Federalist No. 42, at 265 (J. Madison) (praising the Constitution for removing the obstacles that had existed under the Articles of Confederation to federal control over \xe2\x80\x9ctrade with Indians\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); 3 J. Elliot, The Debates in the Several State Conventions on the Adoption of the Federal Constitution 580 (2d ed. 1863) (Adam Stephens, at the Virginia ratifying convention, June 23, 1788, describing the Indian tribes residing near the Mississippi and \xe2\x80\x9cthe variety of articles which might be obtained to advantage by trading with these people\xe2\x80\x9d); The Federalist No. 24, at 158 (A. Hamilton) (arguing that frontier garrisons would \xe2\x80\x9cbe keys to the trade with the Indian nations\xe2\x80\x9d); Brutus, (Letter) X, N.\xc2\xa0Y. J., Jan. 24, 1788, in 15 The Documentary History of the Ratification of the Constitution 462, 465 (J. Kaminski & G. Saladino eds. 2012) (conceding that there must be a standing army for some purposes, including \xe2\x80\x9ctrade with Indians\xe2\x80\x9d). There is little evidence that the ratifiers of the Constitution understood the Indian Commerce Clause to confer anything resembling plenary power over Indian affairs. See Natelson 247\xe2\x80\x93250.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn light of the original understanding of the Indian Commerce Clause, the constitutional problems that would be created by application of the ICWA here are evident. First, the statute deals with \xe2\x80\x9cchild custody proceedings,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71903(1), not \xe2\x80\x9ccommerce.\xe2\x80\x9d It was enacted in response to concerns that \xe2\x80\x9can alarmingly high percentage of Indian families [were] broken up by the removal, often unwarranted, of their children from them by nontribal public and private agencies.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71901(4). The perceived problem was that many Indian children were \xe2\x80\x9cplaced in non-Indian foster and adoptive homes and institutions.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. This problem, however, had nothing to do with commerce.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSecond, the portions of the ICWA at issue here do not regulate Indian tribes as tribes. Sections 1912(d) and (f\xc2\xa0), and \xc2\xa71915(a) apply to all child custody proceedings involving an Indian child, regardless of whether an Indian tribe is involved. This case thus does not directly implicate Congress\xe2\x80\x99 power to \xe2\x80\x9clegislate in respect to Indian tribes.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Lara, \n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 193, \n200 (2004)\n (emphasis added). Baby Girl was never domiciled on an Indian Reservation, and the Cherokee Nation had no jurisdiction over her. Cf. Mississippi Band of Choctaw Indians v. Holyfield, \n\n490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 30\n\xe2\x80\x9354 (1989) (holding that the Indian Tribe had exclusive jurisdiction over child custody proceedings, even though the children were born off the reservation, because the children were \xe2\x80\x9cdomiciled\xe2\x80\x9d on the reservation for purposes of the ICWA). Although Birth Father is a registered member of The Cherokee Nation, he did not live on a reservation either. He was, thus, subject to the laws of the State in which he resided (Oklahoma) and of the State where his daughter resided during the custody proceedings (South Carolina). Nothing in the In-dian Commerce Clause permits Congress to enact spe- cial laws applicable to Birth Father merely because of his status as an Indian.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBecause adoption proceedings like this one involve neither \xe2\x80\x9ccommerce\xe2\x80\x9d nor \xe2\x80\x9cIndian tribes,\xe2\x80\x9d there is simply no constitutional basis for Congress\xe2\x80\x99 assertion of authority over such proceedings. Also, the notion that Congress can direct state courts to apply different rules of evidence and procedure merely because a person of Indian descent is involved raises absurd possibilities. Such plenary power would allow Congress to dictate specific rules of criminal procedure for state-court prosecutions against Indian defendants. Likewise, it would allow Congress to substitute federal law for state law when contract disputes involve Indians. But the Constitution does not grant Congress power to override state law whenever that law happens to be applied to Indians. Accordingly, application of the ICWA to these child custody proceedings would be unconstitutional.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA casual reader of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion could be forgiven for thinking this an easy case, one in which the text of the applicable statute clearly points the way to the only sen- sible result. In truth, however, the path from the text of the Indian Child Welfare Act of 1978 (ICWA) to the result the Court reaches is anything but clear, and its result anything but right.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe reader\xe2\x80\x99s first clue that the majority\xe2\x80\x99s supposedly straightforward reasoning is flawed is that not all Members who adopt its interpretation believe it is compelled by the text of the statute, see ante, at 1 (Thomas, J., concurring); nor are they all willing to accept the consequences it will necessarily have beyond the specific factual sce- nario confronted here, see ante, at 1 (Breyer, J., concurring). The second clue is that the majority begins its analysis by plucking out of context a single phrase from the last clause of the last subsection of the relevant provision, and then builds its entire argument upon it. That is not how we ordinarily read statutes. The third clue is that the major- ity openly professes its aversion to Congress\xe2\x80\x99 explicitly stated purpose in enacting the statute. The majority ex- presses concern that reading the Act to mean what it says will make it more difficult to place Indian children in adoptive homes, see ante, at 14, 16, but the Congress that enacted the statute announced its intent to stop \xe2\x80\x9can alarmingly high percentage of Indian families [from being] broken up\xe2\x80\x9d by, among other things, a trend of \xe2\x80\x9cplac[ing] [Indian children] in non-Indian .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. adoptive homes.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n25 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71901(4). Policy disagreement with Congress\xe2\x80\x99 judg- ment is not a valid reason for this Court to distort the pro- visions of the Act. Unlike the majority, I cannot adopt a reading of ICWA that is contrary to both its text and its stated purpose. I respectfully dissent.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBeginning its reading with the last clause of \xc2\xa71912(f), the majority concludes that a single phrase appearing there\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9ccontinued custody\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94means that the entirety of the subsection is inapplicable to any parent, however committed, who has not previously had physical or legal custody of his child. Working back to front, the majority then concludes that \xc2\xa71912(d), tainted by its association with \xc2\xa71912(f), is also inapplicable; in the majority\xe2\x80\x99s view, a family bond that does not take custodial form is not a family bond worth preserving from \xe2\x80\x9cbreakup.\xe2\x80\x9d Because there are apparently no limits on the contaminating power of this single phrase, the majority does not stop there. Under its reading, \xc2\xa71903(9), which makes biological fathers \xe2\x80\x9cparent[s]\xe2\x80\x9d under this federal statute (and where, again, the phrase \xe2\x80\x9ccontinued custody\xe2\x80\x9d does not appear), has substantive force only when a birth father has physical or state-recognized legal custody of his daughter.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhen it excludes noncustodial biological fathers from the Act\xe2\x80\x99s substantive protections, this textually backward reading misapprehends ICWA\xe2\x80\x99s structure and scope. Moreover, notwithstanding the majority\xe2\x80\x99s focus on the per- ceived parental shortcomings of Birth Father, its rea- soning necessarily extends to all Indian parents who have never had custody of their children, no matter how fully those parents have embraced the financial and emotional responsibilities of parenting. The majority thereby transforms a statute that was intended to provide uniform federal standards for child custody proceedings involving Indian children and their biological parents into an illogical piecemeal scheme.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBetter to start at the beginning and consider the operation of the statute as a whole. Cf. ante, at 13 (\xe2\x80\x9c[S]tatutory construction \xe2\x80\x98is a holistic endeavor[,]\xe2\x80\x99 and .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. \xe2\x80\x98[a] provision that may seem ambiguous in isolation is often clarified by the remainder of the statutory scheme\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting United Sav. Assn. of Tex. v. Timbers of Inwood Forest Associates, Ltd., \n\n484 U.\xc2\xa0S. 365, \n371 (1988)\n)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tICWA commences with express findings. Congress rec- ognized that \xe2\x80\x9cthere is no resource that is more vital to the continued existence and integrity of Indian tribes than their children,\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n25 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71901(3), and it found that this resource was threatened. State authorities insufficiently sensitive to \xe2\x80\x9cthe essential tribal relations of Indian people and the cultural and social standards prevailing in Indian communities and families\xe2\x80\x9d were breaking up Indian families and moving Indian children to non-Indian homes and institutions. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71901(4)\xe2\x80\x93(5). As \xc2\xa71901(4) makes clear, and as this Court recognized in Mississippi Band of Choctaw Indians v. Holyfield, \n\n490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 30, \n33 (1989)\n, adoptive placements of Indian children with non-Indian families contributed significantly to the overall problem. See \xc2\xa71901(4) (finding that \xe2\x80\x9can alarmingly high percentage of [Indian] children are placed in non-Indian .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. adoptive homes\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tConsistent with these findings, Congress declared its purpose \xe2\x80\x9cto protect the best interests of Indian children and to promote the stability and security of Indian tribes and families by the establishment of minimum Federal standards\xe2\x80\x9d applicable to child custody proceedings involving Indian children. \xc2\xa71902. Section 1903 then goes on to establish the reach of these protections through its defi- nitional provisions. For present purposes, two of these definitions are crucial to understanding the statute\xe2\x80\x99s full scope.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFirst, ICWA defines the term \xe2\x80\x9cparent\xe2\x80\x9d broadly to mean \xe2\x80\x9cany biological parent .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of an Indian child or any In- dian person who has lawfully adopted an Indian child.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71903(9). It is undisputed that Baby Girl is an \xe2\x80\x9cIndian child\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning of the statute, see \xc2\xa71903(4); ante, at 2, n.\xc2\xa01, and Birth Father consequently qualifies as a \xe2\x80\x9cparent\xe2\x80\x9d under the Act. The statutory definition of parent \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not include the unwed father where paternity has not been acknowledged or established,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71903(9), but Birth Father\xe2\x80\x99s biological paternity has never been questioned by any party and was confirmed by a DNA test during the state court proceedings, App. to Pet. for Cert. 109a (Sealed).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners and Baby Girl\xe2\x80\x99s guardian ad litem devote many pages of briefing to arguing that the term \xe2\x80\x9cparent\xe2\x80\x9d should be defined with reference to the law of the State in which an ICWA child custody proceeding takes place. See Brief for Petitioners 19\xe2\x80\x9329; Brief for Respondent Guardian Ad Litem 32\xe2\x80\x9341. These arguments, however, are inconsistent with our recognition in Holyfield that Congress intended the critical terms of the statute to have uniform federal definitions. See 490 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 44\xe2\x80\x9345. It is therefore unsurprising, although far from unimportant, that the majority assumes for the purposes of its analysis that Birth Father is an ICWA \xe2\x80\x9cparent.\xe2\x80\x9d See ante, at 7.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn other words, the prospective adoptive couple have to make an evidentiary showing, not undertake person-to-person remedial outreach. The services themselves might be attempted by the Indian child\xe2\x80\x99s Tribe, a state agency, or a private adoption agency. Such remedial efforts are a familiar requirement of child welfare law, including fed- eral child welfare policy. See \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7671(a)(15)(B) (requiring States receiving federal funds for foster care and adoption assistance to make \xe2\x80\x9creasonable efforts .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to preserve and reunify families\xe2\x80\x9d prior to foster care placement or removal of a child from its home).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThere is nothing \xe2\x80\x9cbizarre,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 14, about placing on the party seeking to terminate a father\xe2\x80\x99s parental rights the burden of showing that the step is necessary as well as justified. \xe2\x80\x9cFor .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. natural parents, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the consequence of an erroneous termination [of parental rights] is the unnecessary destruction of their natural family.\xe2\x80\x9d Santosky, 455 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 766. In any event, the question is a nonissue in this case given the family court\xe2\x80\x99s finding that Birth Father is \xe2\x80\x9ca fit and proper person to have custody of his child\xe2\x80\x9d who \xe2\x80\x9chas demonstrated [his] ability to parent effectively\xe2\x80\x9d and who possesses \xe2\x80\x9cunwavering love for this child.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 128a (Sealed). Petitioners cannot show that rehabilitative efforts have \xe2\x80\x9cproved unsuccessful,\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n25 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71912(d), because Birth Father is not in need of rehabilitation.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t11\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOn a more general level, the majority intimates that ICWA grants Birth Father an undeserved windfall: in the majority\xe2\x80\x99s words, an \xe2\x80\x9cICWA trump card\xe2\x80\x9d he can \xe2\x80\x9cplay .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. at the eleventh hour to override the mother\xe2\x80\x99s decision and the child\xe2\x80\x99s best interests.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16. The implicit argument is that Congress could not possibly have intended to recognize a parent-child relationship between Birth Father and Baby Girl that would have to be legally terminated (either by valid consent or involuntary termination) before the adoption could proceed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut this supposed anomaly is illusory. In fact, the law of at least 15 States did precisely that at the time ICWA was passed.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t12\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t And the law of a number of States still does so. The State of Arizona, for example, requires that notice of an adoption petition be given to all \xe2\x80\x9cpotential father[s]\xe2\x80\x9d and that they be informed of their \xe2\x80\x9cright to seek custody.\xe2\x80\x9d Ariz. Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa78\xe2\x80\x93106(G)\xe2\x80\x93(J) (West Supp. 2012). In Washington, an \xe2\x80\x9calleged father[\xe2\x80\x99s]\xe2\x80\x9d consent to adoption is required absent the termination of his parental rights, Wash. Rev. Code \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa726.33.020(1), 26.33.160(1)(b) (2012); and those rights may be terminated only \xe2\x80\x9cupon a showing by clear, cogent, and convincing evidence\xe2\x80\x9d not only that termination is in the best interest of the child and that the father is withholding his consent to adoption contrary to child\xe2\x80\x99s best interests, but also that the father \xe2\x80\x9chas failed to perform parental duties under circumstances showing a substantial lack of regard for his parental obligations,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa726.33.120(2).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t13\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWithout doubt, laws protecting biological fathers\xe2\x80\x99 parental rights can lead\xe2\x80\x94even outside the context of ICWA\xe2\x80\x94to outcomes that are painful and distressing for both would-be adoptive families, who lose a much wanted child, and children who must make a difficult transition. See, e.g., In\xc2\xa0re Adoption of Tobias D., 2012 Me. 45, \xc2\xb627, 40 A. 3d 990, 999 (recognizing that award of custody of 2\xc2\xbd-year-old child to biological father under applicable state law once paternity is established will result in the \xe2\x80\x9cdifficult and pain- ful\xe2\x80\x9d necessity of \xe2\x80\x9cremoving the child from the only home he has ever known\xe2\x80\x9d). On the other hand, these rules recognize that biological fathers have a valid interest in a relationship with their child. See supra, at 6. And children have a reciprocal interest in knowing their biological parents. See Santosky, 455 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 760\xe2\x80\x93761, n.\xc2\xa011 (describing the foreclosure of a newborn child\xe2\x80\x99s opportunity to \xe2\x80\x9cever know his natural parents\xe2\x80\x9d as a \xe2\x80\x9clos[s] [that] cannot be measured\xe2\x80\x9d). These rules also reflect the understanding that the biological bond between a parent and a child is a strong foundation on which a stable and caring relationship may be built. Many jurisdictions apply a custodial preference for a fit natural parent over a party lacking this biological link. See, e.g., Ex\xc2\xa0parte Terry, 494 So.\xc2\xa02d 628, 632 (Ala. 1986); Appeal of H.\xc2\xa0R., 581 A.\xc2\xa02d 1141, 1177 (D.\xc2\xa0C. 1990) (opinion of Ferren, J.); Stuhr v. Stuhr, 240 Neb. 239, 245, 481 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 212, 216 (1992); In\xc2\xa0re Michael B., 80 N.\xc2\xa0Y. 2d 299, 309, 604 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 122, 127 (1992). Cf. Smith v. Organization of Foster Families For Equality & Reform, \n\n431 U.\xc2\xa0S. 816, \n845 (1977)\n (distinguishing a natural parent\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cliberty interest in family privacy,\xe2\x80\x9d which has its source \xe2\x80\x9cin intrinsic human rights,\xe2\x80\x9d with a foster parent\xe2\x80\x99s parallel interest in his or her relationship with a child, which has its \xe2\x80\x9corigins in an arrangement in which the State has been a partner from the outset\xe2\x80\x9d). This preference is founded in the \xe2\x80\x9cpresumption that fit parents act in the best interests of their children.\xe2\x80\x9d Troxel v. Granville, \n\n530 U.\xc2\xa0S. 57, \n68 (2000)\n (plurality opinion). \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[H]istorically [the law] has recognized that natural bonds of affection [will] lead parents\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to promote their child\xe2\x80\x99s well-being. Ibid. (quoting Parham v. J.\xc2\xa0R., \n\n442 U.\xc2\xa0S. 584, \n602 (1979)\n).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBalancing the legitimate interests of unwed biological fathers against the need for stability in a child\xe2\x80\x99s family situation is difficult, to be sure, and States have, over the years, taken different approaches to the problem. Some States, like South Carolina, have opted to hew to the con- stitutional baseline established by this Court\xe2\x80\x99s prece- dents and do not require a biological father\xe2\x80\x99s consent to adoption unless he has provided financial support during pregnancy. See Quilloin v. Walcott, \n\n434 U.\xc2\xa0S. 246\n\xe2\x80\x93256 (1978); Lehr, 463 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 261. Other States, how- ever, have decided to give the rights of biological fathers more robust protection and to afford them consent rights on the basis of their biological link to the child. At the time that ICWA was passed, as noted, over one-fourth of States did so. See supra, at 17\xe2\x80\x9318.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tICWA, on a straightforward reading of the statute, is consistent with the law of those States that protected, and protect, birth fathers\xe2\x80\x99 rights more vigorously. This reading can hardly be said to generate an anomaly. ICWA, as all acknowledge, was \xe2\x80\x9cthe product of rising concern .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [about] abusive child welfare practices that resulted in the separation of large numbers of Indian children from their families.\xe2\x80\x9d Holyfield, 490 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 32. It stands to reason that the Act would not render the legal status of an Indian father\xe2\x80\x99s relationship with his biological child fragile, but would instead grant it a degree of protection commensurate with the more robust state-law standards.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t14\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tC\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority also protests that a contrary result to the one it reaches would interfere with the adoption of Indian children. Ante, at 14, 16. This claim is the most perplexing of all. A central purpose of ICWA is to \xe2\x80\x9cpromote the stability and security of Indian .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. families,\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n25 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71902, in part by countering the trend of placing \xe2\x80\x9can alarmingly high percentage of [Indian] children .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in non-Indian foster and adoptive homes and institutions.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71901(4). The Act accomplishes this goal by, first, protecting the familial bonds of Indian parents and children, see supra, at 4\xe2\x80\x9312; and, second, establishing placement preferences should an adoption take place, see \xc2\xa71915(a). ICWA does not interfere with the adoption of Indian children except to the extent that it attempts to avert the necessity of adoptive placement and makes adoptions of Indian children by non-Indian families less likely.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority may consider this scheme unwise. But no principle of construction licenses a court to interpret a statute with a view to averting the very consequences Congress expressly stated it was trying to bring about. Instead, it is the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98judicial duty to give faithful meaning to the language Congress adopted in the light of the evi- dent legislative purpose in enacting the law in question.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Graham County Soil and Water Conservation Dist. v. United States ex\xc2\xa0rel. Wilson, \n\n559 U.\xc2\xa0S. 280, \n298 (2010)\n (quoting United States v. Bornstein, \n\n423 U.\xc2\xa0S. 303, \n310 (1976)\n).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority further claims that its reading is consistent with the \xe2\x80\x9cprimary\xe2\x80\x9d purpose of the Act, which in the majority\xe2\x80\x99s view was to prevent the dissolution of \xe2\x80\x9cintact\xe2\x80\x9d Indian families. Ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310. We may not, however, give effect only to congressional goals we designate \xe2\x80\x9cprimary\xe2\x80\x9d while casting aside others classed as \xe2\x80\x9csecondary\xe2\x80\x9d; we must apply the entire statute Congress has written. While there are indications that central among Congress\xe2\x80\x99 concerns in enacting ICWA was the removal of Indian children from homes in which Indian parents or other guardians had custody of them, see, e.g., \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71901(4), 1902, Con- gress also recognized that \xe2\x80\x9cthere is no resource that is more vital to the continued existence and integrity of Indian tribes than their children,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71901(3). As we observed in Holyfield, ICWA protects not only Indian parents\xe2\x80\x99 interests but also those of Indian tribes. See 490 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 34, 52. A tribe\xe2\x80\x99s interest in its next generation of citizens is adversely affected by the placement of Indian children in homes with no connection to the tribe, whether or not those children were initially in the custody of an Indian parent.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t15\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMoreover, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s focus on \xe2\x80\x9cintact\xe2\x80\x9d families, ante, at 10, begs the question of what Congress set out to accomplish with ICWA. In an ideal world, perhaps all parents would be perfect. They would live up to their parental responsibilities by providing the fullest possible financial and emotional support to their children. They would never suffer mental health problems, lose their jobs, struggle with substance dependency, or encounter any of the other multitudinous personal crises that can make it difficult to meet these responsibilities. In an ideal world parents would never become estranged and leave their children caught in the middle. But we do not live in such a world. Even happy families do not always fit the custodial-parent mold for which the majority would reserve IWCA\xe2\x80\x99s substantive protections; unhappy families all too often do not. They are families nonetheless. Congress understood as much. ICWA\xe2\x80\x99s definitions of \xe2\x80\x9cparent\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ctermination of parental rights\xe2\x80\x9d provided in \xc2\xa71903 sweep broadly. They should be honored.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tD\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority does not rely on the theory pressed by petitioners and the guardian ad litem that the canon of constitutional avoidance compels the conclusion that ICWA is inapplicable here. See Brief for Petitioners 43\xe2\x80\x9351; Brief for Respondent Guardian Ad Litem 48\xe2\x80\x9358. It states instead that it finds the statute clear.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t16\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Ante, at 17. But the majority nevertheless offers the suggestion that a contrary result would create an equal protection problem. Ibid. Cf. Brief for Petitioners 44\xe2\x80\x9347; Brief for Respondent Guardian Ad Litem 53\xe2\x80\x9355.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is difficult to make sense of this suggestion in light of our precedents, which squarely hold that classifications based on Indian tribal membership are not impermissible racial classifications. See United States v. Antelope, \n\n430 U.\xc2\xa0S. 641\n\xe2\x80\x93647 (1977); Morton v. Mancari, \n\n417 U.\xc2\xa0S. 535\n\xe2\x80\x93554 (1974). The majority\xe2\x80\x99s repeated, analytically unnecessary references to the fact that Baby Girl is 3/256 Cherokee by ancestry do nothing to elucidate its intimation that the statute may violate the Equal Protection Clause as applied here. See ante, at 1, 6; see also ante, at 16 (stating that ICWA \xe2\x80\x9cwould put certain vulner- able children at a great disadvantage solely because an ancestor\xe2\x80\x94even a remote one\xe2\x80\x94was an Indian\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). I see no ground for this Court to second-guess the membership requirements of federally recognized Indian tribes, which are independent political entities. See Santa Clara Pueblo v. Martinez, \n\n436 U.\xc2\xa0S. 49, \n72, n. \n32 (1978)\n. I am particularly averse to doing so when the Federal Government requires Indian tribes, as a prerequisite for official recognition, to make \xe2\x80\x9cdescen[t] from a historical Indian tribe\xe2\x80\x9d a condition of membership. 25 CFR \xc2\xa783.7(e) (2012).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority\xe2\x80\x99s treatment of this issue, in the end, does no more than create a lingering mood of disapprobation of the criteria for membership adopted by the Cherokee Nation that, in turn, make Baby Girl an \xe2\x80\x9cIndian child\xe2\x80\x9d under the statute. Its hints at lurking constitutional problems are, by its own account, irrelevant to its statutory analysis, and accordingly need not detain us any longer.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBecause I would affirm the South Carolina Supreme Court on the ground that \xc2\xa71912 bars the termination of Birth Father\xe2\x80\x99s parental rights, I would not reach the question of the applicability of the adoptive placement preferences of \xc2\xa71915. I note, however, that the majority does not and cannot foreclose the possibility that on remand, Baby Girl\xe2\x80\x99s paternal grandparents or other members of the Cherokee Nation may formally petition for adoption of Baby Girl. If these parties do so, and if on remand Birth Father\xe2\x80\x99s parental rights are terminated so that an adoption becomes possible, they will then be entitled to consideration under the order of preference established in \xc2\xa71915. The majority cannot rule prospectively that \xc2\xa71915 would not apply to an adoption petition that has not yet been filed. Indeed, the statute applies \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n any adoptive placement of an Indian child under State law,\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n25 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71915(a) (emphasis added), and contains no temporal qualifications. It would indeed be an odd result for this Court, in the name of the child\xe2\x80\x99s best in- terests, cf. ante, at 15, to purport to exclude from the pro- ceedings possible custodians for Baby Girl, such as her paternal grandparents, who may have well-established relationships with her.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority opinion turns \xc2\xa71912 upside down, reading it from bottom to top in order to reach a conclusion that is manifestly contrary to Congress\xe2\x80\x99 express purpose in enacting ICWA: preserving the familial bonds between Indian parents and their children and, more broadly, Indian tribes\xe2\x80\x99 relationships with the future citizens who are \xe2\x80\x9cvital to [their] continued existence and integrity.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71901(3).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority casts Birth Father as responsible for the painful circumstances in this case, suggesting that he intervened \xe2\x80\x9cat the eleventh hour to override the mother\xe2\x80\x99s decision and the child\xe2\x80\x99s best interests,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 16. I have no wish to minimize the trauma of removing a 27-month-old child from her adoptive family. It bears remembering, however, that Birth Father took action to assert his parental rights when Baby Girl was four months old, as soon as he learned of the impending adoption. As the South Carolina Supreme Court recognized, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[h]ad the mandate of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. ICWA been followed [in 2010], .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. much potential anguish might have been avoided[;] and in any case the law cannot be applied so as automatically to \xe2\x80\x9creward those who obtain custody, whether lawfully or otherwise, and maintain it during any ensuing (and protracted) litigation.\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 398 S.\xc2\xa0C., at 652, 731 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 564 (quoting Holyfield, 490 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 53\xe2\x80\x9354).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority\xe2\x80\x99s hollow literalism distorts the statute and ignores Congress\xe2\x80\x99 purpose in order to rectify a perceived wrong that, while heartbreaking at the time, was a correct application of federal law and that in any case cannot be undone. Baby Girl has now resided with her father for 18 months. However difficult it must have been for her to leave Adoptive Couple\xe2\x80\x99s home when she was just over 2 years old, it will be equally devastating now if, at the age of 3\xc2\xbd, she is again removed from her home and sent to live halfway across the country. Such a fate is not foreordained, of course. But it can be said with certainty that the anguish this case has caused will only be compounded by today\xe2\x80\x99s decision.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI believe that the South Carolina Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment was correct, and I would affirm it. I respectfully dissent.\n\t\t\t
