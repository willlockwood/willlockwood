\n\t\t\t\tSHELBY COUNTY, ALABAMA v. HOLDER, ATTORNEY GENERAL, et\xc2\xa0al.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the district of columbia circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 12\xe2\x80\x9396.\xe2\x80\x83Argued February 27, 2013\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 25, 2013\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe Voting Rights Act of 1965 was enacted to address entrenched racial discrimination in voting, \xe2\x80\x9can insidious and pervasive evil which had been perpetuated in certain parts of our country through unremitting and ingenious defiance of the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d South Carolina v. Katzenbach, \n\n383 U.\xc2\xa0S. 301\n. Section 2 of the Act, which bans any \xe2\x80\x9cstandard, practice, or procedure\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cresults in a denial or abridgement of the right of any citizen .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to vote on account of race or color,\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71973(a), applies nationwide, is permanent, and is not at issue in this case. Other sections apply only to some parts of the country. Section 4 of the Act provides the \xe2\x80\x9ccoverage formula,\xe2\x80\x9d defining the \xe2\x80\x9ccovered jurisdictions\xe2\x80\x9d as States or political subdivisions that maintained tests or devices as prerequisites to voting, and had low voter registration or turnout, in the 1960s and early 1970s. \xc2\xa71973b(b). In those covered jurisdictions, \xc2\xa75 of the Act provides that no change in voting procedures can take effect until approved by specified federal authorities in Washington, D.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71973c(a). Such approval is known as \xe2\x80\x9cpreclearance.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe coverage formula and preclearance requirement were initially set to expire after five years, but the Act has been reauthorized several times. In 2006, the Act was reauthorized for an additional 25 years, but the coverage formula was not changed. Coverage still turned on whether a jurisdiction had a voting test in the 1960s or 1970s, and had low voter registration or turnout at that time. Shortly after the 2006 reauthorization, a Texas utility district sought to bail out from the Act\xe2\x80\x99s coverage and, in the alternative, challenged the Act\xe2\x80\x99s constitutionality. This Court resolved the challenge on statutory grounds, but expressed serious doubts about the Act\xe2\x80\x99s continued constitutionality. See Northwest Austin Municipal Util. Dist. No. One v. Holder, \n\n557 U.\xc2\xa0S. 193\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioner Shelby County, in the covered jurisdiction of Alabama, sued the Attorney General in Federal District Court in Washington, D.\xc2\xa0C., seeking a declaratory judgment that sections 4(b) and 5 are facially unconstitutional, as well as a permanent injunction against their enforcement. The District Court upheld the Act, finding that the evidence before Congress in 2006 was sufficient to justify reauthorizing \xc2\xa75 and continuing \xc2\xa74(b)\xe2\x80\x99s coverage formula. The D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit affirmed. After surveying the evidence in the record, that court accepted Congress\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that \xc2\xa72 litigation remained inadequate in the covered jurisdictions to protect the rights of minority voters, that \xc2\xa75 was therefore still necessary, and that the coverage formula continued to pass constitutional muster.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0Section 4 of the Voting Rights Act is unconstitutional; its formula can no longer be used as a basis for subjecting jurisdictions to preclearance. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9325. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0In Northwest Austin, this Court noted that the Voting Rights Act \xe2\x80\x9cimposes current burdens and must be justified by current needs\xe2\x80\x9d and concluded that \xe2\x80\x9ca departure from the fundamental principle of equal sovereignty requires a showing that a statute\xe2\x80\x99s disparate geographic coverage is sufficiently related to the problem that it targets.\xe2\x80\x9d 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 203. These basic principles guide review of the question presented here. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9317.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(1)\xc2\xa0State legislation may not contravene federal law. States retain broad autonomy, however, in structuring their governments and pursuing legislative objectives. Indeed, the \n \nTenth Amendment reserves to the States all powers not specifically granted to the Federal Government, including \xe2\x80\x9cthe power to regulate elections.\xe2\x80\x9d Gregory v. Ashcroft, \n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 452\n\xe2\x80\x93462. There is also a \xe2\x80\x9cfundamental principle of equal sovereignty\xe2\x80\x9d among the States, which is highly pertinent in assessing disparate treatment of States. Northwest Austin, supra, at 203.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Voting Rights Act sharply departs from these basic principles. It requires States to beseech the Federal Government for permission to implement laws that they would otherwise have the right to enact and execute on their own. And despite the tradition of equal sovereignty, the Act applies to only nine States (and additional counties). That is why, in 1966, this Court described the Act as \xe2\x80\x9cstringent\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cpotent,\xe2\x80\x9d Katzenbach, 383 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 308, 315, 337. The Court nonetheless upheld the Act, concluding that such an \xe2\x80\x9cuncommon exercise of congressional power\xe2\x80\x9d could be justified by \xe2\x80\x9cexceptional conditions.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 334. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9312.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(2)\xc2\xa0In 1966, these departures were justified by the \xe2\x80\x9cblight of racial discrimination in voting\xe2\x80\x9d that had \xe2\x80\x9cinfected the electoral process in parts of our country for nearly a century,\xe2\x80\x9d Katzenbach, 383 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 308. At the time, the coverage formula\xe2\x80\x94the means of linking the exercise of the unprecedented authority with the problem that warranted it\xe2\x80\x94made sense. The Act was limited to areas where Congress found \xe2\x80\x9cevidence of actual voting discrimination,\xe2\x80\x9d and the covered jurisdictions shared two characteristics: \xe2\x80\x9cthe use of tests and devices for voter registration, and a voting rate in the 1964 presidential election at least 12 points below the national average.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 330. The Court explained that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]ests and devices are relevant to voting discrimination because of their long history as a tool for perpetrating the evil; a low voting rate is pertinent for the obvious reason that widespread disenfranchisement must inevitably affect the number of actual voters.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The Court therefore concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthe coverage formula [was] rational in both practice and theory.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9313.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(3)\xc2\xa0Nearly 50 years later, things have changed dramatically. Largely because of the Voting Rights Act, \xe2\x80\x9c[v]oter turnout and registration rates\xe2\x80\x9d in covered jurisdictions \xe2\x80\x9cnow approach parity. Blatantly discriminatory evasions of federal decrees are rare. And minority candidates hold office at unprecedented levels.\xe2\x80\x9d Northwest Austin, supra, at 202. The tests and devices that blocked ballot access have been forbidden nationwide for over 40 years. Yet the Act has not eased \xc2\xa75\xe2\x80\x99s restrictions or narrowed the scope of \xc2\xa74\xe2\x80\x99s coverage formula along the way. Instead those extraordinary and unprecedented features have been reauthorized as if nothing has changed, and they have grown even stronger. Because \xc2\xa75 applies only to those jurisdictions singled out by \xc2\xa74, the Court turns to consider that provision. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9317.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0Section 4\xe2\x80\x99s formula is unconstitutional in light of current conditions. Pp.\xc2\xa017\xe2\x80\x9325.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(1)\xc2\xa0In 1966, the coverage formula was \xe2\x80\x9crational in both practice and theory.\xe2\x80\x9d Katzenbach, supra, at 330. It looked to cause (discriminatory tests) and effect (low voter registration and turnout), and tailored the remedy (preclearance) to those jurisdictions exhibiting both. By 2009, however, the \xe2\x80\x9ccoverage formula raise[d] serious constitutional questions.\xe2\x80\x9d Northwest Austin, supra, at 204. Coverage today is based on decades-old data and eradicated practices. The formula captures States by reference to literacy tests and low voter registration and turnout in the 1960s and early 1970s. But such tests have been banned for over 40 years. And voter registration and turnout numbers in covered States have risen dramatically. In 1965, the States could be divided into those with a recent history of voting tests and low voter registration and turnout and those without those characteristics. Congress based its coverage formula on that distinction. Today the Nation is no longer divided along those lines, yet the Voting Rights Act continues to treat it as if it were. Pp.\xc2\xa017\xe2\x80\x9318.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(2)\xc2\xa0The Government attempts to defend the formula on grounds that it is \xe2\x80\x9creverse-engineered\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94Congress identified the jurisdictions to be covered and then came up with criteria to describe them. Katzenbach did not sanction such an approach, reasoning instead that the coverage formula was rational because the \xe2\x80\x9cformula .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. was relevant to the problem.\xe2\x80\x9d 383 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 329, 330. The Government has a fallback argument\xe2\x80\x94because the formula was relevant in 1965, its continued use is permissible so long as any discrimination remains in the States identified in 1965. But this does not look to \xe2\x80\x9ccurrent political conditions,\xe2\x80\x9d Northwest Austin, supra, at 203, instead relying on a comparison between the States in 1965. But history did not end in 1965. In assessing the \xe2\x80\x9ccurrent need[\xc2\xa0]\xe2\x80\x9d for a preclearance system treating States differently from one another today, history since 1965 cannot be ignored. The \n \nFifteenth Amendment is not designed to punish for the past; its purpose is to ensure a better future. To serve that purpose, Congress\xe2\x80\x94if it is to divide the States\xe2\x80\x94must identify those jurisdictions to be singled out on a basis that makes sense in light of current conditions. Pp.\xc2\xa018\xe2\x80\x9321.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(3)\xc2\xa0Respondents also rely heavily on data from the record compiled by Congress before reauthorizing the Act. Regardless of how one looks at that record, no one can fairly say that it shows anything approaching the \xe2\x80\x9cpervasive,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cflagrant,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cwidespread,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9crampant\xe2\x80\x9d discrimination that clearly distinguished the covered jurisdictions from the rest of the Nation in 1965. Katzenbach, supra, at 308, 315, 331. But a more fundamental problem remains: Congress did not use that record to fashion a coverage formula grounded in current conditions. It instead re-enacted a formula based on 40-year-old facts having no logical relation to the present day. Pp.\xc2\xa021\xe2\x80\x9322.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Voting Rights Act of 1965 employed extraordinary measures to address an extraordinary problem. Section 5 of the Act required States to obtain federal permission before enacting any law related to voting\xe2\x80\x94a drastic departure from basic principles of federalism. And \xc2\xa74 of the Act applied that requirement only to some States\xe2\x80\x94an equally dramatic departure from the principle that all States enjoy equal sovereignty. This was strong medicine, but Congress determined it was needed to address entrenched racial discrimination in voting, \xe2\x80\x9can insidious and pervasive evil which had been perpetuated in certain parts of our country through unremitting and ingenious defiance of the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d South Carolina v. Katzenbach, \n\n383 U.\xc2\xa0S. 301, \n309 (1966)\n. As we explained in upholding the law, \xe2\x80\x9cexceptional conditions can justify legislative measures not otherwise appropriate.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 334. Reflecting the unprecedented nature of these measures, they were scheduled to expire after five years. See Voting Rights Act of 1965, \xc2\xa74(a), \n\n79Stat.\n438.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNearly 50 years later, they are still in effect; indeed, they have been made more stringent, and are now scheduled to last until 2031. There is no denying, however, that the conditions that originally justified these measures no longer characterize voting in the covered jurisdictions. By 2009, \xe2\x80\x9cthe racial gap in voter registration and turnout [was] lower in the States originally covered by \xc2\xa75 than it [was] nationwide.\xe2\x80\x9d Northwest Austin Municipal Util. Dist. No. One v. Holder, \n\n557 U.\xc2\xa0S. 193\n\xe2\x80\x93204 (2009). Since that time, Census Bureau data indicate that African-American voter turnout has come to exceed white voter turnout in five of the six States originally covered by \xc2\xa75, with a gap in the sixth State of less than one half of one percent. See Dept. of Commerce, Census Bureau, Re-ported Voting and Registration, by Sex, Race and His-panic Origin, for States (Nov. 2012) (Table 4b).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAt the same time, voting discrimination still exists; no one doubts that. The question is whether the Act\xe2\x80\x99s extraordinary measures, including its disparate treatment of the States, continue to satisfy constitutional requirements. As we put it a short time ago, \xe2\x80\x9cthe Act imposes current burdens and must be justified by current needs.\xe2\x80\x9d Northwest Austin, 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 203.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe \n \nFifteenth Amendment was ratified in 1870, in the wake of the Civil War. It provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he right of citizens of the United States to vote shall not be denied or abridged by the United States or by any State on account of race, color, or previous condition of servitude,\xe2\x80\x9d and it gives Congress the \xe2\x80\x9cpower to enforce this article by appropriate legislation.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cThe first century of congressional enforcement of the Amendment, however, can only be regarded as a failure.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 197. In the 1890s, Alabama, Georgia, Louisiana, Mississippi, North Carolina, South Carolina, and Virginia began to enact literacy tests for voter registration and to employ other methods designed to prevent African-Americans from voting. Katzenbach, 383 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 310. Congress passed statutes outlawing some of these practices and facilitating litigation against them, but litigation remained slow and expensive, and the States came up with new ways to discriminate as soon as existing ones were struck down. Voter registration of African-Americans barely improved. Id., at 313\xe2\x80\x93314.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tInspired to action by the civil rights movement, Congress responded in 1965 with the Voting Rights Act. Section 2 was enacted to forbid, in all 50 States, any \xe2\x80\x9cstandard, practice, or procedure .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. imposed or applied .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to deny or abridge the right of any citizen of the United States to vote on account of race or color.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n79Stat.\n437. The current version forbids any \xe2\x80\x9cstandard, practice, or procedure\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cresults in a denial or abridgement of the right of any citizen of the United States to vote on account of race or color.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71973(a). Both the Federal Government and individuals have sued to enforce \xc2\xa72, see, e.g., Johnson v. De Grandy, \n\n512 U.\xc2\xa0S. 997 (1994)\n, and injunctive relief is available in appropriate cases to block voting laws from going into effect, see \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71973j(d). Section 2 is permanent, applies nationwide, and is not at issue in this case.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOther sections targeted only some parts of the country. At the time of the Act\xe2\x80\x99s passage, these \xe2\x80\x9ccovered\xe2\x80\x9d jurisdictions were those States or political subdivisions that had maintained a test or device as a prerequisite to voting as of November 1, 1964, and had less than 50 percent voter registration or turnout in the 1964 Presidential election. \xc2\xa74(b), \n\n79Stat.\n438. Such tests or devices included literacy and knowledge tests, good moral character requirements, the need for vouchers from registered voters, and the like. \xc2\xa74(c), id., at 438\xe2\x80\x93439. A covered jurisdiction could \xe2\x80\x9cbail out\xe2\x80\x9d of coverage if it had not used a test or device in the preceding five years \xe2\x80\x9cfor the purpose or with the effect of denying or abridging the right to vote on account of race or color.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa74(a), id., at 438. In 1965, the covered States included Alabama, Georgia, Louisiana, Mississippi, South Carolina, and Virginia. The additional covered subdivisions included 39 counties in North Carolina and one in Arizona. See 28 CFR pt. 51, App. (2012).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn those jurisdictions, \xc2\xa74 of the Act banned all such tests or devices. \xc2\xa74(a), \n\n79Stat.\n438. Section 5 provided that no change in voting procedures could take effect until it was approved by federal authorities in Washington, D.\xc2\xa0C.\xe2\x80\x94either the Attorney General or a court of three judges. Id., at 439. A jurisdiction could obtain such \xe2\x80\x9cpreclearance\xe2\x80\x9d only by proving that the change had neither \xe2\x80\x9cthe purpose [nor] the effect of denying or abridging the right to vote on account of race or color.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSections 4 and 5 were intended to be temporary; they were set to expire after five years. See \xc2\xa74(a), id., at 438; Northwest Austin, supra, at 199. In South Carolina v. Katzenbach, we upheld the 1965 Act against constitutional challenge, explaining that it was justified to address \xe2\x80\x9cvoting discrimination where it persists on a pervasive scale.\xe2\x80\x9d 383 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 308.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 1970, Congress reauthorized the Act for another five years, and extended the coverage formula in \xc2\xa74(b) to jurisdictions that had a voting test and less than 50 percent voter registration or turnout as of 1968. Voting Rights Act Amendments of 1970, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73\xe2\x80\x934, \n\n84Stat.\n315. That swept in several counties in California, New Hampshire, and New York. See 28 CFR pt. 51, App. Congress also extended the ban in \xc2\xa74(a) on tests and devices nationwide. \xc2\xa76, \n\n84Stat.\n315.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 1975, Congress reauthorized the Act for seven more years, and extended its coverage to jurisdictions that had a voting test and less than 50 percent voter registration or turnout as of 1972. Voting Rights Act Amendments of 1975, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7101, 202, \n\n89Stat.\n400,\n401. Congress also amended the definition of \xe2\x80\x9ctest or device\xe2\x80\x9d to include the practice of providing English-only voting materials in places where over five percent of voting-age citizens spoke a single language other than English. \xc2\xa7203, id., at 401\xe2\x80\x93402. As a result of these amendments, the States of Alaska, Arizona, and Texas, as well as several counties in California, Flor-ida, Michigan, New York, North Carolina, and South Da-kota, became covered jurisdictions. See 28 CFR pt. 51, App. Congress correspondingly amended sections 2 and 5 to forbid voting discrimination on the basis of membership in a language minority group, in addition to discrimination on the basis of race or color. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7203, 206, \n\n89Stat.\n401,\n402. Finally, Congress made the nationwide ban on tests and devices permanent. \xc2\xa7102, id., at 400.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 1982, Congress reauthorized the Act for 25 years, but did not alter its coverage formula. See Voting Rights Act Amendments, \n\n96Stat.\n131. Congress did, however, amend the bailout provisions, allowing political subdivisions of covered jurisdictions to bail out. Among other prerequisites for bailout, jurisdictions and their subdivisions must not have used a forbidden test or device, failed to receive preclearance, or lost a \xc2\xa72 suit, in the ten years prior to seeking bailout. \xc2\xa72, id., at 131\xe2\x80\x93133.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe upheld each of these reauthorizations against constitutional challenge. See Georgia v. United States, \n\n411 U.\xc2\xa0S. 526 (1973)\n; City of Rome v. United States, \n\n446 U.\xc2\xa0S. 156 (1980)\n; Lopez v. Monterey County, \n\n525 U.\xc2\xa0S. 266 (1999)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 2006, Congress again reauthorized the Voting Rights Act for 25 years, again without change to its coverage formula. Fannie Lou Hamer, Rosa Parks, and Coretta Scott King Voting Rights Act Reauthorization and Amend-ments Act, \n\n120Stat.\n577. Congress also amended \xc2\xa75 to prohibit more conduct than before. \xc2\xa75, id., at 580\xe2\x80\x93 581; see Reno v. Bossier Parish School Bd., \n\n528 U.\xc2\xa0S. 320, \n341 (2000)\n (Bossier II); Georgia v. Ashcroft, \n\n539 U.\xc2\xa0S. 461, \n479 (2003)\n. Section 5 now forbids voting changes with \xe2\x80\x9cany discriminatory purpose\xe2\x80\x9d as well as voting changes that diminish the ability of citizens, on account of race, color, or language minority status, \xe2\x80\x9cto elect their preferred candidates of choice.\xe2\x80\x9d 42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71973c(b)\xe2\x80\x93(d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tShortly after this reauthorization, a Texas utility district brought suit, seeking to bail out from the Act\xe2\x80\x99s cover- age and, in the alternative, challenging the Act\xe2\x80\x99s constitutionality. See Northwest Austin, 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 200\xe2\x80\x93201. A three-judge District Court explained that only a State or political subdivision was eligible to seek bailout under the statute, and concluded that the utility district was not a political subdivision, a term that encompassed only \xe2\x80\x9ccounties, parishes, and voter-registering subunits.\xe2\x80\x9d Northwest Austin Municipal Util. Dist. No. One v. Mukasey, 573 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 221, 232 (DC 2008). The District Court also rejected the constitutional challenge. Id., at 283.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe reversed. We explained that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98normally the Court will not decide a constitutional question if there is some other ground upon which to dispose of the case.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Northwest Austin, supra, at 205 (quoting Escambia County v. McMillan, \n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 48, \n51 (1984)\n (per curiam)). Concluding that \xe2\x80\x9cunderlying constitutional concerns,\xe2\x80\x9d among other things, \xe2\x80\x9ccompel[led] a broader reading of the bailout provision,\xe2\x80\x9d we construed the statute to allow the utility district to seek bailout. Northwest Austin, 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 207. In doing so we expressed serious doubts about the Act\xe2\x80\x99s con-tinued constitutionality.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe explained that \xc2\xa75 \xe2\x80\x9cimposes substantial federalism costs\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cdifferentiates between the States, despite our his- toric tradition that all the States enjoy equal sovereignty.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 202, 203 (internal quotation marks omitted). We also noted that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]hings have changed in the South. Voter turnout and registration rates now approach parity. Blatantly discriminatory evasions of federal decrees are rare. And minority candidates hold office at unprece-dented levels.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 202. Finally, we questioned whether the problems that \xc2\xa75 meant to address were still \xe2\x80\x9cconcentrated in the jurisdictions singled out for preclearance.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 203.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEight Members of the Court subscribed to these views, and the remaining Member would have held the Act unconstitutional. Ultimately, however, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s construction of the bailout provision left the constitutional issues for another day.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tShelby County is located in Alabama, a covered jurisdiction. It has not sought bailout, as the Attorney General has recently objected to voting changes proposed from within the county. See App. 87a\xe2\x80\x9392a. Instead, in 2010, the county sued the Attorney General in Federal District Court in Washington, D.\xc2\xa0C., seeking a declaratory judgment that sections 4(b) and 5 of the Voting Rights Act are facially unconstitutional, as well as a permanent injunction against their enforcement. The District Court ruled against the county and upheld the Act. 811 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 424, 508 (2011). The court found that the evidence before Congress in 2006 was sufficient to justify reauthorizing \xc2\xa75 and continuing the \xc2\xa74(b) coverage formula.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court of Appeals for the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit affirmed. In assessing \xc2\xa75, the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit considered six primary categories of evidence: Attorney General objections to voting changes, Attorney General requests for more information regarding voting changes, successful \xc2\xa72 suits in covered jurisdictions, the dispatching of federal observers to monitor elections in covered jurisdictions, \xc2\xa75 preclearance suits involving covered jurisdictions, and the deterrent effect of \xc2\xa75. See 679 F.\xc2\xa03d 848, 862\xe2\x80\x93863 (2012). After extensive analysis of the record, the court accepted Congress\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that \xc2\xa72 litigation remained inadequate in the covered jurisdictions to protect the rights of minority voters, and that \xc2\xa75 was therefore still necessary. Id., at 873.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTurning to \xc2\xa74, the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit noted that the evidence for singling out the covered jurisdictions was \xe2\x80\x9cless robust\xe2\x80\x9d and that the issue presented \xe2\x80\x9ca close question.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 879. But the court looked to data comparing the number of successful \xc2\xa72 suits in the different parts of the country. Coupling that evidence with the deterrent effect of \xc2\xa75, the court concluded that the statute continued \xe2\x80\x9cto single out the jurisdictions in which discrimination is concentrated,\xe2\x80\x9d and thus held that the coverage formula passed constitutional muster. Id., at 883.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJudge Williams dissented. He found \xe2\x80\x9cno positive cor-relation between inclusion in \xc2\xa74(b)\xe2\x80\x99s coverage formula and low black registration or turnout.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 891. Rather, to the extent there was any correlation, it actually went the other way: \xe2\x80\x9ccondemnation under \xc2\xa74(b) is a marker of higher black registration and turnout.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added). Judge Williams also found that \xe2\x80\x9c[c]overed jurisdictions have far more black officeholders as a proportion of the black population than do uncovered ones.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 892. As to the evidence of successful \xc2\xa72 suits, Judge Williams disaggregated the reported cases by State, and concluded that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he five worst uncovered jurisdictions .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. have worse records than eight of the covered jurisdictions.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 897. He also noted that two covered jurisdictions\xe2\x80\x94Arizona and Alaska\xe2\x80\x94had not had any successful reported \xc2\xa72 suit brought against them during the entire 24 years covered by the data. Ibid. Judge Williams would have held the coverage formula of \xc2\xa74(b) \xe2\x80\x9cirrational\xe2\x80\x9d and unconstitutional. Id., at 885.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn Northwest Austin, we stated that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Act imposes current burdens and must be justified by current needs.\xe2\x80\x9d 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 203. And we concluded that \xe2\x80\x9ca departure from the fundamental principle of equal sovereignty requires a showing that a statute\xe2\x80\x99s disparate geographic coverage is sufficiently related to the problem that it targets.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. These basic principles guide our review of the question before us.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Constitution and laws of the United States are \xe2\x80\x9cthe supreme Law of the Land.\xe2\x80\x9d U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Art. VI, cl.\xc2\xa02. State legislation may not contravene federal law. The Federal Government does not, however, have a general right to review and veto state enactments before they go into effect. A proposal to grant such authority to \xe2\x80\x9cnegative\xe2\x80\x9d state laws was considered at the Constitutional Convention, but rejected in favor of allowing state laws to take effect, subject to later challenge under the Supremacy Clause. See 1 Records of the Federal Convention of 1787, pp.\xc2\xa021, 164\xe2\x80\x93168 (M. Farrand ed. 1911); 2 id., at 27\xe2\x80\x9329, 390\xe2\x80\x93392.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOutside the strictures of the Supremacy Clause, States retain broad autonomy in structuring their governments and pursuing legislative objectives. Indeed, the Constitution provides that all powers not specifically granted to the Federal Government are reserved to the States or citizens. Amdt. 10. This \xe2\x80\x9callocation of powers in our federal system preserves the integrity, dignity, and residual sovereignty of the States.\xe2\x80\x9d Bond v. United States, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 9). But the federal balance \xe2\x80\x9cis not just an end in itself: Rather, federalism secures to citizens the liberties that derive from the diffusion of sovereign power.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMore specifically, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the Framers of the Constitution intended the States to keep for themselves, as provided in the \n \nTenth Amendment, the power to regulate elections.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Gregory v. Ashcroft, \n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 452\n\xe2\x80\x93462 (1991) (quoting Sugarman v. Dougall, \n\n413 U.\xc2\xa0S. 634, \n647 (1973)\n; some internal quotation marks omitted). Of course, the Federal Government retains significant control over federal elections. For instance, the Constitution authorizes Congress to establish the time and manner for electing Senators and Representatives. Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa74, cl.\xc2\xa01; see also Arizona v. Inter Tribal Council of Ariz., Inc., ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x936. But States have \xe2\x80\x9cbroad powers to determine the conditions under which the right of suffrage may be exercised.\xe2\x80\x9d Carrington v. Rash, \n\n380 U.\xc2\xa0S. 89, \n91 (1965)\n (internal quotation marks omitted); see also Arizona, ante, at 13\xe2\x80\x9315. And \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ach State has the power to prescribe the qualifications of its officers and the manner in which they shall be chosen.\xe2\x80\x9d Boyd v. Nebraska ex rel. Thayer, \n\n143 U.\xc2\xa0S. 135, \n161 (1892)\n. Drawing lines for congressional districts is likewise \xe2\x80\x9cprimarily the duty and responsibility of the State.\xe2\x80\x9d Perry v. Perez, 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (per curiam) (slip op., at 3) (internal quotation marks omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNot only do States retain sovereignty under the Constitution, there is also a \xe2\x80\x9cfundamental principle of equal sovereignty\xe2\x80\x9d among the States. Northwest Austin, supra, at 203 (citing United States v. Louisiana, \n\n363 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1, \n16 (1960)\n; Lessee of Pollard v. Hagan, 3 How. 212, 223 (1845); and Texas v. White, 7 Wall. 700, 725\xe2\x80\x93726 (1869); emphasis added). Over a hundred years ago, this Court explained that our Nation \xe2\x80\x9cwas and is a union of States, equal in power, dignity and authority.\xe2\x80\x9d Coyle v. Smith, \n\n221 U.\xc2\xa0S. 559, \n567 (1911)\n. Indeed, \xe2\x80\x9cthe constitutional equality of the States is essential to the harmonious operation of the scheme upon which the Republic was organized.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 580. Coyle concerned the admission of new States, and Katzenbach rejected the notion that the principle operated as a bar on differential treatment outside that context. 383 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 328\xe2\x80\x93329. At the same time, as we made clear in Northwest Austin, the fundamental principle of equal sovereignty remains highly pertinent in assessing subsequent disparate treatment of States. 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 203.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Voting Rights Act sharply departs from these basic principles. It suspends \xe2\x80\x9call changes to state election law\xe2\x80\x94however innocuous\xe2\x80\x94until they have been precleared by federal authorities in Washington, D. C.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 202. States must beseech the Federal Government for permission to implement laws that they would otherwise have the right to enact and execute on their own, subject of course to any injunction in a \xc2\xa72 action. The Attorney General has 60 days to object to a preclearance request, longer if he requests more information. See 28 CFR \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa751.9, 51.37. If a State seeks preclearance from a three-judge court, the process can take years. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAnd despite the tradition of equal sovereignty, the Act applies to only nine States (and several additional counties). While one State waits months or years and expends funds to implement a validly enacted law, its neighbor can typically put the same law into effect immediately, through the normal legislative process. Even if a noncovered jurisdiction is sued, there are important differences between those proceedings and preclearance proceedings; the preclearance proceeding \xe2\x80\x9cnot only switches the burden of proof to the supplicant jurisdiction, but also applies substantive standards quite different from those governing the rest of the nation.\xe2\x80\x9d 679 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 884 (Williams, J., dissenting) (case below). \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAll this explains why, when we first upheld the Act in 1966, we described it as \xe2\x80\x9cstringent\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cpotent.\xe2\x80\x9d Katzenbach, 383 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 308, 315, 337. We recognized that it \xe2\x80\x9cmay have been an uncommon exercise of congressional power,\xe2\x80\x9d but concluded that \xe2\x80\x9clegislative measures not oth-erwise appropriate\xe2\x80\x9d could be justified by \xe2\x80\x9cexceptional con-ditions.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 334. We have since noted that the Act \xe2\x80\x9cauthorizes federal intrusion into sensitive areas of state and local policymaking,\xe2\x80\x9d Lopez, 525 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 282, and represents an \xe2\x80\x9cextraordinary departure from the traditional course of relations between the States and the Federal Government,\xe2\x80\x9d Presley v. Etowah County Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, \n\n502 U.\xc2\xa0S. 491\n\xe2\x80\x93501 (1992). As we reiterated in Northwest Austin, the Act constitutes \xe2\x80\x9cextraordinary legislation otherwise unfamiliar to our federal system.\xe2\x80\x9d 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 211.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 1966, we found these departures from the basic features of our system of government justified. The \xe2\x80\x9cblight of racial discrimination in voting\xe2\x80\x9d had \xe2\x80\x9cinfected the electoral process in parts of our country for nearly a century.\xe2\x80\x9d Katzenbach, 383 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 308. Several States had enacted a variety of requirements and tests \xe2\x80\x9cspecifically designed to prevent\xe2\x80\x9d African-Americans from voting. Id., at 310. Case-by-case litigation had proved inadequate to prevent such racial discrimination in voting, in part because States \xe2\x80\x9cmerely switched to discriminatory devices not covered by the federal decrees,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cenacted difficult new tests,\xe2\x80\x9d or simply \xe2\x80\x9cdefied and evaded court orders.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 314. Shortly before enactment of the Voting Rights Act, only 19.4 percent of African-Americans of voting age were registered to vote in Alabama, only 31.8 percent in Louisiana, and only 6.4 percent in Mississippi. Id., at 313. Those figures were roughly 50 percentage points or more below the figures for whites. Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn short, we concluded that \xe2\x80\x9c[u]nder the compulsion of these unique circumstances, Congress responded in a permissibly decisive manner.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 334, 335. We also noted then and have emphasized since that this extra-ordinary legislation was intended to be temporary, set to expire after five years. Id., at 333; Northwest Austin, supra, at 199.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAt the time, the coverage formula\xe2\x80\x94the means of linking the exercise of the unprecedented authority with the problem that warranted it\xe2\x80\x94made sense. We found that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress chose to limit its attention to the geographic areas where immediate action seemed necessary.\xe2\x80\x9d Katzenbach, 383 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 328. The areas where Congress found \xe2\x80\x9cevidence of actual voting discrimination\xe2\x80\x9d shared two characteristics: \xe2\x80\x9cthe use of tests and devices for voter registration, and a voting rate in the 1964 presidential election at least 12 points below the national average.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 330. We explained that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]ests and devices are relevant to voting discrimination because of their long history as a tool for perpetrating the evil; a low voting rate is pertinent for the obvious reason that widespread disenfranchisement must inevitably affect the number of actual voters.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. We therefore concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthe coverage formula [was] rational in both practice and theory.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. It accurately reflected those jurisdictions uniquely characterized by voting discrimination \xe2\x80\x9con a pervasive scale,\xe2\x80\x9d linking coverage to the devices used to effectuate discrimination and to the resulting disenfranchisement. Id., at 308. The formula ensured that the \xe2\x80\x9cstringent remedies [were] aimed at areas where voting discrimination ha[d] been most flagrant.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 315.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tC\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNearly 50 years later, things have changed dramati-cally. Shelby County contends that the preclearance re-quirement, even without regard to its disparate coverage, is now unconstitutional. Its arguments have a good deal of force. In the covered jurisdictions, \xe2\x80\x9c[v]oter turnout and registration rates now approach parity. Blatantly discriminatory evasions of federal decrees are rare. And minority candidates hold office at unprecedented levels.\xe2\x80\x9d Northwest Austin, 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 202. The tests and devices that blocked access to the ballot have been forbidden nationwide for over 40 years. See \xc2\xa76, \n\n84Stat.\n315; \xc2\xa7102, \n\n89Stat.\n400.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThose conclusions are not ours alone. Congress said the same when it reauthorized the Act in 2006, writing that \xe2\x80\x9c[s]ignificant progress has been made in eliminating first generation barriers experienced by minority voters, including increased numbers of registered minority voters, minority voter turnout, and minority representation in Congress, State legislatures, and local elected offices.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72(b)(1), \n\n120Stat.\n577. The House Report elaborated that \xe2\x80\x9cthe number of African-Americans who are registered and who turn out to cast ballots has increased significantly over the last 40 years, particularly since 1982,\xe2\x80\x9d and noted that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n some circumstances, minorities register to vote and cast ballots at levels that surpass those of white voters.\xe2\x80\x9d H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 109\xe2\x80\x93478, p.\xc2\xa012 (2006). That Report also explained that there have been \xe2\x80\x9csignificant increases in the number of African-Americans serving in elected offices\xe2\x80\x9d; more specifically, there has been approximately a 1,000 percent increase since 1965 in the number of African-American elected officials in the six States originally covered by the Voting Rights Act. Id., at 18.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThere is no doubt that these improvements are in large part because of the Voting Rights Act. The Act has proved immensely successful at redressing racial discrimination and integrating the voting process. See \xc2\xa72(b)(1), \n\n120Stat.\n577. During the \xe2\x80\x9cFreedom Summer\xe2\x80\x9d of 1964, in Philadelphia, Mississippi, three men were murdered while working in the area to register African-American voters. See United States v. Price, \n\n383 U.\xc2\xa0S. 787, \n790 (1966)\n. On \xe2\x80\x9cBloody Sunday\xe2\x80\x9d in 1965, in Selma, Alabama, police beat and used tear gas against hundreds marching in sup- port of African-American enfranchisement. See Northwest Austin, supra, at 220, n.\xc2\xa03 (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment in part and dissenting in part). Today both of those towns are governed by African-American mayors. Problems remain in these States and others, but there is no denying that, due to the Voting Rights Act, our Nation has made great strides.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tYet the Act has not eased the restrictions in \xc2\xa75 or narrowed the scope of the coverage formula in \xc2\xa74(b) along the way. Those extraordinary and unprecedented features were reauthorized\xe2\x80\x94as if nothing had changed. In fact, the Act\xe2\x80\x99s unusual remedies have grown even stronger. When Congress reauthorized the Act in 2006, it did so for another 25 years on top of the previous 40\xe2\x80\x94a far cry from the initial five-year period. See \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71973b(a)(8). Congress also expanded the prohibitions in \xc2\xa75. We had previously interpreted \xc2\xa75 to prohibit only those redistricting plans that would have the purpose or effect of worsening the position of minority groups. See Bossier II, 528 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 324, 335\xe2\x80\x93336. In 2006, Congress amended \xc2\xa75 to prohibit laws that could have favored such groups but did not do so because of a discriminatory purpose, see \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71973c(c), even though we had stated that such broadening of \xc2\xa75 coverage would \xe2\x80\x9cexacerbate the substantial federalism costs that the preclearance procedure already exacts, perhaps to the extent of raising concerns about \xc2\xa75\xe2\x80\x99s constitutionality,\xe2\x80\x9d Bossier II, supra, at 336 (citation and internal quotation marks omitted). In addition, Congress expanded \xc2\xa75 to prohibit any voting law \xe2\x80\x9cthat has the purpose of or will have the effect of diminishing the ability of any citizens of the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d on account of race, color, or language minority status, \xe2\x80\x9cto elect their preferred candidates of choice.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71973c(b). In light of those two amendments, the bar that covered jurisdictions must clear has been raised even as the conditions justifying that requirement have dramatically improved.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe have also previously highlighted the concern that \xe2\x80\x9cthe preclearance requirements in one State [might] be unconstitutional in another.\xe2\x80\x9d Northwest Austin, 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 203; see Georgia v. Ashcroft, 539 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 491 (Kennedy, J., concurring) (\xe2\x80\x9cconsiderations of race that would doom a redistricting plan under the \n \nFourteenth Amendment or \xc2\xa72 [of the Voting Rights Act] seem to be what save it under \xc2\xa75\xe2\x80\x9d). Nothing has happened since to alleviate this troubling concern about the current application of \xc2\xa75.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondents do not deny that there have been improvements on the ground, but argue that much of this can be attributed to the deterrent effect of \xc2\xa75, which dissuades covered jurisdictions from engaging in discrimination that they would resume should \xc2\xa75 be struck down. Under this theory, however, \xc2\xa75 would be effectively immune from scrutiny; no matter how \xe2\x80\x9cclean\xe2\x80\x9d the record of covered jurisdictions, the argument could always be made that it was deterrence that accounted for the good behavior.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe provisions of \xc2\xa75 apply only to those jurisdictions singled out by \xc2\xa74. We now consider whether that coverage formula is constitutional in light of current conditions.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhen upholding the constitutionality of the coverage formula in 1966, we concluded that it was \xe2\x80\x9crational in both practice and theory.\xe2\x80\x9d Katzenbach, 383 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 330. The formula looked to cause (discriminatory tests) and ef- fect (low voter registration and turnout), and tailored the remedy (preclearance) to those jurisdictions exhibiting both.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBy 2009, however, we concluded that the \xe2\x80\x9ccoverage formula raise[d] serious constitutional questions.\xe2\x80\x9d Northwest Austin, 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 204. As we explained, a statute\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccurrent burdens\xe2\x80\x9d must be justified by \xe2\x80\x9ccurrent needs,\xe2\x80\x9d and any \xe2\x80\x9cdisparate geographic coverage\xe2\x80\x9d must be \xe2\x80\x9csufficiently related to the problem that it targets.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 203. The coverage formula met that test in 1965, but no longer does so.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCoverage today is based on decades-old data and eradicated practices. The formula captures States by reference to literacy tests and low voter registration and turnout in the 1960s and early 1970s. But such tests have been banned nationwide for over 40 years. \xc2\xa76, \n\n84Stat.\n315; \xc2\xa7102, \n\n89Stat.\n400. And voter registration and turnout numbers in the covered States have risen dramatically in the years since. H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 109\xe2\x80\x93478, at 12. Racial disparity in those numbers was compelling evidence justifying the preclearance remedy and the coverage formula. See, e.g., Katzenbach, supra, at 313, 329\xe2\x80\x93330. There is no longer such a disparity.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 1965, the States could be divided into two groups: those with a recent history of voting tests and low voter registration and turnout, and those without those characteristics. Congress based its coverage formula on that distinction. Today the Nation is no longer divided along those lines, yet the Voting Rights Act continues to treat it as if it were.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government\xe2\x80\x99s defense of the formula is limited. First, the Government contends that the formula is \xe2\x80\x9creverse-engineered\xe2\x80\x9d: Congress identified the jurisdictions to be covered and then came up with criteria to describe them. Brief for Federal Respondent 48\xe2\x80\x9349. Under that reasoning, there need not be any logical relationship be-tween the criteria in the formula and the reason for coverage; all that is necessary is that the formula happen to capture the jurisdictions Congress wanted to single out.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government suggests that Katzenbach sanctioned such an approach, but the analysis in Katzenbach was quite different. Katzenbach reasoned that the coverage formula was rational because the \xe2\x80\x9cformula .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. was relevant to the problem\xe2\x80\x9d: \xe2\x80\x9cTests and devices are relevant to voting discrimination because of their long history as a tool for perpetrating the evil; a low voting rate is pertinent for the obvious reason that widespread disenfranchisement must inevitably affect the number of actual voters.\xe2\x80\x9d 383 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 329, 330.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tHere, by contrast, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s reverse- engineering argument does not even attempt to demonstrate the continued relevance of the formula to the problem it targets. And in the context of a decision as significant as this one\xe2\x80\x94subjecting a disfavored subset of States to \xe2\x80\x9cextraordinary legislation otherwise unfamiliar to our federal system,\xe2\x80\x9d Northwest Austin, supra, at 211\xe2\x80\x94that failure to establish even relevance is fatal.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government falls back to the argument that because the formula was relevant in 1965, its continued use is permissible so long as any discrimination remains in the States Congress identified back then\xe2\x80\x94regardless of how that discrimination compares to discrimination in States unburdened by coverage. Brief for Federal Respondent 49\xe2\x80\x9350. This argument does not look to \xe2\x80\x9ccurrent political conditions,\xe2\x80\x9d Northwest Austin, supra, at 203, but instead relies on a comparison between the States in 1965. That comparison reflected the different histories of the North and South. It was in the South that slavery was upheld by law until uprooted by the Civil War, that the reign of Jim Crow denied African-Americans the most basic freedoms, and that state and local governments worked tirelessly to disenfranchise citizens on the basis of race. The Court invoked that history\xe2\x80\x94rightly so\xe2\x80\x94in sustaining the disparate coverage of the Voting Rights Act in 1966. See Katzenbach, supra, at 308 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe constitutional propriety of the Voting Rights Act of 1965 must be judged with reference to the historical experience which it reflects.\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut history did not end in 1965. By the time the Act was reauthorized in 2006, there had been 40 more years of it. In assessing the \xe2\x80\x9ccurrent need[\xc2\xa0]\xe2\x80\x9d for a preclearance system that treats States differently from one another today, that history cannot be ignored. During that time, largely because of the Voting Rights Act, voting tests were abolished, disparities in voter registration and turnout due to race were erased, and African-Americans attained political office in record numbers. And yet the coverage formula that Congress reauthorized in 2006 ignores these developments, keeping the focus on decades-old data rel-evant to decades-old problems, rather than current data reflecting current needs.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe \n \nFifteenth Amendment commands that the right to vote shall not be denied or abridged on account of race or color, and it gives Congress the power to enforce that command. The Amendment is not designed to punish for the past; its purpose is to ensure a better future. See Rice v. Cayetano, \n\n528 U.\xc2\xa0S. 495, \n512 (2000)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cConsistent with the design of the Constitution, the [Fifteenth] Amendment is cast in fundamental terms, terms transcending the particular controversy which was the immediate impetus for its enactment.\xe2\x80\x9d). To serve that purpose, Congress\xe2\x80\x94if it is to divide the States\xe2\x80\x94must identify those jurisdictions to be singled out on a basis that makes sense in light of current conditions. It cannot rely simply on the past. We made that clear in Northwest Austin, and we make it clear again today. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tC\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn defending the coverage formula, the Government, the intervenors, and the dissent also rely heavily on data from the record that they claim justify disparate coverage. Congress compiled thousands of pages of evidence before reauthorizing the Voting Rights Act. The court below and the parties have debated what that record shows\xe2\x80\x94they have gone back and forth about whether to compare covered to noncovered jurisdictions as blocks, how to disaggregate the data State by State, how to weigh \xc2\xa72 cases as evidence of ongoing discrimination, and whether to consider evidence not before Congress, among other issues. Compare, e.g., 679 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 873\xe2\x80\x93883 (case below), with id., at 889\xe2\x80\x93902 (Williams, J., dissenting). Regardless of how to look at the record, however, no one can fairly say that it shows anything approaching the \xe2\x80\x9cpervasive,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cflagrant,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cwidespread,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9crampant\xe2\x80\x9d discrimination that faced Congress in 1965, and that clearly distinguished the covered jurisdictions from the rest of the Nation at that time. Katzenbach, supra, at 308, 315, 331; Northwest Austin, 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 201.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut a more fundamental problem remains: Congress did not use the record it compiled to shape a coverage formula grounded in current conditions. It instead reenacted a formula based on 40-year-old facts having no logical relation to the present day. The dissent relies on \xe2\x80\x9csecond-generation barriers,\xe2\x80\x9d which are not impediments to the casting of ballots, but rather electoral arrangements that affect the weight of minority votes. That does not cure the problem. Viewing the preclearance requirements as targeting such efforts simply highlights the irrationality of continued reliance on the \xc2\xa74 coverage formula, which is based on voting tests and access to the ballot, not vote dilution. We cannot pretend that we are reviewing an updated statute, or try our hand at updating the statute ourselves, based on the new record compiled by Congress. Contrary to the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s contention, see post, at 23, we are not ignoring the record; we are simply recognizing that it played no role in shaping the statutory formula before us today.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe dissent also turns to the record to argue that, in light of voting discrimination in Shelby County, the county cannot complain about the provisions that subject it to preclearance. Post, at 23\xe2\x80\x9330. But that is like saying that a driver pulled over pursuant to a policy of stopping all redheads cannot complain about that policy, if it turns out his license has expired. Shelby County\xe2\x80\x99s claim is that the coverage formula here is unconstitutional in all its applications, because of how it selects the jurisdictions sub-jected to preclearance. The county was selected based on that formula, and may challenge it in court. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tD\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe dissent proceeds from a flawed premise. It quotes the famous sentence from McCulloch v. Maryland, 4 Wheat. 316, 421 (1819), with the following emphasis: \xe2\x80\x9cLet the end be legitimate, let it be within the scope of the constitution, and all means which are appropriate, which are plainly adapted to that end, which are not prohibited, but consist with the letter and spirit of the constitution, are constitutional.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 9 (emphasis in dissent). But this case is about a part of the sentence that the dissent does not emphasize\xe2\x80\x94the part that asks whether a legislative means is \xe2\x80\x9cconsist[ent] with the letter and spirit of the constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d The dissent states that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t cannot tenably be maintained\xe2\x80\x9d that this is an issue with regard to the Voting Rights Act, post, at 9, but four years ago, in an opinion joined by two of today\xe2\x80\x99s dissenters, the Court expressly stated that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Act\xe2\x80\x99s preclearance requirement and its coverage formula raise serious constitutional questions.\xe2\x80\x9d Northwest Austin, supra, at 204. The dissent does not explain how those \xe2\x80\x9cserious constitutional questions\xe2\x80\x9d became untenable in four short years. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe dissent treats the Act as if it were just like any other piece of legislation, but this Court has made clear from the beginning that the Voting Rights Act is far from ordinary. At the risk of repetition, Katzenbach indicated that the Act was \xe2\x80\x9cuncommon\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cnot otherwise appropriate,\xe2\x80\x9d but was justified by \xe2\x80\x9cexceptional\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cunique\xe2\x80\x9d conditions. 383 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 334, 335. Multiple decisions since have reaffirmed the Act\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cextraordinary\xe2\x80\x9d nature. See, e.g., Northwest Austin, supra, at 211. Yet the dissent goes so far as to suggest instead that the preclearance requirement and disparate treatment of the States should be upheld into the future \xe2\x80\x9cunless there [is] no or almost no evidence of unconstitutional action by States.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 33. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn other ways as well, the dissent analyzes the ques- tion presented as if our decision in Northwest Austin never happened. For example, the dissent refuses to con- sider the principle of equal sovereignty, despite Northwest Austin\xe2\x80\x99s emphasis on its significance. Northwest Austin also emphasized the \xe2\x80\x9cdramatic\xe2\x80\x9d progress since 1965, 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 201, but the dissent describes current levels of discrimination as \xe2\x80\x9cflagrant,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cwidespread,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cpervasive,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 7, 17 (internal quotation marks omitted). Despite the fact that Northwest Austin requires an Act\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cdisparate geographic coverage\xe2\x80\x9d to be \xe2\x80\x9csufficiently related\xe2\x80\x9d to its targeted problems, 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 203, the dissent maintains that an Act\xe2\x80\x99s limited coverage actually eases Congress\xe2\x80\x99s burdens, and suggests that a fortuitous relationship should suffice. Although Northwest Austin stated definitively that \xe2\x80\x9ccurrent burdens\xe2\x80\x9d must be justified by \xe2\x80\x9ccurrent needs,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., the dissent argues that the coverage formula can be justified by history, and that the required showing can be weaker on reenactment than when the law was first passed. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThere is no valid reason to insulate the coverage for-mula from review merely because it was previously enacted 40 years ago. If Congress had started from scratch in 2006, it plainly could not have enacted the present coverage formula. It would have been irrational for Congress to distinguish between States in such a fundamental way based on 40-year-old data, when today\xe2\x80\x99s statistics tell an entirely different story. And it would have been irrational to base coverage on the use of voting tests 40 years ago, when such tests have been illegal since that time. But that is exactly what Congress has done.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tStriking down an Act of Congress \xe2\x80\x9cis the gravest and most delicate duty that this Court is called on to perform.\xe2\x80\x9d Blodgett v. Holden, \n\n275 U.\xc2\xa0S. 142, \n148 (1927)\n (Holmes, J., concurring). We do not do so lightly. That is why, in 2009, we took care to avoid ruling on the constitutionality of the Voting Rights Act when asked to do so, and instead resolved the case then before us on statutory grounds. But in issuing that decision, we expressed our broader concerns about the constitutionality of the Act. Congress could have updated the coverage formula at that time, but did not do so. Its failure to act leaves us today with no choice but to declare \xc2\xa74(b) unconstitutional. The formula in that section can no longer be used as a basis for subjecting jurisdictions to preclearance.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur decision in no way affects the permanent, nationwide ban on racial discrimination in voting found in \xc2\xa72. We issue no holding on \xc2\xa75 itself, only on the coverage formula. Congress may draft another formula based on current conditions. Such a formula is an initial prerequisite to a determination that exceptional conditions still exist justifying such an \xe2\x80\x9cextraordinary departure from the traditional course of relations between the States and the Federal Government.\xe2\x80\x9d Presley, 502 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 500\xe2\x80\x93501. Our country has changed, and while any racial discrimination in voting is too much, Congress must ensure that the legislation it passes to remedy that problem speaks to current conditions. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe judgment of the Court of Appeals is reversed.\n\t\t\t

As the Court explains: \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[V]oter turnout and registration rates now approach parity. Blatantly discriminatory evasions of federal decrees are rare. And minority candidates hold office at un-precedented levels.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314 (quoting Northwest Austin Municipal Util. Dist. No. One v. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn spite of these improvements, however, Congress increased the already significant burdens of \xc2\xa75. \n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn the Court\xe2\x80\x99s view, the very success of \xc2\xa75 of the Voting Rights Act demands its dormancy. Congress was of another mind. Recognizing that large progress has been made, Congress determined, based on a voluminous record, that the scourge of discrimination was not yet extirpated. The question this case presents is who decides whether, as currently operative, \xc2\xa75 remains justifiable,\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t this Court, or a Congress charged with the obligation to enforce the post-Civil War Amendments \xe2\x80\x9cby appropriate legislation.\xe2\x80\x9d With overwhelming support in both Houses, Congress concluded that, for two prime reasons, \xc2\xa75 should continue in force, unabated. First, continuance would facilitate completion of the impressive gains thus far made; and second, continuance would guard against backsliding. Those assessments were well within Congress\xe2\x80\x99 province to make and should elicit this Court\xe2\x80\x99s unstinting approbation.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c[V]oting discrimination still exists; no one doubts that.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 2. But the Court today terminates the remedy that proved to be best suited to block that discrimination. The Voting Rights Act of 1965 (VRA) has worked to combat voting discrimination where other remedies had been tried and failed. Particularly effective is the VRA\xe2\x80\x99s requirement of federal preclearance for all changes to voting laws in the regions of the country with the most aggravated records of rank discrimination against minority voting rights.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA century after the Fourteenth and \n \nFifteenth Amendments guaranteed citizens the right to vote free of discrimination on the basis of race, the \xe2\x80\x9cblight of racial discrimination in voting\xe2\x80\x9d continued to \xe2\x80\x9cinfec[t] the electoral process in parts of our country.\xe2\x80\x9d South Carolina v. Katzenbach, \n\n383 U.\xc2\xa0S. 301, \n308 (1966)\n. Early attempts to cope with this vile infection resembled battling the Hydra. Whenever one form of voting discrimination was identified and prohibited, others sprang up in its place. This Court repeatedly encountered the remarkable \xe2\x80\x9cvariety and persistence\xe2\x80\x9d of laws disenfranchising minority citizens. Id., at 311. To take just one example, the Court, in 1927, held unconstitutional a Texas law barring black voters from participating in primary elections, Nixon v. Herndon, \n\n273 U.\xc2\xa0S. 536\n; in 1944, the Court struck down a \xe2\x80\x9creenacted\xe2\x80\x9d and slightly altered version of the same law, Smith v. Allwright, \n\n321 U.\xc2\xa0S. 649\n; and in 1953, the Court once again confronted an attempt by Texas to \xe2\x80\x9ccircumven[t]\xe2\x80\x9d the \n \nFifteenth Amendment by adopting yet another variant of the all-white primary, Terry v. Adams, \n\n345 U.\xc2\xa0S. 461\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tDuring this era, the Court recognized that discrimination against minority voters was a quintessentially political problem requiring a political solution. As Justice Holmes explained: If \xe2\x80\x9cthe great mass of the white population intends to keep the blacks from voting,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9crelief from [that] great political wrong, if done, as alleged, by the people of a State and the State itself, must be given by them or by the legislative and political department of the government of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Giles v. Harris, \n\n189 U.\xc2\xa0S. 475, \n488 (1903)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress learned from experience that laws targeting particular electoral practices or enabling case-by-case litigation were inadequate to the task. In the Civil Rights Acts of 1957, 1960, and 1964, Congress authorized and then expanded the power of \xe2\x80\x9cthe Attorney General to seek injunctions against public and private interference with the right to vote on racial grounds.\xe2\x80\x9d Katzenbach, 383 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 313. But circumstances reduced the ameliorative potential of these legislative Acts:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cVoting suits are unusually onerous to prepare, sometimes requiring as many as 6,000 man-hours spent combing through registration records in preparation for trial. Litigation has been exceedingly slow, in part because of the ample opportunities for delay afforded voting officials and others involved in the proceed-ings. Even when favorable decisions have finally been obtained, some of the States affected have merely switched to discriminatory devices not covered by the federal decrees or have enacted difficult new tests designed to prolong the existing disparity between white and Negro registration. Alternatively, certain local officials have defied and evaded court orders or have simply closed their registration offices to freeze the voting rolls.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 314 (footnote omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tPatently, a new approach was needed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAnswering that need, the Voting Rights Act became one of the most consequential, efficacious, and amply justified exercises of federal legislative power in our Nation\xe2\x80\x99s his-tory. Requiring federal preclearance of changes in voting laws in the covered jurisdictions\xe2\x80\x94those States and localities where opposition to the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s commands were most virulent\xe2\x80\x94the VRA provided a fit solution for minority voters as well as for States. Under the preclearance regime established by \xc2\xa75 of the VRA, covered jurisdictions must submit proposed changes in voting laws or procedures to the Department of Justice (DOJ), which has 60 days to respond to the changes. \n\n79Stat.\n439, codified at \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71973c(a). A change will be approved unless DOJ finds it has \xe2\x80\x9cthe purpose [or] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the effect of denying or abridging the right to vote on account of race or color.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. In the alternative, the covered jurisdiction may seek approval by a three-judge District Court in the District of Columbia.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAfter a century\xe2\x80\x99s failure to fulfill the promise of the Fourteenth and \n \nFifteenth Amendments, passage of the VRA finally led to signal improvement on this front. \xe2\x80\x9cThe Justice Department estimated that in the five years after [the VRA\xe2\x80\x99s] passage, almost as many blacks registered [to vote] in Alabama, Mississippi, Georgia, Louisiana, North Carolina, and South Carolina as in the entire century before 1965.\xe2\x80\x9d Davidson, The Voting Rights Act: A Brief History, in Controversies in Minority Voting 7, 21 (B. Grofman & C. Davidson eds. 1992). And in assessing the overall effects of the VRA in 2006, Congress found that \xe2\x80\x9c[s]ignificant progress has been made in eliminating first generation barriers experienced by minority voters, including increased numbers of registered minority voters, minority voter turnout, and minority representation in Congress, State legislatures, and local elected offices. This progress is the direct result of the Voting Rights Act of 1965.\xe2\x80\x9d Fannie Lou Hamer, Rosa Parks, and Coretta Scott King Voting Rights Act Reauthorization and Amendments Act of 2006 (hereinafter 2006 Reauthorization), \xc2\xa72(b)(1), \n\n120Stat.\n577. On that matter of cause and effects there can be no genuine doubt.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t Although the VRA wrought dramatic changes in the realization of minority voting rights, the Act, to date, surely has not eliminated all vestiges of discrimination against the exercise of the franchise by minority citizens. Jurisdictions covered by the preclearance requirement continued to submit, in large numbers, proposed changes to voting laws that the Attorney General declined to approve, auguring that barriers to minority voting would quickly resurface were the preclearance remedy elimi-nated. City of Rome v. United States, \n\n446 U.\xc2\xa0S. 156, \n181 (1980)\n. Congress also found that as \xe2\x80\x9cregistration and voting of minority citizens increas[ed], other measures may be resorted to which would dilute increasing minority voting strength.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 94\xe2\x80\x93196, p.\xc2\xa010 (1975)). See also Shaw v. Reno, \n\n509 U.\xc2\xa0S. 630, \n640 (1993)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t soon became apparent that guaranteeing equal access to the polls would not suffice to root out other racially discriminatory voting practices\xe2\x80\x9d such as voting dilution). Efforts to reduce the impact of minority votes, in contrast to direct attempts to block access to the bal- lot, are aptly described as \xe2\x80\x9csecond-generation barriers\xe2\x80\x9d to minority voting.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSecond-generation barriers come in various forms. One of the blockages is racial gerrymandering, the redrawing of legislative districts in an \xe2\x80\x9ceffort to segregate the races for purposes of voting.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 642. Another is adoption of a system of at-large voting in lieu of district-by-district voting in a city with a sizable black minority. By switching to at-large voting, the overall majority could control the election of each city council member, effectively eliminating the potency of the minority\xe2\x80\x99s votes. Grofman & Davidson, The Effect of Municipal Election Structure on Black Representation in Eight Southern States, in Quiet Revolution in the South 301, 319 (C. Davidson & B. Grofman eds. 1994) (hereinafter Quiet Revolution). A similar effect could be achieved if the city engaged in discriminatory annexation by incorporating majority-white areas into city limits, thereby decreasing the effect of VRA-occasioned increases in black voting. Whatever the device employed, this Court has long recognized that vote dilution, when adopted with a discriminatory purpose, cuts down the right to vote as certainly as denial of access to the ballot. Shaw, 509 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 640\xe2\x80\x93641; Allen v. State Bd. of Elections, \n\n393 U.\xc2\xa0S. 544, \n569 (1969)\n; Reynolds v. Sims, \n\n377 U.\xc2\xa0S. 533, \n555 (1964)\n. See also H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 109\xe2\x80\x93478, p.\xc2\xa06 (2006) (although \xe2\x80\x9c[d]iscrimination today is more subtle than the visible methods used in 1965,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cthe effect and results are the same, namely a diminishing of the minority community\xe2\x80\x99s ability to fully participate in the electoral process and to elect their preferred candidates\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn response to evidence of these substituted barriers, Congress reauthorized the VRA for five years in 1970, for seven years in 1975, and for 25 years in 1982. Ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x935. Each time, this Court upheld the reauthorization as a valid exercise of congressional power. Ante, at 5. As the 1982 reauthorization approached its 2007 expiration date, Congress again considered whether the VRA\xe2\x80\x99s preclearance mechanism remained an appropriate response to the problem of voting discrimination in covered jurisdictions.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress did not take this task lightly. Quite the opposite. The 109th Congress that took responsibility for the renewal started early and conscientiously. In October 2005, the House began extensive hearings, which continued into November and resumed in March 2006. S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 109\xe2\x80\x93295, p. 2 (2006). In April 2006, the Senate followed suit, with hearings of its own. Ibid. In May 2006, the bills that became the VRA\xe2\x80\x99s reauthorization were introduced in both Houses. Ibid. The House held further hearings of considerable length, as did the Senate, which continued to hold hearings into June and July. H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. 109\xe2\x80\x93478, at 5; S.\xc2\xa0Rep. 109\xe2\x80\x93295, at 3\xe2\x80\x934. In mid-July, the House considered and rejected four amendments, then passed the reauthorization by a vote of 390 yeas to 33 nays. 152 Cong. Rec. H5207 (July 13, 2006); Persily, The Promise and Pitfalls of the New Voting Rights Act, 117 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J. 174, 182\xe2\x80\x93183 (2007) (hereinafter Persily). The bill was read and debated in the Senate, where it passed by a vote of 98 to 0. 152 Cong. Rec. S8012 (July 20, 2006). President Bush signed it a week later, on July 27, 2006, recognizing the need for \xe2\x80\x9cfurther work .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in the fight against injustice,\xe2\x80\x9d and calling the reauthorization \xe2\x80\x9can example of our continued commitment to a united America where every person is valued and treated with dignity and respect.\xe2\x80\x9d 152 Cong. Rec. S8781 (Aug. 3, 2006).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn the long course of the legislative process, Congress \xe2\x80\x9camassed a sizable record.\xe2\x80\x9d Northwest Austin Municipal Util. Dist. No. One v. Holder, \n\n557 U.\xc2\xa0S. 193, \n205 (2009)\n. See also 679 F.\xc2\xa03d 848, 865\xe2\x80\x93873 (CADC 2012) (describing the \xe2\x80\x9cextensive record\xe2\x80\x9d supporting Congress\xe2\x80\x99 determina-tion that \xe2\x80\x9cserious and widespread intentional discrimination persisted in covered jurisdictions\xe2\x80\x9d). The House and Senate Judiciary Committees held 21 hearings, heard from scores of witnesses, received a number of investigative reports and other written documentation of continuing discrimina-tion in covered jurisdictions. In all, the legislative record Congress compiled filled more than 15,000 pages. H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. 109\xe2\x80\x93478, at 5, 11\xe2\x80\x9312; S.\xc2\xa0Rep. 109\xe2\x80\x93295, at 2\xe2\x80\x934, 15. The compilation presents countless \xe2\x80\x9cexamples of fla-grant racial discrimination\xe2\x80\x9d since the last reauthoriza-tion; Congress also brought to light systematic evidence that \xe2\x80\x9cintentional racial discrimination in voting remains so serious and widespread in covered jurisdictions that section 5 preclearance is still needed.\xe2\x80\x9d 679 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 866.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAfter considering the full legislative record, Congress made the following findings: The VRA has directly caused significant progress in eliminating first-generation barriers to ballot access, leading to a marked increase in minority voter registration and turnout and the number of minority elected officials. 2006 Reauthorization \xc2\xa72(b)(1). But despite this progress, \xe2\x80\x9csecond generation barriers constructed to prevent minority voters from fully participating in the electoral process\xe2\x80\x9d continued to exist, as well as racially polarized voting in the covered jurisdictions, which increased the political vulnerability of racial and language minorities in those jurisdictions. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72(b)(2)\xe2\x80\x93(3), \n\n120Stat.\n577. Extensive \xe2\x80\x9c[e]vidence of continued discrimination,\xe2\x80\x9d Congress concluded, \xe2\x80\x9cclearly show[ed] the continued need for Federal oversight\xe2\x80\x9d in covered jurisdictions. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72(b)(4)\xe2\x80\x93(5), id., at 577\xe2\x80\x93578. The overall record demonstrated to the federal lawmakers that, \xe2\x80\x9cwithout the continuation of the Voting Rights Act of 1965 protections, racial and language minority citizens will be deprived of the opportunity to exercise their right to vote, or will have their votes diluted, undermining the significant gains made by minorities in the last 40 years.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72(b)(9), id., at 578.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBased on these findings, Congress reauthorized preclearance for another 25 years, while also undertaking to reconsider the extension after 15 years to ensure that the provision was still necessary and effective. \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71973b(a)(7), (8) (2006 ed., Supp. V). The question before the Court is whether Congress had the authority under the Constitution to act as it did.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn answering this question, the Court does not write on a clean slate. It is well established that Congress\xe2\x80\x99 judgment regarding exercise of its power to enforce the Fourteenth and \n \nFifteenth Amendments warrants substantial deference. The VRA addresses the combination of race discrimination and the right to vote, which is \xe2\x80\x9cpreservative of all rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Yick Wo v. Hopkins, \n\n118 U.\xc2\xa0S. 356, \n370 (1886)\n. When confronting the most constitutionally invidious form of discrimination, and the most fundamental right in our democratic system, Congress\xe2\x80\x99 power to act is at its height.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe basis for this deference is firmly rooted in both constitutional text and precedent. The \n \nFifteenth Amendment, which targets precisely and only racial discrimination in voting rights, states that, in this domain, \xe2\x80\x9cCongress shall have power to enforce this article by appropriate legislation.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t In choosing this language, the Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s framers invoked Chief Justice Marshall\xe2\x80\x99s formulation of the scope of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 powers under the Necessary and Proper Clause:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cLet the end be legitimate, let it be within the scope of the constitution, and all means which are appropriate, which are plainly adapted to that end, which are not prohibited, but consist with the letter and spirit of the constitution, are constitutional.\xe2\x80\x9d McCulloch v. Maryland, 4 Wheat. 316, 421 (1819) (emphasis added). \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt cannot tenably be maintained that the VRA, an Act of Congress adopted to shield the right to vote from racial discrimination, is inconsistent with the letter or spirit of the \n \nFifteenth Amendment, or any provision of the Constitution read in light of the Civil War Amendments. Nowhere in today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, or in Northwest Austin,\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t is there clear recognition of the transformative effect the \n \nFifteenth Amendment aimed to achieve. Notably, \xe2\x80\x9cthe Founders\xe2\x80\x99 first successful amendment told Congress that it could \xe2\x80\x98make no law\xe2\x80\x99 over a certain domain\xe2\x80\x9d; in contrast, the Civil War Amendments used \xe2\x80\x9clanguage [that] authorized transformative new federal statutes to uproot all vestiges of unfreedom and inequality\xe2\x80\x9d and provided \xe2\x80\x9csweeping enforcement powers .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to enact \xe2\x80\x98appropriate\xe2\x80\x99 legislation targeting state abuses.\xe2\x80\x9d A. Amar, America\xe2\x80\x99s Constitution: A Biography 361, 363, 399 (2005). See also McConnell, Institutions and Interpretation: A Critique of City of Boerne v. Flores, 111 Harv. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 153, 182 (1997) (quoting Civil War-era framer that \xe2\x80\x9cthe remedy for the violation of the fourteenth and fifteenth amendments was expressly not left to the courts. The remedy was legislative.\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe stated purpose of the Civil War Amendments was to arm Congress with the power and authority to protect all persons within the Nation from violations of their rights by the States. In exercising that power, then, Congress may use \xe2\x80\x9call means which are appropriate, which are plainly adapted\xe2\x80\x9d to the constitutional ends declared by these Amendments. McCulloch, 4 Wheat., at 421. So when Congress acts to enforce the right to vote free from racial discrimination, we ask not whether Congress has chosen the means most wise, but whether Congress has rationally selected means appropriate to a legitimate end. \xe2\x80\x9cIt is not for us to review the congressional resolution of [the need for its chosen remedy]. It is enough that we be able to perceive a basis upon which the Congress might resolve the conflict as it did.\xe2\x80\x9d Katzenbach v. Morgan, \n\n384 U.\xc2\xa0S. 641, \n653 (1966)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUntil today, in considering the constitutionality of the VRA, the Court has accorded Congress the full measure of respect its judgments in this domain should garner. South Carolina v. Katzenbach supplies the standard of review: \xe2\x80\x9cAs against the reserved powers of the States, Congress may use any rational means to effectuate the constitu-tional prohibition of racial discrimination in voting.\xe2\x80\x9d 383 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 324. Faced with subsequent reauthorizations of the VRA, the Court has reaffirmed this standard. E.g., City of Rome, 446 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 178. Today\xe2\x80\x99s Court does not purport to alter settled precedent establishing that the dispositive question is whether Congress has employed \xe2\x80\x9crational means.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor three reasons, legislation reauthorizing an existing statute is especially likely to satisfy the minimal requirements of the rational-basis test. First, when reauthorization is at issue, Congress has already assembled a legislative record justifying the initial legislation. Congress is en-titled to consider that preexisting record as well as the record before it at the time of the vote on reauthorization. This is especially true where, as here, the Court has repeatedly affirmed the statute\xe2\x80\x99s constitutionality and Congress has adhered to the very model the Court has upheld. See id., at 174 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe appellants are asking us to do nothing less than overrule our decision in South Carolina v. Katzenbach .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. , in which we upheld the constitutionality of the Act.\xe2\x80\x9d); Lopez v. Monterey County, \n\n525 U.\xc2\xa0S. 266, \n283 (1999)\n (similar).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSecond, the very fact that reauthorization is necessary arises because Congress has built a temporal limitation into the Act. It has pledged to review, after a span of years (first 15, then 25) and in light of contemporary evidence, the continued need for the VRA. Cf. Grutter v. Bollinger, \n\n539 U.\xc2\xa0S. 306, \n343 (2003)\n (anticipating, but not guaranteeing, that, in 25 years, \xe2\x80\x9cthe use of racial preferences [in higher education] will no longer be necessary\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThird, a reviewing court should expect the record supporting reauthorization to be less stark than the record originally made. Demand for a record of violations equivalent to the one earlier made would expose Congress to a catch-22. If the statute was working, there would be less evidence of discrimination, so opponents might argue that Congress should not be allowed to renew the statute. In contrast, if the statute was not working, there would be plenty of evidence of discrimination, but scant reason to renew a failed regulatory regime. See Persily 193\xe2\x80\x93194.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis is not to suggest that congressional power in this area is limitless. It is this Court\xe2\x80\x99s responsibility to ensure that Congress has used appropriate means. The question meet for judicial review is whether the chosen means are \xe2\x80\x9cadapted to carry out the objects the amendments have in view.\xe2\x80\x9d Ex parte Virginia, \n\n100 U.\xc2\xa0S. 339, \n346 (1880)\n. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s role, then, is not to substitute its judgment for that of Congress, but to determine whether the legislative record sufficed to show that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress could rationally have determined that [its chosen] provisions were appropriate methods.\xe2\x80\x9d City of Rome, 446 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 176\xe2\x80\x93177.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn summary, the Constitution vests broad power in Congress to protect the right to vote, and in particular to combat racial discrimination in voting. This Court has repeatedly reaffirmed Congress\xe2\x80\x99 prerogative to use any rational means in exercise of its power in this area. And both precedent and logic dictate that the rational-means test should be easier to satisfy, and the burden on the statute\xe2\x80\x99s challenger should be higher, when what is at issue is the reauthorization of a remedy that the Court has previously affirmed, and that Congress found, from contemporary evidence, to be working to advance the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s legitimate objective.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe 2006 reauthorization of the Voting Rights Act fully satisfies the standard stated in McCulloch, 4 Wheat., at 421: Congress may choose any means \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cplainly adapted to\xe2\x80\x9d a legitimate constitutional end. As we shall see, it is implausible to suggest otherwise.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI begin with the evidence on which Congress based its decision to continue the preclearance remedy. The surest way to evaluate whether that remedy remains in order is to see if preclearance is still effectively preventing discriminatory changes to voting laws. See City of Rome, 446 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 181 (identifying \xe2\x80\x9cinformation on the number and types of submissions made by covered jurisdictions and the number and nature of objections interposed by the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x9d as a primary basis for upholding the 1975 reauthorization). On that score, the record before Congress was huge. In fact, Congress found there were more DOJ objections between 1982 and 2004 (626) than there were between 1965 and the 1982 reauthorization (490). 1 Voting Rights Act: Evidence of Continued Need, Hearing before the Subcommittee on the Constitution of the House Committee on the Judiciary, 109th Cong., 2d Sess., p. 172 (2006) (hereinafter Evidence of Continued Need).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAll told, between 1982 and 2006, DOJ objections blocked over 700 voting changes based on a determination that the changes were discriminatory. H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 109\xe2\x80\x93478, at 21. Congress found that the majority of DOJ objections included findings of discriminatory intent, see 679 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 867, and that the changes blocked by preclearance were \xe2\x80\x9ccalculated decisions to keep minority voters from fully participating in the political process.\xe2\x80\x9d H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. 109\xe2\x80\x93478, at 21. On top of that, over the same time period the DOJ and private plaintiffs succeeded in more than 100 actions to enforce the \xc2\xa75 preclearance requirements. 1 Evidence of Continued Need 186, 250.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn addition to blocking proposed voting changes through preclearance, DOJ may request more information from a jurisdiction proposing a change. In turn, the jurisdiction may modify or withdraw the proposed change. The number of such modifications or withdrawals provides an indication of how many discriminatory proposals are deterred without need for formal objection. Congress received evidence that more than 800 proposed changes were altered or withdrawn since the last reauthorization in 1982. H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 109\xe2\x80\x93478, at 40\xe2\x80\x9341.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Congress also received empirical studies finding that DOJ\xe2\x80\x99s requests for more information had a significant effect on the degree to which covered jurisdictions \xe2\x80\x9ccompl[ied] with their obligatio[n]\xe2\x80\x9d to protect minority voting rights. 2 Evidence of Continued Need 2555.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress also received evidence that litigation under \xc2\xa72 of the VRA was an inadequate substitute for preclearance in the covered jurisdictions. Litigation occurs only after the fact, when the illegal voting scheme has already been put in place and individuals have been elected pursuant to it, thereby gaining the advantages of incumbency. 1 Evidence of Continued Need 97. An illegal scheme might be in place for several election cycles before a \xc2\xa72 plaintiff can gather sufficient evidence to challenge it. 1 Voting Rights Act: Section 5 of the Act\xe2\x80\x94History, Scope, and Purpose: Hearing before the Subcommittee on the Constitution of the House Committee on the Judiciary, 109th Cong., 1st Sess., p. 92 (2005) (hereinafter Section 5 Hearing). And litigation places a heavy financial burden on minority voters. See id., at 84. Congress also received evidence that preclearance lessened the litigation burden on covered jurisdictions themselves, because the preclearance process is far less costly than defending against a \xc2\xa72 claim, and clearance by DOJ substantially reduces the likelihood that a \xc2\xa72 claim will be mounted. Reauthorizing the Voting Rights Act\xe2\x80\x99s Temporary Provisions: Policy Perspectives and Views From the Field: Hearing before the Subcommittee on the Constitution, Civil Rights and Property Rights of the Senate Committee on the Judiciary, 109th Cong., 2d Sess., pp. 13, 120\xe2\x80\x93121 (2006). See also Brief for States of New York, California, Mississippi, and North Carolina as Amici Curiae 8\xe2\x80\x939 (Section 5 \xe2\x80\x9creduc[es] the likelihood that a jurisdiction will face costly and protracted Section 2 litigation\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe number of discriminatory changes blocked or deterred by the preclearance requirement suggests that the state of voting rights in the covered jurisdictions would have been significantly different absent this remedy. Sur-veying the type of changes stopped by the preclearance procedure conveys a sense of the extent to which \xc2\xa75 continues to protect minority voting rights. Set out below are characteristic examples of changes blocked in the years leading up to the 2006 reauthorization:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIn 1995, Mississippi sought to reenact a dual voter registration system, \xe2\x80\x9cwhich was initially enacted in 1892 to disenfranchise Black voters,\xe2\x80\x9d and for that reason, was struck down by a federal court in 1987. H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 109\xe2\x80\x93478, at 39.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tFollowing the 2000 census, the City of Albany, Georgia, proposed a redistricting plan that DOJ found to be \xe2\x80\x9cdesigned with the purpose to limit and retrogress the increased black voting strength .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in the city as a whole.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 37 (internal quotation marks omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIn 2001, the mayor and all-white five-member Board of Aldermen of Kilmichael, Mississippi, abruptly canceled the town\xe2\x80\x99s election after \xe2\x80\x9can unprecedented number\xe2\x80\x9d of African-American can-didates announced they were running for office. DOJ required an election, and the town elected its first black mayor and three black aldermen. Id., at 36\xe2\x80\x9337.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIn 2006, this Court found that Texas\xe2\x80\x99 attempt to redraw a congressional district to reduce the strength of Latino voters bore \xe2\x80\x9cthe mark of intentional discrimination that could give rise to an equal protection violation,\xe2\x80\x9d and ordered the district redrawn in compliance with the VRA. League of United Latin American Citizens v. Perry, \n\n548 U.\xc2\xa0S. 399, \n440 (2006)\n. In response, Texas sought to undermine this Court\xe2\x80\x99s order by curtailing early voting in the district, but was blocked by an action to enforce the \xc2\xa75 preclearance requirement. See Order in League of United Latin American Citizens v. Texas, No. 06\xe2\x80\x93cv\xe2\x80\x931046 (WD Tex.), Doc. 8.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIn 2003, after African-Americans won a majority of the seats on the school board for the first time in history, Charleston County, South Carolina, proposed an at-large voting mechanism for the board. The proposal, made without consulting any of the African-American members of the school board, was found to be an \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98exact replica\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of an earlier voting scheme that, a federal court had determined, violated the VRA. 811 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 424, 483 (DDC 2011). See also S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 109\xe2\x80\x93295, at 309. DOJ invoked \xc2\xa75 to block the proposal.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIn 1993, the City of Millen, Georgia, proposed to delay the election in a majority-black district by two years, leaving that district without representation on the city council while the neighboring majority-white district would have three representatives. 1 Section 5 Hearing 744. DOJ blocked the proposal. The county then sought to move a polling place from a predominantly black neighborhood in the city to an inaccessible location in a predominantly white neighborhood outside city limits. Id., at 816.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIn 2004, Waller County, Texas, threatened to prosecute two black students after they announced their intention to run for office. The county then attempted to reduce the availability of early voting in that election at polling places near a historically black university. 679 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 865\xe2\x80\x93866.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIn 1990, Dallas County, Alabama, whose county seat is the City of Selma, sought to purge its voter rolls of many black voters. DOJ rejected the purge as discriminatory, noting that it would have disquali-fied many citizens from voting \xe2\x80\x9csimply because they failed to pick up or return a voter update form, when there was no valid requirement that they do so.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 Section 5 Hearing 356.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThese examples, and scores more like them, fill the pages of the legislative record. The evidence was indeed sufficient to support Congress\xe2\x80\x99 conclusion that \xe2\x80\x9cracial discrimination in voting in covered jurisdictions [remained] serious and pervasive.\xe2\x80\x9d 679 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 865.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress further received evidence indicating that formal requests of the kind set out above represented only the tip of the iceberg. There was what one commentator described as an \xe2\x80\x9cavalanche of case studies of voting rights violations in the covered jurisdictions,\xe2\x80\x9d ranging from \xe2\x80\x9coutright intimidation and violence against minority voters\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9cmore subtle forms of voting rights deprivations.\xe2\x80\x9d Persily 202 (footnote omitted). This evidence gave Congress ever more reason to conclude that the time had not yet come for relaxed vigilance against the scourge of race discrimination in voting.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTrue, conditions in the South have impressively improved since passage of the Voting Rights Act. Congress noted this improvement and found that the VRA was the driving force behind it. 2006 Reauthorization \xc2\xa72(b)(1). But Congress also found that voting discrimination had evolved into subtler second-generation barriers, and that eliminating preclearance would risk loss of the gains that had been made. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72(b)(2), (9). Concerns of this order, the Court previously found, gave Congress adequate cause to reauthorize the VRA. City of Rome, 446 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 180\xe2\x80\x93182 (congressional reauthorization of the preclearance requirement was justified based on \xe2\x80\x9cthe number and nature of objections interposed by the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x9d since the prior reauthorization; extension was \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary to pre-serve the limited and fragile achievements of the Act and to promote further amelioration of voting discrimination\xe2\x80\x9d) (internal quotation marks omitted). Facing such evidence then, the Court expressly rejected the argument that disparities in voter turnout and number of elected officials were the only metrics capable of justifying reauthorization of the VRA. Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI turn next to the evidence on which Congress based its decision to reauthorize the coverage formula in \xc2\xa74(b). Because Congress did not alter the coverage formula, the same jurisdictions previously subject to preclearance continue to be covered by this remedy. The evidence just described, of preclearance\xe2\x80\x99s continuing efficacy in blocking constitutional violations in the covered jurisdictions, itself grounded Congress\xe2\x80\x99 conclusion that the remedy should be retained for those jurisdictions. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThere is no question, moreover, that the covered jurisdictions have a unique history of problems with racial discrimination in voting. Ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313. Consideration of this long history, still in living memory, was altogether appropriate. The Court criticizes Congress for failing to recognize that \xe2\x80\x9chistory did not end in 1965.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 20. But the Court ignores that \xe2\x80\x9cwhat\xe2\x80\x99s past is prologue.\xe2\x80\x9d W. Shakespeare, The Tempest, act 2, sc. 1. And \xe2\x80\x9c[t]hose who cannot remember the past are condemned to repeat it.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 G. Santayana, The Life of Reason 284 (1905). Congress was especially mindful of the need to reinforce the gains already made and to prevent backsliding. 2006 Reauthorization \xc2\xa72(b)(9).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOf particular importance, even after 40 years and thousands of discriminatory changes blocked by preclearance, conditions in the covered jurisdictions demonstrated that the formula was still justified by \xe2\x80\x9ccurrent needs.\xe2\x80\x9d Northwest Austin, 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 203.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress learned of these conditions through a report, known as the Katz study, that looked at \xc2\xa72 suits between 1982 and 2004. To Examine the Impact and Effectiveness of the Voting Rights Act: Hearing before the Subcommittee on the Constitution of the House Committee on the Judiciary, 109th Cong., 1st Sess., pp. 964\xe2\x80\x931124 (2005) (hereinafter Impact and Effectiveness). Because the private right of action authorized by \xc2\xa72 of the VRA applies nationwide, a comparison of \xc2\xa72 lawsuits in covered and noncovered jurisdictions provides an appropriate yardstick for measuring differences between covered and noncovered jurisdictions. If differences in the risk of voting discrimination between covered and noncovered jurisdictions had disappeared, one would expect that the rate of successful \xc2\xa72 lawsuits would be roughly the same in both areas.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t The study\xe2\x80\x99s findings, however, indicated that racial discrimination in voting remains \xe2\x80\x9cconcentrated in the jurisdictions singled out for preclearance.\xe2\x80\x9d Northwest Austin, 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 203.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAlthough covered jurisdictions account for less than 25 percent of the country\xe2\x80\x99s population, the Katz study revealed that they accounted for 56 percent of successful \xc2\xa72 litigation since 1982. Impact and Effectiveness 974. Controlling for population, there were nearly four times as many successful \xc2\xa72 cases in covered jurisdictions as there were in noncovered jurisdictions. 679 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 874. The Katz study further found that \xc2\xa72 lawsuits are more likely to succeed when they are filed in covered jurisdictions than in noncovered jurisdictions. Impact and Effectiveness 974. From these findings\xe2\x80\x94ignored by the Court\xe2\x80\x94Congress reasonably concluded that the coverage formula continues to identify the jurisdictions of greatest concern.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe evidence before Congress, furthermore, indicated that voting in the covered jurisdictions was more racially polarized than elsewhere in the country. H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 109\xe2\x80\x93478, at 34\xe2\x80\x9335. While racially polarized voting alone does not signal a constitutional violation, it is a factor that increases the vulnerability of racial minorities to dis-criminatory changes in voting law. The reason is twofold. First, racial polarization means that racial minorities are at risk of being systematically outvoted and having their interests underrepresented in legislatures. Second, \xe2\x80\x9cwhen political preferences fall along racial lines, the natural inclinations of incumbents and ruling parties to entrench themselves have predictable racial effects. Under circumstances of severe racial polarization, efforts to gain political advantage translate into race-specific disadvantages.\xe2\x80\x9d Ansolabehere, Persily, & Stewart, Regional Differences in Racial Polarization in the 2012 Presidential Election: Implications for the Constitutionality of Section 5 of the Voting Rights Act, 126 Harv. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. Forum 205, 209 (2013).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn other words, a governing political coalition has an incentive to prevent changes in the existing balance of voting power. When voting is racially polarized, efforts by the ruling party to pursue that incentive \xe2\x80\x9cwill inevitably discriminate against a racial group.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Just as buildings in California have a greater need to be earthquake-proofed, places where there is greater racial polarization in voting have a greater need for prophylactic measures to prevent purposeful race discrimination. This point was understood by Congress and is well recognized in the academic literature. See 2006 Reauthorization \xc2\xa72(b)(3), \n\n120Stat.\n577 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe continued evidence of racially polarized voting in each of the jurisdictions covered by the [preclearance requirement] demonstrates that racial and language minorities remain politically vulnerable\xe2\x80\x9d); H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 109\xe2\x80\x93478, at 35; Davidson, The Recent Evolution of Voting Rights Law Affecting Racial and Language Minorities, in Quiet Revolution 21, 22.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe case for retaining a coverage formula that met needs on the ground was therefore solid. Congress might have been charged with rigidity had it afforded covered jurisdictions no way out or ignored jurisdictions that needed superintendence. Congress, however, responded to this concern. Critical components of the congressional design are the statutory provisions allowing jurisdictions to \xe2\x80\x9cbail out\xe2\x80\x9d of preclearance, and for court-ordered \xe2\x80\x9cbail ins.\xe2\x80\x9d See Northwest Austin, 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 199. The VRA permits a jurisdiction to bail out by showing that it has complied with the Act for ten years, and has engaged in efforts to eliminate intimidation and harassment of vot-ers. \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71973b(a) (2006 ed. and Supp. V). It also authorizes a court to subject a noncovered jurisdiction to federal preclearance upon finding that violations of the Fourteenth and \n \nFifteenth Amendments have occurred there. \xc2\xa71973a(c) (2006 ed.).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress was satisfied that the VRA\xe2\x80\x99s bailout mechanism provided an effective means of adjusting the VRA\xe2\x80\x99s coverage over time. H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 109\xe2\x80\x93478, at 25 (the success of bailout \xe2\x80\x9cillustrates that: (1) covered status is neither permanent nor over-broad; and (2) covered status has been and continues to be within the control of the jurisdiction such that those jurisdictions that have a genuinely clean record and want to terminate coverage have the ability to do so\xe2\x80\x9d). Nearly 200 jurisdictions have successfully bailed out of the preclearance requirement, and DOJ has consented to every bailout application filed by an eligible jurisdiction since the current bailout procedure became effective in 1984. Brief for Federal Respondent 54. The bail-in mechanism has also worked. Several jurisdictions have been subject to federal preclearance by court orders, including the States of New Mexico and Arkansas. App. to Brief for Federal Respondent 1a\xe2\x80\x933a.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis experience exposes the inaccuracy of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s portrayal of the Act as static, unchanged since 1965. Congress designed the VRA to be a dynamic statute, capable of adjusting to changing conditions. True, many covered jurisdictions have not been able to bail out due to recent acts of noncompliance with the VRA, but that truth reinforces the congressional judgment that these jurisdictions were rightfully subject to preclearance, and ought to remain under that regime.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress approached the 2006 reauthorization of the VRA with great care and seriousness. The same cannot be said of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion today. The Court makes no genuine attempt to engage with the massive legislative record that Congress assembled. Instead, it relies on increases in voter registration and turnout as if that were the whole story. See supra, at 18\xe2\x80\x9319. Without even identifying a standard of review, the Court dismissively brushes off arguments based on \xe2\x80\x9cdata from the record,\xe2\x80\x9d and declines to enter the \xe2\x80\x9cdebat[e about] what [the] record shows.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 20\xe2\x80\x9321. One would expect more from an opinion striking at the heart of the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s signal piece of civil-rights legislation.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI note the most disturbing lapses. First, by what right, given its usual restraint, does the Court even address Shelby County\xe2\x80\x99s facial challenge to the VRA? Second, the Court veers away from controlling precedent regarding the \xe2\x80\x9cequal sovereignty\xe2\x80\x9d doctrine without even acknowledging that it is doing so. Third, hardly showing the respect ordinarily paid when Congress acts to implement the Civil War Amendments, and as just stressed, the Court does not even deign to grapple with the legislative record.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tShelby County launched a purely facial challenge to the VRA\xe2\x80\x99s 2006 reauthorization. \xe2\x80\x9cA facial challenge to a legislative Act,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court has other times said, \xe2\x80\x9cis, of course, the most difficult challenge to mount successfully, since the challenger must establish that no set of circumstances exists under which the Act would be valid.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Salerno, \n\n481 U.\xc2\xa0S. 739, \n745 (1987)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c[U]nder our constitutional system[,] courts are not roving commissions assigned to pass judgment on the validity of the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s laws.\xe2\x80\x9d Broadrick v. Oklahoma, \n\n413 U.\xc2\xa0S. 601\n\xe2\x80\x93611 (1973). Instead, the \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial Power\xe2\x80\x9d is limited to deciding particular \xe2\x80\x9cCases\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cControversies.\xe2\x80\x9d U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0III, \xc2\xa72. \xe2\x80\x9cEmbedded in the traditional rules governing constitutional adjudication is the principle that a person to whom a statute may constitutionally be applied will not be heard to challenge that statute on the ground that it may conceivably be applied unconstitutionally to others, in other situations not before the Court.\xe2\x80\x9d Broadrick, 413 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 610. Yet the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in this case contains not a word explaining why Congress lacks the power to subject to preclearance the particular plaintiff that initiated this lawsuit\xe2\x80\x94Shelby County, Alabama. The reason for the Court\xe2\x80\x99s silence is apparent, for as applied to Shelby County, the VRA\xe2\x80\x99s preclearance requirement is hardly contestable.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAlabama is home to Selma, site of the \xe2\x80\x9cBloody Sunday\xe2\x80\x9d beatings of civil-rights demonstrators that served as the catalyst for the VRA\xe2\x80\x99s enactment. Following those events, Martin Luther King, Jr., led a march from Selma to Montgomery, Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s capital, where he called for passage of the VRA. If the Act passed, he foresaw, progress could be made even in Alabama, but there had to be a steadfast national commitment to see the task through to completion. In King\xe2\x80\x99s words, \xe2\x80\x9cthe arc of the moral universe is long, but it bends toward justice.\xe2\x80\x9d G. May, Bending Toward Justice: The Voting Rights Act and the Transformation of American Democracy 144 (2013).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tHistory has proved King right. Although circumstances in Alabama have changed, serious concerns remain. Between 1982 and 2005, Alabama had one of the highest rates of successful \xc2\xa72 suits, second only to its VRA-covered neighbor Mississippi. 679 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 897 (Williams, J., dissenting). In other words, even while subject to the restraining effect of \xc2\xa75, Alabama was found to have \xe2\x80\x9cdeni[ed] or abridge[d]\xe2\x80\x9d voting rights \xe2\x80\x9con account of race or color\xe2\x80\x9d more frequently than nearly all other States in the Union. \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71973(a). This fact prompted the dissenting judge below to concede that \xe2\x80\x9ca more narrowly tailored coverage formula\xe2\x80\x9d capturing Alabama and a handful of other jurisdictions with an established track record of racial discrimination in voting \xe2\x80\x9cmight be defensible.\xe2\x80\x9d 679 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 897 (opinion of Williams, J.). That is an understatement. Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s sorry history of \xc2\xa72 violations alone provides sufficient justification for Congress\xe2\x80\x99 determination in 2006 that the State should remain subject to \xc2\xa75\xe2\x80\x99s preclearance requirement.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t7\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA few examples suffice to demonstrate that, at least in Alabama, the \xe2\x80\x9ccurrent burdens\xe2\x80\x9d imposed by \xc2\xa75\xe2\x80\x99s preclearance requirement are \xe2\x80\x9cjustified by current needs.\xe2\x80\x9d Northwest Austin, 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 203. In the interim between the VRA\xe2\x80\x99s 1982 and 2006 reauthorizations, this Court twice confronted purposeful racial discrimination in Alabama. In Pleasant Grove v. United States, \n\n479 U.\xc2\xa0S. 462 (1987)\n, the Court held that Pleasant Grove\xe2\x80\x94a city in Jefferson County, Shelby County\xe2\x80\x99s neighbor\xe2\x80\x94engaged in purposeful discrimination by annexing all-white areas while rejecting the annexation request of an adjacent black neighborhood. The city had \xe2\x80\x9cshown unambiguous opposition to racial integration, both before and after the passage of the fed-eral civil rights laws,\xe2\x80\x9d and its strategic annexations appeared to be an attempt \xe2\x80\x9cto provide for the growth of a monolithic white voting block\xe2\x80\x9d for \xe2\x80\x9cthe impermissible purpose of minimizing future black voting strength.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 465, 471\xe2\x80\x93472.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTwo years before Pleasant Grove, the Court in Hunter v. Underwood, \n\n471 U.\xc2\xa0S. 222 (1985)\n, struck down a provision of the Alabama Constitution that prohibited individuals convicted of misdemeanor offenses \xe2\x80\x9cinvolving moral turpitude\xe2\x80\x9d from voting. Id., at 223 (internal quotation marks omitted). The provision violated the \n \nFourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Equal Protection Clause, the Court unanimously concluded, because \xe2\x80\x9cits original enactment was motivated by a desire to discriminate against blacks on account of race[,] and the [provision] continues to this day to have that effect.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 233.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPleasant Grove and Hunter were not anomalies. In 1986, a Federal District Judge concluded that the at-large election systems in several Alabama counties violated \xc2\xa72. Dillard v. Crenshaw Cty., 640 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 1347, 1354\xe2\x80\x931363 (MD Ala. 1986). Summarizing its findings, the court stated that \xe2\x80\x9c[f\xc2\xa0]rom the late 1800\xe2\x80\x99s through the present, [Alabama] has consistently erected barriers to keep black persons from full and equal participation in the social, economic, and political life of the state.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1360.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Dillard litigation ultimately expanded to include 183 cities, counties, and school boards employing discriminatory at-large election systems. Dillard v. Baldwin Cty. Bd. of Ed., 686 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 1459, 1461 (MD Ala. 1988). One of those defendants was Shelby County, which eventually signed a consent decree to resolve the claims against it. See Dillard v. Crenshaw Cty., 748 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 819 (MD Ala. 1990).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAlthough the Dillard litigation resulted in overhauls of numerous electoral systems tainted by racial discrimination, concerns about backsliding persist. In 2008, for example, the city of Calera, located in Shelby County, requested preclearance of a redistricting plan that \xe2\x80\x9cwould have eliminated the city\xe2\x80\x99s sole majority-black district, which had been created pursuant to the consent decree in Dillard.\xe2\x80\x9d 811 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 424, 443 (DC 2011). Although DOJ objected to the plan, Calera forged ahead with elections based on the unprecleared voting changes, resulting in the defeat of the incumbent African-American councilman who represented the former majority-black district. Ibid. The city\xe2\x80\x99s defiance required DOJ to bring a \xc2\xa75 enforcement action that ultimately yielded appropriate redress, including restoration of the majority-black district. Ibid.; Brief for Respondent-Intervenors Earl Cunningham et\xc2\xa0al. 20.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA recent FBI investigation provides a further window into the persistence of racial discrimination in state politics. See United States v. McGregor, 824 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1339, 1344\xe2\x80\x931348 (MD Ala. 2011). Recording devices worn by state legislators cooperating with the FBI\xe2\x80\x99s investigation captured conversations between members of the state legislature and their political allies. The recorded conversations are shocking. Members of the state Senate derisively refer to African-Americans as \xe2\x80\x9cAborigines\xe2\x80\x9d and talk openly of their aim to quash a particular gambling-related referendum because the referendum, if placed on the ballot, might increase African-American voter turnout. Id., at 1345\xe2\x80\x931346 (internal quotation marks omitted). See also id., at 1345 (legislators and their allies expressed concern that if the referendum were placed on the ballot, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[e]very black, every illiterate\xe2\x80\x99 would be \xe2\x80\x98bused [to the polls] on HUD financed buses\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). These conversations oc-curred not in the 1870\xe2\x80\x99s, or even in the 1960\xe2\x80\x99s, they took place in 2010. Id., at 1344\xe2\x80\x931345. The District Judge presiding over the criminal trial at which the recorded conversations were introduced commented that the \xe2\x80\x9crecordings represent compelling evidence that political exclusion through racism remains a real and enduring problem\xe2\x80\x9d in Alabama. Id., at 1347. Racist sentiments, the judge observed, \xe2\x80\x9cremain regrettably entrenched in the high echelons of state government.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThese recent episodes forcefully demonstrate that \xc2\xa75\xe2\x80\x99s preclearance requirement is constitutional as applied to Alabama and its political subdivisions.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t8\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t And under our case law, that conclusion should suffice to resolve this case. See United States v. Raines, \n\n362 U.\xc2\xa0S. 17\n\xe2\x80\x9325 (1960) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]f the complaint here called for an application of the statute clearly constitutional under the \n \nFifteenth Amendment, that should have been an end to the question of constitutionality.\xe2\x80\x9d). See also Nevada Dept. of Human Resources v. Hibbs, \n\n538 U.\xc2\xa0S. 721, \n743 (2003)\n (Scalia, J., dissenting) (where, as here, a state or local government raises a facial challenge to a federal statute on the ground that it exceeds Congress\xe2\x80\x99 enforcement powers under the Civil War Amendments, the challenge fails if the opposing party is able to show that the statute \xe2\x80\x9ccould constitutionally be applied to some jurisdictions\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis Court has consistently rejected constitutional challenges to legislation enacted pursuant to Congress\xe2\x80\x99 enforcement powers under the Civil War Amendments upon finding that the legislation was constitutional as applied to the particular set of circumstances before the Court. See United States v. Georgia, \n\n546 U.\xc2\xa0S. 151, \n159 (2006)\n (Title II of the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990 (ADA) validly abrogates state sovereign immunity \xe2\x80\x9cinsofar as [it] creates a private cause of action .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. for conduct that actually violates the \n \nFourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x9d); Tennessee v. Lane, \n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 509\n\xe2\x80\x93534 (2004) (Title II of the ADA is constitutional \xe2\x80\x9cas it applies to the class of cases implicating the fundamental right of access to the courts\xe2\x80\x9d); Raines, 362 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 24\xe2\x80\x9326 (federal statute proscribing deprivations of the right to vote based on race was constitutional as applied to the state officials before the Court, even if it could not constitutionally be applied to other parties). A similar approach is warranted here.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t9\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe VRA\xe2\x80\x99s exceptionally broad severability provision makes it particularly inappropriate for the Court to allow Shelby County to mount a facial challenge to \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa74(b) and 5 of the VRA, even though application of those provisions to the county falls well within the bounds of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 legislative authority. The severability provision states:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cIf any provision of [this Act] or the application thereof to any person or circumstances is held invalid, the remainder of [the Act] and the application of the provision to other persons not similarly situated or to other circumstances shall not be affected thereby.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71973p.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIn other words, even if the VRA could not constitutionally be applied to certain States\xe2\x80\x94e.g., Arizona and Alaska, see ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x94\xc2\xa71973p calls for those unconstitutional applications to be severed, leaving the Act in place for juris-dictions as to which its application does not transgress constitutional limits.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNevertheless, the Court suggests that limiting the jurisdictional scope of the VRA in an appropriate case would be \xe2\x80\x9cto try our hand at updating the statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 22. Just last Term, however, the Court rejected this very argument when addressing a materially identical severability provision, explaining that such a provision is \xe2\x80\x9cCongress\xe2\x80\x99 explicit textual instruction to leave unaffected the remainder of [the Act]\xe2\x80\x9d if any particular \xe2\x80\x9capplication is unconstitutional.\xe2\x80\x9d National Federation of Independent Business v. Sebelius, 567 U.\xc2\xa0S. __, __ (2012) (plurality opinion) (slip op., at 56) (internal quotation marks omitted); id., at __ (Ginsburg, J., concurring in part, concurring in judgment in part, and dissenting in part) (slip op., at 60) (agreeing with the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s severability analysis). See also Raines, 362 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 23 (a statute capable of some constitutional applications may nonetheless be susceptible to a facial challenge only in \xe2\x80\x9cthat rarest of cases where this Court can justifiably think itself able confidently to discern that Congress would not have desired its legislation to stand at all unless it could validly stand in its every application\xe2\x80\x9d). Leaping to resolve Shelby County\xe2\x80\x99s facial challenge without considering whether application of the VRA to Shelby County is constitutional, or even addressing the VRA\xe2\x80\x99s severability provision, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion can hardly be described as an exemplar of restrained and moderate decisionmaking. Quite the opposite. Hubris is a fit word for today\xe2\x80\x99s demolition of the VRA.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court stops any application of \xc2\xa75 by holding that \xc2\xa74(b)\xe2\x80\x99s coverage formula is unconstitutional. It pins this result, in large measure, to \xe2\x80\x9cthe fundamental principle of equal sovereignty.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311, 23. In Katzenbach, however, the Court held, in no uncertain terms, that the principle \xe2\x80\x9capplies only to the terms upon which States are admitted to the Union, and not to the remedies for local evils which have subsequently appeared.\xe2\x80\x9d 383 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 328\xe2\x80\x93329 (emphasis added).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tKatzenbach, the Court acknowledges, \xe2\x80\x9crejected the notion that the [equal sovereignty] principle operate[s] as a bar on differential treatment outside [the] context [of the admission of new States].\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11 (citing 383 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 328\xe2\x80\x93329) (emphasis omitted). But the Court clouds that once clear understanding by citing dictum from Northwest Austin to convey that the principle of equal sovereignty \xe2\x80\x9cremains highly pertinent in assessing subsequent disparate treatment of States.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11 (citing 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 203). See also ante, at 23 (relying on Northwest Austin\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cemphasis on [the] significance\xe2\x80\x9d of the equal-sovereignty principle). If the Court is suggesting that dictum in Northwest Austin silently overruled Katzenbach\xe2\x80\x99s limitation of the equal sovereignty doctrine to \xe2\x80\x9cthe admission of new States,\xe2\x80\x9d the suggestion is untenable. Northwest Austin cited Katzenbach\xe2\x80\x99s holding in the course of declining to decide whether the VRA was constitutional or even what standard of review applied to the question. 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 203\xe2\x80\x93204. In today\xe2\x80\x99s decision, the Court ratchets up what was pure dictum in Northwest Austin, attributing breadth to the equal sovereignty principle in flat contradiction of Katzenbach. The Court does so with nary an explanation of why it finds Katzenbach wrong, let alone any discussion of whether stare decisis nonetheless counsels adherence to Katzenbach\xe2\x80\x99s ruling on the limited \xe2\x80\x9csignificance\xe2\x80\x9d of the equal sovereignty principle.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tToday\xe2\x80\x99s unprecedented extension of the equal sover-eignty principle outside its proper domain\xe2\x80\x94the admission of new States\xe2\x80\x94is capable of much mischief. Federal statutes that treat States disparately are hardly novelties. See, e.g., \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73704 (no State may operate or permit a sports-related gambling scheme, unless that State conducted such a scheme \xe2\x80\x9cat any time during the period beginning January 1, 1976, and ending August 31, 1990\xe2\x80\x9d); \n\n26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7142(l) (EPA required to locate green building project in a State meeting specified population criteria); \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73796bb (at least 50 percent of rural drug enforcement assistance funding must be allocated to States with \xe2\x80\x9ca population density of fifty-two or fewer persons per square mile or a State in which the largest county has fewer than one hundred and fifty thousand people, based on the decennial census of 1990 through fiscal year 1997\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa713925, 13971 (similar population criteria for funding to combat rural domestic violence); \xc2\xa710136 (specifying rules applicable to Nevada\xe2\x80\x99s Yucca Mountain nuclear waste site, and providing that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o State, other than the State of Nevada, may receive financial assistance under this subsection after December 22, 1987\xe2\x80\x9d). Do such provisions remain safe given the Court\xe2\x80\x99s expansion of equal sovereignty\xe2\x80\x99s sway?\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOf gravest concern, Congress relied on our pathmarking Katzenbach decision in each reauthorization of the VRA. It had every reason to believe that the Act\xe2\x80\x99s limited geographical scope would weigh in favor of, not against, the Act\xe2\x80\x99s constitutionality. See, e.g., United States v. Morrison, \n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 598\n\xe2\x80\x93627 (2000) (confining preclearance regime to States with a record of discrimination bolstered the VRA\xe2\x80\x99s constitutionality). Congress could hardly have foreseen that the VRA\xe2\x80\x99s limited geographic reach would render the Act constitutionally suspect. See Persily 195 (\xe2\x80\x9c[S]upporters of the Act sought to develop an evidentiary record for the principal purpose of explaining why the covered jurisdictions should remain covered, rather than justifying the coverage of certain jurisdictions but not others.\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conception, it appears, defenders of the VRA could not prevail upon showing what the record overwhelmingly bears out, i.e., that there is a need for continuing the preclearance regime in covered States. In addition, the defenders would have to disprove the existence of a comparable need elsewhere. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 61\xe2\x80\x9362 (suggesting that proof of egregious episodes of racial discrimination in covered jurisdictions would not suffice to carry the day for the VRA, unless such episodes are shown to be absent elsewhere). I am aware of no precedent for imposing such a double burden on defenders of legislation.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tC\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court has time and again declined to upset legislation of this genre unless there was no or almost no evidence of unconstitutional action by States. See, e.g., City of Boerne v. Flores, \n\n521 U.\xc2\xa0S. 507, \n530 (1997)\n (legislative record \xe2\x80\x9cmention[ed] no episodes [of the kind the legislation aimed to check] occurring in the past 40 years\xe2\x80\x9d). No such claim can be made about the congressional record for the 2006 VRA reauthorization. Given a record replete with examples of denial or abridgment of a paramount federal right, the Court should have left the matter where it belongs: in Congress\xe2\x80\x99 bailiwick.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tInstead, the Court strikes \xc2\xa74(b)\xe2\x80\x99s coverage provision because, in its view, the provision is not based on \xe2\x80\x9ccurrent conditions.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 17. It discounts, however, that one such condition was the preclearance remedy in place in the covered jurisdictions, a remedy Congress designed both to catch discrimination before it causes harm, and to guard against return to old ways. 2006 Reauthorization \xc2\xa72(b)(3), (9). Volumes of evidence supported Congress\xe2\x80\x99 de-termination that the prospect of retrogression was real. Throwing out preclearance when it has worked and is continuing to work to stop discriminatory changes is like throwing away your umbrella in a rainstorm because you are not getting wet.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut, the Court insists, the coverage formula is no good; it is based on \xe2\x80\x9cdecades-old data and eradicated practices.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 18. Even if the legislative record shows, as engaging with it would reveal, that the formula accurately identifies the jurisdictions with the worst conditions of voting discrimination, that is of no moment, as the Court sees it. Congress, the Court decrees, must \xe2\x80\x9cstar[t] from scratch.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 23. I do not see why that should be so.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress\xe2\x80\x99 chore was different in 1965 than it was in 2006. In 1965, there were a \xe2\x80\x9csmall number of States .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. which in most instances were familiar to Congress by name,\xe2\x80\x9d on which Congress fixed its attention. Katzenbach, 383 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 328. In drafting the coverage formula, \xe2\x80\x9cCongress began work with reliable evidence of actual voting discrimination in a great majority of the States\xe2\x80\x9d it sought to target. Id., at 329. \xe2\x80\x9cThe formula [Congress] eventually evolved to describe these areas\xe2\x80\x9d also captured a few States that had not been the subject of congressional factfinding. Ibid. Nevertheless, the Court upheld the formula in its entirety, finding it fair \xe2\x80\x9cto infer a significant danger of the evil\xe2\x80\x9d in all places the formula covered. Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe situation Congress faced in 2006, when it took up reauthorization of the coverage formula, was not the same. By then, the formula had been in effect for many years, and all of the jurisdictions covered by it were \xe2\x80\x9cfamiliar to Congress by name.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 328. The question before Congress: Was there still a sufficient basis to support continued application of the preclearance remedy in each of those already-identified places? There was at that point no chance that the formula might inadvertently sweep in new areas that were not the subject of congressional findings. And Congress could determine from the record whether the jurisdictions captured by the coverage for-mula still belonged under the preclearance regime. If they did, there was no need to alter the formula. That is why the Court, in addressing prior reauthorizations of the VRA, did not question the continuing \xe2\x80\x9crelevance\xe2\x80\x9d of the formula.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tConsider once again the components of the record before Congress in 2006. The coverage provision identified a known list of places with an undisputed history of serious problems with racial discrimination in voting. Recent evidence relating to Alabama and its counties was there for all to see. Multiple Supreme Court decisions had upheld the coverage provision, most recently in 1999. There was extensive evidence that, due to the preclearance mechanism, conditions in the covered jurisdictions had notably improved. And there was evidence that preclearance was still having a substantial real-world effect, having stopped hundreds of discriminatory voting changes in the covered jurisdictions since the last reauthorization. In addition, there was evidence that racial polarization in voting was higher in covered jurisdictions than elsewhere, increasing the vulnerability of minority citizens in those jurisdictions. And countless witnesses, reports, and case studies documented continuing problems with voting dis-crimination in those jurisdictions. In light of this rec- ord, Congress had more than a reasonable basis to conclude that the existing coverage formula was not out of sync with conditions on the ground in covered areas. And certainly Shelby County was no candidate for release through the mechanism Congress provided. See supra, at 22\xe2\x80\x9323, 26\xe2\x80\x9328.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court holds \xc2\xa74(b) invalid on the ground that it is \xe2\x80\x9cirrational to base coverage on the use of voting tests 40 years ago, when such tests have been illegal since that time.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 23. But the Court disregards what Congress set about to do in enacting the VRA. That extraordinary legislation scarcely stopped at the particular tests and devices that happened to exist in 1965. The grand aim of the Act is to secure to all in our polity equal citizenship stature, a voice in our democracy undiluted by race. As the record for the 2006 reauthorization makes abundantly clear, second-generation barriers to minority voting rights have emerged in the covered jurisdictions as attempted substitutes for the first-generation barriers that originally triggered preclearance in those jurisdictions. See supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x936, 8, 15\xe2\x80\x9317. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe sad irony of today\xe2\x80\x99s decision lies in its utter failure to grasp why the VRA has proven effective. The Court appears to believe that the VRA\xe2\x80\x99s success in eliminating the specific devices extant in 1965 means that preclearance is no longer needed. Ante, at 21\xe2\x80\x9322, 23\xe2\x80\x9324. With that belief, and the argument derived from it, history repeats itself. The same assumption\xe2\x80\x94that the problem could be solved when particular methods of voting discrimination are identified and eliminated\xe2\x80\x94was indulged and proved wrong repeatedly prior to the VRA\xe2\x80\x99s enactment. Unlike prior statutes, which singled out particular tests or devices, the VRA is grounded in Congress\xe2\x80\x99 recognition of the \xe2\x80\x9cvariety and persistence\xe2\x80\x9d of measures designed to impair minority voting rights. Katzenbach, 383 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 311; supra, at 2. In truth, the evolution of voting discrimination into more subtle second-generation barriers is powerful evidence that a remedy as effective as preclearance remains vital to protect minority voting rights and prevent backsliding.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBeyond question, the VRA is no ordinary legislation. It is extraordinary because Congress embarked on a mission long delayed and of extraordinary importance: to realize the purpose and promise of the \n \nFifteenth Amendment. For a half century, a concerted effort has been made to end racial discrimination in voting. Thanks to the Voting Rights Act, progress once the subject of a dream has been achieved and continues to be made. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe record supporting the 2006 reauthorization of the VRA is also extraordinary. It was described by the Chairman of the House Judiciary Committee as \xe2\x80\x9cone of the most extensive considerations of any piece of legislation that the United States Congress has dealt with in the 27\xc2\xbd years\xe2\x80\x9d he had served in the House. 152 Cong. Rec. H5143 (July 13, 2006) (statement of Rep. Sensenbrenner). After exhaustive evidence-gathering and deliberative process, Congress reauthorized the VRA, including the coverage provision, with overwhelming bipartisan support. It was the judgment of Congress that \xe2\x80\x9c40 years has not been a sufficient amount of time to eliminate the vestiges of discrimination following nearly 100 years of disregard for the dictates of the 15th amendment and to ensure that the right of all citizens to vote is protected as guaranteed by the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d 2006 Reauthorization \xc2\xa72(b)(7), \n\n120Stat.\n577. That determination of the body empowered to enforce the Civil War Amendments \xe2\x80\x9cby appropriate legislation\xe2\x80\x9d merits this Court\xe2\x80\x99s utmost respect. In my judgment, the Court errs egregiously by overriding Congress\xe2\x80\x99 decision.\n\t\t\t
