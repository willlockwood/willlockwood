CSX TRANSPORTATION, INC. v. McBRIDE

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the seventh circuit

\n

No. 10\xe2\x80\x93235.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 28, 2011\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 23, 2011

\n

Respondent McBride, a locomotive engineer with petitioner CSX Transportation, Inc., an interstate railroad, sustained a debilitating hand injury while switching railroad cars. He filed suit under the Federal Employers\xe2\x80\x99 Liability Act (FELA), which holds railroads liable for employees\xe2\x80\x99 injuries \xe2\x80\x9cresulting in whole or in part from [carrier] negligence.\xe2\x80\x9d 45 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa751. McBride alleged that CSX negligently (1) required him to use unsafe switching equipment and (2) failed to train him to operate that equipment. A verdict for McBride would be in order, the District Court instructed, if the jury found that CSX\xe2\x80\x99s negligence \xe2\x80\x9ccaused or contributed to\xe2\x80\x9d his injury. The court declined CSX\xe2\x80\x99s request for additional charges requiring McBride to \xe2\x80\x9cshow that \xe2\x80\xa6 [CSX\xe2\x80\x99s] negligence was a proximate cause of the injury\xe2\x80\x9d and defining \xe2\x80\x9cproximate cause\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cany cause which, in natural or probable sequence, produced the injury complained of.\xe2\x80\x9d Instead, relying on Rogers v. Missouri Pacific R. Co., 352 U. S. 500, the court gave the Seventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s pattern FELA instruction: \xe2\x80\x9cDefendant \xe2\x80\x98caused or contributed to\xe2\x80\x99 Plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s injury if Defendant\xe2\x80\x99s negligence played a part\xe2\x80\x94no matter how small\xe2\x80\x94in bringing about the injury.\xe2\x80\x9d The jury returned a verdict for McBride.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On appeal, CSX renewed its objection to the failure to instruct on proximate cause, now defining the phrase to require a \xe2\x80\x9cdirect relation between the injury asserted and the injurious conduct alleged.\xe2\x80\x9d The appeals court, however, approved the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s instruction and affirmed its judgment for McBride. Because Rogers had relaxed the proximate cause requirement in FELA cases, the court said, an instruction that simply paraphrased Rogers\xe2\x80\x99 language could not be declared erroneous.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The judgment is affirmed.

\n

\n\n598 F. 3d 388, affirmed.

\n

Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court with respect to all but Part III\xe2\x80\x93A, concluding, in accord with FELA\xe2\x80\x99s text and purpose, Rogers, and the uniform view of the federal appellate courts, that FELA does not incorporate stock \xe2\x80\x9cproximate cause\xe2\x80\x9d standards developed in nonstatutory common-law tort actions. The charge proper in FELA cases simply tracks the language Congress employed, informing juries that a defendant railroad \xe2\x80\x9ccaused or contributed to\xe2\x80\x9d a railroad worker\xe2\x80\x99s injury \xe2\x80\x9cif [the railroad\xe2\x80\x99s] negligence played a part\xe2\x80\x94no matter how small\xe2\x80\x94in bringing about the injury.\xe2\x80\x9d That, indeed, is the test Congress prescribed for proximate causation in FELA cases. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9314, 16\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0CSX\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of Rogers is not persuasive. Pp. 4\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(1)\xc2\xa0Given FELA\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cbroad\xe2\x80\x9d causation language, Urie v. Thompson, 337 U. S. 163, 181, and Congress\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9chumanitarian\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cremedial goal[s]\xe2\x80\x9d in enacting the statute, FELA\xe2\x80\x99s causation standard is \xe2\x80\x9crelaxed\xe2\x80\x9d compared to that applicable in common-law tort litigation, Consolidated Rail Corporation v. Gottshall, 512 U. S. 532, 542\xe2\x80\x93543. Rogers described that relaxed standard as \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the proofs justify with reason the conclusion that employer negligence played any part, even the slightest, in producing the injury or death for which damages are sought.\xe2\x80\x9d 352 U. S., at 506. Because the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s instruction tracked Rogers\xe2\x80\x99 language, the instruction was plainly proper so long as Rogers actually prescribes the causation definition applicable under FELA. See Patterson v. McLean Credit Union, 491 U. S. 164, 172. CSX, however, contends that Rogers was a narrowly focused decision that did not displace common-law formulations of \xe2\x80\x9cproximate cause.\xe2\x80\x9d Drawing largely on Justice Souter\xe2\x80\x99s concurrence in Norfolk Southern R.\xc2\xa0Co. v. Sorrell, 549 U. S. 158, 173, CSX urges that Rogers\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cany part \xe2\x80\xa6 in producing the injury\xe2\x80\x9d test displaced only common-law restrictions on recovery for injuries involving contributory negligence or other multiple causes, but did not address the requisite directness of a cause. Pp. 4\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(2)\xc2\xa0In Rogers, the employee was burning vegetation that lined his employer\xe2\x80\x99s railroad tracks. A passing train fanned the flames, which spread to the top of the culvert where he was standing. Attempting to escape, he slipped and fell on the sloping gravel covering the culvert, sustaining serious injuries. 352 U. S., at 501\xe2\x80\x93503. The state-court jury returned a verdict for him, but the Missouri Supreme Court reversed. Even if the railroad had been negligent in failing to maintain a flat surface, the court reasoned, the employee was at fault because of his lack of attention to the spreading fire. As the fire \xe2\x80\x9cwas something extraordinary, unrelated to, and disconnected from the incline of the gravel,\xe2\x80\x9d the court found that \xe2\x80\x9cplaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s injury was not the natural and probable consequence of any negligence of defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. This Court reversed. FELA, this Court affirmed, did not incorporate any traditional common-law formulation of \xe2\x80\x9cproximate causation[,] which [requires] the jury [to] find that the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s negligence was the sole, efficient, producing cause of injury.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 506. Whether the railroad\xe2\x80\x99s negligent act was the \xe2\x80\x9cimmediate reason\xe2\x80\x9d for the fall, the Court added, was \xe2\x80\x9cirrelevant.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 503. The Court then announced its \xe2\x80\x9cany part \xe2\x80\xa6 in producing the injury\xe2\x80\x9d test, id., at 506.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Rogers is most sensibly read as a comprehensive statement of FELA\xe2\x80\x99s causation standard. The State Supreme Court there acknowledged that a FELA injury might have multiple causes, but considered the respondent railroad\xe2\x80\x99s part too indirect to establish the requisite causation. That is the very reasoning this Court rejected in Rogers. It is also the reasoning CSX asks this Court to resurrect. The interpretation adopted today is informed by the statutory history, see Trainmen v. Virginia ex rel. Virginia State Bar, 377 U. S. 1, 3, the precedents on which Rogers drew, see, e.g., Coray v. Southern Pacific Co., 335 U. S. 520, 523\xe2\x80\x93524, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s subsequent decisions, see, e.g., Ferguson v. Moore-McCormack Lines, Inc., 352 U. S. 521, 523\xe2\x80\x93524, the decisions of every Court of Appeals that reviews FELA cases, and the overwhelming majority of state courts and scholars. This understanding of Rogers \xe2\x80\x9chas been accepted as settled law for several decades.\xe2\x80\x9d IBP, Inc. v. Alvarez, 546 U. S. 21, 32. To discard or restrict the instruction now would ill serve stare decisis. Pp. 6\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0CSX nonetheless worries that the Rogers \xe2\x80\x9cany part\xe2\x80\x9d instruction opens the door to unlimited liability, inviting juries to impose liability on the basis of \xe2\x80\x9cbut for\xe2\x80\x9d causation. A half century\xe2\x80\x99s experience with Rogers gives little cause for concern: CSX has not identified even one trial in which the instruction generated an absurd or untoward award.

\n

\n\nFELA\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cin whole or in part\xe2\x80\x9d language is straightforward. \xe2\x80\x9c[R]easonable foreseeability of harm is an essential ingredient of [FELA] negligence,\xe2\x80\x9d Gallick v. Baltimore & Ohio R. Co., 372 U. S. 108, 117 (emphasis added). If negligence is proved, however, and is shown to have \xe2\x80\x9cplayed any part, even the slightest, in producing the injury,\xe2\x80\x9d Rogers, 352 U. S., at 506, then the carrier is answerable in damages even if \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the extent of the [injury] or the manner in which it occurred\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d was not \xe2\x80\x9c[p]robable\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cforeseeable.\xe2\x80\x9d Gallick, 372 U. S., at 120\xe2\x80\x93121, and n.\xc2\xa08. Properly instructed on negligence and causation, and told, as is standard practice in FELA cases, to use their \xe2\x80\x9ccommon sense\xe2\x80\x9d in reviewing the evidence, juries would have no warrant to award damages in far out \xe2\x80\x9cbut for\xe2\x80\x9d scenarios, and judges would have no warrant to submit such cases to the jury. Pp. 12\xe2\x80\x9314, 16\xe2\x80\x9319.

CSX TRANSPORTATION, INC., PETITIONER v.\nROBERT McBRIDE

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the seventh circuit

\n

[June 23, 2011]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court, except as to Part III\xe2\x80\x93A.*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case concerns the standard of causation applicable in cases arising under the Federal Employers\xe2\x80\x99 Liability Act (FELA), 45 U. S. C. \xc2\xa751 et seq. FELA renders railroads liable for employees\xe2\x80\x99 injuries or deaths \xe2\x80\x9cresulting in whole or in part from [carrier] negligence.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa751. In accord with the text and purpose of the Act, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Rogers v. Missouri Pacific R. Co., 352 U. S. 500 (1957), and the uniform view of federal appellate courts, we conclude that the Act does not incorporate \xe2\x80\x9cproximate cause\xe2\x80\x9d standards developed in nonstatutory common-law tort actions. The charge proper in FELA cases, we hold, simply tracks the language Congress employed, informing juries that a defendant railroad caused or contributed to a plaintiff employee\xe2\x80\x99s injury if the railroad\xe2\x80\x99s negligence played any part in bringing about the injury.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent Robert McBride worked as a locomotive engineer for petitioner CSX Transportation, Inc., which operates an interstate system of railroads. On April 12, 2004, CSX assigned McBride to assist on a local run between Evansville, Indiana, and Mount Vernon, Illinois. The run involved frequent starts and stops to add and remove individual rail cars, a process known as \xe2\x80\x9cswitching.\xe2\x80\x9d The train McBride was to operate had an unusual engine configuration: two \xe2\x80\x9cwide-body\xe2\x80\x9d engines followed \nby three smaller conventional cabs. McBride protested that the configuration was unsafe, because switching with heavy, wide-body engines required constant use of a hand-operated independent brake. But he was told to take the train as is. About ten hours into the run, McBride injured his hand while using the independent brake. Despite two surgeries and extensive physical therapy, he never regained full use of the hand.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Seeking compensation for his injury, McBride commenced a FELA action against CSX in the U. S. District Court for the Southern District of Illinois. He alleged that CSX was twice negligent: First, the railroad required him to use equipment unsafe for switching; second, CSX failed to train him to operate that equipment. App. 24a\xe2\x80\x9326a. A verdict for McBride would be in order, the District Court instructed, if the jury found that CSX \xe2\x80\x9cwas negligent\xe2\x80\x9d and that the \xe2\x80\x9cnegligence caused or contributed to\xe2\x80\x9d McBride\xe2\x80\x99s injury. Id., at 23a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0CSX sought additional charges that the court declined to give. One of the rejected instructions would have required \xe2\x80\x9cthe plaintiff [to] show that \xe2\x80\xa6 the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s negligence was a proximate cause of the injury.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 34a. Another would have defined \xe2\x80\x9cproximate cause\xe2\x80\x9d to mean \xe2\x80\x9cany cause which, in natural or probable sequence, produced the injury complained of,\xe2\x80\x9d with the qualification that a proximate cause \xe2\x80\x9cneed not be the only cause, nor the last or nearest cause.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 32a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Instead, the District Court employed, as McBride requested, the Seventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s pattern instruction for FELA cases, which reads:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cDefendant \xe2\x80\x98caused or contributed to\xe2\x80\x99 Plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s injury if Defendant\xe2\x80\x99s negligence played a part\xe2\x80\x94no matter how small\xe2\x80\x94in bringing about the injury. The mere fact that an injury occurred does not necessarily mean that the injury was caused by negligence.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 31a.

\n

\nFor this instruction, the Seventh Circuit relied upon this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Rogers v. Missouri Pacific R. Co., 352 U. S. 500 (1957). The jury returned a verdict for McBride, setting total damages at $275,000, but reducing that amount by one-third, the percentage the jury attributed to plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s negligence. App. 29a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0CSX appealed to the Seventh Circuit, renewing its ob-jection to the failure to instruct on \xe2\x80\x9cproximate cause.\xe2\x80\x9d Before the appellate court, CSX \xe2\x80\x9cmaintain[ed] that the correct definition of proximate causation is a \xe2\x80\x98direct relation between the injury asserted and the injurious conduct alleged.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 598 F.\xc2\xa03d 388, 393, n.\xc2\xa03 (2010) (quoting Holmes v. Securities Investor Protection Corporation, 503 U. S. 258, 268 (1992)). A properly instructed jury, CSX contended, might have found that the chain of causation was too indirect, or that the engine configuration was unsafe because of its propensity to cause crashes during switching, not because of any risk to an engineer\xe2\x80\x99s hands. Brief for Defendant-Appellant in No. 08\xe2\x80\x933557 (CA7), pp. 49\xe2\x80\x9352.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals approved the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s instruction and affirmed the judgment entered on the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict. Rogers had \xe2\x80\x9crelaxed the proximate cause requirement\xe2\x80\x9d in FELA cases, the Seventh Circuit concluded, a view of Rogers \xe2\x80\x9cechoed by every other court of appeals.\xe2\x80\x9d 598 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 399. While acknowledging that a handful of state courts \xe2\x80\x9cstill appl[ied] traditional formulations of proximate cause in FELA cases,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 404, n. 7, the Seventh Circuit said it could hardly declare erroneous an instruction that \xe2\x80\x9csimply paraphrase[d] the Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s own words in Rogers,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 406.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari to decide whether the causation instruction endorsed by the Seventh Circuit is proper in FELA cases. 562 U. S. ___ (2010). That instruction does not include the term \xe2\x80\x9cproximate cause,\xe2\x80\x9d but does tell the jury defendant\xe2\x80\x99s negligence must \xe2\x80\x9cpla[y] a part\xe2\x80\x94no matter how small\xe2\x80\x94in bringing about the [plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s] injury.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 31a.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The railroad business was exceptionally hazardous at the dawn of the twentieth century. As we have recounted, \xe2\x80\x9cthe physical dangers of railroading \xe2\x80\xa6 resulted in the death or maiming of thousands of workers every year,\xe2\x80\x9d Consolidated Rail Corporation v. Gottshall, 512 U. S. 532, 542 (1994), including 281,645 casualties in the year 1908 alone, S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 61\xe2\x80\x93432, p.\xc2\xa02 (1910). Enacted that same year in an effort to \xe2\x80\x9cshif[t] part of the human overhead of doing business from employees to their employers,\xe2\x80\x9d Gottshall, 512 U. S., at 542 (internal quotation marks omitted), FELA prescribes:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cEvery common carrier by railroad \xe2\x80\xa6 shall be liable in damages to any person suffering injury while he is employed by such carrier \xe2\x80\xa6 for such injury or death resulting in whole or in part from the negligence of any of the officers, agents, or employees of such carrier \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d 45 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa751 (emphasis added).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Liability under FELA is limited in these key respects: Railroads are liable only to their employees, and only for injuries sustained in the course of employment. FELA\xe2\x80\x99s language on causation, however, \xe2\x80\x9cis as broad as could be framed.\xe2\x80\x9d Urie v. Thompson, 337 U. S. 163, 181 (1949). Given the breadth of the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cresulting in whole or in part from the [railroad\xe2\x80\x99s] negligence,\xe2\x80\x9d and Congress\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9chumanitarian\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cremedial goal[s],\xe2\x80\x9d we have recognized that, in comparison to tort litigation at common law, \xe2\x80\x9ca relaxed standard of causation applies under FELA.\xe2\x80\x9d Gottshall, 512 U. S., at 542\xe2\x80\x93543. In our 1957 decision in Rogers, we described that relaxed standard as follows:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cUnder [FELA] the test of a jury case is simply whether the proofs justify with reason the conclusion that employer negligence played any part, even the slightest, in producing the injury or death for which damages are sought.\xe2\x80\x9d 352 U. S., at 506.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the Seventh Circuit emphasized, the instruction the District Court gave in this case, permitting a verdict for McBride if \xe2\x80\x9c[railroad] negligence played a part\xe2\x80\x94no matter how small\xe2\x80\x94in bringing about the injury,\xe2\x80\x9d tracked the language of Rogers. If Rogers prescribes the definition of causation applicable under FELA, that instruction was plainly proper. See Patterson v. McLean Credit Union, 491 U. S. 164, 172 (1989) (\xe2\x80\x9cConsiderations of stare decisis have special force in the area of statutory interpretation \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d). While CSX does not ask us to disturb Rogers, the railroad contends that lower courts have overread that opinion. In CSX\xe2\x80\x99s view, shared by the dissent, post, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310, Rogers was a narrowly focused decision that did not touch, concern, much less displace common-law formulations of \xe2\x80\x9cproximate cause.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Understanding this argument requires some background. The term \xe2\x80\x9cproximate cause\xe2\x80\x9d is shorthand for a concept: Injuries have countless causes, and not all should give rise to legal liability. See W. Keeton, D. Dobbs, \nR. Keeton, & D. Owen, Prosser and Keeton on Law of Torts \xc2\xa742, p. 273 (5th ed. 1984) (hereinafter Prosser and Keeton). \xe2\x80\x9cWhat we . . . mean by the word \xe2\x80\x98proximate,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d one noted jurist has explained, is simply this: \xe2\x80\x9c[B]ecause of convenience, of public policy, of a rough sense of justice, the law arbitrarily declines to trace a series of events beyond a certain point.\xe2\x80\x9d Palsgraf v. Long Island R.\xc2\xa0Co., 248 N.\xc2\xa0Y. 339, 352, 162 N.\xc2\xa0E. 99, 103 (1928) (Andrews, J., dissenting). Common-law \xe2\x80\x9cproximate cause\xe2\x80\x9d formulations varied, and were often both constricted and difficult to comprehend. See T. Cooley, Law of Torts 73\xe2\x80\x9377, 812\xe2\x80\x93813 (2d ed. 1888) (describing, for example, prescriptions precluding recovery in the event of any \xe2\x80\x9cintervening\xe2\x80\x9d cause or any contributory negligence). Some courts cut off liability if a \xe2\x80\x9cproximate cause\xe2\x80\x9d was not the sole proximate cause. Prosser and Keeton \xc2\xa765, p. 452 (noting \xe2\x80\x9ctendency \xe2\x80\xa6 to look for some single, principal, dominant, \xe2\x80\x98proximate\xe2\x80\x99 cause of every injury\xe2\x80\x9d). Many used definitions resembling those CSX proposed to the District Court or urged in the Court of Appeals. See supra, at 2\xe2\x80\x933 (CSX proposed key words \xe2\x80\x9cnatural or probable\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cdirect\xe2\x80\x9d to describe required relationship between injury and alleged negligent conduct); Prosser and Keeton \xc2\xa743, pp. 282\xe2\x80\x93283.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Drawing largely on Justice Souter\xe2\x80\x99s concurring opinion in Norfolk Southern R.\xc2\xa0Co. v. Sorrell, 549 U. S. 158, 173 (2007), CSX contends that the Rogers \xe2\x80\x9cany part\xe2\x80\x9d test displaced only common-law restrictions on recovery for injuries involving contributory negligence or other \xe2\x80\x9cmultiple causes.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 35 (internal quotation marks omitted).[Footnote 1] Rogers \xe2\x80\x9cdid not address the requisite directness of a cause,\xe2\x80\x9d CSX argues, hence that question continues to be governed by restrictive common-law formulations. Ibid.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To evaluate CSX\xe2\x80\x99s argument, we turn first to the facts of Rogers. The employee in that case was injured while burning off weeds and vegetation that lined the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s railroad tracks. A passing train had fanned the flames, which spread from the vegetation to the top of a culvert where the employee was standing. Attempting to escape, the employee slipped and fell on the sloping gravel covering the culvert, sustaining serious injuries. 352 U. S., at 501\xe2\x80\x93503. A Missouri state-court jury returned a verdict for the employee, but the Missouri Supreme Court reversed. Even if the railroad had been negligent in failing to maintain a flat surface, the court reasoned, the employee was at fault because of his lack of attention to the spreading fire. Rogers v. Thompson, 284 S.\xc2\xa0W. 2d \n467, 472 (Mo. 1955). As the fire \xe2\x80\x9cwas something extraordi-nary, unrelated to, and disconnected from the incline of the gravel,\xe2\x80\x9d the court felt \xe2\x80\x9cobliged to say [that] plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s injury was not the natural and probable consequence of any negligence of defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We held that the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict should not have been upset. Describing two potential readings of the Missouri Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, we condemned both. First, the court erred in concluding that the employee\xe2\x80\x99s negligence was the \xe2\x80\x9csole\xe2\x80\x9d cause of the injury, for the jury reasonably found that railroad negligence played a part. Rogers, 352 U. S., at 504\xe2\x80\x93505. Second, the court erred insofar as it held that the railroad\xe2\x80\x99s negligence was not a sufficient cause unless it was the more \xe2\x80\x9cprobable\xe2\x80\x9d cause of the injury. Id., at 505. FELA, we affirmed, did not incorporate any traditional common-law formulation of \xe2\x80\x9cproximate causation[,] which [requires] the jury [to] find that the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s negligence was the sole, efficient, producing cause of injury.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 506. Whether the railroad\xe2\x80\x99s negligent act was the \xe2\x80\x9cimmediate reason\xe2\x80\x9d for the fall, we added, was \xe2\x80\x9can irrelevant consideration.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 503. We then announced the \xe2\x80\x9cany part\xe2\x80\x9d test, id., at 506, and reiterated it several times. See, e.g., id., at 507 (\xe2\x80\x9cnarro[w]\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9csingle inquiry\xe2\x80\x9d is whether \xe2\x80\x9cnegligence of the employer played \nany part at all\xe2\x80\x9d in bringing about the injury); id., at 508 (FELA case \xe2\x80\x9crarely presents more than the single question whether negligence of the employer played any part, how-ever small, in the injury\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Rogers is most sensibly read as a comprehensive statement of the FELA causation standard. Notably, the Missouri Supreme Court in Rogers did not doubt that a FELA injury might have multiple causes, including railroad negligence and employee negligence. See 284 S.\xc2\xa0W. 2d, at 472 (reciting FELA\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cin whole or in part\xe2\x80\x9d language). But the railroad\xe2\x80\x99s part, according to the state court, was too indirect, not sufficiently \xe2\x80\x9cnatural and probable,\xe2\x80\x9d to establish the requisite causation. Ibid. That is the very reasoning the Court rejected in Rogers. It is also the reasoning CSX asks us to resurrect.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our understanding is informed by the statutory history and precedent on which Rogers drew. Before FELA was enacted, the \xe2\x80\x9charsh and technical\xe2\x80\x9d rules of state common law had \xe2\x80\x9cmade recovery difficult or even impossible\xe2\x80\x9d for in-jured railroad workers. Trainmen v. Virginia ex rel. Virginia State Bar, 377 U. S. 1, 3 (1964). \xe2\x80\x9c[D]issatisfied with the [railroad\xe2\x80\x99s] common-law duty,\xe2\x80\x9d Congress sought to \xe2\x80\x9csup-plan[t] that duty with [FELA\xe2\x80\x99s] far more drastic duty of paying damages for injury or death at work due in \nwhole or in part to the employer\xe2\x80\x99s negligence.\xe2\x80\x9d Rogers, 352 U. S., at 507. Yet, Rogers observed, the Missouri court and other lower courts continued to ignore FELA\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csignifican[t]\xe2\x80\x9d departures from the \xe2\x80\x9cordinary common-law negligence\xe2\x80\x9d scheme, to reinsert common-law formulations of causation involving \xe2\x80\x9cprobabilities,\xe2\x80\x9d and consequently to \xe2\x80\x9cdeprive litigants of their right to a jury determination.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 507, 509\xe2\x80\x93510. Aiming to end lower court disre-\ngard of congressional purpose, the Rogers Court repeatedly called the \xe2\x80\x9cany part\xe2\x80\x9d test the \xe2\x80\x9csingle\xe2\x80\x9d inquiry determining causation in FELA cases. Id., at 507, 508 (emphasis added). In short, CSX\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the Rogers standard concerns only division of responsibility among multiple actors, and not causation more generally, misses the thrust of our decision in that case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Tellingly, in announcing the \xe2\x80\x9cany part \xe2\x80\xa6 in producing the injury\xe2\x80\x9d test, Rogers cited Coray v. Southern Pacific Co., 335 U. S. 520 (1949), a decision emphasizing that FELA had parted from traditional common-law formulations of causation. What qualified as a \xe2\x80\x9cproximate\xe2\x80\x9d or legally sufficient cause in FELA cases, Coray had explained, was determined by the statutory phrase \xe2\x80\x9cresulting in whole or in part,\xe2\x80\x9d which Congress \xe2\x80\x9cselected \xe2\x80\xa6 to fix liability\xe2\x80\x9d in language that was \xe2\x80\x9csimple and direct.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 524. That straightforward phrase, Coray observed, was incompatible with \xe2\x80\x9cdialectical subtleties\xe2\x80\x9d that common-law courts employed to determine whether a particular cause was sufficiently \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial\xe2\x80\x9d to constitute a proximate cause. Id., at 523\xe2\x80\x93524.[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our subsequent decisions have confirmed that Rogers announced a general standard for causation in FELA cases, not one addressed exclusively to injuries involv-\ning multiple potentially cognizable causes. The very day Rogers was announced, we applied its \xe2\x80\x9cany part\xe2\x80\x9d instruction in a case in which the sole causation issue was the directness or foreseeability of the connection between the carrier\xe2\x80\x99s negligence and the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s injury. See Ferguson v. Moore-McCormack Lines, Inc., 352 U. S. 521, 523\xe2\x80\x93524 (1957) (plurality opinion).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A few years later, in Gallick v. Baltimore & Ohio R. Co., 372 U. S. 108 (1963), we held jury findings for the plaintiff proper in a case presenting the following facts: For years, the railroad had allowed a fetid pool, containing \xe2\x80\x9cdead and decayed rats and pigeons,\xe2\x80\x9d to accumulate near its right-of-way; while standing near the pool, the plaintiff-employee suffered an insect bite that became infected and required amputation of his legs. Id., at 109. The appellate court had concluded there was insufficient evidence of causation to warrant submission of the case to the jury. Id., at \n112. We reversed, reciting the causation standard Rogers announced. Id., at 116\xe2\x80\x93117, 120\xe2\x80\x93121. See also Crane v. Cedar Rapids & Iowa City R. Co., 395 U. S. 164, 166\xe2\x80\x93167 (1969) (contrasting suit by railroad employee, who \xe2\x80\x9cis not required to prove common-law proximate causation but only that his injury resulted \xe2\x80\x98in whole or in part\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0from the railroad\xe2\x80\x99s violation,\xe2\x80\x9d with suit by nonemployee, where \xe2\x80\x9cdefinition of causation \xe2\x80\xa6 [is] left to state law\xe2\x80\x9d); Gottshall, 512 U. S., at 543 (\xe2\x80\x9crelaxed standard of causation applies under FELA\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In reliance on Rogers, every Court of Appeals that reviews judgments in FELA cases has approved jury instructions on causation identical or substantively equivalent to the Seventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s instruction.[Footnote 5] Each appellate court has rejected common-law formulations of proximate cause of the kind CSX requested in this case. See supra, at 2\xe2\x80\x933. The current model federal instruction, recognizing that the \xe2\x80\x9cFELA causation standard is distinct from the usual proximate cause standard,\xe2\x80\x9d reads:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe fourth element [of a FELA action] is whether an injury to the plaintiff resulted in whole or part from the negligence of the railroad or its employees or agents. In other words, did such negligence play any part, even the slightest, in bringing about an injury to the plaintiff?\xe2\x80\x9d 5 L. Sand et\xc2\xa0al., Modern Federal Jury Instructions\xe2\x80\x93Civil \xc2\xb689.02, pp. 89\xe2\x80\x9338, 89\xe2\x80\x9340, and comment (2010) (hereinafter Sand).

\n

\nSince shortly after Rogers was decided, charges of this order have been accepted as the federal model. See W. Mathes & E. Devitt, Federal Jury Practice and Instructions \xc2\xa784.12, p. 517 (1965) (under FELA, injury \xe2\x80\x9cis proximately caused by\xe2\x80\x9d the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s negligence if the negligence \xe2\x80\x9cplayed any part, no matter how small, in bringing about or actually causing the injury\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 6] The overwhelming majority of state courts[Footnote 7] and scholars[Footnote 8] similarly comprehend FELA\xe2\x80\x99s causation standard.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, the understanding of Rogers we here affirm \xe2\x80\x9chas been accepted as settled law for several decades.\xe2\x80\x9d IBP, Inc. v. Alvarez, 546 U. S. 21, 32 (2005). \xe2\x80\x9cCongress has had [more than 50] years in which it could have corrected our decision in [Rogers] if it disagreed with it, and has not chosen to do so.\xe2\x80\x9d Hilton v. South Carolina Public Railways Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 502 U. S. 197, 202 (1991). Countless judges have instructed countless juries in language drawn from Rogers. To discard or restrict the Rogers instruction now would ill serve the goals of \xe2\x80\x9cstability\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cpredictability\xe2\x80\x9d that the doctrine of statutory stare decisis aims to ensure. Ibid.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0CSX nonetheless insists that proximate causation, as captured in the charge and definitions CSX requested, is a concept fundamental to actions sounding in negligence. The Rogers \xe2\x80\x9cany part\xe2\x80\x9d instruction opens the door to unlimited liability, CSX worries, inviting juries to impose liability on the basis of \xe2\x80\x9cbut for\xe2\x80\x9d causation. The dissent shares these fears. Post, at 5\xe2\x80\x936, 15\xe2\x80\x9316. But a half century\xe2\x80\x99s experience with Rogers gives us little cause for concern: CSX\xe2\x80\x99s briefs did not identify even one trial in which the instruction generated an absurd or untoward award.[Footnote 9] Nor has the dissent managed to uncover such a case. Post, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314 (citing no actual case but conjuring up images of falling pianos and spilled coffee).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While some courts have said that Rogers eliminated the concept of proximate cause in FELA cases,[Footnote 10] we think it \xe2\x80\x9cmore accurate \xe2\x80\xa6 to recognize that Rogers describes the test for proximate causation applicable in FELA suits.\xe2\x80\x9d Sorrell, 549 U. S., at 178 (Ginsburg, J., concurring in judgment). That understanding was expressed by the drafters of the 1965 federal model instructions, see supra, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312: Under FELA, injury \xe2\x80\x9cis proximately caused\xe2\x80\x9d by the railroad\xe2\x80\x99s negligence if that negligence \xe2\x80\x9cplayed any part \xe2\x80\xa6 in \xe2\x80\xa6 causing the injury.\xe2\x80\x9d Avoiding \xe2\x80\x9cdialectical subtleties\xe2\x80\x9d that confound attempts to convey intelligibly to juries just what \xe2\x80\x9cproximate cause\xe2\x80\x9d means, see Coray, 335 U. S., at 524, the Rogers instruction uses the everyday words contained in the statute itself. Jurors can comprehend those words and apply them in light of their ex-perience and common sense. Unless and until Congress orders otherwise, we see no good reason to tamper with an instruction tied to FELA\xe2\x80\x99s text, long employed by lower courts, and hardly shown to be unfair or unworkable.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As we have noted, see supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x936, the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cproximate cause\xe2\x80\x9d is shorthand for the policy-based judgment that not all factual causes contributing to an injury should be legally cognizable causes. Prosser and Keeton explain: \xe2\x80\x9cIn a philosophical sense, the consequences of an act go forward to eternity, and the causes of an event go back to the dawn of human events, and beyond.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa741, p. 264. To prevent \xe2\x80\x9cinfinite liability,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., courts and legislatures appropriately place limits on the chain of causation that may support recovery on any particular claim.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The term \xe2\x80\x9cproximate cause\xe2\x80\x9d itself is hardly essential to the imposition of such limits. It is a term notoriously confusing. See, e.g., Prosser and Keeton \xc2\xa742, p. 273 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe word \xe2\x80\x99proximate\xe2\x80\x99 is a legacy of Lord Chancellor Bacon, who in his time committed other sins\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. It is an unfortunate word, which places an entirely wrong emphasis upon the factor of physical or mechanical closeness. For this reason \xe2\x80\x98legal cause\xe2\x80\x99 or perhaps even \xe2\x80\x98responsible cause\xe2\x80\x99 would be a more appropriate term.\xe2\x80\x9d (footnotes omitted)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0And the lack of consensus on any one definition of \xe2\x80\x9cproximate cause\xe2\x80\x9d is manifest. Id., \xc2\xa741, p. 263. Common-law formulations include, inter alia, the \xe2\x80\x9cimmediate\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cnearest\xe2\x80\x9d antecedent test; the \xe2\x80\x9cefficient, producing cause\xe2\x80\x9d test; the \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial factor\xe2\x80\x9d test; and the \xe2\x80\x9cprobable,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cnatural and probable,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cforeseeable\xe2\x80\x9d consequence test. Smith, Legal Cause in Actions of Tort, 25 Harv. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 103, 106\xe2\x80\x93121 (1911); Smith, Legal Cause in Actions of Tort (Concluded), 25 Harv. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 303, 311 (1912).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Notably, CSX itself did not settle on a uniform definition of the term \xe2\x80\x9cproximate cause\xe2\x80\x9d in this litigation, nor does the dissent. In the District Court, CSX requested a jury instruction defining \xe2\x80\x9cproximate cause\xe2\x80\x9d to mean \xe2\x80\x9cany cause which, in natural or probable sequence, produced the injury complained of.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 32a. On appeal, \xe2\x80\x9cCSX maintain[ed] that the correct definition \xe2\x80\xa6 is a \xe2\x80\x98direct relation between the injury asserted and the injurious conduct alleged.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 598 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 393, n.\xc2\xa03. Before this Court, CSX called for \xe2\x80\x9ca demonstration that the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s injury resulted from the wrongful conduct in a way that was natural, probable, and foreseeable.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 9\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Lay triers, studies show, are scarcely aided by charges so phrased. See Steele & Thornburg, Jury Instructions: A Persistent Failure to Communicate, 67 N.\xc2\xa0C. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 77, 88\xe2\x80\x9392, 110 (1988) (85% of actual and potential jurors were unable to understand a pattern proximate cause instruction similar to the one requested by CSX); Charrow & Charrow, Making Legal Language Understandable: A Psycholinguistic Study of Jury Instructions, 79 Colum. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1306, 1353 (1979) (nearly one quarter of subjects misunderstood proximate cause to mean \xe2\x80\x9capproximate cause\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cestimated cause\xe2\x80\x9d). In light of the potential of \xe2\x80\x9cproximate cause\xe2\x80\x9d instructions to leave jurors at sea, it is not surprising that the drafters of the Restatement (Third) of Torts avoided the term altogether. See 1 Restatement (Third) of Torts: Liability for Physical and Emotional Harm \xc2\xa729 (2005) (confining liability to \xe2\x80\x9charms that result from the risks that made the actor\xe2\x80\x99s conduct tortious\xe2\x80\x9d); id., Comment b.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress, it is true, has written the words \xe2\x80\x9cproximate cause\xe2\x80\x9d into a number of statutes.[Footnote 11] But when the legislative text uses less legalistic language, e.g., \xe2\x80\x9ccaused by,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9coccasioned by,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cin consequence of,\xe2\x80\x9d or, as in FELA, \n\xe2\x80\x9cresulting in whole or in part from,\xe2\x80\x9d and the legislative purpose is to loosen constraints on recovery, there is \nlittle reason for courts to hark back to stock, judge-made proximate-cause formulations. See Smith, Legal Cause in Actions of Tort (Continued), 25 Harv. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 223, 235 (1912).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0FELA\xe2\x80\x99s language is straightforward: railroads are made answerable in damages for an employee\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cinjury or death resulting in whole or in part from [carrier] negligence.\xe2\x80\x9d 45 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa751. The argument for importing into FELA\xe2\x80\x99s text \xe2\x80\x9cprevious judicial definitions or dicta\xe2\x80\x9d originating in nonstatutory common-law actions, see Smith, Legal Cause in Actions of Tort (Continued), supra, at 235, misapprehends how foreseeability figures in FELA cases.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c[R]easonable foreseeability of harm,\xe2\x80\x9d we clarified in Gallick, is indeed \xe2\x80\x9can essential ingredient of [FELA] negligence.\xe2\x80\x9d 372 U. S., at 117 (emphasis added). The jury, therefore, must be asked, initially: Did the carrier \xe2\x80\x9cfai[l] to observe that degree of care which people of ordinary prudence and sagacity would use under the same or similar circumstances[?]\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 118. In that regard, the jury may be told that \xe2\x80\x9c[the railroad\xe2\x80\x99s] duties are measured by what is reasonably foreseeable under like circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted). Thus, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f a per-son has no reasonable ground to anticipate that a par-ticular condition \xe2\x80\xa6 would or might result in a mishap and injury, then the party is not required to do anything to correct [the] condition.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 118, n.\xc2\xa07 (internal quotation marks omitted).[Footnote 12] If negligence is proved, however, and is shown to have \xe2\x80\x9cplayed any part, even the slightest, in producing the injury,\xe2\x80\x9d Rogers, 352 U. S., at 506 (emphasis added),[Footnote 13] then the carrier is answerable in damages even if \xe2\x80\x9cthe extent of the [injury] or the manner in which it occurred\xe2\x80\x9d was not \xe2\x80\x9c[p]robable\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cforeseeable.\xe2\x80\x9d Gallick, 372 U. S., at 120\xe2\x80\x93121, and n.\xc2\xa08 (internal quotation marks omitted); see 4 F. Harper, F. James, & O. Gray, Law of Torts \xc2\xa720.5(6), p. 203 (3d ed. 2007); 5 Sand 89\xe2\x80\x9321.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Properly instructed on negligence and causation, and told, as is standard practice in FELA cases, to use their \xe2\x80\x9ccommon sense\xe2\x80\x9d in reviewing the evidence, see Tr. 205 (Aug. 19, 2008), juries would have no warrant to award damages in far out \xe2\x80\x9cbut for\xe2\x80\x9d scenarios. Indeed, judges would have no warrant to submit such cases to the jury. See Nicholson v. Erie R. Co., 253 F.\xc2\xa02d 939, 940\xe2\x80\x93941 (CA2 1958) (alleged negligence was failure to provide lavatory for female employee; employee was injured by a suitcase while looking for a lavatory in a passenger car; applying Rogers, appellate court affirmed lower court\xe2\x80\x99s dismissal for lack of causation); Moody v. Boston and Maine Corp., 921 F.\xc2\xa02d 1, 2\xe2\x80\x935 (CA1 1990) (employee suffered stress-related heart attack after railroad forced him to work more than 12 hours with inadequate breaks; applying Rogers, appellate court affirmed grant of summary judgment for lack of causation). See also supra, at 13 (Rogers has generated no extravagant jury awards or appellate court decisions).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In addition to the constraints of common sense, FELA\xe2\x80\x99s limitations on who may sue, and for what, reduce the risk of exorbitant liability. As earlier noted, see supra, at 4, the statute confines the universe of compensable injuries to those sustained by employees, during employment. \xc2\xa751. Hence there are no unforeseeable plaintiffs in FELA cases. And the statute weeds out the injuries most likely to bear only a tenuous relationship to railroad negligence, namely, those occurring outside the workplace.[Footnote 14]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is a real risk, on the other hand, that the \xe2\x80\x9cin natural or probable sequence\xe2\x80\x9d charge sought by CSX would mislead. If taken to mean the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s injury must probably (\xe2\x80\x9cmore likely than not\xe2\x80\x9d) follow from the railroad\xe2\x80\x99s negligent conduct, then the force of FELA\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cresulting in whole or in part\xe2\x80\x9d language would be blunted. Railroad negligence would \xe2\x80\x9cprobably\xe2\x80\x9d cause a worker\xe2\x80\x99s in-jury only if that negligence was a dominant contributor to the injury, not merely a contributor in any part.

\n

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For the reasons stated, it is not error in a FELA case to refuse a charge embracing stock proximate cause terminology. Juries in such cases are properly instructed that a defendant railroad \xe2\x80\x9ccaused or contributed to\xe2\x80\x9d a railroad worker\xe2\x80\x99s injury \xe2\x80\x9cif [the railroad\'s] negligence played a part\xe2\x80\x94no matter how small\xe2\x80\x94in bringing about the injury.\xe2\x80\x9d That, indeed, is the test Congress prescribed for proximate causation in FELA cases. See supra, at 9, 13. As the courts below so held, the judgment of the U. S. Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit is

S., at 508, as synonymous with \xe2\x80\x9cnegligence played any part, even the slightest,\xe2\x80\x9d see id., at 506, and the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cin producing the injury\xe2\x80\x9d as synonymous with the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cin bringing about the injury.\xe2\x80\x9d We therefore approve both the Seventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s instruction and the \xe2\x80\x9cany part, even the slightest, in producing the injury\xe2\x80\x9d formulation. The host of definitions of proximate cause, in contrast, are hardly synonymous.

\nFootnote 4

\xc2\xa0CSX and the dissent observe, correctly, that some of our pre-Rogers decisions invoked common-law formulations of proximate cause. See, e.g., Brady v. Southern R. Co., 320 U. S. 476, 483 (1943) (injury must \nbe \xe2\x80\x9cthe natural and probable consequence of the negligence\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). Indeed, the \xe2\x80\x9cnatural or probable\xe2\x80\x9d charge that CSX requested was drawn from Brady, which in turn relied on a pre-FELA case, Milwaukee & St. Paul R. Co. v. Kellogg, 94 U. \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Chief Justice Roberts, with whom Justice Scalia, Justice Kennedy, and Justice Alito join, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cIt is a well established principle of [the common] law, that in all cases of loss we are to attribute it to the proximate cause, and not to any remote cause: causa proxima non remota spectatur.\xe2\x80\x9d Waters v. Merchants\xe2\x80\x99 Louisville Ins. Co., 11 Pet. 213, 223 (1837) (Story, J.). The Court today holds that this principle does not apply to actions under the Federal Employers\xe2\x80\x99 Liability Act (FELA), and that those suing under that statute may recover for injuries that were not proximately caused by the negligence\nof their employers. This even though we have held that FELA generally follows the common law, unless the Act expressly provides otherwise; even though FELA expressly abrogated common law rules in four other respects, but said nothing about proximate cause; and even though our own cases, for 50 years after the passage of FELA, repeatedly recognized that proximate cause was required for recovery under that statute.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court is wrong to dispense with that familiar element of an action seeking recovery for negligence, an ele-ment \xe2\x80\x9cgenerally thought to be a necessary limitation on liability,\xe2\x80\x9d Exxon Co., U. S. A. v. Sofec, Inc., 517 U. S. 830, 838 (1996). The test the Court would substitute\xe2\x80\x94whether negligence played any part, even the slightest, in producing the injury\xe2\x80\x94is no limit at all. It is simply \xe2\x80\x9cbut for\xe2\x80\x9d causation. Nothing in FELA itself, or our decision in Rogers v. Missouri Pacific R. Co., 352 U. S. 500 (1957), supports such a boundless theory of liability.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I respectfully dissent.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cUnlike a typical workers\xe2\x80\x99 compensation scheme, which provides relief without regard to fault, \xe2\x80\xa6 FELA provides a statutory cause of action sounding in negligence.\xe2\x80\x9d Norfolk Southern R.\xc2\xa0Co. v. Sorrell, 549 U. S. 158, 165 (2007). When Congress creates such a federal tort, \xe2\x80\x9cwe start from the premise\xe2\x80\x9d that Congress \xe2\x80\x9cadopts the background of general tort law.\xe2\x80\x9d Staub v. Proctor Hospital, 562 U. S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 5). With respect to FELA in particular, we have explained that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]bsent express language to the contrary, the elements of a FELA claim are determined by reference to the common law.\xe2\x80\x9d Sorrell, supra, at 165\xe2\x80\x93166; see Urie v. Thompson, 337 U. S. 163, 182 (1949).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Recovery for negligence has always required a showing of proximate cause. \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98In a philosophical sense, the con-sequences of an act go forward to eternity.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Holmes v. Securities Investor Protection Corporation, 503 U. S. 258, 266, n. 10 (1992) (quoting W. Keeton, D. Dobbs, R. Keeton, & D. Owen, Prosser and Keeton on Law of Torts \xc2\xa741, p. 264 (5th ed. 1984)). Law, however, is not philosophy, and the concept of proximate cause developed at common law in response to the perceived need to distinguish \xe2\x80\x9cbut for\xe2\x80\x9d cause from those more direct causes of injury that can form the basis for liability at law.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The plurality breaks no new ground in criticizing the variety of formulations of the concept of proximate cause, ante, at 14\xe2\x80\x9315; courts, commentators, and first-year law students have been doing that for generations. See Exxon, supra, at 838. But it is often easier to disparage the product of centuries of common law than to devise a plausible substitute\xe2\x80\x94which may explain why Congress did not at-tempt to do so in FELA. Proximate cause is hardly the only enduring common law concept that is useful despite its imprecision, see ante, at 14. It is in good company with proof beyond a reasonable doubt, necessity, willfulness, and unconscionability\xe2\x80\x94to name just a few.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Proximate cause refers to the basic requirement that before recovery is allowed in tort, there must be \xe2\x80\x9csome direct relation between the injury asserted and the injurious conduct alleged,\xe2\x80\x9d Holmes, 503 U. S., at 268. It excludes from the scope of liability injuries that are \xe2\x80\x9ctoo remote,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cpurely contingent,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cindirect[\xc2\xa0].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 268, 271, 274. Recognizing that liability must not attach to \xe2\x80\x9cevery conceivable harm that can be traced to alleged wrongdoing,\xe2\x80\x9d proximate cause requires a \xe2\x80\x9ccausal connection between the wrong and the injury,\xe2\x80\x9d Associated Gen. Contractors of Cal., Inc. v. Carpenters, 459 U. S. 519, 536, 533, n. 26 (1983), that is not so \xe2\x80\x9ctenuous \xe2\x80\xa6 that what is claimed to be consequence is only fortuity,\xe2\x80\x9d Exxon, supra, at 838 (internal quotation marks omitted). It limits liability at some point before the want of a nail leads to loss of the kingdom. When FELA was passed, as now, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he question whether damage in a given case is proximate or remote [was] one of great importance\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. [T]he determination of it determines legal right,\xe2\x80\x9d 1 T. Street, Foundations of Legal Liability 110 (1906) (reprint 1980).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0FELA expressly abrogated common law tort principles in four specific ways. See Sorrell, supra, at 166, 168; Con-solidated Rail Corporation v. Gottshall, 512 U. S. 532, 542\xe2\x80\x93543 (1994). As enacted in 1908, the Act abolished the common law contributory negligence rule, which barred plaintiffs whose negligence had contributed to their injuries from recovering for the negligence of another. See Act of Apr. 22, \xc2\xa73, 35 Stat. 66. FELA also abandoned the so-called fellow-servant rule, \xc2\xa71, prohibited an assumption of risk defense in certain cases, \xc2\xa74, and barred employees from contractually releasing their employers from liability, \xc2\xa75.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But \xe2\x80\x9c[o]nly to the extent of these explicit statutory alterations is FELA an avowed departure from the rules of the common law.\xe2\x80\x9d Gottshall, supra, at 544 (internal quotation marks omitted). FELA did not abolish the familiar requirement of proximate cause. Because \xe2\x80\x9cCongress ex-pressly dispensed with [certain] common-law doctrines\xe2\x80\x9d\nin FELA but \xe2\x80\x9cdid not deal at all with [other] equally well-established doctrine[s],\xe2\x80\x9d I do not believe that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress intended to abrogate [the other] doctrine[s] sub silentio.\xe2\x80\x9d Monessen Southwestern R. Co. v. Morgan, 486 U. S. 330, 337\xe2\x80\x93338 (1988).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have applied the standard requirement of proximate cause to actions under federal statutes where the text did not expressly provide for it. See Dura Pharmaceuticals, Inc. v. Broudo, 544 U. S. 336, 342\xe2\x80\x93346 (2005) (securities fraud); Holmes, supra, at 268\xe2\x80\x93270 (Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act); Associated Gen. Contractors of Cal., Inc., supra, at 529\xe2\x80\x93535 (Clayton Act); cf. Metropolitan Edison Co. v. People Against Nuclear Energy, 460 U. S. 766, 774 (1983) (\xe2\x80\x9cthe terms \xe2\x80\x98environmental ef-fect\xe2\x80\x99 and \xe2\x80\x98environmental impact\xe2\x80\x99 in [the National Environmental Policy Act of 1969 should] be read to include a requirement of a reasonably close causal relationship between a change in the physical environment and the effect at issue \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. like the familiar doctrine of proximate cause from tort law\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court does not explicitly rest its argument on its own reading of FELA\xe2\x80\x99s text. The jury instruction on causation it approves, however, derives from Section 1 of FELA, 45 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa751. See ante, at 1, 16\xe2\x80\x9317. But nothing in Section 1 is similar to the \xe2\x80\x9cexpress language\xe2\x80\x9d Congress employed elsewhere in FELA when it wanted to abrogate a common law rule, Sorrell, supra, at 165\xe2\x80\x93166. See, e.g., \xc2\xa753 (\xe2\x80\x9cthe fact that the employee may have been guilty of contributory negligence shall not bar a recovery\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa754 (\xe2\x80\x9cemployee shall not be held to have assumed the risks of his employment\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the very first section of the statute, Section 1 simply outlines who could be sued by whom and for what types\nof injuries. It provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]very common carrier by railroad \xe2\x80\xa6 shall be liable in damages to any person suffering injury while he is employed by such carrier \xe2\x80\xa6 for such injury or death resulting in whole or in part from the negligence of any of the officers, agents, or employees of such carrier.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa751. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s theory seems to be that the words \xe2\x80\x9cin whole or in part\xe2\x80\x9d signal a departure from the historic requirement of proximate cause. But those words served a very different purpose. They did indeed mark an important departure from a common law principle, but it was the principle of contributory negligence\xe2\x80\x94not proximate cause.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As noted, FELA abolished the defense of contributory negligence; the \xe2\x80\x9cin whole or in part\xe2\x80\x9d language simply re-flected the fact that the railroad would remain liable\neven if its negligence was not the sole cause of injury. See Sorrell, 549 U. S., at 170. The Congress that was so clear when it was abolishing common law limits on recovery elsewhere in FELA did not abrogate the fundamental principle of proximate cause in the oblique manner the Court suggests. \xe2\x80\x9c[I]f Congress had intended such a sea change\xe2\x80\x9d in negligence principles \xe2\x80\x9cit would have said so clearly.\xe2\x80\x9d Board of Trustees of Leland Stanford Junior Univ. v. Roche Molecular Systems, Inc., 563 U. S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 14).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The language the Court adopts as an instruction on causation requires only that negligence have \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98played any part, even the slightest, in producing the injury.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 17 (quoting Rogers, 352 U. S., at 506; emphasis deleted); see also ante, at 18\xe2\x80\x9319 (\xe2\x80\x9cJuries in such cases are properly instructed that a defendant railroad \xe2\x80\x98caused or contributed to\xe2\x80\x99 a railroad worker\xe2\x80\x99s injury \xe2\x80\x98if [the railroad\xe2\x80\x99s] negligence played a part\xe2\x80\x94no matter how small\xe2\x80\x94in bringing about the injury\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). If that is proved, \xe2\x80\x9cthen the carrier is answerable in damages even if the extent of the [injury] or the manner in which it occurred was not \xe2\x80\x98[p]robable\xe2\x80\x99 or \xe2\x80\x98foreseeable.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 17 (some internal quotation marks omitted). There is nothing in that language that requires anything other than \xe2\x80\x9cbut for\xe2\x80\x9d cause. The terms \xe2\x80\x9ceven the slightest\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cno matter how small\xe2\x80\x9d make clear to juries that even the faintest whisper of \xe2\x80\x9cbut for\xe2\x80\x9d causation will do.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At oral argument, counsel for McBride explained that the correct standard for recovery under FELA is \xe2\x80\x9cbut-for plus a relaxed form of legal cause.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 44. There is no \xe2\x80\x9cplus\xe2\x80\x9d in the rule the Court announces today. In this very case defense counsel was free to argue \xe2\x80\x9cbut for\xe2\x80\x9d cause pure and simple to the jury. In closing, counsel informed the jury: \xe2\x80\x9cWhat we also have to show is defendant\xe2\x80\x99s negligence caused or contributed to [McBride\xe2\x80\x99s] injury. It never would have happened but for [CSX] giving him that train.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 67a (emphasis added).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At certain points in its opinion, the Court acknowledges that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]njuries have countless causes,\xe2\x80\x9d not all of which \xe2\x80\x9cshould give rise to legal liability.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 5. But the causation test the Court embraces contains no limit on causation at all.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court, from the time of FELA\xe2\x80\x99s enactment, understood FELA to require plaintiffs to prove that an employer\xe2\x80\x99s negligence \xe2\x80\x9cis a proximate cause of the accident,\xe2\x80\x9d Davis v. Wolfe, 263 U. S. 239, 243 (1923). See, e.g., ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9cThe rule clearly deducible from [prior] cases is that \xe2\x80\xa6 an employee cannot recover \xe2\x80\xa6 if the [employer\xe2\x80\x99s] failure \xe2\x80\xa6 is not a proximate cause of the accident \xe2\x80\xa6 but merely creates an incidental condition or situation in which the accident, otherwise caused, results in such injury\xe2\x80\x9d); Carter v. Atlanta & St. Andrews Bay R. Co., 338 U. S. 430, 435 (1949) (\xe2\x80\x9cif the jury determines that the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s breach is a contributory proximate cause of injury, it may find for the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)); O\xe2\x80\x99Donnell v. Elgin, J. & E. R. Co., 338 U. S. 384, 394 (1949) (\xe2\x80\x9cplaintiff was entitled to a[n] \xe2\x80\xa6 instruction \xe2\x80\xa6 which rendered defendant liable for injuries proximately resulting therefrom\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A comprehensive treatise written shortly after Congress enacted FELA confirmed that \xe2\x80\x9cthe plaintiff must \xe2\x80\xa6 show that the alleged negligence was the proximate cause of\nthe damage\xe2\x80\x9d in order to recover. 1 M. Roberts, Federal Li-abilities of Carriers \xc2\xa7538, p. 942 (1918). As Justice Souter has explained, for the half century after the enactment\nof FELA, the Court \xe2\x80\x9cconsistently recognized and applied proximate cause as the proper standard in FELA suits.\xe2\x80\x9d Sorrell, supra, at 174 (concurring opinion).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0No matter. For the Court, time begins in 1957, with our opinion in Rogers v. Missouri Pacific R. Co., 352 U. S. 500.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That opinion, however, \xe2\x80\x9cleft this law where it was.\xe2\x80\x9d Sor-rell, supra, at 174 (Souter, J., concurring). A jury in that case awarded Rogers damages against his railroad employer, but the Supreme Court of Missouri reversed the jury verdict. As the Court explains today, we suggested in Rogers that there were \xe2\x80\x9ctwo potential readings\xe2\x80\x9d of the lower court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion and that both were wrong. Ante, at 7. In doing so, we clarified the consequences of FELA\xe2\x80\x99s elimination of the common law contributory negligence rule. We did not do what Congress chose not to do, and abrogate the rule of proximate cause.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, we rejected the idea \xe2\x80\x9cthat [Rogers\xe2\x80\x99s] conduct was the sole cause of his mishap.\xe2\x80\x9d 352 U. S., at 504 (emphasis added); contra, Rogers v. Thompson, 284 S. W. 2d 467, 472 (Mo. 1955) (while \xe2\x80\x9c[Rogers] was confronted by an emergency[,] \xe2\x80\xa6 it was an emergency brought about by himself\xe2\x80\x9d). There were, we explained, \xe2\x80\x9cprobative facts from which the jury could find that [the railroad] was or should have been aware of conditions which created a likelihood that [Rogers] \xe2\x80\xa6 would suffer just such an injury as he did.\xe2\x80\x9d 352 U. S., at 503. We noted that \xe2\x80\x9c[c]ommon experience teaches both that a passing train will fan the flames of a fire, and that a person suddenly enveloped in flames and smoke will instinctively react by retreating from the danger.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. In referring to this predictable sequence\nof events, we described\xe2\x80\x94in familiar terms\xe2\x80\x94sufficient evi-dence of proximate cause. We therefore held that the railroad\xe2\x80\x99s negligence could have been a cause of Rogers\xe2\x80\x99s injury regardless of whether \xe2\x80\x9cthe immediate reason\xe2\x80\x9d why Rogers slipped was the railroad\xe2\x80\x99s negligence in permitting gravel to remain on the surface or some other cause. Ibid. (emphasis added).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Rogers thereby clarified that, under a statute in which employer and employee could both be proximate causes of an injury, a railroad\xe2\x80\x99s negligence need not be the sole or last cause in order to be proximate. That is an application of proximate cause, not a repudiation of it. See Street 111 (\xe2\x80\x9ca cause may be sufficiently near in law to the damage to be considered its effective legal cause without by any means being the nearest or most proximate to the causes which contribute of the injury\xe2\x80\x9d); 1 D. Dobbs, Law of Torts \xc2\xa7180, p. 445 (2001).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We then considered a second interpretation. The Missouri Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion could alternatively be read as having held that Rogers\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cconduct was at least as probable a cause for his mishap as any negligence of the [railroad],\xe2\x80\x9d and that\xe2\x80\x94in those circumstances\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cthere was no case for the jury.\xe2\x80\x9d 352 U. S., at 505 (emphasis added). If this was the principle the court applied below, it was also wrong and for many of the same reasons.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under a comparative negligence scheme in which multiple causes may act concurrently, we clarified that a railroad\xe2\x80\x99s negligence need not be the \xe2\x80\x9csole, efficient, produc-\ning cause of injury,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 506. The question was simply whether \xe2\x80\x9cemployer negligence played any part, even the slightest, in producing the injury.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. \xe2\x80\x9cIt does not matter,\xe2\x80\x9d we continued, \xe2\x80\x9cthat, from the evidence, the jury may also with reason, on grounds of probability, attribute the result to other causes, including the employee\xe2\x80\x99s contributory negligence.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court today takes the \xe2\x80\x9cany part, even the slightest\xe2\x80\x9d language out of context and views it as a rejection of proximate cause. But Rogers was talking about contributory negligence\xe2\x80\x94it said so\xe2\x80\x94and the language it chose confirms just that. \xe2\x80\x9cSlight\xe2\x80\x9d negligence was familiar usage in this context. The statute immediately preceding FELA, passed just two years earlier in 1906, moved part way from contributory to comparative negligence. It provided that \xe2\x80\x9cthe fact that the employee may have been guilty of contributory negligence shall not bar a recovery where his contrib-utory negligence was slight and that of the employer\nwas gross in comparison.\xe2\x80\x9d Act of June 11, 1906, \xc2\xa72, 34 Stat. 232. Other statutes similarly made this halfway stop on the road from contributory to pure comparative negligence, again using the term \xe2\x80\x9cslight.\xe2\x80\x9d See Dobbs \xc2\xa7201, at 503 (\xe2\x80\x9cOne earlier [version of comparative fault] \xe2\x80\xa6 allowed the negligent plaintiff to recover if the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s negligence was slight and the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s gross\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. Modern comparative negligence law works differently, reducing the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s recovery in proportion to the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s fault\xe2\x80\x9d); V. Schwartz, Comparative Negligence \xc2\xa72.01[b][2], p. 33 (5th ed. 2010) (a \xe2\x80\x9cmajor form of modified comparative negligence is the \xe2\x80\x98slight-gross\xe2\x80\x99 system\xe2\x80\x9d); id. \xc2\xa73.04[b], at\n75. In 1908, FELA completed the transition to pure comparative negligence with respect to rail workers. See Dobbs \xc2\xa7201, at 503. Under FELA, it does not matter whose negligence was \xe2\x80\x9cslight\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cgross.\xe2\x80\x9d The use of the term \xe2\x80\x9ceven the slightest\xe2\x80\x9d in Rogers makes perfect sense when the decision is understood to be about multiple causes\xe2\x80\x94not about how direct any particular cause must be. See Sorrell, 549 U. S., at 175 (Souter, J., concurring) (pertinent language concerned \xe2\x80\x9cmultiplicity of causations,\xe2\x80\x9d not \xe2\x80\x9cthe necessary directness of \xe2\x80\xa6 causation\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court views Rogers as \xe2\x80\x9cdescrib[ing] the test for proximate causation\xe2\x80\x9d under FELA, ante, at 13 (internal quotation marks omitted), but Rogers itself says nothing of the sort. See 352 U. S., at 506 (describing its test as \xe2\x80\x9cthe test of a jury case\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). Rogers did not set forth a novel standard for proximate cause\xe2\x80\x94much less an instruction designed to guide jurors in determining causation. Indeed, the trial court in Rogers used the term \xe2\x80\x9cproximate cause\xe2\x80\x9d in its jury instruction and directed the jury to find that Rogers could not recover if his injuries \xe2\x80\x9cwere not directly \xe2\x80\xa6 caused by\xe2\x80\x9d the railroad\xe2\x80\x99s negligence. Id., at 505, n.\xc2\xa09 (internal quotation marks omitted). Our opinion quoted that instruction, ibid., but \xe2\x80\x9ctook no issue with [it] in this respect,\xe2\x80\x9d Sorrell, supra, at 176 (Souter, J., concurring).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A few of our cases have characterized Rogers as hold-\ning that \xe2\x80\x9ca relaxed standard of causation applies under FELA.\xe2\x80\x9d Gottshall, 512 U. S., at 543; see Crane v. Cedar Rapids & Iowa City R. Co., 395 U. S. 164, 166 (1969). Fair enough; but these passing summations of Rogers do not alter its holding. FELA did, of course, change common law rules relating to causation in one respect: Under FELA, a railroad\xe2\x80\x99s negligence did not have to be the exclusive cause of an injury. See Gottshall, supra, at 542\xe2\x80\x93543 (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress did away with several common-law tort defenses \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. Specifically, the statute \xe2\x80\xa6 rejected the doctrine of contributory negligence in favor of that of comparative negligence\xe2\x80\x9d). And, unlike under FELA\xe2\x80\x99s predecessor, the proportionate degree of the employee\xe2\x80\x99s negligence would not necessarily bar his recovery. But we have never held\xe2\x80\x94until today\xe2\x80\x94that FELA entirely eliminates proximate cause as a limit on liability.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court is correct that the federal courts of appeals have read Rogers to support the adoption of instructions like the one given here. But we do not resolve questions such as the one before us by a show of hands. See Buckhannon Board & Care Home, Inc. v. West Virginia Dept. of Health and Human Resources, 532 U. S. 598, 605 (2001); id., at 621 (Scalia, J., concurring) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe dissent\xe2\x80\x99s insistence that we defer to the \xe2\x80\x98clear majority\xe2\x80\x99 of Circuit opinion is particularly peculiar in the present case, since that majority has been nurtured and preserved by our own misleading dicta\xe2\x80\x9d); cf. McNally v. United States, 483 U. S. 350, 365 (1987) (Stevens, J., dissenting) (pointing out that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]very court to consider the matter\xe2\x80\x9d had disagreed with the majority\xe2\x80\x99s holding).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In addition, the Court discounts the views of those state courts of last resort that agree FELA did not relegate proximate cause to the dustbin. Those courts either reject the position the Court adopts today or suggest that FELA does not entirely eliminate proximate cause. See Ballard v. Union Pacific R.\xc2\xa0Co., 279 Neb. 638, 644, 781 N.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa02d 47, 53 (2010) (\xe2\x80\x9can employee must prove the employer\xe2\x80\x99s negligence and that the alleged negligence is a proximate cause of the employee\xe2\x80\x99s injury\xe2\x80\x9d); CSX Transp., Inc. v. Miller, 46 So.\xc2\xa03d 434, 450 (Ala. 2010) (\xe2\x80\x9cthe jury in this case was properly instructed by the trial court that [respondent] could not be compensated for any injury not proximately caused by [petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s] negligence\xe2\x80\x9d), cf. id., at 461 (quoting Rogers); Raab v. Utah R.\xc2\xa0Co., 2009 UT 61, \xc2\xb620, 221 P.\xc2\xa03d 219, 225 (\xe2\x80\x9cRogers did not speak to the issue of proximate cause\xe2\x80\x9d); Gardner v. CSX Transp., Inc., 201 W.\xc2\xa0Va. 490, 500, 498 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 473, 483 (1997) (\xe2\x80\x9cwe hold that to prevail on a claim under [FELA] \xe2\x80\xa6 a plaintiff employee must establish that the defendant employer acted negligently and that such negligence contributed proximately, in whole or in part, to plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s injury\xe2\x80\x9d); Snipes v. Chicago, Central, & Pacific R.\xc2\xa0Co., 484 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 162, 164\xe2\x80\x93165 (Iowa 1992) (stating that \xe2\x80\x9c[r]ecovery under the FELA requires an injured employee to prove that the defendant employer was negligent and that the negligence proximately caused, in whole or in part, the accident,\xe2\x80\x9d while noting that Rogers\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cthreshold for recovery\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9clow\xe2\x80\x9d); Marazzato v. Burlington No.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0Co., 249 Mont. 487, 491, 817 P.\xc2\xa02d 672, 675 (1991) (\xe2\x80\x9cplaintiff has the burden of proving that defendant\xe2\x80\x99s negligence was the proximate cause in whole or in part of the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s [death]\xe2\x80\x9d); Reed v. Pennsylvania R.\xc2\xa0Co., 171 Ohio St. 433, 436, 171 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 718, 721\xe2\x80\x93722 (1961) (\xe2\x80\x9csuch violation could not legally amount to a proximate cause of the injury to plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s leg\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Hager v. Norfolk & W. R.\xc2\xa0Co., No. 87553, 2006 WL 3634373, *6 (Ohio App., Dec. 14, 2006) (\xe2\x80\x9cthe standard for proximate cause is broader under FELA than the common law\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If nothing more, the views of these courts show that the question whether\xe2\x80\x94and to what extent\xe2\x80\x94FELA dispenses with proximate cause is not as \xe2\x80\x9csettled\xe2\x80\x9d as the Court would have it, ante, at 12 (internal quotation marks omitted). Under these circumstances, it seems important to correct an interpretation of our own case law that has run, so to speak, off its own rails.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even the Court seems to appreciate that it is creating a troubling gap in the FELA negligence action and ought to do something to patch it over. The something it proposes is \xe2\x80\x9c[r]easonable foreseeability of harm,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 16 (internal quotation marks omitted). Foreseeability as a test for proximate causation would be one thing; foreseeability has, after all, long been an aspect of proximate cause. But that is not the test the Court prescribes. It instead limits the foreseeability inquiry to whether the defendant was negligent in the first place.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court observes that juries may be instructed that a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s negligence depends on \xe2\x80\x9cwhat a reasonably prudent person would anticipate or foresee as creating a potential for harm.\xe2\x80\x9d 5 L. Sand et\xc2\xa0al., Modern Federal Jury Instructions\xe2\x80\x93Civil \xc2\xb689.10, p. 89\xe2\x80\x9321 (2010); see ante, at 16\xe2\x80\x9317. That\xe2\x80\x99s all fine and good when a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s negligence results directly in the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s injury (nevermind that no \xe2\x80\x9creasonable foreseeability\xe2\x80\x9d instruction was given in this case). For instance, if I drop a piano from a window and it falls on a person, there is no question that I was negligent and could have foreseen that the piano would hit someone\xe2\x80\x94as, in fact, it did. The problem for the Court\xe2\x80\x99s test arises when the negligence does not directly produce the injury to the plaintiff: I drop a piano; it cracks the sidewalk; during sidewalk repairs weeks later a man barreling down the sidewalk on a bicycle hits a cone that repairmen have placed around their worksite, and is injured. Was\nI negligent in dropping the piano because I could have foreseen \xe2\x80\x9ca mishap and injury,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 17 (emphasis added; internal quotation marks omitted)? Yes. Did my negligence cause \xe2\x80\x9c[the] mishap and injury\xe2\x80\x9d that resulted? It depends on what is meant by cause. My negligence was a \xe2\x80\x9cbut for\xe2\x80\x9d cause of the injury: If I had not dropped the piano, the bicyclist would not have crashed. But is it a legal cause? No.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In one respect the Court\xe2\x80\x99s test is needlessly rigid. If courts must instruct juries on foreseeability as an aspect of negligence, why not instruct them on foreseeability as an aspect of causation? And if the jury is simply supposed to intuit that there should also be limits on the legal chain of causation\xe2\x80\x94and that \xe2\x80\x9cbut for\xe2\x80\x9d cause is not enough\xe2\x80\x94why hide the ball? Why not simply tell the jury? Finally, if\nthe Court intends \xe2\x80\x9cforeseeability of harm\xe2\x80\x9d to be a kind\nof poorman\xe2\x80\x99s proximate cause, then where does the Court find that requirement in the test Rogers\xe2\x80\x94or FELA\xe2\x80\x94pre-scribes? Could it be derived from the common law?

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Where does \xe2\x80\x9cforeseeability of harm\xe2\x80\x9d as the sole protection against limitless liability run out of steam? An answer would seem only fair to the common law.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A railroad negligently fails to maintain its boiler, which overheats. An employee becomes hot while repairing it and removes his jacket. When finished with the repairs, he grabs a thermos of coffee, which spills on his now-bare arm, burning it. Was the risk that someone would be harmed by the failure to maintain the boiler foreseeable? Was the risk that an employee would be burned while repairing the overheated boiler foreseeable? Can the railroad be liable under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s test for the coffee burn? According to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, it does not matter that the \xe2\x80\x9cmanner in which [the injury] occurred was not \xe2\x80\xa6 foreseeable,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 17 (internal quotation marks omitted), so long as some negligence\xe2\x80\x94any negligence at all\xe2\x80\x94can be established.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion fails to settle on a single test for answering these questions: Is it that the railroad\xe2\x80\x99s negligence \xe2\x80\x9cpla[y] a part\xe2\x80\x94no matter how small\xe2\x80\x94in bringing about the [plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s] injury,\xe2\x80\x9d as the Court indicates on pages 5, 17 note 13, and 19, or that \xe2\x80\x9cnegligence play any part, even the slightest, in producing the injury,\xe2\x80\x9d as suggested at pages 8 note 2, 11 note 4, and 17? The Court says there is no difference, see ante, at 9, n.\xc2\xa03, but I suspect lawyers litigating FELA cases will prefer one instruction over the other, depending on whether they represent the employer or the employee. In any event, if the Court\xe2\x80\x99s test\xe2\x80\x94whichever version\xe2\x80\x94provides answers to these hypo-theticals, the Court keeps them to itself.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Proximate cause supplies the vocabulary for answering such questions. It is useful to ask whether the injury that resulted was within the scope of the risk created by the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s negligent act; whether the injury was a natural or probable consequence of the negligence; whether there was a superseding or intervening cause; whether the negligence was anything more than an antecedent event without which the harm would not have occurred.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The cases do not provide a mechanical or uniform test and have been criticized for that. But they do \xe2\x80\x9cfurnish illustrations of situations which judicious men upon careful consideration have adjudged to be on one side of the line or the other.\xe2\x80\x9d Exxon, 517 U. S., at 839 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court forswears all these inquiries and\xe2\x80\x94with them\xe2\x80\x94an accumulated common law history that might provide guidance for courts and juries faced with causation questions. See ante, at 1 (FELA \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not incorporate \xe2\x80\x98proximate cause\xe2\x80\x99 standards developed in nonstatutory common-law tort actions\xe2\x80\x9d); ante, at 18 (\xe2\x80\x9cit is not error in a FELA case to refuse a charge embracing stock proximate cause terminology\xe2\x80\x9d). It is not necessary to accept every verbal formulation of proximate cause ever articulated to recognize that these standards provide useful guidance\xe2\x80\x94and that juries should receive some instruction\xe2\x80\x94on the type of link required between a railroad\xe2\x80\x99s negligence and an employee\xe2\x80\x99s injury.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Law has its limits. But no longer when it comes to the causal connection between negligence and a resulting injury covered by FELA. A new maxim has replaced the old: Caelum terminus est\xe2\x80\x94the sky\xe2\x80\x99s the limit.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I respectfully dissent.

\n

As the court below noted, \xe2\x80\x9c[s]ince Rogers, the Supreme Court has not explained in detail how broadly or narrowly Rogers should be read by the lower federal courts.\xe2\x80\x9d 598 F.\xc2\xa03d 388, 397 (CA7 2010).
