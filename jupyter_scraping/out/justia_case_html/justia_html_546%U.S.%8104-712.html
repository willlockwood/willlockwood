\nOCTOBER TERM, 2005
\nLINCOLN PROPERTY CO. V. ROCHE
\n

\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

LINCOLN PROPERTY CO. et\xc2\xa0al. v. ROCHE et ux.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fourth circuit

\n

No. 04\xe2\x80\x93712.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 11, 2005\xe2\x80\x94Decided November 29, 2005

\n

Title 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71441 authorizes the removal of civil actions from state court to federal court when the state-court action is one that could have been brought, originally, in federal court. When federal-court jurisdiction is predicated on the parties\xe2\x80\x99 diversity of citizenship, see \xc2\xa71332, removal is permissible \xe2\x80\x9conly if none of the parties in interest properly joined and served as defendants is a citizen of the State in which [the] action [was] brought.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71441(b).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Christophe and Juanita Roche, plaintiffs below, respondents here, leased an apartment in a Virginia complex, Westfield Village, managed by Lincoln Property Company (Lincoln). The Roches commenced suit in state court against diverse defendants, including Lincoln, asserting serious medical ailments from their exposure to toxic mold in their apartment, and alleging loss, theft, or destruction of personal property left in the care of Lincoln and the mold treatment firm during the remediation process. The Roches identified themselves as Virginia citizens and defendant Lincoln as a Texas corporation. Defendants removed the litigation to a Federal District Court, invoking that court\xe2\x80\x99s diversity-of-citizenship jurisdiction. In their consolidated federal-court complaint, the Roches identified themselves and Lincoln just as they did in their state-court complaints. Lincoln, in its answer, admitted that it managed Westfield Village, and did not seek to avoid liability by asserting that some other entity was responsible for managing the property. After discovery, the District Court granted defendants\xe2\x80\x99 motion for summary judgment, but before judgment was entered, the Roches moved to remand the case to state court. The District Court denied the motion, but the Fourth Circuit reversed, holding the removal improper on the ground that Lincoln failed to show the nonexistence of an affiliated Virginia entity that was a real party in interest.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Defendants may remove an action on the basis of diversity of citizenship if there is complete diversity between all named plaintiffs and all named defendants, and no defendant is a citizen of the forum State. It is not incumbent on the named defendants to negate the existence of a potential defendant whose presence in the action would destroy diversity. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The Fourth Circuit correctly identified Lincoln as a proper party, but erred in insisting that some other entity affiliated with Lincoln should have been joined as a codefendant, and that it was Lincoln\xe2\x80\x99s obligation to name that entity and show that its joinder would not destroy diversity. This Court stresses, first, that the existence of complete diversity between the Roches and Lincoln is plain and no longer subject to debate. The Court turns next to the reasons why the Fourth Circuit erred in determining that diversity jurisdiction was not proved by the removing parties. Since Strawbridge v. Curtiss, 3 Cranch 267, this Court has read the statutory formulation \xe2\x80\x9cbetween \xe2\x80\xa6 citizens of different States,\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71332(a)(1), to require complete diversity between all plaintiffs and all defendants. While \xc2\xa71332 allows plaintiffs to invoke diversity jurisdiction, \xc2\xa71441 gives defendants a corresponding opportunity. The scales are not evenly balanced, however. An in-state plaintiff may invoke diversity jurisdiction, but \xc2\xa71441(b) bars removal on the basis of diversity if any \xe2\x80\x9cpart[y] in interest properly joined and served as [a] defendan[t] is a citizen of the State in which [the] action is brought.\xe2\x80\x9d In this case, Virginia plaintiffs joined and served no Virginian as a party defendant. Hence, the action qualified for the removal defendants effected. Neither Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 17(a), captioned \xe2\x80\x9cReal Party in Interest,\xe2\x80\x9d nor Rule 19, captioned \xe2\x80\x9cJoinder of Persons Needed for Just Adjudication,\xe2\x80\x9d requires plaintiffs or defendants to name and join any additional parties to this action. Both Rules address party joinder, not federal-court subject-matter jurisdiction. The Fourth Circuit and the Roches draw from this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions a jurisdictional \xe2\x80\x9creal parties to the controversy\xe2\x80\x9d rule applicable in diversity cases to complaining and defending parties alike. But the Court is aware of no decision supporting the burden the Fourth Circuit placed on a properly joined defendant to negate the existence of a potential codefendant whose presence in the action would destroy diversity. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0This Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions employing \xe2\x80\x9creal party to the controversy\xe2\x80\x9d terminology bear scant resemblance to the Roches\xe2\x80\x99 action. No party here has been \xe2\x80\x9cimproperly or collusively\xe2\x80\x9d named solely to create federal jurisdiction, see, e.g., 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71359, Kramer v. Caribbean Mills, Inc., 394 U. S. 823, 830. Nor are cases in which actions against a state agency have been regarded as suits against the State itself, see State Highway Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of Wyo. v. Utah Constr. Co., 278 U. S. 194, 199\xe2\x80\x93200, relevant to suits between private parties. Unlike cases in which a party was named to satisfy state pleading rules, e.g., McNutt ex rel. Leggett, Smith, & Lawrence v. Bland, 2 How. 9, 14, or was joined only as designated performer of a ministerial act, e.g., Walden v. Skinner, 101 U. S. 577, 589, or otherwise had no control of, impact, or stake in the controversy, e.g., Wood v. Davis, 18 How. 467, 469\xe2\x80\x93470, Lincoln has a vital interest in this case. Indeed, Lincoln accepted responsibility, in the event the Roches prevailed on the merits, by admitting that it managed Westfield Village. In any event, the Fourth Circuit had no warrant in this case to inquire whether some other person might have been joined as an additional or substitute defendant. Congress, empowered to prescribe the jurisdiction of the federal courts, sometimes has specified that a named party\xe2\x80\x99s own citizenship does not determine its diverse status. But Congress has not directed that a corporation, for diversity purposes, shall be deemed to have acquired the citizenship of all or any of its affiliates. For cases like the Roches\xe2\x80\x99, Congress has provided simply and only that \xe2\x80\x9ca corporation shall be deemed to be a citizen of any State by which it has been incorporated and of the State where it has its principal place of business,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71332(c)(1). The jurisdictional rule governing here is unambiguous and not amenable to judicial enlargement. Under \xc2\xa71332(c)(1), Lincoln is a citizen of Texas alone, and under \xc2\xa71441(a) and (b), this case was properly removed. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

373 F.\xc2\xa03d 610, reversed and remanded.

\nOPINION OF THE COURT

\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 04-712

\n

LINCOLN PROPERTY COMPANY, et\xc2\xa0al., PETI-\nTIONERS v. CHRISTOPHE ROCHE et ux.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the fourth circuit

\n

[November 29, 2005]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case concerns 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71441, which authorizes the removal of civil actions from state court to federal court when the action initiated in state court is one that could have been brought, originally, in a federal district court. \xc2\xa71441(a). When federal-court jurisdiction is predicated on the parties\xe2\x80\x99 diversity of citizenship, see \xc2\xa71332, removal is permissible \xe2\x80\x9conly if none of the parties in interest properly joined and served as defendants is a citizen of the State in which [the] action [was] brought.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71441(b).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Christophe and Juanita Roche, plaintiffs below, respondents here, are citizens of Virginia. They commenced suit in state court against diverse defendants, including Lincoln Property Company (Lincoln), a corporation chartered and having its principal place of business in Texas. The defendants removed the litigation to a Federal District Court where, after discovery proceedings, they successfully moved for summary judgment. Holding the removal improper, the Court of Appeals instructed remand of the action to state court. 373 F.\xc2\xa03d 610, 620\xe2\x80\x93622 (CA4 2004). The appellate court so ruled on the ground that the Texas defendant failed to show the nonexistence of an affiliated Virginia entity that was the \xe2\x80\x9creal party in interest.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 622.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeals. Defendants may remove an action on the basis of diversity of citizenship if there is complete diversity between all named plaintiffs and all named defendants, and no defendant is a citizen of the forum State. It is not incumbent on the named defendants to negate the existence of a potential defendant whose presence in the action would destroy diversity.[Footnote 1]

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Christophe and Juanita Roche leased an apartment in the Westfield Village complex in Fairfax County, Virginia. About a year after moving in, they discovered evidence of toxic mold in their apartment. Expert inspection confirmed the presence of mold, which the inspection report linked to hair loss, headaches, irritation of the respiratory tract, fatigue, and dermatitis. App. 184\xe2\x80\x93190. The report stated that spores from toxigenic mold species were airborne in the apartment and had likely contaminated the carpeting and fabric surfaces throughout the dwelling. Id., at 188. The Roches moved out of their apartment for the remediation process, leaving their personal belongings in the care of Lincoln, the designated property manager of Westfield Village, and the mold treatment firm. 373 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 612.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Some months later, the Roches commenced suit, filing two substantially similar complaints in the Circuit Court for Fairfax County, Virginia. App. 27\xe2\x80\x9350, 53\xe2\x80\x9375. Both complaints asserted serious medical ailments from the Roches\xe2\x80\x99 year-long exposure to toxic mold, and sought damages under multiple headings, including negligence, breach of contract, actual fraud, constructive fraud, and violations of Virginia housing regulations. Id., at 38\xe2\x80\x9348, 64\xe2\x80\x9374. In addition, the Roches alleged loss, theft, or destruction of their personal property (including irreplaceable family keepsakes) during the remediation process. Regarding these losses, they sought damages for conversion and infliction of emotional distress. Id., at 49\xe2\x80\x9350, 74\xe2\x80\x9375.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In state court, the Roches\xe2\x80\x99 complaints named three defendants: Lincoln; INVESCO Institutional, an investment management group; and State of Wisconsin Investment Board, the alleged owner of Westfield Village. Id., at 26\xe2\x80\x9328, 52\xe2\x80\x9354. The complaints described Lincoln as \xe2\x80\x9ca developer and manager of residential communities, including \xe2\x80\xa6 Westfield Village.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 27, 53. \xe2\x80\x9c[A]cting by and through [its] agents,\xe2\x80\x9d the Roches alleged, Lincoln caused the personal injuries of which they complained. Id., at 30, 56.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Defendants timely removed the twin cases to the United States District Court for the Eastern District of Virginia, invoking that court\xe2\x80\x99s diversity-of-citizenship jurisdiction. See 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71332(a)(1), 1441(a). The notice of removal described Lincoln as a Texas corporation with its principal place of business in Texas, INVESCO as a Delaware corporation with its principal place of business in Georgia, and State of Wisconsin Investment Board as an independent agency of Wisconsin. App. 81. In their consolidated federal-court complaint, the Roches identified themselves as citizens of Virginia and Lincoln as a corporation headquartered in Texas, just as they did in their state-court complaints. Id., at 27, 53, 114\xe2\x80\x93115.[Footnote 2] Further, they stated affirmatively that the federal court \xe2\x80\x9chas jurisdiction of this matter.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 114. Lincoln, in its answer to the complaint, admitted that, through its regional offices, \xe2\x80\x9cit manages Westfield Village.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 137, 138. Lincoln did not seek to avoid liability by asserting that some other entity was responsible for managing the property.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In both their state- and federal-court complaints, the Roches stated that, \xe2\x80\x9c[u]pon further discovery in the case,\xe2\x80\x9d they would \xe2\x80\x9cdetermine if additional defendant or defendants will be named.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 28, 54, 116. Although they engaged in some discovery concerning Lincoln\xe2\x80\x99s affiliates, their efforts in this regard were not extensive, Tr. of Oral Arg. 9, 14, 38\xe2\x80\x9339, 48\xe2\x80\x9349, 53, and at no point did they seek to join any additional defendant.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After discovery, the parties cross-moved for summary judgment. The District Court granted defendants\xe2\x80\x99 motion and denied plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 motion, noting that it would set forth its reasons in a forthcoming memorandum order. App. to Pet. for Cert. 20a\xe2\x80\x9321a. The promised memorandum order issued a few months later, id., at 22a\xe2\x80\x9340a, and the District Court entered final judgment for the defendants the same day, id., at 41a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Six days after the District Court granted defendants\xe2\x80\x99 motion for summary judgment, but before final judgment was entered, the Roches moved to remand the case to the state court, alleging for the first time the absence of federal subject-matter jurisdiction.[Footnote 3] Specifically, the Roches alleged that Lincoln \xe2\x80\x9cis not a Texas Corporation, but a Partnership with one of its partners residing in the Commonwealth of Virginia.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 226.[Footnote 4] The District Court denied the remand motion, concluding that Lincoln is a Texas corporation and that removal was proper because the requisite complete diversity existed between all plaintiffs and all defendants. App. to Pet. for Cert. 84a\xe2\x80\x9393a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals for the Fourth Circuit reversed and instructed the District Court to remand the case to the state court. 373 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 622. Although recognizing that Lincoln is a Texas citizen and a proper party to the action, id., at 620\xe2\x80\x93621, the Court of Appeals observed that \xe2\x80\x9cLincoln operates under many different structures,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 617. Describing Lincoln as \xe2\x80\x9cthe nominal party and ultimate parent company,\xe2\x80\x9d the appellate court suspected that an unidentified \xe2\x80\x9cVirginia subsidiary, be it a partnership, corporation or otherwise, rather than the Texas parent\xe2\x80\x9d was \xe2\x80\x9cthe real and substantial party in interest.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 620\xe2\x80\x93621. Lincoln, the party invoking federal-court jurisdiction, had not demonstrated the nonexistence of \xe2\x80\x9cthe Virginia sub-\xe2\x80\x98partnership,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the Court of Appeals reasoned, id., at 621, and therefore had not met its burden of establishing diversity, id., at 621\xe2\x80\x93622.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari, 543 U. S. 1186 (2005), to resolve a division among the Circuits on the question whether an entity not named or joined as a defendant can nonetheless be deemed a real party in interest whose presence would destroy diversity. Compare 373 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 620\xe2\x80\x93622, with Plains Growers, Inc. v. Ickes-Braun Glasshouses, Inc., 474 F.\xc2\xa02d 250, 252 (CA5 1973) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe citizenship of one who has an interest in the lawsuit but who has not been made a party \xe2\x80\xa6 by plaintiff cannot be used by plaintiff on a motion to remand to defeat diversity jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d), and Simpson v. Providence Washington Ins. Group, 608 F.\xc2\xa02d 1171, 1173\xe2\x80\x931175 (CA9 1979) (Kennedy, J.) (upholding removal where Alaska plaintiff sued Rhode Island parent company without joining as well potentially liable Alaska subsidiary, and the parties did not act collusively to create diversity jurisdiction).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals correctly identified Lincoln as a proper party to the action, but it erred in insisting that some other entity affiliated with Lincoln should have been joined as a codefendant, and that it was Lincoln\xe2\x80\x99s obligation to name that entity and show that its joinder would not destroy diversity.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We stress, first, that, at this stage of the case, the existence of complete diversity between the Roches and Lincoln is not in doubt. The Roches, both citizens of Virginia, acknowledge that Lincoln is indeed a corporation, not a partnership, and that Lincoln is chartered in and has its principal place of business in Texas. Tr. of Oral Arg. 40\xe2\x80\x9341; see App. 114 (\xe2\x80\x9cUpon information and belief, Lincoln Property Company is a corporation with corporate headquarters [in] Texas.\xe2\x80\x9d); 373 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 620. Accordingly, for jurisdictional purposes, Lincoln is a citizen of Texas and of no other State. 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71332(c)(1) (\xe2\x80\x9ca corporation shall be deemed to be a citizen of any State by which it has been incorporated and of the State where it has its principal place of business\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We turn now to the reasons why the Fourth Circuit erred in determining that diversity jurisdiction was not proved by the removing parties. 373 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 612 (concluding that \xe2\x80\x9cDefendants failed to carry their burden of proof with respect to their allegedly diverse citizenship\xe2\x80\x9d). The principal federal statute governing diversity jurisdiction, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71332, gives federal district courts original jurisdiction of all civil actions \xe2\x80\x9cbetween \xe2\x80\xa6 citizens of different States\xe2\x80\x9d where the amount in controversy exceeds $75,000. \xc2\xa71332(a)(1).[Footnote 5] Since Strawbridge v. Curtiss, 3 Cranch 267 (1806), we have read the statutory formulation \xe2\x80\x9cbetween . . . citizens of different States\xe2\x80\x9d to require complete diversity between all plaintiffs and all defendants. Caterpillar Inc. v. Lewis, 519 U. S. 61, 68 (1996); cf. State Farm Fire & Casualty Co. v. Tashire, 386 U. S. 523, 530\xe2\x80\x93531 (1967) (explaining that complete diversity is not constitutionally required and upholding interpleader under \xc2\xa71335 based on minimal diversity, i.e., diversity between two or more adverse parties).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While \xc2\xa71332 allows plaintiffs to invoke the federal courts\xe2\x80\x99 diversity jurisdiction, \xc2\xa71441 gives defendants a corresponding opportunity. Section 1441(a) states: \xe2\x80\x9cExcept as otherwise expressly provided by Act of Congress, any civil action brought in a State court of which the district courts of the United States have original jurisdiction, may be removed by the defendant or the defendants, to the district court of the United States for the district and division embracing the place where such action is pending.\xe2\x80\x9d The scales are not evenly balanced, however. An in-state plaintiff may invoke diversity jurisdiction, but \xc2\xa71441(b) bars removal on the basis of diversity if any \xe2\x80\x9cpart[y] in interest properly joined and served as [a] defendan[t] is a citizen of the State in which [the] action is brought.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 6] In the instant case, Virginia plaintiffs Christophe and Juanita Roche joined and served no Virginian as a party defendant. Hence the action qualified for the removal defendants effected.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Neither Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 17(a), captioned \xe2\x80\x9cReal Party in Interest,\xe2\x80\x9d nor Federal Rule 19, captioned \xe2\x80\x9cJoinder of Persons Needed for Just Adjudication,\xe2\x80\x9d requires plaintiffs or defendants to name and join any additional parties to this action. Both Rules, we note, address party joinder, not federal-court subject-matter jurisdiction. See Rule 82 (\xe2\x80\x9c[The Federal Rules of Civil Procedure] shall not be construed to extend or limit the jurisdiction of the United States district courts \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d); Advisory Committee\xe2\x80\x99s Notes on Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 19, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. App., pp. 696\xe2\x80\x93698. Rule 17(a) directs that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]very action shall be prosecuted in the name of the real party in interest.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.) That Rule, as its text displays, speaks to joinder of plaintiffs, not defendants.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Rule 19 provides for the joinder of parties who should or must take part in the litigation to achieve a \xe2\x80\x9c[j]ust [a]djudication.\xe2\x80\x9d See Provident Tradesmens Bank & Trust Co. v. Patterson, 390 U. S. 102, 118\xe2\x80\x93123 (1968). The Roches place no reliance on Rule 19 and maintain that the Rule \xe2\x80\x9cplayed no part, explicitly or implicitly, in the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 conclusion.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 36. Given Lincoln\xe2\x80\x99s admission that it managed Westfield Village when mold contaminated the Roches\xe2\x80\x99 apartment, see supra, at 4, 7, it does indeed appear that no absent person, formally or practically, was \xe2\x80\x9c[n]eeded for [j]ust [a]djudication.\xe2\x80\x9d Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 19; cf. Simpson, 608 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 1174 (diverse corporate defendant accepted full liability for any eventual adverse judgment; nondiverse subsidiary need not be joined as a defendant, although arguably it had joint liability with its parent); 16 J. Moore et\xc2\xa0al., Moore\xe2\x80\x99s Federal Practice \xc2\xa7107.14[2][c], p. 107\xe2\x80\x9367 (3d ed. 2005) (\xe2\x80\x9cIn general, the plaintiff is the master of the complaint and has the option of naming only those parties the plaintiff chooses to sue, subject only to the rules of joinder [of] necessary parties.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While Rule 17(a) applies only to joinder of parties who assert claims, the Court of Appeals and the Roches draw from decisions of this Court a jurisdictional \xe2\x80\x9creal parties to the controversy\xe2\x80\x9d rule applicable in diversity cases to complaining and defending parties alike. See Navarro Savings Assn. v. Lee, 446 U. S. 458, 462, n.\xc2\xa09 (1980) (citing Note, Diversity Jurisdiction over Unincorporated Business Entities: The Real Party in Interest as a Jurisdictional Rule, 56 Texas L. Rev. 243, 247\xe2\x80\x93250 (1978)). But no decision called to our attention supports the burden the Court of Appeals placed on a properly joined defendant to negate the existence of a potential codefendant whose presence in the action would destroy diversity.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our decisions employing \xe2\x80\x9creal party to the controversy\xe2\x80\x9d terminology in describing or explaining who counts and who can be discounted for diversity purposes bear scant resemblance to the action the Roches have commenced. No party here has been \xe2\x80\x9cimproperly or collusively\xe2\x80\x9d named solely to create federal jurisdiction, see 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71359 (\xe2\x80\x9cA district court shall not have jurisdiction of a civil action in which any party, by assignment or otherwise, has been improperly or collusively made or joined to invoke the jurisdiction of such court.\xe2\x80\x9d); Kramer v. Caribbean Mills, Inc., 394 U. S. 823, 830 (1969) (assignment for collection only, motivated by desire to make diversity jurisdiction available, falls within the \xe2\x80\x9cvery core\xe2\x80\x9d of \xc2\xa71359); Little v. Giles, 118 U. S. 596, 600\xe2\x80\x93607 (1886) (where land was purportedly sold to out-of-state farmer but no money or deed changed hands, quiet title action could not be maintained based on farmer\xe2\x80\x99s diverse citizenship), nor to defeat it, see Cheaspeake & Ohio R. Co. v. Cockrell, 232 U. S. 146, 152 (1914) (diverse defendants, upon showing that joinder of nondiverse party was \xe2\x80\x9cwithout right and made in bad faith,\xe2\x80\x9d may successfully remove the action to federal court).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor are the Roches aided by cases in which actions against a state agency have been regarded as suits against the State itself. See, e.g., State Highway Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of Wyo. v. Utah Constr. Co., 278 U. S. 194, 199\xe2\x80\x93200 (1929) (\xe2\x80\x9c[State] Commission was but the arm or alter ego of the State with no funds or ability to respond in damages.\xe2\x80\x9d). Decisions of this genre are bottomed on this Court\xe2\x80\x99s recognition of a State\xe2\x80\x99s asserted Eleventh Amendment right not to be haled into federal court. See, e.g., Federal Maritime Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. South Carolina Ports Authority, 535 U. S. 743, 769 (2002).[Footnote 7] They are not pertinent to suits between private parties.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Unlike cases in which a party was named to satisfy state pleading rules, e.g., McNutt ex rel. Leggett, Smith, & Lawrence v. Bland, 2 How. 9, 14 (1844), or was joined only as designated performer of a ministerial act, e.g., Walden v. Skinner, 101 U. S. 577, 589 (1880), or otherwise had no control of, impact on, or stake in the controversy, e.g., Wood v. Davis, 18 How. 467, 469\xe2\x80\x93470 (1856), Lincoln has a vital interest in this case.[Footnote 8] Indeed, Lincoln accepted responsibility, in the event that the Roches prevailed on the merits of their claims, by admitting that, \xe2\x80\x9c[since 1996,] it has managed Westfield Village Apartments.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 137. A named defendant who admits involvement in the controversy and would be liable to pay a resulting judgment is not \xe2\x80\x9cnominal\xe2\x80\x9d in any sense except that it is named in the complaint. Cf. Knapp v. Railroad Co., 20 Wall. 117, 122 (1874).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In any event, we emphasize, the Fourth Circuit had no warrant in this case to inquire whether some other person might have been joined as an additional or substitute defendant. See id., at 122 (federal courts should not \xe2\x80\x9cinquir[e] outside of the case in order to ascertain whether some other person may not have an equitable interest in the cause of action\xe2\x80\x9d); Little, 118 U. S., at 603 (if named party\xe2\x80\x99s interest is real, the fact that other interested parties are not joined \xe2\x80\x9cwill not affect the jurisdiction of the [federal courts]\xe2\x80\x9d); 16 Moore, supra, \xc2\xa7107.14[2][c], p. 107\xe2\x80\x9367 (\xe2\x80\x9cOrdinarily, a court will not interfere with the consequences of a plaintiff\'s selection in naming parties, unless the plaintiff has impermissibly manufactured diversity or used an unacceptable device to defeat diversity.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress, empowered to prescribe the jurisdiction of the federal courts, sometimes has specified that a named party\xe2\x80\x99s own citizenship does not determine its diverse status. Thus, as a procedural matter, executors, administrators, and guardians \xe2\x80\x9cmay sue in [their] own name[s] without joining the party for whose benefit the action is brought.\xe2\x80\x9d Rule 17(a). As to diversity jurisdiction, however, \xc2\xa71332(c)(2) directs that \xe2\x80\x9cthe legal representative of [a decedent\xe2\x80\x99s] estate \xe2\x80\xa6 shall be deemed to be a citizen only of the same State as the decedent, and the legal representative of an infant or incompetent shall be deemed to be a citizen only of the same State as the infant or incompetent.\xe2\x80\x9d Congress has also provided that in direct action suits against insurers to which the insured is not made a party, the \xe2\x80\x9cinsurer shall be deemed a citizen of the State of which the insured is a citizen, as well as of any State by which the insurer has been incorporated and of the State where it has its principal place of business.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71332(c)(1).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But Congress surely has not directed that a corporation, for diversity-of-citizenship purposes, shall be deemed to have acquired the citizenship of all or any of its affiliates. For cases of the kind the Roches have instituted, Congress has provided simply and only this instruction: \xe2\x80\x9c[A] corporation shall be deemed to be a citizen of any State by which it has been incorporated and of the State where it has its principal place of business.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The jurisdictional rule governing here is unambiguous and it is not amenable to judicial enlargement. Under \xc2\xa71332(c)(1), Lincoln is a citizen of Texas alone, and under \xc2\xa71441(a) and (b), this case was properly removed.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Roches sued the entity they thought responsible for managing their apartment. Lincoln affirmed that it was so responsible. Complete diversity existed. The potential liability of other parties was a matter plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 counsel might have assiduously explored through discovery devices. It was not incumbent on Lincoln to propose as additional defendants persons the Roches, as masters of their complaint, permissively might have joined.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For the reasons stated, the judgment of the United States Court of Appeals for the Fourth Circuit is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.
