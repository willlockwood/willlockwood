\nSYLLABUS
\nOCTOBER TERM, 2008
\nHAWAII V. OFFICE OF HAWAIIAN AFFAIRS
\n
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\n
\n

HAWAII et\xc2\xa0al. v. OFFICE OF HAWAIIAN AFFAIRS et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the supreme court of hawaii

\n

No. 07\xe2\x80\x931372.\xe2\x80\x83Argued February 25, 2009\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 31, 2009

\n

After the overthrow of the Hawaiian monarchy in 1893, Congress annexed the Territory of Hawaii pursuant to the Newlands Resolution, under which Hawaii ceded to the United States the \xe2\x80\x9cabsolute fee\xe2\x80\x9d and ownership of all public, government, and crown lands. In 1959, the Admission Act made Hawaii a State, granting it \xe2\x80\x9call the public lands \xe2\x80\xa6 held by the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa75(b), and requiring these lands, \xe2\x80\x9ctogether with the proceeds from [their] sale \xe2\x80\xa6 , [to] be held by [the] State as a public trust,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa75(f). Hawaii state law also authorizes the State to use or sell the ceded lands, provided the proceeds are held in trust for Hawaiian citizens. In 1993, Congress\xe2\x80\x99 joint Apology Resolution \xe2\x80\x9capologize[d]\xe2\x80\x9d for this country\xe2\x80\x99s role in overthrowing the Hawaiian monarchy, \xc2\xa71, and declared that nothing in the resolution was \xe2\x80\x9cintended to serve as a settlement of any claims against the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The \xe2\x80\x9cLeiali\xe2\x80\x99i parcel,\xe2\x80\x9d a Maui tract of former crown land, was ceded to the United States at annexation and has been held by the State since 1959 as part of the Admission Act \xc2\xa75(f) trust. Hawaii\xe2\x80\x99s affordable housing agency (HFDC) received approval to remove the parcel from the trust and redevelop it upon compensating respondent Office of Hawaiian Affairs (OHA), which manages funds from the use or sale of ceded lands for the benefit of native Hawaiians. After HFDC refused OHA\xe2\x80\x99s demand that the payment include a disclaimer preserving any native Hawaiian claims to lands transferred from the trust for redevelopment, respondents sued to enjoin the sale or transfer of the Leiali\xe2\x80\x99i parcel and any other of the ceded lands until final determination of native Hawaiians\xe2\x80\x99 claims. The state trial court entered judgment against respondents, but the Hawaiian Supreme Court vacated that ruling. Relying on the Apology Resolution, the court granted the injunction that respondents requested, rejecting petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 argument that the Admission Act and state law give the State explicit power to sell ceded lands.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0This Court has jurisdiction. Respondents argue to no avail that the case does not raise a federal question because the State Supreme Court merely held that the sale of ceded lands would constitute a breach of the State\xe2\x80\x99s fiduciary duty to Native Hawaiians under state law. The Court has jurisdiction whenever \xe2\x80\x9ca state court decision fairly appears to rest primarily on federal law, or to be interwoven with the federal law.\xe2\x80\x9d Michigan v. Long, 463 U. S. 1032, 1040. Far from providing a plain statement that its decision rested on state law, the state court plainly held that the decision was dictated by federal law, particularly the Apology Resolution. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0The Apology Resolution did not strip Hawaii of its sovereign authority to alienate the lands the United States held in absolute fee and granted to the State upon its admission to the Union. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Neither of the resolution\xe2\x80\x99s substantive provisions justifies the judgment below. The first such provision\xe2\x80\x99s six verbs\xe2\x80\x94i.e., Congress \xe2\x80\x9cacknowledge[d] the historical significance\xe2\x80\x9d of the monarchy\xe2\x80\x99s overthrow, \xe2\x80\x9crecognize[d] and commend[ed] efforts of reconciliation\xe2\x80\x9d with native Hawaiians, \xe2\x80\x9capologize[d] to [them]\xe2\x80\x9d for the overthrow, \xe2\x80\x9cexpresse[d] [the] commitment to acknowledge [the overthrow\xe2\x80\x99s] ramifications,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9curge[d] the President \xe2\x80\xa6 to also acknowledge [those] ramifications,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71\xe2\x80\x94are all conciliatory or precatory. This is not the kind of language Congress uses to create substantive rights, especially rights enforceable against the cosovereign States. See, e.g., Pennhurst State School and Hospital v. Halderman, 451 U. S. 1, 17\xe2\x80\x9318. The resolution\xe2\x80\x99s second substantive provision, the \xc2\xa73 disclaimer, by its terms speaks only to those who may or may not have \xe2\x80\x9cclaims against the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d The State Supreme Court, however, read \xc2\xa73 as a congressional recognition\xe2\x80\x94and preservation\xe2\x80\x94of claims against Hawaii. There is no justification for turning an express disclaimer of claims against one sovereign into an affirmative recognition of claims against another. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The State Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that the 37 \xe2\x80\x9cwhereas\xe2\x80\x9d clauses prefacing the Apology Resolution clearly recognize native Hawaiians\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cunrelinquished\xe2\x80\x9d claims over the ceded lands is wrong for at least three reasons. First, such \xe2\x80\x9cwhereas\xe2\x80\x9d clauses cannot bear the weight that the lower court placed on them. See, e.g., District of Columbia v. Heller, 554 U. S. ___, ___, n.\xc2\xa03. Second, even if the clauses had some legal effect, they did not restructure Hawaii\xe2\x80\x99s rights and obligations, as the lower court found. \xe2\x80\x9c[R]epeals by implication are not favored and will not be presumed unless the intention of the legislature to repeal [is] clear and manifest.\xe2\x80\x9d National Assn. of Home Builders v. Defenders of Wildlife, 551 U. S. 644, ___. The Apology Resolution reveals no such intention, much less a clear and manifest one. Third, because the resolution would raise grave constitutional concerns if it purported to \xe2\x80\x9ccloud\xe2\x80\x9d Hawaii\xe2\x80\x99s title to its sovereign lands more than three decades after the State\xe2\x80\x99s admission to the Union, see, e.g., Idaho v. United States, 533 U. S. 262, 280, n.\xc2\xa09, the Court refuses to read the nonsubstantive \xe2\x80\x9cwhereas\xe2\x80\x9d clauses to create such a \xe2\x80\x9ccloud\xe2\x80\x9d retroactively, see, e.g., Clark v. Martinez, 543 U. S. 371, 381\xe2\x80\x93382. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

117 Haw. 174, 177 P.\xc2\xa03d 884, reversed and remanded.

\n

\nOPINION OF THE COURT
\nHAWAII V. OFFICE OF HAWAIIAN AFFAIRS
\n556 U. S. ____ (2009)
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 07-1372
\n
\n

HAWAII, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. OFFICE OF\nHAWAIIAN AFFAIRS et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of hawaii

\n

[March 31, 2009]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Alito delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case presents the question whether Congress stripped the State of Hawaii of its authority to alienate its sovereign territory by passing a joint resolution to apologize for the role that the United States played in overthrowing the Hawaiian monarchy in the late 19th century. Relying on Congress\xe2\x80\x99 joint resolution, the Supreme Court of Hawaii permanently enjoined the State from alienating certain of its lands, pending resolution of native Hawaiians\xe2\x80\x99 land claims that the court described as \xe2\x80\x9cunrelinquished.\xe2\x80\x9d We reverse.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1893, \xe2\x80\x9c[a] so-called Committee of Safety, a group of professionals and businessmen, with the active assistance of John Stevens, the United States Minister to Hawaii, acting with the United States Armed Forces, replaced the [Hawaiian] monarchy with a provisional government.\xe2\x80\x9d Rice v. Cayetano, 528 U. S. 495, 504\xe2\x80\x93505 (2000). \xe2\x80\x9cThat government sought annexation by the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 505, which the United States granted, see Joint Resolution to Provide for Annexing the Hawaiian Islands to the United States, No. 55, 30 Stat. 750 (hereinafter Newlands Resolution). Pursuant to the Newlands Resolution, the Republic of Hawaii \xe2\x80\x9ccede[d] absolutely and without reserve to the United States of America all rights of sovereignty of whatsoever kind\xe2\x80\x9d and further \xe2\x80\x9ccede[d] and transfer[red] to the United States the absolute fee and ownership of all public, Government, or Crown lands, public buildings or edifices, ports, harbors, military equipment, and all other public property of every kind and description belonging to the Government of the Hawaiian Islands, together with every right and appurtenance thereunto appertaining\xe2\x80\x9d (hereinafter ceded lands).[Footnote 1] Ibid. The Newlands Resolution further provided that all \xe2\x80\x9cproperty and rights\xe2\x80\x9d in the ceded lands \xe2\x80\x9care vested in the United States of America.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Two years later, Congress established a government for the Territory of Hawaii. See Act of Apr. 30, 1900, ch. 339, 31 Stat. 141 (hereinafter Organic Act). The Organic Act reiterated the Newlands Resolution and made clear that the new Territory consisted of the land that the United States acquired in \xe2\x80\x9cabsolute fee\xe2\x80\x9d under that resolution. See \xc2\xa72, ibid. The Organic Act further provided:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he portion of the public domain heretofore known as Crown land is hereby declared to have been, on [the effective date of the Newlands Resolution], and prior thereto, the property of the Hawaiian government, and to be free and clear from any trust of or concerning the same, and from all claim of any nature whatsoever, upon the rents, issues, and profits thereof. It shall be subject to alienation and other uses as may be provided by law.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa799, id., at 161; see also \xc2\xa791, id., at 159.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1959, Congress admitted Hawaii to the Union. See Pub. L. 86\xe2\x80\x933, 73 Stat. 4 (hereinafter Admission Act). Under the Admission Act, with exceptions not relevant here, \xe2\x80\x9cthe United States grant[ed] to the State of Hawaii, effective upon its admission into the Union, the United States\xe2\x80\x99 title to all the public lands and other public property within the boundaries of the State of Hawaii, title to which is held by the United States immediately prior to its admission into the Union.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa75(b), id., at 5. These lands, \xe2\x80\x9ctogether with the proceeds from the sale or other disposition of [these] lands and the income therefrom, shall be held by [the] State as a public trust\xe2\x80\x9d to promote various public purposes, including supporting public education, bettering conditions of Native Hawaiians, developing home ownership, making public improvements, and providing lands for public use. \xc2\xa75(f), id., at 6. Hawaii state law also authorizes the State to use or sell the ceded lands, provided that the proceeds are held in trust for the benefit of the citizens of Hawaii. See, e.g., Haw. Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7171\xe2\x80\x9345, 171\xe2\x80\x9318 (1993).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1993, Congress enacted a joint resolution \xe2\x80\x9cto acknowledge the historic significance of the illegal overthrow of the Kingdom of Hawaii, to express its deep regret to the Native Hawaiian people, and to support the reconciliation efforts of the State of Hawaii and the United Church of Christ with Native Hawaiians.\xe2\x80\x9d Joint Resolution to Acknowledge the 100th Anniversary of the January 17, 1893 Overthrow of the Kingdom of Hawaii, Pub. L. 103\xe2\x80\x93150, 107 Stat. 1510, 1513 (hereinafter Apology Resolution). In a series of the preambular \xe2\x80\x9cwhereas\xe2\x80\x9d clauses, Congress made various observations about Hawaii\xe2\x80\x99s history. For example, the Apology Resolution states that \xe2\x80\x9cthe indigenous Hawaiian people never directly relinquished their claims \xe2\x80\xa6 over their national lands to the United States\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9cthe health and well-being of the Native Hawaiian people is intrinsically tied to their deep feelings and attachment to the land.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1512. In the same vein, the resolution\xe2\x80\x99s only substantive section\xe2\x80\x94entitled \xe2\x80\x9cAcknowledgement and Apology\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94states that Congress:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(1)\xe2\x80\x82\xe2\x80\xa6 acknowledges the historical significance of this event which resulted in the suppression of the inherent sovereignty of the Native Hawaiian people;

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(2)\xe2\x80\x82recognizes and commends efforts of reconciliation initiated by the State of Hawaii and the United Church of Christ with Native Hawaiians;

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(3)\xe2\x80\x82apologizes to Native Hawaiians on behalf of the people of the United States for the overthrow of the Kingdom of Hawaii on January 17, 1893 with the participation of agents and citizens of the United States, and the deprivation of the rights of Native Hawaiians to self-determination;

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(4)\xe2\x80\x82expresses its commitment to acknowledge the ramifications of the overthrow of the Kingdom of Hawaii, in order to provide a proper foundation for reconciliation between the United States and the Native Hawaiian people; and

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(5)\xe2\x80\x82urges the President of the United States to also acknowledge the ramifications of the overthrow of the Kingdom of Hawaii and to support reconciliation efforts between the United States and the Native Hawaiian people.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1513.

\n

\nFinally, \xc2\xa73 of the Apology Resolution states that \xe2\x80\x9cNothing in this Joint Resolution is intended to serve as a settlement of any claims against the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1514.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This suit involves a tract of former crown land on Maui, now known as the \xe2\x80\x9cLeiali\xe2\x80\x99i parcel,\xe2\x80\x9d that was ceded in \xe2\x80\x9cabsolute fee\xe2\x80\x9d to the United States at annexation and has been held by the State since 1959 as part of the trust established by \xc2\xa75(f) of the Admission Act. The Housing Finance and Development Corporation (HFDC)\xe2\x80\x94Hawaii\xe2\x80\x99s affordable housing agency\xe2\x80\x94received approval to remove the Leiali\xe2\x80\x99i parcel from the \xc2\xa75(f) trust and redevelop it. In order to transfer the Leiali\xe2\x80\x99i parcel out of the public trust, HFDC was required to compensate respondent Office of Hawaiian Affairs (OHA), which was established to receive and manage funds from the use or sale of the ceded lands for the benefit of native Hawaiians. Haw. Const., Art. XII, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa74\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this case, however, OHA demanded more than monetary compensation. Relying on the Apology Resolution, respondent OHA demanded that HFDC include a disclaimer preserving any native Hawaiian claims to ownership of lands transferred from the public trust for redevelopment. HFDC declined to include the requested disclaimer because \xe2\x80\x9cto do so would place a cloud on title, rendering title insurance unavailable.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 207a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Again relying on the Apology Resolution, respondents then sued the State, its Governor, HFDC (since renamed), and its officials. Respondents sought \xe2\x80\x9cto enjoin the defendants from selling or otherwise transferring the Leiali\xe2\x80\x99i parcel to third parties and selling or otherwise transferring to third parties any of the ceded lands in general until a determination of the native Hawaiians\xe2\x80\x99 claims to the ceded lands is made.\xe2\x80\x9d Office of Hawaiian Affairs v. Housing and Community Development Corp. of Hawaii, 117 Haw. 174, 189, 177 P.\xc2\xa03d 884, 899 (2008). Respondents \xe2\x80\x9calleged that an injunction was proper because, in light of the Apology Resolution, any transfer of ceded lands by the State to third-parties would amount to a breach of trust \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 188, 177 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 898.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The state trial court entered judgment against respondents, but the Supreme Court of Hawaii vacated the lower court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling. Relying on a \xe2\x80\x9cplain reading of the Apology Resolution,\xe2\x80\x9d which \xe2\x80\x9cdictate[d]\xe2\x80\x9d its conclusion, id., at 212, 177 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 988, the State Supreme Court ordered \xe2\x80\x9can injunction against the defendants from selling or otherwise transferring to third parties (1) the Leiali\xe2\x80\x99i parcel and (2) any other ceded lands from the public lands trust until the claims of the native Hawaiians to the ceded lands have been resolved,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 218, 177 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 928. In doing so, the court rejected petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 argument that \xe2\x80\x9cthe State has the undoubted and explicit power to sell ceded lands pursuant to the terms of the Admission Act and pursuant to state law.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 211, 177 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 920 (internal quotation marks and alterations omitted). We granted certiorari. 555 U. S. ___ (2008).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Before turning to the merits, we first must address our jurisdiction. According to respondents, the Supreme Court of Hawaii \xe2\x80\x9cmerely held that, in light of the ongoing reconciliation process, the sale of ceded lands would constitute a breach of the State\xe2\x80\x99s fiduciary duty to Native Hawaiians under state law.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 17. Because respondents believe that this case does not raise a federal question, they urge us to dismiss for lack of jurisdiction.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although respondents dwell at length on that argument, see id., at 19\xe2\x80\x9334, we need not tarry long to reject it. This Court has jurisdiction whenever \xe2\x80\x9ca state court decision fairly appears to rest primarily on federal law, or to be interwoven with the federal law, and when the adequacy and independence of any possible state law ground is not clear from the face of the opinion.\xe2\x80\x9d Michigan v. Long, 463 U. S. 1032, 1040\xe2\x80\x931041 (1983). Far from providing a \xe2\x80\x9cplain statement\xe2\x80\x9d that its decision rested on state law, id., at 1041, the State Supreme Court plainly held that its decision was \xe2\x80\x9cdictate[d]\xe2\x80\x9d by federal law\xe2\x80\x94in particular, the Apology Resolution, see 117 Haw., at 212, 177 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 922. Indeed, the court explained that the Apology Resolution lies \xe2\x80\x9c[a]t the heart of [respondents\xe2\x80\x99] claims,\xe2\x80\x9d that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9ccurrent claim for injunctive relief is \xe2\x80\xa6 based largely upon the Apology Resolution,\xe2\x80\x9d and that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 arguments presuppose that the Apology Resolution \xe2\x80\x9cchanged the legal landscape and restructured the rights and obligations of the State.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 189\xe2\x80\x93190, 177 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 899\xe2\x80\x93900 (internal quotation marks omitted). The court noted that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he primary question before this court on appeal is whether, in light of the Apology Resolution, this court should issue an injunction\xe2\x80\x9d against sale of the trust lands, id., at 210, 177 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 920, and it concluded, \xe2\x80\x9c[b]ased on a plain reading\xe2\x80\x9d of the Apology Resolution, that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress has clearly recognized that the native Hawaiian people have unrelinquished claims over the ceded lands,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 191, 177 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 901.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Based on these and the remainder of the State Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s 77 references to the Apology Resolution, we have no doubt that the decision below rested on federal law.[Footnote 2] We are therefore satisfied that this Court has jurisdiction. See 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71257.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Turning to the merits, we must decide whether the Apology Resolution \xe2\x80\x9cstrips Hawaii of its sovereign authority to sell, exchange, or transfer\xe2\x80\x9d (Pet. for Cert. i) the lands that the United States held in \xe2\x80\x9cabsolute fee\xe2\x80\x9d (30 Stat. 750) and \xe2\x80\x9cgrant[ed] to the State of Hawaii, effective upon its admission into the Union\xe2\x80\x9d (73 Stat. 5). We conclude that the Apology Resolution has no such effect.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cWe begin, as always, with the text of the statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Permanent Mission of India to United Nations v. City of New York, 551 U. S. 193, 197 (2007). The Apology Resolution contains two substantive provisions. See 107 Stat. 1513\xe2\x80\x931514. Neither justifies the judgment below.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The resolution\xe2\x80\x99s first substantive provision uses six verbs, all of which are conciliatory or precatory. Specifically, Congress \xe2\x80\x9cacknowledge[d] the historical significance\xe2\x80\x9d of the Hawaiian monarchy\xe2\x80\x99s overthrow, \xe2\x80\x9crecognize[d] and commend[ed] efforts of reconciliation\xe2\x80\x9d with native Hawaiians, \xe2\x80\x9capologize[d] to [n]ative Hawaiians\xe2\x80\x9d for the monarchy\xe2\x80\x99s overthrow, \xe2\x80\x9cexpresse[d] [Congress\xe2\x80\x99s] commitment to acknowledge the ramifications of the overthrow,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9curge[d] the President of the United States to also acknowledge the ramifications of the overthrow \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71. Such terms are not the kind that Congress uses to create substantive rights\xe2\x80\x94especially those that are enforceable against the cosovereign States. See, e.g., Pennhurst State School and Hospital v. Halderman, 451 U. S. 1, 17\xe2\x80\x9318 (1981).[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Apology Resolution\xe2\x80\x99s second and final substantive provision is a disclaimer, which provides: \xe2\x80\x9cNothing in this Joint Resolution is intended to serve as a settlement of any claims against the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73. By its terms, \xc2\xa73 speaks only to those who may or may not have \xe2\x80\x9cclaims against the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d The court below, however, held that the only way to save \xc2\xa73 from superfluity is to construe it as a congressional recognition\xe2\x80\x94and preservation\xe2\x80\x94of claims against Hawaii and as \xe2\x80\x9cthe foundation (or starting point) for reconciliation\xe2\x80\x9d between the State and native Hawaiians. 117 Haw., at 192, 177 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 902.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cWe must have regard to all the words used by Congress, and as far as possible give effect to them,\xe2\x80\x9d Louisville & Nashville R. Co. v. Mottley, 219 U. S. 467, 475 (1911), but that maxim is not a judicial license to turn an irrelevant statutory provision into a relevant one. And we know of no justification for turning an express disclaimer of claims against one sovereign into an affirmative recognition of claims against another.[Footnote 4] Cf. Pacific Bell Telephone Co. v. linkLine Communications, Inc., 555 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (slip op., at 17) (\xe2\x80\x9cTwo wrong claims do not make one that is right\xe2\x80\x9d). The Supreme Court of Hawaii erred in reading \xc2\xa73 as recognizing claims inconsistent with the title held in \xe2\x80\x9cabsolute fee\xe2\x80\x9d by the United States (30 Stat. 750) and conveyed to the State of Hawaii at statehood. See supra, at 2\xe2\x80\x933.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Rather than focusing on the operative words of the law, the court below directed its attention to the 37 \xe2\x80\x9cwhereas\xe2\x80\x9d clauses that preface the Apology Resolution. See 107 Stat. 1510\xe2\x80\x931513. \xe2\x80\x9cBased on a plain reading of\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9cwhereas\xe2\x80\x9d clauses, the Supreme Court of Hawaii held that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress has clearly recognized that the native Hawaiian people have unrelinquished claims over the ceded lands.\xe2\x80\x9d 117 Haw., at 191, 177 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 901. That conclusion is wrong for at least three reasons.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, \xe2\x80\x9cwhereas\xe2\x80\x9d clauses like those in the Apology Resolution cannot bear the weight that the lower court placed on them. As we recently explained in a different context, \xe2\x80\x9cwhere the text of a clause itself indicates that it does not have operative effect, such as \xe2\x80\x98whereas\xe2\x80\x99 clauses in federal legislation \xe2\x80\xa6 , a court has no license to make it do what it was not designed to do.\xe2\x80\x9d District of Columbia v. Heller, 554 U. S. ___, ___, n.\xc2\xa03 (2008) (slip op., at 4, n.\xc2\xa03). See also Yazoo & Mississippi Valley R. Co. v. Thomas, 132 U. S. 174, 188 (1889) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]s the preamble is no part of the act, and cannot enlarge or confer powers, nor control the words of the act, unless they are doubtful or ambiguous, the necessity of resorting to it to assist in ascertaining the true intent and meaning of the legislature is in itself fatal to the claim set up\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, even if the \xe2\x80\x9cwhereas\xe2\x80\x9d clauses had some legal effect, they did not \xe2\x80\x9cchang[e] the legal landscape and restructur[e] the rights and obligations of the State.\xe2\x80\x9d 117 Haw., at 190, 177 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 900. As we have emphasized, \xe2\x80\x9crepeals by implication are not favored and will not be presumed unless the intention of the legislature to repeal [is] clear and manifest.\xe2\x80\x9d National Assn. of Home Builders v. Defenders of Wildlife, 551 U. S. 644, 662 (2007) (internal quotation marks omitted). The Apology Resolution reveals no indication\xe2\x80\x94much less a \xe2\x80\x9cclear and manifest\xe2\x80\x9d one\xe2\x80\x94that Congress intended to amend or repeal the State\xe2\x80\x99s rights and obligations under Admission Act (or any other federal law); nor does the Apology Resolution reveal any evidence that Congress intended sub silentio to \xe2\x80\x9ccloud\xe2\x80\x9d the title that the United States held in \xe2\x80\x9cabsolute fee\xe2\x80\x9d and transferred to the State in 1959. On that score, we find it telling that even respondent OHA has now abandoned its argument, made below, that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress \xe2\x80\xa6 enacted the Apology Resolution and thus \xe2\x80\xa6 change[d]\xe2\x80\x9d the Admission Act. App. 114a; see also Tr. of Oral Arg. 31, 37\xe2\x80\x9338.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Third, the Apology Resolution would raise grave constitutional concerns if it purported to \xe2\x80\x9ccloud\xe2\x80\x9d Hawaii\xe2\x80\x99s title to its sovereign lands more than three decades after the State\xe2\x80\x99s admission to the Union. We have emphasized that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress cannot, after statehood, reserve or convey submerged lands that have already been bestowed upon a State.\xe2\x80\x9d Idaho v. United States, 533 U. S. 262, 280, n.\xc2\xa09 (2001) (internal quotation marks and alteration omitted); see also id., at 284 (Rehnquist, C.\xc2\xa0J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he consequences of admission are instantaneous, and it ignores the uniquely sovereign character of that event \xe2\x80\xa6 to suggest that subsequent events somehow can diminish what has already been bestowed\xe2\x80\x9d). And that proposition applies a fortiori where virtually all of the State\xe2\x80\x99s public lands\xe2\x80\x94not just its submerged ones\xe2\x80\x94are at stake. In light of those concerns, we must not read the Apology Resolution\xe2\x80\x99s nonsubstantive \xe2\x80\x9cwhereas\xe2\x80\x9d clauses to create a retroactive \xe2\x80\x9ccloud\xe2\x80\x9d on the title that Congress granted to the State of Hawaii in 1959. See, e.g., Clark v. Martinez, 543 U. S. 371, 381\xe2\x80\x93382 (2005) (the canon of constitutional avoidance \xe2\x80\x9cis a tool for choosing between competing plausible interpretations of a statutory text, resting on the reasonable presumption that Congress did not intend the alternative which raises serious constitutional doubts\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When a state supreme court incorrectly bases a decision on federal law, the court\xe2\x80\x99s decision improperly prevents the citizens of the State from addressing the issue in question through the processes provided by the State\xe2\x80\x99s constitution. Here, the State Supreme Court incorrectly held that Congress, by adopting the Apology Resolution, took away from the citizens of Hawaii the authority to resolve an issue that is of great importance to the people of the State. Respondents defend that decision by arguing that they have both state-law property rights in the land in question and \xe2\x80\x9cbroader moral and political claims for compensation for the wrongs of the past.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 18. But we have no authority to decide questions of Hawaiian law or to provide redress for past wrongs except as provided for by federal law. The judgment of the Supreme Court of Hawaii is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.
