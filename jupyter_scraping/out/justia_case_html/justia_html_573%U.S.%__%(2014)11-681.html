\n

HARRIS et\xc2\xa0al. v. QUINN, GOVERNOR OF ILLINOIS, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the seventh circuit

\n

No. 11\xe2\x80\x93681.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 21, 2014\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 30, 2014

\n

Illinois\xe2\x80\x99 Home Services Program (Rehabilitation Program) allows Medicaid recipients who would normally need institutional care to hire a \xe2\x80\x9cpersonal assistant\xe2\x80\x9d (PA) to provide homecare services. Under State law, the homecare recipients (designated \xe2\x80\x9ccustomers\xe2\x80\x9d) and the State both play some role in the employment relationship with the PAs. Customers control most aspects of the employment relationship, including the hiring, firing, training, supervising, and disciplining of PAs; they also define the PA\xe2\x80\x99s duties by proposing a \xe2\x80\x9cService Plan.\xe2\x80\x9d Other than compensating PAs, the State\xe2\x80\x99s involvement in employment matters is minimal. Its employer status was created by executive order, and later codified by the legislature, solely to permit PAs to join a labor union and engage in collective bargaining under Illinois\xe2\x80\x99 Public Labor Relations Act (PLRA).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Pursuant to this scheme, respondent SEIU Healthcare Illinois & Indiana (SEIU\xe2\x80\x93HII) was designated the exclusive union representative for Rehabilitation Program employees. The union entered into collective-bargaining agreements with the State that contained an agency-fee provision, which requires all bargaining unit members who do not wish to join the union to pay the union a fee for the cost of certain activities, including those tied to the collective-bargaining process. A group of Rehabilitation Program PAs brought a class action against SEIU\xe2\x80\x93HII and other respondents in Federal District Court, claiming that the PLRA violated the\n \nFirst Amendment insofar as it authorized the agency-fee provision. The District Court dismissed their claims, and the Seventh Circuit affirmed in relevant part, concluding that the PAs were state employees within the meaning of Abood v. Detroit Bd. of Ed.,\n\n431 U.\xc2\xa0S. 209\n.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The\n \nFirst Amendment prohibits the collection of an agency fee from Rehabilitation Program PAs who do not want to join or support the union. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9340.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (a)\xc2\xa0In upholding the Illinois law\xe2\x80\x99s constitutionality, the Seventh Circuit relied on Abood, which, in turn, relied on Railway Employes v. Hanson,\n\n351 U.\xc2\xa0S. 225\n, and Machinists v. Street,\n\n367 U.\xc2\xa0S. 740\n. Unlike Abood, those cases involved private-sector collective-bargaining agreements. The Abood Court treated the\n \nFirst Amendment issue as largely settled by Hanson and Street and understood those cases to have upheld agency fees based on the desirability of \xe2\x80\x9clabor peace\xe2\x80\x9d and the problem of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98free riders[hip].\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 431 U.\xc2\xa0S., 220\xe2\x80\x93222, 224. However, \xe2\x80\x9cpreventing nonmembers from free-riding on the union\xe2\x80\x99s efforts\xe2\x80\x9d is a rationale \xe2\x80\x9cgenerally insufficient to overcome\n \nFirst Amendment objections,\xe2\x80\x9d Knox v. Service Employees, 567 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, and in this respect, Abood is \xe2\x80\x9csomething of an anomaly,\xe2\x80\x9d 567 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Abood Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis is questionable on several grounds. The\n \nFirst Amendment analysis in Hanson was thin, and Street was not a constitutional decision. And the Court fundamentally misunderstood Hanson\xe2\x80\x99s narrow holding, which upheld the authorization, not imposition, of an agency fee. The Abood Court also failed to appreciate the distinction between core union speech in the public sector and core union speech in the private sector, as well as the conceptual difficulty in public-sector cases of distinguishing union expenditures for collective bargaining from those designed for political purposes. Nor does the Abood Court seem to have anticipated the administrative problems that would result in attempting to classify union expenditures as either chargeable or nonchargeable, see, e.g., Lehnert v. Ferris Faculty Assn.,\n\n500 U.\xc2\xa0S. 507\n, or the practical problems that would arise from the heavy burden facing objecting nonmembers wishing to challenge the union\xe2\x80\x99s actions. Finally, the Abood Court\xe2\x80\x99s critical \xe2\x80\x9clabor peace\xe2\x80\x9d analysis rests on the unsupported empirical assumption that exclusive representation in the public sector depends on the right to collect an agency fee from nonmembers. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9320.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (b)\xc2\xa0Because of Abood\xe2\x80\x99s questionable foundations, and because Illinois\xe2\x80\x99 PAs are quite different from full-fledged public employees, this Court refuses to extend Abood to the situation here. Pp.\xc2\xa020\xe2\x80\x9329.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (1)\xc2\xa0PAs are much different from public employees. Unlike full-fledged public employees, PAs are almost entirely answerable to the customers and not to the State, do not enjoy most of the rights and benefits that inure to state employees, and are not indemnified by the State for claims against them arising from actions taken during the course of their employment. Even the scope of collective bargaining on their behalf is sharply limited. Pp.\xc2\xa020\xe2\x80\x9325.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (2)\xc2\xa0Abood\xe2\x80\x99s rationale is based on the assumption that the union possesses the full scope of powers and duties generally available under American labor law. Even the best argument for Abood\xe2\x80\x99s anomalous approach is a poor fit here. What justifies the agency fee in the Abood context is the fact that the State compels the union to promote and protect the interests of nonmembers in \xe2\x80\x9cnegotiating and administering a collective-bargaining agreement and representing the interests of employees in settling disputes and processing grievances.\xe2\x80\x9d Lehnert, supra, at 556. That rationale has little application here, where Illinois law requires that all PAs receive the same rate of pay and the union has no authority with respect to a PA\xe2\x80\x99s grievances against a customer. Pp.\xc2\xa025\xe2\x80\x9327.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (3)\xc2\xa0Extending Abood\xe2\x80\x99s boundaries to encompass partial public employees would invite problems. State regulations and benefits affecting such employees exist along a continuum, and it is unclear at what point, short of full-fledged public employment, Abood should apply. Under respondents\xe2\x80\x99 view, a host of workers who currently receive payments from a government entity for some sort of service would become candidates for inclusion within Abood\xe2\x80\x99s reach, and it would be hard to see where to draw the line. Pp.\xc2\xa027\xe2\x80\x9329.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (c)\xc2\xa0Because Abood does not control here, generally applicable\n \nFirst Amendment standards apply. Thus, the agency-fee provision here must serve a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98compelling state interes[t] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that cannot be achieved through means significantly less restrictive of associational freedoms.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Knox, supra, at ___. None of the interests that respondents contend are furthered by the agency-fee provision is sufficient. Pp.\xc2\xa029\xe2\x80\x9334.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (1)\xc2\xa0Their claim that the agency-fee provision promotes \xe2\x80\x9clabor peace\xe2\x80\x9d misses the point. Petitioners do not contend that they have a\n \nFirst Amendment right to form a rival union or that SEIU\xe2\x80\x93HII has no authority to serve as the exclusive bargaining representative. This, along with examples from some federal agencies and many state laws, demonstrates that a union\xe2\x80\x99s status as exclusive bargaining agent and the right to collect an agency fee from nonmembers are not inextricably linked. Features of the Illinois scheme\xe2\x80\x94e.g., PAs do not work together in a common state facility and the union\xe2\x80\x99s role is very restricted\xe2\x80\x94further undermine the \xe2\x80\x9clabor peace\xe2\x80\x9d argument. Pp.\xc2\xa031\xe2\x80\x9332.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (2)\xc2\xa0Respondents also argue that the agency-fee provision promotes the welfare of PAs, thereby contributing to the Rehabilitation Program\xe2\x80\x99s success. Even assuming that SEIU\xe2\x80\x93HII has been an effective advocate, the agency-fee provision cannot be sustained unless the union could not adequately advocate without the receipt of nonmember agency fees. No such showing has been made. Pp.\xc2\xa032\xe2\x80\x9334.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (d)\xc2\xa0Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 additional arguments for sustaining the Illinois scheme are unconvincing. First, they urge the application of a balancing test derived from Pickering v. Board of Ed. of Township High School Dist. 205, Will Cty.,\n\n391 U.\xc2\xa0S. 563\n. This Court has never viewed Abood and its progeny as based on Pickering balancing. And even assuming that Pickering applies, that case\xe2\x80\x99s balancing test clearly tips in favor of the objecting employees\xe2\x80\x99\n \nFirst Amendment interests. Second, respondents err in contending that a refusal to extend Abood here will call into question this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions in Keller v. State Bar of Cal.,\n\n496 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n, and Board of Regents of Univ. of Wis. System v. Southworth,\n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 217\n, for those decisions fit comfortably within the framework applied here. Pp.\xc2\xa034\xe2\x80\x9340.

\n

656 F.\xc2\xa03d 692, reversed in part, affirmed in part, and remanded.

PAMELA HARRIS, et al, PETITIONERS v. PAT QUINN, GOVERNOR of ILLINOIS, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the seventh circuit

\n

[June 30, 2014]

\n

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Justice Alito delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This case presents the question whether the\n \nFirst Amendment permits a State to compel personal care providers to subsidize speech on matters of public concern by a union that they do not wish to join or support. We hold that it does not, and we therefore reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeals.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Millions of Americans, due to age, illness, or injury, are unable to live in their own homes without assistance and are unable to afford the expense of in-home care. In order to prevent these individuals from having to enter a nursing home or other facility, the federal Medicaid program funds state-run programs that provide in-home services to individuals whose conditions would otherwise require institutionalization. See\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71396n(c)(1). A State that adopts such a program receives federal funds to compensate persons who attend to the daily needs of individuals needing in-home care. Ibid.; see also 42 CFR \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7440.180, 441.300\xe2\x80\x93441.310 (2013). Almost every State has established such a program. See Dept. of Health and Human Services, Understanding Medicaid Home and Community Services: A Primer (2010).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n One of those States is Illinois, which has created the Illinois Department of Human Services Home Services Program, known colloquially as the state \xe2\x80\x9cRehabilitation Program.\xe2\x80\x9d Ill. Comp. Stat., ch. 20, \xc2\xa72405/3(f\xc2\xa0) (West 2012); 89 Ill. Admin. Code \xc2\xa7676.10 (2007). \xe2\x80\x9c[D]esigned to prevent the unnecessary institutionalization of individuals who may instead be satisfactorily maintained at home at a lesser cost to the State,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7676.10(a), the Rehabilitation Program allows participants to hire a \xe2\x80\x9cpersonal assistant\xe2\x80\x9d who provides homecare services tailored to the individual\xe2\x80\x99s needs. Many of these personal assistants are relatives of the person receiving care, and some of them provide care in their own homes. See App. 16\xe2\x80\x9318. \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Illinois law establishes an employer-employee relationship between the person receiving the care and the person providing it. The law states explicitly that the person receiving home care\xe2\x80\x94the \xe2\x80\x9ccustomer\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cshall be the employer of the [personal assistant].\xe2\x80\x9d 89 Ill. Admin. Code \xc2\xa7676.30(b) (emphasis added). A \xe2\x80\x9cpersonal assistant\xe2\x80\x9d is defined as \xe2\x80\x9can individual employed by the customer to provide .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. varied services that have been approved by the customer\xe2\x80\x99s physician,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7676.30(p) (emphasis added), and the law makes clear that Illinois \xe2\x80\x9cshall not have control or input in the employment relationship between the customer and the personal assistants.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7676.10(c). \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Other provisions of the law emphasize the customer\xe2\x80\x99s employer status. The customer \xe2\x80\x9cis responsible for controlling all aspects of the employment relationship between the customer and the [personal assistant (or PA)], including, without limitation, locating and hiring the PA, training the PA, directing, evaluating and otherwise supervising the work performed by the personal assistant, imposing .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. disciplinary action against the PA, and terminating the employment relationship between the customer and the PA.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7676.30(b).[1] In general, the customer \xe2\x80\x9chas complete discretion in which Personal Assistant he/she wishes to hire.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7684.20(b).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n A customer also controls the contents of the document, the Service Plan, that lists the services that the customer will receive. \xc2\xa7684.10(a). No Service Plan may take effect without the approval of both the customer and the customer\xe2\x80\x99s physician. See\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa7684.10, 684.40, 684.50, 684.75. Service Plans are highly individualized. The Illinois State Labor Relations Board noted in 1985 that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here is no typical employment arrangement here, public or otherwise; rather, there simply exists an arrangement whereby the state of Illinois pays individuals .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to work under the direction and control of private third parties.\xe2\x80\x9d Illinois Dept. of Central Management Serv., No. S\xe2\x80\x93RC\xe2\x80\x93115, 2 PERI \xc2\xb62007, p. VIII\xe2\x80\x9330, (1985), superseded, 2003 Ill. Laws p. 1929.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n While customers exercise predominant control over their employment relationship with personal assistants, the State, subsidized by the federal Medicaid program, pays the personal assistants\xe2\x80\x99 salaries. The amount paid varies depending on the services provided, but as a general matter, it \xe2\x80\x9ccorresponds to the amount the State would expect to pay for the nursing care component of institutionalization if the individual chose institutionalization.\xe2\x80\x9d 89 Ill. Admin. Code \xc2\xa7679.50(a).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Other than providing compensation, the State\xe2\x80\x99s role is comparatively small. The State sets some basic threshold qualifications for employment. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7686.10(h)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(10).[2] (For example, a personal assistant must have a Social Security number, must possess basic communication skills, and must complete an employment agreement with the customer. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7686.10, 686.20, 686.40.) The State mandates an annual performance review by the customer, helps the customer conduct that review, and mediates disagreements between customers and their personal assistants. \xc2\xa7686.30. The State suggests certain duties that personal assistants should assume, such as performing \xe2\x80\x9chousehold tasks,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cshopping,\xe2\x80\x9d providing \xe2\x80\x9cpersonal care,\xe2\x80\x9d performing \xe2\x80\x9cincidental health care tasks,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cmonitoring to ensure the health and safety of the cus-tomer.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7686.20. In addition, a state employee must \xe2\x80\x9cidentify the appropriate level of service provider\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cbased on the customer\xe2\x80\x99s approval of the initial Service Plan,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7684.20(a) (emphasis added), and must sign each customer\xe2\x80\x99s Service Plan. \xc2\xa7684.10.

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Section 6 of the Illinois Public Labor Relations Act (PLRA) authorizes state employees to join labor unions and to bargain collectively on the terms and conditions of employment. Ill. Comp. Stat., ch. 5, \xc2\xa7315/6(a). This law applies to \xe2\x80\x9c[e]mployees of the State and any political subdivision of the State,\xe2\x80\x9d subject to certain exceptions, and it provides for a union to be recognized if it is \xe2\x80\x9cdesignated by the [Public Labor Relations] Board as the representative of the majority of public employees in an appropriate unit .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7315/6(a), (c).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The PLRA contains an agency-fee provision, i.e., a provision under which members of a bargaining unit who do not wish to join the union are nevertheless required to pay a fee to the union. See Workers v. Mobil Oil Corp.,\n\n426 U.\xc2\xa0S. 407,\n409, n.\n1 (1976)\n. Labeled a \xe2\x80\x9cfair share\xe2\x80\x9d provision, this section of the PLRA provides: \xe2\x80\x9cWhen a collective bargaining agreement is entered into with an exclusive representative, it may include in the agreement a provision requiring employees covered by the agreement who are not members of the organization to pay their proportionate share of the costs of the collective-bargaining process, contract administration and pursuing matters affecting wages, hours and conditions of employment.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7315/6(e). This payment is \xe2\x80\x9cdeducted by the employer from the earnings of the nonmember employees and paid to the em-ployee organization.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In the 1980\xe2\x80\x99s, the Service Employees International Union (SEIU) petitioned the Illinois Labor Relations Board for permission to represent personal assistants employed by customers in the Rehabilitation Program, but the board rebuffed this effort. Illinois Dept. of Central Management Servs., supra, at VIII\xe2\x80\x9330. The board concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cit is clear .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that [Illinois] does not exercise the type of control over the petitioned-for employees necessary to be considered, in the collective bargaining context envisioned by the [PLRA], their \xe2\x80\x98employer\xe2\x80\x99 or, at least, their sole employer.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In March 2003, however, Illinois\xe2\x80\x99 newly elected Governor, Rod Blagojevich, circumvented this decision by issuing Executive Order 2003\xe2\x80\x9308. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 45a\xe2\x80\x9347a. The order noted the Illinois Labor Relations Board decision but nevertheless called for state recognition of a union as the personal assistants\xe2\x80\x99 exclusive representative for the purpose of collective bargaining with the State. This was necessary, Gov. Blagojevich declared, so that the State could \xe2\x80\x9creceive feedback from the personal assistants in order to effectively and efficiently deliver home services.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 46a. Without such representation, the Governor proclaimed, personal assistants \xe2\x80\x9ccannot effectively voice their concerns about the organization of the Home Services program, their role in the program, or the terms and conditions of their employment under the Program.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Several months later, the Illinois Legislature codified that executive order by amending the PLRA. Pub. Act no. 93\xe2\x80\x93204, \xc2\xa75, 2003 Ill. Laws p. 1930. While acknowledging \xe2\x80\x9cthe right of the persons receiving services .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to hire and fire personal assistants or supervise them,\xe2\x80\x9d the Act declared personal assistants to be \xe2\x80\x9cpublic employees\xe2\x80\x9d of the State of Illinois\xe2\x80\x94but \xe2\x80\x9c[s]olely for the purposes of coverage under the Illinois Public Labor Relations Act.\xe2\x80\x9d Ill. Comp. Stat., ch. 20, \xc2\xa72405/3(f\xc2\xa0). The statute emphasized that personal assistants are not state employees for any other purpose, \xe2\x80\x9cincluding but not limited to, purposes of vicarious liability in tort and purposes of statutory retirement or health insurance benefits.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Following a vote, SEIU Healthcare Illinois & Indiana (SEIU\xe2\x80\x93HII) was designated as the personal assistants\xe2\x80\x99 exclusive representative for purposes of collective bargaining. See App. 23. The union and the State subsequently entered into collective-bargaining agreements that require all personal assistants who are not union members to pay a \xe2\x80\x9cfair share\xe2\x80\x9d of the union dues. Id., at 24\xe2\x80\x9325. These payments are deducted directly from the personal assistants\xe2\x80\x99 Medicaid payments. Ibid. The record in this case shows that each year, personal assistants in Illinois pay SEIU\xe2\x80\x93HII more than $3.6 million in fees. Id., at 25.

\n

C

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Three of the petitioners in the case now before us\xe2\x80\x94Theresa Riffey, Susan Watts, and Stephanie Yencer-Price\xe2\x80\x94are personal assistants under the Rehabilitation Program. They all provide in-home services to family members or other individuals suffering from disabilities.[3] Susan Watts, for example, serves as personal assistant for her daughter, who requires constant care due to quadriplegic cerebral palsy and other conditions. See App. 18.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In 2010, these petitioners filed a putative class action on behalf of all Rehabilitation Program personal assistants in the United States District Court for the Northern District of Illinois. See 656 F.\xc2\xa03d 692, 696 (CA7 2011). Their complaint, which named the Governor and the union as defendants, sought an injunction against enforcement of the fair-share provision and a declaration that the Illinois PLRA violates the\n \nFirst Amendment insofar as it requires personal assistants to pay a fee to a union that they do not wish to support. Ibid.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The District Court dismissed their claims with prejudice, and the Seventh Circuit affirmed in relevant part, concluding that the case was controlled by this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Abood v. Detroit Bd. of Ed.\n\n431 U.\xc2\xa0S. 209 (1977)\n. 656 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 698. The Seventh Circuit held that Illinois and the customers who receive in-home care are \xe2\x80\x9cjoint employers\xe2\x80\x9d of the personal assistants, and the court stated that it had \xe2\x80\x9cno difficulty concluding that the State employs personal assistants within the meaning of Abood.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Petitioners sought certiorari. Their petition pointed out that other States were following Illinois\xe2\x80\x99 lead by enacting laws or issuing executive orders that deem personal assistants to be state employees for the purpose of unionization and the assessment of fair-share fees. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 22a. Petitioners also noted that Illinois has enacted a law that deems \xe2\x80\x9cindividual maintenance home health workers\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94a category that includes registered nurses, licensed practical nurses, and certain therapists who work in private homes\xe2\x80\x94to be \xe2\x80\x9cpublic employees\xe2\x80\x9d for similar purposes. Ill. Pub. Act no. 97\xe2\x80\x931158, 2012 Ill. Laws p. 7823.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In light of the important\n \nFirst Amendment questions these laws raise, we granted certiorari. 570 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2013).

\n

II

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In upholding the constitutionality of the Illinois law, the Seventh Circuit relied on this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Abood supra, which held that state employees who choose not to join a public-sector union may nevertheless be compelled to pay an agency fee to support union work that is related to the collective-bargaining process. Id., at 235\xe2\x80\x93236. Two Terms ago, in Knox v. Service Employees, 567 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012), we pointed out that Abood is \xe2\x80\x9csomething of an anomaly.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 11). \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98The primary purpose\xe2\x80\x99 of permitting unions to collect fees from nonmembers,\xe2\x80\x9d we noted, \xe2\x80\x9cis \xe2\x80\x98to prevent nonmembers from free-riding on the union\xe2\x80\x99s efforts, sharing the employment benefits obtained by the union\xe2\x80\x99s collective bargaining without sharing the costs incurred.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 10) (quoting Davenport v. Washington Ed. Assn.,\n\n551 U.\xc2\xa0S. 177,\n181 (2007)\n). But \xe2\x80\x9c[s]uch free-rider arguments .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. are generally insufficient to overcome\n \nFirst Amendment objections.\xe2\x80\x9d 567 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 10\xe2\x80\x9311).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n For this reason, Abood stands out, but the State of Illinois now asks us to sanction what amounts to a very significant expansion of Abood\xe2\x80\x94so that it applies, not just to full-fledged public employees, but also to others who are deemed to be public employees solely for the purpose of unionization and the collection of an agency fee. Faced with this argument, we begin by examining the path that led to this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Abood.

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The starting point was Railway Employes v. Hanson,\n\n351 U.\xc2\xa0S. 225 (1956)\n, a case in which the\n \nFirst Amendment was barely mentioned. The dispute in Hanson resulted from an amendment to the Railway Labor Act (RLA). Id., at 229, 232. As originally enacted in 1926, the Act did not permit a collective-bargaining agreement to require employees to join or make any payments to a union. See Machinists v. Street,\n\n367 U.\xc2\xa0S. 740,\n750 (1961)\n. At that time and for many years thereafter, there was \xe2\x80\x9ca strong and long-standing tradition of voluntary unionism on the part of the standard rail unions.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Eventually, however, the view of the unions changed. See id., at 760\xe2\x80\x93761. The RLA\xe2\x80\x99s framework for resolving labor disputes \xe2\x80\x9cis more complex than that of any other industry,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 755, and amendments enacted in 1934 increased the financial burden on unions by creating the 36-member National Railroad Adjustment Board, one-half of whose members were appointed and paid by the unions. Id., at 759\xe2\x80\x93760. In seeking authorization to enter into union-shop agreements, i.e., agreements requiring all employees to join a union and thus pay union dues, see Oil Workers, 426 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 409, n.\xc2\xa01, the unions\xe2\x80\x99 principal argument \xe2\x80\x9cwas based on their role in this regulatory framework.\xe2\x80\x9d Street, 367 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 761. A union spokesman argued that the financial burdens resulting from the Act\xe2\x80\x99s unique and complex scheme justified union-shop provisions in order to provide the unions with needed dues. Ibid.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n These arguments were successful, and the Act was amended in 1951 to permit a railroad and a union to enter into an agreement containing a union-shop provision. This amendment brought the Act into conflict with the laws of States that guaranteed the \xe2\x80\x9cright to work\xe2\x80\x9d and thereby outlawed the union shop. Nebraska, the setting of Hanson, was one such State. 351 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 228.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In Hanson, the Union Pacific Railroad Company and its unionized workers entered into a collective-bargaining agreement that contained a provision requiring employees, \xe2\x80\x9cas a condition of their continued employment,\xe2\x80\x9d to join and remain members of the union. Id., at 227. Employees who did not want to join the union brought suit in state court, contending that the union-shop provision violated a provision of the Nebraska Constitution banning adverse employment actions \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98because of refusal to join or affiliate with a labor organization.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 228 (quoting Neb. Const., Art. XV, \xc2\xa713). The employer countered that the RLA trumped the Nebraska provision, but the Nebraska courts agreed with the employees and struck down the union-shop agreement.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n When the case reached this Court, the primary issue was whether the provision of the RLA that authorized union-shop agreements was \xe2\x80\x9cgermane to the exercise of power under the Commerce Clause.\xe2\x80\x9d 351 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 234\xe2\x80\x93235. In an opinion by Justice Douglas, the Court held that this provision represented a permissible regulation of commerce. The Court reasoned that the challenged provision \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98stabilized labor-management relations\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and thus furthered \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98industrial peace.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 233\xe2\x80\x93234.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The employees also raised what amounted to a facial constitutional challenge to the same provision of the RLA. The employees claimed that a \xe2\x80\x9cunion shop agreement forces men into ideological and political associations which violate their right to freedom of conscience, freedom of association, and freedom of thought protected by the Bill of Rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 236. But because the lawsuit had been filed shortly after the collective-bargaining agreement was approved, the record contained no evidence that the union had actually engaged in political or ideological activities.[4]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Hanson Court dismissed the objecting employees\xe2\x80\x99\n \nFirst Amendment argument with a single sentence. The Court wrote: \xe2\x80\x9cOn the present record, there is no more an infringement or impairment of\n \nFirst Amendment rights than there would be in the case of a lawyer who by state law is required to be a member of an integrated bar.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 238.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This explanation was remarkable for two reasons. First, the Court had never previously held that compulsory membership in and the payment of dues to an integrated bar was constitutional, and the constitutionality of such a requirement was hardly a foregone conclusion. Indeed, that issue did not reach the Court until five years later, and it produced a plurality opinion and four separate writings. See Lathrop v. Donohue,\n\n367 U.\xc2\xa0S. 820 (1961)\n (plurality opinion).[5]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Second, in his Lathrop dissent, Justice Douglas, the author of Hanson, came to the conclusion that the\n \nFirst Amendment did not permit compulsory membership in an integrated bar. See 367 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 878\xe2\x80\x93880. The analogy drawn in Hanson, he wrote, fails. \xe2\x80\x9cOnce we approve this measure,\xe2\x80\x9d he warned, \xe2\x80\x9cwe sanction a device where men and women in almost any profession or calling can be at least partially regimented behind causes which they oppose.\xe2\x80\x9d 367 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 884. He continued:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cI look on the Hanson case as a narrow exception to be closely confined. Unless we so treat it, we practically give carte blanche to any legislature to put at least professional people into goose-stepping brigades. Those brigades are not compatible with the\n \nFirst Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 884\xe2\x80\x93885 (footnote omitted).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The\n \nFirst Amendment analysis in Hanson was thin, and the Court\xe2\x80\x99s resulting\n \nFirst Amendment holding was narrow. As the Court later noted, \xe2\x80\x9call that was held in Hanson was that [the RLA] was constitutional in its bare authorization of union-shop contracts requiring workers to give \xe2\x80\x98financial support\xe2\x80\x99 to unions legally authorized to act as their collective bargaining agents.\xe2\x80\x9d Street, 367 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 749 (emphasis added). The Court did not suggest that \xe2\x80\x9cindustrial peace\xe2\x80\x9d could justify a law that \xe2\x80\x9cforces men into ideological and political associations which violate their right to freedom of conscience, freedom of association, and freedom of thought,\xe2\x80\x9d or a law that forces a person to \xe2\x80\x9cconform to [a union\xe2\x80\x99s] ideology.\xe2\x80\x9d Hanson, supra, at 236\xe2\x80\x93237. The RLA did not compel such results, and the record in Hanson did not show that this had occurred.

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Five years later, in Street, supra, the Court considered another case in which workers objected to a union shop. Employees of the Southern Railway System raised a\n \nFirst Amendment challenge, contending that a substantial part of the money that they were required to pay to the union was used to support political candidates and causes with which they disagreed. A Georgia court enjoined the enforcement of the union-shop provision and entered judgment for the dissenting employees in the amount of the payments that they had been forced to make to the union. The Georgia Supreme Court affirmed. Id., at 742\xe2\x80\x93745.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Reviewing the State Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision, this Court recognized that the case presented constitutional questions \xe2\x80\x9cof the utmost gravity,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 749, but the Court found it unnecessary to reach those questions. Instead, the Court construed the RLA \xe2\x80\x9cas not vesting the unions with unlimited power to spend exacted money.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 768. Specifically, the Court held, the Act \xe2\x80\x9cis to be construed to deny the unions, over an employee\xe2\x80\x99s objection, the power to use his exacted funds to support political causes which he opposes.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 768\xe2\x80\x93769.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Having construed the RLA to contain this restriction, the Street Court then went on to discuss the remedies available for employees who objected to the use of union funds for political causes. The Court suggested two: The dissenting employees could be given a refund of the portion of their dues spent by the union for political or ideological purposes, or they could be given a refund of the portion spent on those political purposes that they had advised the union they disapproved.[6] Id., at 774\xe2\x80\x93775.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Justice Black, writing in dissent, objected to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s suggested remedies, and he accurately predicted that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s approach would lead to serious practical problems. Id., at 796\xe2\x80\x93797. That approach, he wrote, while \xe2\x80\x9cvery lucrative to special masters, accountants and lawyers,\xe2\x80\x9d would do little for \xe2\x80\x9cthe individual workers whose\n \nFirst Amendment freedoms have been flagrantly violated.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 796. He concluded:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cUnions composed of a voluntary membership, like all other voluntary groups, should be free in this country to fight in the public forum to advance their own causes, to promote their choice of candidates and parties and to work for the doctrines or the laws they favor. But to the extent that Government steps in to force people to help espouse the particular causes of a group, that group\xe2\x80\x94whether composed of railroad workers or lawyers\xe2\x80\x94loses its status as a voluntary group.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Justice Frankfurter, joined by Justice Harlan, also dissented, arguing that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s remedy was conceptually flawed because a union may further the objectives of members by political means. See id., at 813\xe2\x80\x93815. He noted, for example, that reports from the AFL\xe2\x80\x93CIO Executive Council \xe2\x80\x9cemphasize that labor\xe2\x80\x99s participation in urging legislation and candidacies is a major one.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 813. In light of \xe2\x80\x9cthe detailed list of national and international problems on which the AFL\xe2\x80\x93CIO speaks,\xe2\x80\x9d he opined, \xe2\x80\x9cit seems rather naive\xe2\x80\x9d to believe \xe2\x80\x9cthat economic and political concerns are separable.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 814.

\n

C

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This brings us to Abood, which, unlike Hanson and Street, involved a public-sector collective-bargaining agreement. The Detroit Federation of Teachers served \xe2\x80\x9cas the exclusive representative of teachers employed by the Detroit Board of Education.\xe2\x80\x9d 431 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 211\xe2\x80\x93212. The collective-bargaining agreement between the union and the board contained an agency-shop clause requiring every teacher to \xe2\x80\x9cpay the Union a service charge equal to the regular dues required of Union members.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 212. A putative class of teachers sued to invalidate this clause. Asserting that \xe2\x80\x9cthey opposed collective bargaining in the public sector,\xe2\x80\x9d the plaintiffs argued that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a substantial part\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of their dues would be used to fund union \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98activities and programs which are economic, political, professional, scientific and religious in nature of which Plaintiffs do not approve, and in which they will have no voice.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 212\xe2\x80\x93213.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This Court treated the\n \nFirst Amendment issue as largely settled by Hanson and Street. 431 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 217, 223.The Court acknowledged that Street was resolved as a matter of statutory construction without reaching any constitutional issues, 431 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 220, and the Court recognized that forced membership and forced contributions impinge on free speech and associational rights, id., at 223. But the Court dismissed the objecting teachers\xe2\x80\x99 constitutional arguments with this observation: \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he judgment clearly made in Hanson and Street is that such interference as exists is constitutionally justified by the legislative assessment of the important contribution of the union shop to the system of labor relations established by Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 222.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Abood Court understood Hanson and Street to have upheld union-shop agreements in the private sector based on two primary considerations: the desirability of \xe2\x80\x9clabor peace\xe2\x80\x9d and the problem of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98free riders[hip].\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 431 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 220\xe2\x80\x93222, 224.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court thought that agency-shop provisions promote labor peace because the Court saw a close link between such provisions and the \xe2\x80\x9cprinciple of exclusive union representation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 220. This principle, the Court explained, \xe2\x80\x9cprevents inter-union rivalries from creating dissension within the work force and eliminating the advantages to the employee of collectivization.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 220\xe2\x80\x93221. In addition, the Court noted, the \xe2\x80\x9cdesignation of a single representative avoids the confusion that would result from attempting to enforce two or more agreements specifying different terms and conditions of employment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 220. And the Court pointed out that exclusive representation \xe2\x80\x9cfrees the employer from the possibility of facing conflicting demands from different unions, and permits the employer and a single union to reach agreements and settlements that are not subject to attack from rival labor organizations.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 221.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Turning to the problem of free ridership, Abood noted that a union must \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98fairly and equitably .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. represent all employees\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d regardless of union membership, and the Court wrote as follows: The \xe2\x80\x9cunion-shop arrangement has been thought to distribute fairly the cost of these activities among those who benefit, and it counteracts the incentive that employees might otherwise have to become \xe2\x80\x98free riders\xe2\x80\x99 to refuse to contribute to the union while obtaining benefits of union representation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 221\xe2\x80\x93222.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The plaintiffs in Abood argued that Hanson and Street should not be given much weight because they did not arise in the public sector, and the Court acknowledged that public-sector bargaining is different from private-sector bargaining in some notable respects. 431 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 227\xe2\x80\x93228. For example, although public and private employers both desire to keep costs down, the Court recognized that a public employer \xe2\x80\x9clacks an important discipline against agreeing to increases in labor costs that in a market system would require price increases.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 228. The Court also noted that \xe2\x80\x9cdecisionmaking by a public employer is above all a political process\xe2\x80\x9d undertaken by people \xe2\x80\x9cultimately responsible to the electorate.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Thus, whether a public employer accedes to a union\xe2\x80\x99s demands, the Court wrote, \xe2\x80\x9cwill depend upon a blend of political ingredients,\xe2\x80\x9d thereby giving public employees \xe2\x80\x9cmore influence in the decisionmaking process that is possessed by employees similarly organized in the private sector.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. But despite these acknowledged differences between private- and public-sector bargaining, the Court treated Hanson and Street as essentially controlling.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Instead of drawing a line between the private and public sectors, the Abood Court drew a line between, on the one hand, a union\xe2\x80\x99s expenditures for \xe2\x80\x9ccollective-bargaining, contract administration, and grievance-adjustment purposes,\xe2\x80\x9d 431 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 232, and, on the other, expenditures for political or ideological purposes. Id., at 236.

\n

D

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Abood Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis is questionable on several grounds. Some of these were noted or apparent at or before the time of the decision, but several have become more evident and troubling in the years since then.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Abood Court seriously erred in treating Hanson and Street as having all but decided the constitutionality of compulsory payments to a public-sector union. As we have explained, Street was not a constitutional decision at all, and Hanson disposed of the critical question in a single, unsupported sentence that its author essentially abandoned a few years later. Surely a\n \nFirst Amendment issue of this importance deserved better treatment.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Abood Court fundamentally misunderstood the holding in Hanson, which was really quite narrow. As the Court made clear in Street, \xe2\x80\x9call that was held in Hanson was that [the RLA] was constitutional in its bare authorization of union-shop contracts requiring workers to give \xe2\x80\x98financial support\xe2\x80\x99 to unions legally authorized to act as their collective bargaining agents.\xe2\x80\x9d 367 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 749 (emphasis added). In Abood, on the other hand, the State of Michigan did more than simply authorize the imposition of an agency fee. A state instrumentality, the Detroit Board of Education, actually imposed that fee. This presented a very different question.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Abood failed to appreciate the difference between the core union speech involuntarily subsidized by dissenting public-sector employees and the core union speech involuntarily funded by their counterparts in the private sector. In the public sector, core issues such as wages, pensions, and benefits are important political issues, but that is generally not so in the private sector. In the years since Abood, as state and local expenditures on employee wages and benefits have mushroomed, the importance of the difference between bargaining in the public and private sectors has been driven home.[7]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Abood failed to appreciate the conceptual difficulty of distinguishing in public-sector cases between union expenditures that are made for collective-bargaining pur-poses and those that are made to achieve political ends. In the private sector, the line is easier to see. Collective bargaining concerns the union\xe2\x80\x99s dealings with the em-ployer; political advocacy and lobbying are directed at the government. But in the public sector, both collective-bargaining and political advocacy and lobbying are directed at the government.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Abood does not seem to have anticipated the magnitude of the practical administrative problems that would result in attempting to classify public-sector union expenditures as either \xe2\x80\x9cchargeable\xe2\x80\x9d (in Abood\xe2\x80\x99s terms, expendituresfor \xe2\x80\x9ccollective-bargaining, contract administration, andgrievance-adjustment purposes,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 232) or noncharge-able (i.e., expenditures for political or ideological purposes, id., at 236). In the years since Abood, the Court has strug-gled repeatedly with this issue. See Ellis v. Railway Clerks,\n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 435 (1984)\n; Teachers v. Hudson,\n\n475 U.\xc2\xa0S. 292 (1986)\n; Lehnert v. Ferris Faculty Assn.,\n\n500 U.\xc2\xa0S. 507 (1991)\n; Locke v. Karass,\n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 207 (2009)\n. In Lehnert, the Court held that \xe2\x80\x9cchargeable activities must (1) be \xe2\x80\x98germane\xe2\x80\x99 to collective-bargaining activity; (2) be justified by the government\xe2\x80\x99s vital policy interest in labor peace and avoiding \xe2\x80\x98free riders\xe2\x80\x99; and (3) not significantly add to the burdening of free speech that is inherent in the allowance of an agency or union shop.\xe2\x80\x9d 500 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 519. But as noted in Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s dissent in that case, \xe2\x80\x9ceach one of the three \xe2\x80\x98prongs\xe2\x80\x99 of the test involves a substantial judgment call (What is \xe2\x80\x98germane\xe2\x80\x99? What is \xe2\x80\x98justified\xe2\x80\x99? What is a \xe2\x80\x98significant\xe2\x80\x99 additional burden).\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 551 (opinion concurring in judgment in part and dissenting in part).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Abood likewise did not foresee the practical problems that would face objecting nonmembers. Employees who suspect that a union has improperly put certain expenses in the \xe2\x80\x9cgermane\xe2\x80\x9d category must bear a heavy burden if they wish to challenge the union\xe2\x80\x99s actions. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he onus is on the employees to come up with the resources to mount the legal challenge in a timely fashion,\xe2\x80\x9d Knox, 567 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 19) (citing Lehnert, supra, at 513), and litigating such cases is expensive. Because of the open-ended nature of the Lehnert test, classifying particular categories of expenses may not be straightforward. See Jibson v. Michigan Ed. Assn.\xe2\x80\x93NEA, 30 F.\xc2\xa03d 723, 730 (CA6 1994)). And although Hudson required that a union\xe2\x80\x99s books be audited, auditors do not themselves review the correctness of a union\xe2\x80\x99s categorization. See Knox, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 18\xe2\x80\x9319) (citing Andrews v. Education Assn. of Cheshire, 829 F.\xc2\xa02d 335, 340 (CA2 1987)). See also American Federation of Television and Recording Artists, Portland Local, 327 N.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0B. 474, 477 (1999) (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is settled that determinations concerning whether particular expenditures are chargeable are legal determinations which are outside the expertise of the auditor. Thus, as we have stated, the function of the auditor is to verify that the expenditures that the union claims it made were in fact made for the purposes claimed, not to pass on the correctness of the union\xe2\x80\x99s allocation of expenditures to the chargeable and nonchargeable categories\xe2\x80\x9d); California Saw and Knife Works, 320 N.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0B. 224, 241 (1995) (\xe2\x80\x9cWe first agree [that the company at issue] did not violate its duty of fair representation by failing to use an independent auditor to determine the allocation of chargeable and nonchargeable expenditures\xe2\x80\x9d); Price v. International Union, United Auto, Aerospace & Agricultural Implement Workers of Am., 927 F.\xc2\xa02d 88, 93\xe2\x80\x9394 (CA2 1991) (\xe2\x80\x9cHudson requires only that the usual function of an auditor be performed, i.e., to determine that the expenses claimed were in fact made. That function does not require that the auditor make a legal decision as to the appropriateness of the allocation of expenses to the chargeable and non-chargeable categories\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Finally, a critical pillar of the Abood Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis rests on an unsupported empirical assumption, namely, that the principle of exclusive representation in the public sector is dependent on a union or agency shop. As we will explain, see infra, at 31\xe2\x80\x9334, this assumption is unwarranted.

\n

III

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Despite all this, the State of Illinois now asks us to approve a very substantial expansion of Abood\xe2\x80\x99s reach. Abood involved full-fledged public employees, but in this case, the status of the personal assistants is much different. The Illinois Legislature has taken pains to specify that personal assistants are public employees for one purpose only: collective bargaining. For all other pur-poses, Illinois regards the personal assistants as private-sector employees. This approach has important practical consequences.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n For one thing, the State\xe2\x80\x99s authority with respect to these two groups is vastly different. In the case of full-fledged public employees, the State establishes all of the duties imposed on each employee, as well as all of the qualifications needed for each position. The State vets applicants and chooses the employees to be hired. The State provides or arranges for whatever training is needed, and it supervises and evaluates the employees\xe2\x80\x99 job performance and imposes corrective measures if appropriate. If a state employee\xe2\x80\x99s performance is deficient, the State may discharge the employee in accordance with whatever procedures are required by law.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n With respect to the personal assistants involved in this case, the picture is entirely changed. The job duties of personal assistants are specified in their individualized Service Plans, which must be approved by the customer and the customer\xe2\x80\x99s physician. 89 Ill. Admin. Code \xc2\xa7684.10. Customers have complete discretion to hire any personal assistant who meets the meager basic qualifications that the State prescribes in \xc2\xa7686.10. See \xc2\xa7676.30(b) (the customer \xe2\x80\x9cis responsible for controlling all aspects of the employment relationship between the customer and the [personal assistant], including, without limitation, locating and hiring the [personal assistant]\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); \xc2\xa7684.20(b) (\xe2\x80\x9ccomplete discretion in which Per-sonal Assistant [the customer] wishes to hire\xe2\x80\x9d subject to baseline eligibility requirements).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Customers supervise their personal assistants on a daily basis, and no provision of the Illinois statute or implementing regulations gives the State the right to enter the home in which the personal assistant is employed for the purpose of checking on the personal assistant\xe2\x80\x99s job performance. Cf. \xc2\xa7676.20(b) (customer controls \xe2\x80\x9cwithout limitation .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. supervising the work performed by the [personal assistant], imposing .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. disciplinary action against the [personal assistant]\xe2\x80\x9d). And while state law mandates an annual review of each personal assistant\xe2\x80\x99s work, that evaluation is also controlled by the customer. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7686.10(k), 686.30. A state counselor is assigned to assist the customer in performing the review but has no power to override the customer\xe2\x80\x99s evaluation. See ibid. Nor do the regulations empower the State to discharge a personal assistant for substandard performance. See n.\xc2\xa01, supra. Discharge, like hiring, is entirely in the hands of the customer. See \xc2\xa7676.30.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Consistent with this scheme, under which personal assistants are almost entirely answerable to the customers and not to the State, Illinois withholds from personal assistants most of the rights and benefits enjoyed by full-fledged state employees. As we have noted already, state law explicitly excludes personal assistants from statutory retirement and health insurance benefits. Ill. Comp. Stat., ch. 20, \xc2\xa72405/3(f\xc2\xa0). It also excludes personal assistants from group life insurance and certain other employee benefits provided under the State Employees Group Insurance Act of 1971. Ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9cPersonal assistants shall not be covered by the State Employees Group Insurance Act of 1971\xe2\x80\x9d). And the State \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not provide paid vacation, holiday, or sick leave\xe2\x80\x9d to personal assistants. 89 Ill. Admin. Code \xc2\xa7686.10(h)(7).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Personal assistants also appear to be ineligible for a host of benefits under a variety of other state laws, including the State Employee Vacation Time Act (see Ill. Stat., ch.\xc2\xa05, \xc2\xa7360/1); the State Employee Health Savings Account Law (see Ill. Stat., ch.\xc2\xa05, \xc2\xa7377/10\xe2\x80\x931); the State Employee Job Sharing Act (see Ill. Stat., ch.\xc2\xa05, \xc2\xa7380/0.01); the State Employee Indemnification Act (see Ill. Stat., ch.\xc2\xa05, \xc2\xa7350/2); and the Sick Leave Bank Act. See Ill. Stat., ch.\xc2\xa05, \xc2\xa7400/1. Personal assistants are apparently not entitled to the protection that the Illinois Whistleblower Act provides for full-fledged state employees. See Ill. Stat., ch. 740, \xc2\xa7174/1. And it likewise appears that personal assistants are shut out of many other state employee programs and benefits. The Illinois Department of Central Management Services lists many such programs and benefits, including a deferred compensation program, full worker\xe2\x80\x99s compensation privileges,[8] behavioral health programs, a program that allows state employees to retain health insurance for a time after leaving state employment, a commuter savings program, dental and vision programs, and a flexible spending program.[9] All of these programs and benefits appear to fall within the provision of the Rehabilitation Program declaring that personal assistants are not state employees for \xe2\x80\x9cany purposes\xe2\x80\x9d other than collective bargaining. See Ill. Comp. Stat., ch.\xc2\xa020, \xc2\xa72405/3(f\xc2\xa0).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Just as the State denies personal assistants most of the rights and benefits enjoyed by full-fledged state workers, the State does not assume responsibility for actions taken by personal assistants during the course of their employment. The governing statute explicitly disclaims \xe2\x80\x9cvicarious liability in tort.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. So if a personal assistant steals from a customer, neglects a customer, or abuses a customer, the State washes its hands.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Illinois deems personal assistants to be state employees for one purpose only, collective bargaining,[10] but the scope of bargaining that may be conducted on their behalf is sharply limited. Under the governing Illinois statute, collective bargaining can occur only for \xe2\x80\x9cterms and conditions of employment that are within the State\xe2\x80\x99s control.\xe2\x80\x9d Ill. Comp. Stat., ch. 20, \xc2\xa72405/3(f\xc2\xa0). That is not very much.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n As an illustration, consider the subjects of mandatory bargaining under federal and state labor law that are out

\n

of bounds when it comes to personal assistants. Under federal law, mandatory subjects include the days of the week and the hours of the day during which an employee must work,[11] lunch breaks,[12] holidays,[13] vacations,[14] termination of employment,[15] and changes in job duties.[16] Illinois law similarly makes subject to mandatory collective-bargaining decisions concerning the \xe2\x80\x9chours and terms and conditions of employment.\xe2\x80\x9d Belvidere v. Illinois State Labor Relations Bd., 181 Ill. 2d 191, 201, 692 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 295, 301 (1998); see also, e.g., Aurora Sergeants Assn., 24 PERI \xc2\xb625 (2008) (holding that days of the week worked by police officers is subject to mandatory collective bargaining). But under the Rehabilitation Program, all these topics are governed by the Service Plan, with respect to which the union has no role. See \xc2\xa7676.30(b) (the customer \xe2\x80\x9cis responsible for controlling all aspects of the employment relationship between the customer and the PA, including, without limitation, locating and hiring the PA, training the PA, directing, evaluating, and otherwise supervising the work performed by the PA, imposing .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. disciplinary action against the PA, and terminating the employment relationship between the customer and the PA\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa7684.50 (the Service Plan must specify \xe2\x80\x9cthe frequency with which the specific tasks are to be provided\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cthe number of hours each task is to be provided per month\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

B

\n

1

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The unusual status of personal assistants has important implications for present purposes. Abood\xe2\x80\x99s rationale, whatever its strengths and weaknesses, is based on the assumption that the union possesses the full scope of powers and duties generally available under American labor law. Under the Illinois scheme now before us, however, the union\xe2\x80\x99s powers and duties are sharply circumscribed, and as a result, even the best argument for the \xe2\x80\x9cextraordinary power\xe2\x80\x9d that Abood allows a union to wield, see Davenport, 551 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 184, is a poor fit.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In our post-Abood cases involving public-sector agency-fee issues, Abood has been a given, and our task has been to attempt to understand its rationale and to apply it in a way that is consistent with that rationale. In that vein, Abood\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning has been described as follows. The mere fact that nonunion members benefit from union speech is not enough to justify an agency fee because \xe2\x80\x9cprivate speech often furthers the interests of nonspeakers, and that does not alone empower the state to compel the speech to be paid for.\xe2\x80\x9d Lehnert, 500 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 556 (opinion of Scalia, J.). What justifies the agency fee, the argument goes, is the fact that the State compels the union to promote and protect the interests of nonmembers. Ibid. Specifically, the union must not discriminate between members and nonmembers in \xe2\x80\x9cnegotiating and administering a collective-bargaining agreement and representing the interests of employees in settling disputes and processing grievances.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. This means that the union \xe2\x80\x9ccannot, for example, negotiate particularly high wage increases for its members in exchange for accepting no increases for others.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. And it has the duty to provide equal and effective representation for nonmembers in grievance proceedings, see Ill. Comp. Stat. Ann., ch. 5, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7315/6, 315/8, an undertaking that can be very involved. See, e.g., SEIU: Member Resources, available at www.seiu.or\xc2\xa0/a/members\xc2\xa0/disputes-and-grievances-rights-procedures-and-best-practices.php (detailing the steps involved in adjusting grievances).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This argument has little force in the situation now before us. Illinois law specifies that personal assistants \xe2\x80\x9cshall be paid at the hourly rate set by law,\xe2\x80\x9d see 89 Ill. Admin. Code \xc2\xa7686.40(a), and therefore the union cannot be in the position of having to sacrifice higher pay for its members in order to protect the nonmembers whom it is obligated to represent. And as for the adjustment of grievances, the union\xe2\x80\x99s authority and responsibilities are narrow, as we have seen. The union has no authority with respect to any grievances that a personal assistant may have with a customer, and the customer has virtually complete control over a personal assistant\xe2\x80\x99s work.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The union\xe2\x80\x99s limited authority in this area has important practical implications. Suppose, for example that a customer fires a personal assistant because the customer wrongly believes that the assistant stole a fork. Or suppose that a personal assistant is discharged because the assistant shows no interest in the customer\xe2\x80\x99s favorite daytime soaps. Can the union file a grievance on behalf of the assistant? The answer is no.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n It is true that Illinois law requires a collective-bargaining agreement to \xe2\x80\x9ccontain a grievance resolution procedure which shall apply to all employees in the bargaining unit,\xe2\x80\x9d Ill. Comp. Stat., ch. 5, \xc2\xa7315/8, but in the situation here, this procedure appears to relate solely to any grievance that a personal assistant may have with the State,[17] not with the customer for whom the personal assistant works.[18]

\n

2

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Because of Abood\xe2\x80\x99s questionable foundations, and because the personal assistants are quite different from full-fledged public employees, we refuse to extend Abood to the new situation now before us.[19] Abood itself has clear boundaries; it applies to public employees. Extending those boundaries to encompass partial-public employees, quasi-public employees, or simply private employees would invite problems. Consider a continuum, ranging, on the one hand, from full-fledged state employees to, on the other hand, individuals who follow a common calling and benefit from advocacy or lobbying conducted by a group to which they do not belong and pay no dues. A State may not force every person who benefits from this group\xe2\x80\x99s efforts to make payments to the group. See Lehnert, 500 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 556 (opinion of Scalia, J.). But what if regulation of this group is increased? What if the Federal Government or a State begins to provide or increases subsidies in this area? At what point, short of the point at which the individuals in question become full-fledged state employees, should Abood apply?

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n If respondents\xe2\x80\x99 and the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s views were adopted, a host of workers who receive payments from a governmental entity for some sort of service would be candidates for inclusion within Abood\xe2\x80\x99s reach. Medicare-funded home health employees may be one such group. See Brief for Petitioners 51;\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71395x(m); 42 CFR \xc2\xa7424.22(a). The same goes for adult foster care providers in Oregon (Ore. Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa7443.733 (2013)) and Washington (Wash. Rev. Code \xc2\xa741.56.029 (2012)) and certain workers under the federal Child Care and Development Fund programs (45 CFR \xc2\xa798.2).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n If we allowed Abood to be extended to those who are not full-fledged public employees, it would be hard to see just where to draw the line,[20] and we therefore confine Abood\xe2\x80\x99s reach to full-fledged state employees.[21]

\n

IV

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Because Abood is not controlling, we must analyze the constitutionality of the payments compelled by Illinois law under generally applicable\n \nFirst Amendment standards. As we explained in Knox, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he government may not prohibit the dissemination of ideas that it disfavors, nor compel the endorsement of ideas that it approves.\xe2\x80\x9d 567 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op. at 8\xe2\x80\x939); see also, e.g., R.A.V. v. St. Paul,\n\n505 U.\xc2\xa0S. 377,\n382 (1992)\n; Riley v. National Federation of Blind of N.C.,\n\n487 U.\xc2\xa0S. 781,\n797 (1988)\n West Virginia Bd. of Ed. v. Barnette,\n\n319 U.\xc2\xa0S. 624 (1943)\n; Wooley v. Maynard,\n\n430 U.\xc2\xa0S. 705\n\xe2\x80\x93715 (1977). And \xe2\x80\x9ccompelled funding of the speech of other private speakers or groups\xe2\x80\x9d presents the same dangers as compelled speech. Knox, supra, at ___ (slip op. at 9). As a result, we explained in Knox that an agency-fee provision imposes \xe2\x80\x9ca \xe2\x80\x98significant impingement on\n \nFirst Amendment rights,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and this cannot be tolerated unless it passes \xe2\x80\x9cexacting\n \nFirst Amendment scrutiny.\xe2\x80\x9d 567 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op. at 9\xe2\x80\x9310).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In Knox, we considered specific features of an agency-shop agreement\xe2\x80\x94allowing a union to impose upon nonmembers a special assessment or dues increase without providing notice and without obtaining the nonmembers\xe2\x80\x99 affirmative agreement\xe2\x80\x94and we held that these features could not even satisfy the standard employed in United States v. United Foods, Inc.,\n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 405,\n415 (2001)\n, where we struck down a provision that compelled the subsidization of commercial speech. We did not suggest, however, that the compelled speech in Knox was like the commercial speech in United Foods. On the contrary, we observed that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he subject of the speech at issue [in United Foods]\xe2\x80\x94promoting the sale of mushrooms\xe2\x80\x94was not one that is likely to stir the passions of many, but the mundane commercial nature of that speech only highlights the importance of our analysis and our holding.\xe2\x80\x9d Knox, supra, at ___ (slip op. at 9).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n While the features of the agency-fee provision in Knox could not meet even the commercial-speech standard employed in United Foods, it is apparent that the speech compelled in this case is not commercial speech. Our precedents define commercial speech as \xe2\x80\x9cspeech that does no more than propose a commercial transaction,\xe2\x80\x9d United Foods, supra, at 409 (citing Virginia Bd. of Pharmacy v. Virginia Citizens Consumer Council, Inc., 425 U.\xc2\xa0S.748, 761\xe2\x80\x93762 (1976)), and the union speech in question in this case does much more than that. As a consequence,it is arguable that the United Foods standard is toopermissive.

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n For present purposes, however, no fine parsing of levels of\n \nFirst Amendment scrutiny is needed because the agency-fee provision here cannot satisfy even the test used in Knox. Specifically, this provision does not serve a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98compelling state interes[t] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that cannot be achieved through means significantly less restrictive of associational freedoms.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Knox, supra, at ___ (slip op. at 10) (quoting Roberts v. United States Jaycees,\n\n468 U.\xc2\xa0S. 609,\n623 (1984)\n). Respondents contend that the agency-fee provision in this case furthers several important interests, but none is sufficient.

\n

1

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Focusing on the benefits of the union\xe2\x80\x99s status as the exclusive bargaining agent for all employees in the unit, respondents argue that the agency-fee provision promotes \xe2\x80\x9clabor peace,\xe2\x80\x9d but their argument largely misses the point. Petitioners do not contend that they have a\n \nFirst Amendment right to form a rival union. Nor do they challenge the authority of the SEIU\xe2\x80\x93HII to serve as the exclusive representative of all the personal assistants in bargaining with the State. All they seek is the right not to beforced to contribute to the union, with which they broadly disagree.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n A union\xe2\x80\x99s status as exclusive bargaining agent and the right to collect an agency fee from non-members are not inextricably linked. For example, employees in some federal agencies may choose a union to serve as the exclusive bargaining agent for the unit, but no employee is required to join the union or to pay any union fee. Under federal law, in agencies in which unionization is permitted, \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ach employee shall have the right to form, join, or assist any labor organization, or to refrain from any such activity, freely and without fear of penalty or reprisal, and each employee shall be protected in the exercise of such right.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77102 (emphasis added).[22]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Moreover, even if the agency fee provision at issue here were tied to the union\xe2\x80\x99s status as exclusive bargaining agents, features of the Illinois scheme would still undermine the argument that the agency fee plays an important role in maintaining labor peace. For one thing, any threat to labor peace is diminished because the personal assistants do not work together in a common state facility but instead spend all their time in private homes, either the customers\xe2\x80\x99 or their own. Cf. Perry Ed. Assn. v. Perry Local Educators\xe2\x80\x99 Assn.,\n\n460 U.\xc2\xa0S. 37,\n51 (1983)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[E]xclusion of the rival union may reasonably be considered a means of insuring labor-peace within the schools\xe2\x80\x9d). Federal labor law reflects the fact that the organization of household workers like the personal assistants does not further the interest of labor peace. \xe2\x80\x9c[A]ny individual employed .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in the domestic service of any family or person at his home\xe2\x80\x9d is excluded from coverage under the National Labor Relations Act. See\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7152(3).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The union\xe2\x80\x99s very restricted role under the Illinois lawis also significant. Since the union is largely limited to petitioning the State for greater pay and benefits, the specter of conflicting demands by personal assistants is lessened. And of course, State officials must deal on a daily basis with conflicting pleas for funding in many contexts.

\n

2

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Respondents also maintain that the agency-fee provision promotes the welfare of personal assistants and thus contributes to the success of the Rehabilitation Program. As a result of unionization, they claim, the wages and benefits of personal assistants have been substantially improved;[23] orientation and training programs, background checks, and a program to deal with lost and erroneous paychecks have been instituted;[24] and a procedure was established to resolve grievances arising under the collective-bargaining agreement (but apparently not grievances relating to a Service Plan or actions taken by a customer).[25]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The thrust of these arguments is that the union has been an effective advocate for personal assistants in the State of Illinois, and we will assume that this is correct. But in order to pass exacting scrutiny, more must be shown. The agency-fee provision cannot be sustained unless the cited benefits for personal assistants could not have been achieved if the union had been required to depend for funding on the dues paid by those personal assistants who chose to join. No such showing has been made.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In claiming that the agency fee was needed to bring about the cited improvements, the State is in a curious position. The State is not like the closed-fisted employer that is bent on minimizing employee wages and benefits and that yields only grudgingly under intense union pressure. As Governor Blagojevich put it in the executive order that first created the Illinois program, the State took the initiative because it was eager for \xe2\x80\x9cfeedback\xe2\x80\x9d regarding the needs and views of the personal assistants. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 46. Thereafter, a majority of the personal assistants voted to unionize. When they did so, they must have realized that this would require the payment of union dues, and therefore it may be presumed that a high percentage of these personal assistants became union members and are willingly paying union dues. Why are these dues insufficient to enable the union to provide \xe2\x80\x9cfeedback\xe2\x80\x9d to a State that is highly receptive to suggestions for increased wages and other improvements? A host of organizations advocate on behalf of the interests of persons falling within an occupational group, and many of these groups are quite successful even though they are dependent on voluntary contributions. Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 showing falls far short of what the\n \nFirst Amendment demands.

\n

V

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Respondents and their supporting amici make two additional arguments that must be addressed.

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n First, respondents and the Solicitor General urge us to apply a balancing test derived from Pickering v. Board of Ed. of Township High School Dist. 205, Will Cty.,\n\n391 U.\xc2\xa0S. 563 (1968)\n. See Brief for Respondent Quinn 25\xe2\x80\x9326; Brief for SEIU\xe2\x80\x93HII 35\xe2\x80\x9336; Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 11. And they claim that under the Picker-ing analysis, the Illinois scheme must be sustained. This argument represents an effort to find a new justification for the decision in Abood, because neither in that case nor in any subsequent related case have we seen Abood as based on Pickering balancing.[26]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In any event, this effort to recast Abood falls short. To begin, the Pickering test is inapplicable because with respect to the personal assistants, the State is not acting in a traditional employer role.[27] But even if it were, application of Pickering would not sustain the agency-feeprovision.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Pickering and later cases in the same line concern the constitutionality of restrictions on speech by public employees. Under those cases, employee speech is unprotected if it is not on a matter of public concern (or is pursuantto an employee\xe2\x80\x99s job duties), but speech on matters of public concern may be restricted only if \xe2\x80\x9cthe interest of the state, as an employer, in promoting the efficiency of the public services it performs through its employees\xe2\x80\x9d outweighs \xe2\x80\x9cthe interests of the [employee], as a citizen, in commenting upon matters of public concern.\xe2\x80\x9d 391 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 568. See also Borough of Duryea v. Guarnieri, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2011); Garcetti v. Ceballos,\n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 410 (2006)\n; Waters v. Churchill,\n\n511 U.\xc2\xa0S. 661,\n674 (1994)\n (plurality opinion); Connick v. Myers,\n\n461 U.\xc2\xa0S. 138 (1983)\n.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Attempting to fit Abood into the Pickering framework, the United States contends that union speech that is germane to collective bargaining does not address matters of public concern and, as a result, is not protected. Taking up this argument, the dissent insists that the speech at issue here is not a matter of public concern. According to the dissent, this is \xe2\x80\x9cthe prosaic stuff of collective bargaining.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 9. Does it have any effect on the public? The dissent\xe2\x80\x99s answer is: \xe2\x80\x9cnot terribly much.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 20. As the dissent sees it, speech about such funding is not qualitatively different from the complaints of a small-town police chief regarding such matters as the denial of $338 in overtime pay or directives concerning the use of police vehicles and smoking in the police station. See post, at 20; Borough of Duryea, supra, at ___ (slip op. at 3).[28]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This argument flies in the face of reality. In this case, for example, the category of union speech that is germane to collective bargaining unquestionably includes speech in favor of increased wages and benefits for personal assistants. Increased wages and benefits for personal assistants would almost certainly mean increased expenditures under the Medicaid program, and it is impossible to argue that the level of Medicaid funding (or, for that matter, state spending for employee benefits in general) is not a matter of great public concern.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In recent years, Medicaid expenditures have represented nearly a quarter of all state expenditures. See National Association of State Budget Officers, Summary: Fall 2013 Fiscal Survey of States (Dec. 10, 2013), online at http:// www.nasbo.org. \xe2\x80\x9cMedicaid has steadily eaten up a growing share of state budgets.\xe2\x80\x9d[29] In fiscal year 2014, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]hirty-five states increased spending for Medicaid for a net increase of $6.8 billion.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Accordingly, speech by a powerful union that relates to the subject of Medicaid funding cannot be equated with the sort of speech that our cases have treated as concerning matters of only private concern. See, e.g., San Diego v. Roe,\n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 77 (2004)\n (per curiam); Connick, supra, at 148 (speech that \xe2\x80\x9creflect[ed] one employee\xe2\x80\x99s dissatisfaction with a transfer and an attempt to turn that displeasure into a cause c\xc3\xa9l\xc3\xa8bre\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n For this reason, if Pickering were to be applied, it would be necessary to proceed to the next step of the analysis prescribed in that case, and this would require an assessment of both the degree to which the agency-fee provision promotes the efficiency of the Rehabilitation Program and the degree to which that provision interferes with the\n \nFirst Amendment interests of those personal assistants who do not wish to support the union.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We need not discuss this analysis at length because it is covered by what we have already said. Agency-fee provisions unquestionably impose a heavy burden on the\n \nFirst Amendment interests of objecting employees. See Knox, 567 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op. at 19) (citing Lehnert, 500 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 513; Jibson v. Michigan Ed. Assn., 30 F.\xc2\xa03d 723, 730 (CA6 1994). And on the other side of the balance, the arguments on which the United States relies\xe2\x80\x94relating to the promotion of labor peace and the problem of free riders\xe2\x80\x94have already been discussed. Thus, even if the permissibility of the agency-shop provision in the collective-bargaining agreement now at issue were analyzed under Pickering, that provision could not be upheld.

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Respondents contend, finally, that a refusal to extend Abood to cover the situation presented in this case will call into question our decisions in Keller v. State Bar of Cal.\n\n496 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1 (1990)\n, and Board of Regents of Univ. of Wis. System v. Southworth,\n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 217 (2000)\n. Respondents are mistaken.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In Keller, we considered the constitutionality of a rule applicable to all members of an \xe2\x80\x9cintegrated\xe2\x80\x9d bar, i.e., \xe2\x80\x9can association of attorneys in which membership and dues are required as a condition of practicing law.\xe2\x80\x9d 496 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 5. We held that members of this bar could not be required to pay the portion of bar dues used for political or ideological purposes but that they could be required to pay the portion of the dues used for activities connected with proposing ethical codes and disciplining bar members. Id., at 14.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This decision fits comfortably within the framework applied in the present case. Licensed attorneys are subject to detailed ethics rules, and the bar rule requiring the payment of dues was part of this regulatory scheme. The portion of the rule that we upheld served the \xe2\x80\x9cState\xe2\x80\x99s interest in regulating the legal profession and improving the quality of legal services.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. States also have a strong interest in allocating to the members of the bar, rather than the general public, the expense of ensuring that attorneys adhere to ethical practices. Thus, our decision in this case is wholly consistent with our holding in Keller.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Contrary to respondents\xe2\x80\x99 submission, the same is true with respect to Southworth, supra. In that case, we upheld the constitutionality of a university-imposed mandatory student activities fee that was used in part to support a wide array of student groups that engaged in expressive activity. The mandatory fee was challenged by students who objected to some of the expression that the fee was used to subsidize, but we rejected that challenge, and our holding is entirely consistent with our decision in this case.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Public universities have a compelling interest in promoting student expression in a manner that is viewpoint neutral. See Rosenberger v. Rector and Visitors of Univ. of Va.,\n\n515 U.\xc2\xa0S. 819 (1995)\n. This may be done by providing funding for a broad array of student groups. If the groups funded are truly diverse, many students are likely to disagree with things that are said by some groups. And if every student were entitled to a partial exemption from the fee requirement so that no portion of the student\xe2\x80\x99s fee went to support a group that the student did not wish to support, the administrative problems would likely be insuperable. Our decision today thus does not undermine Southworth.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n For all these reasons, we refuse to extend Abood in the manner that Illinois seeks. If we accepted Illinois\xe2\x80\x99 argument, we would approve an unprecedented violation of the bedrock principle that, except perhaps in the rarest of circumstances, no person in this country may be compelled to subsidize speech by a third party that he or she does not wish to support. The\n \nFirst Amendment prohibits the collection of an agency fee from personal assistants in the Rehabilitation Program who do not want to join or support the union.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The judgment of the Court of Appeals is reversed in part and affirmed in part,[30] and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Abood v. Detroit Bd. of Ed.,\n\n431 U.\xc2\xa0S. 209 (1977)\n, answers the question presented in this case. Abood held that a government entity may, consistently with the\n \nFirst Amendment, require public employees to pay a fair share of the cost that a union incurs negotiating on their behalf for better terms of employment. That is exactly what Illinois did in entering into collective bargaining agreements with the Service Employees International Union Healthcare (SEIU) which included fair-share provisions. Contrary to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision, those agreements fall squarely within Abood\xe2\x80\x99s holding. Here, Illinois employs, jointly with individuals suffering from disabilities, the in-home care providers whom the SEIU represents. Illinois establishes, following negotiations with the union, the most important terms of their employment, including wages, benefits, and basic qualifications. And Illinois\xe2\x80\x99s interests in imposing fair-share fees apply no less to those caregivers than to other state workers. The petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 challenge should therefore fail.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n And that result would fully comport with our decisions applying the\n \nFirst Amendment to public employment. Abood is not, as the majority at one point describes it, \xe2\x80\x9csomething of an anomaly,\xe2\x80\x9d allowing uncommon interference with individuals\xe2\x80\x99 expressive activities. Ante, at 8. Rather, the lines it draws and the balance it strikes reflect the way courts generally evaluate claims that a condition of public employment violates the\n \nFirst Amendment. Our decisions have long afforded government entities broad latitude to manage their workforces, even when that affects speech they could not regulate in other contexts. Abood is of a piece with all those decisions: While protecting an employee\xe2\x80\x99s most significant expression, that decision also enables the government to advance its interests in operating effectively\xe2\x80\x94by bargaining, if it so chooses, with a single employee representative and preventing free riding on that union\xe2\x80\x99s efforts.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n For that reason, one aspect of today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion is cause for satisfaction, though hardly applause. As this case came to us, the principal question it presented was whether to overrule Abood: The petitioners devoted the lion\xe2\x80\x99sshare of their briefing and argument to urging us to overturn that nearly 40-year-old precedent (and the respondents and amici countered in the same vein). Today\xe2\x80\x99s majority cannot resist taking potshots at Abood, see ante, at 17\xe2\x80\x9320, but it ignores the petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 invitation to depart from principles of stare decisis. And the essential work in the majority\xe2\x80\x99s opinion comes from its extended (though mistaken) distinction of Abood, see ante, at 20\xe2\x80\x9328, not from its gratuitous dicta critiquing Abood\xe2\x80\x99s foundations. That is to the good\xe2\x80\x94or at least better than it might be. The Abood rule is deeply entrenched, and is the foundation for not tens or hundreds, but thousands of contracts between unions and governments across the Nation. Our precedent about precedent, fairly understood and applied, makes it impossible for this Court to reverse that decision.

\n

I

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n I begin where this case should also end\xe2\x80\x94with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Abood. There, some public school teachers in Detroit challenged a clause in their collective bargaining agreement compelling non-union members to pay the union a service charge equivalent to regular dues. The Court upheld the requirement so long as the union was using the money for \xe2\x80\x9ccollective bargaining, contract administration, and grievance adjustment,\xe2\x80\x9d rather than for political or ideological activities. 431 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 225\xe2\x80\x93226. In so doing, the Court acknowledged that such a fair-share provision \xe2\x80\x9chas an impact upon [public employees\xe2\x80\x99]\n \nFirst Amendment interests\xe2\x80\x9d; employees, after all, might object to policies adopted or \xe2\x80\x9cactivities undertaken by the union in its role as exclusive representative.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 222. Still, the Court thought, the government\xe2\x80\x99s own interests \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutionally justified\xe2\x80\x9d the interference. Ibid. Detroit had decided, the Court explained, that bargaining with a single employee representative would promote \xe2\x80\x9clabor stability\xe2\x80\x9d and peaceful labor relations\xe2\x80\x94by ensuring, for example, that different groups of employees did not present \xe2\x80\x9cconflicting demands.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 221, 229. And because such an exclusive bargaining agent has a legal duty to represent all employees, rather than just its own members, a compulsory surcharge fairly distributes \xe2\x80\x9cthe cost of [bargaining] among those who benefit\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ccounteracts the incentive that employees might otherwise have to become \xe2\x80\x98free riders.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 222.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This case thus raises a straightforward question: Does Abood apply equally to Illinois\xe2\x80\x99s care providers as to Detroit\xe2\x80\x99s teachers? No one thinks that the fair-share provisions in the two cases differ in any relevant respect. Nor do the petitioners allege that the SEIU is crossing the line Abood drew by using their payments for political or ideological activities. The only point in dispute is whether it matters that the personal assistants here are employees not only of the State but also of the disabled persons for whom they care. Just as the Court of Appeals held, that fact should make no difference to the analysis. See 656 F.\xc2\xa03d 692, 698 (CA7 2011).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n To see how easily Abood resolves this case, consider how Illinois structured the petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 employment, and also why it did so. The petitioners work in Illinois\xe2\x80\x99s Medicaid-funded Rehabilitation Program, which provides in-home services to persons with disabilities who otherwise would face institutionalization. Under the program, each disabled person (the State calls them \xe2\x80\x9ccustomers\xe2\x80\x9d) receives care from a personal assistant; the total workforce exceeds 20,000. The State could have asserted comprehensive control over all the caregivers\xe2\x80\x99 activities. But because of the personalized nature of the services provided, Illinois instead chose (as other States have as well) to share authority with the customers themselves. The result is that each caregiver has joint employers\xe2\x80\x94the State and the customer\xe2\x80\x94with each controlling significant aspects of the assistant\xe2\x80\x99s work.[1]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n For its part, Illinois sets all the workforce-wide terms of employment. Most notably, the State determines and pays the employees\xe2\x80\x99 wages and benefits, including health insurance (while also withholding taxes). See 89 Ill.Admin. Code \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7686.10(h)(10), 686.40(a)\xe2\x80\x93(b) (2007); App. 44\xe2\x80\x9346. By regulation, Illinois establishes the job\xe2\x80\x99s basic qualifications: for example, the assistant must provide references or recommendations and have adequate experience and training for the services given. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7686.10(c), (f). So too, the State describes the services any personal assistant may provide, and prescribes the terms of standard employment contracts entered into between personal assistants and customers. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7686.10(h), 686.20.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Illinois as well structures the individual relationship between the customer and his assistant (in ways the majority barely acknowledges). Along with both the customer and his physician, a state-employed counselor develops a service plan laying out the assistant\xe2\x80\x99s specificjob responsibilities, hours, and working conditions. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7684.10, 684.50. That counselor also assists the customer in conducting a state-mandated annual performance review, based on state-established criteria, and mediates any resulting disagreements. See \xc2\xa7686.30.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Within the structure designed by the State, the cus-tomer of course has crucial responsibilities. He exercises day-to-day supervisory control over the personal assistant. See \xc2\xa7676.30(b). And he gets both to hire a particular caregiver (from among the pool of applicants Illinois has deemed qualified) and to impose any needed discipline, up to and including discharge. See ibid.; \xc2\xa7677.40(d). But even as to those matters, the State plays a role. Before a customer may hire an assistant, the counselor must sign off on the employee\xe2\x80\x99s ability to follow the customer\xe2\x80\x99s directions and communicate with him. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7686.10(d)\xe2\x80\x93(e) (requiring that the employee demonstrate these capabilities \xe2\x80\x9cto the satisfaction of\xe2\x80\x9d the counselor). And although only a customer can actually fire an assistant, the State can effectively do so by refusing to pay one who fails to \xe2\x80\x9cmeet [state] standards.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7677.40(d). The majority reads that language narrowly, see ante, at 3, n.\xc2\xa01, 22, but the State does not: It has made clear not just in its litigation papers, but also in its collective bargaining agreements and customer guidance that it will withhold payment from an assistant (or altogether disqualify her from the program) based on credible allegations of customer abuse, neglect, or financial exploitation. See App. 55; Brief for Respondent Quinn 3, 50; Ill. Dept. of Human Servs., Customer Guidance for Managing Providers 8, online at http://\xc2\xa0www.\xc2\xa0dhs.state.il.us\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0OneNetLibrary\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa027897\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0documents\xc2\xa0/Brochures/4365.pdf (as visited June 27, 2014, and available in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file).[2]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Given that set of arrangements, Abood should control. Although a customer can manage his own relationship with a caregiver, Illinois has sole authority over every workforce-wide term and condition of the assistants\xe2\x80\x99 employment\xe2\x80\x94in other words, the issues most likely to be the subject of collective bargaining. In particular, if an assistant wants an increase in pay, she must ask the State, not the individual customer. So too if she wants better benefits. (Although the majority notes that caregivers do not receive statutory retirement and health insurance benefits, see ante, at 22, that is irrelevant: Collective bargaining between the State and SEIU has focused on benefits from the beginning, and has produced state-funded health insurance for personal assistants.) And because it is Illinois that would sit down at a bargaining table to address those subjects\xe2\x80\x94the ones that matter most to employees and so most affect workforce stability\xe2\x80\x94the State\xe2\x80\x99s stake in a fair-share provision is the same as in Abood. Here too, the State has an interest in promoting effective operations by negotiating with an equitably and ade-quately funded exclusive bargaining agent over termsand conditions of employment. That Illinois has delegated to program customers various individualized employment issues makes no difference to those state interests. If anything, as the State has contended, the dispersion of employees across numerous workplaces and the absence of day-to-day state supervision provides an additional reason for Illinois to want to \xe2\x80\x9caddress concerns common to all personal assistants\xe2\x80\x9d by negotiating with a single representative: Only in that way, the State explains, can the employees effectively convey their concerns about employment under the Rehabilitation Program. App. to Pet. for Cert. 46a (Exec. Order No. 2003\xe2\x80\x938).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Indeed, the history of that program forcefully demonstrates Illinois\xe2\x80\x99s interest in bargaining with an adequately funded exclusive bargaining agent\xe2\x80\x94that is, the interest Abood recognized and protected. Workforce shortages and high turnover have long plagued in-home care programs, principally because of low wages and benefits. That labor instability lessens the quality of care, which in turn, forces disabled persons into institutions and (massively) in-creases costs to the State. See Brief for Paraprofessional Healthcare Institute as Amicus Curiae 16\xe2\x80\x9326; Brief for State of California et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 4\xe2\x80\x935. The individual customers are powerless to address those systemic issues; rather, the State\xe2\x80\x94because of its control over workforce-wide terms of employment\xe2\x80\x94is the single employer that can do so. And here Illinois determined (as have nine other States, see Brief for Respondent SEIU 51, n.\xc2\xa014) that negotiations with an exclusive representative offered the best chance to set the Rehabilitation Program on firmer footing. Because of that bargaining, as the majority acknowledges, home-care assistants have nearly doubled their wages in less than 10 years, obtained state-funded health insurance, and benefited from better training and workplace safety measures. See ante, at 32\xe2\x80\x9333; Brief for Respondent Quinn 7; App. 44\xe2\x80\x9348. The State, in return, has obtained guarantees against strikes or other work stoppages, see id., at 55\xe2\x80\x94and most important, believes it has gotten a more stable workforce providing higher qual-ity care, thereby avoiding the costs associated with institu-tionalization. Illinois\xe2\x80\x99s experience thus might serve as a veritable poster child for Abood\xe2\x80\x94not, as the majority would have it, some strange extension of that decision.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n It is not altogether easy to understand why the majority thinks what it thinks: Today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion takes the tack of throwing everything against the wall in the hope that something might stick. A vain hope, as it turns out. Even once disentangled, the various strands of the majority\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning do not distinguish this case from Abood.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Parts of the majority\xe2\x80\x99s analysis appear to rest on the simple presence of another employer, possessing significant responsibilities, in addition to the State. See ante, at 20\xe2\x80\x9322, 24. But this Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases provide no warrant for holding that joint public employees are not real ones. To the contrary, the Court has made clear that the government\xe2\x80\x99s wide latitude to manage its workforce extends to such employees, even as against their\n \nFirst Amendment claims. The government\xe2\x80\x99s prerogative as employer, we recently explained, turns not on the \xe2\x80\x9cformal status\xe2\x80\x9d of an employee, but on the nature of the public \xe2\x80\x9cinterests at stake\xe2\x80\x9d; we therefore rejected the view that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Government\xe2\x80\x99s broad authority in managing its affairs should apply with diminished force\xe2\x80\x9d to contract employees whose \xe2\x80\x9cdirect employment relationship\xe2\x80\x9d is with another party. NASA v. Nelson, 562 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 14\xe2\x80\x9315). And indeed, we reached the same result (in language that might have been written for this case) when such employees \xe2\x80\x9cd[id] not work at the government\xe2\x80\x99s workplace[,] d[id] not interact daily with government officers and employees,\xe2\x80\x9d and were not subject to the government\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cday-to-day control\xe2\x80\x9d over \xe2\x80\x9cthe details of how work is done.\xe2\x80\x9d Board of Comm\xe2\x80\x99rs, Wabaunsee Cty. v. Umbehr,\n\n518 U.\xc2\xa0S. 668\n\xe2\x80\x93677 (1996).[3] Here, as I have explained, Illinois\xe2\x80\x99s interests as an employer and program administrator are substantial, see supra, at 4\xe2\x80\x936; and accordingly, the State\xe2\x80\x99s sharing of employment responsibilities with another party should not matter.[4]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Next, the majority emphasizes that the Illinois Legislature deemed personal assistants \xe2\x80\x9cpublic employees\xe2\x80\x9d solely \xe2\x80\x9cfor the purposes of coverage under the Illinois Public Labor Relations Act\xe2\x80\x9d and not for other purposes, like granting statutory benefits and incurring vicarious liability in tort. Ill. Comp. Stat., ch. 20, \xc2\xa72405/3(f) (West 2012); see ante, at 6, 22\xe2\x80\x9323; but cf. Martin v. Illinois, 2005 WL 2267733, *5\xe2\x80\x93*8 (Ill. Workers\xe2\x80\x99 Compensation Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, July 26, 2005) (treating caregivers as public employees for purposes of workers\xe2\x80\x99 compensation).[5] But once again, it is hard to see why that fact is relevant. The majority must agree (this Court has made the point often enough) that \xe2\x80\x9cstate law labels,\xe2\x80\x9d adopted for a whole host of reasons, do not determine whether the State is acting as an employer for purposes of the\n \nFirst Amendment. E.g., Umbehr, 518 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 679. The true issue is whether Illinois has a sufficient stake in, and control over, the petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 terms and conditions of employment to implicate Abood\xe2\x80\x99s rationales and trigger its application. And once more, that question has a clear answer: As I have shown, Illinois negotiates all workforce-wide terms of the caregivers\xe2\x80\x99 employment as part of its effort to promote labor stability and effectively administer its Rehabilitation Program. See supra, at 6\xe2\x80\x938. As contrasted to that all-important fact, whether Illinois incurs vicarious liability for caregivers\xe2\x80\x99 torts, see ante, at 23, or grants them certain statutory benefits like health insurance, see ante, at 22, is beside the point. And still more so because the State and SEIU can bargain over most such matters; for example, as I have noted, the two have reached agreement on providing state-funded health coverage, see supra, at 7.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Further, the majority claims, \xe2\x80\x9cthe scope of bargaining\xe2\x80\x9d that the SEIU may conduct for caregivers is \xe2\x80\x9ccircumscribed\xe2\x80\x9d because the customer has authority over individualized employment matters like hiring and firing. Ante, at 23\xe2\x80\x9325. But (at the risk of sounding like a broken record) so what? Most States limit the scope of permissible bargaining in the public sector\xe2\x80\x94often ruling out of bounds similar, individualized decisions. See R. Kearney & P. Mareschal, Labor Relations in the Public Sector 75\xe2\x80\x9377 (5th ed. 2014) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe great majority of state statutes\xe2\x80\x9d exclude \xe2\x80\x9ccertain matters from the scope of negotiations,\xe2\x80\x9d including, for example, personnel decisions respecting \xe2\x80\x9chiring, promotion, and dismissal\xe2\x80\x9d); Note, Developments in the Law\xe2\x80\x94Public Employment, 97 Harv. L. Rev. 1611, 1684 (1984) (Many state statutes \xe2\x80\x9cexplicitly limit[ ] the scope of bargaining, typically by excluding decisions on personnel management\xe2\x80\x9d). Here, the scope of collective bargaining\xe2\x80\x94over wages and benefits, as well as basic duties and qualifications\xe2\x80\x94more than suffices to implicate the state interests justifying Abood. Those are the matters, after all, most likely to concern employees generally and thus most likely to affect the nature and quality of the State\xe2\x80\x99s workforce. The idea that Abood applies only if a union can bargain with the State over every issue comes from nowhere and relates to nothing in that decision\xe2\x80\x94and would revolutionize public labor law.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Finally, the majority places weight on an idiosyncrasy of Illinois law: that a regulation requires uniform wages for all personal assistants. See ante, at 25. According to the majority, that means Abood\xe2\x80\x99s free-rider rationale \xe2\x80\x9chas little force in the situation now before us\xe2\x80\x9d: Even absent the duty of fair representation (requiring the union to work on behalf of all employees, members and non-members alike, see infra, at 22\xe2\x80\x9323), the union could not bargain one employee\xe2\x80\x99s wages against another\xe2\x80\x99s. Ante, at 26.[6] But that idea is doubly wrong. First, the Illinois regulation applies only to wages. It does not cover, for example, the significant health benefits that the SEIU has obtained for in-home caregivers, or any other benefits for which it may bargain in the future. Nor does the regulation prevent preferential participation in the grievance process, which governs all disputes between Illinois and caregivers arising from the terms of their agreement. See n.\xc2\xa02, supra. And second, even if the regulation covered everything subject to collective bargaining, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning is a non-sequitur. All the regulation would do then is serve as suspenders to the duty of fair representation\xe2\x80\x99s belt: That Illinois has two ways to ensure that the results of collective bargaining redound to the benefit of all employees serves to compound, rather than mitigate, the union\xe2\x80\x99s free-rider problem.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n As far as I can tell, that covers the majority\xe2\x80\x99s reasons for distinguishing this case from Abood. And even when considered in combination, as the majority does, they do not succeed. What makes matters still worse is the perverse result of the majority\xe2\x80\x99s decision: It penalizes the State for giving disabled persons some control over their own care. If Illinois had structured the program, as it could have, to centralize every aspect of the employment relationship, no question could possibly have arisen about Abood\xe2\x80\x99s application. Nothing should change because the State chose to respect the dignity and independence of program beneficiaries by allowing them to select and discharge, as well as supervise day-to-day, their own caregivers. A joint employer remains an employer, and here, as I have noted, Illinois kept authority over all workforce-wide terms of employment\xe2\x80\x94the very issues most likely to be the subject of collective bargaining. The State thus should also retain the prerogative\xe2\x80\x94as part of its effort to \xe2\x80\x9censure efficient and effective delivery of personal care services\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94to require all employees to contribute fairly to their bargaining agent. App. to Pet. for Cert. 45a (Exec. Order No. 2003\xe2\x80\x938).

\n

II

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Perhaps recognizing the difficulty of plausibly distinguishing this case from Abood, the petitioners raised a more fundamental question: the continued viability of Abood as to all public employees, even what the majority calls \xe2\x80\x9cfull-fledged\xe2\x80\x9d ones. Ante, at 9. That issue occupied the brunt of the briefing and argument in this Court. See, e.g., Brief for Petitioners 16\xe2\x80\x9324; Brief for Respondent SEIU 15\xe2\x80\x9344; Brief for Respondent Quinn 15\xe2\x80\x9329; Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 14\xe2\x80\x9328; Tr. of Oral Arg. 5\xe2\x80\x9321, 32\xe2\x80\x9339, 42\xe2\x80\x9347, 50\xe2\x80\x9360. The majority declines the petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 request to overturn precedent\xe2\x80\x94and rightly so: This Court does not have anything close to the special justification necessary to overturn Abood. Still, the majority cannot restrain itself from providing a critique of that decision, suggesting that it might have resolved the case differently in the first instance. That dicta is off-base: Abood corresponds precisely to this Court\xe2\x80\x99s overall framework for assessing public employees\xe2\x80\x99\n \nFirst Amendment claims. To accept that framework, while holding Abood at arms-length, is to wish for a sui generis rule, lacking in justification, applying exclusively to union fees.

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This Court\xe2\x80\x99s view of stare decisis makes plain why the majority cannot\xe2\x80\x94and did not\xe2\x80\x94overturn Abood. That doctrine, we have stated, is a \xe2\x80\x9cfoundation stone of the rule of law.\xe2\x80\x9d Michigan v. Bay Mills Indian Community, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 15). It \xe2\x80\x9cpromotes the evenhanded, predictable, and consistent development of legal principles [and] fosters reliance on judicial decisions.\xe2\x80\x9d Payne v. Tennessee,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 808,\n827 (1991)\n. As important, it \xe2\x80\x9ccontributes to the actual and perceived integrity of the judicial process,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., by ensuring that decisions are \xe2\x80\x9cfounded in the law rather than in the proclivities of individuals,\xe2\x80\x9d Vasquez v. Hillery,\n\n474 U.\xc2\xa0S. 254,\n265 (1986)\n. For all those reasons, this Court has always held that \xe2\x80\x9cany departure\xe2\x80\x9d from precedent \xe2\x80\x9cdemands special justification.\xe2\x80\x9d Arizona v. Rumsey,\n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 203,\n212 (1984)\n.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n And Abood is not just any precedent: It is entrenched in a way not many decisions are. Over nearly four decades, we have cited Abood favorably numerous times, and we have repeatedly affirmed and applied its core distinction between the costs of collective bargaining (which the government can demand its employees share) and those of political activities (which it cannot). See, e.g., Locke v. Karass,\n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 207\n\xe2\x80\x93214 (2009); Lehnert v. Ferris Faculty Assn.,\n\n500 U.\xc2\xa0S. 507,\n519 (1991)\n; Teachers v. Hudson,\n\n475 U.\xc2\xa0S. 292\n\xe2\x80\x93302 (1986); Ellis v. Railway Clerks,\n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 435\n\xe2\x80\x93457 (1984). Reviewing those decisions, this Court recently\xe2\x80\x94and unanimously\xe2\x80\x94called the Abood rule \xe2\x80\x9ca general\n \nFirst Amendment principle.\xe2\x80\x9d Locke, 555 U.\xc2\xa0S., 213\xe2\x80\x93215. And indeed, the Court has relied on that rule in deciding cases involving compulsory fees outside the labor context\xe2\x80\x94which today\xe2\x80\x99s majority reaffirms as good law, see ante, at 37\xe2\x80\x9339. See, e.g., Keller v. State Bar of Cal.,\n\n496 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n\xe2\x80\x9317 (1990) (state bar fees); Board of Regents of Univ. of Wis. System v. Southworth,\n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 217\n\xe2\x80\x93232 (2000) (public university student fees); Glickman v. Wileman Brothers & Elliott, Inc.,\n\n521 U.\xc2\xa0S. 457\n\xe2\x80\x93473 (1997) (commercial advertising assessments). Not until two years ago, in Knox v. Service Employees, 567 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012), did the Court so much as whisper (there without the benefit of briefing or argument, see id., at ___ (Sotomayor, J., concurring in judgment) (slip op., at 1\xe2\x80\x936)) that it had any misgivings about Abood.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Perhaps still more important, Abood has created enormous reliance interests. More than 20 States have enacted statutes authorizing fair-share provisions, and on that basis public entities of all stripes have entered into multi-year contracts with unions containing such clauses. \xe2\x80\x9cStare decisis has added force,\xe2\x80\x9d we have held, when overturning a precedent would require \xe2\x80\x9cStates to reexamine [and amend] their statutes.\xe2\x80\x9d Hilton v. South Carolina Public Railways Comm\xe2\x80\x99n,\n\n502 U.\xc2\xa0S. 197\n\xe2\x80\x93203 (1991). And on top of that, \xe2\x80\x9c[c]onsiderations in favor of stare decisis are at their acme in cases involving property and contract rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Payne, 501 U. S., at 828. Here, governments and unions across the country have entered into thousands of contracts involving millions of employees in reliance on Abood. Reliance interests do not come any stronger.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The majority\xe2\x80\x99s criticisms of Abood do not remotely defeat those powerful reasons for adhering to the decision. The special justifications needed to reverse an opinion must go beyond demonstrations (much less assertions) that it was wrong; that is the very point of stare decisis. And the majority\xe2\x80\x99s critique extends no further. It is mostly just a catalog of errors Abood supposedly committed\xe2\x80\x94reproaches that could have been leveled as easily 40 years ago as today. Only the idea that Abood did not \xe2\x80\x9canticipate\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cforesee\xe2\x80\x9d the difficulties of distinguishing between collective bargaining and political activities, see ante, at 18\xe2\x80\x9319, might be thought different. But in fact, Abood predicted precisely those issues. See 431 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 236 (\xe2\x80\x9cThere will, of course, be difficult problems in drawing lines between collective-bargaining .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and ideological activities\xe2\x80\x9d). It simply disagreed with today\xe2\x80\x99s majority about whether in this context, as in many others, lines that are less than pristine are still worth using. And in any event, the majority much overstates the difficulties of classifying union expenditures. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s most recent decision on the subject unanimously resolved the single issue that had divided lower courts. See Locke, 555 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 217\xe2\x80\x93221. So it is not surprising that the majority fails to offer any concrete examples of thorny classification problems. If the kind of hand-wringing about blurry lines that the majority offers were enough to justify breaking with precedent, we might have to discard whole volumes of the U.\xc2\xa0S. Reports.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n And the majority says nothing to the contrary: It does not pretend to have the requisite justifications to overrule Abood. Readers of today\xe2\x80\x99s decision will know that Abood does not rank on the majority\xe2\x80\x99s top-ten list of favorite precedents\xe2\x80\x94and that the majority could not restrain itself from saying (and saying and saying) so. Yet they will also know that the majority could not, even after receiving full-dress briefing and argument, come up with reasons anywhere near sufficient to reverse the decision. Much has gone wrong in today\xe2\x80\x99s ruling, but this has not: Save for an unfortunate hiving off of ostensibly \xe2\x80\x9cpartial-public\xe2\x80\x9d employees, ante, at 28, Abood remains the law.

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n And even apart from stare decisis, that result is as it should be; indeed, it is the only outcome that makes sense in the context of our caselaw. In numerous cases decided over many decades, this Court has addressed the government\xe2\x80\x99s authority to adopt measures limiting expression in the capacity not of sovereign but of employer. Abood fits\xe2\x80\x94fits hand-in-glove\xe2\x80\x94with all those cases, in both reasoning and result. Were that rule not in place, our law respecting public employees\xe2\x80\x99 speech rights would contain a serious anomaly\xe2\x80\x94a different legal standard (and not a good one) applying exclusively to union fees.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This Court has long acknowledged that the government has wider constitutional latitude when it is acting as employer than as sovereign. See Engquist v. Oregon Dept. of Agriculture,\n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 591,\n598 (2008)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]here is a crucial difference, with respect to constitutional analysis, between the government exercising the power to regulate .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and the government acting .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to manage [its] internal operation\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). \xe2\x80\x9cTime and again our cases have recognized that the Government has a much freer hand\xe2\x80\x9d in dealing with its employees than with other citizens. NASA, 562 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 12). We have explained that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he government\xe2\x80\x99s interest in achieving its goals as effectively and efficiently as possible is elevated\xe2\x80\x9d in the public workplace\xe2\x80\x94that the government must have the ability to decide how to manage its employees in order to best provide services to the public. Engquist, 553 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 598. In effect, we have tried to place the government-qua-employer in a similar (though not identical) position to the private employer, recognizing that both face comparable challenges in maintaining a productive workforce. The result is that a public employee \xe2\x80\x9cmust accept certain limitations on his or her freedom.\xe2\x80\x9d Garcetti v. Ceballos,\n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 410,\n418 (2006)\n. \xe2\x80\x9c[A]lthough government employees do not lose their constitutional rights when they accept their positions, those rights must be balanced against the realities of the employment context.\xe2\x80\x9d Engquist, 553 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 600.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Further, this Court has developed and applied those principles in numerous cases involving\n \nFirst Amendment claims. \xe2\x80\x9cGovernment employers, like private employers,\xe2\x80\x9d we have explained, \xe2\x80\x9cneed a significant degree of control over their employees\xe2\x80\x99 words\xe2\x80\x9d in order to \xe2\x80\x9cefficient[ly] provi[de] public services.\xe2\x80\x9d Garcetti, 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 418. Accordingly, we have devised methods for distinguishing between speech restrictions reflecting the kind of concerns private employers often hold (which are constitutional) and those exploiting the employment relationship to restrict employees\xe2\x80\x99 speech as private citizens (which are not). Most notably, the Court uses a two-step test originating in Pickering v. Board of Ed. of Township High School Dist. 205, Will Cty.,\n\n391 U.\xc2\xa0S. 563 (1968)\n. First, if the expression at issue does not relate to \xe2\x80\x9ca matter of public concern,\xe2\x80\x9d the employee \xe2\x80\x9chas no\n \nFirst Amendment cause of action.\xe2\x80\x9d Garcetti, 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 418. Second, even if the speech addresses a matter of public concern, a court is to determine whether the government \xe2\x80\x9chad an adequate justification\xe2\x80\x9d for its action, ibid., by balancing \xe2\x80\x9cthe interests of the [employee] as a citizen .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and the interest of the State, as an employer, in promoting the efficiency of the public services it performs through its employees,\xe2\x80\x9d Pickering, 391 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 568.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Abood is of a piece with all those decisions; and indeed, its core analysis mirrors Pickering\xe2\x80\x99s. The Abood Court recognized that fair-share provisions function as prerequisites to employment, assessed to cover the costs of representing employees in collective bargaining. Private employers, Abood noted, often established such employment conditions, to ensure adequate funding of an exclusive bargaining agent, and thus to promote labor stability. Abood acknowledged (contrary to the majority\xe2\x80\x99s statement, see ante, at 17) certain \xe2\x80\x9cdifferences in the nature of collective bargaining in the public and private sectors.\xe2\x80\x9d 431 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 227; see id., at 227\xe2\x80\x93229. But the Court concluded that the government, acting as employer, should have the same prerogative as a private business in deciding how best to negotiate with its employees over such matters as wages and benefits. See id., at 229 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]here can be no principled basis for\xe2\x80\x9d distinguishing between a public and private employer\xe2\x80\x99s view that a fair-share clause will promote \xe2\x80\x9clabor stability\xe2\x80\x9d). At the same time, the Court recognized the need for some mechanism to ensure that the government could not leverage its power as employer to impinge on speech its employees undertook as citizens on matters of public import. See id., at 234\xe2\x80\x93236.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court struck the appropriate balance by drawing a line, corresponding to Pickering\xe2\x80\x99s, between fees for collective bargaining and those for political activities. On the one side, Abood decided, speech within the employment relationship about pay and working conditions pertains mostly to private concerns and implicates the government\xe2\x80\x99s interests as employer; thus, the government could compel fair-share fees for collective bargaining. On the other side, speech in political campaigns relates to matters of public concern and has no bearing on the government\xe2\x80\x99s interest in structuring its workforce; thus, compelled fees for those activities are forbidden. In that way, the law surrounding fair-share provisions coheres with the law relating to public employees\xe2\x80\x99 speech generally. Or, said otherwise, an anomaly in the government\xe2\x80\x99s regulation of its workforce would arise in Abood\xe2\x80\x99s absence: Public employers could then pursue all policies, except this single one, reasonably designed to manage personnel and enhance the effectiveness of their programs.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The majority\xe2\x80\x99s critique of Abood principally goes astray by deeming all this irrelevant. This Court, the majority insists, has never \xe2\x80\x9cseen Abood as based on Pickering balancing.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 34. But to rely on Abood\xe2\x80\x99s failure to cite Pickering more often, as the majority does, see ante, at 34, n. 26, is to miss the essential point. Although stemming from different historic antecedents, the two decisions addressed variants of the same issue: the extent of the government\xe2\x80\x99s power to adopt employment conditions affecting expression. And as just discussed, the twogave strikingly parallel answers, providing a coherent framework to adjudicate the constitutionality of those regulations.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n To the extent the majority engages with that framework, its analysis founders at the first step, in assessing the\n \nFirst Amendment value of the speech at issue here. A running motif of the majority opinion is that collective bargaining in the public sector raises significant questions about the level of government spending. Ante, at 17\xe2\x80\x9318 and n.\xc2\xa07, 36 and nn.\xc2\xa028\xe2\x80\x9329. By financing the SEIU\xe2\x80\x99s collective bargaining over wages and benefits, the majority suggests, in-home caregivers\xe2\x80\x94whether they wish to or not\xe2\x80\x94take one side in a debate about those issues.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n But that view of the\n \nFirst Amendment interests at stake blinks decades\xe2\x80\x99 worth of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent. Our decisions (tracing from Pickering as well as Abood) teach that internal workplace speech about public employees\xe2\x80\x99 wages, benefits, and such\xe2\x80\x94that is, the prosaic stuff of collective bargaining\xe2\x80\x94does not become speech of \xe2\x80\x9cpublic concern\xe2\x80\x9d just because those employment terms may have broader consequence. To the contrary, we have made clear that except in narrow circumstances we will not allow an employee to make a \xe2\x80\x9cfederal constitutional issue\xe2\x80\x9d out of basic \xe2\x80\x9cemployment matters, including working conditions, pay, discipline, promotions, leave, vacations, and terminations.\xe2\x80\x9d Borough of Duryea v. Guarnieri, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 10); see Umbehr, 518 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 675 (public employees\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cspeech on merely private employment matters is unprotected\xe2\x80\x9d). Indeed, even Abood\xe2\x80\x99s original detractors conceded that an employee\xe2\x80\x99s interest in expressing views, within the workplace context, about \xe2\x80\x9cnarrowly defined economic issues [like] salaries and pension benefits\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9crelatively insignificant\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cweak.\xe2\x80\x9d 431 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 263, n.\xc2\xa016 (Powell, J., concurring in judgment). (Those Justices saved their fire for teachers\xe2\x80\x99 speech relating to education policy. See ibid.) And nowhere has the Court ever suggested, as the majority does today, see ante, at 35\xe2\x80\x9336 and n. 28, that if a certain dollar amount is at stake (but how much, exactly?), the constitutional treatment of an employee\xe2\x80\x99s expression becomes any different.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Consider an analogy, not involving union fees: Suppose an employee violates a government employer\xe2\x80\x99s work rules by demanding, at various inopportune times and places, higher wages for both himself and his co-workers (which, of course, will drive up public spending). The government employer disciplines the employee, and he brings a\n \nFirst Amendment claim. Would the Court consider his speech a matter of public concern under Pickering? I cannot believe it would, and indeed the petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 own counsel joins me in that view. He maintained at oral argument that such speech would concern merely an \xe2\x80\x9cinternal proprietary matter,\xe2\x80\x9d thus allowing the employer to take disciplinary action. Tr. of Oral Arg. 6, 10. If the majority thinks otherwise, government entities across the country should prepare themselves for unprecedented limitations on their ability to regulate their workforces. But again, I doubt they need to worry, because this Court has never come close to holding that any matter of public employment affecting public spending (which is to say most such matters) becomes for that reason alone an issue of public concern. (And on the off-chance that both the petitioners and I are wrong on that score, I am doubly confident that the government would prevail under Pickering\xe2\x80\x99s balancing test.)

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n I can see no reason to treat the expressive interests of workers objecting to payment of union fees, like the petitioners here, as worthy of greater consideration. The subject matter of the speech is the same: wages and benefits for public employees. Or to put the point more fully: In both cases (mine and the real one), the employer is sanctioning employees for choosing either to say or not to say something respecting their terms and conditions of employment. Of course, in my hypothetical, the employer is stopping the employee from speaking, whereas in this or any other case involving union fees, the employer is forcing the employee to support such expression. But I am sure the majority would agree that that difference does not make a difference\xe2\x80\x94in other words, that the \xe2\x80\x9cdifference between compelled speech and compelled silence\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cwithout constitutional significance.\xe2\x80\x9d Riley v. National Federation of Blind of N. C., Inc.,\n\n487 U.\xc2\xa0S. 781,\n796 (1988)\n. Hence, in analyzing the kind of expression involved in this case, Abood corresponds to Pickering (and vice versa)\xe2\x80\x94with each permitting a government to regulate such activity in aid of managing its workforce to provide public services.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Perhaps, though, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s skepticism about Abood comes from a different source: its failure to fully grasp the government\xe2\x80\x99s interest in bargaining with an adequately funded exclusive bargaining representative. One of the ma-jority\xe2\x80\x99s criticisms of Abood, stated still more prominently in Knox, 567 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 10\xe2\x80\x9311), goes something as follows. Abood (so the majority says) wrongly saw a government\xe2\x80\x99s interest in bargaining with an exclusive representative as \xe2\x80\x9cinextricably linked\xe2\x80\x9d with a fair-share agreement. Ante, at 31; see ante, at 20. A State, the majority (a bit grudgingly) acknowledges, may well have reasons to bargain with a single agent for all employees; and without a fair-share agreement, that union\xe2\x80\x99s activities will benefit employees who do not pay dues. Yet \xe2\x80\x9c[s]uch free-rider arguments,\xe2\x80\x9d the majority avers, \xe2\x80\x9care generally insufficient to overcome\n \nFirst Amendment objections.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939 (quoting Knox, 567 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 10\xe2\x80\x9311)). In the majority\xe2\x80\x99s words: \xe2\x80\x9cA host of organizations advocate on behalf of the interests of persons fallingwithin an occupational group, and many of these groups are quite successful even though they are dependent on voluntary contributions.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 33\xe2\x80\x9334.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n But Abood and a host of our other opinions have explained and relied on an essential distinction between unions and special-interest organizations generally. See, e.g., Abood, 431 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 221\xe2\x80\x93222 and n.\xc2\xa015; Communications Workers v. Beck,\n\n487 U.\xc2\xa0S. 735,\n750 (1988)\n; Machinists v. Street,\n\n367 U.\xc2\xa0S. 740,\n762 (1961)\n. The law compels unions to represent\xe2\x80\x94and represent fairly\xe2\x80\x94every worker in a bargaining unit, regardless whether they join or contribute to the union. That creates a collective action problem of far greater magnitude than in the typical interest group, because the union cannot give any special advantages to its own backers. In such a circumstance, not just those who oppose but those who favor a union have an economic incentive to withhold dues; only altruism or loyalty\xe2\x80\x94as against financial self-interest\xe2\x80\x94can explain their support. Hence arises the legal rule countenancing fair-share agreements: It ensures that a union will receive adequate funding, notwithstanding its legally imposed disability\xe2\x80\x94and so that a government wishing to bargain with an exclusive representative will have a viable counterpart.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n As is often the case, Justice Scalia put the point best:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cWhere the state imposes upon the union a duty to deliver services, it may permit the union to demand reimbursement for them; or, looked at from the other end, where the state creates in the nonmembers a legal entitlement from the union, it may compel them to pay the cost. The \xe2\x80\x98compelling state interest\xe2\x80\x99 that justifies this constitutional rule is not simply elimination of the inequity arising from the fact that some union activity redounds to the benefit of \xe2\x80\x98free-riding\xe2\x80\x99 nonmembers; private speech often furthers the interests of nonspeakers, and that does not alone empower the state to compel the speech to be paid for. What is distinctive, however, about the \xe2\x80\x98free riders\xe2\x80\x99 [in unions] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is that .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the law requires the union to carry [them]\xe2\x80\x94indeed, requires the union to go out of its way to benefit [them], even at the expense of its other interests.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [T]he free ridership (if it were left to be that) would be not incidental but calculated, not imposed by circumstances but mandated by government decree.\xe2\x80\x9d Lehnert, 500 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 556 (opinion concurring in judgment in part and dissenting in part).

\n

And in other parts of its opinion, the majority itself mimics the point, thus recognizing the core rationale of Abood: What justifies the agency fee, the majority notes, is \xe2\x80\x9cthe fact that the State compels the union to promote and protect the interests of nonmembers.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 25; see ante, at 27, n.\xc2\xa018. Exactly right; indeed, that is as clear a one-sentence account of Abood\xe2\x80\x99s free-rider rationale as appears in this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Still, the majority too quickly says, it has no worries in this case: Given that Illinois\xe2\x80\x99s caregivers voted to unionize, \xe2\x80\x9cit may be presumed that a high percentage of [them] became union members and are willingly paying union dues.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 33. But in fact nothing of the sort may be so presumed, given that union supporters (no less than union detractors) have an economic incentive to free ride. See supra, at 22\xe2\x80\x9323. The federal workforce, on which the majority relies, see ante, at 31, provides a case in point. There many fewer employees pay dues than have voted for a union to represent them.[7] And why, after all, should that endemic free-riding be surprising? Does the majority think that public employees are immune from basic principles of economics? If not, the majority can have no basis for thinking that absent a fair-share clause, a union can attract sufficient dues to adequately support its functions.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This case in fact offers a prime illustration of how a fair-share agreement may serve important government interests. Recall that Illinois decided that collective bargaining with an exclusive representative of in-home caregivers would enable it to provide improved services through its Rehabilitation Program. See supra, at 7\xe2\x80\x938. The State thought such bargaining would enable it to attract a better and more stable workforce to serve disabled patients, preventing their institutionalization and thereby decreasing total state expenditures. The majority does not deny the State\xe2\x80\x99s legitimate interest in choosing to negotiate with an exclusive bargaining agent, in service of administering an effective program. See ante, at 32\xe2\x80\x9333. But the majority does deny Illinois the means it reasonably deemed appropriate to effectuate that policy\xe2\x80\x94a fair-share provision ensuring that the union has the funds necessary to carry out its responsibilities on behalf of in-home caregivers. The majority does so against the weight of all precedent, and based on \xe2\x80\x9cempirical assumption[s],\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 20, lacking any foundation. Abood got this matter right; the majority gets it wrong: Illinois has a more than sufficient interest, in managing its workforce and administering the Rehabilitation Program, to require employees to pay a fair share of a union\xe2\x80\x99s costs of collective bargaining.

\n

III

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n For many decades, Americans have debated the pros and cons of right-to-work laws and fair-share requirements. All across the country and continuing to the present day, citizens have engaged in passionate argument about the issue and have made disparate policy choices. The petitioners in this case asked this Court to end that discussion for the entire public sector, by overruling Abood and thus imposing a right-to-work regime for all government employees. The good news out of this case is clear: The majority declined that radical request. The Court did not, as the petitioners wanted, deprive every state and local government, in the management of their employees and programs, of the tool that many have thought necessary and appropriate to make collective bargaining work.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The bad news is just as simple: The majority robbed Illinois of that choice in administering its in-home care program. For some 40 years, Abood has struck a stable balance\xe2\x80\x94consistent with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s general framework for assessing public employees\xe2\x80\x99\n \nFirst Amendment claims\xe2\x80\x94between those employees\xe2\x80\x99 rights and government entities\xe2\x80\x99 interests in managing their workforces. The majority today misapplies Abood, which properly should control this case. Nothing separates, for purposes of that decision, Illinois\xe2\x80\x99s personal assistants from any other public employees. The balance Abood struck thus should have defeated the petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 demand to invalidate Illinois\xe2\x80\x99s fair-share agreement. I respectfully dissent.
