SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

Syllabus

\n

KIMBLE et\xc2\xa0al. v. MARVEL ENTERTAINMENT, LLC, successor to MARVEL ENTERPRISES, INC.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 13\xe2\x80\x93720.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 31, 2015\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 22, 2015

\n

Respondent Marvel Entertainment\xe2\x80\x99s corporate predecessor agreed to purchase petitioner Stephen Kimble\xe2\x80\x99s patent for a Spider-Man toy in exchange for a lump sum plus a 3% royalty on future sales. The agreement set no end date for royalties. As the patent neared the end of its statutory 20-year term, Marvel discovered Brulotte v. Thys Co.,\n\n379 U.\xc2\xa0S. 29\n, in which this Court held that a patentee cannot continue to receive royalties for sales made after his patent expires. Marvel then sought a declaratory judgment in federal district court confirming that it could stop paying Kimble royalties. The district court granted relief, and the Ninth Circuit affirmed. Kimble now asks this Court to overrule Brulotte.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Stare decisis requires this Court to adhere to Brulotte. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0A patent typically expires 20 years from its application date.\n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7154(a)(2). At that point, the unrestricted right to make or use the article passes to the public. See Sears, Roebuck & Co. v. Stiffel Co.,\n\n376 U.\xc2\xa0S. 225\n. This Court has carefully guarded the significance of that expiration date, declining to enforce laws and contracts that restrict free public access to formerly patented, as well as unpatentable, inventions. See, e.g., id., at 230\xe2\x80\x93233; Scott Paper Co. v. Marcalus Mfg. Co.,\n\n326 U.\xc2\xa0S. 249\n\xe2\x80\x93256.

\n

Brulotte applied that principle to a patent licensing agreement that provided for the payment of royalties accruing after the patent\xe2\x80\x99s expiration. 379 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 30. The Court held that the post-patent royalty provision was \xe2\x80\x9cunlawful per\xc2\xa0se,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 30, 32, because it continued \xe2\x80\x9cthe patent monopoly beyond the [patent] period,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 33, and, in so doing, conflicted with patent law\xe2\x80\x99s policy of establishing a \xe2\x80\x9cpost-expiration .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. public domain,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid.

\n

The Brulotte rule may prevent some parties from entering into deals they desire, but parties can often find ways to achieve similar outcomes. For example, Brulotte leaves parties free to defer payments for pre-expiration use of a patent, tie royalties to non-patent rights, or make non-royalty-based business arrangements. Contending that such alternatives are not enough, Kimble asks this Court to abandon Brulotte\xe2\x80\x99s bright-line rule in favor of a case-by-case approach based on antitrust law\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9crule of reason.\xe2\x80\x9d Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0The doctrine of stare decisis provides that today\xe2\x80\x99s Court should stand by yesterday\xe2\x80\x99s decisions. Application of that doctrine, though \xe2\x80\x9cnot an inexorable command,\xe2\x80\x9d is the \xe2\x80\x9cpreferred course.\xe2\x80\x9d Payne v. Tennessee,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 808\n. Overruling a case always requires \xe2\x80\x9cspecial justification\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94over and above the belief \xe2\x80\x9cthat the precedent was wrongly decided.\xe2\x80\x9d Halliburton Co. v. Erica P. John Fund, Inc., 573 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. Where, as here, the precedent interprets a statute, stare decisis carries enhanced force, since critics are free to take their objections to Congress. See e.g., Patterson v. McLean Credit Union,\n\n491 U.\xc2\xa0S. 164\n\xe2\x80\x93173. Congress, moreover, has spurned multiple opportunities to reverse Brulotte, see Watson v. United States,\n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 74\n\xe2\x80\x9383, and has even rebuffed bills that would have replaced Brulotte\xe2\x80\x99s per\xc2\xa0se rule with the standard Kimble urges. In addition, Brulotte implicates property and contract law, two contexts in which considerations favoring stare decisis are \xe2\x80\x9cat their acme,\xe2\x80\x9d Payne, 501 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 828, because parties are especially likely to rely on such precedents when ordering their affairs.

\n

Given those good reasons for adhering to stare decisis in this case, this Court would need a very strong justification for overruling Brulotte. But traditional justifications for abandoning stare decisis do not help Kimble here. First, Brulotte\xe2\x80\x99s doctrinal underpinnings have not eroded over time. The patent statute at issue in Brulotte is essentially unchanged. And the precedent on which the Brulotte Court primarily relied, like other decisions enforcing a patent\xe2\x80\x99s cut-off date, remains good law. Indeed, Brulotte\xe2\x80\x99s close relation to a whole web of precedents means that overruling it could threaten others. Second, nothing about Brulotte has proved unworkable. See Patterson, 491 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 173. To the contrary, the decision itself is simple to apply\xe2\x80\x94particularly as compared to Kimble\xe2\x80\x99s proposed alternative, which can produce high litigation costs and unpredictable results. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0Neither of the justifications Kimble offers gives cause to overrule Brulotte. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

(1) Kimble first argues the Brulotte hinged on an economic error\xe2\x80\x94i.e., an assumption that post-expiration royalties are always anticompetitive. This Court sees no error in Kimble\xe2\x80\x99s economic analysis. But even assuming Kimble is right that Brulotte relied on an economic misjudgment, Congress is the right entity to fix it. The patent laws are not like the Sherman Act, which gives courts exceptional authority to shape the law and reconsider precedent based on better economic analysis. Moreover, Kimble\xe2\x80\x99s argument is based not on evolving economic theory but rather on a claim that the Brulotte Court simply made the wrong call. That claim fails to clear stare decisis\xe2\x80\x99s high bar. In any event, Brulotte did not even turn on the notion that post-patent royalties harm competition. Instead, the Brulotte Court simply applied the categorical principle that all patent-related benefits must end when the patent term expires. Kimble\xe2\x80\x99s real complaint may go to the merits of that principle as a policy matter. But Congress, not this Court, gets to make patent policy. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

(2)\xc2\xa0Kimble also argues that Brulotte suppresses technological innovation and harms the national economy by preventing parties from reaching agreements to commercialize patents. This Court cannot tell whether that is true. Brulotte leaves parties free to enter alternative arrangements that may suffice to accomplish parties\xe2\x80\x99 payment deferral and risk-spreading goals. And neither Kimble nor his amici offer any empirical evidence connecting Brulotte to decreased innovation. In any event, claims about a statutory precedent\xe2\x80\x99s consequences for innovation are \xe2\x80\x9cmore appropriately addressed to Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Halliburton, 573 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___. Pp.\xc2\xa016\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

727 F.\xc2\xa03d 856, affirmed.

\n

STEPHEN KIMBLE, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. MARVEL ENTERTAINMENT, LLC, successor to MARVEL ENTERPRISES, INC.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[June 22, 2015]

\n

Justice Kagan delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

In Brulotte v. Thys Co.,\n\n379 U.\xc2\xa0S. 29 (1964)\n, this Court held that a patent holder cannot charge royalties for the use of his invention after its patent term has expired. The sole question presented here is whether we should overrule Brulotte. Adhering to principles of stare decisis, we decline to do so. Critics of the Brulotte rule must seek relief not from this Court but from Congress.

\n

I

\n

In 1990, petitioner Stephen Kimble obtained a patent on a toy that allows children (and young-at-heart adults) to role-play as \xe2\x80\x9ca spider person\xe2\x80\x9d by shooting webs\xe2\x80\x94really, pressurized foam string\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cfrom the palm of [the] hand.\xe2\x80\x9d U.\xc2\xa0S. Patent No. 5,072,856, Abstract (filed May 25, 1990).[1] Respondent Marvel Entertainment, LLC (Marvel) makes and markets products featuring Spider-Man, among other comic-book characters. Seeking to sell or license his patent, Kimble met with the president of Marvel\xe2\x80\x99s corporate predecessor to discuss his idea for web-slinging fun. Soon afterward, but without remunerating Kimble, that com-pany began marketing the \xe2\x80\x9cWeb Blaster\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94a toy that, like Kimble\xe2\x80\x99s patented invention, enables would-be action heroes to mimic Spider-Man through the use of a polyester glove and a canister of foam.

\n

Kimble sued Marvel in 1997 alleging, among other things, patent infringement. The parties ultimately settled that litigation. Their agreement provided that Marvel would purchase Kimble\xe2\x80\x99s patent in exchange for a lump sum (of about a half-million dollars) and a 3% royalty on Marvel\xe2\x80\x99s future sales of the Web Blaster and similar products. The parties set no end date for royalties, apparently contemplating that they would continue for as long as kids want to imitate Spider-Man (by doing whatever a spider can).

\n

And then Marvel stumbled across Brulotte, the case at the heart of this dispute. In negotiating the settlement, neither side was aware of Brulotte. But Marvel must have been pleased to learn of it. Brulotte had read the patent laws to prevent a patentee from receiving royalties for sales made after his patent\xe2\x80\x99s expiration. See 379 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 32. So the decision\xe2\x80\x99s effect was to sunset the settlement\xe2\x80\x99s royalty clause.[2] On making that discovery, Marvel sought a declaratory judgment in federal district court confirming that the company could cease paying royalties come 2010\xe2\x80\x94the end of Kimble\xe2\x80\x99s patent term. The court approved that relief, holding that Brulotte made \xe2\x80\x9cthe royalty provision .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. unenforceable after the expiration of the Kimble patent.\xe2\x80\x9d 692 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1156, 1161 (Ariz. 2010). The Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit affirmed, though making clear that it was none too happy about doing so. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he Brulotte rule,\xe2\x80\x9d the court complained, \xe2\x80\x9cis counterintuitive and its rationale is arguably unconvincing.\xe2\x80\x9d 727 F.\xc2\xa03d 856, 857 (2013).

\n

We granted certiorari, 574 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014), to decide whether, as some courts and commentators have suggested, we should overrule Brulotte.[3] For reasons of stare decisis, we demur.

\n

II

\n

Patents endow their holders with certain superpowers, but only for a limited time. In crafting the patent laws, Congress struck a balance between fostering innovation and ensuring public access to discoveries. While a patent lasts, the patentee possesses exclusive rights to the patented article\xe2\x80\x94rights he may sell or license for royalty payments if he so chooses. See\n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7154(a)(1). But a patent typically expires 20 years from the day the application for it was filed. See \xc2\xa7154(a)(2). And when the patent expires, the patentee\xe2\x80\x99s prerogatives expire too, and the right to make or use the article, free from all restriction, passes to the public. See Sears, Roebuck & Co. v. Stiffel Co.,\n\n376 U.\xc2\xa0S. 225,\n230 (1964)\n.

\n

This Court has carefully guarded that cut-off date, just as it has the patent laws\xe2\x80\x99 subject-matter limits: In case after case, the Court has construed those laws to preclude measures that restrict free access to formerly patented, as well as unpatentable, inventions. In one line of cases, we have struck down state statutes with that consequence. See, e.g., id., at 230\xe2\x80\x93233; Bonito Boats, Inc. v. Thunder Craft Boats, Inc.,\n\n489 U.\xc2\xa0S. 141\n\xe2\x80\x93168 (1989); Compco Corp. v. Day-Brite Lighting, Inc.,\n\n376 U.\xc2\xa0S. 234\n\xe2\x80\x93238 (1964). By virtue of federal law, we reasoned, \xe2\x80\x9can article on which the patent has expired,\xe2\x80\x9d like an unpatentable article, \xe2\x80\x9cis in the public domain and may be made and sold by whoever chooses to do so.\xe2\x80\x9d Sears, 376 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 231. In a related line of decisions, we have deemed unenforceable private contract provisions limiting free use of such inventions. In Scott Paper Co. v. Marcalus Mfg. Co.,\n\n326 U.\xc2\xa0S. 249 (1945)\n, for example, we determined that a manufacturer could not agree to refrain from challenging a patent\xe2\x80\x99s validity. Allowing even a single com-pany to restrict its use of an expired or invalid patent, we explained, \xe2\x80\x9cwould deprive .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the consuming public of the advantage to be derived\xe2\x80\x9d from free exploitation of the discovery. Id., at 256. And to permit such a result, whether or not authorized \xe2\x80\x9cby express contract,\xe2\x80\x9d would impermissibly undermine the patent laws. Id., at 255\xe2\x80\x93256; see also, e.g., Edward Katzinger Co. v. Chicago Metallic Mfg. Co.,\n\n329 U.\xc2\xa0S. 394\n\xe2\x80\x93401 (1947) (ruling that Scott Paper applies to licensees); Lear, Inc. v. Adkins,\n\n395 U.\xc2\xa0S. 653\n\xe2\x80\x93675 (1969) (refusing to enforce a contract requiring a licensee to pay royalties while contesting a patent\xe2\x80\x99s validity).

\n

Brulotte was brewed in the same barrel. There, an inventor licensed his patented hop-picking machine to farmers in exchange for royalties from hop crops harvested both before and after his patents\xe2\x80\x99 expiration dates. The Court (by an 8-1 vote) held the agreement unenforceable\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cunlawful per\xc2\xa0se\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94to the extent it provided for the payment of royalties \xe2\x80\x9caccru[ing] after the last of the patents incorporated into the machines had expired.\xe2\x80\x9d 379 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 30, 32. To arrive at that conclusion, the Court began with the statutory provision setting the length of a patent term. See id., at 30 (quoting the then-current version of \xc2\xa7154). Emphasizing that a patented invention \xe2\x80\x9cbecome[s] public property once [that term] expires,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court then quoted from Scott Paper: Any attempt to limit a licensee\xe2\x80\x99s post-expiration use of the invention, \xe2\x80\x9cwhatever the legal device employed, runs counter to the policy and purpose of the patent laws.\xe2\x80\x9d 379 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 31 (quoting 326 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 256). In the Brulotte Court\xe2\x80\x99s view, contracts to pay royalties for such use continue \xe2\x80\x9cthe patent monopoly beyond the [patent] period,\xe2\x80\x9d even though only as to the licensee affected. 379 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 33. And in so doing, those agreements conflict with patent law\xe2\x80\x99s policy of establishing a \xe2\x80\x9cpost-expiration . . . public domain\xe2\x80\x9d in which every person can make free use of a formerly patented product. Ibid.

\n

The Brulotte rule, like others making contract provisions unenforceable, prevents some parties from entering into deals they desire. As compared to lump-sum fees, royalty plans both draw out payments over time and tie those payments, in each month or year covered, to a product\xe2\x80\x99s commercial success. And sometimes, for some parties, the longer the arrangement lasts, the better\xe2\x80\x94not just up to but beyond a patent term\xe2\x80\x99s end. A more extended payment period, coupled (as it presumably would be) with a lower rate, may bring the price the patent holder seeks within the range of a cash-strapped licensee. (Anyone who has bought a product on installment can relate.) See Brief for Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 17. Or such an extended term may better allocate the risks and rewards associated with commercializing inventions\xe2\x80\x94most notably, when years of development work stand between licensing a patent and bringing a product to market. See, e.g., 3 R. Milgrim & E. Bensen, Milgrim on Licensing \xc2\xa718.05, p. 18\xe2\x80\x939 (2013). As to either goal, Brulotte may pose an obstacle.

\n

Yet parties can often find ways around Brulotte, enabling them to achieve those same ends. To start, Brulotte allows a licensee to defer payments for pre-expiration use of a patent into the post-expiration period; all the decision bars are royalties for using an invention after it has moved into the public domain. See 379 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 31; Zenith Radio Corp. v. Hazeltine Research, Inc.,\n\n395 U.\xc2\xa0S. 100,\n136 (1969)\n. A licensee could agree, for example, to pay the licensor a sum equal to 10% of sales during the 20-year patent term, but to amortize that amount over 40 years. That arrangement would at least bring down early outlays, even if it would not do everything the parties might want to allocate risk over a long timeframe. And parties have still more options when a licensing agreement covers either multiple patents or additional non-patent rights. Under Brulotte, royalties may run until the latest-running patent covered in the parties\xe2\x80\x99 agreement expires. See 379 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 30. Too, post-expiration royalties are allowable so long as tied to a non-patent right\xe2\x80\x94even when closely related to a patent. See, e.g., 3 Milgrim on Licensing \xc2\xa718.07, at 18\xe2\x80\x9316 to 18\xe2\x80\x9317. That means, for example, that a license involving both a patent and a trade secret can set a 5% royalty during the patent period (as compensation for the two combined) and a 4% royalty afterward (as payment for the trade secret alone). Finally and most broadly, Brulotte poses no bar to business arrangements other than royalties\xe2\x80\x94all kinds of joint ventures, for example\xe2\x80\x94that enable parties to share the risks and rewards of commercializing an invention.

\n

Contending that such alternatives are not enough, Kimble asks us to abandon Brulotte in favor of \xe2\x80\x9cflexible, case-by-case analysis\xe2\x80\x9d of post-expiration royalty clauses \xe2\x80\x9cunder the rule of reason.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 45. Used in antitrust law, the rule of reason requires courts to evaluate a practice\xe2\x80\x99s effect on competition by \xe2\x80\x9ctaking into account a variety of factors, including specific information about the relevant business, its condition before and after the [practice] was imposed, and the [practice\xe2\x80\x99s] history, nature, and effect.\xe2\x80\x9d State Oil Co. v. Khan,\n\n522 U.\xc2\xa0S. 3,\n10 (1997)\n. Of primary importance in this context, Kimble posits, is whether a patent holder has power in the relevant market and so might be able to curtail competition. See Brief for Petitioners 47\xe2\x80\x9348; Illinois Tool Works Inc. v. Independent Ink, Inc.,\n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 28,\n44 (2006)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] patent does not necessarily confer market power\xe2\x80\x9d). Resolving that issue, Kimble notes, entails \xe2\x80\x9ca full-fledged economic inquiry into the definition of the market, barriers to entry, and the like.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 48 (quoting 1 H. Hovenkamp, M. Janis, M. Lemley, & C. Leslie, IP and Antitrust \xc2\xa73.2e, p.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9312.1 (2d ed., Supp. 2014) (Hovenkamp)).

\n

III

\n

Overruling precedent is never a small matter. Stare decisis\xe2\x80\x94in English, the idea that today\xe2\x80\x99s Court should stand by yesterday\xe2\x80\x99s decisions\xe2\x80\x94is \xe2\x80\x9ca foundation stone of the rule of law.\xe2\x80\x9d Michigan v. Bay Mills Indian Commu-nity, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 15). Application of that doctrine, although \xe2\x80\x9cnot an inexorable command,\xe2\x80\x9d is the \xe2\x80\x9cpreferred course because it promotes the evenhanded, predictable, and consistent development of legal principles, fosters reliance on judicial decisions, and contributes to the actual and perceived integrity of the judicial process.\xe2\x80\x9d Payne v. Tennessee,\n\n501 U. S. 808\n\xe2\x80\x93828 (1991). It also reduces incentives for challenging settled precedents, saving parties and courts the expense of endless relitigation.

\n

Respecting stare decisis means sticking to some wrong decisions. The doctrine rests on the idea, as Justice Brandeis famously wrote, that it is usually \xe2\x80\x9cmore important that the applicable rule of law be settled than that it be settled right.\xe2\x80\x9d Burnet v. Coronado Oil & Gas Co., 285 U.\xc2\xa0S. 393, 406 (1932) (dissenting opinion). Indeed, stare decisis has consequence only to the extent it sustains incorrect decisions; correct judgments have no need for that principle to prop them up. Accordingly, an argument that we got something wrong\xe2\x80\x94even a good argument to that effect\xe2\x80\x94cannot by itself justify scrapping settled precedent. Or otherwise said, it is not alone sufficient that we would decide a case differently now than we did then. To reverse course, we require as well what we have termed a \xe2\x80\x9cspecial justification\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94over and above the belief \xe2\x80\x9cthat the precedent was wrongly decided.\xe2\x80\x9d Halliburton Co. v. Erica P. John Fund, Inc., 573 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 4).

\n

What is more, stare decisis carries enhanced force when a decision, like Brulotte, interprets a statute. Then, unlike in a constitutional case, critics of our ruling can take their objections across the street, and Congress can correct any mistake it sees. See, e.g., Patterson v. McLean Credit Union,\n\n491 U. S. 164\n\xe2\x80\x93173 (1989). That is true, contrary to the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s view, see post, at 6\xe2\x80\x937 (opinion of Alito, J.), regardless whether our decision focused only on statutory text or also relied, as Brulotte did, on the policies and purposes animating the law. See, e.g., Bilski v. Kappos,\n\n561 U. S. 593\n\xe2\x80\x93602 (2010). Indeed, we apply statutory stare decisis even when a decision has announced a \xe2\x80\x9cjudicially created doctrine\xe2\x80\x9d designed to implement a federal statute. Halliburton, 573 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 12). All our interpretive decisions, in whatever way reasoned, effectively become part of the statutory scheme, subject (just like the rest) to congressional change. Absent special justification, they are balls tossed into Congress\xe2\x80\x99s court, for acceptance or not as that branch elects.

\n

And Congress has spurned multiple opportunities to reverse Brulotte\xe2\x80\x94openings as frequent and clear as this Court ever sees. Brulotte has governed licensing agreements for more than half a century. See Watson v. United States,\n\n552 U. S. 74\n\xe2\x80\x9383 (2007) (stating that \xe2\x80\x9clong congressional acquiescence,\xe2\x80\x9d there totaling just 14 years, \xe2\x80\x9cenhance[s] even the usual precedential force we accord to our interpretations of statutes\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). During that time, Congress has repeatedly amended the patent laws, including the specific provision (\n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7154) on which Brulotte rested. See, e.g., Uruguay Round Agreements Act, \xc2\xa7532(a),\n\n108Stat.\n4983 (1994) (increasing the length of the patent term); Act of Nov. 19, 1988, \xc2\xa7201,\n\n102Stat.\n4676 (limiting patent-misuse claims). Brulotte survived every such change. Indeed, Congress has rebuffed bills that would have replaced Brulotte\xe2\x80\x99s per\xc2\xa0se rule with the same antitrust-style analysis Kimble now urges. See, e.g., S. 1200, 100th Cong., 1st Sess., Tit. II (1987) (providing that no patent owner would be guilty of \xe2\x80\x9cillegal extension of the patent right by reason of his or her licensing practices .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. unless such practices .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. violate the antitrust laws\xe2\x80\x9d); S. 438, 100th Cong., 2d Sess., \xc2\xa7201(3) (1988) (same). Congress\xe2\x80\x99s continual reworking of the patent laws\xe2\x80\x94but never of the Brulotte rule\xe2\x80\x94further supports leaving the decision in place.

\n

Nor yet are we done, for the subject matter of Brulotte adds to the case for adhering to precedent. Brulotte lies at the intersection of two areas of law: property (patents) and contracts (licensing agreements). And we have often recognized that in just those contexts\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9ccases involving property and contract rights\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94considerations favoring stare decisis are \xe2\x80\x9cat their acme.\xe2\x80\x9d E.g., Payne, 501 U. S., at 828; Khan, 522 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 20. That is because parties are especially likely to rely on such precedents when ordering their affairs. To be sure, Marvel and Kimble disagree about whether Brulotte has actually generated reliance. Marvel says yes: Some parties, it claims, do not specify an end date for royalties in their licensing agreements, instead relying on Brulotte as a default rule. Brief for Respondent 32\xe2\x80\x9333; see 1 D. Epstein, Eckstrom\xe2\x80\x99s Licensing in Foreign and Domestic Operations \xc2\xa73.13, p.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9313, and n.\xc2\xa02 (2014) (noting that it is not \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary to specify the term .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of the license\xe2\x80\x9d when a decision like Brulotte limits it \xe2\x80\x9cby law\xe2\x80\x9d). Overturning Brulotte would thus upset expectations, most so when long-dormant licenses for long-expired patents spring back to life. Not true, says Kimble: Unfair surprise is unlikely, because no \xe2\x80\x9cmeaningful number of [such] license agreements .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. actually exist.\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief 18. To be honest, we do not know (nor, we suspect, do Marvel and Kimble). But even uncertainty on this score cuts in Marvel\xe2\x80\x99s direction. So long as we see a reasonable possibility that parties have structured their business transactions in light of Brulotte, we have one more reason to let it stand.

\n

As against this superpowered form of stare decisis, we would need a superspecial justification to warrant reversing Brulotte. But the kinds of reasons we have most often held sufficient in the past do not help Kimble here. If anything, they reinforce our unwillingness to do what he asks.

\n

First, Brulotte\xe2\x80\x99s statutory and doctrinal underpinnings have not eroded over time. When we reverse our statutory interpretations, we most often point to subsequent legal developments\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9ceither the growth of judicial doctrine or further action taken by Congress\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94that have removed the basis for a decision. Patterson, 491 U. S., at 173 (calling this \xe2\x80\x9cthe primary reason\xe2\x80\x9d for overruling statutory precedent). But the core feature of the patent laws on which Brulotte relied remains just the same: Section 154 now, as then, draws a sharp line cutting off patent rights after a set number of years. And this Court has continued to draw from that legislative choice a broad policy favoring unrestricted use of an invention after its patent\xe2\x80\x99s expiration. See supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x934. Scott Paper\xe2\x80\x94the decision on which Brulotte primarily relied\xe2\x80\x94remains good law. So too do this Court\xe2\x80\x99s other decisions refusing to enforce either state laws or private contracts constraining individuals\xe2\x80\x99 free use of formerly patented (or unpatentable) discoveries. See supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x934. Brulotte, then, is not the kind of doctrinal dinosaur or legal last-man-standing for whichwe sometimes depart from stare decisis. Compare, e.g., Alleyne v. United States, 570 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2013) (Sotomayor, J., concurring) (slip op., at 2\xe2\x80\x935). To the contrary, the decision\xe2\x80\x99s close relation to a whole web of precedents means that reversing it could threaten others. If Brulotte is outdated, then (for example) is Scott Paper too? We would prefer not to unsettle stable law.[4]

\n

And second, nothing about Brulotte has proved unworkable. See, e.g., Patterson, 491 U. S., at 173 (identifying unworkability as another \xe2\x80\x9ctraditional justification\xe2\x80\x9d for overruling precedent). The decision is simplicity itself to apply. A court need only ask whether a licensing agreement provides royalties for post-expiration use of a patent. If not, no problem; if so, no dice. Brulotte\xe2\x80\x99s ease of use appears in still sharper relief when compared to Kimble\xe2\x80\x99s proposed alternative. Recall that he wants courts toemploy antitrust law\xe2\x80\x99s rule of reason to identify and invali-date those post-expiration royalty clauses with anti-competitive consequences. See supra, at 6\xe2\x80\x937. But whatever its merits may be for deciding antitrust claims, that \xe2\x80\x9celaborate inquiry\xe2\x80\x9d produces notoriously high litigation costs and unpredictable results. Arizona v. Maricopa County Medical Soc.,\n\n457 U. S. 332,\n343 (1982)\n. For that reason, trading in Brulotte for the rule of reason would make the law less, not more, workable than it is now. Once again, then, the case for sticking with long-settled precedent grows stronger: Even the most usual reasons for abandoning stare decisis cut the other way here.

\n

IV

\n

Lacking recourse to those traditional justifications for overruling a prior decision, Kimble offers two different ones. He claims first that Brulotte rests on a mistaken view of the competitive effects of post-expiration royalties. He contends next that Brulotte suppresses technological innovation and so harms the nation\xe2\x80\x99s economy. (The dissent offers versions of those same arguments. See post, at 1\xe2\x80\x934.) We consider the two claims in turn, but our answers to both are much the same: Kimble\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning may give Congress cause to upset Brulotte, but does not warrant this Court\xe2\x80\x99s doing so.

\n

A

\n

According to Kimble, we should overrule Brulotte because it hinged on an error about economics: It assumed that post-patent royalty \xe2\x80\x9carrangements are invariably anticompetitive.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 37. That is not true, Kimble notes; indeed, such agreements more often increase than inhibit competition, both before and after the patent expires. See id., at 36\xe2\x80\x9340. As noted earlier, a longer payment period will typically go hand-in-hand with a lower royalty rate. See supra, at 5. During the patent term, those reduced rates may lead to lower consumer prices, making the patented technology more competitive with alternatives; too, the lesser rates may enable more companies to afford a license, fostering competition among the patent\xe2\x80\x99s own users. See Brief for Petitioners 38. And after the patent\xe2\x80\x99s expiration, Kimble continues, further benefits follow: Absent high barriers to entry (a material caveat, as even he would agree, see Tr. of Oral Arg. 12\xe2\x80\x9313, 23), the licensee\xe2\x80\x99s continuing obligation to pay royalties encourages new companies to begin making the product, figuring that they can quickly attract customers by undercutting the licensee on price. See Brief for Petitioners 38\xe2\x80\x9339. In light of those realities, Kimble concludes, \xe2\x80\x9cthe Brulotte per\xc2\xa0se rule makes little sense.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 11.

\n

We do not join issue with Kimble\xe2\x80\x99s economics\xe2\x80\x94only with what follows from it. A broad scholarly consensus supports Kimble\xe2\x80\x99s view of the competitive effects of post-expiration royalties, and we see no error in that shared analysis. See id., at 13\xe2\x80\x9318 (citing numerous treatises and articles critiquing Brulotte). Still, we must decide what that means for Brulotte. Kimble, of course, says it means the decision must go. Positing that Brulotte turned on the belief that post-expiration royalties are always anticompetitive, he invokes decisions in which this Court abandoned antitrust precedents premised on similarly shaky economic reasoning. See Brief for Petitioners 55\xe2\x80\x9356 (citing, e.g., Leegin Creative Leather Products, Inc. v. PSKS, Inc.,\n\n551 U.\xc2\xa0S. 877 (2007)\n; Illinois Tool Works,\n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 28\n). But to agree with Kimble\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion, we must resolve two questions in his favor. First, even assuming Kimble accurately characterizes Brulotte\xe2\x80\x99s basis, does the decision\xe2\x80\x99s economic mistake suffice to overcome stare decisis? Second and more fundamentally, was Brulotte actually founded, as Kimble contends, on an analysis of competitive effects?

\n

If Brulotte were an antitrust rather than a patent case, we might answer both questions as Kimble would like. This Court has viewed stare decisis as having less-than-usual force in cases involving the Sherman Act. See, e.g., Khan, 522 U. S., at 20\xe2\x80\x9321. Congress, we have explained, intended that law\xe2\x80\x99s reference to \xe2\x80\x9crestraint of trade\xe2\x80\x9d to have \xe2\x80\x9cchanging content,\xe2\x80\x9d and authorized courts to oversee the term\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cdynamic potential.\xe2\x80\x9d Business Electronics Corp. v. Sharp Electronics Corp.,\n\n485 U.\xc2\xa0S. 717\n\xe2\x80\x93732 (1988). We have therefore felt relatively free to revise our legal analysis as economic understanding evolves and (just as Kimble notes) to reverse antitrust precedents that misperceived a practice\xe2\x80\x99s competitive consequences. See Leegin, 551 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 899\xe2\x80\x93900. Moreover, because the question in those cases was whether the challenged activity restrained trade, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s rulings necessarily turned on its understanding of economics. See Business Electronics Corp., 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 731. Accordingly, to overturn the decisions in light of sounder economic reasoning was to take them \xe2\x80\x9con [their] own terms.\xe2\x80\x9d Halliburton, 573 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 9).

\n

But Brulotte is a patent rather than an antitrust case, and our answers to both questions instead go against Kimble. To begin, even assuming that Brulotte relied on an economic misjudgment, Congress is the right entity to fix it. By contrast with the Sherman Act, the patent laws do not turn over exceptional law-shaping authority to the courts. Accordingly, statutory stare decisis\xe2\x80\x94in which this Court interprets and Congress decides whether to amend\xe2\x80\x94retains its usual strong force. See supra, at 8. And as we have shown, that doctrine does not ordinarily bend to \xe2\x80\x9cwrong on the merits\xe2\x80\x9d-type arguments; it instead assumes Congress will correct whatever mistakes we commit. See supra, at 7\xe2\x80\x938. Nor does Kimble offer any reason to think his own \xe2\x80\x9cthe Court erred\xe2\x80\x9d claim is special. Indeed, he does not even point to anything that has changed since Brulotte\xe2\x80\x94no new empirical studies or advances in economic theory. Compare, e.g., Halliburton, 573 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 9\xe2\x80\x9312) (considering, though finding insufficient, recent economic research). On his argument, the Brulotte Court knew all it needed to know to determine that post-patent royalties are not usually anticompetitive; it just made the wrong call. See Brief for Petitioners 36\xe2\x80\x9340. That claim, even if itself dead-right, fails to clear stare decisis\xe2\x80\x99s high bar.

\n

And in any event, Brulotte did not hinge on the mistake Kimble identifies. Although some of its language invoked economic concepts, see n.\xc2\xa04, supra, the Court did not rely on the notion that post-patent royalties harm competition. Nor is that surprising. The patent laws\xe2\x80\x94unlike the Sherman Act\xe2\x80\x94do not aim to maximize competition (to a large extent, the opposite). And the patent term\xe2\x80\x94unlike the \xe2\x80\x9crestraint of trade\xe2\x80\x9d standard\xe2\x80\x94provides an all-encompassing bright-line rule, rather than calling for practice-specific analysis. So in deciding whether post-expiration royalties comport with patent law, Brulotte did not undertake to assess that practice\xe2\x80\x99s likely competitive effects. Instead, it applied a categorical principle that all patents, and all benefits from them, must end when their terms expire. See Brulotte, 379 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 30\xe2\x80\x9332; supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x935. Or more specifically put, the Court held, as it had in Scott Paper, that Congress had made a judgment: that the day after a patent lapses, the formerly protected invention must be available to all for free. And further: that post-expiration restraints on even a single licensee\xe2\x80\x99s access to the invention clash with that principle. See Brulotte, 379 U. S., at 31\xe2\x80\x9332 (a licensee\xe2\x80\x99s obligation to pay post-patent royalties conflicts with the \xe2\x80\x9cfree market visualized for the post-expiration period\xe2\x80\x9d and so \xe2\x80\x9cruns counter to the policy and purpose of the patent laws\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Scott Paper, 326 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 256)). That patent (not antitrust) policy gave rise to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that post-patent royalty contracts are unenforceable\xe2\x80\x94utterly \xe2\x80\x9cregardless of a demonstrable effect on competition.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 Hovenkamp \xc2\xa73.2d, at 3\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

Kimble\xe2\x80\x99s real complaint may go to the merits of such a patent policy\xe2\x80\x94what he terms its \xe2\x80\x9cformalis[m],\xe2\x80\x9d its \xe2\x80\x9crigid[ity]\xe2\x80\x9d, and its detachment from \xe2\x80\x9ceconomic reality.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 27\xe2\x80\x9328. But that is just a different version of the argument that Brulotte is wrong. And it is, if anything, a version less capable than the last of trumping statutory stare decisis. For the choice of what patent policy should be lies first and foremost with Congress. So if Kimble thinks patent law\xe2\x80\x99s insistence on unrestricted access to formerly patented inventions leaves too little room for pro-competitive post-expiration royalties, then Congress, not this Court, is his proper audience.

\n

B

\n

Kimble also seeks support from the wellspring of all patent policy: the goal of promoting innovation. Brulotte, he contends, \xe2\x80\x9cdiscourages technological innovation and does significant damage to the American economy.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 29. Recall that would-be licensors and licensees may benefit from post-patent royalty arrangements because they allow for a longer payment period and a more precise allocation of risk. See supra, at 5. If the parties\xe2\x80\x99 ideal licensing agreement is barred, Kimble reasons, they may reach no agreement at all. See Brief for Petitioners 32. And that possibility may discourage invention in the first instance. The bottom line, Kimble concludes, is that some \xe2\x80\x9cbreakthrough technologies will never see the light of day.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 33.

\n

Maybe. Or, then again, maybe not. While we recognize that post-patent royalties are sometimes not anticompetitive, we just cannot say whether barring them imposes any meaningful drag on innovation. As we have explained, Brulotte leaves open various ways\xe2\x80\x94involving both licensing and other business arrangements\xe2\x80\x94to accomplish payment deferral and risk-spreading alike. See supra, at 6. Those alternatives may not offer the parties the precise set of benefits and obligations they would prefer. But they might still suffice to bring patent holders and product developers together and ensure that inventions get to the public. Neither Kimble nor his amici have offered any empirical evidence connecting Brulotte to decreased innovation; they essentially ask us to take their word for the problem. And the United States, which acts as both a licensor and a licensee of patented inventions while also implementing patent policy, vigorously disputes that Brulotte has caused any \xe2\x80\x9csignificant real-world economic harm.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 30. Truth be told, if forced to decide that issue, we would not know where or how to start.

\n

Which is one good reason why that is not our job. Claims that a statutory precedent has \xe2\x80\x9cserious and harmful consequences\xe2\x80\x9d for innovation are (to repeat this opinion\xe2\x80\x99s refrain) \xe2\x80\x9cmore appropriately addressed to Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Halliburton, 573 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 15). That branch, far more than this one, has the capacity to assess Kimble\xe2\x80\x99s charge that Brulotte suppresses technological progress. And if it concludes that Brulotte works such harm, Congress has the prerogative to determine the exact right response\xe2\x80\x94choosing the policy fix, among many conceivable ones, that will optimally serve the public interest. As we have noted, Congress legislates actively with respect to patents, considering concerns of just the kind Kimble raises. See supra, at 9. In adhering to our precedent as against such complaints, we promote the rule-of-law values to which courts must attend while leaving matters of public policy to Congress.

\n

V

\n

What we can decide, we can undecide. But stare decisis teaches that we should exercise that authority sparingly. Cf. S. Lee and S. Ditko, Amazing Fantasy No. 15: \xe2\x80\x9cSpider-Man,\xe2\x80\x9d p. 13 (1962) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]n this world, with great power there must also come\xe2\x80\x94great responsibility\xe2\x80\x9d). Finding many reasons for staying the stare decisis course and no \xe2\x80\x9cspecial justification\xe2\x80\x9d for departing from it, we decline Kimble\xe2\x80\x99s invitation to overrule Brulotte.

\n

For the reasons stated, the judgment of the Court of Appeals is affirmed.

\n

It is so ordered.

STEPHEN KIMBLE, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. MARVEL ENTERTAINMENT, LLC, successor to MARVEL ENTERPRISES, INC.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[June 22, 2015]

\n

\n

Justice Alito, with whom The Chief Justice and Justice Thomas join, dissenting.

\n

The Court employs stare decisis, normally a tool of restraint, to reaffirm a clear case of judicial overreach. Our decision in Brulotte v. Thys Co.,\n\n379 U.\xc2\xa0S. 29 (1964)\n, held that parties cannot enter into a patent licensing agreement that provides for royalty payments to continue after the term of the patent expires. That decision was not based on anything that can plausibly be regarded as an interpretation of the terms of the Patent Act. It was based instead on an economic theory\xe2\x80\x94and one that has been debunked. The decision interferes with the ability of parties to negotiate licensing agreements that reflect the true value of a patent, and it disrupts contractual expectations. Stare decisis does not require us to retain this baseless and damaging precedent.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

The Patent Act provides that a patent grants certain exclusive rights to the patentee and \xe2\x80\x9chis heirs or assigns\xe2\x80\x9d for a term of 20 years. 35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7154(a)(1) and (2). The Act says nothing whatsoever about post-expiration royalties. In Brulotte, however, the Court held that such royalties are per\xc2\xa0se unlawful. The Court made little pretense of finding support for this holding in the language of the Act. Instead, the Court reasoned that allowing post-expiration royalties would subject \xe2\x80\x9cthe free market visualized for the post-expiration period .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to monopoly influences that have no proper place there.\xe2\x80\x9d 379 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 32\xe2\x80\x9333. Invoking antitrust concepts, the decision suggested that such arrangements are \xe2\x80\x9can effort to enlarge the monopoly of the patent by t[y]ing the sale or use of the patented article to the purchase or use of unpatented ones.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 33.

\n

Whatever the merits of this economic argument, it does not represent a serious attempt to interpret the Patent Act. A licensing agreement that provides for the payment of royalties after a patent\xe2\x80\x99s term expires does not enlarge the patentee\xe2\x80\x99s monopoly or extend the term of the patent. It simply gives the licensor a contractual right. Thus, nothing in the text of the Act even arguably forbids licensing agreements that provide for post-expiration royalties.

\n

Brulotte was thus a bald act of policymaking. It was not simply a case of incorrect statutory interpretation. It was not really statutory interpretation at all.

\n

B

\n

Not only was Brulotte based on policymaking, it was based on a policy that is difficult to defend. Indeed, in the intervening 50 years, its reasoning has been soundly refuted. See, e.g., 10 P. Areeda & H. Hovenkamp, Antitrust Law: An Analysis of Antitrust Principles and Their Application \xc2\xb61782c.3, pp. 554\xe2\x80\x93556 (3d ed. 2011); See & Caprio, The Trouble with Brulotte: The Patent Royalty Term and Patent Monopoly Extension, 1990 Utah L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 813, 846\xe2\x80\x93851; Scheiber v. Dolby Labs., Inc., 293 F.\xc2\xa03d 1014, 1017 (CA7 2002); Brief for Petitioners 23\xe2\x80\x9325, and n.\xc2\xa011 (collecting sources); ante, at 3, n.\xc2\xa03.

\n

Brulotte misperceived the purpose and effect of post-expiration royalties. The decision rested on the view that post-expiration royalties extend the patent term by means of an anti-competitive tying arrangement. As the Court understood such an arrangement, the patent holder leverages its monopoly power during the patent term to require payments after the term ends, when the invention would otherwise be available for free public use. But agreements to pay licensing fees after a patent expires do not \xe2\x80\x9cenlarge the monopoly of the patent.\xe2\x80\x9d 379 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 33. Instead, \xe2\x80\x9c[o]nce the patent term expires, the power to exclude is gone,\xe2\x80\x9d and all that is left \xe2\x80\x9cis a problem about optimal contract design.\xe2\x80\x9d Easterbrook, Contract and Copyright, 42 Hous. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 953, 955 (2005).

\n

The economics are simple: Extending a royalty term allows the parties to spread the licensing fees over a longer period of time, which naturally has the effect of reduc-ing the fees during the patent term. See ante, at 5. Restricting royalty payments to the patent term, as Brulotte requires, compresses payment into a shorter period of higher fees. The Patent Act does not prefer one approach over the other.

\n

There are, however, good reasons why parties sometimes prefer post-expiration royalties over upfront fees, and why such arrangements have pro-competitive effects. Patent holders and licensees are often unsure whether a patented idea will yield significant economic value, and it often takes years to monetize an innovation. In those circumstances, deferred royalty agreements are economically efficient. They encourage innovators, like universities, hospitals, and other institutions, to invest in research that might not yield marketable products until decades down the line. See Brief for Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 8\xe2\x80\x9312. And they allow producers to hedge their bets and develop more products by spreading licensing fees over longer periods. See ibid. By prohibiting these arrangements, Brulotte erects an obstacle to efficient patent use. In patent law and other areas, we have abandoned per\xc2\xa0se rules with similarly disruptive effects. See, e.g., Illinois Tool Works Inc. v. Independent Ink, Inc.,\n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 28 (2006)\n; Leegin Creative Leather Products, Inc. v. PSKS, Inc., \n\n551 U.\xc2\xa0S. 877 (2007)\n.

\n

The majority downplays this harm by insisting that \xe2\x80\x9cparties can often find ways around Brulotte.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 6. But the need to avoid Brulotte is an economic inefficiency in itself. Parties are not always aware of the prohibition\xe2\x80\x94as this case amply demonstrates. And the suggested alternatives do not provide the same benefits as post-expiration royalty agreements. For instance, although an agreement to amortize payments for sales during the patent term would \xe2\x80\x9cbring down early outlays,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court admits that such an agreement might not reflect the parties\xe2\x80\x99 risk preferences. Ante, at 6. Moreover, such an arrangement would not necessarily yield the same amount of total royalties, particularly for an invention or a medical breakthrough that takes decades to develop into a marketable product. The sort of agreements that Brulotte prohibits would allow licensees to spread their costs, while also allowing patent holders to capitalize on slow-developing inventions.

\n

C

\n

On top of that, Brulotte most often functions to upset the parties\xe2\x80\x99 expectations.

\n

This case illustrates the point. No one disputes that, when \xe2\x80\x9cnegotiating the settlement, neither side was aware of Brulotte.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 2. Without knowledge of our per\xc2\xa0se rule, the parties agreed that Marvel would pay 3% in royalties on all of its future sales involving the Web Blaster and similar products. If the parties had been aware of Brulotte, they might have agreed to higher payments during the patent term. Instead, both sides expected the royalty payments to continue until Marvel stopped selling toys that fit the terms of the agreement. But that is not what happened. When Marvel discovered Brulotte, it used that decision to nullify a key part of the agreement. The parties\xe2\x80\x99 contractual expectations were shattered, and petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 rights were extinguished.

\n

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion that some parties have come to rely on Brulotte is fanciful. The Court believes that there is a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable possibility that parties have structured their business transactions in light of Brulotte.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10. Its only support for this conclusion is Marvel\xe2\x80\x99s self-serving and unsupported assertion that some contracts might not specify an end date for royalties because the parties expect Brulotte to supply the default rule. To its credit, the Court stops short of endorsing this unlikely prediction, saying only that \xe2\x80\x9cuncertainty on this score cuts in Marvel\xe2\x80\x99s direction.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10.

\n

But there is no real uncertainty. \xe2\x80\x9c[W]e do not know\xe2\x80\x9d if Marvel\xe2\x80\x99s assertion is correct because Marvel has provided no evidence to support it. Ibid. And there are reasons to believe that, if parties actually relied on Brulotte to supply a default rule, courts would enforce the contracts as the parties expected. See, e.g., 27 R. Lord, Williston on Contracts \xc2\xa770:124 (4th ed. 2003). What we know for sure, however, is that Brulotte has upended the parties\xe2\x80\x99 expectations here and in many other cases. See, e.g., Scheiber, 293 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1016; Boggild v. Kenner Products, 853 F.\xc2\xa02d 465, 466\xe2\x80\x93467 (CA6 1988); Pitney Bowes, Inc. v. Mestre, 701 F.\xc2\xa02d 1365, 1367, 1373 (CA11 1983). These confirmed problems with retaining Brulotte clearly outweigh Marvel\xe2\x80\x99s hypothetical fears.

\n

II

\n

In the end, Brulotte\xe2\x80\x99s only virtue is that we decided it. But that does not render it invincible. Stare decisis is important to the rule of law, but so are correct judicial decisions. Adherence to prior decisions \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98promotes the evenhanded, predictable, and consistent development of legal principles, fosters reliance on judicial decisions, and contributes to the actual and perceived integrity of the judicial process.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Pearson v. Callahan,\n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 223,\n233 (2009)\n (quoting Payne v. Tennessee,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 808,\n827 (1991)\n). But stare decisis is not an \xe2\x80\x9cinexorable command.\xe2\x80\x9d Payne, supra, at 828; Washington v. W. C. Dawson & Co.,\n\n264 U.\xc2\xa0S. 219,\n238 (1924)\n (Brandeis, J., dissenting). \xe2\x80\x9cRevisiting precedent is particularly appropriate where, as here, a departure would not upset expectations, the precedent consists of a judge-made rule .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. , and experience has pointed up the precedent\xe2\x80\x99s shortcomings.\xe2\x80\x9d Pearson, supra, at 233.

\n

Our traditional approach to stare decisis does not require us to retain Brulotte\xe2\x80\x99s per\xc2\xa0se rule. Brulotte\xe2\x80\x99s holding had no basis in the law. Its reasoning has been thoroughly disproved. It poses economic barriers that stifle innovation. And it unsettles contractual expectations.

\n

It is not decisive that Congress could have altered Brulotte\xe2\x80\x99s rule. In general, we are especially reluctant to overturn decisions interpreting statutes because those decisions can be undone by Congress. See, e.g., John R. Sand & Gravel Co.\xc2\xa0v. United States,\n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 130,\n139 (2008)\n; Patterson v. McLean Credit Union,\n\n491 U.\xc2\xa0S. 164\n\xe2\x80\x93173 (1989). The Court calls this a \xe2\x80\x9csuperpowered form of stare decisis\xe2\x80\x9d that renders statutory interpretation decisions nearly impervious to challenge. Ante, at 10. I think this goes a bit too far.

\n

As an initial matter, we do not give super-duper protection to decisions that do not actually interpret a statute. When a precedent is based on a judge-made rule and is not grounded in anything that Congress has enacted, we cannot \xe2\x80\x9cproperly place on the shoulders of Congress\xe2\x80\x9d the entire burden of correcting \xe2\x80\x9cthe Court\xe2\x80\x99s own error.\xe2\x80\x9d Girouard v. United States,\n\n328 U.\xc2\xa0S. 61\n\xe2\x80\x9370 (1946). On the contrary, we have recognized that it is appropriate for us to correct rules of this sort. See, e.g., Leegin, 551 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 899\xe2\x80\x93900; State Oil Co. v. Khan,\n\n522 U.\xc2\xa0S. 3\n\xe2\x80\x9321 (1997).

\n

The Court says that it might agree if Brulotte were an antitrust precedent because stare decisis has \xe2\x80\x9cless-than-usual force in cases involving the Sherman Act.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14. But this distinction is unwarranted. We have been more willing to reexamine antitrust precedents because they have attributes of common-law decisions. I see no reason why the same approach should not apply where the precedent at issue, while purporting to apply a statute, is actually based on policy concerns. Indeed, we should be even more willing to reconsider such a precedent because the role implicitly assigned to the federal courts under the Sherman Act has no parallel in Patent Act cases.

\n

Even taking the Court on its own terms, Brulotte was an antitrust decision masquerading as a patent case. The Court was principally concerned with patentees improp-erly leveraging their monopoly power. See 379 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 32\xe2\x80\x9333. And it expressly characterized post-expiration royalties as anti-competitive tying arrangements. See id., at 33. It makes no sense to afford greater stare decisis protection to Brulotte\xe2\x80\x99s thinly veiled antitrust reasoning than to our Sherman Act decisions.

\n

The Court also places too much weight on Congress\xe2\x80\x99 failure to overturn Brulotte. We have long cautioned that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t is at best treacherous to find in congressional silence alone the adoption of a controlling rule of law.\xe2\x80\x9d Girouard, supra, at 69. Even where Congress has considered, but not adopted, legislation that would abrogate a judicial ruling, it cannot be inferred that Congress\xe2\x80\x99 failure to act shows that it approves the ruling. See Central Bank of Denver, N.\xc2\xa0A. v. First Interstate Bank of Denver, N.\xc2\xa0A.,\n\n511 U.\xc2\xa0S. 164,\n187 (1994)\n. \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[S]everal equally tenable inferences may be drawn from such inaction.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation v. LTV Corp., 496 U.\xc2\xa0S. 633, 650 (1990)).

\n

Passing legislation is no easy task. A federal statute must withstand the \xe2\x80\x9cfinely wrought\xe2\x80\x9d procedure of bicameralism and presentment. INS v. Chadha,\n\n462 U.\xc2\xa0S. 919,\n951 (1983)\n; Clinton v. City of New York,\n\n524 U.\xc2\xa0S. 417,\n440 (1998)\n; see U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Art. I, \xc2\xa77. Within that onerous process, there are additional practical hurdles. A law must be taken up for discussion and not passed over in favor of more pressing matters, and Senate rules require 60 votes to end debate on most legislation. And even if the House and Senate agree on a general policy, the details of the measure usually must be hammered out in a conference committee and repassed by both Houses.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

A proper understanding of our doctrine of stare decisis does not prevent us from reexamining Brulotte. Even the Court does not defend the decision on the merits. I would reconsider and overrule our obvious mistake. For these reasons, I respectfully dissent.
