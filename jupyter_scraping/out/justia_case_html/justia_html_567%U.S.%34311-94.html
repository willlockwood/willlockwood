\n\t\t\t\tSOUTHERN UNION CO. v. UNITED STATES\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the first circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 11\xe2\x80\x9394.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 19, 2012\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 21, 2012\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tPetitioner Southern Union Company was convicted by a jury in federal court on one count of violating the Resource Conservation and Recovery Act of 1976 (RCRA) for having knowingly stored liquid mercury without a permit at a subsidiary\xe2\x80\x99s facility \xe2\x80\x9con or about September 19, 2002 to October 19, 2004.\xe2\x80\x9d Violations of the RCRA are punishable by, inter alia, a fine of not more than $50,000 for each day of violation. \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa76928(d). At sentencing, the probation office calculated a maximum fine of $38.1 million, on the basis that Southern Union violated the RCRA for each of the 762 days from September 19, 2002, through October 19, 2004. Southern Union argued that imposing any fine greater than the 1-day penalty of $50,000 would be unconstitutional under Apprendi v. New Jersey, \n\n530 U.\xc2\xa0S. 466\n, which holds that the \n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s jury-trial guarantee requires that any fact (other than the fact of a prior conviction) that increases the maximum punishment authorized for a particular crime be proved to a jury beyond a reasonable doubt. Southern Union contended that, based on the jury verdict and the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s instructions, the only violation the jury necessarily found was for one day. The District Court held that Apprendi applies to criminal fines, but concluded from the \xe2\x80\x9ccontent and context of the verdict all together\xe2\x80\x9d that the jury found a 762-day violation. The court therefore set a maximum potential fine of $38.1 million, from which it imposed a fine of $6 million and a \xe2\x80\x9ccommunity service obligation\xe2\x80\x9d of $12 million. On appeal, the First Circuit disagreed with the District Court that the jury necessarily found a violation of 762 days. But the First Circuit affirmed the sentence because it held that Apprendi does not apply to criminal fines.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0The rule of Apprendi applies to the imposition of criminal fines. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x88\x9216.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0Apprendi\xe2\x80\x99s rule is \xe2\x80\x9crooted in longstanding common-law practice,\xe2\x80\x9d Cunningham v. California, \n\n549 U.\xc2\xa0S. 270\n, and preserves the \xe2\x80\x9chistoric jury function\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9cdetermining whether the prosecution has proved each element of an offense beyond a reasonable doubt,\xe2\x80\x9d Oregon v. Ice, \n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 160\n. This Court has repeatedly affirmed Apprendi\xe2\x80\x99s rule by applying it to a variety of sentencing schemes that allow judges to find facts that increase a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s maximum authorized sentence. See Cunningham, 549 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 274\xe2\x88\x92275; United States v. Booker, \n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 220\n\xe2\x80\x93227; Blakely v. Washington, \n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 296\n\xe2\x80\x93300; Ring v. Arizona, \n\n536 U.\xc2\xa0S. 584\n\xe2\x80\x93589; Ap-prendi, 530 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 468\xe2\x80\x93469. While the punishments at stake in these cases were imprisonment or a death sentence, there is no principled basis under Apprendi to treat criminal fines differently. Apprendi\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccore concern\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94to reserve to the jury \xe2\x80\x9cthe determination of facts that warrant punishment for a specific statutory offense,\xe2\x80\x9d Ice, 555 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 170\xe2\x80\x94applies whether the sentence is a criminal fine or imprisonment or death. Criminal fines, like these other forms of punishment, are penalties inflicted by the sovereign for the commission of offenses. Fines were by far the most common form of noncapital punishment in colonial America and they continue to be frequently imposed today. And, the amount of a fine, like the maximum term of imprisonment or eligibility for the death penalty, is often determined by reference to particular facts. The Government argues that fines are less onerous than incarceration and the death sentence and therefore should be exempt from Apprendi. But where a fine is substantial enough to trigger the \n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s jury-trial guarantee, Apprendi applies in full. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x88\x928.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0The \xe2\x80\x9chistorical role of the jury at common law,\xe2\x80\x9d which informs the \xe2\x80\x9cscope of the constitutional jury right,\xe2\x80\x9d Ice, 555 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 170, supports applying Apprendi to criminal fines. To be sure, judges in the colonies and during the founding era had much discretion in determining whether to impose a fine and in what amount. But the exercise of such discretion is fully consistent with Apprendi, which permits courts to impose \xe2\x80\x9cjudgment within the range prescribed by statute.\xe2\x80\x9d 530 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 481 (emphasis in original). The more salient question is what role the jury played in prosecutions for offenses that pegged the amount of a fine to the determination of specified facts. A review of both state and federal decisions discloses that the predominant practice was for such facts to be alleged in the indictment and proved to the jury. The rule that juries must determine facts that set a fine\xe2\x80\x99s maximum amount is an application of the \xe2\x80\x9ctwo longstanding tenets of common-law criminal jurisprudence\xe2\x80\x9d on which Apprendi is based: first, \xe2\x80\x9cthe \xe2\x80\x98truth of every accusation\xe2\x80\x99 against a defendant \xe2\x80\x98should afterwards be confirmed by the unanimous suffrage of twelve of his equals and neighbours.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Blakely, 542 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 301. And second, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98an accusation which lacks any particular fact which the law makes essential to the punishment is .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. no accusation within the requirements of the common law, and is no accusation in reason.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Contrary to the Government\xe2\x80\x99s contentions, neither United States v. Murphy, 16 Pet. 203, nor United States v. Tyler, 7 Cranch 285, overcomes the ample historical evidence that juries routinely found facts that set maximum criminal fines. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x88\x9214.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0The Government\xe2\x80\x99s remaining arguments, echoed by the dissent, are unpersuasive. The Government claims that facts relevant to a fine\xe2\x80\x99s amount typically quantify the harm caused by the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s offense, and do not define a separate set of acts for punishment. The Government contends that only the latter determination implicates Apprendi\xe2\x80\x99s concerns. But this argument rests on the rejected assumption that, in determining the maximum punishment for an offense, there is a constitutionally significant difference between a fact that is an \xe2\x80\x9celement\xe2\x80\x9d of the offense and one that is a \xe2\x80\x9csentencing factor.\xe2\x80\x9d Further, the facts the District Court found in imposing a fine on Southern Union are not fairly characterized as merely quantifying the harm the company caused.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government also argues that applying Apprendi to criminal fines will prevent States and the Federal Government from enact- ing statutes that calibrate the amount of a fine to a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s culp-ability. But legislatures are free to enact such statutes, so long as the statutes are administered in conformance with the \n \nSixth Amendment.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, the Government contends that requiring juries to determine facts related to fines will cause confusion, prejudice defendants, or be impractical. These policy arguments rehearse those made by the dissents in our prior Apprendi cases. They must be rejected because the rule the Government espouses is unconstitutional. In addition, because Apprendi is now more than a decade old, the reliance interests underlying the Government\xe2\x80\x99s arguments are by this point attenuated. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x88\x9216.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t630 F.\xc2\xa03d 17, reversed and remanded.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Sotomayor delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe \n \nSixth Amendment reserves to juries the determination of any fact, other than the fact of a prior conviction, that increases a criminal defendant\xe2\x80\x99s maximum potential sentence. Apprendi v. New Jersey, \n\n530 U.\xc2\xa0S. 466 (2000)\n; Blakely v. Washington, \n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 296 (2004)\n. We have applied this principle in numerous cases where the sentence was imprisonment or death. The question here is whether the same rule applies to sentences of criminal fines. We hold that it does.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioner Southern Union Company is a natural gas distributor. Its subsidiary stored liquid mercury, a hazardous substance, at a facility in Pawtucket, Rhode Is-land. In September 2004, youths from a nearby apartment complex broke into the facility, played with the mer-cury, and spread it around the facility and complex. The complex\xe2\x80\x99s residents were temporarily displaced during the cleanup and most underwent testing for mercury poisoning.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 2007, a grand jury indicted Southern Union on multiple counts of violating federal environmental statutes. As relevant here, the first count alleged that the company knowingly stored liquid mercury without a permit at the Pawtucket facility \xe2\x80\x9c[f]rom on or about September 19, 2002 until on or about October 19, 2004,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 104, in viola- tion of the Resource Conservation and Recovery Act of 1976 (RCRA). See \n\n90Stat.\n2812, as amended, \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa76928(d)(2)(A). A jury convicted Southern Union on this count following a trial in the District Court for the District of Rhode Island. The verdict form stated that Southern Union was guilty of unlawfully storing liquid mercury \xe2\x80\x9con or about September 19, 2002 to October 19, 2004.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 140.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tViolations of the RCRA are punishable by, inter alia, \xe2\x80\x9ca fine of not more than $50,000 for each day of violation.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa76928(d). At sentencing, the probation office set a maximum fine of $38.1 million, on the basis that Southern Union violated the RCRA for each of the 762 days from September 19, 2002, through October 19, 2004. Southern Union objected that this calculation violated Apprendi because the jury was not asked to determine the precise duration of the violation. The company noted that the ver-dict form listed only the violation\xe2\x80\x99s approximate start date (i.e., \xe2\x80\x9con or about\xe2\x80\x9d), and argued that the court\xe2\x80\x99s instructions permitted conviction if the jury found even a 1-day violation. Therefore, Southern Union maintained, the only violation the jury necessarily found was for one day, and imposing any fine greater than the single-day penalty of $50,000 would require factfinding by the court, in contravention of Apprendi.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government acknowledged the jury was not asked to specify the duration of the violation, but argued that Apprendi does not apply to criminal fines. The District Court disagreed and held that Apprendi applies. But the court concluded from the \xe2\x80\x9ccontent and context of the verdict all together\xe2\x80\x9d that the jury found a 762-day violation. App. to Pet. for Cert. 46a. The court therefore set a maximum potential fine of $38.1 million, from which it imposed a fine of $6 million and a \xe2\x80\x9ccommunity service obligatio[n]\xe2\x80\x9d of $12 million. App. 154.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOn appeal, the United States Court of Appeals for the First Circuit rejected the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that the jury necessarily found a violation of 762 days. 630 F.\xc2\xa03d 17, 36 (2010). But the Court of Appeals affirmed the sentence because it also held, again in contrast to the District Court, that Apprendi does not apply to criminal fines. 630 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 33\xe2\x80\x9336. Other Circuits have reached the opposite conclusion. See United States v. Pfaff, 619 F.\xc2\xa03d 172 (CA2 2010) (per\xc2\xa0curiam); United States v. LaGrou Distribution Sys., Inc., 466 F.\xc2\xa03d 585 (CA7 2006). We granted certiorari to resolve the conflict, 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2011), and now reverse.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis case requires us to consider the scope of the \n \nSixth Amendment right of jury trial, as construed in Apprendi. Under Apprendi, \xe2\x80\x9c[o]ther than the fact of a prior conviction, any fact that increases the penalty for a crime beyond the prescribed statutory maximum must be submitted to a jury, and proved beyond a reasonable doubt.\xe2\x80\x9d 530 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 490. The \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98statutory maximum\xe2\x80\x99 for Apprendi purposes is the maximum sentence a judge may impose solely on the basis of the facts reflected in the jury verdict or admitted by the defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d Blakely, 542 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 303 (emphasis deleted). Thus, while judges may exercise discretion in sentencing, they may not \xe2\x80\x9cinflic[t] punishment that the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict alone does not allow.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 304.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tApprendi\xe2\x80\x99s rule is \xe2\x80\x9crooted in longstanding common-law practice.\xe2\x80\x9d Cunningham v. California, \n\n549 U.\xc2\xa0S. 270, \n281 (2007)\n. It preserves the \xe2\x80\x9chistoric jury function\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9cdetermining whether the prosecution has proved each element of an offense beyond a reasonable doubt.\xe2\x80\x9d Oregon v. Ice, \n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 160, \n163 (2009)\n. We have repeatedly affirmed this rule by applying it to a variety of sentencing schemes that allowed judges to find facts that increased a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s maximum authorized sentence. See Cunningham, 549 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 274\xe2\x80\x93275 (elevated \xe2\x80\x9cupper term\xe2\x80\x9d of impris-onment); United States v. Booker, \n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 220\n\xe2\x80\x93 227, 233\xe2\x80\x93234 (2005) (increased imprisonment range for defendant under then-mandatory Federal Sentencing Guidelines); Blakely, 542 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 299\xe2\x80\x93300 (increased im-prisonment above statutorily prescribed \xe2\x80\x9cstandard range\xe2\x80\x9d); Ring v. Arizona, \n\n536 U.\xc2\xa0S. 584\n\xe2\x80\x93589 (2002) (death penalty authorized upon finding existence of aggravating factors); Apprendi, 530 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 468\xe2\x80\x93469 (extended term of imprisonment based on violation of a \xe2\x80\x9chate crime\xe2\x80\x9d statute).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhile the punishments at stake in those cases were imprisonment or a death sentence, we see no principled basis under Apprendi for treating criminal fines differ-ently. Apprendi\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccore concern\xe2\x80\x9d is to reserve to the jury \xe2\x80\x9cthe determination of facts that warrant punishment for a specific statutory offense.\xe2\x80\x9d Ice, 555 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 170. That concern applies whether the sentence is a criminal fine or imprisonment or death. Criminal fines, like these other forms of punishment, are penalties inflicted by the sovereign for the commission of offenses. Fines were by far the most common form of noncapital punishment in colonial America.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t They are frequently imposed today, especially upon organizational defendants who cannot be imprisoned.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t And the amount of a fine, like the maximum term of imprisonment or eligibility for the death penalty, is of-ten calculated by reference to particular facts. Sometimes, as here, the fact is the duration of a statutory violation;\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t under other statutes it is the amount of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s gain or the victim\xe2\x80\x99s loss, or some other factor.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t In all such cases, requiring juries to find beyond a reasonable doubt facts that determine the fine\xe2\x80\x99s maximum amount is necessary to implement Apprendi\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9canimating principle\xe2\x80\x9d: the \xe2\x80\x9cpreservation of the jury\xe2\x80\x99s historic role as a bulwark between the State and the accused at the trial for an alleged offense.\xe2\x80\x9d Ice, 555 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 168. In stating Apprendi\xe2\x80\x99s rule, we have never distinguished one form of punishment from another. Instead, our decisions broadly prohibit judicial factfinding that increases maximum criminal \xe2\x80\x9csentence[s],\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cpenalties,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cpunishment[s]\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94terms that each undeniably embrace fines. E.g., Blakely, 542 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 304; Apprendi, 530 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 490; Ring, 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 589.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government objects, however, that fines are less onerous than incarceration and the death sentence. The Government notes that Apprendi itself referred to the physical deprivation of liberty that imprisonment occasions, see 530 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 484, and that we have placed more weight on imprisonment than on fines when construing the scope of the \n \nSixth Amendment rights to counsel and jury trial. See Blanton v. North Las Vegas, \n\n489 U.\xc2\xa0S. 538\n\xe2\x80\x93543 (1989) (jury trial); Scott v. Illinois, \n\n440 U.\xc2\xa0S. 367\n\xe2\x80\x93374 (1979) (counsel). Therefore, the Government concludes, fines categorically \xe2\x80\x9cdo not implicate\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9cprimary concerns motivating Apprendi.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 23\xe2\x80\x9325.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis argument fails because its conclusion does not fol-low from its premise. Where a fine is so insubstantial that the underlying offense is considered \xe2\x80\x9cpetty,\xe2\x80\x9d the \n \nSixth Amendment right of jury trial is not triggered, and no Apprendi issue arises. See, e.g., Muniz v. Hoffman, \n\n422 U.\xc2\xa0S. 454, \n477 (1975)\n ($10,000 fine imposed on labor union does not entitle union to jury trial); see also Blanton, 489 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 541 (no jury trial right for \xe2\x80\x9cpetty\xe2\x80\x9d offenses, as measured by the \xe2\x80\x9cseverity of the maximum authorized penalty\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). The same, of course, is true of offenses punishable by relatively brief terms of imprisonment\xe2\x80\x94these, too, do not entitle a defendant to a jury trial. See id., at 543 (establishing a rebuttable presumption that offenses punishable by six months\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment or less are petty); Duncan v. Louisiana, \n\n391 U.\xc2\xa0S. 145\n\xe2\x80\x93162 (1968).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut not all fines are insubstantial, and not all offenses punishable by fines are petty. See, e.g., Mine Workers v. Bagwell, \n\n512 U.\xc2\xa0S. 821\n, n.\xc2\xa05 (1994) (criminal contempt fine of $52 million imposed on union \xe2\x80\x9cunquestionably is a serious contempt sanction\xe2\x80\x9d that triggers right of jury trial). The federal twice-the-gain-or-loss statute, in particular, see \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73571(d), has been used to obtain substantial judgments against organizational defendants. See, e.g., Amended Judgment in United States v. LG Display Co., Ltd., No. 08\xe2\x80\x93CR\xe2\x80\x93803\xe2\x80\x93SI (ND Cal.), pp.\xc2\xa01\xe2\x80\x932 ($400 million fine for conviction of single count of violating Sherman Antitrust Act); Judgment in United States v. Siemens Aktiengesellschaft, No. 08\xe2\x80\x93CR\xe2\x80\x93367\xe2\x80\x93RJL (D DC), pp.\xc2\xa01\xe2\x80\x932, 5 ($448.5 million fine for two violations of Foreign Corrupt Practices Act); United States Sentencing Commission, 2010 Annual Report, ch. 5, p. 38 (noting fine of $1.195 billion imposed on pharmaceutical corporation for violations of food and drug laws). And, where the defendant is an individual, a large fine may \xe2\x80\x9cengender \xe2\x80\x98a significant infringement of personal freedom.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Blanton, 489 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 542 (quoting Frank v. United States, \n\n395 U.\xc2\xa0S. 147, \n151 (1969)\n); see also \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73572(a)(2) (requiring court to consider \xe2\x80\x9cthe burden that the fine will impose upon the defendant\xe2\x80\x9d in determining whether to impose a fine and in what amount).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government thus asks the wrong question by comparing the severity of criminal fines to that of other punishments. So far as Apprendi is concerned, the relevant question is the significance of the fine from the perspective of the \n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s jury trial guarantee. Where a fine is substantial enough to trigger that right, Apprendi applies in full. As we said in Cunningham, \xe2\x80\x9cAsking whether a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s basic jury-trial right is preserved, though some facts essential to punishment are reserved for determination by the judge, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is the very inquiry Apprendi\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x98bright-line rule\xe2\x80\x99 was designed to exclude.\xe2\x80\x9d 549 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 291.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis case is exemplary. The RCRA subjects Southern Union to a maximum fine of $50,000 for each day of violation. \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa76928(d). The Government does not deny that, in light of the seriousness of that punishment, the company was properly accorded a jury trial. And the Government now concedes the District Court made factual findings that increased both the \xe2\x80\x9cpotential and actual\xe2\x80\x9d fine the court imposed. Brief for United States 28. This is exactly what Apprendi guards against: judicial factfinding that enlarges the maximum punishment a defendant faces beyond what the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict or the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s admissions allow.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn concluding that the rule of Apprendi does not apply to criminal fines, the Court of Appeals relied on our decision in Ice. Ice addressed the question whether, when a defendant is convicted of multiple offenses, Apprendi forbids judges to determine facts that authorize the imposition of consecutive sentences. 555 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 164. In holding that Apprendi does not, Ice emphasized that juries historically played no role in deciding whether sentences should run consecutively or concurrently. See 555 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 168\xe2\x80\x93169. The Court of Appeals reasoned that juries were similarly uninvolved in setting criminal fines. 630 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 35.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court of Appeals was correct to examine the histor-ical record, because \xe2\x80\x9cthe scope of the constitutional jury right must be informed by the historical role of the jury at common law.\xe2\x80\x9d Ice, 555 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 170. See also, e.g., Blakely, 542 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 301\xe2\x80\x93302; Apprendi, 530 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 477\xe2\x80\x93484. But in our view, the record supports applying Apprendi to criminal fines. To be sure, judges in the col-onies and during the founding era \xe2\x80\x9cpossessed a great deal of discretion\xe2\x80\x9d in determining whether to impose a fine and in what amount. Lillquist 640\xe2\x80\x93641; see also Preyer 350. Often, a fine\xe2\x80\x99s range \xe2\x80\x9cwas apparently without limit except insofar as it was within the expectation on the part of the court that it would be paid.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. For some other offenses, the maximum fine was capped by statute. See, e.g., id., at 333 (robbery, larceny, burglary, and other offenses punishable in Massachusetts Bay Colony \xe2\x80\x9cby fines of up to \xc2\xa35\xe2\x80\x9d); Act of Feb. 28, 1803, ch. 9, \xc2\xa77, \n\n2Stat.\n205 (any consul who gives a false certificate shall \xe2\x80\x9cforfeit and pay a fine not exceeding ten thousand dollars, at the discretion of the court\xe2\x80\x9d); K. Stith & J. Cabranes, Fear of Judging: Sentencing Guidelines in the Federal Courts 9 (1998) (describing federal practice).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe exercise of such sentencing discretion is fully consistent with Apprendi, which permits courts to impose \xe2\x80\x9cjudgment within the range prescribed by statute.\xe2\x80\x9d 530 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 481 (emphasis in original). Nor, a fortiori, could there be an Apprendi violation where no maximum is prescribed. Indeed, in surveying the historical record that formed the basis of our holding in Apprendi, we specifi-cally considered the English practice with respect to fines, which, as was true of many colonial offenses, made sentencing largely \xe2\x80\x9cdependent upon judicial discretion.\xe2\x80\x9d See id., at 480, n.\xc2\xa07; see also Jones v. United States, \n\n526 U.\xc2\xa0S. 227\n\xe2\x80\x93245 (1999); 4 W. Blackstone, Commentaries on the Laws of England 372\xe2\x80\x93373 (1769) (hereinafter Blackstone). And even then, as the dissent acknowledges, post, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312 (opinion of Breyer, J.), there is authority suggesting that English juries were required to find facts that determined the authorized pecuniary punishment. See 1 T. Starkie, A Treatise on Criminal Pleading 187\xe2\x80\x93188 (1814) (In cases \xe2\x80\x9cwhere the offence, or its defined measure of punishment, depends upon\xe2\x80\x9d property\xe2\x80\x99s specific value, the value \xe2\x80\x9cmust be proved precisely as it is laid [in the indictment], and any variance will be fatal\xe2\x80\x9d); see also id., at 188 (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]n the case of usury, where the judgment depends upon the quantum taken, the usurious contract must be averred according to the fact; and a variance from it, in evidence, would be fatal, because the penalty is apportioned to the value\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis in original)); 2 W. Hawkins, A Treatise of the Pleas of the Crown, ch. 25, \xc2\xa775, pp. 234\xe2\x80\x93235 (3d ed. 1739) (doubting whether \xe2\x80\x9cit be need-ful to set forth the Value of the Goods in an Indictment of Trespass for any other Purpose than to aggravate the Fine\xe2\x80\x9d). \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn any event, the salient question here is what role the jury played in prosecutions for offenses that did peg the amount of a fine to the determination of specified facts\xe2\x80\x94often, the value of damaged or stolen property. See Apprendi, 530 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 502, n.\xc2\xa02 (Thomas, J., concurring). Our review of state and federal decisions discloses that the predominant practice was for such facts to be alleged in the indictment and proved to the jury. See, e.g., Commonwealth v. Smith, 1 Mass. 245, 247 (1804) (declining to award judgment of treble damages for all stolen items in larceny prosecution when indictment alleged value of only some of the items); Clark v. People, 2 Ill. 117, 120\xe2\x80\x93121 (1833) (arson indictment must allege value of destroyed building because statute imposed \xe2\x80\x9ca fine equal in value to the property burned\xe2\x80\x9d); State v. Garner, 8 Port. 447, 448 (Ala. 1839) (same in malicious mischief prosecution where punishment was fine \xe2\x80\x9cnot exceeding four fold the value of the property injured or destroyed\xe2\x80\x9d); Ritchey v. State, 7 Blackf. 168, 169 (Ind. 1844) (same in arson prosecution because, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n addition to imprisonment in the penitentiary, the guilty person is liable to a fine not exceeding double the value of the property destroyed\xe2\x80\x9d); Hope v. Commonwealth, 50 Mass. 134, 137 (1845) (the \xe2\x80\x9cvalue of the property alleged to be stolen must be set forth in the indictment\xe2\x80\x9d in part because \xe2\x80\x9c[o]ur statutes .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. prescribe the punishment for larceny, with reference to the value of the property stolen\xe2\x80\x9d); State v. Goodrich, 46 N.\xc2\xa0H. 186, 188 (1865) (\xe2\x80\x9cIt may also be suggested, that, in the case of simple larceny, the respondent may be sentenced to pay the owner of the goods stolen, treble the value thereof, which is an additional reason for requiring the [value of the stolen items] to be stated [in the indictment]\xe2\x80\x9d); United States v. Woodruff, 68 F. 536, 538 (Kan. 1895) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he defendant is entitled to his constitutional right of trial by jury\xe2\x80\x9d to ascertain \xe2\x80\x9cthe exact sum for which a fine may be imposed\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe rule that juries must determine facts that set a fine\xe2\x80\x99s maximum amount is an application of the \xe2\x80\x9ctwo longstanding tenets of common-law criminal jurisprudence\xe2\x80\x9d on which Apprendi is based: First, \xe2\x80\x9cthe \xe2\x80\x98truth of every accusation\xe2\x80\x99 against a defendant \xe2\x80\x98should afterwards be confirmed by the unanimous suffrage of twelve of his equals and neighbours.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Blakely, 542 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 301 (quoting 4 Blackstone 343). And second, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98an accusation which lacks any particular fact which the law makes essential to the punishment is .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. no accusation within the requirements of the common law, and it is no accusation in reason.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 542 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 301\xe2\x80\x93302 (quoting 1 J. Bishop, Criminal Procedure \xc2\xa787, p. 55 (2d ed. 1872)). Indeed, Bishop\xe2\x80\x99s leading treatise on criminal procedure specifically identified cases involving fines as evidence of the proposition that \xe2\x80\x9cthe indictment must, in order to inform the court what punishment to inflict, contain an averment of every particular thing which enters into the punishment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., \xc2\xa7540, at 330 (discussing Clark and Garner). This principle, Bishop explained, \xe2\x80\x9cpervades the entire system of the adjudged law of criminal procedure. It is not made apparent to our understandings by a single case only, but by all the cases.\xe2\x80\x9d Criminal Procedure \xc2\xa781, at 51. See also Ap-prendi, 530 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 510\xe2\x80\x93511 (Thomas, J., concurring) (explaining that Bishop grounded this principle in \xe2\x80\x9cwell-established common-law practice .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and in the provisions of Federal and State Constitutions guaranteeing notice of an accusation in all criminal cases, indictment by a grand jury for serious crimes, and trial by jury\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs counterevidence that juries historically did not determine facts relevant to criminal fines, the Government points to two decisions from this Court. One is United States v. Murphy, 16 Pet. 203 (1842), which considered whether an interested witness was competent to testify in a larceny prosecution brought under a provision of the Crimes Act of 1790. Murphy\xe2\x80\x99s only relevance to this case is that the Crimes Act authorized a fine of up to four times the value of the stolen property, and the Court remarked that \xe2\x80\x9cthe fine is, as to its amount, purely in the discretion of the Court.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 209. But this statement is best read as permitting the court to select a fine from within the maximum authorized by jury-found facts\xe2\x80\x94a practice, as noted, that accords with Apprendi. Such a reading is consistent with the fact that the indictment in Murphy alleged the value of the stolen items, see 16 Pet., at 207\xe2\x80\x93208, and with the practice of contemporary courts addressing the same statute, see United States v. Holland, 26 F.\xc2\xa0Cas. 343, 345 (No. 15,378) (CC SDNY 1843) (trial court instructs jury \xe2\x80\x9cto assess the value of the property taken\xe2\x80\x9d in order to determine maximum fine); Pye v. United States, 20 F.\xc2\xa0Cas. 99 (No. 11,488) (CC DC 1842) (value of stolen items alleged in indictment).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government and dissent place greater reliance on United States v. Tyler, 7 Cranch 285 (1812). But like Murphy, this decision involved no constitutional question. Rather, it construed a federal embargo statute that imposed a fine of four times the value of the property intended to be exported. The indictment identified the property at issue as \xe2\x80\x9cpearl-ashes,\xe2\x80\x9d but the jury\xe2\x80\x99s guilty verdict re-ferred instead to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98pot-ashes [that] were worth two hundred and eighty dollars.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Tyler, 7 Cranch, at 285.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t7\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t The question was whether the discrepancy rendered the verdict \xe2\x80\x9cnot sufficiently certain as to the value of the property charged in the indictment,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., pearl-ashes. Ibid. The Court held that the discrepancy was immaterial, on the ground that \xe2\x80\x9cunder this law, no valuation by the jury was necessary to enable the Circuit Court to impose the proper fine.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning is somewhat opaque, but appears to rest on the text of the embargo statute, which directed that the defendant \xe2\x80\x9cshall, upon conviction, be .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. fined a sum by the Court.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. In any event, nothing in the decision purports to construe the \n \nSixth Amendment. And, insofar as Tyler reflects prevailing practice, it bears noting that both the indictment and ver-dict identified the value of the property at issue. See Tr. 2 in Tyler, 7 Cranch 285, reprinted in Appellate Case Files of the Supreme Court of the United States, 1792\xe2\x80\x931831, National Archives Microfilm Publications No.\xc2\xa0214 (1962), roll 18 (indictment: \xe2\x80\x9cnineteen barrels of pearlashes, which were then and there of the value of six hundred dollars\xe2\x80\x9d). Whatever the precise meaning of this decision, it does not outweigh the ample historical evidence showing that juries routinely found facts that set the maximum amounts of fines.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government\xe2\x80\x99s remaining arguments, echoed by the dissent (see post, at 23\xe2\x80\x9328), are unpersuasive. The Government first submits that, when it comes to fines, \xe2\x80\x9cthe judicially found facts typically involve only quantifying the harm caused by the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s offense\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94for example, how long did the violation last, or how much money did the defendant gain (or the victim lose)?\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cas opposed to de-fining a separate set of acts for punishment.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 25. Only the latter determination, the Government contends, implicates Apprendi\xe2\x80\x99s concerns.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis argument has two defects. First, it rests on an assumption that Apprendi and its progeny have uniformly rejected: that in determining the maximum punishment for an offense, there is a constitutionally significant difference between a fact that is an \xe2\x80\x9celement\xe2\x80\x9d of the offense and one that is a \xe2\x80\x9csentencing factor.\xe2\x80\x9d See, e.g., 530 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 478; Ring, 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 605. Second, we doubt the coherence of this distinction. This case proves the point. Under \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa76928(d), the fact that will ultimately determine the maximum fine Southern Union faces is the number of days the company violated the statute. Such a finding is not fairly characterized as merely \xe2\x80\x9cquantifying the harm\xe2\x80\x9d Southern Union caused. Rather, it is a determination that for each given day, the Government has proved that Southern Union committed all of the acts constituting the offense. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government next contends that applying Apprendi to fines will prevent States and the Federal Government from enacting statutes that, like \xc2\xa76928(d), calibrate fines to a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s culpability, thus providing just punishment and reducing unwarranted sentencing disparity. But the Government presents a false choice. As was true in our prior Apprendi cases, and remains so here, legislatures are free to enact statutes that constrain judges\xe2\x80\x99 discretion in sentencing\xe2\x80\x94Apprendi requires only that such provisions be administered in conformance with the \n \nSixth Amendment.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tLast, the Government argues that requiring juries to determine facts related to fines will cause confusion (because expert testimony might be needed to guide the inquiry); or prejudice the defendant (who might have to deny violating a statute while simultaneously arguing that any violation was minimal); or be impractical (at least when the relevant facts are unknown or unknowable until the trial is completed).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t8\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t These arguments rehearse those made by the dissents in our prior Apprendi cases. See Booker, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 329 (Breyer, J., dissenting in part); Blakely, 542 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 318\xe2\x80\x93320 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., dissenting); id., at 330\xe2\x80\x93340 (Breyer, J., dissenting); Apprendi, 530 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 555\xe2\x80\x93559 (same). Here, as there, they must be rejected. For even if these predictions are ac-curate, the rule the Government espouses is unconstitutional. That \xe2\x80\x9cshould be the end of the matter.\xe2\x80\x9d Blakely, 542 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 313.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut here there is particular reason to doubt the strength of these policy concerns. Apprendi is now more than a decade old. The reliance interests that underlie many of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s arguments are by this point attenuated. Nor, in our view, does applying Apprendi\xe2\x80\x99s rule to criminal fines mark an unexpected extension of the doctrine. Most Circuits to have addressed the issue already embrace this position, see Pfaff, 619 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 175\xe2\x80\x93176; LaGrou Distribution Sys., 466 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 594; United States v. Yang, 144 Fed. Appx. 521, 524 (CA6 2005), as did the Government prior to Ice, see Brief in Opposition 11, n.\xc2\xa02. In light of the reasons given in this opinion, the dramatic departure from precedent would be to hold criminal fines exempt from Apprendi.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe hold that the rule of Apprendi applies to the imposition of criminal fines. The judgment of the Court of Appeals is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.\n\t\t\t

And as Bishop explained, this requirement of proof originated not from a unique fea-ture of jury sentencing, but from longstanding common-law princi-ples\xe2\x80\x94a point to which the dissent notably does not respond. 1 J. Bishop, Criminal Procedure \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa781, 540 (2d ed. 1872). See infra, at 12.\nNor, for that matter, do larceny cases \xe2\x80\x9cpresen[t] a special circumstance.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 20. Such decisions invoked the same reasoning as the other cases just mentioned. See, e.g., Hope, 50 Mass., at 137 (value must be proved because, among other things, \xe2\x80\x9c[o]ur statutes .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. prescribe the punishment for larceny .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. with reference to the value of the property stolen\xe2\x80\x9d); Goodrich, 46 N.\xc2\xa0H., at 188 (same). Bishop made this point explicit: \xe2\x80\x9c[Value] must be alleged wherever it is an element tobe considered by the court in determining the punishment, and it is immaterial whether the particular crime is larceny or any other crime.\xe2\x80\x9d Criminal Procedure \xc2\xa7541, at 331 (footnote omitted and emphasis added). At the end of the day, the only evidence the dissent musters that judges found fine-enhancing facts are United States v. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Breyer, with whom Justice Kennedy and Justice Alito join, dissenting.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhere a criminal fine is at issue, I believe the \n \nSixth Amendment permits a sentencing judge to determine sen- tencing facts\xe2\x80\x94facts that are not elements of the crime but are relevant only to the amount of the fine the judge will impose. Those who framed the Bill of Rights understood that \xe2\x80\x9cthe finding of a particular fact\xe2\x80\x9d of this kind was ordinarily a matter for a judge and not necessarily \xe2\x80\x9cwithin \xe2\x80\x98the domain of the jury.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Oregon v. Ice, \n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 160, \n168 (2009)\n (quoting Harris v. United States, \n\n536 U.\xc2\xa0S. 545, \n557 (2002)\n (plurality opinion)). The Court\xe2\x80\x99s contrary conclusion, I believe, is ahistorical and will lead to increased problems of unfairness in the administration of our criminal justice system.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAlthough this dissent does not depend upon the dissents in Apprendi v. New Jersey, \n\n530 U.\xc2\xa0S. 466 (2000)\n, and its progeny, summarizing those earlier dissents will help the reader understand this one. See id., at 523\xe2\x80\x93554 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., dissenting); id., at 555\xe2\x80\x93556 (Breyer, J., dissenting); see also United States v. Booker, \n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 220, \n327 (2005)\n (Breyer, J., dissenting in part) (citing cases); Blakely v. Washington, \n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 296, \n329 (2004)\n (Breyer, J., dissenting) (same). The Apprendi dissenters argued that the law had long distinguished between (1) facts that constitute elements of the offense and (2) facts relevant only to sentencing. The term \xe2\x80\x9celements of the offense\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9cconstituent parts of a crime .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that the prosecution must prove to sustain a conviction.\xe2\x80\x9d Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 597 (9th ed. 2009). The statute that creates the crime in question typically sets forth those constituent parts. And a jury must find the existence of each such element \xe2\x80\x9cbeyond a reasonable doubt.\xe2\x80\x9d See, e.g., United States v. Gaudin, \n\n515 U.\xc2\xa0S. 506, \n510 (1995)\n; In re Winship, \n\n397 U.\xc2\xa0S. 358, \n364 (1970)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThus, a bank robbery statute might prohibit an offender from (1) taking by force or by intimidation (2) in the presence of another person (3) a thing of value (4) belonging to, or in custody of, a bank. In that case, the jury can convict only if it finds the existence of each of these four factual \xe2\x80\x9celements\xe2\x80\x9d beyond a reasonable doubt. But it need not find other facts beyond a reasonable doubt, for these four factual elements alone constitute the crime.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOther facts may be relevant to the length or kind of sentence the court will impose upon a convicted offender. These sentencing facts typically characterize the manner in which the offender carried out the crime or set forth relevant features characterizing the offender. For example, in respect to manner, an offender might have carried out a particular bank robbery\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cwith (or without) a gun, which the robber kept hidden (or brandished), might have frightened (or merely warned), injured seriously (or less seriously), tied up (or simply pushed) a guard, teller, or customer, at night (or at noon), in an effort to obtain money for other crimes (or for other purposes), in the company of a few (or many) other robbers .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d United States Sentencing Commission, Guidelines Manual \xc2\xa71A1.3, p.\xc2\xa03 (Nov. 2011) (USSG).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIn respect to characteristics of the offender, a current bank robbery conviction might be that offender\xe2\x80\x99s first (or his fourth) criminal conviction.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTraditionally, sentencing facts help the sentencing judge determine where, within say a broad statutory range of, say, up to 20 years of imprisonment, the particular bank robber\xe2\x80\x99s punishment should lie. The Apprendi dissenters concluded that the Constitution did not require the jury to find the existence of those facts beyond a reasonable doubt. Rather the law, through its rules, statutes, and the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s Due Process Clause would typically offer the defendant factfinding protection. See, e.g., Fed. Rule Crim. Proc. 32 (federal presentence report prepared by probation office sets forth facts, which defendant may contest at sentencing proceeding); Almendarez-Torres v. United States, \n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 224\n\xe2\x80\x93247 (1998) (constitutional inquiry).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe dispute in Apprendi and its line of cases arose after Congress and many States codified these sentencing facts during the sentencing reform movement of the 1970\xe2\x80\x99s and 1980\xe2\x80\x99s. Congress, for example, concluded that too many different judges were imposing too many different sentences upon too many similar offenders who had com- mitted similar crimes in similar ways. It subsequently enacted the Sentencing Reform Act of 1984, creating a federal Sentencing Commission which would produce greater uniformity in sentencing through the promulgation of mandatory uniform Guidelines structuring how judges, in ordinary cases, should typically use sentencing facts to determine sentences. 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7991, 994 (2006 ed. and Supp. IV); see also 18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73553(b)(1), 3742(e). The Apprendi-line majority agreed that, where a statute set a higher maximum sentence, a Commission might structure how a judge found sentencing facts relevant to the sentence imposed below that otherwise applicable maximum, at least if the resulting guidelines were not mandatory. See Booker, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 245. But the majority held that where a sentencing fact increased the otherwise applicable maximum penalty, that fact had to be found by a jury. Apprendi, 530 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 490.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs I have said, the dissenters thought that the \n \nSixth Amendment did not require a jury to find any of these sentencing facts. Why, asked the dissenters, should Congress\xe2\x80\x99 or a State\xe2\x80\x99s desire for greater sentencing uniformity achieved through statutes seeking more uniform treatment (of similar offenders committing similar offenses in similar ways) suddenly produce new \n \nSixth Amendment jury trial requirements?\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThose requirements would work against greater sentencing fairness. To treat all sentencing facts (where so specified in a statute or rule) as if they were elements of the offense could lead Congress simply to set high maximum ranges for each crime, thereby avoiding Appren- di\xe2\x80\x99s jury trial requirement. Alternatively, Congress might enact statutes that more specifically tied particular punishments to each crime (limiting or removing judicial discretion), for example, mandatory minimum statutes. But this system would threaten disproportionality by in- sisting that similar punishments be applied to very dif- ferent kinds of offense behavior or offenders. Apprendi\xe2\x80\x99s jury trial requirements might also prove unworkable. Con- sider the difficulty of juries\xe2\x80\x99 having to find the differ- ent facts in the bank robbery example I have set forth above. Moreover, how is a defendant, arguing that he did not have a gun, alternatively to argue that, in any event, he did not fire the gun?\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, the dissenters took a different view of \n \nSixth Amendment history. They believed that under the common law and at the time the Constitution was ratified, judges, not juries, often found sentencing facts, i.e., facts relevant only to the determination of the offender\xe2\x80\x99s punishment. See, e.g., Booker, supra, at 329 (Breyer, J., dissenting in part); Apprendi, 530 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 527\xe2\x80\x93529 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., dissenting).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe dissenters lost the argument. The Court in Apprendi held that (other than the fact of a prior conviction) \xe2\x80\x9cany fact that increases the penalty for a crime beyond the prescribed statutory maximum must be submitted to a jury, and proved beyond a reasonable doubt.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 490. But the dissenters\xe2\x80\x99 views help to explain why I continue to believe this Court should not extend Apprendi\xe2\x80\x99s rule beyond \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the central sphere of [its] concern.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ice, 555 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 172 (quoting Cunningham v. California, \n\n549 U.\xc2\xa0S. 270, \n295 (2007)\n (Kennedy, J., dissenting)). That is the Court\xe2\x80\x99s view, too, as set forth in Ice. And I base my dissent here primarily upon Ice.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis case involves sentencing facts, not elements of a crime. The criminal statute at issue constitutes one part of the Resource Conservation and Recovery Act of 1976 (RCRA), which, among others things, authorizes the Environmental Protection Agency to create a list of hazardous wastes. \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa76921. The criminal statute says:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cAny person who .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. knowingly treats, stores, or disposes of any hazardous waste identified or listed under [RCRA] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. without [an RCRA-authorized] permit .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall, upon conviction, be subject to a fine of not more than $50,000 for each day of violation, or imprisonment not to exceed .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. five years .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0, or both.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa76928(d)(2)(A).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo one here denies that this statute creates a crime with four elements: (1) knowing treatment, storage, or disposal of a waste (2) that is hazardous, (3) without a permit, and (4) knowing that the waste has a substantial potential of causing harm to others or to the environment. App. 129\xe2\x80\x93130; see Brief for Petitioner 30.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe number of \xe2\x80\x9cday[s] of each violation,\xe2\x80\x9d however, is not an additional element of the crime. The statute says that the number of days becomes relevant only \xe2\x80\x9cupon conviction\xe2\x80\x9d of the crime as previously defined. Moreover, the number of days is relevant to application of only one of two kinds of punishment that the statute mentions (fine and imprisonment); one cannot easily read this statute as creating two separate crimes identical but for the punishment. Finally, Congress did not include here, as it sometimes has done, statutory words such as \xe2\x80\x9ceach day of violation shall constitute a separate offense.\xe2\x80\x9d E.g., \n\n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7223(b); see also \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa74910(b). Rather, as in many other similar statutes, the statute here sets forth the crime and kinds of punishments (fine and imprisonment), while separately specifying facts that determine the maximum punishment of one kind (fines).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn this particular case, the indictment set forth a violation period of 762 days (from \xe2\x80\x9con or about September 19, 2002 until on or about October 19, 2004\xe2\x80\x9d). App. 104. The jury\xe2\x80\x99s guilty verdict did not specify the number of days on which the defendant committed the offense. Id., at 141. But after the conviction and sentencing hearing, the judge found that, among other things, the \xe2\x80\x9cclear and essentially irrefutable evidence\xe2\x80\x9d at trial supported the conclusion set forth in the presentence report, namely, that the maximum fine available amounted to $50,000 per day for 762 days\xe2\x80\x94or $38.1 million. App. to Pet. for Cert. 47a\xe2\x80\x9348a. The judge imposed a fine of $6 million along with a $12 million community service obligation. App. 162\xe2\x80\x93163.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tApprendi says that \xe2\x80\x9cany fact that increases the penalty for a crime beyond the prescribed statutory maximum must be submitted to a jury, and proved beyond a reasonable doubt.\xe2\x80\x9d 530 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 490. The number of days (beyond one) on which the defendant violated this criminal statute is such a fact. Nonetheless, like the majority, I believe that Apprendi\xe2\x80\x99s rule does not automatically control the outcome in this case.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThat is because this case concerns a fine, not, as in Apprendi, a term of imprisonment. And we made clear in Oregon v. Ice, \n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 160\n, that Apprendi does not encompass every kind of fact-related sentencing decision that increases the statutory maximum. In Ice, we considered Apprendi\xe2\x80\x99s application to a sentencing decision about whether two prison sentences for conviction of two separate crimes (e.g., illegal drug possession and illegal gun possession) would run concurrently or consecutively. 555 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 163. An Oregon statute required a concurrent sentence unless the sentencing judge found certain facts. Id., at 165. Those facts could make a large difference in a term of imprisonment. Their presence could mean that a 5-year sentence for illegal drug possession and a 5-year sentence for illegal gun possession would amount to 10 years of imprisonment rather than 5 (indeed, in Ice itself, the judge\xe2\x80\x99s factfinding increased the sentence by 20 years, see id., at 166, and n.\xc2\xa05). Thus, the presence of those \xe2\x80\x9cfact[s]\xe2\x80\x9d could \xe2\x80\x9cincreas[e] the penalty\xe2\x80\x9d beyond what would otherwise be \xe2\x80\x9cthe prescribed statutory maximum.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 167 (internal quotation marks omitted). Nonetheless, we held that the \n \nSixth Amendment permitted a judge\xe2\x80\x94it did not require a jury\xe2\x80\x94to make that factual determination. Id., at 164.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe consequently concluded that Apprendi does not encompass every kind of fact-related sentencing decision that increases the statutory maximum. In doing so, we wrote that the \xe2\x80\x9canimating principle\xe2\x80\x9d of Apprendi\xe2\x80\x99s rule \xe2\x80\x9cis the preservation of the jury\xe2\x80\x99s historic role as a bul- wark between the State and the accused at the trial for an alleged offense.\xe2\x80\x9d 555 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 168. And we refused to extend Apprendi\xe2\x80\x99s rule to a new category of sentence-related facts for two basic reasons.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFirst, we considered a historical question, namely, whether \xe2\x80\x9cthe finding of a particular fact was understood as within \xe2\x80\x98the domain of the jury .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. by those who framed the Bill of Rights.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 555 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 168 (quoting Harris, 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 557). And we read the \xe2\x80\x9chistorical record\xe2\x80\x9d as showing that \xe2\x80\x9cin England before the founding of our Nation, and in the early American States,\xe2\x80\x9d the jury \xe2\x80\x9cplayed no role in the decision to impose sentences consecutively or concurrently.\xe2\x80\x9d 555 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 168\xe2\x80\x93169 (footnote omitted). Rather, that decision \xe2\x80\x9crested exclusively with the judge.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 168.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSecond, recognizing that \xe2\x80\x9cadministration of a discrete criminal justice system is among the [States\xe2\x80\x99] basic sovereign prerogatives,\xe2\x80\x9d we considered the need to \xe2\x80\x9crespect .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. state sovereignty.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. We expressed concern lest application of Apprendi to this kind of decision inhibit state legislative efforts to establish a fairer sentencing system by helping to bring about more uniform sentencing. Ice, 555 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 171. We concluded that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]either Apprendi nor our \n \nSixth Amendment traditions compel straitjacketing the States\xe2\x80\x9d in this respect. Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis case presents another new category of fact-related sentencing decisions, namely, decisions about the amount of a fine. Thus, as the majority recognizes, we must be- gin with a historical question. Ante, at 8. Who\xe2\x80\x94judge or jury\xe2\x80\x94found the facts that determine the amount of a criminal fine \xe2\x80\x9cin England before the founding of our Nation, and in the early American States?\xe2\x80\x9d Ice, supra, at 169 (footnote omitted). Unlike the majority, I believe the answer to this question is that, in most instances, the judge made that determination.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIV\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tApprendi relied heavily upon the fact that in \xe2\x80\x9cEngland before the founding of our Nation\xe2\x80\x9d the prescribed punishment for more serious crimes, i.e., felonies, was typically fixed\xe2\x80\x94indeed, fixed at death. 530 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 478\xe2\x80\x93480; see J. Baker, An Introduction to English Legal History 512 (4th ed. 2007); J. Beattie, Crime and the Courts in England, 1660\xe2\x80\x931800, pp. 409, 450\xe2\x80\x93451 (1986) (hereinafter Beattie); Langbein, The English Criminal Trial on the Eve of the French Revolution, in The Trial Jury in England, France, Germany 1700\xe2\x80\x931900, pp.\xc2\xa013, 16, 36\xe2\x80\x9337 (A. Schioppa ed. 1987). The facts related to the application of that punishment were typically elements of the crime. And the jury, not the judge, determined the existence of those facts. See 4 W. Blackstone, Commentaries on the Laws of England 343 (1769) (hereinafter Blackstone); see also Baker, supra, at 512\xe2\x80\x93518 (in practice, the jury or judge could ameliorate capital punishment through application of doctrines such as \xe2\x80\x9cpious perjury,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cbenefit of clergy,\xe2\x80\x9d and reprieves, or the King could grant a royal pardon); Beattie 419\xe2\x80\x93435 (same).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPunishment for lesser crimes, however, included fines. And under the common law, the judge, not the jury, determined the amount of the fine and the sentencing facts relevant to the setting of that amount. See Baker, supra, at 512; Beattie 459. Pertinent sentencing facts typically concerned the manner in which the offender committed the crime and the characteristics of that offender. See id., at 456\xe2\x80\x93460. Thus, in 1769, Blackstone wrote:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cOur statute law has not therefore often ascertained the quantity of fines, nor the common law ever; it directing such an offense to be punished by fine, in general, without specifying the certain sum.\xe2\x80\x9d Blackstone 372 (emphasis added).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThat is because\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cthe quantum, in particular, of pecuniary fines neither can, nor ought to be, ascertained by any invariable law. The value of money itself changes from a thousand causes; and, at all events, what is ruin to one man\xe2\x80\x99s fortune, may be a matter of indifference to another.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 371.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tMoreover, the \xe2\x80\x9cquantum\xe2\x80\x9d of pecuniary fines\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cmust frequently vary, from aggravations or otherwise of the offense [i.e., the manner in which the crime was committed], the quality and conditions of the parties [e.g., the offender\xe2\x80\x99s characteristics], and from innumerable other circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSimilarly, the 18th-century statesman and treatise-writer Baron Auckland pointed out that in 10th-century England pre-Norman law had attached a fixed financial penalty to each specific crime. Principles of Penal Law 69 (2d ed. 1771). That law, for example, imposed a penalty of 3 cows for perjury and 12 cows for the rape of a maid. Ibid. This system, Baron Auckland added, ignored variations in, for example, the differing value of a fixed fine, say a cow, over time and among individuals; it also ignored the manner in which the offense was committed and the characteristics of the offender. Id., at 69\xe2\x80\x9372. For those reasons, 18th-century English law ordinarily left \xe2\x80\x9cthe quantum of the fine\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9cthe discretion of the Judges.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 68 (emphasis deleted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c[Because t]he enormity and tendency of the crime, the malice and wilfulness of the intention, the inconsiderateness and suddenness of the act, the age, faculties, and fortune of the offender, form a chain of complex questions; which can be resolved only by the evidence of each separate charge, and for which no human foresight can provide .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. then arises a necessary appeal to the breast of the judge.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 72 (emphasis added).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe only generally applicable limitations on the judge, when imposing the fine, were those contained in the English Bill of Rights and the Magna Carta. 1 W. & M., ch.\xc2\xa02, \xc2\xa711, in 3 Eng. Stat. at Large 440 (forbidding \xe2\x80\x9cexcessive Fines\xe2\x80\x9d); Magna Carta \xc2\xa720, 9 Hen. III, \xc2\xa714, in 1 Eng. Stat. at Large 5 (1225) (fine cannot deprive offender of means of livelihood); see Auckland, supra, at 73 (so interpreting Magna Carta); Blackstone 372\xe2\x80\x93373 (same).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo be sure, the jury, not the judge, would determine the facts that made up the elements of the crime, even though those elements might be relevant to whether a fine could apply and, if so, the amount of the fine imposed as well. The common law, for example, defined larceny as the theft of goods that had some intrinsic value and divided the offense into grand larceny, which was theft of goods valued at more than a shilling, and petit larceny, which was theft of goods worth less than a shilling. Blackstone 229\xe2\x80\x93234; Langbein, supra, at 16\xe2\x80\x9317; see also Beattie 424 (whether \xe2\x80\x9cbenefit of clergy\xe2\x80\x9d was available depended on value stolen). Consequently, the jury would determine the value of the goods in question. In doing so, the jury might \xe2\x80\x9cmanipulate the sentence by valuing the goods at under a shilling and thereby spare the defendant the capital sanction.\xe2\x80\x9d Lillquist, The Puzzling Return of Jury Sentencing: Misgivings About Apprendi, 82 N.\xc2\xa0C. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 621, 636 (2004). But otherwise \xe2\x80\x9cthe jury could not influence what other penalties\xe2\x80\x9d like fines the defendant might face because in \xe2\x80\x9cnon-capital criminal cases\xe2\x80\x9d the amount of punishment \xe2\x80\x9cwas left solely in the hands of the justices.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI cannot determine with any certainty the extent to which 18th-century law placed other relevant limitations upon the judges\xe2\x80\x99 authority to determine fine-related sentencing facts. I have found an 1814 English treatise on criminal pleading that says, unlike in cases \xe2\x80\x9cwhere to constitute the offence the value must [only] be of a certain amount,\xe2\x80\x9d in cases \xe2\x80\x9cwhere the offence, or its defined measure of punishment, depends upon the quantity of that excess .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a variance from the amount averred .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. will be fatal.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 T. Starkie, A Treatise on Criminal Pleading 187\xe2\x80\x93188 (emphasis deleted). It then adds that \xe2\x80\x9cin the case of usury, where the judgment depends upon the quantum taken, the usurious contract must be averred according to the fact; and a variance from it, in evidence, would be fatal, because the penalty is apportioned to the value.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 188. And an 18th-century treatise says that it is questionable whether it is necessary \xe2\x80\x9cto set forth the Value of the Goods in an Indictment of Trespass for any other Purpose than to aggravate the Fine.\xe2\x80\x9d 2 W. Hawkins, A Treatise of the Pleas of the Crown, ch. 25, \xc2\xa775, pp. 234\xe2\x80\x93235 (3d ed. 1739) (emphasis added). One might read these statements as supporting the majority, for they might in- dicate that, where a statute sets forth facts that deter- mine a pecuniary penalty, then a jury, not judge, would determine those facts.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut whether that is the correct reading is unclear. For one thing, prosecutions for economic crimes were usually brought by injured parties and the \xe2\x80\x9cfine\xe2\x80\x9d in such cases went in whole or in part to compensate that party for damages. See Beattie 35\xe2\x80\x9336, 192. For example, immediately following the sentence I have just quoted, Hawkins wrote that it is questionable whether it is necessary \xe2\x80\x9cto set forth the Value of the Goods .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in an Indictment of Larceny for any other Purpose than to sh[o]w that the Crime amounts to grand Larceny, and to ascertain the Goods, thereby the better to [e]ntitle the Prosecutor to a Resti- tution.\xe2\x80\x9d Hawkins, supra, at 234\xe2\x80\x93235 (emphasis added; footnote omitted). Likewise, Blackstone dated English usury law back to a 1545 statute that provided as the penalty that the offending lender shall both \xe2\x80\x9cmake f[i]ne .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. at the King\xe2\x80\x99s will and pleasure\xe2\x80\x9d and forfeit \xe2\x80\x9ctreble value\xe2\x80\x9d of the money borrowed\xe2\x80\x94with half to the King and the other half \xe2\x80\x9cto him or them that will sue for the same.\xe2\x80\x9d 37 Hen. VIII, ch. 9, in 3 Stat. of Realm 997 (emphasis added); see Blackstone 156; see also M. Ord, An Essay on the Law of Usury 122\xe2\x80\x93123 (3d ed. 1809) (treble-value forfeitures recovered through information qui\xc2\xa0tam but dis- cretionary fines recovered through criminal indictment). Thus, the statutes at issue were what American courts would later call quasi-civil statutes\xe2\x80\x94part civil, part criminal; see also Beattie 457.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tParliament consequently would have had a special rea- son for requiring jury determinations of the amount of the pecuniary penalty. And Parliament had the authority to depart from the common law and to insist that juries determine sentencing facts without establishing a generally applicable principle. The relevant question here is how often and for what purposes Parliament did so. Blackstone himself wrote that such statutes fixing fines in amounts were both in derogation of the common law and uncommon. Blackstone 372. Finally, no one here argues that we adopt the rule actually suggested by the treatises. That rule is not that sentencing is to be done according to value found by the jury but instead that a discrepancy between the value alleged and value found by the jury might render the entire case fatal. See Starkie, supra, at 188.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThus, I cannot place great weight upon these statutes. The parties did not refer to them in their briefs. And in any event, the historical sources taken together make clear that the predominant practice in 18th-century England was for a judge, not a jury, to find sentencing facts related to the imposition of a fine.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIndeed, the Court in Apprendi conceded the point. It distinguished 18th-century punishments for greater crimes (fixed punishments) from punishments for lesser crimes (included fines). 530 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 480, n.\xc2\xa07. And it wrote that \xe2\x80\x9cjudges most commonly imposed discretionary \xe2\x80\x98sentences\xe2\x80\x99 of fines .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. upon misdemeanants.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added). Insofar as 18th-century English practice helps determine what the Framers would have thought about the scope of the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s terms\xe2\x80\x94here, the \n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s right to trial by an impartial jury\xe2\x80\x94that practice suggests they would not have expected that right to include determination of sentencing facts relevant only to the imposition of a fine.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPractice in the \xe2\x80\x9cearly American States\xe2\x80\x9d is even less am- biguous. In the colonial era, judges would normally determine the amount of a fine (within an unlimited or otherwise broad range) while also determining related sentencing facts (say, about the manner in which the offender committed the crime and the offender\xe2\x80\x99s characteristics). Legal historians tell us that in the American colonies a criminal fine was \xe2\x80\x9coverwhelmingly the most common of the non-capital punishments,\xe2\x80\x9d that in most instances the range of the fine was \xe2\x80\x9capparently without limit except insofar as it was within the expectation of the court that it would be paid,\xe2\x80\x9d that the judge established the precise amount of the fine, and that the amount was \xe2\x80\x9ctailored individually to the particular case.\xe2\x80\x9d Preyer, Penal Measures in the American Colonies: An Overview, 26 Am. J. Legal Hist. 326, 350 (1982). \xe2\x80\x9c[C]olonial judges, like their English brethren, possessed a great deal of discretion\xe2\x80\x9d and could set the amount of fine \xe2\x80\x9cdepending upon the nature of the defendant and the crime.\xe2\x80\x9d Lillquist, 82 N.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0L. Rev., at 640\xe2\x80\x93641.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEnactment of the Constitution and Bill of Rights did not change this practice. Some early American statutes specified that the judge has discretion to set the amount of the fine while saying nothing about amount. E.g., Crimes Act of 1790, ch. 9, \xc2\xa721, \n\n1Stat.\n117 (any person who bribes a judge \xe2\x80\x9con conviction thereof shall be fined and imprisoned at the discretion of the court\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa728, \n\n1Stat.\n118 (any person who does violence to an ambassador or public min- ister, \xe2\x80\x9con conviction, shall be imprisoned not exceeding three years, and fined at the discretion of the court\xe2\x80\x9d). Others set only a maximum limitation. E.g., Act of Mar. 3, 1791, ch. 15, \xc2\xa739, \n\n1Stat.\n208 (officer of inspection con- victed of oppression or extortion \xe2\x80\x9cshall be fined not exceed- ing five hundred dollars, or imprisoned not exceeding six months, or both, at the discretion of the court\xe2\x80\x9d). In respect to these statutes, Justice Iredell wrote in 1795 that the \xe2\x80\x9ccommon law practice .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. must be adhered to; that is to say, the jury are to find whether the prisoner be guilty, and .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the court must assess the fine.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Mundell, 27 F.\xc2\xa0Cas. 23, 24 (No. 15,834) (CC Va.).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tStill other statutes, as in England, specifically keyed the amount of the fine to a specific factual finding. A section of the Crimes Act of 1790, for example, said that any person who upon United States property or the high seas \xe2\x80\x9cshall take and carry away, with an intent to steal or purloin the personal goods of another .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall, on conviction, be fined not exceeding the fourfold value of the property so stolen.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa716, \n\n1Stat.\n116. This crime has several elements: (1) taking and (2) carrying away (3) with intent to steal (4) personal goods (5) belonging to another (6) on United States property or the high seas. The jury must find the existence of these facts beyond a reasonable doubt to establish a conviction. But the statute also says that the fine cannot exceed \xe2\x80\x9cthe fourfold value of the property so stolen.\xe2\x80\x9d And it thereby requires the finding of a sentencing fact, namely the value of the stolen property. Who would make this determination\xe2\x80\x94judge or jury?\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUnlike in 18th-century England, in the United States there is case law directly answering the question. In United States v. Tyler, 7 Cranch 285 (1812), this Court considered a federal embargo statute making it a crime to \xe2\x80\x9cput\xe2\x80\x9d certain \xe2\x80\x9cgoods\xe2\x80\x9d on board a ship with intent to \xe2\x80\x9cexport\xe2\x80\x9d them outside of the United States. See Act of Jan. 9, 1809, ch.\xc2\xa05, \xc2\xa71, \n\n2Stat.\n506. The statute also provided that an offender\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cgoods\xe2\x80\x9d and ship \xe2\x80\x9cshall be forfeited,\xe2\x80\x9d and the offender, \xe2\x80\x9cshall, upon conviction,\xe2\x80\x9d be \xe2\x80\x9cfined a sum, by the court before which the conviction is had, equal to four times the value of such specie, goods, wares and merchandise.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The statute thereby required determination of a sentencing fact, namely \xe2\x80\x9cthe value of such .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. goods.\xe2\x80\x9d Was the finding of this sentencing fact for the judge or for the jury?\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn Tyler, the defendant had been indicted for attempting to export 19 barrels of pearl-ashes, valued at $600. Ante, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314. The jury convicted the defendant, but when doing so, it said that it found the defendant guilty of having tried to export \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98pot-ashes .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. worth two hundred and eighty dollars.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 7 Cranch, at 285 (emphasis deleted). The defendant appealed, claiming a difference between the jury\xe2\x80\x99s basis for conviction and the crime as charged in the indictment. The difference between the words \xe2\x80\x9cpearl-ashes\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cpot-ashes\xe2\x80\x9d is unlikely to have mattered, for pearl-ash is simply a refined grade of pot-ash (potassium carbonate). See T. Barker, R. Dickinson, & D. Hardie, Origins of the Synthetic Alkali Industry in Britain, 23 Economica 158, 163 (1956). Thus, the defendant did not focus upon that difference. Rather, he claimed that the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict \xe2\x80\x9cwas not sufficiently certain as to the value of the property charged in the indictment.\xe2\x80\x9d Tyler, 7 Cranch, at 285 (emphasis added). Because $280 differs from $600, the jury had not found him guilty of the crime charged.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Supreme Court, however, found that the jury\xe2\x80\x99s finding as to valuation was not relevant. It upheld the conviction because it was \xe2\x80\x9cof the opinion that, under this law, no valuation by the jury was necessary to enable the Circuit Court to impose the proper fine.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added). The Court did not say explicitly that the \n \nSixth Amendment permitted the judge to find the relevant sen- tencing fact. See ante, at 14. But it seems unlikely that a Court that included Chief Justice John Marshall, Justice Joseph Story, and others familiar with both the common law and the Constitution would have interpreted a federal statute as they did if either contemporary legal practice or the Constitution suggested or required a different interpretation.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNor can we say that the Court did not fully consider the matter. Justice Story later authoritatively interpreted Tyler. Sitting as a Circuit Justice in United States v. Mann, 26 F.\xc2\xa0Cas. 1153 (No. 15,717) (CCNH 1812), he considered the same judge/jury question in respect to the same embargo statute. His court wrote that in \xe2\x80\x9cTyler, 7 Cranch 285, in a prosecution on this same clause, the court held that the fine and quadruple value must be assessed and adjudged by the court, and not by the jury.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1153 (emphasis added); see also 26 F.\xc2\xa0Cas. 1153, 1155 (No.\xc2\xa015,718) (CCNH 1812) (Story, J.) (noting that the Su- preme Court would not have reached its result unless satisfied \xe2\x80\x9cthat the fine was to be imposed by the court, and not found by the jury\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThus, nothing in early American practice suggests that the Framers thought that the \n \nSixth Amendment jury trial right encompassed a right to have a jury determine fine-related sentencing facts. But, to the contrary, there is a Supreme Court opinion, namely Tyler, that holds, or at least strongly indicates, the opposite.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tC\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority reaches a different conclusion. But the majority does not pose what I believe to be the relevant historical question, namely whether traditionally \xe2\x80\x9cin England before the founding of our Nation, and in the early American States,\xe2\x80\x9d see Ice, 555 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 169 (footnote omitted), judges, not juries, normally determined fine-related sentencing facts. Instead, it asks whether a jury, rather than the judge, found those facts in that subclass of cases where a statute \xe2\x80\x9cpeg[ged] the amount of a fine to the determination of specified facts.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10. It concludes that \xe2\x80\x9cthe predominant practice was for such facts to be alleged in the indictment and proved to the jury.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPutting the question this way invites a circular response. As is true of the English usury cases, nothing prohibits a legislature from requiring a jury to find a sentencing fact in a particular subset of cases. And obviously when a State does so, the jury will indeed have to find those facts. Thus, if, say, 10 States decide to make juries find facts that will set the fine for, say, simple larceny, then jury practice in those States (during, say, the 19th century) will include the jury\xe2\x80\x99s finding of those sentencing facts. But that circumstance tells us only that in those 10 States for those specific statutes the legislatures so required. It tells us little, if anything, about practices in most States, and it tells us nothing at all about traditional practice in England or 18th-century America. Nor does a discovery that, say, 10 state legislatures once required juries, rather than judges, generally to set fines tell us about the scope of the \n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s constitu- tional right to trial by jury. The matter is important be- cause the majority rests its conclusion almost exclusively upon reports of mid-19th-century jury trials in a handful of States, namely Alabama, Illinois, Indiana, Massachusetts, and New Hampshire, and a treatise that bases its statements upon those cases. Ante, at 10\xe2\x80\x9312.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tScholars tell us that in fact there were about 10 States\xe2\x80\x94including Alabama, Illinois, and Indiana\xe2\x80\x94that (after ratification of the \n \nSixth Amendment) enacted statutes that required juries, not judges, to determine a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s punishment, including not only the length of a prison term but also the amount of a fine. See Iontcheva, Jury Sentencing as Democratic Practice, 89 Va. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 311, 317 (2003); King, Origins of Felony Jury Sentencing in the United States 78 Chi.-Kent L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 937, 963 (2003). The courts that considered this practice, however, did not believe that the constitutional right to jury trial compelled it.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAlabama\xe2\x80\x99s Supreme Court, for example, explained that its State\xe2\x80\x99s jury-sentencing system, which allowed the jury \xe2\x80\x9cto determine both the fine and imprisonment,\xe2\x80\x9d was in derogation of, and created \xe2\x80\x9can innovation upon[,] the rules of the common law, so far as it transfers [those] powers from the court to the jury.\xe2\x80\x9d Hawkins v. State, 3 Stew. & P. 63 (1832). Thus, in State v. Garner, 8 Port. 447 (1839), see ante, at 10, the malicious mischief statute at issue said that the offender would \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98be fined in such sum as the jury trying the same may assess, not exceeding four fold the value of the property injured or destroyed.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 8 Port., at 448 (emphasis added). The statute, in other words, transferred all sentencing facts to the jury and was not illus- trative of 18th-century practice. Further, the statute said that the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98fine shall be paid to the party injured.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The court held that it was consequently proper to allege the amount of the property\xe2\x80\x99s value in the indictment, not because the State\xe2\x80\x99s constitution required any such thing, but because \xe2\x80\x9cthe fine thus assesse[d] is for the benefit of the injured party\xe2\x80\x9d; the case \xe2\x80\x9cis, therefore, a quasi civil proceeding\xe2\x80\x9d; and for that reason \xe2\x80\x9cit would be more con- sonant to the rules of pleading, and to the principles which govern analogous cases, that the indictment should contain an averment of the value of the property.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; Ord, Law of Usury, at 122\xe2\x80\x93123 (usury as quasi-civil proceeding).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIllinois law was similar. Illinois became a jury-sentencing State in 1831. See Iontcheva, supra, at 317, n.\xc2\xa028 (citing Act of Feb. 15, 1831, \xc2\xa742, 1830 Ill. Laws 103, 113). The Illinois Supreme Court subsequently wrote that, even though \xe2\x80\x9cat common law .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. juries .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. never were invested with the power of determining the character or extent of the punishment .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0, we are to be governed entirely the provisions and enactments of our code of criminal jurisprudence.\xe2\x80\x9d Blevings v. People, 1 Scam. 172 (1835). And in Clark v. People, 1 Scam. 117 (1833), see ante, at 10, the court made clear that the arson statute at issue\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cha[d] changed the common law, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [that the] fine equal in value to the property burne[d] is imposed as part of the punishment[; hence,] [t]he indictment .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. should have charged the value of the property destroyed, [for] otherwise it could not properly have been inquired into by the jury.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 Scam., at 117.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIndiana was another jury-sentencing State. Iontcheva, supra, at 317, n.\xc2\xa028; King, supra, at 937. Indiana case law decided before Indiana changed its system indicates that the judge could decide certain facts required to set the applicable maximum fine. E.g., Morris v. State, 1 Blackf. 37 (1819). But after Indiana became a jury-sentencing State, its courts held, not surprisingly, that under Indiana law the jury must determine sentencing facts. See Ritchey v. State, 7 Blackf. 168, 169 (1844); ante, at 10.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMassachusetts presents a special circumstance. The two Massachusetts cases that the majority cites, ante, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311, are larceny cases. Value traditionally was an element of the crime of larceny\xe2\x80\x94both because larceny was theft of goods that had some intrinsic value and because value distinguished grand larceny from petit larceny\xe2\x80\x94and thus juries traditionally had to determine at least some facts about the value of the property stolen. See Blackstone 229, 234. Massachusetts had abolished the distinction between grand and petit larceny before its courts decided the two cases the majority cites. See Commonwealth v. Smith, 1 Mass. 245, 246 (1804). But those decisions nonetheless rest in significant part upon the jury\xe2\x80\x99s traditional larceny factfinding role. In Hope v. Commonwealth, 9 Metcalf 134 (1845), for example, the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court wrote:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cThe well settled practice, familiar to us all, has been that of stating in the indictment the value of the article alleged to have been stolen.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. The reason for requiring this allegation and finding of value may have been, originally, that a distinction might appear between the offences of grand and petit larceny .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. Our statutes .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. prescribe the punishment for larceny, with reference to the value of the property stolen; and for this reason, as well as because it is in conformity with long established practice, the court are of opinion that the value of the property alleged to be stolen must be set forth in the indictment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 136\xe2\x80\x93137.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe \xe2\x80\x9clong established practice\xe2\x80\x9d to which the court refers is larceny case practice, not practice in all criminal cases.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe New Hampshire case to which the majority refers, State v. Goodrich, 46 N.\xc2\xa0H. 186 (1865), ante, at 11, is also a larceny case that relied on the \xe2\x80\x9cestablished\xe2\x80\x9d larceny case practice. The court explained:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cThe indictment ought to state the value of the articles stolen that it may appear whether the offence be grand or petit larceny, and such we believe is the settled practice.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. It has been held in some jurisdictions, that, in case no value is alleged, the offence charged may be regarded as simple larceny, and a conviction be had accordingly .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. but we think it best to adhere to the well established doctrine in such cases .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. It may also be suggested, that, in the case of simple larceny, the respondent may be sentenced to pay the owner of the goods stolen, treble the value thereof, which is an additional reason for requiring the character of the offence to be stated.\xe2\x80\x9d 46 N.\xc2\xa0H., at 187\xe2\x80\x93188.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe court wrote nothing to suggest that its holding rested on generally applicable constitutional grounds. And it was in the New Hampshire federal circuit court a half-century earlier when Justice Story had indicated that the Federal Constitution did not impose any such requirement. See Mann, 26 F.\xc2\xa0Cas., at 1155 (No.\xc2\xa015,718).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThat leaves the majority\xe2\x80\x99s puzzling 1895 Federal District Court case from Kansas. United States v. Woodruff, 68 F. 536 (D. Kan. 1895); ante, at 11. The circumstances of this case are highly unusual, and the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning as to why no fine could be set seems to have rested on a combination of statutory construction and constitutional principle. See Woodruff v. United States, 58 F. 766, 767\xe2\x80\x93768 (CC Kan. 1893); Woodruff, 68 F., at 538\xe2\x80\x93539. Still, I concede this case to the majority\xe2\x80\x94as the lone swallow that cannot make the majority\xe2\x80\x99s summer.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTaken together, the 19th-century cases upon which the majority rests its holding do not show anything about practice in the vast majority of States. They concede that common-law practice was to the contrary. And they tell us little about the meaning of the \n \nSixth Amendment. Even were that not so, I do not understand why these mid-19th-century cases should tell us more about the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s meaning than, say, the common 20th-century practice of leaving sentencing fact determinations to the judge. This Court apparently once approved the latter practice as constitutional. E.g., McMillan v. Pennsylvania, \n\n477 U.\xc2\xa0S. 79 (1986)\n; Almendarez-Torres, \n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 224\n. And these cases seem more closely related to the present topic.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tD\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe upshot is that both 18th-century English common law and 18th-century American law typically provided judges with broad discretion to assess fines. The judge, not the jury, would normally determine fine-related sentencing facts. In this respect, ordinary 18th-century sentencing practice related to fines was unlike sentencing practice in respect to felonies. In the latter case, in Apprendi\xe2\x80\x99s view, punishment was normally \xe2\x80\x9cfixed\xe2\x80\x9d and the judge\xe2\x80\x99s sentencing role was consequently minimal. 530 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 478\xe2\x80\x93480. In the former case, namely fines, the judge\xe2\x80\x99s role was not normally minimal, but the opposite. For these reasons, I believe that allowing a judge to determine sentencing facts related to imposition of a fine does not invade the historic province of the jury. The historical test that we set forth in Ice is satisfied.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tV\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn Ice, we also took account of the practical extent to which extending Apprendi\xe2\x80\x99s rule beyond the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98central sphere of [its] concern\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d would \xe2\x80\x9cdiminish\xe2\x80\x9d the States\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9crole\xe2\x80\x9d in \xe2\x80\x9cdevising solutions to difficult legal problems .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. absent impelling reason to do so.\xe2\x80\x9d 555 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 171\xe2\x80\x93172. In particular, we feared that insisting that juries determine the relevant sentencing facts (concerning concurrent, as opposed to consecutive, punishment) would unjustifiably interfere with a State\xe2\x80\x99s legislative efforts \xe2\x80\x9cto rein in the discretion judges possessed at common law to impose consecutive sentences at will.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 171. It would in- hibit (indeed \xe2\x80\x9cstraightjacke[t]\xe2\x80\x9d) States seeking to make \xe2\x80\x9cconcurrent sentences the rule, and consecutive sentences the exception.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. We said that we were \xe2\x80\x9cunclear how many other state initiatives would fall\xe2\x80\x9d if Apprendi were extended, and that expansion would be \xe2\x80\x9cdifficult for States to administer.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 171\xe2\x80\x93172. We believed that these considerations argued strongly against any such \xe2\x80\x9cexpansion.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tHere, the same kinds of considerations similarly argue against \xe2\x80\x9cexpansion\xe2\x80\x9d of Apprendi\xe2\x80\x99s rule. Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision applies to the States. In the 1950\xe2\x80\x99s and thereafter, States as well as the Federal Government recognized a serious problem in respect to the sentencing of corporations. Fines, imposed as a punishment upon corporate offenders, were both nonuniform (treating identical offenders differently) and too often they were set too low. Judges would frequently fine corporations in amounts that failed to approximate the harm a corporation had caused or the gain that it had obtained through its illegal activity, both because often the statutory maximums were low and be- cause often the fines imposed tended to be substantially lower than those maximums. See Gruner, Towards an Organizational Jurisprudence: Transforming Corporate Criminal Law Through Federal Sentencing Reform, 36 Ariz. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 407, 408 (1994); Kadish, Some Observa- tions on the Use of Criminal Sanctions in Enforcing Economic Regulations, 30 U. Chi. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 423, 435, n.\xc2\xa055 (1963); Nagel & Swenson, Federal Sentencing Guidelines for Corporations: Their Development, Theoretical Underpinnings, and Some Thoughts About Their Future, 71 Wash. U. L.\xc2\xa0Q. 205, 215 (1993).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tConsequently, the authors of the Model Penal Code adopted a model provision stating that, in respect to offenses involving financial gain, a court could impose an alternative \xe2\x80\x9chigher\xe2\x80\x9d fine \xe2\x80\x9cequal to double the pecuniary gain derived from the offense by the offender.\xe2\x80\x9d Model Penal Code \xc2\xa76.03(5), 10A U.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0A. 259 (2001). New York soon thereafter adopted such a provision. N.\xc2\xa0Y. Penal Law Ann. \xc2\xa780.10(2)(b) (West 2009). And other States followed New York\xe2\x80\x99s example with similar provisions permitting judges to set fines equal to twice the gain to the offender or twice the loss to the victim, thereby helping to diminish disparity while helping potential victims by increasing deterrence. E.g., Conn. Gen. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa753a\xe2\x80\x9344 (West 2007); Fla. Stat. \xc2\xa7775.083(1)(f) (2010). Many of these statutes say in particular that the \xe2\x80\x9ccourt\xe2\x80\x9d shall make the finding of gain or loss, in a separate hearing if necessary. E.g., N.\xc2\xa0Y. Penal Law Ann. \xc2\xa780.00(3) (West 2009); N.\xc2\xa0J. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa72C:43\xe2\x80\x933(e) (West 2005).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Federal Government followed suit. In some instances, such as RCRA, where environmental harm likely varies with the length of the violation period, Congress advanced its uniformity and deterrence goals by tying a dollar-limited fine to the length of time during which that violation took place. \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa76928(d)(2)(A). In other instances, it did so through a new general gain-or-loss provision, applying to all offenses, including such crimes as corporate fraud, antitrust violations, and environmental pollution. That provision says:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cAlternative Fine Based on Gain or Loss.\xe2\x80\x94If any person derives pecuniary gain from the offense, or if the offense results in pecuniary loss to a person other than the defendant, the defendant may be fined not more than the greater of twice the gross gain or twice the gross loss, unless imposition of a fine under this subsection would unduly complicate or prolong the sentencing process.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n18 U. S. C. \xc2\xa73571(d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo apply Apprendi\xe2\x80\x99s rule to the fines set forth in such statutes, no less than in Ice, would weaken or destroy the States\xe2\x80\x99 and Federal Government\xe2\x80\x99s efforts \xe2\x80\x9cto rein in the discretion judges possessed at common law,\xe2\x80\x9d Ice, 555 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 171, over fines. Congress, in enacting such statutes, expected judges, not juries, to determine fine-related sen- tencing facts because doing so will often involve highly complex determinations. Where, say, major fraud is at issue, the full extent of the loss (or gain) may be unknown at the time of indictment or at any other time prior to the conclusion of the trial. And in an antitrust or an environmental pollution case, the jury may have particular difficulty assessing different estimates of resulting losses.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe consequence of the majority\xe2\x80\x99s holding, insisting that juries make such determinations, is likely to diminish the fairness of the criminal trial process. A defendant will not find it easy to show the jury at trial that (1) he committed no environmental crime, but (2) in any event, he committed the crime only on 20 days, not 30. Moreover, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s holding will sometimes permit prosecutors to introduce newly relevant evidence that would otherwise have been kept from the jury on the ground that it was cumulative or unduly prejudicial. If victims\xe2\x80\x99 losses are relevant, the prosecutor may be able to produce witness after witness testifying only about the amount of life savings lost to the fraud. The defendant in this case, for example, thought the introduction of evidence about the discovery of mercury and remediation and evacuation of a nearby apartment complex was unduly prejudicial. Brief for United States 51 (citing App. 15 (defendant\xe2\x80\x99s motion in limine to exclude such evidence)). But even if that were so, that evidence might now be admitted as showing the amount of harm caused or the number of days upon which the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s unlawful activity took place.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAdministrative problems here may prove more serious than where, as in Apprendi, prison terms were at stake. In part, that is because corporate criminal cases often focus upon complex frauds, criminal price fixing, extended environmental pollution, food-and-drug safety violations, and the like. Both Congress and the Sentencing Commission have recognized as much. The federal criminal fine statute to which I earlier referred specifically creates an exception where assessing total loss or gain \xe2\x80\x9cwould unduly complicate or prolong the sentencing process.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73571(d). Similarly, Sentencing Guidelines applicable to corporations exclude fine provisions for environmental crimes (along with most crimes involving export violations, food-and-drug safety, agricultural-and-consumer products, and RICO violations) because of the \xe2\x80\x9cpotential difficulty .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of defining and computing loss.\xe2\x80\x9d Nagel & Swenson, supra, at 256; see USSG \xc2\xa78C2.1, and comment., \xc2\xa78C2.10. Where the defendant is a human being, the Government can avoid problems of proof simply by abandoning any effort to obtain a fine; instead, perhaps to the individual defendant\xe2\x80\x99s dismay, the prosecution can seek a longer prison term. Where the criminal defendant is a corporation, however, no such possibility exists.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIf, as seems likely, it becomes too difficult to prove fine-related sentencing facts to a jury, legislatures will have to change their statutes. Some may choose to return to highly discretionary sentencing, with its related risks of nonuniformity. Others may link conviction with fines specified in amount, rather like the 10th-century pre-Norman system of three cows for perjury or more modern mandatory minimum penalties. As Blackstone pointed out, those systems produce sentences that are not proportionate; they tend to treat alike offenders who committed the same crime in very different ways. See 4 Blackstone 371\xe2\x80\x93372.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority believes that 10 years of experience with Apprendi \xe2\x80\x9cattenuate[s]\xe2\x80\x9d any legal claim of reliance on different rule of constitutional law here. Ante, at 16. Perhaps so. Perhaps that experience shows that Apprendi\xe2\x80\x99s jury trial requirement is workable. But there is another less optimistic possibility.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPerhaps that experience, like the canary in a mine-shaft, tells us only that our criminal justice system is no longer the jury-trial-based adversarial system that it once was. We have noted that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]inety-seven percent of fed- eral convictions and ninety-four percent of state convictions are the result of guilty pleas.\xe2\x80\x9d Missouri v. Frye, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 7). We have added that today \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98plea bargaining\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cnot some adjunct to the criminal justice system; it is the criminal justice system.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Scott & Stuntz, Plea Bargaining as Contract, 101 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J. 1909, 1912 (1992)). And in such a system, complex jury trial requirements may affect the strength of a party\xe2\x80\x99s bargaining position rather than the conduct of many actual trials.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAt the same time, the prosecutor in such a system, perhaps armed with statutes providing for mandatory minimum sentences, can become the ultimate adjudicator. The prosecutor/adjudicator plays an important role in many \xe2\x80\x9cEuropean inquisitorial\xe2\x80\x9d systems. But those prosecutors, unlike ours, typically are trained formally to be more like neutral adjudicators than advocates. Cf. Langbein & Weinreb, Continental Criminal Procedure: \xe2\x80\x9cMyth\xe2\x80\x9d and Reality, 87 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J. 1549, 1559 (1978); see, e.g., \xc3\x88cole Nationale de la Magistrature. Today\xe2\x80\x99s holding, by unnecessarily complicating the trial process, may prove workable only because it nudges our system slightly further in this direction. I see no virtue in doing so.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor these reasons, with respect, I dissent.\n\t\t\t
