SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

\n

IN RE UNITED STATES, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on petition for writ of mandamus

\n

No. 17\xe2\x80\x93801.\xe2\x80\x83Decided December 20, 2017

\n

Per Curiam.

\n

This case arises from five related lawsuits that challenge a determination adopted by the Acting Secretary of the Department of Homeland Security (DHS). The determination, announced by the Acting Secretary, is to take immediate steps to rescind a program known as Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals, or DACA, by March 5, 2018. The Acting Secretary stated that her determination was based in part on the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that DACA is unlawful and likely would be enjoined in potentially imminent litigation.

\n

The five suits were filed in the United States District Court for the Northern District of California, and the plaintiffs in those actions are the respondents in the matter now before this Court. The defendants in the District Court, and the petitioners here, include the Govern-ment of the United States, the Acting Secretary, and the President of the United States, all referred to here as the Government.

\n

In the District Court litigation respondents argue that the Acting Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s determination to rescind DACA in the near future is unlawful because, among other reasons, it violates the Administrative Procedure Act (APA) and the Due Process Clause of the\n \nFifth Amendment, including the equal protection guarantee implicit in that Clause.

\n

The issue to be considered here involves respondents\xe2\x80\x99 contention that the administrative record the Government filed to support the Acting Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s determination to rescind DACA is incomplete. The record consists of 256 pages of documents, and the Government contends that it contains all of the nondeliberative material considered by the Acting Secretary in reaching her determination. (Nearly 200 pages consist of published opinions from various federal courts.)

\n

On October 17, the District Court, on respondents\xe2\x80\x99 motion, ordered the Government to complete the administrative record. See Regents of Univ. of Cal. v. Department of Homeland Security, App. C to Pet. for Mandamus, 2017 WL 4642324 (ND Cal., Oct. 17, 2017) (District Court Order). The details of that order are recounted further below. See infra, at 3.

\n

The Government petitioned for a writ of mandamus in the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit. The Court of Appeals, in a divided opinion, denied the Government\xe2\x80\x99s petition. See 875 F.\xc2\xa03d 1200 (2017).

\n

On November 19, three days after the Court of Appeals issued its opinion, respondents moved the District Court to stay its order requiring completion of the administrative record until after the District Court resolved the Government\xe2\x80\x99s motion to dismiss and respondents\xe2\x80\x99 motion for a preliminary injunction. See Motion to Stay in No. 17\xe2\x80\x93cv\xe2\x80\x935211 (Nov. 19, 2017), Doc. 190. The District Court did not grant respondents\xe2\x80\x99 request, instead staying its order for one month.

\n

Still objecting to the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order, the Government now seeks relief in this Court. It has filed here a petition for a writ of mandamus to the District Court, or, in the alternative, for a writ of certiorari to the Court of Appeals.

\n

The Court now grants the petition for a writ of certiorari, vacates the order of the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit, and remands the case.

\n

The District Court\xe2\x80\x99s October 17 order requires the Government to turn over all \xe2\x80\x9cemails, letters, memoranda, notes, media items, opinions and other materials\xe2\x80\x9d that fall within the following categories:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(1) all materials actually seen or considered, however briefly, by Acting Secretary [Elaine] Duke in connection with the potential or actual decision to rescind DACA . . . , (2) all DACA-related materials considered by persons (anywhere in the government) who thereafter provided Acting Secretary Duke with written advice or input regarding the actual or potential rescission of DACA, (3) all DACA-related materials considered by persons (anywhere in the government) who thereafter provided Acting Secretary Duke with verbal input regarding the actual or potential rescission of DACA, (4) all comments and questions propounded by Acting Secretary Duke to advisors or subordinates or others regarding the actual or potential rescission of DACA and their responses, and (5) all materials directly or indirectly considered by former Secretary of DHS John Kelly leading to his February 2017 memorandum not to rescind DACA.\xe2\x80\x9d District Court Order, 2017 WL 4642324, at *8.

\n

The Government makes serious arguments that at least portions of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order are overly broad. (The Government appears to emphasize certain materials in categories 2, 3, and 4.) Under the specific facts of this case, the District Court should have granted respondents\xe2\x80\x99 motion on November 19 to stay implementation of the challenged October 17 order and first resolved the Government\xe2\x80\x99s threshold arguments (that the Acting Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s determination to rescind DACA is unreviewable because it is \xe2\x80\x9ccommitted to agency discretion,\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n5 U. S. C. \xc2\xa7701(a)(2), and that the Immigration and Nationality Act deprives the District Court of jurisdiction). Either of those arguments, if accepted, likely would eliminate the need for the District Court to examine a complete administrative record.

\n

On remand of the case, the Court of Appeals shall take appropriate action so that the following steps can betaken. The District Court should proceed to rule on the Government\xe2\x80\x99s threshold arguments and, in doing so, may consider certifying that ruling for interlocutory appeal under\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71292(b) if appropriate. Thereafter, the Court of Appeals or the District Court in the first instance may consider whether narrower amendments to the record are necessary and appropriate. In any event, the District Court may not compel the Government to disclose any document that the Government believes is privileged without first providing the Government with the opportunity to argue the issue.

\n

This order does not suggest any view on the merits of respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims or the Government\xe2\x80\x99s defenses, or that the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s rulings on the Government\xe2\x80\x99s motion to dismiss and respondents\xe2\x80\x99 motion for preliminary injunction should be delayed.

\n

The judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit is vacated, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

\n

It is so ordered.

SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

IN RE UNITED STATES, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on application for stay and petition for writ of mandamus

\n

[December 8, 2017]

\n

The application for a stay presented to Justice Kennedy and by him referred to the Court is granted, and the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s September 22, 2017, October 17, 2017, and November 20, 2017 orders, to the extent they require discovery and addition to the administrative record filed by the Government, are stayed pending disposition of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s petition for a writ of mandamus or in the alternative a writ of certiorari.

\n

Responses to the Government\xe2\x80\x99s petition for a writ of mandamus or in the alternative a writ of certiorari must be filed by Wednesday, December 13, 2017 at 4:00 p.m.

\n

Justice Breyer, with whom Justice Ginsburg, Justice Sotomayor, and Justice Kagan join, dissenting from grant of stay.

\n

On September 5, 2017, the Government announced its decision to terminate the Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals (DACA) program, effective March 5, 2018. The Department of Homeland Security (DHS) had adopted DACA in 2012. Since that time, DACA has provided that immigrants brought to the United States illegally as children who meet certain other requirements could obtain work authorization, a social security number, and permission to travel overseas and lawfully return to the United States. Nearly 800,000 people have benefited from the program.

\n

After the Government announced its decision to terminate DACA, respondents filed suit in the U.\xc2\xa0S. District Court for the Northern District of California to challenge the Government\xe2\x80\x99s termination of the program under the Administrative Procedure Act (APA) and on other grounds. The merits of that challenge have not yet been addressed by the District Court, and they are not before us. But the Government has filed a petition for a writ of mandamus in this Court to challenge the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order that it provide additional documents to complete the administrative record concerning the Government\xe2\x80\x99s decision to terminate DACA. The U.\xc2\xa0S. Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit previously denied the Government most of the relief the Government seeks here. See In\xc2\xa0re United States, ___ F.\xc2\xa03d ___ (2017). I would do the same.

\n

A writ of mandamus is \xe2\x80\x9ca \xe2\x80\x98drastic and extraordinary\xe2\x80\x99 remedy \xe2\x80\x98reserved for really extraordinary causes.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Cheney v. United States Dist. Court for D. C.,\n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 367,\n380 (2004)\n (quoting Ex parte Fahey,\n\n332 U.\xc2\xa0S. 258\n\xe2\x80\x93260 (1947)). In my view, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s arguments do not come close to carrying the heavy burden that the Government bears in seeking such extraordinary relief. With respect, I therefore dissent from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to grant a stay pending further consideration of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s petition for a writ of mandamus.

\n

I

\n

The Government\xe2\x80\x99s primary argument is that \xe2\x80\x9cthe district court plainly erred by .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. ordering the government to \xe2\x80\x98complete\xe2\x80\x99 the administrative record with materials beyond those presented by the agency to the court,\xe2\x80\x9d because a reviewing court\xe2\x80\x99s sole task under the APA is to \xe2\x80\x9cdetermine whether the agency\xe2\x80\x99s action may be upheld on the basis of the reasons the agency provides and \xe2\x80\x98the record the agency presents to the reviewing court.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Mandamus 19, 24 (quoting ___ F.\xc2\xa03d, at ___ (Watford, J., dissenting) (slip op., at 1)). The Government thus contends that review of its decision terminating DACA must be based exclusively on the documents that the Government itself unilaterally selected for submission to the District Court. I am not aware of any precedent supporting the Government\xe2\x80\x99s position.

\n

The APA is clear that a court reviewing agency action must review \xe2\x80\x9cthe whole record\xe2\x80\x9d to determine whether that action is lawful.\n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7706. The basic question here is what constitutes \xe2\x80\x9cthe whole record\xe2\x80\x9d that the court must review. We held in Citizens to Preserve Overton Park, Inc. v. Volpe,\n\n401 U.\xc2\xa0S. 402,\n420 (1971)\n, that the \xe2\x80\x9cwhole record\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9cthe full administrative record that was before the Secretary at the time he made his decision.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Neither this Court nor the lower courts has ever read Overton Park to limit the \xe2\x80\x9cfull administrative record\xe2\x80\x9d to those materials that the agency unilaterally decides should be considered by the reviewing court.

\n

Indeed, judicial review cannot function if the agency is permitted to decide unilaterally what documents it submits to the reviewing court as the administrative record. Effective review depends upon the administrative record containing all relevant materials presented to the agency, including not only materials supportive of the government\xe2\x80\x99s decision but also materials contrary to the government\xe2\x80\x99s decision. See Motor Vehicle Mfrs. Assn. of United States, Inc. v. State Farm Mut. Automobile Ins. Co.,\n\n463 U.\xc2\xa0S. 29\n\xe2\x80\x9344 (1983). Otherwise, the reviewing court cannot engage in the \xe2\x80\x9cthorough, probing, in-depth review\xe2\x80\x9d that the APA requires. Overton Park, 401 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 415\xe2\x80\x93416. A court deprived of a full administrative record could not consider, for example, whether the decision was based on the consideration of irrelevant factors, id., at 411\xe2\x80\x93412; whether it considered the relevant factors, id., at 416; whether the decision was \xe2\x80\x9carbitrary, capricious, an abuse of discretion, or otherwise not in accordance with the law,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7706(2)(A); or whether the decision was unlawful for some other reason.

\n

Perhaps for this reason, the lower courts seem to have unanimously rejected the Government\xe2\x80\x99s position that the agency may unilaterally determine the contents of the administrative record that a court may review. In Thompson v. Department of Labor, 885 F.\xc2\xa02d 551 (CA9 1989), for example, the Ninth Circuit explained:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe whole administrative record .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is not necessarily those documents that the agency has compiled and submitted as \xe2\x80\x98the\xe2\x80\x99 administrative record. The \xe2\x80\x98whole\xe2\x80\x99 administrative record, therefore, consists of all documents and materials directly or indirectly considered by agency decision-makers and includes evidence contrary to the agency\xe2\x80\x99s position.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 555 (citation and some internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

See also, e.g., Bar MK Ranches v. Yuetter, 994 F.\xc2\xa02d 735, 739 (CA10 1993) (\xe2\x80\x9cAn agency may not unilaterally determine what constitutes the Administrative Record\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

To be sure, we also said in Overton Park (referring to the famous case of United States v. Morgan,\n\n313 U.\xc2\xa0S. 409,\n422 (1941)\n), that \xe2\x80\x9cinquiry into the mental processes of administrative decisionmakers is usually to be avoided\xe2\x80\x9d absent a showing of bad faith or improper conduct. 401 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 420. But we said that in the context of explaining the circumstances under which officials \xe2\x80\x9cwho participated in the decision\xe2\x80\x9d could be required \xe2\x80\x9cto give testimony explaining their action.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added); see also Morgan, supra, at 422 (discussing the testimony of the Secretary of Agriculture).

\n

Probing a decisionmaker\xe2\x80\x99s subjective mental reasoning\xe2\x80\x94what was at issue in Morgan and Overton Park\xe2\x80\x94is distinct from the ordinary judicial task of evaluating whether the decision itself was objectively valid, considering all of the materials before the decisionmaker at the time he made the decision. Overton Park, supra, at 420. And the testimony of the decisionmaker, at issue in Morgan and Overton Park, cannot be deemed properly part of the administrative record in any event, because it did not exist until after the agency decision had been made. See Florida Power & Light Co. v. Lorion,\n\n470 U.\xc2\xa0S. 729,\n743 (1985)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[T]he focal point for judicial review should be the administrative record already in existence, not some new record made initially in the reviewing court\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Camp v. Pitts,\n\n411 U.\xc2\xa0S. 138,\n142 (1973)\n (per curiam)).

\n

The documents that the District Court ordered the Government to provide are documents that were considered by the decisionmaker or those advising her and that were \xe2\x80\x9calready in existence\xe2\x80\x9d at the time of the relevant agency decision. At least facially, these documents do not seem to involve \xe2\x80\x9cinquiry into the mental processes\xe2\x80\x9d of the decisionmaker at all. They thus do not implicate the bad faith or improper conduct standard from Overton Park.

\n

In taking the position that the agency unilaterally decides which documents make up the administrative record, the Government relies heavily on the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision in San Luis Obispo Mothers for Peace v. NRC, 789 F.\xc2\xa02d 26, 44\xe2\x80\x9345 (CADC 1986) (en banc). San Luis Obispo expanded Morgan and Overton Park\xe2\x80\x99s bad faith or improper conduct standard to apply to one narrow category of pre-existing, documentary materials: records of the closed deliberations of the members of a multimember agency. See San Luis Obispo, 789 F. 2d, at 44\xe2\x80\x9345. Such records, the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit has explained, are functionally equivalent to deposing an agency head to explain her decision\xe2\x80\x94they represent the \xe2\x80\x9ccollective mental processes of the agency.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 44. This Court has never passed on this extension of Morgan and Overton Park. But in any event, it does not help the Government here because DHS, which made the decision to terminate DACA, is not a multimember agency, and so the records the District Court ordered produced are not protected by San Luis Obispo either.

\n

The Government also relies on our decision in Cheney,\n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 367\n. But Cheney concerned only requests for documents from the White House itself. Such documents seem to form at most only a small portion of the documents that the District Court ordered the Government to add to the administrative record, so Cheney cannot justify the broad relief from any obligation to complete the administrative record that the Government seeks. And, moreover, the Government has failed to argue with any specificity about the burden that the requirement to include White House documents in particular in the administrative record will impose, so it is impossible for this Court to perform the sort of balancing analysis that we said was necessary in Cheney. Id., at 385.

\n

Finally, the Government relies on dictum from Florida Power & Light, 470 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 742\xe2\x80\x93744, that judicial review is to be conducted \xe2\x80\x9cbased on the record the agency presents to the reviewing court.\xe2\x80\x9d But the Government takes that language out of context. The quoted language comes as part of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s explanation for why there is often no need for independent district court factfinding as part of APA review: namely, because that review will be based on \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the administrative record already in existence, not some new record made initially in the reviewing court.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 743 (quoting Camp, supra, at 142). Thus, what we meant by the phrase the Government quotes was that review is to be \xe2\x80\x9cbased on the record the agency proceedings present to the reviewing court,\xe2\x80\x9d 470 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 744\xe2\x80\x94just what we said in Overton Park. No more was at issue in Florida Power & Light. That case did not hold that the Government gets to define unilaterally the scope of the documents it submits to the reviewing court as the administrative record.

\n

In sum, the cases, both in this Court and in lower courts, hold or are consistent with the proposition that a reviewing court has the power to order the Government to supplement documents already provided with other documents where necessary to give the court \xe2\x80\x9cthe full administrative record.\xe2\x80\x9d Overton Park, 401 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 420.

\n

II

\n

The Government also raises various other concerns, but they similarly fail to show the Government\xe2\x80\x99s entitlement to extraordinary mandamus relief.

\n

The Government asserts that some of the documents the District Court ordered be included in the administrative record are protected by various privileges. But the Government has not developed a specific claim of privilege as to any particular document to us here or to any court below. See Application for Stay 24; see also ___ F.\xc2\xa03d, at ___, n.\xc2\xa08 (noting that the Government \xe2\x80\x9cprovided little in the way of argument regarding the specific documents ordered disclosed by the district court\xe2\x80\x9d). The closest the Government comes is with repeated references to a single document out of the 35 that the District Court found not privileged and ordered it to produce. The Government describes it as a memorandum from the White House Counsel\xe2\x80\x99s office to the President. But even as to this one document, the Government offers no legal or factual support for its claim of privilege aside from its bare description. The District Court reviewed that document, and the other 34, and concluded that they were not privileged. We have not seen these documents, and we consequently have no basis to question the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion.

\n

As for any additional documents that the Government may believe are privileged, the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order leaves the Government free to withhold privileged documents from the administrative record. The Government simply has to explain the basis for its privilege claim and provide the documents in camera for the District Court to review. Given that the District Court concluded that of the first 84 privileged documents the Government attempted to withhold from the administrative record, 35 were not in fact privileged, the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that the Government justify its future privilege claims and file the documents for review in camera seems to be a reasonable exercise of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s considerable discretion in this area.

\n

The Government also complains about the burden imposed by the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order, but that argument is also beside the point. The Government complains that it must review 21,000 documents as potentially part of the administrative record. But the underlying agency action here is important, and that is by no means an unusually large number of documents; administrative records often contain hundreds of thousands of documents. See, e.g., Georgia ex. rel. Olens v. McCarthy, 833 F.\xc2\xa03d 1317, 1320 (CA11 2016) (noting that the administrative record \xe2\x80\x9cis more than a million pages long\xe2\x80\x9d). And, moreover, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s argument about burden is based almost entirely on how quickly it must comply with the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order. See Application for Stay 28\xe2\x80\x9331. But the current December 22 deadline was set by the District Court on November 20, 2017\xe2\x80\x94three days after the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 decision. The Government is free to request an additional extension of time from the District Court or to seek mandamus relief from the deadline in the Court of Appeals. The Government has done neither. And so the Government\xe2\x80\x99s challenge to that deadline, and thus to what it says is the unreasonable burden to review thousands of documents in such a short period of time, seems to be barred by this Court\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 23.3, because it fails to explain \xe2\x80\x9cwith particularity why the relief sought is not available in any other court.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

The same is true of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s objections to the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order that discovery of documents and information outside the administrative record will begin on December 22. The Government has not challenged any particular discovery order as overbroad in the District Court, much less in the Court of Appeals. The Government\xe2\x80\x99s objections are thus premature. Concerning depositions, for example, the most the Government can say is that \xe2\x80\x9cthe district court will likely allow\xe2\x80\x9d depositions of \xe2\x80\x9cnumerous witnesses.\xe2\x80\x9d Reply in Support of Application for Stay 13\xe2\x80\x9314 (emphasis added). Perhaps the District Court will allow those depositions and perhaps it will not. But I do not see how we can restrain by mandamus an order that the Government merely fears that the District Court might enter in the future.

\n

III

\n

The Government also argues that a stay of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s orders is appropriate because judicial review of the agency decision at issue is precluded by the APA as \xe2\x80\x9ccommitted to agency discretion by law,\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7701(a)(2), and by the Immigration and Nationality Act,\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71252(g). But that argument goes to the merits of the respondents\xe2\x80\x99 underlying lawsuit, which have not yet been addressed by the District Court and are not now before this Court, rather than to the proper contents of the administrative record assuming that the agency decision is subject to review. The District Court on September 21 offered the Government the opportunity to file an early motion to dismiss and thus obtain a decision on its threshold objections before the preparation of the administrative record. The Government rejected that offer, preferring instead to defer the issue to summary judgment motions. I see no reason to grant a writ of mandamus to relieve the Government of the consequences of that decision.

\n

IV

\n

Except in the most extraordinary circumstances, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s long-settled practice has been to leave these sorts of burden and discovery-related procedural disputes to the district courts, with occasional court of appeals intervention. We follow this practice for good reason. To understand whether a particular discovery order is overly burdensome typically requires a deep understanding of the overall factual context and procedural history of an individual case. This Court is thus poorly positioned to second-guess district courts\xe2\x80\x99 determinations in this area.

\n

The Court today abandons its practice of nonintervention in this kind of discovery-related dispute. In addition to disrupting the progress of this litigation, I fear that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to intervene here means we will be asked to address run-of-the-mill discovery disputes in many other matters, certainly when the Government is involved and potentially when it is not involved. In my view, the Court should maintain its usual policy of abstaining from disputes like this one.

\n

For these reasons, with respect, I dissent from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s grant of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s application for a stay pending further consideration of its petition for a writ of mandamus.
