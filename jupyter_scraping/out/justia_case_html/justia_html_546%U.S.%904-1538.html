ANTHONY KANE, WARDEN v. JOE GARCIA ESPITIA

\n

on petition for writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 04\xe2\x80\x931538.\xe2\x80\x83Decided October 31, 2005

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Per Curiam.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent Garcia Espitia, a criminal defendant who chose to proceed pro se, was convicted in California state court of carjacking and other offenses. He had received no law library access while in jail before trial\xe2\x80\x94despite his repeated requests and court orders to the contrary\xe2\x80\x94and only about four hours of access during trial, just before closing arguments. (Of course, he had declined, as was his right, to be represented by a lawyer with unlimited access to legal materials.) The California courts rejected his argument that his restricted library access violated his Sixth Amendment rights. Once his sentence became final, he petitioned in Federal District Court for a writ of habeas corpus under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254. The District Court denied relief, but the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit reversed, holding that \xe2\x80\x9cthe lack of any pretrial access to lawbooks violated Espitia\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional right to represent himself as established by the Supreme Court in Faretta [v. California, 422 U. S. 806 (1975)].\xe2\x80\x9d Garcia Espitia v. Ortiz, 113 Fed. Appx. 802, 804 (2004). The warden\xe2\x80\x99s petition for certiorari and respondent\xe2\x80\x99s motion for leave to proceed in forma pauperis are granted, the judgment below reversed, and the case remanded.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A necessary condition for federal habeas relief here is that the state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision be \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary to, or involv[e] an unreasonable application of, clearly established Federal law, as determined by the Supreme Court of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1). Neither the opinion below, nor any of the appellate cases it relies on, identifies a source in our case law for the law library access right other than Faretta. See 113 Fed. Appx., at 804 (relying on Bribiesca v. Galaza, 215 F.\xc2\xa03d 1015, 1020 (CA9 2000) (quoting Milton v. Morris, 767 F.\xc2\xa02d 1443, 1446 (CA9 1985)); ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9cFaretta controls this case\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The federal appellate courts have split on whether Faretta, which establishes a Sixth Amendment right to self-representation, implies a right of the pro se defendant to have access to a law library. Compare Milton, supra, with United States v. Smith, 907 F.\xc2\xa02d 42, 45 (CA6 1990) (\xe2\x80\x9c[B]y knowingly and intelligently waiving his right to counsel, the appellant also relinquished his access to a law library\xe2\x80\x9d); United States ex rel. George v. Lane, 718 F.\xc2\xa02d 226, 231 (CA7 1983) (similar). That question cannot be resolved here, however, as it is clear that Faretta does not, as \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) requires, \xe2\x80\x9cclearly establis[h]\xe2\x80\x9d the law library access right. In fact, Faretta says nothing about any specific legal aid that the State owes a pro se criminal defendant. The Bribiesca court and the court below therefore erred in holding, based on Faretta, that a violation of a law library access right is a basis for federal habeas relief.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment below is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.
