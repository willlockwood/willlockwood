SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

\n

MARISA N. PAVAN, et\xc2\xa0al. v. NATHANIEL SMITH

\n

on petition for writ of certiorari to the supreme court of arkansas

\n

No. 16\xe2\x80\x93992.\xe2\x80\x83Decided June 26, 2017

\n

\n

Per Curiam.

\n

As this Court explained in Obergefell v. Hodges, 576 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015), the Constitution entitles same-sex couples to civil marriage \xe2\x80\x9con the same terms and conditions as opposite-sex couples.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 23). In the decision below, the Arkansas Supreme Court considered the effect of that holding on the State\xe2\x80\x99s rules governing the issuance of birth certificates. When a married woman gives birth in Arkansas, state law generally requires the name of the mother\xe2\x80\x99s male spouse to appear on the child\xe2\x80\x99s birth certificate\xe2\x80\x94regardless of his biological relationship to the child. According to the court below, however, Arkansas need not extend that rule to similarly situated same-sex couples: The State need not, in other words, issue birth certificates including the female spouses of women who give birth in the State. Because that differential treatment infringes Obergefell\xe2\x80\x99s commitment to provide same-sex couples \xe2\x80\x9cthe constellation of benefits that the States have linked to marriage,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at ___ (slip op., at 17), we reverse the state court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment.

\n

The petitioners here are two married same-sex couples who conceived children through anonymous sperm donation. Leigh and Jana Jacobs were married in Iowa in 2010, and Terrah and Marisa Pavan were married in New Hampshire in 2011. Leigh and Terrah each gave birth to a child in Arkansas in 2015. When it came time to secure birth certificates for the newborns, each couple filled out paperwork listing both spouses as parents\xe2\x80\x94Leigh and Jana in one case, Terrah and Marisa in the other. Both times, however, the Arkansas Department of Health issued certificates bearing only the birth mother\xe2\x80\x99s name.

\n

The department\xe2\x80\x99s decision rested on a provision of Arkansas law, Ark. Code \xc2\xa720\xe2\x80\x9318\xe2\x80\x93401 (2014), that specifies which individuals will appear as parents on a child\xe2\x80\x99s state-issued birth certificate. \xe2\x80\x9cFor the purposes of birth registration,\xe2\x80\x9d that statute says, \xe2\x80\x9cthe mother is deemed to be the woman who gives birth to the child.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa720\xe2\x80\x9318\xe2\x80\x93401(e). And \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f the mother was married at the time of either conception or birth,\xe2\x80\x9d the statute instructs that \xe2\x80\x9cthe name of [her] husband shall be entered on the certificate as the father of the child.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa720\xe2\x80\x9318\xe2\x80\x93401(f)(1). There are some limited exceptions to the latter rule\xe2\x80\x94for example, another man may appear on the birth certificate if the \xe2\x80\x9cmother\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9chusband\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cputative father\xe2\x80\x9d all file affidavits vouching for the putative father\xe2\x80\x99s paternity. Ibid. But as all parties agree, the requirement that a married woman\xe2\x80\x99s husband appear on her child\xe2\x80\x99s birth certificate applies in cases where the couple conceived by means of artificial insemination with the help of an anonymous sperm donor. See Pet. for Cert. 4; Brief in Opposition 3\xe2\x80\x934; see also Ark.Code \xc2\xa79\xe2\x80\x9310\xe2\x80\x93201(a) (2015) (\xe2\x80\x9cAny child born to a married woman by means of artificial insemination shall be deemed the legitimate natural child of the woman and the woman\xe2\x80\x99s husband if the husband consents in writing to the artificial insemination\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

The Jacobses and Pavans brought this suit in Arkansas state court against the director of the Arkansas Department of Health\xe2\x80\x94seeking, among other things, a declaration that the State\xe2\x80\x99s birth-certificate law violates the Constitution. The trial court agreed, holding that the relevant portions of \xc2\xa720\xe2\x80\x9318\xe2\x80\x93401 are inconsistent with Obergefell because they \xe2\x80\x9ccategorically prohibi[t] every same-sex married couple .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. from enjoying the same spousal benefits which are available to every opposite-sex married couple.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 59a. But a divided Arkansas Supreme Court reversed that judgment, concluding that the statute \xe2\x80\x9cpass[es] constitutional muster.\xe2\x80\x9d 2016 Ark. 437, 505 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d 169, 177. In that court\xe2\x80\x99s view, \xe2\x80\x9cthe statute centers on the relationship of the biological mother and the biological father to the child, not on the marital relationship of husband and wife,\xe2\x80\x9d and so it \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not run afoul of Obergefell.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 178. Two justices dissented from that view, maintaining that under Obergefell \xe2\x80\x9ca same-sex married couple is entitled to a birth certificate on the same basis as an opposite-sex marriedcouple.\xe2\x80\x9d 505 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 184 (Brill, C.\xc2\xa0J., concurring in part and dissenting in part); accord, id., at 190 (Danielson, J., dissenting).

\n

The Arkansas Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision, we conclude, denied married same-sex couples access to the \xe2\x80\x9cconstellation of benefits that the Stat[e] ha[s] linked to marriage.\xe2\x80\x9d Obergefell, 576 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 17). As already explained, when a married woman in Arkansas conceives a child by means of artificial insemination, the State will\xe2\x80\x94indeed, must\xe2\x80\x94list the name of her male spouse on the child\xe2\x80\x99s birth certificate. See \xc2\xa720\xe2\x80\x9318\xe2\x80\x93401(f\xc2\xa0)(1); see also \xc2\xa79\xe2\x80\x9310\xe2\x80\x93201; supra, at 2. And yet state law, as interpreted by the court below, allows Arkansas officials in those very same circumstances to omit a married woman\xe2\x80\x99s female spouse from her child\xe2\x80\x99s birth certificate. See 505 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 177\xe2\x80\x93178. As a result, same-sex parents in Arkansas lack the same right as opposite-sex parents to be listed on a child\xe2\x80\x99s birth certificate, a document often used for important transactions like making medical decisions for a child or enrolling a child in school. See Pet. for Cert. 5\xe2\x80\x937 (listing situations in which a parent might be required to present a child\xe2\x80\x99s birth certificate).

\n

Obergefell proscribes such disparate treatment. As we explained there, a State may not \xe2\x80\x9cexclude same-sexcouples from civil marriage on the same terms and conditions as opposite-sex couples.\xe2\x80\x9d 576 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 23). Indeed, in listing those terms and conditions\xe2\x80\x94the \xe2\x80\x9crights, benefits, and responsibilities\xe2\x80\x9d to which same-sex couples, no less than opposite-sex couples, must have access\xe2\x80\x94we expressly identified \xe2\x80\x9cbirth and death certificates.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 17). That was no accident: Several of the plaintiffs in Obergefell challenged a State\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to recognize their same-sex spouses on their children\xe2\x80\x99s birth certificates. See DeBoer v. Snyder, 772 F.\xc2\xa03d 388, 398\xe2\x80\x93399 (CA6 2014). In considering those challenges, we held the relevant state laws unconstitutional to the extent they treated same-sex couples differently from opposite-sex couples. See 576 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 23). That holding applies with equal force to \xc2\xa720\xe2\x80\x9318\xe2\x80\x93401.

\n

Echoing the court below, the State defends its birth-certificate law on the ground that being named on a child\xe2\x80\x99s birth certificate is not a benefit that attends marriage. Instead, the State insists, a birth certificate is simply a device for recording biological parentage\xe2\x80\x94regardless of whether the child\xe2\x80\x99s parents are married. But Arkansas law makes birth certificates about more than just genetics. As already discussed, when an opposite-sex couple conceives a child by way of anonymous sperm donation\xe2\x80\x94just as the petitioners did here\xe2\x80\x94state law requires the placement of the birth mother\xe2\x80\x99s husband on the child\xe2\x80\x99s birth certificate. See supra, at 2. And that is so even though (as the State concedes) the husband \xe2\x80\x9cis definitively not the biological father\xe2\x80\x9d in those circumstances. Brief in Opposition 4.[1]* Arkansas has thus chosen to make its birth certificates more than a mere marker of biological relationships: The State uses those certificates to give married parents a form of legal recognition that is not available to unmarried parents. Having made that choice, Arkansas may not, consistent with Obergefell, deny married same-sex couples that recognition.

\n

The petition for a writ of certiorari and the pending motions for leave to file briefs as amici curiae are granted. The judgment of the Arkansas Supreme Court is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

on petition for writ of certiorari to the supreme court of arkansas

\n

No. 16\xe2\x80\x93992.\xe2\x80\x83Decided June 26, 2017

\n

Justice Gorsuch, with whom Justice Thomas and Justice Alito join, dissenting.

\n

Summary reversal is usually reserved for cases where \xe2\x80\x9cthe law is settled and stable, the facts are not in dispute, and the decision below is clearly in error.\xe2\x80\x9d Schweiker v. Hansen,\n\n450 U.\xc2\xa0S. 785,\n791 (1981)\n (Marshall, J., dissenting). Respectfully, I don\xe2\x80\x99t believe this case meets that standard.

\n

To be sure, Obergefell addressed the question whether a State must recognize same-sex marriages. But nothing in Obergefell spoke (let alone clearly) to the question whether \xc2\xa720\xe2\x80\x9318\xe2\x80\x93401 of the Arkansas Code, or a state supreme court decision upholding it, must go. The statute in question establishes a set of rules designed to ensure that the biological parents of a child are listed on the child\xe2\x80\x99s birth certificate. Before the state supreme court, the State argued that rational reasons exist for a biology based birth registration regime, reasons that in no way offend Obergefell\xe2\x80\x94like ensuring government officials can identify public health trends and helping individuals determine their biological lineage, citizenship, or susceptibility to genetic disorders. In an opinion that did not in any way seek to defy but rather earnestly engage Obergefell, the state supreme court agreed. And it is very hard to see what is wrong with this conclusion for, just as the state court recognized, nothing in Obergefell indicates that a birth registration regime based on biology, one no doubt with many analogues across the country and throughout his-tory, offends the Constitution. To the contrary, to the extent they speak to the question at all, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents suggest just the opposite conclusion. See, e.g., Michael H. v. Gerald D.,\n\n491 U.\xc2\xa0S. 110\n\xe2\x80\x93125 (1989); Tuan Anh Nguyen v. INS,\n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 53,\n73 (2001)\n. Neither does anything in today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion purport to identify any constitutional problem with a biology based birth registration regime. So whatever else we might do with this case, summary reversal would not exactly seem the obvious course.

\n

What, then, is at work here? If there isn\xe2\x80\x99t a problem with a biology based birth registration regime, perhaps the concern lies in this particular regime\xe2\x80\x99s exceptions. For it turns out that Arkansas\xe2\x80\x99s general rule of registration based on biology does admit of certain more specific exceptions. Most importantly for our purposes, the State acknowledges that \xc2\xa79\xe2\x80\x9310\xe2\x80\x93201 of the Arkansas Code controls how birth certificates are completed in cases of artificial insemination like the one before us. The State acknowledges, too, that this provision, written some time ago, indicates that the mother\xe2\x80\x99s husband generally shall be treated as the father\xe2\x80\x94and in this way seemingly anticipates only opposite-sex marital unions.

\n

But if the artificial insemination statute is the concern, it\xe2\x80\x99s still hard to see how summary reversal should follow for at least a few reasons. First, petitioners didn\xe2\x80\x99t actually challenge \xc2\xa79\xe2\x80\x9310\xe2\x80\x93201 in their lawsuit. Instead, petitioners sought and the trial court granted relief eliminating the State\xe2\x80\x99s authority under \xc2\xa720\xe2\x80\x9318\xe2\x80\x93401 to enforce a birth registration regime generally based on biology. On appeal, the state supreme court simply held that this overbroad remedy wasn\xe2\x80\x99t commanded by Obergefell or the Constitution. And, again, nothing in today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion for the Court identifies anything wrong, let alone clearly wrong, in that conclusion. Second, though petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 lawsuit didn\xe2\x80\x99t challenge \xc2\xa79\xe2\x80\x9310\xe2\x80\x93201, the State has repeatedly conceded that the benefits afforded nonbiological parents under \xc2\xa79\xe2\x80\x9310\xe2\x80\x93201 must be afforded equally to both same-sex and opposite-sex couples. So that in this particular case and all others of its kind, the State agrees, the female spouse of the birth mother must be listed on birth certificates too. Third, further proof still of the state of the law in Arkansas today is the fact that, when it comes to adoption (a situation not present in this case but another one in which Arkansas departs from biology based registration), the State tells us that adopting parents are eligible forplacement on birth certificates without respect to sexual orientation.

\n

Given all this, it seems far from clear what here warrants the strong medicine of summary reversal. Indeed, it is not even clear what the Court expects to happen on remand that hasn\xe2\x80\x99t happened already. The Court does not offer any remedial suggestion, and none leaps to mind. Perhaps the state supreme court could memorialize the State\xe2\x80\x99s concession on \xc2\xa79\xe2\x80\x9310\xe2\x80\x93201, even though that law wasn\xe2\x80\x99t fairly challenged and such a chore is hardly the usual reward for seeking faithfully to apply, not evade, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s mandates.

\n

I respectfully dissent.
