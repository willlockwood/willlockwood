COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES, CALIFORNIA, et\xc2\xa0al. v. MENDEZ et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 16\xe2\x80\x93369.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 22, 2017\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 30, 2017

\n

The Los Angeles County Sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s Department received word from a confidential informant that a potentially armed and dangerous parolee-at-large had been seen at a certain residence. While other officers searched the main house, Deputies Conley and Pederson searched the back of the property where, unbeknownst to the deputies, respondents Mendez and Garcia were napping inside a shack where they lived. Without a search warrant and without announcing their presence, the deputies opened the door of the shack. Mendez rose from the bed, holding a BB gun that he used to kill pests. Deputy Conley yelled, \xe2\x80\x9cGun!\xe2\x80\x9d and the deputies immediately opened fire, shooting Mendez and Garcia multiple times. Officers did not find the parolee in the shack or elsewhere on the property.

\n

Mendez and Garcia sued Deputies Conley and Pederson and the County under\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983, pressing three\n \nFourth Amendment claims: a warrantless entry claim, a knock-and-announce claim, and an excessive force claim. On the first two claims, the District Court awarded Mendez and Garcia nominal damages. On the excessive force claim, the court found that the deputies\xe2\x80\x99 use of force was reasonable under Graham v. Connor,\n\n490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 386\n, but held them liable nonetheless under the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s provocation rule, which makes an officer\xe2\x80\x99s otherwise reasonable use of force unreasonable if (1) the officer \xe2\x80\x9cintentionally or recklessly provokes a violent confrontation\xe2\x80\x9d and (2) \xe2\x80\x9cthe provocation is an independent\n \nFourth Amendment violation,\xe2\x80\x9d Billington v. Smith, 292 F.\xc2\xa03d 1177, 1189. On appeal, the Ninth Circuit held that the officers were entitled to qualified immunity on the knock-and-announce claim and that the warrantless entry violated clearly established law. It also affirmed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s application of the provocation rule, and held, in the alternative, that basic notions of proximate cause would support liability even without the provocation rule.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The\n \nFourth Amendment provides no basis for the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cprovocation rule.\xe2\x80\x9d Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0The provocation rule is incompatible with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s excessive force jurisprudence, which sets forth a settled and exclusive framework for analyzing whether the force used in making a seizure complies with the\n \nFourth Amendment. See Graham, supra, at 395. The operative question in such cases is \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the totality of the circumstances justifie[s] a particular sort of search or seizure.\xe2\x80\x9d Tennessee v. Garner,\n\n471 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n\xe2\x80\x939. When an officer carries out a seizure that is reasonable, taking into account all relevant circumstances, there is no valid excessive force claim. The provocation rule, however, instructs courts to look back in time to see if a different\n \nFourth Amendment violation was somehow tied to the eventual use of force, an approach that mistakenly conflates distinct\n \nFourth Amendment claims. The proper framework is set out in Graham. To the extent that a plaintiff has other\n \nFourth Amendment claims, they should be analyzed separately.

\n

The Ninth Circuit attempts to cabin the provocation rule by defining a two-prong test: First, the separate constitutional violation must \xe2\x80\x9ccreat[e] a situation which led to\xe2\x80\x9d the use of force; and second, the separate constitutional violation must be committed recklessly or intentionally. 815 F.\xc2\xa03d 1178, 1193. Neither limitation, however, solves the fundamental problem: namely, that the provocation rule is an unwarranted and illogical expansion of Graham. In addition, each limitation creates problems of its own. First, the rule relies on a vague causal standard. Second, while the reasonableness of a search or seizure is almost always based on objective factors, the provocation rule looks to the subjective intent of the officers who carried out the seizure.

\n

There is no need to distort the excessive force inquiry in this way in order to hold law enforcement officers liable for the foreseeable consequences of all their constitutional torts. Plaintiffs can, subject to qualified immunity, generally recover damages that are proximately caused by any\n \nFourth Amendment violation. See, e.g., Heck v. Humphrey,\n\n512 U.\xc2\xa0S. 477\n. Here, if respondents cannot recover on their excessive force claim, that will not foreclose recovery for injuries proximately caused by the warrantless entry. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s proximate-cause holding is similarly tainted. Its analysis appears to focus solely on the risks foreseeably associated with the failure to knock and announce\xe2\x80\x94the claim on which the court concluded that the deputies had qualified immunity\xe2\x80\x94rather than the warrantless entry. On remand, the court should revisit the question whether proximate cause permits respondents to recover damages for their injuries based on the deputies\xe2\x80\x99 failure to secure a warrant at the outset. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

815 F.\xc2\xa03d 1178, vacated and remanded.

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES, CALIFORNIA, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. ANGEL MENDEZ, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[May 30, 2017]

\n

Justice Alito delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

If law enforcement officers make a \xe2\x80\x9cseizure\xe2\x80\x9d of a person using force that is judged to be reasonable based on a consideration of the circumstances relevant to that determination, may the officers nevertheless be held liable for injuries caused by the seizure on the ground that they committed a separate\n \nFourth Amendment violation that contributed to their need to use force? The Ninth Circuit has adopted a \xe2\x80\x9cprovocation rule\xe2\x80\x9d that imposes liability in such a situation.

\n

We hold that the\n \nFourth Amendment provides no basis for such a rule. A different\n \nFourth Amendment violation cannot transform a later, reasonable use of force into an unreasonable seizure.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

In October 2010, deputies from the Los Angeles County Sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s Department were searching for a parolee-at-large named Ronnie O\xe2\x80\x99Dell. A felony arrest warrant had been issued for O\xe2\x80\x99Dell, who was believed to be armed and dangerous and had previously evaded capture. Findings of Fact and Conclusions of Law, No. 2:11\xe2\x80\x93cv\xe2\x80\x9304771 (CD Cal.), App. to Pet. for Cert. 56a, 64a. Deputies Christopher Conley and Jennifer Pederson were assigned to assist the task force searching for O\xe2\x80\x99Dell. Id., at 57a\xe2\x80\x9358a. The task force received word from a confidential informant that O\xe2\x80\x99Dell had been seen on a bicycle at a home in Lancaster, California, owned by Paula Hughes, and the officers then mapped out a plan for apprehending O\xe2\x80\x99Dell. Id., at 58a. Some officers would approach the front door of the Hughes residence, while Deputies Conley and Pederson would search the rear of the property and cover the back door of the residence. Id., at 59a. During this briefing, it was announced that a man named Angel Mendez lived in the backyard of the Hughes home with a pregnant woman named Jennifer Garcia (now Mrs. Jennifer Mendez). Ibid. Deputy Pederson heard this announcement, but at trial Deputy Conley testified that he did not remember it. Ibid.

\n

When the officers reached the Hughes residence around midday, three of them knocked on the front door while Deputies Conley and Pederson went to the back of the property. Id., at 63a. At the front door, Hughes asked if the officers had a warrant. Ibid. A sergeant responded that they did not but were searching for O\xe2\x80\x99Dell and had a warrant for his arrest. Ibid. One of the officers heard what he thought were sounds of someone running inside the house. Id., at 64a. As the officers prepared to open the door by force, Hughes opened the door and informed them that O\xe2\x80\x99Dell was not in the house. Ibid. She was placed under arrest, and the house was searched, but O\xe2\x80\x99Dell was not found. Ibid.

\n

Meanwhile, Deputies Conley and Pederson, with guns drawn, searched the rear of the residence, which was cluttered with debris and abandoned automobiles. Id., at 60a, 65a. The property included three metal storage sheds and a one-room shack made of wood and plywood. Id., at 60a. Mendez had built the shack, and he and Garcia had lived inside for about 10 months. Id., at 61a. The shack had a single doorway covered by a blue blanket. Ibid. Amid the debris on the ground, an electrical cord ran into the shack, and an air conditioner was mounted on the side. Id., at 62a. A gym storage locker and clothes and other possessions were nearby. Id., at 61a. Mendez kept a BB rifle in the shack for use on rats and other pests. Id., at 62a. The BB gun \xe2\x80\x9cclosely resembled a small caliber rifle.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

Deputies Conley and Pederson first checked the three metal sheds and found no one inside. Id., at 65a. They then approached the door of the shack. Id., at 66a. Unbeknownst to the officers, Mendez and Garcia were in the shack and were napping on a futon. Id., at 67a. The deputies did not have a search warrant and did not knock and announce their presence. Id., at 66a. When Deputy Conley opened the wooden door and pulled back the blanket, Mendez thought it was Ms. Hughes and rose from the bed, picking up the BB gun so he could stand up and place it on the floor. Id., at 68a. As a result, when the deputies entered, he was holding the BB gun, and it was \xe2\x80\x9cpoint[ing] somewhat south towards Deputy Conley.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 69a. Deputy Conley yelled, \xe2\x80\x9cGun!\xe2\x80\x9d and the deputies immediately opened fire, discharging a total of 15 rounds. Id., at 69a\xe2\x80\x9370a. Mendez and Garcia \xe2\x80\x9cwere shot multiple times and suffered severe injuries,\xe2\x80\x9d and Mendez\xe2\x80\x99s right leg was later amputated below the knee. Id., at 70a. O\xe2\x80\x99Dell was not in the shack or anywhere on the property. Ibid.

\n

B

\n

Mendez and his wife (respondents here) filed suit under Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa71976,\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983, against petitioners, the County of Los Angeles and Deputies Conley and Pederson. As relevant here, they pressed three\n \nFourth Amendment claims. First, they claimed that the deputies executed an unreasonable search by entering the shack without a warrant (the \xe2\x80\x9cwarrantless entry claim\xe2\x80\x9d); second, they asserted that the deputies performed an unreason-able search because they failed to announce their presence before entering the shack (the \xe2\x80\x9cknock-and-announce claim\xe2\x80\x9d); and third, they claimed that the deputies effected an unreasonable seizure by deploying excessive force in opening fire after entering the shack (the \xe2\x80\x9cexcessive force claim\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

After a bench trial, the District Court ruled largely in favor of respondents. App. to Pet. for Cert. 135a\xe2\x80\x93136a. The court found Deputy Conley liable on the warrantless entry claim, and the court also found both deputies liable on the knock-and-announce claim. But the court awarded nominal damages for these violations because \xe2\x80\x9cthe act of pointing the BB gun\xe2\x80\x9d was a superseding cause \xe2\x80\x9cas far as damage [from the shooting was] concerned.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 238.

\n

The District Court then addressed respondents\xe2\x80\x99 excessive force claim. App. to Pet. for Cert. 105a\xe2\x80\x93127a. The court began by evaluating whether the deputies used excessive force under Graham v. Connor,\n\n490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 386 (1989)\n. The court held that, under Graham, the deputies\xe2\x80\x99 use of force was reasonable \xe2\x80\x9cgiven their belief that a man was holding a firearm rifle threatening their lives.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 108a. But the court did not end its excessive force analysis at this point. Instead, the court turned to the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s provocation rule, which holds that \xe2\x80\x9can officer\xe2\x80\x99s otherwise reasonable (and lawful) defensive use of force is unreasonable as a matter of law, if (1) the officer intentionally or recklessly provoked a violent response, and (2) that provocation is an independent constitutional violation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 111a. Based on this rule, the District Court held the deputies liable for excessive force and awarded respondents around $4 million in damages. Id., at 135a\xe2\x80\x93136a.

\n

The Court of Appeals affirmed in part and reversed in part. 815 F.\xc2\xa03d 1178 (CA9 2016). Contrary to the District Court, the Court of Appeals held that the officers were entitled to qualified immunity on the knock-and-announce claim. Id., at 1191\xe2\x80\x931193. But the court concluded that the warrantless entry of the shack violated clearly established law and was attributable to both deputies. Id., at 1191, 1195. Finally, and most important for present purposes, the court affirmed the application of the provocation rule. The Court of Appeals did not disagree with the conclusion that the shooting was reasonable under Graham; instead, like the District Court, the Court of Appeals applied the provocation rule and held the deputies liable for the use of force on the theory that they had intentionally and recklessly brought about the shooting by entering the shack without a warrant in violation of clearly established law. 815 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1193.

\n

The Court of Appeals also adopted an alternative rationale for its judgment. It held that \xe2\x80\x9cbasic notions of proximate cause\xe2\x80\x9d would support liability even without the provocation rule because it was \xe2\x80\x9creasonably foreseeable\xe2\x80\x9d that the officers would meet an armed homeowner when they \xe2\x80\x9cbarged into the shack unannounced.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1194\xe2\x80\x931195.

\n

We granted certiorari. 580 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2016).

\n

II

\n

The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s provocation rule permits an excessive force claim under the\n \nFourth Amendment \xe2\x80\x9cwhere an officer intentionally or recklessly provokes a violent confrontation, if the provocation is an independent\n \nFourth Amendment violation.\xe2\x80\x9d Billington v. Smith, 292 F.\xc2\xa03d 1177, 1189 (CA9 2002). The rule comes into play after a forceful seizure has been judged to be reasonable under Graham. Once a court has made that determination, the rule instructs the court to ask whether the law enforcement officer violated the\n \nFourth Amendment in some other way in the course of events leading up to the seizure. If so, that separate\n \nFourth Amendment violation may \xe2\x80\x9crender the officer\xe2\x80\x99s otherwise reasonable defensive use of force unreasonable as a matter of law.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1190\xe2\x80\x931191.

\n

The provocation rule, which has been \xe2\x80\x9csharply questioned\xe2\x80\x9d outside the Ninth Circuit, City and County of San Francisco v. Sheehan, 575 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, n.\xc2\xa04 (2015) (slip op., at 14, n.\xc2\xa04), is incompatible with our excessive force jurisprudence. The rule\xe2\x80\x99s fundamental flaw is that it uses another constitutional violation to manufacture an excessive force claim where one would not otherwise exist.

\n

The\n \nFourth Amendment prohibits \xe2\x80\x9cunreasonable searches and seizures.\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c[R]easonableness is always the touchstone of\n \nFourth Amendment analysis,\xe2\x80\x9d Birchfield v. North Dakota, 579 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2016) (slip op., at 37), and reasonableness is generally assessed by carefully weighing \xe2\x80\x9cthe nature and quality of the intrusion on the individual\xe2\x80\x99s\n \nFourth Amendment interests against the importance of the governmental interests alleged to justify the intrusion.\xe2\x80\x9d Tennessee v. Garner,\n\n471 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1,\n8 (1985)\n (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

Our case law sets forth a settled and exclusive framework for analyzing whether the force used in making a seizure complies with the\n \nFourth Amendment. See Graham, 490 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 395. As in other areas of our\n \nFourth Amendment jurisprudence, \xe2\x80\x9c[d]etermining whether the force used to effect a particular seizure is \xe2\x80\x98reasonable\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d requires balancing of the individual\xe2\x80\x99s\n \nFourth Amendment interests against the relevant government interests. Id., at 396. The operative question in excessive force cases is \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the totality of the circumstances justifie[s] a particular sort of search or seizure.\xe2\x80\x9d Garner, supra, at 8\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

The reasonableness of the use of force is evaluated under an \xe2\x80\x9cobjective\xe2\x80\x9d inquiry that pays \xe2\x80\x9ccareful attention to the facts and circumstances of each particular case.\xe2\x80\x9d Graham, supra, at 396. And \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he \xe2\x80\x98reasonableness\xe2\x80\x99 of a particular use of force must be judged from the perspective of a reasonable officer on the scene, rather than with the 20/20 vision of hindsight.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. \xe2\x80\x9cExcessive force claims .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. are evaluated for objective reasonableness based upon the information the officers had when the conduct occurred.\xe2\x80\x9d Saucier v. Katz,\n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 194,\n207 (2001)\n. That inquiry is dispositive: When an officer carries out a seizure that is reasonable, taking into account all relevant circumstances, there is no valid excessive force claim.

\n

The basic problem with the provocation rule is that it fails to stop there. Instead, the rule provides a novel and unsupported path to liability in cases in which the use of force was reasonable. Specifically, it instructs courts to look back in time to see if there was a different\n \nFourth Amendment violation that is somehow tied to the eventual use of force. That distinct violation, rather than the forceful seizure itself, may then serve as the foundation of the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s excessive force claim. Billington, supra, at 1190 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe basis of liability for the subsequent use of force is the initial constitutional violation .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. \xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

This approach mistakenly conflates distinct\n \nFourth Amendment claims. Contrary to this approach, the objective reasonableness analysis must be conducted separately for each search or seizure that is alleged to be unconstitutional. An excessive force claim is a claim that a law enforcement officer carried out an unreasonable seizure through a use of force that was not justified under the relevant circumstances. It is not a claim that an officer used reasonable force after committing a distinct\n \nFourth Amendment violation such as an unreasonable entry.

\n

By conflating excessive force claims with other\n \nFourth Amendment claims, the provocation rule permits excessive force claims that cannot succeed on their own terms. That is precisely how the rule operated in this case. The District Court found (and the Ninth Circuit did not dispute) that the use of force by the deputies was reasonable under Graham. However, respondents were still able to recover damages because the deputies committed a separate constitutional violation (the warrantless entry into the shack) that in some sense set the table for the use of force. That is wrong. The framework for analyzing excessive force claims is set out in Graham. If there is no excessive force claim under Graham, there is no excessive force claim at all. To the extent that a plaintiff has other\n \nFourth Amendment claims, they should be analyzed separately.[1]*

\n

The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s efforts to cabin the provocation rule only undermine it further. The Ninth Circuit appears to recognize that it would be going entirely too far to suggest that any \n \nFourth Amendment violation that is connected to a reasonable use of force should create a valid excessive force claim. See, e.g., Beier v. Lewiston, 354 F.\xc2\xa03d 1058, 1064 (CA9 2004) (\xe2\x80\x9cBecause the excessive force and false arrest factual inquiries are distinct, establishing a lack of probable cause to make an arrest does not establish an excessive force claim, and vice-versa\xe2\x80\x9d). Instead, that court has endeavored to limit the rule to only those distinct\n \nFourth Amendment violations that in some sense \xe2\x80\x9cprovoked\xe2\x80\x9d the need to use force. The concept of provocation, in turn, has been defined using a two-prong test. First, the separate constitutional violation must \xe2\x80\x9ccreat[e] a situation which led to\xe2\x80\x9d the use of force; second, the separate constitutional violation must be committed recklessly or intentionally. 815 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1193 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

Neither of these limitations solves the fundamental problem of the provocation rule: namely, that it is an unwarranted and illogical expansion of Graham. But in addition, each of the limitations creates problems of its own. First, the rule includes a vague causal standard. It applies when a prior constitutional violation \xe2\x80\x9ccreated a situation which led to\xe2\x80\x9d the use of force. The rule does not incorporate the familiar proximate cause standard. Indeed, it is not clear what causal standard is being applied. Second, while the reasonableness of a search or seizure is almost always based on objective factors, see Whren v. United States,\n\n517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 806,\n814 (1996)\n, the provocation rule looks to the subjective intent of the officers who carried out the seizure. As noted, under the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s rule, a prior\n \nFourth Amendment violation may be held to have provoked a later, reasonable use of force only if the prior violation was intentional or reckless.

\n

The provocation rule may be motivated by the notion that it is important to hold law enforcement officers liable for the foreseeable consequences of all of their constitutional torts. See Billington, 292 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1190 (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]f an officer\xe2\x80\x99s provocative actions are objectively unreasonable under the\n \nFourth Amendment, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. liability is established, and the question becomes .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. what harms the constitutional violation proximately caused\xe2\x80\x9d). However, there is no need to distort the excessive force inquiry in order to accomplish this objective. To the contrary, both parties accept the principle that plaintiffs can\xe2\x80\x94subject to qualified immunity\xe2\x80\x94generally recover damages that are proximately caused by any\n \nFourth Amendment violation. See, e.g., Heck v. Humphrey,\n\n512 U.\xc2\xa0S. 477,\n483 (1994)\n (\xc2\xa71983 \xe2\x80\x9ccreates a species of tort liability\xe2\x80\x9d informed by tort principles regarding \xe2\x80\x9cdamages and the prerequisites for their recovery\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)); Memphis Community School Dist. v. Stachura,\n\n477 U.\xc2\xa0S. 299,\n306 (1986)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hen \xc2\xa71983 plaintiffs seek damages for violations of constitutional rights, the level of damages is ordinarily determined according to principles derived from the common law of torts\xe2\x80\x9d). Thus, there is no need to dress up every\n \nFourth Amendment claim as an excessive force claim. For example, if the plaintiffs in this case cannot recover on their excessive force claim, that will not foreclose recovery for injuries proximately caused by the warrantless entry. The harm proximately caused by these two torts may overlap, but the two claims should not be confused.

\n

III

\n

The Court of Appeals also held that \xe2\x80\x9ceven without relying on [the] provocation theory, the deputies are liable for the shooting under basic notions of proximate cause.\xe2\x80\x9d 815 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1194. In other words, the court apparently concluded that the shooting was proximately caused by the deputies\xe2\x80\x99 warrantless entry of the shack. Proper analysis of this proximate cause question required consideration of the \xe2\x80\x9cforeseeability or the scope of the risk created by the predicate conduct,\xe2\x80\x9d and required the court to conclude that there was \xe2\x80\x9csome direct relation between the injury asserted and the injurious conduct alleged.\xe2\x80\x9d Paroline v. United States, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 7) (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

Unfortunately, the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 proximate cause analysis appears to have been tainted by the same errors that cause us to reject the provocation rule. The court reasoned that when officers make a \xe2\x80\x9cstartling entry\xe2\x80\x9d by \xe2\x80\x9cbarg[ing] into\xe2\x80\x9d a home \xe2\x80\x9cunannounced,\xe2\x80\x9d it is reasonably foreseeable that violence may result. 815 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1194\xe2\x80\x931195 (internal quotation marks omitted). But this appears to focus solely on the risks foreseeably associated with the failure to knock and announce, which could not serve as the basis for liability since the Court of Appeals concluded that the officers had qualified immunity on that claim. By contrast, the Court of Appeals did not identify the foreseeable risks associated with the relevant constitutional violation (the warrantless entry); nor did it explain how, on these facts, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 injuries were proximately caused by the warrantless entry. In other words, the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 proximate cause analysis, like the provocation rule, conflated distinct\n \nFourth Amendment claims and required only a murky causal link between the warrantless entry and the injuries attributed to it. On remand, the court should revisit the question whether proximate cause permits respondents to recover damages for their shooting injuries based on the deputies\xe2\x80\x99 failure to secure a warrant at the outset. See Bank of America Corp. v. Miami, ante, at 12 (declining to \xe2\x80\x9cdraw the precise boundaries of proximate cause\xe2\x80\x9d in the first instance). The arguments made on this point by the parties and by the United States as amicus provide a useful starting point for this inquiry. See Brief for Petitioners 42\xe2\x80\x9356; Brief for Respondents 20\xe2\x80\x9331, 51\xe2\x80\x9359; Reply Brief 17\xe2\x80\x9324; Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 26\xe2\x80\x9332.

\n

\n

For these reasons, the judgment of the Court of Appeals is vacated, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.
