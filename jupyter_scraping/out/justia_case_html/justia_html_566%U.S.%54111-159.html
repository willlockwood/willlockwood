\n\t\t\t\tASTRUE, COMMISSIONER OF SOCIAL SECURITY v. CAPATO, on behalf of B.\xc2\xa0N.\xc2\xa0C. et\xc2\xa0al.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the third circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 11\xe2\x80\x93159.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 19, 2012\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 21, 2012\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tEighteen months after her husband, Robert Capato, died of cancer, respondent Karen Capato gave birth to twins conceived through in vitro fertilization using her husband\xe2\x80\x99s frozen sperm. Karen applied for Social Security survivors benefits for the twins. The Social Security Administration (SSA) denied her application, and the District Court affirmed. In accord with the SSA\xe2\x80\x99s construction of the Social Security Act (Act), the court determined that the twins would qualify for benefits only if, as \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7416(h)(2)(A) specifies, they could inherit from the deceased wage earner under state intestacy law. The court then found that Robert was domiciled in Florida at his death, and that under Florida law, posthumously conceived children do not qualify for inheritance through intestate succession. The Third Circuit reversed. It concluded that, under \xc2\xa7416(e), which defines child to mean, inter alia, \xe2\x80\x9cthe child or legally adopted child of an [insured] individual,\xe2\x80\x9d the undisputed biological children of an insured and his widow qualify for survivors benefits without regard to state intestacy law. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0The SSA\xe2\x80\x99s reading is better attuned to the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text and its design to benefit primarily those supported by the deceased wage earner in his or her lifetime. Moreover, even if the SSA\xe2\x80\x99s longstanding interpretation is not the only reasonable one, it is at least a permissible construction entitled to deference under Chevron U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., \n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 837\n. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9316.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0Congress amended the Act in 1939 to provide that, as relevant here, \xe2\x80\x9c[e]very child (as defined in section 416(e) of this title)\xe2\x80\x9d of a deceased insured individual \xe2\x80\x9cshall be entitled to a child\xe2\x80\x99s insurance benefit.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7402(d). Section 416(e), in turn, defines \xe2\x80\x9cchild\xe2\x80\x9d to mean: \xe2\x80\x9c(1) the child or legally adopted child of an individual, (2) a stepchild [under certain circumstances], and (3) .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the grandchild or stepgrandchild of an individual or his spouse [under certain conditions].\xe2\x80\x9d Unlike \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7416(e)(2) and (3), \xc2\xa7416(e)(1) lacks any elaboration of the conditions under which a child qualifies for benefits. Section 416(h)(2)(A), however, further addresses the term \xe2\x80\x9cchild,\xe2\x80\x9d providing: \xe2\x80\x9cIn determining whether an applicant is the child or parent of [an] insured individual for purposes of this subchapter, the Commissioner of Social Security shall apply [the intestacy law of the insured individual\xe2\x80\x99s domiciliary State].\xe2\x80\x9d An applicant who does not meet \xc2\xa7416(h)(2)(A)\xe2\x80\x99s intestacy-law criterion may nonetheless qualify for benefits under other criteria set forth in \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7416(h)(2)(B) and (h)(3), but respondent does not claim eligibility under those other criteria. Regulations promulgated by the SSA closely track \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7416(h)(2) and (3) in defining \xe2\x80\x9c[w]ho is the insured\xe2\x80\x99s natural child,\xe2\x80\x9d 20 CFR \xc2\xa7404.355. As the SSA reads the statute, \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7416(h) governs the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cchild\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa7416(e)(1) and serves as a gateway through which all applicants for insurance benefits as a \xe2\x80\x9cchild\xe2\x80\x9d must pass. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x937.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0While the SSA regards \xc2\xa7416(h) as completing \xc2\xa7416(e)\xe2\x80\x99s sparse definition of \xe2\x80\x9cchild,\xe2\x80\x9d the Third Circuit held, and respondent contends, that \xc2\xa7416(e) alone governs whenever the claimant is a married couple\xe2\x80\x99s biological child. There are conspicuous flaws in the Third Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s and respondent\xe2\x80\x99s reading; the SSA offers the more persuasive interpretation. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9315.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(1)\xc2\xa0Nothing in \xc2\xa7416(e)\xe2\x80\x99s tautological definition suggests that Congress understood the word \xe2\x80\x9cchild\xe2\x80\x9d to refer only to the children of married parents. The dictionary definitions offered by respondent are not so confined. Moreover, elsewhere in the Act, Congress expressly limited the category of children covered to offspring of a marital union, see \xc2\xa7402(d)(3)(A), and contemporaneous statutes similarly distinguish child of a marriage from the unmodified term \xe2\x80\x9cchild.\xe2\x80\x9d Nor does \xc2\xa7416(e) indicate that Congress intended \xe2\x80\x9cbiological\xe2\x80\x9d parentage to be prerequisite to \xe2\x80\x9cchild\xe2\x80\x9d status. A biological parent is not always a child\xe2\x80\x99s parent under law. Furthermore, marriage does not necessarily make a child\xe2\x80\x99s parentage certain, nor does the absence of marriage necessarily make a child\xe2\x80\x99s parentage uncertain. Finally, it is far from obvious that respondent\xe2\x80\x99s proposed definition would cover her posthumously conceived twins, for under Florida law a marriage ends upon the death of a spouse. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9310.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(2)\xc2\xa0The SSA finds a key textual cue in \xc2\xa7416(h)(2)(A)\xe2\x80\x99s opening instruction: \xe2\x80\x9cIn determining whether an applicant is the child .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of [an] insured individual for purposes of this subchapter,\xe2\x80\x9d the Commissioner shall apply state intestacy law. Respondent notes the absence of any cross-reference in \xc2\xa7416(e) to \xc2\xa7416(h), but she overlooks that \xc2\xa7416(h) provides the crucial link: It requires reference to state intestacy law to determine child status not just for \xc2\xa7416(h) purposes, but \xe2\x80\x9cfor purposes of this subchapter,\xe2\x80\x9d which includes both \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7402(d) and 416(e). Having explicitly complemented \xc2\xa7416(e) by the definitional provisions contained in \xc2\xa7416(h), Congress had no need to place a redundant cross-reference in \xc2\xa7416(e).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Act commonly refers to state law on matters of family status, including an applicant\xe2\x80\x99s status as a wife, widow, husband, or widower. See, e.g., \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7416(b), (h)(1)(A). The Act also sets duration-of-relationship limitations, see Weinberger v. Salfi, \n\n422 U.\xc2\xa0S. 749\n\xe2\x80\x93782, and time limits qualify the statutes of several States that accord inheritance rights to posthumously conceived children. In contrast, no time constraint attends the Third Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s ruling in this case, under which the biological child of married parents is eligible for survivors benefits, no matter the length of time between the father\xe2\x80\x99s death and the child\xe2\x80\x99s conception and birth. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBecause a child who may take from a father\xe2\x80\x99s estate is more likely to \xe2\x80\x9cbe dependent during the parent\xe2\x80\x99s life and at his death,\xe2\x80\x9d Mathews v. Lucas, \n\n427 U.\xc2\xa0S. 495\n, reliance on state intestacy law to determine who is a \xe2\x80\x9cchild\xe2\x80\x9d serves the Act\xe2\x80\x99s driving objective, which is to \xe2\x80\x9cprovide .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. dependent members of [a wage earner\xe2\x80\x99s] family with protection against the hardship occasioned by [the] loss of [the insured\xe2\x80\x99s] earnings,\xe2\x80\x9d Califano v. Jobst, \n\n434 U.\xc2\xa0S. 47\n. Although the Act and regulations set different eligibility requirements for adopted children, stepchildren, grandchildren, and stepgrandchildren, it hardly follows, as respondent argues, that applicants in those categories are treated more advantageously than are children who must meet a \xc2\xa7416(h) criterion. Respondent charges that the SSA\xe2\x80\x99s construction of the Act raises serious constitutional concerns under the equal protection component of the Due Process Clause. But under rational-basis review, the appropriate standard here, the regime passed by Congress easily passes inspection. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9315.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0Because the SSA\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the relevant provisions, is at least reasonable, the agency\xe2\x80\x99s reading is entitled to this Court\xe2\x80\x99s deference under Chevron, \n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 837\n. Chevron deference is appropriate \xe2\x80\x9cwhen it appears that Congress delegated authority to the agency generally to make rules carrying the force of law, and that the agency interpretation claiming deference was promulgated in the exercise of that authority.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Mead Corp., \n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 218\n\xe2\x80\x93227. Here, the SSA\xe2\x80\x99s longstanding interpretation, set forth in regulations published after notice-and-comment rulemaking, is neither \xe2\x80\x9carbitrary or capricious in substance, [n]or manifestly contrary to the statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Mayo Foundation for Medical Ed. and Research v. United States, 562 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. It therefore warrants the Court\xe2\x80\x99s approbation. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9316.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t631 F.\xc2\xa03d 626, reversed and remanded.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tKaren and Robert Capato married in 1999. Robert died of cancer less than three years later. With the help of in vitro fertilization, Karen gave birth to twins 18 months after her husband\xe2\x80\x99s death. Karen\xe2\x80\x99s application for Social Security survivors benefits for the twins, which the Social Security Administration (SSA) denied, prompted this lit-igation. The technology that made the twins\xe2\x80\x99 conception and birth possible, it is safe to say, was not contemplated by Congress when the relevant provisions of the Social Security Act (Act) originated (1939) or were amended to read as they now do (1965).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tKaren Capato, respondent here, relies on the Act\xe2\x80\x99s initial definition of \xe2\x80\x9cchild\xe2\x80\x9d in \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7416(e): \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[C]hild\xe2\x80\x99 means .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the child or legally adopted child of an [insured] individual.\xe2\x80\x9d Robert was an insured individual, and the twins, it is uncontested, are the biological children of Karen and Robert. That satisfies the Act\xe2\x80\x99s terms, and no further inquiry is in order, Karen maintains. The SSA, however, identifies subsequent provisions, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7416(h)(2) and (h)(3)(C), as critical, and reads them to entitle biological children to benefits only if they qualify for inheritance from the decedent under state intestacy law, or satisfy one of the statutory alternatives to that requirement.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe conclude that the SSA\xe2\x80\x99s reading is better attuned to the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text and its design to benefit primarily those supported by the deceased wage earner in his or her lifetime. And even if the SSA\xe2\x80\x99s longstanding interpretation is not the only reasonable one, it is at least a permissible construction that garners the Court\xe2\x80\x99s respect under Chevron U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., \n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 837 (1984)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tKaren Capato married Robert Capato in May 1999. Shortly thereafter, Robert was diagnosed with esophageal cancer and was told that the chemotherapy he required might render him sterile. Because the couple wanted children, Robert, before undergoing chemotherapy, deposited his semen in a sperm bank, where it was frozen and stored. Despite Robert\xe2\x80\x99s aggressive treatment regime, Karen conceived naturally and gave birth to a son in August 2001. The Capatos, however, wanted their son to have a sibling.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRobert\xe2\x80\x99s health deteriorated in late 2001, and he died in Florida, where he and Karen then resided, in March 2002. His will, executed in Florida, named as beneficiaries the son born of his marriage to Karen and two children from a previous marriage. The will made no provision for children conceived after Robert\xe2\x80\x99s death, although the Capatos had told their lawyer they wanted future offspring to be placed on a par with existing children. Shortly after Robert\xe2\x80\x99s death, Karen began in vitro fertilization using her husband\xe2\x80\x99s frozen sperm. She conceived in January 2003 and gave birth to twins in September 2003, 18 months after Robert\xe2\x80\x99s death.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tKaren Capato claimed survivors insurance benefits on behalf of the twins. The SSA denied her application, and the U.\xc2\xa0S. District Court for the District of New Jersey affirmed the agency\xe2\x80\x99s decision. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 33a (decision of the Administrative Law Judge); id., at 15a (District Court opinion). In accord with the SSA\xe2\x80\x99s construction of the statute, the District Court determined that the twins would qualify for benefits only if, as \xc2\xa7416(h)(2)(A) specifies, they could inherit from the deceased wage earner under state intestacy law. Robert Capato died domiciled in Florida, the court found. Under that State\xe2\x80\x99s law, the court noted, a child born posthu-mously may inherit through intestate succession only if conceived during the decedent\xe2\x80\x99s lifetime. Id., at 27a\xe2\x80\x9328a.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit reversed. Under \xc2\xa7416(e), the appellate court concluded, \xe2\x80\x9cthe undisputed biological children of a deceased wage earner and his widow\xe2\x80\x9d qualify for survivors benefits without regard to state intestacy law. 631 F.\xc2\xa03d 626, 631 (2011).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Courts of Appeals have divided on the statutory interpretation question this case presents. Compare ibid. and Gillett-Netting v. Barnhart, 371 F.\xc2\xa03d 593, 596\xe2\x80\x93597 (CA9 2004) (biological but posthumously conceived child of insured wage earner and his widow qualifies for benefits), with Beeler v. Astrue, 651 F.\xc2\xa03d 954, 960\xe2\x80\x93964 (CA8 2011), and Schafer v. Astrue, 641 F.\xc2\xa03d 49, 54\xe2\x80\x9363 (CA4 2011) (posthumously conceived child\xe2\x80\x99s qualification for benefits depends on intestacy law of State in which wage earner was domiciled). To resolve the conflict, we granted the Commissioner\xe2\x80\x99s petition for a writ of certiorari. 565\xc2\xa0U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0___ (2011).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress amended the Social Security Act in 1939 to provide a monthly benefit for designated surviving fam- ily members of a deceased insured wage earner. \xe2\x80\x9cChild\xe2\x80\x99s insurance benefits\xe2\x80\x9d are among the Act\xe2\x80\x99s family-protective measures. \n\n53Stat.\n1364, as amended, \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7402(d). An applicant qualifies for such benefits if she meets the Act\xe2\x80\x99s definition of \xe2\x80\x9cchild,\xe2\x80\x9d is unmarried, is below specified age limits (18 or 19) or is under a disability which began prior to age 22, and was dependent on the insured at the time of the insured\xe2\x80\x99s death. \xc2\xa7402(d)(1).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo resolve this case, we must decide whether the Capato twins rank as \xe2\x80\x9cchild[ren]\xe2\x80\x9d under the Act\xe2\x80\x99s definitional provisions. Section 402(d) provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]very child (as defined in section 416(e) of this title)\xe2\x80\x9d of a deceased insured individual \xe2\x80\x9cshall be entitled to a child\xe2\x80\x99s insurance benefit.\xe2\x80\x9d Section 416(e), in turn, states: \xe2\x80\x9cThe term \xe2\x80\x98child\xe2\x80\x99 means (1) the child or legally adopted child of an individ-ual, (2) a stepchild [under certain circumstances], and (3) .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the grandchild or stepgrandchild of an individual or his spouse [who meets certain conditions].\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe word \xe2\x80\x9cchild,\xe2\x80\x9d we note, appears twice in \xc2\xa7416(e)\xe2\x80\x99s opening sentence: initially in the prefatory phrase, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he term \xe2\x80\x98child\xe2\x80\x99 means .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. ,\xe2\x80\x9d and, immediately thereafter, in subsection (e)(1) (\xe2\x80\x9cchild or legally adopted child\xe2\x80\x9d), deline-ating the first of three beneficiary categories. Unlike \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7416(e)(2) and (3), which specify the circumstances under which stepchildren and grandchildren qualify for benefits, \xc2\xa7416(e)(1) lacks any elaboration. Compare \xc2\xa7416(e)(1) (referring simply to \xe2\x80\x9cthe child .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of an individual\xe2\x80\x9d) with, e.g., \xc2\xa7416(e)(2) (applicant must have been a stepchild for at least nine months before the insured individual\xe2\x80\x99s death).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA subsequent definitional provision further addresses the term \xe2\x80\x9cchild.\xe2\x80\x9d Under the heading \xe2\x80\x9cDetermination of family status,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7416(h)(2)(A) provides: \xe2\x80\x9cIn determining whether an applicant is the child or parent of [an] insured individual for purposes of this subchapter, the Commissioner of Social Security shall apply [the intestacy law of the insured individual\xe2\x80\x99s domiciliary State].\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAn applicant for child benefits who does not meet \xc2\xa7416(h)(2)(A)\xe2\x80\x99s intestacy-law criterion may nonetheless qualify for benefits under one of several other criteria the Act prescribes. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe point out, first, some conspicuous flaws in the Third Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s and respondent Karen Capato\xe2\x80\x99s reading of the Act\xe2\x80\x99s provisions, and then explain why we find the SSA\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation persuasive.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNothing in \xc2\xa7416(e)\xe2\x80\x99s tautological definition (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98child\xe2\x80\x99 means .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the child .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of an individual\xe2\x80\x9d) suggests that Congress understood the word \xe2\x80\x9cchild\xe2\x80\x9d to refer only to the children of married parents. The dictionary definitions offered by respondent are not so confined. See Webster\xe2\x80\x99s New International Dictionary 465 (2d ed. 1934) (defining \xe2\x80\x9cchild\xe2\x80\x9d as, inter alia, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n Law, legitimate offspring; also, sometimes, esp. in wills, an adopted child, or an illegitimate offspring, or any direct descendant, as a grandchild, as the intention may appear\xe2\x80\x9d); Merriam-Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Collegiate Dictionary 214 (11th ed. 2003) (\xe2\x80\x9cchild\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9cson or daughter,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cdescendant\xe2\x80\x9d). \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNor does \xc2\xa7416(e) indicate that Congress intended \xe2\x80\x9cbiological\xe2\x80\x9d parentage to be prerequisite to \xe2\x80\x9cchild\xe2\x80\x9d status under that provision. As the SSA points out, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n 1939, there was no such thing as a scientifically proven biological relationship between a child and a father, which is .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. part of the reason that the word \xe2\x80\x98biological\xe2\x80\x99 appears nowhere in the Act.\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief 6. Notably, a biological parent is not necessarily a child\xe2\x80\x99s parent under law. Ordinarily, \xe2\x80\x9ca parent-child relationship does not exist between an adoptee and the adoptee\xe2\x80\x99s genetic parents.\xe2\x80\x9d Uniform Probate Code \xc2\xa72\xe2\x80\x93119(a), 8 U.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0A. 55 (Supp. 2011) (amended 2008). Code Ann. \xc2\xa77613(b) (West Supp. 2012) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe donor of semen .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. for use in artificial insemination or in vitro fertilization of a woman other than the donor\xe2\x80\x99s wife is treated in law as if he were not the natural father of a child thereby conceived, unless otherwise agreed to in a writing signed by the donor and the woman prior to the conception of the child.\xe2\x80\x9d); Mass. Gen. Laws, ch. 46, \xc2\xa74B (West 2010) (\xe2\x80\x9cAny child born to a married woman as a result of artificial insemination with the consent of her husband, shall be considered the legitimate child of the mother and such husband.\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

See Reply Brief 11 (\xe2\x80\x9cRespondent errs in treating \xe2\x80\x98marital\xe2\x80\x99 and \xe2\x80\x98undisputed\xe2\x80\x99 as having the same meaning.\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, it is far from obvious that Karen Capato\xe2\x80\x99s proposed definition\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cbiological child of married parents,\xe2\x80\x9d see Brief for Respondent 9\xe2\x80\x94would cover the posthumously conceived Capato twins. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tResisting the importation of words not found in \xc2\xa7416(e)\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cchild\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9cthe biological child of married parents,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 9\xe2\x80\x94the SSA finds a key textual cue in \xc2\xa7416(h)(2)(A)\xe2\x80\x99s opening instruction: \xe2\x80\x9cIn determining whether an applicant is the child .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of [an] insured individual for purposes of this subchapter,\xe2\x80\x9d the Commissioner shall apply state intestacy law. (Emphasis added.) Respondent notes the absence of any cross-reference in \xc2\xa7416(e) to \xc2\xa7416(h). Brief for Respondent 18. She overlooks, however, that \xc2\xa7416(h) provides the crucial link. The \xe2\x80\x9csubchapter\xe2\x80\x9d to which \xc2\xa7416(h) refers is Subchapter II of the Act, which spans \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7401 through 434. Section 416(h)\xe2\x80\x99s reference to \xe2\x80\x9cthis subchapter\xe2\x80\x9d thus includes both \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7402(d) and 416(e). Having explicitly complemented \xc2\xa7416(e) by the definitional provisions contained in \xc2\xa7416(h), Congress had no need to place a redundant cross-reference in \xc2\xa7416(e). See Schafer, 641 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 54 (Congress, in \xc2\xa7416(h)(2)(A), provided \xe2\x80\x9cplain and explicit instruction on how the determination of child status should be made\xe2\x80\x9d; on this point, the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text \xe2\x80\x9ccould hardly be more clear.\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe original version of today\xe2\x80\x99s \xc2\xa7416(h) was similarly drafted. It provided that, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n determining whether an applicant is the .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. child .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of [an] insured individual for purposes of sections 401\xe2\x80\x93409 of this title, the Board shall apply [state intestacy law].\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n42 U. S. C. \xc2\xa7409(m) (1940 ed.) (emphasis added). Sections 401\xe2\x80\x93409 embraced \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7402(c) and 409(k), the statutory predecessors of 42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7402(d) and 416(e) (2006 ed.), respectively.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tReference to state law to determine an applicant\xe2\x80\x99s status as a \xe2\x80\x9cchild\xe2\x80\x9d is anything but anomalous. Quite the opposite. The Act commonly refers to state law on matters of family status. For example, the Act initially defines \xe2\x80\x9cwife\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cthe wife of an [insured] individual,\xe2\x80\x9d if certain conditions are satisfied. \xc2\xa7416(b). Like \xc2\xa7416(e), \xc2\xa7416(b) is, at least in part, tautological (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98wife\xe2\x80\x99 means the [insured\xe2\x80\x99s] wife\xe2\x80\x9d). One must read on, although there is no ex-press cross-reference, to \xc2\xa7416(h) (rules on \xe2\x80\x9c[d]etermination of family status\xe2\x80\x9d) to complete the definition. Section \xc2\xa7416(h)(1)(A) directs that, \xe2\x80\x9cfor purposes of this subchapter,\xe2\x80\x9d the law of the insured\xe2\x80\x99s domicile determines whether \xe2\x80\x9c[the] applicant and [the] insured individual were validly married,\xe2\x80\x9d and if they were not, whether the applicant would nevertheless have \xe2\x80\x9cthe same status\xe2\x80\x9d as a wife under the State\xe2\x80\x99s intestacy law. (Emphasis added.) The Act similarly defines the terms \xe2\x80\x9cwidow,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9chusband,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cwidower.\xe2\x80\x9d See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7416(c), (f), (g), (h)(1)(A).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIndeed, as originally enacted, a single provision mandated the use of state intestacy law for \xe2\x80\x9cdetermining whether an applicant is the wife, widow, child, or parent of [an] insured individual.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7409(m) (1940 ed.). All wife, widow, child, and parent applicants thus had to satisfy the same criterion. To be sure, children born during their parents\xe2\x80\x99 marriage would have readily qualified under the 1939 formulation because of their eligibility to inherit under state law. But requiring all \xe2\x80\x9cchild\xe2\x80\x9d ap-plicants to qualify under state intestacy law installed a simple test, one that ensured benefits for persons plainly within the legislators\xe2\x80\x99 contemplation, while avoiding con-gressional entanglement in the traditional state-law realm of family relations.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJust as the Act generally refers to state law to determine whether an applicant qualifies as a wife, widow, husband, widower, \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7416(h)(1) (2006 ed.), child or parent, \xc2\xa7416(h)(2)(A), so in several sections (\xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7416(b), (c), (e)(2), (f), (g)), the Act sets duration-of-relationship limitations. See Weinberger v. Salfi, \n\n422 U.\xc2\xa0S. 749\n\xe2\x80\x93782 (1975) (discussing \xc2\xa7416(e)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that, as a check against deathbed marriages, a parent-stepchild relationship must exist \xe2\x80\x9cnot less than nine months immediately preceding [insured\xe2\x80\x99s death]\xe2\x80\x9d). Time limits also qualify the statutes of several States that accord inheritance rights to posthumously conceived children. See Cal. Prob. Code Ann. \xc2\xa7249.5(c) (West Supp. 2012) (allowing inheritance if child is in utero within two years of parent\xe2\x80\x99s death); Colo. Rev. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa715\xe2\x80\x9311\xe2\x80\x93120(11) (2011) (child in utero within three years or born within 45 months); Iowa Code Ann. \xc2\xa7633.220A(1) (West Supp. 2012) (child born within two years); La. Rev. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa79:391.1(A) (West 2008) (child born within three years); N. D. Cent. Code Ann. \xc2\xa730.1\xe2\x80\x9304\xe2\x80\x9319(11) (Lexis 2001) (child in utero within three years or born within 45 months). See also Uniform Probate Code \xc2\xa72\xe2\x80\x93120(k), 8 U.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0A. 58 (Supp. 2011) (treating a posthumously conceived child as \xe2\x80\x9cin gestation at the individual\xe2\x80\x99s death,\xe2\x80\x9d but only if specified time limits are met). No time constraints attend the Third Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s ruling in this case, under which the biological child of married parents is eligible for survivors benefits, no matter the length of time between the father\xe2\x80\x99s death and the child\xe2\x80\x99s conception and birth. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 36\xe2\x80\x9337 (counsel for Karen Capato acknowledged that, under the preemptive federal rule he advocated, and the Third Circuit adopted, a child born four years after her father\xe2\x80\x99s death would be eligible for benefits).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe paths to receipt of benefits laid out in the Act and regulations, we must not forget, proceed from Congress\xe2\x80\x99 perception of the core purpose of the legislation. The aim was not to create a program \xe2\x80\x9cgenerally benefiting needy persons\xe2\x80\x9d; it was, more particularly, to \xe2\x80\x9cprovide .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. dependent members of [a wage earner\xe2\x80\x99s] family with protection against the hardship occasioned by [the] loss of [the insured\xe2\x80\x99s] earnings.\xe2\x80\x9d Califano v. Jobst, \n\n434 U.\xc2\xa0S. 47, \n52 (1977)\n. We have recognized that \xe2\x80\x9cwhere state intestacy law provides that a child may take personal property from a father\xe2\x80\x99s estate, it may reasonably be thought that the child will more likely be dependent during the parent\xe2\x80\x99s life and at his death.\xe2\x80\x9d Mathews v. Lucas, \n\n427 U.\xc2\xa0S. 495, \n514 (1976)\n. Reliance on state intestacy law to determine who is a \xe2\x80\x9cchild\xe2\x80\x9d thus serves the Act\xe2\x80\x99s driving objective. True, the intestacy criterion yields benefits to some children outside the Act\xe2\x80\x99s central concern. Intestacy laws in a number of States, as just noted, do provide for inheritance by posthumously conceived children, see supra, at 12,\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t9\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t and under federal law, a child conceived shortly before her father\xe2\x80\x99s death may be eligible for benefits even though she never actually received her father\xe2\x80\x99s support. It was nonetheless Congress\xe2\x80\x99 prerogative to legislate for the generality of cases. It did so here by employing eligibility to inherit under state intestacy law as a workable substitute for bur-densome case-by-case determinations whether the child was, in fact, dependent on her father\xe2\x80\x99s earnings.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondent argues that on the SSA\xe2\x80\x99s reading, natural children alone must pass through a \xc2\xa7416(h) gateway. Adopted children, stepchildren, grandchildren, and step-grandchildren, it is true, are defined in \xc2\xa7416(e), and are not further defined in \xc2\xa7416(h). Respondent overlooks, however, that although not touched by \xc2\xa7416(h), beneficiaries described in \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7416(e)(2) and (e)(3) must meet other statutorily prescribed criteria. In short, the Act and regulations set different eligibility requirements for adopted children, stepchildren, grandchildren, and stepgrandchildren, see 20 CFR \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7404.356\xe2\x80\x93404.358, but it hardly follows that applicants in those categories are treated more advantageously than are children who must meet a \xc2\xa7416(h) criterion.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe SSA\xe2\x80\x99s construction of the Act, respondent charges, raises serious constitutional concerns under the equal pro-tection component of the Due Process Clause. Brief for Respondent 42; see Weinberger v. Wiesenfeld, \n\n420 U.\xc2\xa0S. 636, \n638, n. \n2 (1975)\n. She alleges: \xe2\x80\x9cUnder the government\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0, posthumously conceived children are treated as an inferior subset of natural children who are ineligible for government benefits simply because of their date of birth and method of conception.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 42\xe2\x80\x9343.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEven the Courts of Appeals that have accepted the reading of the Act respondent advances have rejected this argument. See 631 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 628, n.\xc2\xa01 (citing Vernoff v. Astrue, 568 F.\xc2\xa03d 1102, 1112 (CA9 2009)). We have applied an intermediate level of scrutiny to laws \xe2\x80\x9cburden[ing] illegitimate children for the sake of punishing the illicit relations of their parents, because \xe2\x80\x98visiting this condemnation on the head of an infant is illogical and unjust.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Clark v. Jeter, \n\n486 U.\xc2\xa0S. 456, \n461 (1988)\n (quoting Weber v. Aetna Casualty & Surety Co., \n\n406 U.\xc2\xa0S. 164, \n175 (1972)\n). No showing has been made that posthumously conceived children share the characteristics that prompted our skepticism of classifications disadvantaging children of unwed parents. We therefore need not decide whether heightened scrutiny would be appropriate were that the case.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t10\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Under rational-basis review, the regime Congress adopted easily passes inspection. As the Ninth Circuit held, that regime is \xe2\x80\x9creasonably related to the government\xe2\x80\x99s twin interests in [reserving] benefits [for] those children who have lost a parent\xe2\x80\x99s support, and in using reasonable presumptions to minimize the administrative burden of proving dependency on a case-by-case basis.\xe2\x80\x9d Vernoff, 568 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1112 (citing Mathews, 427 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 509).\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs we have explained, \xc2\xa7416(e)(1)\xe2\x80\x99s statement, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he term \xe2\x80\x98child\xe2\x80\x99 means .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the child .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of an individual,\xe2\x80\x9d is a definition of scant utility without aid from neighboring provisions. See Schafer, 641 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 54. That aid is supplied by \xc2\xa7416(h)(2)(A), which completes the definition of \xe2\x80\x9cchild\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cfor purposes of th[e] subchapter\xe2\x80\x9d that includes \xc2\xa7416(e)(1). Under the completed definition, which the SSA employs, \xc2\xa7416(h)(2)(A) refers to state law to determine the status of a posthumously conceived child. The SSA\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the relevant provisions, adhered to without deviation for many decades, is at least reasonable; the agency\xe2\x80\x99s reading is therefore entitled to this Court\xe2\x80\x99s deference under Chevron, \n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 837\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tChevron deference is appropriate \xe2\x80\x9cwhen it appears that Congress delegated authority to the agency generally to make rules carrying the force of law, and that the agency interpretation claiming deference was promulgated in the exercise of that authority.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Mead Corp., \n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 218\n\xe2\x80\x93227 (2001). Here, as already noted, the SSA\xe2\x80\x99s longstanding interpretation is set forth in regu-lations published after notice-and-comment rulemaking. See supra, at 6\xe2\x80\x937. Congress gave the Commissioner authority to promulgate rules \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary or appropriate to carry out\xe2\x80\x9d the Commissioner\xe2\x80\x99s functions and the relevant statutory provisions. See 42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7405(a), 902(a)(5). The Commissioner\xe2\x80\x99s regulations are neither \xe2\x80\x9carbitrary or capricious in substance, [n]or manifestly contrary to the statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Mayo Foundation for Medical Ed. and Research v. United States, 562 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 7) (internal quotation marks omitted). They thus warrant the Court\xe2\x80\x99s approbation. See Barnhart v. Walton, \n\n535 U.\xc2\xa0S. 212\n\xe2\x80\x93222, 225 (2002) (deferring to the Commissioner\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cconsiderable authority\xe2\x80\x9d to interpret the Social Security Act).\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTragic circumstances\xe2\x80\x94Robert Capato\xe2\x80\x99s death before he and his wife could raise a family\xe2\x80\x94gave rise to this case. But the law Congress enacted calls for resolution of Karen Capato\xe2\x80\x99s application for child\xe2\x80\x99s insurance benefits by reference to state intestacy law. We cannot replace that reference by creating a uniform federal rule the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text scarcely supports.\n\t\t\t
