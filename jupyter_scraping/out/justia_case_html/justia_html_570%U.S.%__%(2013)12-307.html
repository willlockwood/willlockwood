\n\t\t\t\tSyllabus\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tUNITED STATES v. WINDSOR, executor of the ESTATE OF SPYER, et\xc2\xa0al.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the second circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 12\xe2\x80\x93307.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 27, 2013\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 26, 2013\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe State of New York recognizes the marriage of New York residents Edith Windsor and Thea Spyer, who wed in Ontario, Canada, in 2007. When Spyer died in 2009, she left her entire estate to Windsor. Windsor sought to claim the federal estate tax exemption for surviving spouses, but was barred from doing so by \xc2\xa73 of the federal Defense of Marriage Act (DOMA), which amended the Dictionary Act\xe2\x80\x94a law providing rules of construction for over 1,000 federal laws and the whole realm of federal regulations\xe2\x80\x94to define \xe2\x80\x9cmarriage\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cspouse\xe2\x80\x9d as excluding same-sex partners. Windsor paid $363,053 in estate taxes and sought a refund, which the Internal Revenue Service denied. Windsor brought this refund suit, contending that DOMA violates the principles of equal protection incorporated in the \n \nFifth Amendment. While the suit was pending, the Attorney General notified the Speaker of the House of Representatives that the Department of Justice would no longer defend \xc2\xa73\xe2\x80\x99s constitutionality. In response, the Bipartisan Legal Advisory Group (BLAG) of the House of Representatives voted to intervene in the litigation to defend \xc2\xa73\xe2\x80\x99s constitutionality. The District Court permitted the intervention. On the merits, the court ruled against the United States, finding \xc2\xa73 unconstitutional and ordering the Treasury to refund Windsor\xe2\x80\x99s tax with interest. The Second Circuit affirmed. The United States has not complied with the judgment.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t1.\xc2\xa0This Court has jurisdiction to consider the merits of the case. This case clearly presented a concrete disagreement between opposing parties that was suitable for judicial resolution in the District Court, but the Executive\xe2\x80\x99s decision not to defend \xc2\xa73\xe2\x80\x99s constitutionality in court while continuing to deny refunds and assess deficiencies introduces a complication. Given the Government\xe2\x80\x99s concession, amicus contends, once the District Court ordered the refund, the case should have ended and the appeal been dismissed. But this argument elides the distinction between Article III\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdictional requirements and the prudential limits on its exercise, which are \xe2\x80\x9cessentially matters of judicial self-governance.\xe2\x80\x9d Warth v. Seldin, \n\n422 U.\xc2\xa0S. 490\n. Here, the United States retains a stake sufficient to support Article III jurisdiction on appeal and in this Court. The refund it was ordered to pay Windsor is \xe2\x80\x9ca real and immediate economic injury,\xe2\x80\x9d Hein v. Freedom From Religion Foundation, Inc., \n\n551 U.\xc2\xa0S. 587\n, even if the Executive disagrees with \xc2\xa73 of DOMA. Windsor\xe2\x80\x99s ongoing claim for funds that the United States refuses to pay thus establishes a controversy sufficient for Article III jurisdiction. Cf. INS v. Chadha, \n\n462 U.\xc2\xa0S. 919\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPrudential considerations, however, demand that there be \xe2\x80\x9cconcrete adverseness which sharpens the presentation of issues upon which the court so largely depends for illumination of difficult constitutional questions.\xe2\x80\x9d Baker v. Carr, \n\n369 U.\xc2\xa0S. 186\n. Unlike Article III requirements\xe2\x80\x94which must be satisfied by the parties before judicial consideration is appropriate\xe2\x80\x94prudential factors that counsel against hearing this case are subject to \xe2\x80\x9ccountervailing considerations [that] may outweigh the concerns underlying the usual reluctance to exert judicial power.\xe2\x80\x9d Warth, supra, at 500\xe2\x80\x93501. One such consideration is the extent to which adversarial presentation of the issues is ensured by the participation of amici curiae prepared to defend with vigor the legislative act\xe2\x80\x99s constitutionality. See Chadha, supra, at 940. Here, BLAG\xe2\x80\x99s substantial adversarial argument for \xc2\xa73\xe2\x80\x99s constitutionality satisfies prudential concerns that otherwise might counsel against hearing an appeal from a decision with which the principal parties agree. This conclusion does not mean that it is appropriate for the Executive as a routine exercise to challenge statutes in court instead of making the case to Congress for amendment or repeal. But this case is not routine, and BLAG\xe2\x80\x99s capable defense ensures that the prudential issues do not cloud the merits question, which is of immediate importance to the Federal Government and to hundreds of thousands of persons. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9313.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t2.\xc2\xa0DOMA is unconstitutional as a deprivation of the equal liberty of persons that is protected by the \n \nFifth Amendment. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9326.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0By history and tradition the definition and regulation of marriage has been treated as being within the authority and realm of the separate States. Congress has enacted discrete statutes to regulate the meaning of marriage in order to further federal policy, but DOMA, with a directive applicable to over 1,000 federal statues and the whole realm of federal regulations, has a far greater reach. Its operation is also directed to a class of persons that the laws of New York, and of 11 other States, have sought to protect. Assessing the validity of that intervention requires discussing the historical and traditional extent of state power and authority over marriage.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSubject to certain constitutional guarantees, see, e.g., Loving v. Virginia, \n\n388 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n, \xe2\x80\x9cregulation of domestic relations\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9can area that has long been regarded as a virtually exclusive province of the States,\xe2\x80\x9d Sosna v. Iowa, \n\n419 U.\xc2\xa0S. 393\n. The significance of state responsibilities for the definition and regulation of marriage dates to the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s beginning; for \xe2\x80\x9cwhen the Constitution was adopted the common understanding was that the domestic relations of husband and wife and parent and child were matters reserved to the States,\xe2\x80\x9d Ohio ex\xc2\xa0rel. Popovici v. Agler, \n\n280 U.\xc2\xa0S. 379\n\xe2\x80\x93384. Marriage laws may vary from State to State, but they are consistent within each State.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tDOMA rejects this long-established precept. The State\xe2\x80\x99s decision to give this class of persons the right to marry conferred upon them a dignity and status of immense import. But the Federal Government uses the state-defined class for the opposite purpose\xe2\x80\x94to impose restrictions and disabilities. The question is whether the resulting injury and indignity is a deprivation of an essential part of the liberty protected by the \n \nFifth Amendment, since what New York treats as alike the federal law deems unlike by a law designed to injure the same class the State seeks to protect. New York\xe2\x80\x99s actions were a proper exercise of its sovereign authority. They reflect both the community\xe2\x80\x99s considered perspective on the historical roots of the institution of marriage and its evolving understanding of the meaning of equality. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9320.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0By seeking to injure the very class New York seeks to protect, DOMA violates basic due process and equal protection principles applicable to the Federal Government. The Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s guarantee of equality \xe2\x80\x9cmust at the very least mean that a bare congressional desire to harm a politically unpopular group cannot\xe2\x80\x9d justify disparate treatment of that group. Department of Agriculture v. Moreno, \n\n413 U.\xc2\xa0S. 528\n\xe2\x80\x93535. DOMA cannot survive under these principles. Its unusual deviation from the tradition of recognizing and accepting state definitions of marriage operates to deprive same-sex couples of the benefits and responsibilities that come with federal recognition of their marriages. This is strong evidence of a law having the purpose and effect of disapproval of a class recognized and protected by state law. DOMA\xe2\x80\x99s avowed purpose and practical effect are to impose a disadvantage, a separate status, and so a stigma upon all who enter into same-sex marriages made lawful by the unquestioned authority of the States.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tDOMA\xe2\x80\x99s history of enactment and its own text demonstrate that interference with the equal dignity of same-sex marriages, conferred by the States in the exercise of their sovereign power, was more than an incidental effect of the federal statute. It was its essence. BLAG\xe2\x80\x99s arguments are just as candid about the congressional purpose. DOMA\xe2\x80\x99s operation in practice confirms this purpose. It frustrates New York\xe2\x80\x99s objective of eliminating inequality by writing inequality into the entire United States Code.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tDOMA\xe2\x80\x99s principal effect is to identify and make unequal a subset of state-sanctioned marriages. It contrives to deprive some couples married under the laws of their State, but not others, of both rights and responsibilities, creating two contradictory marriage regimes within the same State. It also forces same-sex couples to live as married for the purpose of state law but unmarried for the purpose of federal law, thus diminishing the stability and predictability of basic personal relations the State has found it proper to acknowledge and protect. Pp.\xc2\xa020\xe2\x80\x9326.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t699 F.\xc2\xa03d 169, affirmed.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tUNITED STATES, PETITIONER v. EDITH SCHLAIN WINDSOR, in her capacity as executor of the ESTATE OF THEA CLARA SPYER, et\xc2\xa0al.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\ton writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the second circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t[June 26, 2013]\n\t\t\t

\n

\n

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Kennedy delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTwo women then resident in New York were married in a lawful ceremony in Ontario, Canada, in 2007. Edith Windsor and Thea Spyer returned to their home in New York City. When Spyer died in 2009, she left her entire estate to Windsor. Windsor sought to claim the estate tax exemption for surviving spouses. She was barred from doing so, however, by a federal law, the Defense of Marriage Act, which excludes a same-sex partner from the definition of \xe2\x80\x9cspouse\xe2\x80\x9d as that term is used in federal statutes. Windsor paid the taxes but filed suit to challenge the constitutionality of this provision. The United States District Court and the Court of Appeals ruled that this portion of the statute is unconstitutional and ordered the United States to pay Windsor a refund. This Court granted certiorari and now affirms the judgment in Windsor\xe2\x80\x99s favor.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 1996, as some States were beginning to consider the concept of same-sex marriage, see, e.g., Baehr v. Lewin, 74 Haw. 530, 852 P.\xc2\xa02d 44 (1993), and before any State had acted to permit it, Congress enacted the Defense of Marriage Act (DOMA), \n\n110Stat.\n2419. DOMA contains two operative sections: Section 2, which has not been challenged here, allows States to refuse to recognize same-sex marriages performed under the laws of other States. See \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71738C.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSection 3 is at issue here. It amends the Dictionary Act in Title 1, \xc2\xa77, of the United States Code to provide a fed- eral definition of \xe2\x80\x9cmarriage\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cspouse.\xe2\x80\x9d Section 3 of DOMA provides as follows:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cIn determining the meaning of any Act of Congress, or of any ruling, regulation, or interpretation of the various administrative bureaus and agencies of the United States, the word \xe2\x80\x98marriage\xe2\x80\x99 means only a legal union between one man and one woman as husband and wife, and the word \xe2\x80\x98spouse\xe2\x80\x99 refers only to a person of the opposite sex who is a husband or a wife.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n1 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe definitional provision does not by its terms forbid States from enacting laws permitting same-sex marriages or civil unions or providing state benefits to residents in that status. The enactment\xe2\x80\x99s comprehensive definition of marriage for purposes of all federal statutes and other regulations or directives covered by its terms, however, does control over 1,000 federal laws in which marital or spousal status is addressed as a matter of federal law. See GAO, D. Shah, Defense of Marriage Act: Update to Prior Report 1 (GAO\xe2\x80\x9304\xe2\x80\x93353R, 2004).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEdith Windsor and Thea Spyer met in New York City in 1963 and began a long-term relationship. Windsor and Spyer registered as domestic partners when New York City gave that right to same-sex couples in 1993. Concerned about Spyer\xe2\x80\x99s health, the couple made the 2007 trip to Canada for their marriage, but they continued to reside in New York City. The State of New York deems their Ontario marriage to be a valid one. See 699 F.\xc2\xa03d 169, 177\xe2\x80\x93178 (CA2 2012).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSpyer died in February 2009, and left her entire estate to Windsor. Because DOMA denies federal recognition to same-sex spouses, Windsor did not qualify for the marital exemption from the federal estate tax, which excludes from taxation \xe2\x80\x9cany interest in property which passes or has passed from the decedent to his surviving spouse.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72056(a). Windsor paid $363,053 in estate taxes and sought a refund. The Internal Revenue Service denied the refund, concluding that, under DOMA, Windsor was not a \xe2\x80\x9csurviving spouse.\xe2\x80\x9d Windsor commenced this refund suit in the United States District Court for the Southern District of New York. She contended that DOMA violates the guarantee of equal protection, as applied to the Federal Government through the \n \nFifth Amendment.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhile the tax refund suit was pending, the Attorney General of the United States notified the Speaker of the House of Representatives, pursuant to \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7530D, that the Department of Justice would no longer defend the constitutionality of DOMA\xe2\x80\x99s \xc2\xa73. Noting that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Department has previously defended DOMA against .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. challenges involving legally married same-sex couples,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 184, the Attorney General informed Congress that \xe2\x80\x9cthe President has concluded that given a number of factors, including a documented history of discrimination, classifications based on sexual orientation should be subject to a heightened standard of scrutiny.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 191. The Department of Justice has submitted many \xc2\xa7530D letters over the years refusing to defend laws it deems unconstitutional, when, for instance, a federal court has rejected the Government\xe2\x80\x99s defense of a statute and has issued a judgment against it. This case is unusual, however, because the \xc2\xa7530D letter was not preceded by an adverse judgment. The letter instead reflected the Executive\xe2\x80\x99s own conclusion, relying on a definition still being debated and considered in the courts, that heightened equal protection scrutiny should apply to laws that classify on the basis of sexual orientation.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAlthough \xe2\x80\x9cthe President .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. instructed the Department not to defend the statute in Windsor,\xe2\x80\x9d he also decided \xe2\x80\x9cthat Section 3 will continue to be enforced by the Executive Branch\xe2\x80\x9d and that the United States had an \xe2\x80\x9cinterest in providing Congress a full and fair opportunity to participate in the litigation of those cases.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 191\xe2\x80\x93193. The stated rationale for this dual-track procedure (determination of unconstitutionality coupled with ongoing enforcement) was to \xe2\x80\x9crecogniz[e] the judiciary as the final arbiter of the constitutional claims raised.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 192.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn response to the notice from the Attorney General, the Bipartisan Legal Advisory Group (BLAG) of the House of Representatives voted to intervene in the litigation to defend the constitutionality of \xc2\xa73 of DOMA. The Department of Justice did not oppose limited intervention by BLAG. The District Court denied BLAG\xe2\x80\x99s motion to enter the suit as of right, on the rationale that the United States already was represented by the Department of Justice. The District Court, however, did grant intervention by BLAG as an interested party. See Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 24(a)(2).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOn the merits of the tax refund suit, the District Court ruled against the United States. It held that \xc2\xa73 of DOMA is unconstitutional and ordered the Treasury to refund the tax with interest. Both the Justice Department and BLAG filed notices of appeal, and the Solicitor General filed a petition for certiorari before judgment. Before this Court acted on the petition, the Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit affirmed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment. It applied heightened scrutiny to classifications based on sexual orientation, as both the Department and Windsor had urged. The United States has not complied with the judg- ment. Windsor has not received her refund, and the Ex- ecutive Branch continues to enforce \xc2\xa73 of DOMA.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn granting certiorari on the question of the constitutionality of \xc2\xa73 of DOMA, the Court requested argument on two additional questions: whether the United States\xe2\x80\x99 agreement with Windsor\xe2\x80\x99s legal position precludes further review and whether BLAG has standing to appeal the case. All parties agree that the Court has jurisdiction to decide this case; and, with the case in that framework, the Court appointed Professor Vicki Jackson as amicus curiae to argue the position that the Court lacks jurisdiction to hear the dispute. 568 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012). She has ably discharged her duties.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn an unrelated case, the United States Court of Appeals for the First Circuit has also held \xc2\xa73 of DOMA to be unconstitutional. A petition for certiorari has been filed in that case. Pet. for Cert. in Bipartisan Legal Advisory Group v. Gill, O.\xc2\xa0T. 2012, No. 12\xe2\x80\x9313.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is appropriate to begin by addressing whether either the Government or BLAG, or both of them, were entitled to appeal to the Court of Appeals and later to seek certiorari and appear as parties here.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThere is no dispute that when this case was in the District Court it presented a concrete disagreement between opposing parties, a dispute suitable for judicial resolution. \xe2\x80\x9c[A] taxpayer has standing to challenge the collection of a specific tax assessment as unconstitutional; being forced to pay such a tax causes a real and immediate economic injury to the individual taxpayer.\xe2\x80\x9d Hein v. Freedom From Religion Foundation, Inc., \n\n551 U.\xc2\xa0S. 587, \n599 (2007)\n (plurality opinion) (emphasis deleted). Windsor suffered a redressable injury when she was required to pay estate taxes from which, in her view, she was exempt but for the alleged invalidity of \xc2\xa73 of DOMA.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe decision of the Executive not to defend the constitutionality of \xc2\xa73 in court while continuing to deny refunds and to assess deficiencies does introduce a complication. Even though the Executive\xe2\x80\x99s current position was announced before the District Court entered its judgment, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s agreement with Windsor\xe2\x80\x99s position would not have deprived the District Court of jurisdiction to entertain and resolve the refund suit; for her injury (fail- ure to obtain a refund allegedly required by law) was concrete, persisting, and unredressed. The Government\xe2\x80\x99s position\xe2\x80\x94agreeing with Windsor\xe2\x80\x99s legal contention but refusing to give it effect\xe2\x80\x94meant that there was a justiciable controversy between the parties, despite what the claimant would find to be an inconsistency in that stance. Windsor, the Government, BLAG, and the amicus appear to agree upon that point. The disagreement is over the standing of the parties, or aspiring parties, to take an appeal in the Court of Appeals and to appear as parties in further proceedings in this Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe amicus\xe2\x80\x99 position is that, given the Government\xe2\x80\x99s concession that \xc2\xa73 is unconstitutional, once the District Court ordered the refund the case should have ended; and the amicus argues the Court of Appeals should have dismissed the appeal. The amicus submits that once the President agreed with Windsor\xe2\x80\x99s legal position and the District Court issued its judgment, the parties were no longer adverse. From this standpoint the United States was a prevailing party below, just as Windsor was. Accordingly, the amicus reasons, it is inappropriate for this Court to grant certiorari and proceed to rule on the merits; for the United States seeks no redress from the judgment entered against it.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis position, however, elides the distinction between two principles: the jurisdictional requirements of Article III and the prudential limits on its exercise. See Warth v. Seldin, \n\n422 U.\xc2\xa0S. 490, \n498 (1975)\n. The latter are \xe2\x80\x9cessentially matters of judicial self-governance.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 500. The Court has kept these two strands separate: \xe2\x80\x9cArticle III standing, which enforces the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s case-or-controversy requirement, see Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife, \n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 555\n\xe2\x80\x93562 (1992); and prudential standing, which embodies \xe2\x80\x98judicially self-imposed limits on the exer- cise of federal jurisdiction,\xe2\x80\x99 Allen [v. Wright,] 468 U.\xc2\xa0S. [737,] 751 [(1984)].\xe2\x80\x9d Elk Grove Unified School Dist. v. Newdow, \n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n\xe2\x80\x9312 (2004).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe requirements of Article III standing are familiar:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cFirst, the plaintiff must have suffered an \xe2\x80\x98injury in fact\xe2\x80\x99\xe2\x80\x94an invasion of a legally protected interest which is (a) concrete and particularized, and (b) \xe2\x80\x98actual or imminent, not \xe2\x80\x9cconjectural or hypothetical.\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99 Second, there must be a causal connection between the injury and the conduct complained of\xe2\x80\x94the injury has to be \xe2\x80\x98fairly .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. trace[able] to the challenged action of the defendant, and not .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. th[e] result [of] the independent action of some third party not before the court.\xe2\x80\x99 Third, it must be \xe2\x80\x98likely,\xe2\x80\x99 as opposed to merely \xe2\x80\x98speculative,\xe2\x80\x99 that the injury will be \xe2\x80\x98redressed by a favor- able decision.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Lujan, supra, at 560\xe2\x80\x93561 (footnote and citations omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tRules of prudential standing, by contrast, are more flex- ible \xe2\x80\x9crule[s] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of federal appellate practice,\xe2\x80\x9d Deposit Guaranty Nat. Bank v. Roper, \n\n445 U.\xc2\xa0S. 326, \n333 (1980)\n, designed to protect the courts from \xe2\x80\x9cdecid[ing] abstract questions of wide public significance even [when] other governmental institutions may be more competent to ad- dress the questions and even though judicial intervention may be unnecessary to protect individual rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Warth, supra, at 500.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn this case the United States retains a stake sufficient to support Article III jurisdiction on appeal and in proceedings before this Court. The judgment in question orders the United States to pay Windsor the refund she seeks. An order directing the Treasury to pay money is \xe2\x80\x9ca real and immediate economic injury,\xe2\x80\x9d Hein, 551 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 599, indeed as real and immediate as an order directing an individual to pay a tax. That the Executive may welcome this order to pay the refund if it is accompanied by the constitutional ruling it wants does not eliminate the injury to the national Treasury if payment is made, or to the taxpayer if it is not. The judgment orders the United States to pay money that it would not disburse but for the court\xe2\x80\x99s order. The Government of the United States has a valid legal argument that it is injured even if the Executive disagrees with \xc2\xa73 of DOMA, which results in Windsor\xe2\x80\x99s liability for the tax. Windsor\xe2\x80\x99s ongoing claim for funds that the United States refuses to pay thus establishes a controversy sufficient for Article III jurisdiction. It would be a different case if the Executive had taken the further step of paying Windsor the refund to which she was entitled under the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis Court confronted a comparable case in INS v. Chadha, \n\n462 U.\xc2\xa0S. 919 (1983)\n. A statute by its terms allowed one House of Congress to order the Immigration and Naturalization Service (INS) to deport the respondent Chadha. There, as here, the Executive determined that the statute was unconstitutional, and \xe2\x80\x9cthe INS presented the Executive\xe2\x80\x99s views on the constitutionality of the House action to the Court of Appeals.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 930. The INS, however, continued to abide by the statute, and \xe2\x80\x9cthe INS brief to the Court of Appeals did not alter the agency\xe2\x80\x99s decision to comply with the House action ordering deportation of Chadha.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. This Court held \xe2\x80\x9cthat the INS was sufficiently aggrieved by the Court of Appeals deci- sion prohibiting it from taking action it would otherwise take,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., regardless of whether the agency welcomed the judgment. The necessity of a \xe2\x80\x9ccase or controversy\xe2\x80\x9d to satisfy Article III was defined as a requirement that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98decision will have real meaning: if we rule for Chadha, he will not be deported; if we uphold [the statute], the INS will execute its order and deport him.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 939\xe2\x80\x93940 (quoting Chadha v. INS, 634 F.\xc2\xa02d 408, 419 (CA9 1980)). This conclusion was not dictum. It was a necessary predicate to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding that \xe2\x80\x9cprior to Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intervention, there was adequate Art.\xc2\xa0III adverseness.\xe2\x80\x9d 462 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 939. The holdings of cases are instructive, and the words of Chadha make clear its holding that the refusal of the Executive to provide the relief sought suffices to preserve a justiciable dispute as required by Article III. In short, even where \xe2\x80\x9cthe Government largely agree[s] with the opposing party on the merits of the controversy,\xe2\x80\x9d there is sufficient adverseness and an \xe2\x80\x9cadequate basis for jurisdiction in the fact that the Government intended to enforce the challenged law against that party.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 940, n.\xc2\xa012.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is true that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] party who receives all that he has sought generally is not aggrieved by the judgment affording the relief and cannot appeal from it.\xe2\x80\x9d Roper, supra, at 333, see also Camreta v. Greene, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 8) (\xe2\x80\x9cAs a matter of practice and prudence, we have generally declined to consider cases at the request of a prevailing party, even when the Constitution allowed us to do so\xe2\x80\x9d). But this rule \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not have its source in the jurisdictional limitations of Art. III. In an appropriate case, appeal may be permitted .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. at the behest of the party who has prevailed on the merits, so long as that party retains a stake in the appeal satisfying the requirements of Art. III.\xe2\x80\x9d Roper, supra, at 333\xe2\x80\x93334.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhile these principles suffice to show that this case presents a justiciable controversy under Article III, the prudential problems inherent in the Executive\xe2\x80\x99s unusual position require some further discussion. The Executive\xe2\x80\x99s agreement with Windsor\xe2\x80\x99s legal argument raises the risk that instead of a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98real, earnest and vital controversy,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the Court faces a \xe2\x80\x9cfriendly, non-adversary, proceeding .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [in which] \xe2\x80\x98a party beaten in the legislature [seeks to] transfer to the courts an inquiry as to the constitutionality of the legislative act.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ashwander v. TVA, \n\n297 U.\xc2\xa0S. 288, \n346 (1936)\n (Brandeis, J., concurring) (quoting Chicago & Grand Trunk R. Co. v. Wellman, \n\n143 U.\xc2\xa0S. 339, \n345 (1892)\n). Even when Article III permits the exercise of federal jurisdiction, prudential considerations demand that the Court insist upon \xe2\x80\x9cthat concrete adverseness which sharpens the presentation of issues upon which the court so largely depends for illumination of difficult constitutional questions.\xe2\x80\x9d Baker v. Carr, \n\n369 U.\xc2\xa0S. 186, \n204 (1962)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThere are, of course, reasons to hear a case and issue a ruling even when one party is reluctant to prevail in its position. Unlike Article III requirements\xe2\x80\x94which must be satisfied by the parties before judicial consideration is appropriate\xe2\x80\x94the relevant prudential factors that counsel against hearing this case are subject to \xe2\x80\x9ccountervailing considerations [that] may outweigh the concerns underlying the usual reluctance to exert judicial power.\xe2\x80\x9d Warth, 422 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 500\xe2\x80\x93501. One consideration is the extent to which adversarial presentation of the issues is assured by the participation of amici curiae prepared to defend with vigor the constitutionality of the legislative act. With respect to this prudential aspect of standing as well, the Chadha Court encountered a similar situation. It noted that \xe2\x80\x9cthere may be prudential, as opposed to Art. III, concerns about sanctioning the adjudication of [this case] in the absence of any participant supporting the validity of [the statute]. The Court of Appeals properly dispelled any such concerns by inviting and accepting briefs from both Houses of Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d 462 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 940. Chadha was not an anomaly in this respect. The Court adopts the practice of entertaining arguments made by an amicus when the Solicitor General confesses error with respect to a judgment below, even if the confession is in effect an admission that an Act of Congress is unconstitutional. See, e.g., Dickerson v. United States, \n\n530 U.\xc2\xa0S. 428 (2000)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn the case now before the Court the attorneys for BLAG present a substantial argument for the constitutionality of \xc2\xa73 of DOMA. BLAG\xe2\x80\x99s sharp adversarial presentation of the issues satisfies the prudential concerns that otherwise might counsel against hearing an appeal from a decision with which the principal parties agree. Were this Court to hold that prudential rules require it to dismiss the case, and, in consequence, that the Court of Appeals erred in failing to dismiss it as well, extensive litigation would ensue. The district courts in 94 districts throughout the Nation would be without precedential guidance not only in tax refund suits but also in cases involving the whole of DOMA\xe2\x80\x99s sweep involving over 1,000 federal statutes and a myriad of federal regulations. For instance, the opinion of the Court of Appeals for the First Circuit, addressing the validity of DOMA in a case involving regulations of the Department of Health and Human Services, likely would be vacated with instructions to dismiss, its ruling and guidance also then erased. See Massachusetts v. United States Dept. of Health and Human Servs., 682 F.\xc2\xa03d 1 (CA1 2012). Rights and privileges of hundreds of thousands of persons would be adversely affected, pending a case in which all prudential concerns about justiciability are absent. That numerical prediction may not be certain, but it is certain that the cost in judicial resources and expense of litigation for all persons adversely affected would be immense. True, the very extent of DOMA\xe2\x80\x99s mandate means that at some point a case likely would arise without the prudential concerns raised here; but the costs, uncertainties, and alleged harm and injuries likely would continue for a time measured in years before the issue is resolved. In these unusual and urgent circumstances, the very term \xe2\x80\x9cprudential\xe2\x80\x9d counsels that it is a proper exercise of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s responsibility to take jurisdiction. For these reasons, the prudential and Article III requirements are met here; and, as a consequence, the Court need not decide whether BLAG would have standing to challenge the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling and its affirmance in the Court of Appeals on BLAG\xe2\x80\x99s own authority.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that this petition may be heard on the merits does not imply that no difficulties would ensue if this were a common practice in ordinary cases. The Executive\xe2\x80\x99s failure to defend the constitutionality of an Act of Congress based on a constitutional theory not yet established in judicial decisions has created a procedural dilemma. On the one hand, as noted, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s agreement with Windsor raises questions about the propriety of entertaining a suit in which it seeks affirmance of an order invalidating a federal law and ordering the United States to pay money. On the other hand, if the Execu- tive\xe2\x80\x99s agreement with a plaintiff that a law is unconsti- tutional is enough to preclude judicial review, then the Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s primary role in determining the constitutionality of a law that has inflicted real injury on a plaintiff who has brought a justiciable legal claim would become only secondary to the President\xe2\x80\x99s. This would undermine the clear dictate of the separation-of-powers principle that \xe2\x80\x9cwhen an Act of Congress is alleged to conflict with the Constitution, \xe2\x80\x98[i]t is emphatically the province and duty of the judicial department to say what the law is.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Zivotofsky v. Clinton, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 7) (quoting Marbury v. Madison, 1 Cranch 137, 177 (1803)). Similarly, with respect to the legislative power, when Congress has passed a statute and a President has signed it, it poses grave challenges to the separation of powers for the Executive at a particular moment to be able to nullify Congress\xe2\x80\x99 enactment solely on its own initiative and without any determination from the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdictional holding, it must be underscored, does not mean the arguments for dismissing this dispute on prudential grounds lack substance. Yet the difficulty the Executive faces should be acknowledged. When the Executive makes a principled determination that a statute is unconstitutional, it faces a difficult choice. Still, there is no suggestion here that it is appropriate for the Executive as a matter of course to challenge statutes in the judicial forum rather than making the case to Congress for their amendment or repeal. The integrity of the political process would be at risk if difficult consti- tutional issues were simply referred to the Court as a routine exercise. But this case is not routine. And the capable defense of the law by BLAG ensures that these prudential issues do not cloud the merits question, which is one of immediate importance to the Federal Government and to hundreds of thousands of persons. These cir- cumstances support the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to proceed to the merits.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhen at first Windsor and Spyer longed to marry, neither New York nor any other State granted them that right. After waiting some years, in 2007 they traveled to Ontario to be married there. It seems fair to conclude that, until recent years, many citizens had not even considered the possibility that two persons of the same sex might aspire to occupy the same status and dignity as that of a man and woman in lawful marriage. For marriage between a man and a woman no doubt had been thought of by most people as essential to the very definition of that term and to its role and function throughout the history of civilization. That belief, for many who long have held it, became even more urgent, more cherished when challenged. For others, however, came the beginnings of a new perspective, a new insight. Accordingly some States concluded that same-sex marriage ought to be given recognition and validity in the law for those same-sex couples who wish to define themselves by their commitment to each other. The limitation of lawful marriage to heterosexual couples, which for centuries had been deemed both necessary and fundamental, came to be seen in New York and certain other States as an unjust exclusion.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSlowly at first and then in rapid course, the laws of New York came to acknowledge the urgency of this issue for same-sex couples who wanted to affirm their commitment to one another before their children, their family, their friends, and their community. And so New York recognized same-sex marriages performed elsewhere; and then it later amended its own marriage laws to permit same-sex marriage. New York, in common with, as of this writing, 11 other States and the District of Columbia, decided that same-sex couples should have the right to marry and so live with pride in themselves and their union and in a status of equality with all other married persons. After a statewide deliberative process that enabled its citizens to discuss and weigh arguments for and against same- sex marriage, New York acted to enlarge the definition of marriage to correct what its citizens and elected representatives perceived to be an injustice that they had not earlier known or understood. See Marriage Equality Act, 2011 N.\xc2\xa0Y. Laws 749 (codified at N.\xc2\xa0Y. Dom. Rel. Law Ann. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa710\xe2\x80\x93a, 10\xe2\x80\x93b, 13 (West 2013)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAgainst this background of lawful same-sex marriage in some States, the design, purpose, and effect of DOMA should be considered as the beginning point in deciding whether it is valid under the Constitution. By history and tradition the definition and regulation of marriage, as will be discussed in more detail, has been treated as being within the authority and realm of the separate States. Yet it is further established that Congress, in enacting discrete statutes, can make determinations that bear on marital rights and privileges. Just this Term the Court upheld the authority of the Congress to pre-empt state laws, allowing a former spouse to retain life insurance proceeds under a federal program that gave her priority, because of formal beneficiary designation rules, over the wife by a second marriage who survived the husband. Hillman v. Maretta, 569 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2013); see also Ridgway v. Ridgway, \n\n454 U.\xc2\xa0S. 46 (1981)\n; Wissner v. Wissner, \n\n338 U.\xc2\xa0S. 655 (1950)\n. This is one example of the general principle that when the Federal Government acts in the exercise of its own proper authority, it has a wide choice of the mechanisms and means to adopt. See McCulloch v. Maryland, 4 Wheat. 316, 421 (1819). Congress has the power both to ensure efficiency in the administration of its programs and to choose what larger goals and policies to pursue.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOther precedents involving congressional statutes which affect marriages and family status further illustrate this point. In addressing the interaction of state domestic relations and federal immigration law Congress determined that marriages \xe2\x80\x9centered into for the purpose of procuring an alien\xe2\x80\x99s admission [to the United States] as an immigrant\xe2\x80\x9d will not qualify the noncitizen for that status, even if the noncitizen\xe2\x80\x99s marriage is valid and proper for state-law purposes. \n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71186a(b)(1) (2006 ed. and Supp. V). And in establishing income-based criteria for Social Security benefits, Congress decided that although state law would determine in general who qualifies as an applicant\xe2\x80\x99s spouse, common-law marriages also should be recognized, regardless of any particular State\xe2\x80\x99s view on these relationships. \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71382c(d)(2).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThough these discrete examples establish the constitutionality of limited federal laws that regulate the meaning of marriage in order to further federal policy, DOMA has a far greater reach; for it enacts a directive applicable to over 1,000 federal statutes and the whole realm of federal regulations. And its operation is directed to a class of persons that the laws of New York, and of 11 other States, have sought to protect. See Goodridge v. Department of Public Health, 440 Mass. 309, 798 N.\xc2\xa0E.\xc2\xa02d 941 (2003); An Act Implementing the Guarantee of Equal Protection Under the Constitution of the State for Same Sex Couples, 2009 Conn. Pub. Acts no. 09\xe2\x80\x9313; Varnum v. Brien, 763 N.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa02d 862 (Iowa 2009); Vt. Stat. Ann., Tit. 15, \xc2\xa78 (2010); N.\xc2\xa0H. Rev. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa7457:1\xe2\x80\x93a (West Supp. 2012); Religious Freedom and Civil Marriage Equality Amendment Act of 2009, 57 D.\xc2\xa0C. Reg. 27 (Dec. 18, 2009); N.\xc2\xa0Y. Dom. Rel. Law Ann. \xc2\xa710\xe2\x80\x93a (West Supp. 2013); Wash. Rev. Code \xc2\xa726.04.010 (2012); Citizen Initiative, Same- Sex Marriage, Question 1 (Me. 2012) (results online at http:\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0w\xc2\xa0w\xc2\xa0w.maine.gov/sos/cec/elec/2012/tab\xc2\xa0-\xc2\xa0ref-2012.html (all Internet sources as visited June 18, 2013, and avail- able in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file)); Md. Fam. Law Code Ann. \xc2\xa72\xe2\x80\x93201 (Lexis 2012); An Act to Amend Title 13 of the Delaware Code Relating to Domestic Relations to Provide for Same-Gender Civil Marriage and to Convert Exist- ing Civil Unions to Civil Marriages, 79 Del. Laws ch. 19 (2013); An act relating to marriage; providing for civil marriage between two persons; providing for exemptions and protections based on religious association, 2013 Minn. Laws ch. 74; An Act Relating to Domestic Relations\xe2\x80\x94Persons Eligible to Marry, 2013 R.\xc2\xa0I. Laws ch. 4.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn order to assess the validity of that intervention it is necessary to discuss the extent of the state power and au- thority over marriage as a matter of history and tradi- tion. State laws defining and regulating marriage, of course, must respect the constitutional rights of persons, see, e.g., Loving v. Virginia, \n\n388 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1 (1967)\n; but, subject to those guarantees, \xe2\x80\x9cregulation of domestic relations\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9can area that has long been regarded as a virtually exclusive province of the States.\xe2\x80\x9d Sosna v. Iowa, \n\n419 U.\xc2\xa0S. 393, \n404 (1975)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe recognition of civil marriages is central to state domestic relations law applicable to its residents and citizens. See Williams v. North Carolina, \n\n317 U.\xc2\xa0S. 287, \n298 (1942)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cEach state as a sovereign has a rightful and legitimate concern in the marital status of persons domiciled within its borders\xe2\x80\x9d). The definition of marriage is the foundation of the State\xe2\x80\x99s broader authority to regulate the subject of domestic relations with respect to the \xe2\x80\x9c[p]rotection of offspring, property interests, and the enforcement of marital responsibilities.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he states, at the time of the adoption of the Constitution, possessed full power over the subject of marriage and divorce .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0[and] the Constitution delegated no authority to the Government of the United States on the subject of marriage and divorce.\xe2\x80\x9d Haddock v. Haddock, \n\n201 U.\xc2\xa0S. 562, \n575 (1906)\n; see also In re Burrus, \n\n136 U.\xc2\xa0S. 586\n\xe2\x80\x93594 (1890) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe whole subject of the domestic relations of husband and wife, parent and child, belongs to the laws of the States and not to the laws of the United States\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tConsistent with this allocation of authority, the Federal Government, through our history, has deferred to state-law policy decisions with respect to domestic relations. In De Sylva v. Ballentine, \n\n351 U.\xc2\xa0S. 570 (1956)\n, for example, the Court held that, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o decide who is the widow or widower of a deceased author, or who are his executors or next of kin,\xe2\x80\x9d under the Copyright Act \xe2\x80\x9crequires a reference to the law of the State which created those legal relationships\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9cthere is no federal law of domestic relations.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 580. In order to respect this principle, the federal courts, as a general rule, do not adjudicate issues of marital status even when there might otherwise be a basis for federal jurisdiction. See Ankenbrandt v. Richards, \n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 689, \n703 (1992)\n. Federal courts will not hear divorce and custody cases even if they arise in diversity because of \xe2\x80\x9cthe virtually exclusive primacy .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of the States in the regulation of domestic relations.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 714 (Blackmun, J., concurring in judgment).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe significance of state responsibilities for the definition and regulation of marriage dates to the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s beginning; for \xe2\x80\x9cwhen the Constitution was adopted the common understanding was that the domestic relations of husband and wife and parent and child were matters reserved to the States.\xe2\x80\x9d Ohio ex\xc2\xa0rel. Popovici v. Agler, \n\n280 U.\xc2\xa0S. 379\n\xe2\x80\x93384 (1930). Marriage laws vary in some respects from State to State. For example, the required minimum age is 16 in Vermont, but only 13 in New Hampshire. Compare Vt. Stat. Ann., Tit. 18, \xc2\xa75142 (2012), with N.\xc2\xa0H. Rev. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa7457:4 (West Supp. 2012). Likewise the permissible degree of consanguinity can vary (most States permit first cousins to marry, but a handful\xe2\x80\x94such as Iowa and Washington, see Iowa Code \xc2\xa7595.19 (2009); Wash. Rev. Code \xc2\xa726.04.020 (2012)\xe2\x80\x94prohibit the practice). But these rules are in every event consistent within each State.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAgainst this background DOMA rejects the long-established precept that the incidents, benefits, and obligations of marriage are uniform for all married couples within each State, though they may vary, subject to constitutional guarantees, from one State to the next. Despite these considerations, it is unnecessary to decide whether this federal intrusion on state power is a violation of the Constitution because it disrupts the federal balance. The State\xe2\x80\x99s power in defining the marital relation is of central relevance in this case quite apart from principles of federalism. Here the State\xe2\x80\x99s decision to give this class of persons the right to marry conferred upon them a dignity and status of immense import. When the State used its historic and essential authority to define the marital relation in this way, its role and its power in making the decision enhanced the recognition, dignity, and protection of the class in their own community. DOMA, because of its reach and extent, departs from this history and tra- dition of reliance on state law to define marriage. \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[D]is-criminations of an unusual character especially sug- gest careful consideration to determine whether they are obnoxious to the constitutional provision.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Romer v. Evans, \n\n517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 620, \n633 (1996)\n (quoting Louisville Gas & Elec. Co. v. Coleman, \n\n277 U.\xc2\xa0S. 32\n\xe2\x80\x9338 (1928)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Federal Government uses this state-defined class for the opposite purpose\xe2\x80\x94to impose restrictions and dis- abilities. That result requires this Court now to address whether the resulting injury and indignity is a deprivation of an essential part of the liberty protected by the \n \nFifth Amendment. What the State of New York treats as alike the federal law deems unlike by a law designed to injure the same class the State seeks to protect.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn acting first to recognize and then to allow same-sex marriages, New York was responding \xe2\x80\x9cto the initiative of those who [sought] a voice in shaping the destiny of their own times.\xe2\x80\x9d Bond v. United States, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 9). These actions were without doubt a proper exercise of its sovereign authority within our fed- eral system, all in the way that the Framers of the Constitu-tion intended. The dynamics of state government in the federal system are to allow the formation of consensus respecting the way the members of a discrete community treat each other in their daily contact and constant interaction with each other.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe States\xe2\x80\x99 interest in defining and regulating the marital relation, subject to constitutional guarantees, stems from the understanding that marriage is more than a routine classification for purposes of certain statutory benefits. Private, consensual sexual intimacy between two adult persons of the same sex may not be punished by the State, and it can form \xe2\x80\x9cbut one element in a personal bond that is more enduring.\xe2\x80\x9d Lawrence v. Texas, \n\n539 U.\xc2\xa0S. 558, \n567 (2003)\n. By its recognition of the validity of same-sex marriages performed in other jurisdictions and then by authorizing same-sex unions and same-sex marriages, New York sought to give further protection and dignity to that bond. For same-sex couples who wished to be married, the State acted to give their lawful conduct a lawful status. This status is a far-reaching legal acknowledgment of the intimate relationship between two people, a relationship deemed by the State worthy of dignity in the community equal with all other marriages. It reflects both the community\xe2\x80\x99s considered perspective on the historical roots of the institution of marriage and its evolving understanding of the meaning of equality.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIV\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tDOMA seeks to injure the very class New York seeks to protect. By doing so it violates basic due process and equal protection principles applicable to the Federal Government. See U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Amdt. 5; Bolling v. Sharpe, \n\n347 U.\xc2\xa0S. 497 (1954)\n. The Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s guarantee of equality \xe2\x80\x9cmust at the very least mean that a bare con- gressional desire to harm a politically unpopular group cannot\xe2\x80\x9d justify disparate treatment of that group. Depart- ment of Agriculture v. Moreno, \n\n413 U.\xc2\xa0S. 528\n\xe2\x80\x93535 (1973). In determining whether a law is motived by an improper animus or purpose, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[d]iscriminations of an un- usual character\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d especially require careful considera- tion. Supra, at 19 (quoting Romer, supra, at 633). DOMA cannot survive under these principles. The responsibility of the States for the regulation of domestic relations is an important indicator of the substantial societal impact the State\xe2\x80\x99s classifications have in the daily lives and customs of its people. DOMA\xe2\x80\x99s unusual deviation from the usual tradition of recognizing and accepting state definitions of marriage here operates to deprive same-sex couples of the benefits and responsibilities that come with the federal recognition of their marriages. This is strong evidence of a law having the purpose and effect of disapproval of that class. The avowed purpose and practical effect of the law here in question are to impose a disadvantage, a separate status, and so a stigma upon all who enter into same-sex marriages made lawful by the unquestioned authority of the States.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe history of DOMA\xe2\x80\x99s enactment and its own text demonstrate that interference with the equal dignity of same-sex marriages, a dignity conferred by the States in the exercise of their sovereign power, was more than an incidental effect of the federal statute. It was its essence. The House Report announced its conclusion that \xe2\x80\x9cit is both appropriate and necessary for Congress to do what it can to defend the institution of traditional heterosexual marriage. .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. H.\xc2\xa0R. 3396 is appropriately entitled the \xe2\x80\x98Defense of Marriage Act.\xe2\x80\x99 The effort to redefine \xe2\x80\x98marriage\xe2\x80\x99 to extend to homosexual couples is a truly radical proposal that would fundamentally alter the institution of marriage.\xe2\x80\x9d H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 104\xe2\x80\x93664, pp. 12\xe2\x80\x9313 (1996). The House concluded that DOMA expresses \xe2\x80\x9cboth moral disapproval of homosexuality, and a moral conviction that heterosexuality better comports with traditional (especially Judeo-Christian) morality.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 16 (footnote deleted). The stated purpose of the law was to promote an \xe2\x80\x9cinterest in protecting the traditional moral teachings reflected in heterosexual-only marriage laws.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Were there any doubt of this far-reaching purpose, the title of the Act confirms it: The Defense of Marriage.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe arguments put forward by BLAG are just as candid about the congressional purpose to influence or interfere with state sovereign choices about who may be married. As the title and dynamics of the bill indicate, its purpose is to discourage enactment of state same-sex marriage laws and to restrict the freedom and choice of couples married under those laws if they are enacted. The congressional goal was \xe2\x80\x9cto put a thumb on the scales and influence a state\xe2\x80\x99s decision as to how to shape its own marriage laws.\xe2\x80\x9d Massachusetts, 682 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313. The Act\xe2\x80\x99s demonstrated purpose is to ensure that if any State decides to recognize same-sex marriages, those unions will be treated as second-class marriages for purposes of federal law. This raises a most serious question under the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s \n \nFifth Amendment.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tDOMA\xe2\x80\x99s operation in practice confirms this purpose. When New York adopted a law to permit same-sex marriage, it sought to eliminate inequality; but DOMA frustrates that objective through a system-wide enactment with no identified connection to any particular area of fed- eral law. DOMA writes inequality into the entire United States Code. The particular case at hand concerns the estate tax, but DOMA is more than a simple determi- nation of what should or should not be allowed as an estate tax refund. Among the over 1,000 statutes and numerous federal regulations that DOMA controls are laws pertaining to Social Security, housing, taxes, criminal sanctions, copyright, and veterans\xe2\x80\x99 benefits.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tDOMA\xe2\x80\x99s principal effect is to identify a subset of state-sanctioned marriages and make them unequal. The principal purpose is to impose inequality, not for other reasons like governmental efficiency. Responsibilities, as well as rights, enhance the dignity and integrity of the person. And DOMA contrives to deprive some couples married under the laws of their State, but not other couples, of both rights and responsibilities. By creating two contradictory marriage regimes within the same State, DOMA forces same-sex couples to live as married for the purpose of state law but unmarried for the purpose of federal law, thus diminishing the stability and predictability of basic personal relations the State has found it proper to acknowledge and protect. By this dynamic DOMA undermines both the public and private significance of state-sanctioned same-sex marriages; for it tells those couples, and all the world, that their otherwise valid marriages are unworthy of federal recognition. This places same-sex couples in an unstable position of being in a second-tier marriage. The differentiation demeans the couple, whose moral and sexual choices the Constitution protects, see Lawrence, \n\n539 U.\xc2\xa0S. 558\n, and whose relationship the State has sought to dignify. And it humiliates tens of thousands of children now being raised by same-sex couples. The law in question makes it even more difficult for the children to understand the integrity and closeness of their own family and its concord with other families in their community and in their daily lives.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUnder DOMA, same-sex married couples have their lives burdened, by reason of government decree, in visible and public ways. By its great reach, DOMA touches many aspects of married and family life, from the mundane to the profound. It prevents same-sex married couples from obtaining government healthcare benefits they would otherwise receive. See 5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa78901(5), 8905. It deprives them of the Bankruptcy Code\xe2\x80\x99s special protections for domestic-support obligations. See 11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7101(14A), 507(a)(1)(A), 523(a)(5), 523(a)(15). It forces them to follow a complicated procedure to file their state and federal taxes jointly. Technical Bulletin TB\xe2\x80\x9355, 2010 Vt. Tax LEXIS 6 (Oct. 7, 2010); Brief for Federalism Scholars as Amici Curiae 34. It prohibits them from being buried together in veterans\xe2\x80\x99 cemeteries. National Cemetery Administration Directive 3210/1, p. 37 (June 4, 2008).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor certain married couples, DOMA\xe2\x80\x99s unequal effects are even more serious. The federal penal code makes it a crime to \xe2\x80\x9cassaul[t], kidna[p], or murde[r] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a member of the immediate family\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9ca United States official, a United States judge, [or] a Federal law enforcement officer,\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7115(a)(1)(A), with the intent to influence or retaliate against that official, \xc2\xa7115(a)(1). Although a \xe2\x80\x9cspouse\xe2\x80\x9d qualifies as a member of the officer\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cimmediate family,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7115(c)(2), DOMA makes this protection inapplicable to same-sex spouses.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tDOMA also brings financial harm to children of same-sex couples. It raises the cost of health care for families by taxing health benefits provided by employers to their workers\xe2\x80\x99 same-sex spouses. See \n\n26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7106; Treas. Reg. \xc2\xa71.106\xe2\x80\x931, 26 CFR \xc2\xa71.106\xe2\x80\x931 (2012); IRS Private Letter Ruling 9850011 (Sept. 10, 1998). And it denies or re- duces benefits allowed to families upon the loss of a spouse and parent, benefits that are an integral part of family security. See Social Security Administration, Social Security Survivors Benefits 5 (2012) (benefits available to a surviving spouse caring for the couple\xe2\x80\x99s child), online at http://www.ssa.gov/pubs/EN-05-10084.pdf.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tDOMA divests married same-sex couples of the duties and responsibilities that are an essential part of married life and that they in most cases would be honored to accept were DOMA not in force. For instance, because it is expected that spouses will support each other as they pursue educational opportunities, federal law takes into consideration a spouse\xe2\x80\x99s income in calculating a student\xe2\x80\x99s fed- eral financial aid eligibility. See \n\n20 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71087nn(b). Same-sex married couples are exempt from this requirement. The same is true with respect to federal ethics rules. Federal executive and agency officials are prohibited from \xe2\x80\x9cparticipat[ing] personally and substantially\xe2\x80\x9d in matters as to which they or their spouses have a financial interest. \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7208(a). A similar statute prohibits Senators, Senate employees, and their spouses from accepting high-value gifts from certain sources, see \n\n2 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x932(a)(1), and another mandates detailed financial disclosures by numerous high-ranking officials and their spouses. See 5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. App. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7102(a), (e). Under DOMA, however, these Government-integrity rules do not apply to same-sex spouses.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe power the Constitution grants it also restrains. And though Congress has great authority to design laws to fit its own conception of sound national policy, it cannot deny the liberty protected by the Due Process Clause of the \n \nFifth Amendment.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhat has been explained to this point should more than suffice to establish that the principal purpose and the necessary effect of this law are to demean those persons who are in a lawful same-sex marriage. This requires the Court to hold, as it now does, that DOMA is unconstitutional as a deprivation of the liberty of the person protected by the \n \nFifth Amendment of the Constitution.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe liberty protected by the \n \nFifth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Due Process Clause contains within it the prohibition against denying to any person the equal protection of the laws. See Bolling, 347 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 499\xe2\x80\x93500; Adarand Constructors, Inc. v. Pe\xc3\xb1a, \n\n515 U.\xc2\xa0S. 200\n\xe2\x80\x93218 (1995). While the \n \nFifth Amendment itself withdraws from Government the power to degrade or demean in the way this law does, the equal protection guarantee of the \n \nFourteenth Amendment makes that \n \nFifth Amendment right all the more specific and all the better understood and preserved.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe class to which DOMA directs its restrictions and restraints are those persons who are joined in same-sex marriages made lawful by the State. DOMA singles out a class of persons deemed by a State entitled to recognition and protection to enhance their own liberty. It imposes a disability on the class by refusing to acknowledge a status the State finds to be dignified and proper. DOMA instructs all federal officials, and indeed all persons with whom same-sex couples interact, including their own children, that their marriage is less worthy than the marriages of others. The federal statute is invalid, for no legitimate purpose overcomes the purpose and effect to disparage and to injure those whom the State, by its marriage laws, sought to protect in personhood and dignity. By seeking to displace this protection and treating those persons as living in marriages less respected than others, the federal statute is in violation of the \n \nFifth Amendment. This opinion and its holding are confined to those lawful marriages.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis case is about power in several respects. It is about the power of our people to govern themselves, and the power of this Court to pronounce the law. Today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion aggrandizes the latter, with the predictable consequence of diminishing the former. We have no power to decide this case. And even if we did, we have no power under the Constitution to invalidate this democratically adopted leg- islation. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s errors on both points spring forth from the same diseased root: an exalted conception of the role of this institution in America.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court is eager\xe2\x80\x94hungry\xe2\x80\x94to tell everyone its view of the legal question at the heart of this case. Standing in the way is an obstacle, a technicality of little interest to anyone but the people of We the People, who created it as a barrier against judges\xe2\x80\x99 intrusion into their lives. They gave judges, in Article III, only the \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial Power,\xe2\x80\x9d a power to decide not abstract questions but real, concrete \xe2\x80\x9cCases\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cControversies.\xe2\x80\x9d Yet the plaintiff and the Gov- ernment agree entirely on what should happen in this lawsuit. They agree that the court below got it right; and they agreed in the court below that the court below that one got it right as well. What, then, are we doing here?\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe answer lies at the heart of the jurisdictional portion of today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, where a single sentence lays bare the majority\xe2\x80\x99s vision of our role. The Court says that we have the power to decide this case because if we did not, then our \xe2\x80\x9cprimary role in determining the constitutionality of a law\xe2\x80\x9d (at least one that \xe2\x80\x9chas inflicted real injury on a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x9d) would \xe2\x80\x9cbecome only secondary to the President\xe2\x80\x99s.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12. But wait, the reader wonders\xe2\x80\x94Windsor won below, and so cured her injury, and the President was glad to see it. True, says the majority, but judicial review must march on regardless, lest we \xe2\x80\x9cundermine the clear dictate of the separation-of-powers principle that when an Act of Congress is alleged to conflict with the Constitution, it is emphatically the province and duty of the judicial department to say what the law is.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks and brackets omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThat is jaw-dropping. It is an assertion of judicial supremacy over the people\xe2\x80\x99s Representatives in Congress and the Executive. It envisions a Supreme Court standing (or rather enthroned) at the apex of government, empowered to decide all constitutional questions, always and every- where \xe2\x80\x9cprimary\xe2\x80\x9d in its role.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis image of the Court would have been unrecognizable to those who wrote and ratified our national charter. They knew well the dangers of \xe2\x80\x9cprimary\xe2\x80\x9d power, and so created branches of government that would be \xe2\x80\x9cperfectly co-ordinate by the terms of their common commission,\xe2\x80\x9d none of which branches could \xe2\x80\x9cpretend to an exclusive or superior right of settling the boundaries between their respec- tive powers.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist, No. 49, p. 314 (C. Rossiter ed. 1961) (J. Madison). The people did this to protect themselves. They did it to guard their right to self-rule against the black-robed supremacy that today\xe2\x80\x99s majority finds so attractive. So it was that Madison could confidently state, with no fear of contradiction, that there was nothing of \xe2\x80\x9cgreater intrinsic value\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cstamped with the authority of more enlightened patrons of liberty\xe2\x80\x9d than a government of separate and coordinate powers. Id., No. 47, at 301.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor this reason we are quite forbidden to say what the law is whenever (as today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion asserts) \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98an Act of Congress is alleged to conflict with the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12. We can do so only when that allegation will determine the outcome of a lawsuit, and is contradicted by the other party. The \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial Power\xe2\x80\x9d is not, as the major- ity believes, the power \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98to say what the law is,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., giving the Supreme Court the \xe2\x80\x9cprimary role in determining the constitutionality of laws.\xe2\x80\x9d The majority must have in mind one of the foreign constitutions that pronounces such primacy for its constitutional court and allows that primacy to be exercised in contexts other than a lawsuit. See, e.g., Basic Law for the Federal Republic of Germany, Art. 93. The judicial power as Americans have understood it (and their English ancestors before them) is the power to adjudicate, with conclusive effect, disputed govern- ment claims (civil or criminal) against private persons, and disputed claims by private persons against the government or other private persons. Sometimes (though not always) the parties before the court disagree not with regard to the facts of their case (or not only with regard to the facts) but with regard to the applicable law\xe2\x80\x94in which event (and only in which event) it becomes the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98province and duty of the judicial department to say what the law is.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn other words, declaring the compatibility of state or federal laws with the Constitution is not only not the \xe2\x80\x9cprimary role\xe2\x80\x9d of this Court, it is not a separate, free-standing role at all. We perform that role incidentally\xe2\x80\x94by accident, as it were\xe2\x80\x94when that is necessary to resolve the dispute before us. Then, and only then, does it become \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the province and duty of the judicial department to say what the law is.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d That is why, in 1793, we politely declined the Washington Administration\xe2\x80\x99s request to \xe2\x80\x9csay what the law is\xe2\x80\x9d on a particular treaty matter that was not the subject of a concrete legal controversy. 3 Correspondence and Public Papers of John Jay 486\xe2\x80\x93489 (H. Johnston ed. 1893). And that is why, as our opinions have said, some questions of law will never be presented to this Court, because there will never be anyone with standing to bring a lawsuit. See Schlesinger v. Reservists Comm. to Stop the War, \n\n418 U.\xc2\xa0S. 208, \n227 (1974)\n; United States v. Richardson, \n\n418 U.\xc2\xa0S. 166, \n179 (1974)\n. As Justice Bran- deis put it, we cannot \xe2\x80\x9cpass upon the constitutionality of legislation in a friendly, non-adversary, proceeding\xe2\x80\x9d; absent a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98real, earnest and vital controversy between individuals,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d we have neither any work to do nor any power to do it. Ashwander v. TVA, \n\n297 U.\xc2\xa0S. 288, \n346 (1936)\n (concurring opinion) (quoting Chicago & Grand Trunk R. Co. v. Wellman, \n\n143 U.\xc2\xa0S. 339, \n345 (1892)\n). Our authority begins and ends with the need to adjudge the rights of an injured party who stands before us seeking redress. Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife, \n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 555, \n560 (1992)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThat is completely absent here. Windsor\xe2\x80\x99s injury was cured by the judgment in her favor. And while, in ordinary circumstances, the United States is injured by a directive to pay a tax refund, this suit is far from ordinary. Whatever injury the United States has suffered will surely not be redressed by the action that it, as a litigant, asks us to take. The final sentence of the Solicitor General\xe2\x80\x99s brief on the merits reads: \xe2\x80\x9cFor the foregoing reasons, the judgment of the court of appeals should be affirmed.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States (merits) 54 (emphasis added). That will not cure the Government\xe2\x80\x99s injury, but carve it into stone. One could spend many fruitless afternoons ransacking our library for any other petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s brief seeking an affirmance of the judgment against it.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t What the petitioner United States asks us to do in the case before us is exactly what the respondent Windsor asks us to do: not to provide relief from the judgment below but to say that that judgment was correct. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority\xe2\x80\x99s discussion of the requirements of Article III bears no resemblance to our jurisprudence. It accuses the amicus (appointed to argue against our jurisdiction) of \xe2\x80\x9celid[ing] the distinction between .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the jurisdictional requirements of Article III and the prudential limits on its exercise.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 6. It then proceeds to call the requirement of adverseness a \xe2\x80\x9cprudential\xe2\x80\x9d aspect of standing. Of standing. That is incomprehensible. A plaintiff (or appellant) can have all the standing in the world\xe2\x80\x94satisfying all three standing requirements of Lujan that the majority so carefully quotes, ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x94and yet no Article III controversy may be before the court. Article III requires not just a plaintiff (or appellant) who has standing to complain but an opposing party who denies the validity of the complaint. It is not the amicus that has done the eliding of distinctions, but the majority, calling the quite separate Article III requirement of adverseness between the parties an element (which it then pronounces a \xe2\x80\x9cprudential\xe2\x80\x9d element) of standing. The question here is not whether, as the majority puts it, \xe2\x80\x9cthe United States retains a stake sufficient to support Article III jurisdiction,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. the question is whether there is any controversy (which requires contradiction) between the United States and Ms. Windsor. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe authorities the majority cites fall miles short of supporting the counterintuitive notion that an Article III \xe2\x80\x9ccontroversy\xe2\x80\x9d can exist without disagreement between the parties. In Deposit Guaranty Nat. Bank v. Roper, \n\n445 U.\xc2\xa0S. 326 (1980)\n, the District Court had entered judgment in the individual plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s favor based on the defendant bank\xe2\x80\x99s offer to pay the full amount claimed. The \xe2\x80\x9cprudential\xe2\x80\x9d discretion to which both those cases refer was the discretion to deny an appeal even when a live controversy exists\xe2\x80\x94not the discretion to grant one when it does not. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt may be argued that if what we say is true some Presidential determinations that statutes are unconstitutional will not be subject to our review. That is as it should be, when both the President and the plaintiff agree that the statute is unconstitutional. Where the Executive is en- forcing an unconstitutional law, suit will of course lie; but if, in that suit, the Executive admits the unconstitution- ality of the law, the litigation should end in an order or a consent decree enjoining enforcement. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs I have observed before, the Constitution does not forbid the government to enforce traditional moral and sexual norms. See Lawrence v. Texas, \n\n539 U.\xc2\xa0S. 558, \n599 (2003)\n (Scalia, J., dissenting). I will not swell the U.\xc2\xa0S. Reports with restatements of that point. It is enough to say that the Constitution neither requires nor forbids our society to approve of same-sex marriage, much as it neither requires nor forbids us to approve of no-fault divorce, polygamy, or the consumption of alcohol.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tHowever, even setting aside traditional moral disapproval of same-sex marriage (or indeed same-sex sex), there are many perfectly valid\xe2\x80\x94indeed, downright boring\xe2\x80\x94justifying rationales for this legislation. Their existence ought to be the end of this case. For they give the lie to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that only those with hateful hearts could have voted \xe2\x80\x9caye\xe2\x80\x9d on this Act. And more importantly, they serve to make the contents of the legislators\xe2\x80\x99 hearts quite irrelevant: \xe2\x80\x9cIt is a familiar principle of constitutional law that this Court will not strike down an otherwise constitutional statute on the basis of an alleged illicit legislative motive.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. O\xe2\x80\x99Brien, \n\n391 U.\xc2\xa0S. 367, \n383 (1968)\n. Or at least it was a familiar principle. By holding to the contrary, the majority has declared open season on any law that (in the opinion of the law\xe2\x80\x99s opponents and any panel of like-minded federal judges) can be characterized as mean-spirited.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority concludes that the only motive for this Act was the \xe2\x80\x9cbare .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. desire to harm a politically unpopular group.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 20. Bear in mind that the object of this condemnation is not the legislature of some once-Confederate Southern state (familiar objects of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s scorn, see, e.g., Edwards v. Aguillard, \n\n482 U.\xc2\xa0S. 578 (1987)\n), but our respected coordinate branches, the Congress and Presidency of the United States. Laying such a charge against them should require the most extraordinary evidence, and I would have thought that every attempt would be made to indulge a more anodyne explanation for the statute. The majority does the opposite\xe2\x80\x94affirmatively concealing from the reader the arguments that exist in justification. It makes only a passing mention of the \xe2\x80\x9carguments put forward\xe2\x80\x9d by the Act\xe2\x80\x99s defenders, and does not even trouble to paraphrase or describe them. See ante, at 21. I imagine that this is because it is harder to maintain the illusion of the Act\xe2\x80\x99s supporters as unhinged members of a wild-eyed lynch mob when one first describes their views as they see them.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo choose just one of these defenders\xe2\x80\x99 arguments, DOMA avoids difficult choice-of-law issues that will now arise absent a uniform federal definition of marriage. See, e.g., Baude, Beyond DOMA: Choice of State Law in Fed- eral Statutes, 64 Stan. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1371 (2012). Imagine a pair of women who marry in Albany and then move to Alabama, which does not \xe2\x80\x9crecognize as valid any marriage of parties of the same sex.\xe2\x80\x9d Ala. Code \xc2\xa730\xe2\x80\x931\xe2\x80\x9319(e) (2011). When the couple files their next federal tax return, may it be a joint one? Which State\xe2\x80\x99s law controls, for federal-law purposes: their State of celebration (which recognizes the marriage) or their State of domicile (which does not)? (Does the answer depend on whether they were just visiting in Albany?) Are these questions to be answered as a matter of federal common law, or perhaps by borrowing a State\xe2\x80\x99s choice-of-law rules? If so, which State\xe2\x80\x99s? And what about States where the status of an out-of-state same-sex marriage is an unsettled question under local law? See Godfrey v. Spano, 13 N.\xc2\xa0Y. 3d 358, 920 N. E. 2d 328 (2009). DOMA avoided all of this uncertainty by speci- fying which marriages would be recognized for federal purposes. That is a classic purpose for a definitional provision.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFurther, DOMA preserves the intended effects of prior legislation against then-unforeseen changes in circumstance. When Congress provided (for example) that a special estate-tax exemption would exist for spouses, this exemption reached only opposite-sex spouses\xe2\x80\x94those being the only sort that were recognized in any State at the time of DOMA\xe2\x80\x99s passage. When it became clear that changes in state law might one day alter that balance, DOMA\xe2\x80\x99s definitional section was enacted to ensure that state-level experimentation did not automatically alter the basic operation of federal law, unless and until Congress made the further judgment to do so on its own. That is not animus\xe2\x80\x94just stabilizing prudence. Congress has hardly demonstrated itself unwilling to make such further, revising judgments upon due deliberation. See, e.g., Don\xe2\x80\x99t Ask, Don\xe2\x80\x99t Tell Repeal Act of 2010, \n\n124Stat.\n3515.\n\t\t\t

First, Raines dealt with individual Members of Congress and specifically pointed to the individual Members\xe2\x80\x99 lack of institutional endorsement as a sign of their standing problem: \xe2\x80\x9cWe attach some importance to the fact that appellees have not been authorized to represent their respective Houses of Congress in this action, and indeed both Houses actively oppose their suit.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 829; see also ibid., n.\xc2\xa010 (citing cases to the effect that \xe2\x80\x9cmembers of collegial bodies do not have standing to perfect an appeal the body itself has declined to take\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSecond, the Members in Raines\xe2\x80\x94unlike the state senators in Coleman\xe2\x80\x94were not the pivotal figures whose votes would have caused the Act to fail absent some challenged action. Indeed, it is telling that Raines characterized Coleman as standing \xe2\x80\x9cfor the proposition that legislators whose votes would have been sufficient to defeat (or enact) a specific legislative Act have standing to sue if that legislative action goes into effect (or does not go into effect), on the ground that their votes have been completely nullified.\xe2\x80\x9d 521 U. S., at 823. Here, by contrast, passage by the House was needed for DOMA to become law. U. S. Const., Art. I, \xc2\xa77 (bicameralism and presentment requirements for legislation).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI appreciate the argument that the Constitution confers on the President alone the authority to defend federal law in litigation, but in my view, as I have explained, that argument is contrary to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding in Chadha, and it is certainly contrary to the Chadha Court\xe2\x80\x99s endorsement of the principle that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress is the proper party to defend the validity of a statute\xe2\x80\x9d when the Executive refuses to do so on constitutional grounds. 462 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 940. See also \n\n2 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7288h(7) (Senate Legal Counsel shall defend the constitutionality of Acts of Congress when placed in issue).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Accordingly, in the narrow category of cases in which a court strikes down an Act of Congress and the Executive declines to defend the Act, Congress both has standing to defend the undefended statute and is a proper party to do so.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWindsor and the United States argue that \xc2\xa73 of DOMA violates the equal protection principles that the Court has found in the \n \nFifth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Due Process Clause. See Brief for Respondent Windsor (merits) 17\xe2\x80\x9362; Brief for United States (merits) 16\xe2\x80\x9354; cf. Bolling v. Sharpe, \n\n347 U.\xc2\xa0S. 497 (1954)\n. The Court rests its holding on related arguments. See ante, at 24\xe2\x80\x9325.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSame-sex marriage presents a highly emotional and important question of public policy\xe2\x80\x94but not a difficult ques- tion of constitutional law. The Constitution does not \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tguarantee the right to enter into a same-sex marriage. Indeed, no provision of the Constitution speaks to the issue.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court has sometimes found the Due Process Clauses to have a substantive component that guarantees liber- ties beyond the absence of physical restraint. And the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding that \xe2\x80\x9cDOMA is unconstitutional as a dep- rivation of the liberty of the person protected by the \n \nFifth Amendment of the Constitution,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 25, suggests that substantive due process may partially underlie the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision today. But it is well established that any \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive\xe2\x80\x9d component to the Due Process Clause protects only \xe2\x80\x9cthose fundamental rights and lib- erties which are, objectively, \xe2\x80\x98deeply rooted in this Nation\xe2\x80\x99s history and tradition,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Washington v. Glucksberg, \n\n521 U.\xc2\xa0S. 702\n\xe2\x80\x93721 (1997); Snyder v. Massachusetts, \n\n291 U.\xc2\xa0S. 97, \n105 (1934)\n (referring to fundamental rights as those that are so \xe2\x80\x9crooted in the traditions and conscience of our people as to be ranked as fundamental\xe2\x80\x9d), as well as \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98implicit in the concept of ordered liberty,\xe2\x80\x99 such that \xe2\x80\x98neither liberty nor justice would exist if they were sacrificed.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Glucksberg, supra, at 721 (quoting Palko v. Connecticut, \n\n302 U.\xc2\xa0S. 319\n\xe2\x80\x93326 (1937)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is beyond dispute that the right to same-sex marriage is not deeply rooted in this Nation\xe2\x80\x99s history and tradition. In this country, no State permitted same-sex marriage until the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court held in 2003 that limiting marriage to opposite-sex couples violated the State Constitution. See Goodridge v. Department of Public Health, 440 Mass. 309, 798 N.\xc2\xa0E.\xc2\xa02d 941. Nor is the right to same-sex marriage deeply rooted in the traditions of other nations. No country allowed same-sex couples to marry until the Netherlands did so in 2000.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhat Windsor and the United States seek, therefore, is not the protection of a deeply rooted right but the recognition of a very new right, and they seek this innovation not from a legislative body elected by the people, but from unelected judges. Faced with such a request, judges have cause for both caution and humility.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe family is an ancient and universal human institution. Family structure reflects the characteristics of a civilization, and changes in family structure and in the popular understanding of marriage and the family can have profound effects. Past changes in the understand- ing of marriage\xe2\x80\x94for example, the gradual ascendance of the idea that romantic love is a prerequisite to marriage\xe2\x80\x94have had far-reaching consequences. But the process by which such consequences come about is complex, involving the interaction of numerous factors, and tends to occur over an extended period of time.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe can expect something similar to take place if same-sex marriage becomes widely accepted. The long-term consequences of this change are not now known and are unlikely to be ascertainable for some time to come.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t There are those who think that allowing same-sex marriage will seriously undermine the institution of marriage. See, e.g., S. Girgis, R. Anderson, & R. George, What is Marriage? Man and Woman: A Defense 53\xe2\x80\x9358 (2012); Finnis, Marriage: A Basic and Exigent Good, 91 The Monist 388, 398 \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t(2008).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Others think that recognition of same-sex marriage will fortify a now-shaky institution. See, e.g., A. Sullivan, Virtually Normal: An Argument About Homosexuality 202\xe2\x80\x93203 (1996); J. Rauch, Gay Marriage: Why It Is Good for Gays, Good for Straights, and Good for Amer- ica 94 (2004).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAt present, no one\xe2\x80\x94including social scientists, philosophers, and historians\xe2\x80\x94can predict with any certainty what the long-term ramifications of widespread acceptance of same-sex marriage will be. And judges are \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertainly not equipped to make such an assessment. The Members of this Court have the authority and the responsibility to interpret and apply the Constitution. Thus, if the Constitution contained a provision guaranteeing the right to marry a person of the same sex, it would be our duty to enforce that right. But the Constitution simply does not speak to the issue of same-sex marriage. In our system of government, ultimate sovereignty rests with the people, and the people have the right to control their own destiny. Any change on a question so fundamental should be made by the people through their elected officials.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPerhaps because they cannot show that same-sex marriage is a fundamental right under our Constitution, Windsor and the United States couch their arguments in equal protection terms. They argue that \xc2\xa73 of DOMA discriminates on the basis of sexual orientation, that classifications based on sexual orientation should trigger a form of \xe2\x80\x9cheightened\xe2\x80\x9d scrutiny, and that \xc2\xa73 cannot survive such scrutiny. They further maintain that the governmental interests that \xc2\xa73 purports to serve are not sufficiently important and that it has not been adequately shown that \xc2\xa73 serves those interests very well. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding, too, seems to rest on \xe2\x80\x9cthe equal protection guarantee of the \n \nFourteenth Amendment,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 25\xe2\x80\x94although the Court is careful not to adopt most of Windsor\xe2\x80\x99s and the United States\xe2\x80\x99 argument.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn my view, the approach that Windsor and the United States advocate is misguided. Our equal protection frame- work, upon which Windsor and the United States rely, is a judicial construct that provides a useful mechanism for analyzing a certain universe of equal protection cases. But that framework is ill suited for use in evaluating the constitutionality of laws based on the traditional understanding of marriage, which fundamentally turn on what marriage is.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUnderlying our equal protection jurisprudence is the central notion that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] classification \xe2\x80\x98must be reasonable, not arbitrary, and must rest upon some ground of difference having a fair and substantial relation to the object of the legislation, so that all persons similarly circumstanced shall be treated alike.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Reed v. Reed, \n\n404 U.\xc2\xa0S. 71, \n76 (1971)\n (quoting F. S. Royter Guano Co. v. Virginia, \n\n253 U.\xc2\xa0S. 412, \n415 (1920)\n). The modern tiers of scrutiny\xe2\x80\x94on which Windsor and the United States rely so heavily\xe2\x80\x94are a heuristic to help judges determine when classifications have that \xe2\x80\x9cfair and substantial relation to the object of the legislation.\xe2\x80\x9d Reed, supra, at 76.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSo, for example, those classifications subject to strict scrutiny\xe2\x80\x94i.e., classifications that must be \xe2\x80\x9cnarrowly tailored\xe2\x80\x9d to achieve a \xe2\x80\x9ccompelling\xe2\x80\x9d government interest, Parents Involved in Community Schools v. Seattle School Dist. No. 1, \n\n551 U.\xc2\xa0S. 701, \n720 (2007)\n (internal quotation marks omitted)\xe2\x80\x94are those that are \xe2\x80\x9cso seldom relevant to the achievement of any legitimate state interest that laws grounded in such considerations are deemed to reflect prejudice and antipathy.\xe2\x80\x9d Cleburne v. Cleburne Living Center, Inc., \n\n473 U.\xc2\xa0S. 432, \n440 (1985)\n; cf. id., at 452\xe2\x80\x93453 (Stevens, J., concurring) (\xe2\x80\x9cIt would be utterly irrational to limit the franchise on the basis of height or weight; it is equally invalid to limit it on the basis of skin color. None of these attributes has any bearing at all on the citizen\xe2\x80\x99s willingness or ability to exercise that civil right\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn contrast, those characteristics subject to so-called intermediate scrutiny\xe2\x80\x94i.e., those classifications that must be \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98substantially related\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to the achievement of \xe2\x80\x9cim- portant governmental objective[s],\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Virginia, \n\n518 U.\xc2\xa0S. 515, \n524 (1996)\n; id., at 567 (Scalia, J., dissenting)\xe2\x80\x94are those that are sometimes relevant considerations to be taken into account by legislators, but \xe2\x80\x9cgenerally provid[e] no sensible ground for different treatment,\xe2\x80\x9d Cleburne, supra, at 440. For example, the Court has held that statutory rape laws that criminalize sexual intercourse with a woman under the age of 18 years, but place no similar liability on partners of underage men, are grounded in the very real distinction that \xe2\x80\x9cyoung men and young women are not similarly situated with respect to the problems and the risks of sexual intercourse.\xe2\x80\x9d Michael M. v. Superior Court, Sonoma Cty., \n\n450 U.\xc2\xa0S. 464, \n471 (1981)\n (plurality opnion). The plurality reasoned that \xe2\x80\x9c[o]nly women may become pregnant, and they suffer disproportionately the profound physical, emotional, and psychological consequences of sexual activity.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. In other contexts, however, the Court has found that classifications based on gender are \xe2\x80\x9carbitrary,\xe2\x80\x9d Reed, supra, at 76, and based on \xe2\x80\x9coutmoded notions of the relative capabilities of men and women,\xe2\x80\x9d Cleburne, supra, at 441, as when a State provides that a man must always be preferred to an equally qualified woman when both seek to administer the estate of a deceased party, see Reed, supra, at 76\xe2\x80\x9377.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, so-called rational-basis review applies to classifications based on \xe2\x80\x9cdistinguishing characteristics relevant to interests the State has the authority to implement.\xe2\x80\x9d Cleburne, supra, at 441. We have long recognized that \xe2\x80\x9cthe equal protection of the laws must coexist with the practical necessity that most legislation classifies for one purpose or another, with resulting disadvantages to various groups or persons.\xe2\x80\x9d Romer v. Evans, \n\n517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 620, \n631 (1996)\n. As a result, in rational-basis cases, where the court does not view the classification at issue as \xe2\x80\x9cinher- ently suspect,\xe2\x80\x9d Adarand Constructors, Inc. v. Pe\xc3\xb1a, \n\n515 U.\xc2\xa0S. 200, \n218 (1995)\n (internal quotation marks omitted), \xe2\x80\x9cthe courts have been very reluctant, as they should be in our federal system and with our respect for the separation of powers, to closely scrutinize legislative choices as to whether, how, and to what extent those interests should be pursued.\xe2\x80\x9d Cleburne, supra, at 441\xe2\x80\x93442.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn asking the Court to determine that \xc2\xa73 of DOMA is subject to and violates heightened scrutiny, Windsor and the United States thus ask us to rule that the presence of two members of the opposite sex is as rationally related to marriage as white skin is to voting or a Y-chromosome is to the ability to administer an estate. That is a striking request and one that unelected judges should pause before granting. Acceptance of the argument would cast all those who cling to traditional beliefs about the nature of marriage in the role of bigots or superstitious fools.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBy asking the Court to strike down DOMA as not satisfying some form of heightened scrutiny, Windsor and the United States are really seeking to have the Court resolve a debate between two competing views of marriage.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe first and older view, which I will call the \xe2\x80\x9ctradi- tional\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cconjugal\xe2\x80\x9d view, sees marriage as an intrinsically opposite-sex institution. BLAG notes that virtually every culture, including many not influenced by the Abrahamic religions, has limited marriage to people of the opposite sex. Brief for Respondent BLAG (merits) 2 (citing Hernandez v. Robles, 7 N.\xc2\xa0Y. 3d 338, 361, 855 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 1, 8 (2006) (\xe2\x80\x9cUntil a few decades ago, it was an accepted truth for almost everyone who ever lived, in any society in which marriage existed, that there could be marriages only between participants of different sex\xe2\x80\x9d)). And BLAG attempts to explain this phenomenon by arguing that the institution of marriage was created for the purpose of channeling heterosexual intercourse into a structure that supports child rearing. Brief for Respondent BLAG 44\xe2\x80\x9346, 49. Others explain the basis for the institution in more philosophical terms. They argue that marriage is essentially the solemnizing of a comprehensive, exclusive, permanent union that is intrinsically ordered to producing new life, even if it does not always do so. See, e.g., Girgis, Anderson, & George, What is Marriage? Man and Woman: A Defense, at 23\xe2\x80\x9328. While modern cultural changes have weakened the link between marriage and procreation in the popular mind, there is no doubt that, throughout human history and across many cultures, marriage has been viewed as an exclusively opposite-sex institution and as one inextricably linked to procreation and biological kinship.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe other, newer view is what I will call the \xe2\x80\x9cconsent-based\xe2\x80\x9d vision of marriage, a vision that primarily defines marriage as the solemnization of mutual commitment\xe2\x80\x94marked by strong emotional attachment and sexual attraction\xe2\x80\x94between two persons. At least as it applies to heterosexual couples, this view of marriage now plays a very prominent role in the popular understanding of the institution. Indeed, our popular culture is infused with this understanding of marriage. Proponents of same-sex marriage argue that because gender differentiation is not relevant to this vision, the exclusion of same-sex couples from the institution of marriage is rank discrimination.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Constitution does not codify either of these views of marriage (although I suspect it would have been hard at the time of the adoption of the Constitution or the \n \nFifth Amendment to find Americans who did not take the traditional view for granted). The silence of the Constitution on this question should be enough to end the matter as far as the judiciary is concerned. Yet, Windsor and the United States implicitly ask us to endorse the consent-based view of marriage and to reject the traditional view, thereby arrogating to ourselves the power to decide a question that philosophers, historians, social scientists, and theologians are better qualified to explore.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t7\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Because our consti- tutional order assigns the resolution of questions of this nature to the people, I would not presume to en- shrine either vision of marriage in our constitutional jurisprudence.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tLegislatures, however, have little choice but to decide between the two views. We have long made clear that neither the political branches of the Federal Government nor state governments are required to be neutral between competing visions of the good, provided that the vision of the good that they adopt is not countermanded by the Constitution. See, e.g., Rust v. Sullivan, \n\n500 U.\xc2\xa0S. 173, \n192 (1991)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he government \xe2\x80\x98may make a value judgment favoring childbirth over abortion\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Maher v. Rue, \n\n432 U.\xc2\xa0S. 464, \n474 (1977)\n)). Accordingly, both Congress and the States are entitled to enact laws recognizing either of the two understandings of marriage. And given the size of government and the degree to which it now regulates daily life, it seems unlikely that either Congress or the States could maintain complete neutrality even if they tried assiduously to do so.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRather than fully embracing the arguments made by Windsor and the United States, the Court strikes down \xc2\xa73 of DOMA as a classification not properly supported by its objectives. The Court reaches this conclusion in part because it believes that \xc2\xa73 encroaches upon the States\xe2\x80\x99 sovereign prerogative to define marriage. See ante, at 21\xe2\x80\x9322 (\xe2\x80\x9cAs the title and dynamics of the bill indicate, its purpose is to discourage enactment of state same-sex marriage laws and to restrict the freedom and choice of couples married under those laws if they are enacted. The congressional goal was \xe2\x80\x98to put a thumb on the scales and influence a state\xe2\x80\x99s decision as to how to shape its own marriage laws\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Massachusetts v. United States Dept. of Health and Human Servs., 682 F.\xc2\xa03d 1, 12\xe2\x80\x9313 (CA1 2012))). Indeed, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s ultimate conclusion is that DOMA falls afoul of the \n \nFifth Amendment because it \xe2\x80\x9csingles out a class of persons deemed by a State entitled to recognition and protection to enhance their own liberty\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cimposes a disability on the class by refusing to acknowledge a status the State finds to be dignified and proper.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 25 (emphasis added).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo the extent that the Court takes the position that the question of same-sex marriage should be resolved primar- ily at the state level, I wholeheartedly agree. I hope that the Court will ultimately permit the people of each State to decide this question for themselves. Unless the Court is willing to allow this to occur, the whiffs of federalism in the today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion of the Court will soon be scattered to the wind.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn any event, \xc2\xa73 of DOMA, in my view, does not encroach on the prerogatives of the States, assuming of course that the many federal statutes affected by DOMA have not already done so. Section 3 does not prevent any State from recognizing same-sex marriage or from extending to same-sex couples any right, privilege, benefit, or obligation stemming from state law. All that \xc2\xa73 does is to define a class of persons to whom federal law extends cer- tain special benefits and upon whom federal law imposes certain special burdens. In these provisions, Congress used marital status as a way of defining this class\xe2\x80\x94in part, I assume, because it viewed marriage as a valua- ble institution to be fostered and in part because it viewed married couples as comprising a unique type of economic unit that merits special regulatory treatment. Assuming that Congress has the power under the Constitution to enact the laws affected by \xc2\xa73, Congress has the power to define the category of persons to whom those laws apply.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor these reasons, I would hold that \xc2\xa73 of DOMA does not violate the \n \nFifth Amendment. I respectfully dissent.\n\t\t\t
