\n\t\t\t\tSyllabus\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tCHAFIN v. CHAFIN\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 11\xe2\x80\x931347.\xe2\x80\x83Argued December 5, 2012\xe2\x80\x94Decided February 19, 2013\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe Hague Convention on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction requires the judicial or administrative authority of a Contracting State to order a child returned to her country of habitual residence if the authority finds that the child has been wrongfully removed to or retained in the Contracting State. The International Child Abduction Remedies Act (ICARA) implements the Convention in the United States, granting federal and state courts concurrent jurisdiction over Convention actions and directing those courts to decide cases in accordance with the Convention. ICARA also requires defendants to pay various expenses incurred by plaintiffs associated with the return of children.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioner Mr. Chafin, a United States citizen and member of the military, married respondent Ms. Chafin, a United Kingdom citizen, in Germany, where they later had a daughter, E.\xc2\xa0C. When Mr. Chafin was deployed to Afghanistan, Ms. Chafin took E.\xc2\xa0C. to Scotland. Mr. Chafin was later transferred to Huntsville, Alabama, and Ms. Chafin eventually traveled there with E.\xc2\xa0C. Soon after Ms. Chafin\xe2\x80\x99s arrival, Mr. Chafin filed for divorce and child custody in Alabama. Ms. Chafin was subsequently deported, but E.\xc2\xa0C. remained in Alabama with Mr. Chafin. Several months later, Ms. Chafin filed a petition under the Convention and ICARA, seeking E.\xc2\xa0C.\xe2\x80\x99s return to Scotland. The District Court concluded that E.\xc2\xa0C.\xe2\x80\x99s country of habitual residence was Scotland and granted the petition for return. Ms. Chafin immediately departed for Scotland with E.\xc2\xa0C. Ms. Chafin then initiated custody proceedings in Scotland and was granted interim custody and a preliminary injunction prohibiting Mr. Chafin from removing E.\xc2\xa0C. from Scotland. Mr. Chafin appealed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order, but the Eleventh Circuit dismissed the appeal as moot, on the ground that once a child has been returned to a foreign country, a U.\xc2\xa0S. court becomes powerless to grant relief. On remand, the District Court ordered Mr. Chafin to reimburse Ms. Chafin for court costs, attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees, and travel expenses.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0The return of a child to a foreign country pursuant to a Convention return order does not render an appeal of that order moot. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9314.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0Article III restricts the power of federal courts to \xe2\x80\x9cCases\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cControversies,\xe2\x80\x9d and this \xe2\x80\x9crequirement subsists through all stages of [the] proceedings,\xe2\x80\x9d Lewis v. Continental Bank Corp., \n\n494 U.\xc2\xa0S. 472\n. No case or controversy exists, and a suit becomes moot, \xe2\x80\x9cwhen the issues presented are no longer \xe2\x80\x98live\xe2\x80\x99 or the parties lack a legally cognizable interest in the outcome,\xe2\x80\x9d Already, LLC v. Nike, Inc., 568 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. But a case \xe2\x80\x9cbecomes moot only when it is impossible for a court to grant any effectual relief whatever to the prevailing party,\xe2\x80\x9d Knox v. Service Employees, 567 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. As \xe2\x80\x9clong as the parties have a concrete interest, however small, in the outcome of the litigation, the case is not moot,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x936.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0Because the Chafins continue to vigorously contest the question of where their daughter will be raised, this dispute is very much alive. This case does not address \xe2\x80\x9ca hypothetical state of facts,\xe2\x80\x9d Lewis, supra, at 477, and there continues to exist between the parties \xe2\x80\x9cthat concrete adverseness which sharpens the presentation of issues,\xe2\x80\x9d Camreta v. Greene, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9311. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(1)\xc2\xa0Mr. Chafin seeks typical appellate relief: reversal of the District Court determination that E.\xc2\xa0C.\xe2\x80\x99s habitual residence was Scotland and, upon reversal, an order that E.\xc2\xa0C. be returned to the United States. The question is whether such relief would be effectual. In arguing that this case is moot because the District Court has no authority to issue a re-return order either under the Convention or pursuant to its inherent equitable powers, Ms. Chafin confuses mootness with the merits. See, e.g., Powell v. McCormack, \n\n395 U.\xc2\xa0S. 486\n. Mr. Chafin\xe2\x80\x99s claim for re-return cannot be dismissed as so implausible that it is insufficient to preserve jurisdiction, and his prospects of success are therefore not pertinent to the mootness inquiry. As to the effectiveness of any relief, even if Scotland were to ignore a re-return order, this case would not be moot. The U.\xc2\xa0S. courts continue to have personal jurisdiction over Ms. Chafin and may command her to take action under threat of sanctions. She could decide to comply with an order against her and return E.\xc2\xa0C. to the United States. Enforcement of the order may be uncertain if Ms. Chafin chooses to defy it, but such uncertainty does not typically render cases moot. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9310.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(2)\xc2\xa0Mr. Chafin also seeks, if he prevails, vacatur of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s expense orders. That too is common relief on appeal, and the mootness inquiry comes down to its effectiveness. In contending that this case is moot due to Mr. Chafin\xe2\x80\x99s failure to pursue an appeal of the expense orders, which were entered as separate judgments, Ms. Chafin again confuses mootness with the merits. Because there is authority for the proposition that failure to appeal such judgments separately does not preclude relief, it is for lower courts at later stages of the litigation to decide whether Mr. Chafin is in fact entitled to the relief he seeks. That relief would not be \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98fully satisfactory,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d but \xe2\x80\x9ceven the availability of a \xe2\x80\x98partial remedy\xe2\x80\x99 is \xe2\x80\x98sufficient to prevent [a] case from being moot,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Calderon v. Moore, \n\n518 U.\xc2\xa0S. 149\n. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9311.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0Manipulating constitutional doctrine and holding these cases moot is not necessary to achieve the ends of the Convention and ICARA, and may undermine the treaty\xe2\x80\x99s goals and harm the children meant to be protected. If these cases were to become moot upon return, courts would be more likely to grant stays as a matter of course, to prevent the loss of any right to appeal. Such routine stays would conflict with the Convention\xe2\x80\x99s mandate of prompt return. Courts should instead apply traditional factors in considering whether to stay a return order, see, e.g., Nken v. Holder, \n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 418\n, thus ensuring that each case will receive the individualized treatment necessary for appropriate consideration of the child\xe2\x80\x99s best interests. Finally, at both the district and appellate court level, courts should take steps to decide these cases as expeditiously as possible. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9314.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tVacated and remanded.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tJEFFREY LEE CHAFIN, PETITIONER v. LYNNE HALES CHAFIN\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\ton writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tChief Justice Roberts delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Hague Convention on the Civil Aspects of Inter- national Child Abduction generally requires courts in the United States to order children returned to their countries of habitual residence, if the courts find that the children have been wrongfully removed to or retained in the United States. The question is whether, after a child is returned pursuant to such an order, any appeal of the order is moot.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Hague Conference on Private International Law adopted the Hague Convention on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction in 1980. T. I. A. S. No. 11670, S. Treaty Doc. No. 99\xe2\x80\x9311. In 1988, the United States ratified the treaty and passed implementing legislation, known as the International Child Abduction Remedies Act (ICARA), \n\n102Stat.\n437, \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa711601 et\xc2\xa0seq. See generally Abbott v. Abbott, 560 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2010) (slip op., at 4\xe2\x80\x935).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Convention seeks \xe2\x80\x9cto secure the prompt return of children wrongfully removed to or retained in any Contracting State\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cto ensure that rights of custody and of access under the law of one Contracting State are ef- fectively respected in the other Contracting States.\xe2\x80\x9d Art. 1, S. Treaty Doc. No. 99\xe2\x80\x9311, at 7. Article 3 of the Convention provides that the \xe2\x80\x9cremoval or the retention of a child is to be considered wrongful\xe2\x80\x9d when \xe2\x80\x9cit is in breach of rights of custody attributed to a person, an institution or any other body, either jointly or alone, under the law of the State in which the child was habitually resident immediately before the removal or retention\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cat the time of removal or retention those rights were actually exercised, either jointly or alone, or would have been so exercised but for the removal or retention.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tArticle 12 then states:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cWhere a child has been wrongfully removed or retained in terms of Article 3 and, at the date of the commencement of the proceedings before the judicial or administrative authority of the Contracting State where the child is, a period of less than one year has elapsed from the date of the wrongful removal or retention, the authority concerned shall order the return of the child forthwith.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 9.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThere are several exceptions to that command. Return is not required if the parent seeking it was not exercising custody rights at the time of removal or had consented to removal, if there is a \xe2\x80\x9cgrave risk\xe2\x80\x9d that return will result in harm, if the child is mature and objects to return, or if return would conflict with fundamental principles of freedom and human rights in the state from which return is requested. Arts. 13, 20, id., at 10, 11. Finally, the Convention directs Contracting States to \xe2\x80\x9cdesignate a Central Authority to discharge the duties which are imposed by the Convention.\xe2\x80\x9d Art. 6, id., at 8; see also Art. 7, ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress established procedures for implementing the Convention in ICARA. See \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa711601(b)(1). The Act grants federal and state courts concurrent jurisdiction over actions arising under the Convention, \xc2\xa711603(a), and directs them to \xe2\x80\x9cdecide the case in accordance with the Convention,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa711603(d). If those courts find children to have been wrongfully removed or retained, the children \xe2\x80\x9care to be promptly returned.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa711601(a)(4). ICARA also provides that courts ordering children returned generally must require defendants to pay various expenses incurred by plaintiffs, including court costs, legal fees, and transportation costs associated with the return of the children. \xc2\xa711607(b)(3). ICARA instructs the President to designate the U.\xc2\xa0S. Central Authority, \xc2\xa711606(a), and the President has designated the Office of Children\xe2\x80\x99s Issues in the State Department\xe2\x80\x99s Bureau of Consular Affairs, 22 CFR \xc2\xa794.2 (2012).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEighty-nine nations are party to the Convention as of this writing. Hague Conference on Private Int\xe2\x80\x99l Law, Status Table, Convention of 25 October 1980 on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction, http:// www.hcch.net. In the 2009 fiscal year, 324 children removed to or retained in other countries were returned to the United States under the Convention, while 154 children removed to or retained in the United States were returned to their countries of habitual residence. Dept. of State, Report on Compliance with the Hague Convention on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction 6 (2010).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioner Jeffrey Lee Chafin is a citizen of the United States and a sergeant first class in the U.\xc2\xa0S. Army. While stationed in Germany in 2006, he married respondent Lynne Hales Chafin, a citizen of the United Kingdom. Their daughter E.\xc2\xa0C. was born the following year.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tLater in 2007, Mr. Chafin was deployed to Afghanistan, and Ms. Chafin took E.\xc2\xa0C. to Scotland. Mr. Chafin was eventually transferred to Huntsville, Alabama, and in February 2010, Ms. Chafin traveled to Alabama with E.\xc2\xa0C. Soon thereafter, however, Mr. Chafin filed for divorce and for child custody in Alabama state court. Towards the end of the year, Ms. Chafin was arrested for domestic violence, an incident that alerted U.\xc2\xa0S. Citizenship and Immigration Services to the fact that she had overstayed her visa. She was deported in February 2011, and E.\xc2\xa0C. remained in Mr. Chafin\xe2\x80\x99s care for several more months.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn May 2011, Ms. Chafin initiated this case in the U.\xc2\xa0S. District Court for the Northern District of Alabama. She filed a petition under the Convention and ICARA seeking an order for E.\xc2\xa0C.\xe2\x80\x99s return to Scotland. On October 11 and 12, 2011, the District Court held a bench trial. Upon the close of arguments, the court ruled in favor of Ms. Chafin, concluding that E.\xc2\xa0C.\xe2\x80\x99s country of habitual residence was Scotland and granting the petition for return. Mr. Chafin immediately moved for a stay pending appeal, but the court denied his request. Within hours, Ms. Chafin left the country with E.\xc2\xa0C., headed for Scotland. By December 2011, she had initiated custody proceedings there. The Scottish court soon granted her interim custody and a preliminary injunction, prohibiting Mr. Chafin from removing E.\xc2\xa0C. from Scotland. In the meantime, Mr. Chafin had appealed the District Court order to the Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn February 2012, the Eleventh Circuit dismissed Mr. Chafin\xe2\x80\x99s appeal as moot in a one-paragraph order, citing Bekier v. Bekier, 248 F.\xc2\xa03d 1051 (2001). App. to Pet. for Cert. 1\xe2\x80\x932. In Bekier, the Eleventh Circuit had concluded that an appeal of a Convention return order was moot when the child had been returned to the foreign country, because the court \xe2\x80\x9cbecame powerless\xe2\x80\x9d to grant relief. 248 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1055. In accordance with Bekier, the Court of Appeals remanded this case to the District Court with instructions to dismiss the suit as moot and vacate its order.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOn remand, the District Court did so, and also ordered Mr. Chafin to pay Ms. Chafin over $94,000 in court costs, attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees, and travel expenses. Meanwhile, the Alabama state court had dismissed the child custody proceeding initiated by Mr. Chafin for lack of jurisdiction. The Alabama Court of Civil Appeals affirmed, relying in part on the U.\xc2\xa0S. District Court\xe2\x80\x99s finding that the child\xe2\x80\x99s habitual residence was not Alabama, but Scotland.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe granted certiorari to review the judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit. 567 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tArticle III of the Constitution restricts the power of federal courts to \xe2\x80\x9cCases\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cControversies.\xe2\x80\x9d Accordingly, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o invoke the jurisdiction of a federal court, a litigant must have suffered, or be threatened with, an actual injury traceable to the defendant and likely to be redressed by a favorable judicial decision.\xe2\x80\x9d Lewis v. Continental Bank Corp., \n\n494 U.\xc2\xa0S. 472, \n477 (1990)\n. Federal courts may not \xe2\x80\x9cdecide questions that cannot affect the rights of litigants in the case before them\xe2\x80\x9d or give \xe2\x80\x9copinion[s] advising what the law would be upon a hypothetical state of facts.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting North Carolina v. Rice, \n\n404 U.\xc2\xa0S. 244, \n246 (1971)\n (per curiam); internal quotation marks omitted). The \xe2\x80\x9ccase-or-controversy requirement subsists through all stages of federal judicial proceedings, trial and appellate.\xe2\x80\x9d Lewis, 494 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 477. \xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is not enough that a dispute was very much alive when suit was filed\xe2\x80\x9d; the parties must \xe2\x80\x9ccontinue to have a \xe2\x80\x98personal stake\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in the ultimate disposition of the lawsuit. Id., at 477\xe2\x80\x93478 (quoting Los Angeles v. Lyons, \n\n461 U.\xc2\xa0S. 95, \n101 (1983)\n; some internal quotation marks omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThere is thus no case or controversy, and a suit becomes moot, \xe2\x80\x9cwhen the issues presented are no longer \xe2\x80\x98live\xe2\x80\x99 or the parties lack a legally cognizable interest in the outcome.\xe2\x80\x9d Already, LLC v. Nike, Inc., 568 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2013) (slip op., at 4) (quoting Murphy v. Hunt, \n\n455 U.\xc2\xa0S. 478, \n481 (1982)\n (per curiam); some internal quotation marks omitted). But a case \xe2\x80\x9cbecomes moot only when it is impossible for a court to grant any effectual relief whatever to the prevailing party.\xe2\x80\x9d Knox v. Service Employees, 567 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 7) (internal quotation marks omitted); see also Church of Scientology of Cal. v. United States, \n\n506 U.\xc2\xa0S. 9, \n12 (1992)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cif an event occurs while a case is pending on appeal that makes it impossible for the court to grant \xe2\x80\x98any effectual relief whatever\xe2\x80\x99 to a prevailing party, the appeal must be dismissed\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Mills v. Green, \n\n159 U.\xc2\xa0S. 651, \n653 (1895)\n)). \xe2\x80\x9cAs long as the parties have a concrete interest, however small, in the outcome of the litigation, the case is not moot.\xe2\x80\x9d Knox, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 7) (internal quotation marks and brackets omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis dispute is still very much alive. Mr. Chafin con- tinues to contend that his daughter\xe2\x80\x99s country of habitual residence is the United States, while Ms. Chafin maintains that E.\xc2\xa0C.\xe2\x80\x99s home is in Scotland. Mr. Chafin also argues that even if E.\xc2\xa0C.\xe2\x80\x99s habitual residence was Scotland, she should not have been returned because the Convention\xe2\x80\x99s defenses to return apply. Mr. Chafin seeks custody of E.\xc2\xa0C., and wants to pursue that relief in the United States, while Ms. Chafin is pursuing that right for herself in Scotland. And Mr. Chafin wants the orders that he pay Ms. Chafin over $94,000 vacated, while Ms. Chafin asserts the money is rightfully owed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOn many levels, the Chafins continue to vigorously contest the question of where their daughter will be raised. This is not a case where a decision would address \xe2\x80\x9ca hypothetical state of facts.\xe2\x80\x9d Lewis, supra, at 477 (quoting Rice, supra, at 246; internal quotation marks omitted). And there is not the slightest doubt that there continues to exist between the parties \xe2\x80\x9cthat concrete adverseness which sharpens the presentation of issues.\xe2\x80\x9d Camreta v. Greene, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 5) (quoting Lyons, supra, at 101; internal quotations marks omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAt this point in the ongoing dispute, Mr. Chafin seeks reversal of the District Court determination that E.\xc2\xa0C.\xe2\x80\x99s habitual residence was Scotland and, if that determination is reversed, an order that E.\xc2\xa0C. be returned to the United States (or \xe2\x80\x9cre-return,\xe2\x80\x9d as the parties have put it). In short, Mr. Chafin is asking for typical appellate relief: that the Court of Appeals reverse the District Court and that the District Court undo what it has done. See Arkadelphia Milling Co. v. St. Louis Southwestern R. Co., \n\n249 U.\xc2\xa0S. 134\n\xe2\x80\x93146 (1919); Northwestern Fuel Co. v. Brock, \n\n139 U.\xc2\xa0S. 216, \n219 (1891)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cJurisdiction to correct what had been wrongfully done must remain with the court so long as the parties and the case are properly before it, either in the first instance or when remanded to it by an appellate tribunal\xe2\x80\x9d). The question is whether such relief would be effectual in this case.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMs. Chafin argues that this case is moot because the District Court lacks the authority to issue a re-return order either under the Convention or pursuant to its in- herent equitable powers. But that argument\xe2\x80\x94which goes to the meaning of the Convention and the legal availability of a certain kind of relief\xe2\x80\x94confuses mootness with the merits. In Powell v. McCormack, \n\n395 U.\xc2\xa0S. 486 (1969)\n, this Court held that a claim for backpay saved the case from mootness, even though the defendants argued that the backpay claim had been brought in the wrong court and therefore could not result in relief. As the Court explained, \xe2\x80\x9cthis argument .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. confuses mootness with whether [the plaintiff] has established a right to recover .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. , a question which it is inappropriate to treat at this stage of the litigation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 500. Mr. Chafin\xe2\x80\x99s claim for re-return\xe2\x80\x94under the Convention itself or according to general equitable principles\xe2\x80\x94cannot be dismissed as so implausible that it is insufficient to preserve jurisdiction, see Steel Co. v. Citizens for Better Environment, \n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 83, \n89 (1998)\n, and his prospects of success are therefore not pertinent to the mootness inquiry.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs to the effectiveness of any relief, Ms. Chafin asserts that even if the habitual residence ruling were reversed and the District Court were to issue a re-return order, that relief would be ineffectual because Scotland would simply ignore it.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t But even if Scotland were to ignore a U.\xc2\xa0S. re-return order, or decline to assist in enforcing it, this case would not be moot. The U.\xc2\xa0S. courts continue to have personal jurisdiction over Ms. Chafin, may command her to take action even outside the United States, and may back up any such command with sanctions. See Steele v. Bulova Watch Co., \n\n344 U.\xc2\xa0S. 280, \n289 (1952)\n; cf. Leman v. Krentler-Arnold Hinge Last Co., \n\n284 U.\xc2\xa0S. 448\n\xe2\x80\x93452 (1932). No law of physics prevents E.\xc2\xa0C.\xe2\x80\x99s return from Scotland, see Fawcett v. McRoberts, 326 F.\xc2\xa03d 491, 496 (CA4 2003), abrogated on other grounds by Abbott v. Abbott, 560 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2010), and Ms. Chafin might decide to comply with an order against her and return E.\xc2\xa0C. to the United States, see, e.g., Larbie v. Larbie, 690 F.\xc2\xa03d 295, 303\xe2\x80\x93304 (CA5 2012) (mother who had taken child to United Kingdom complied with Texas court sanctions order and order to return child to United States for trial), cert. pending, No. 12\xe2\x80\x93304.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t After all, the consequence of compliance presumably would not be relinquishment of custody rights, but simply custody proceedings in a different forum.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEnforcement of the order may be uncertain if Ms. Cha- fin chooses to defy it, but such uncertainty does not typically render cases moot. Courts often adjudicate disputes where the practical impact of any decision is not assured. For example, courts issue default judgments against defendants who failed to appear or participate in the proceedings and therefore seem less likely to comply. See Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 55. Similarly, the fact that a defendant is insolvent does not moot a claim for damages. See 13C C. Wright, A. Miller, & E. Cooper, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa73533.3, p. 3 (3d ed. 2008) (cases not moot \xe2\x80\x9ceven though the defendant does not seem able to pay any portion of the damages claimed\xe2\x80\x9d). Courts also decide cases against foreign nations, whose choices to respect final rulings are not guaranteed. See, e.g., Republic of Austria v. Altmann, \n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 677 (2004)\n (suit against Austria for return of paintings); Republic of Argentina v. Weltover, Inc., \n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 607 (1992)\n (suit against Argentina for repayment of bonds). And we have heard the Government\xe2\x80\x99s appeal from the reversal of a conviction, even though the defendants had been deported, reducing the practical impact of any decision; we concluded that the case was not moot because the defendants might \xe2\x80\x9cre-enter this country on their own\xe2\x80\x9d and encounter the consequences of our ruling. United States v. Villamonte-Marquez, \n\n462 U.\xc2\xa0S. 579, \n581, n. \n2 (1983)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSo too here. A re-return order may not result in the return of E.\xc2\xa0C. to the United States, just as an order that an insolvent defendant pay $100 million may not make the plaintiff rich. But it cannot be said that the parties here have no \xe2\x80\x9cconcrete interest\xe2\x80\x9d in whether Mr. Chafin secures a re-return order. Knox, 567 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 7) (internal quotation marks omitted). \xe2\x80\x9c[H]owever small\xe2\x80\x9d that concrete interest may be due to potential difficulties in enforcement, it is not simply a matter of academic debate, and is enough to save this case from mootness. Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMr. Chafin also seeks, if he prevails, vacatur of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s expense orders. The District Court ordered Mr. Chafin to pay Ms. Chafin over $94,000 in court costs, attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees, and travel expenses. See Civ. No. 11\xe2\x80\x931461 (ND Ala., Mar. 7, 2012), pp. 15\xe2\x80\x9316; Civ. No. 11\xe2\x80\x931461 (ND Ala., June 5, 2012), p. 2. That award was predicated on the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s earlier judgment allowing Ms. Chafin to return with her daughter to Scotland. See Civ. No. 11\xe2\x80\x931461 (ND Ala., Mar. 7, 2012), pp. 2\xe2\x80\x933, and n.\xc2\xa02.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Thus, in conjunction with reversal of the judgment, Mr. Chafin desires vacatur of the award. That too is common relief on appeal, see, e.g., Fawcett, supra, at 501, n.\xc2\xa06 (reversing costs and fees award when reversing on the issue of wrongful removal), and the mootness inquiry comes down to its effectiveness.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAt oral argument, Ms. Chafin contended that such relief was \xe2\x80\x9cgone in this case,\xe2\x80\x9d and that the case was therefore moot, because Mr. Chafin had failed to pursue an appeal of the expense orders, which had been entered as separate judgments. Tr. of Oral Arg. 33; see Civ. No. 11\xe2\x80\x931461 (ND Ala., Mar. 7, 2012); Civ. No. 11\xe2\x80\x931461 (ND Ala., June 5, 2012). But this is another argument on the merits. Mr. Chafin\xe2\x80\x99s requested relief is not so implausible that it may be disregarded on the question of jurisdiction; there is authority for the proposition that failure to appeal such judgments separately does not preclude relief. See 15B Wright, Miller, & Cooper, supra, \xc2\xa73915.6, at 230, and n. 39.5 (2d ed., Supp. 2012) (citing cases). It is thus for lower courts at later stages of the litigation to decide whether Mr. Chafin is in fact entitled to the relief he seeks\xe2\x80\x94vacatur of the expense orders.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSuch relief would of course not be \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98fully satisfactory,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d but with respect to the case as whole, \xe2\x80\x9ceven the availability of a \xe2\x80\x98partial remedy\xe2\x80\x99 is \xe2\x80\x98sufficient to prevent [a] case from being moot.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Calderon v. Moore, \n\n518 U.\xc2\xa0S. 149, \n150 (1996)\n (per curiam) (quoting Church of Scientology, 506 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 13).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIV\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMs. Chafin is correct to emphasize that both the Hague Convention and ICARA stress the importance of the prompt return of children wrongfully removed or retained. We are also sympathetic to the concern that shuttling children back and forth between parents and across international borders may be detrimental to those children. But courts can achieve the ends of the Convention and ICARA\xe2\x80\x94and protect the well-being of the affected children\xe2\x80\x94through the familiar judicial tools of expediting proceedings and granting stays where appropriate. There is no need to manipulate constitutional doctrine and hold these cases moot. Indeed, doing so may very well undermine the goals of the treaty and harm the children it is meant to protect.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIf these cases were to become moot upon return, courts would be more likely to grant stays as a matter of course, to prevent the loss of any right to appeal. See, e.g., Garrison v. Hudson, \n\n468 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1301, \n1302 (1984)\n (Burger, C.\xc2\xa0J., in chambers) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhen .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the normal course of appellate review might otherwise cause the case to become moot, issuance of a stay is warranted\xe2\x80\x9d (citation and internal quotation marks omitted)); Nicolson v. Pappalardo, Civ. No. 10\xe2\x80\x931125 (CA1, Feb. 19, 2010) (\xe2\x80\x9cWithout necessarily finding a clear probability that appellant will prevail, we grant the stay because .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a risk exists that the case could effectively be mooted by the child\xe2\x80\x99s departure\xe2\x80\x9d). In cases in which a stay would not be granted but for the prospect of mootness, a child would lose precious months when she could have been readjusting to life in her country of habitual residence, even though the appeal had little chance of success. Such routine stays due to mootness would be likely but would conflict with the Convention\xe2\x80\x99s mandate of prompt return to a child\xe2\x80\x99s country of habitual residence.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRoutine stays could also increase the number of appeals. Currently, only about 15% of Hague Convention cases are appealed. Hague Conference on Private Int\xe2\x80\x99l Law, N. Lowe, A Statistical Analysis of Applications Made in 2008 Under the Hague Convention of 25 October 1980 on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction, Pt. III\xe2\x80\x93National Reports 207 (2011). If losing parents were effectively guaranteed a stay, it seems likely that more would appeal, a scenario that would undermine the goal of prompt return and the best interests of children who should in fact be returned. A mootness holding here might also encourage flight in future Hague Convention cases, as prevailing parents try to flee the jurisdiction to moot the case. See Bekier, 248 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1055 (mootness holding \xe2\x80\x9cto some degree conflicts with the purposes of the Convention: to prevent parents from fleeing jurisdictions to find a more favorable judicial forum\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCourts should apply the four traditional stay factors in considering whether to stay a return order: \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98(1) whether the stay applicant has made a strong showing that he is likely to succeed on the merits; (2) whether the applicant will be irreparably injured absent a stay; (3) whether issuance of the stay will substantially injure the other parties interested in the proceeding; and (4) where the public interest lies.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Nken v. Holder, \n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 418, \n434 (2009)\n (quoting Hilton v. Braunskill, \n\n481 U.\xc2\xa0S. 770, \n776 (1987)\n). In every case under the Hague Convention, the well-being of a child is at stake; application of the traditional stay factors ensures that each case will receive the individualized treatment necessary for appropriate consideration of the child\xe2\x80\x99s best interests.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tImportantly, whether at the district or appellate court level, courts can and should take steps to decide these cases as expeditiously as possible, for the sake of the children who find themselves in such an unfortunate situation. Many courts already do so. See Federal Judicial Center, J. Garbolino, The 1980 Hague Convention on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction: A Guide for Judges 116, n. 435 (2012) (listing courts that expedite appeals). Cases in American courts often take over two years from filing to resolution; for a six-year-old such as E.\xc2\xa0C., that is one-third of her lifetime. Expedition will help minimize the extent to which uncertainty adds to the challenges confronting both parents and child.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Hague Convention mandates the prompt return of children to their countries of habitual residence. But such return does not render this case moot; there is a live dispute between the parties over where their child will be raised, and there is a possibility of effectual relief for the prevailing parent. The courts below therefore continue to have jurisdiction to adjudicate the merits of the parties\xe2\x80\x99 respective claims.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t The driving objective of the Hague Convention on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction (Convention) is to facilitate custody adjudications, promptly and exclusively, in the place where the child habitually resides. See Convention, Oct. 25, 1980, T. I. A. S. No. 11670, Arts.\xc2\xa01, 3, S. Treaty Doc. No. 99\xe2\x80\x9311, p.\xc2\xa07 (Treaty Doc.). To that end, the Convention instructs Contracting States to use \xe2\x80\x9cthe most expeditious procedures available\xe2\x80\x9d to secure the return of a child wrongfully removed or retained away from her place of habitual residence. Art. 2, ibid.; see Art. 11, id., at 9 (indicating six weeks as the target time for decision of a return-order petition); Hague Conference on Private International Law, Guide to Good Practice Under the Hague Convention of 25 October 1980 on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction, Part I\xe2\x80\x93Central Authority Practice, \xc2\xa71.5.1, p. 19 (2010) (Guide to Good Practice) (\xe2\x80\x9cExpeditious procedures are essential at all stages of the Convention process.\xe2\x80\x9d). While \xe2\x80\x9c[the] obligation to process return applications expeditiously . . . extends to appeal procedures,\xe2\x80\x9d id., Part IV\xe2\x80\x93Enforcement, \xc2\xa72.2, \xc2\xb651, at 13, the Convention does not prescribe modes of, or time frames for, appellate review of first instance decisions. It therefore rests with each Contracting State to ensure that appeals proceed with dispatch. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAlthough alert to the premium the Convention places on prompt return, see \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa711601(a)(4), Congress did not specifically address appeal proceedings in the legislation implementing the Convention. The case before us illus-trates the protraction likely to ensue when the finality of a return order is left in limbo.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUpon determining that the daughter of Jeffrey Chafin and Lynne Chafin resided in Scotland, the District Court denied Mr. Chafin\xe2\x80\x99s request for a stay pending appeal, and authorized the child\xe2\x80\x99s immediate departure for Scotland. The Eleventh Circuit, viewing the matter as a fait accompli, dismissed the appeal filed by Mr. Chafin as moot.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t As the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion explains, the Eleventh Circuit erred in holding that the child\xe2\x80\x99s removal to Scotland rendered further adjudication in the U.\xc2\xa0S. meaningless. Reversal of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s return order, I agree, could provide Mr. Chafin with meaningful relief. A determination that the child\xe2\x80\x99s habitual residence was Alabama, not Scotland, would open the way for an order directing Ms. Chafin to \xe2\x80\x9cre-return\xe2\x80\x9d the child to the United States and for Mr. Chafin to seek a custody adjudication in an Alabama state court.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t But that prospect is unsettling. \xe2\x80\x9c[S]huttling children back and forth between parents and across international borders may be detrimental to those children,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 12, whose welfare led the Contracting States to draw up the Convention, see 1980 Conf\xc3\xa9rence de La Haye de droit international priv\xc3\xa9, Enl\xc3\xa8vement d\xe2\x80\x99enfants, E. P\xc3\xa9rez-Vera, Explanatory Report, in 3 Actes et Documents de la Quatorzi\xc3\xa8me session, \xc2\xb623, p. 431 (1982). And the advent of rival custody proceedings in Scotland and Alabama is just what the Convention aimed to stave off.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis case highlights the need for both speed and certainty in Convention decisionmaking. Most Contracting States permit challenges to first instance return orders. See Guide to Good Practice, Part IV\xe2\x80\x93Enforcement, \xc2\xa72.3, \xc2\xb657, at 14. How might appellate review proceed consistent with the Convention\xe2\x80\x99s emphasis on expedition? According to a Federal Judicial Center guide, \xe2\x80\x9c[e]xpedited procedures for briefing and handling of [return-order] appeals have become common in most circuits.\xe2\x80\x9d J. Garbolino, The 1980 Hague Convention on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction: A Guide for Judges 116 (2012).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t As an example, the guide describes Charalambous v. Charalambous, 627 F.\xc2\xa03d 462 (CA1 2010) (per\xc2\xa0curiam), in which the Court of Appeals stayed a return order, expedited the appeal, and issued a final judgment affirming the return order 57 days after its entry.
