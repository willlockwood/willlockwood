CONNICK, DISTRICT ATTORNEY, et\xc2\xa0al. v.\nTHOMPSON

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit

\n

No. 09\xe2\x80\x93571.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 6, 2010\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 29, 2011

\n

Petitioner the Orleans Parish District Attorney\xe2\x80\x99s Office concedes that, in prosecuting respondent Thompson for attempted armed robbery, prosecutors violated Brady v. Maryland, 373 U. S. 83, by failing to disclose a crime lab report. Because of his robbery conviction, Thompson elected not to testify at his later murder trial and was convicted. A month before his scheduled execution, the lab report was discovered. A reviewing court vacated both convictions, and Thompson was found not guilty in a retrial on the murder charge. He then filed suit against the district attorney\xe2\x80\x99s office under 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983, alleging, inter alia, that the Brady violation was caused by the office\xe2\x80\x99s deliberate indifference to an obvious need to train prosecutors to avoid such constitutional violations. The district court held that, to prove deliberate indifference, Thompson did not need to show a pattern of similar Brady violations when he could demonstrate that the need for training was obvious. The jury found the district attorney\xe2\x80\x99s office liable for failure to train and awarded Thompson damages. The Fifth Circuit affirmed by an equally divided court.

\n

\nHeld:\xc2\xa0A district attorney\xe2\x80\x99s office may not be held liable under \xc2\xa71983 for failure to train its prosecutors based on a single Brady violation. Pp. 6\xe2\x80\x9320.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Plaintiffs seeking to impose \xc2\xa71983 liability on local governments must prove that their injury was caused by \xe2\x80\x9caction pursuant to official municipal policy,\xe2\x80\x9d which includes the decisions of a government\xe2\x80\x99s lawmakers, the acts of its policymaking officials, and practices so persistent and widespread as to practically have the force of law. Monell v. New York City Dept. of Social Servs., 436 U. S. 658, 691. A local government\xe2\x80\x99s decision not to train certain employees about their legal duty to avoid violating citizens\xe2\x80\x99 rights may rise to the level of an official government policy for \xc2\xa71983 purposes, but the failure to train must amount to \xe2\x80\x9cdeliberate indifference to the rights of persons with whom the [untrained employees] come into contact.\xe2\x80\x9d Canton v. Harris, 489 U. S. 378, 388. Deliberate indifference in this context requires proof that city policymakers disregarded the \xe2\x80\x9cknown or obvious consequence\xe2\x80\x9d that a particular omission in their training program would cause city employees to violate citizens\xe2\x80\x99 constitutional rights. Board of Comm\xe2\x80\x99rs of Bryan Cty. v. Brown, 520 U. S. 397, 410. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0A pattern of similar constitutional violations by untrained employees is \xe2\x80\x9cordinarily necessary\xe2\x80\x9d to demonstrate deliberate indifference. Bryan Cty., supra, at 409. Without notice that a course of training is deficient, decisionmakers can hardly be said to have deliberately chosen a training program that will cause violations of constitutional rights. Thompson does not contend that he proved a pattern of similar Brady violations, and four reversals by Louisiana courts for dissimilar Brady violations in the 10 years before the robbery trial could not have put the district attorney\xe2\x80\x99s office on notice of the need for specific training. Pp. 9\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Thompson mistakenly relies on the \xe2\x80\x9csingle-incident\xe2\x80\x9d liability hypothesized in Canton, contending that the Brady violation in his case was the \xe2\x80\x9cobvious\xe2\x80\x9d consequence of failing to provide specific Brady training and that this \xe2\x80\x9cobviousness\xe2\x80\x9d showing can substitute for the pattern of violations ordinarily necessary to establish municipal culpability. In Canton, the Court theorized that if a city armed its police force and deployed them into the public to capture fleeing felons without training the officers in the constitutional limitation on the use of deadly force, the failure to train could reflect the city\xe2\x80\x99s deliberate indifference to the highly predictable consequence, namely, violations of constitutional rights. Failure to train prosecutors in their Brady obligations does not fall within the narrow range of Canton\xe2\x80\x99s hypothesized single-incident liability. The obvious need for specific legal training present in Canton\xe2\x80\x99s scenario\xe2\x80\x94police academy applicants are unlikely to be familiar with constitutional constraints on deadly force and, absent training, cannot obtain that knowledge\xe2\x80\x94is absent here. Attorneys are trained in the law and equipped with the tools to interpret and apply legal principles, understand constitutional limits, and exercise legal judgment. They receive training before entering the profession, must usually satisfy continuing education requirements, often train on the job with more experienced attorneys, and must satisfy licensing standards and ongoing ethical obligations. Prosecutors not only are equipped but are ethically bound to know what Brady entails and to perform legal research when they are uncertain. Thus, recurring constitutional violations are not the \xe2\x80\x9cobvious consequence\xe2\x80\x9d of failing to provide prosecutors with formal in-house training. The nuance of the allegedly necessary training also distinguishes the case from the example in Canton. Here, the prosecutors were familiar with the general Brady rule. Thus, Thompson cannot rely on the lack of an ability to cope with constitutional situations that underlies the Canton hypothetical, but must assert that prosecutors were not trained about particular Brady evidence or the specific scenario related to the violation in his case. That sort of nuance simply cannot support an inference of deliberate indifference here. Contrary to the holding below, it does not follow that, because Brady has gray areas and some Brady decisions are difficult, prosecutors will so obviously make wrong decisions that failing to train them amounts, as it must, to \xe2\x80\x9ca decision by the city itself to violate the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Canton, 489 U. S., at 395 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part). Pp. 11\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

578 F. 3d 293, reversed.

HARRY F. CONNICK, DISTRICT ATTORNEY, et\xc2\xa0al.,\nPETITIONERS v. JOHN THOMPSON

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the fifth circuit

\n

[March 29, 2011]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Thomas delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Orleans Parish District Attorney\xe2\x80\x99s Office now concedes that, in prosecuting respondent John Thompson for attempted armed robbery, prosecutors failed to disclose evidence that should have been turned over to the defense under Brady v. Maryland, 373 U. S. 83 (1963). Thompson was convicted. Because of that conviction Thompson elected not to testify in his own defense in his later trial for murder, and he was again convicted. Thompson spent 18 years in prison, including 14 years on death row. One month before Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s scheduled execution, his investigator discovered the undisclosed evidence from his armed robbery trial. The reviewing court determined that the evidence was exculpatory, and both of Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s convictions were vacated.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After his release from prison, Thompson sued petitioner Harry Connick, in his official capacity as the Orleans Parish District Attorney, for damages under Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa71979, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983. Thompson alleged that Connick had failed to train his prosecutors adequately about their duty to produce exculpatory evidence and that the lack of training had caused the nondisclosure in Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s rob-bery case. The jury awarded Thompson $14 million, \nand the Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit affirmed by an evenly divided en banc court. We granted certiorari to decide whether a district attorney\xe2\x80\x99s office may be held liable under \xc2\xa71983 for failure to train based on a single Brady violation. We hold that it cannot.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In early 1985, John Thompson was charged with the murder of Raymond T. Liuzza, Jr. in New Orleans. Publicity following the murder charge led the victims of an unrelated armed robbery to identify Thompson as their attacker. The district attorney charged Thompson with attempted armed robbery.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As part of the robbery investigation, a crime scene technician took from one of the victims\xe2\x80\x99 pants a swatch of fabric stained with the robber\xe2\x80\x99s blood. Approximately one week before Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s armed robbery trial, the swatch was sent to the crime laboratory. Two days before the trial, assistant district attorney Bruce Whittaker received the crime lab\xe2\x80\x99s report, which stated that the perpetrator had blood type B. There is no evidence that the prosecutors ever had Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s blood tested or that they knew what his blood type was. Whittaker claimed he placed the report on assistant district attorney James Williams\xe2\x80\x99 desk, but Williams denied seeing it. The report was never disclosed to Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s counsel.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Williams tried the armed robbery case with assistant district attorney Gerry Deegan. On the first day of trial, Deegan checked all of the physical evidence in the case out of the police property room, including the blood-stained swatch. Deegan then checked all of the evidence but the swatch into the courthouse property room. The prosecutors did not mention the swatch or the crime lab report at trial, and the jury convicted Thompson of attempted armed robbery.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A few weeks later, Williams and special prosecutor Eric Dubelier tried Thompson for the Liuzza murder. Because of the armed robbery conviction, Thompson chose not to testify in his own defense. He was convicted and sentenced to death. State v. Thompson, 516 So.\xc2\xa02d 349 (La. 1987). In the 14 years following Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s murder conviction, state and federal courts reviewed and denied his challenges to the conviction and sentence. See State ex rel. Thompson v. Cain, 95\xe2\x80\x932463 (La. 4/25/96), 672 So.\xc2\xa02d 906; Thompson v. Cain, 161 F.\xc2\xa03d 802 (CA5 1998). The State scheduled Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s execution for May 20, 1999.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In late April 1999, Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s private investigator discovered the crime lab report from the armed robbery investigation in the files of the New Orleans Police Crime Laboratory. Thompson was tested and found to have blood type O, proving that the blood on the swatch was not his. Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s attorneys presented this evidence to the district attorney\xe2\x80\x99s office, which, in turn, moved to stay the execution and vacate Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s armed robbery conviction.[Footnote 1] The Louisiana Court of Appeals then reversed Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s murder conviction, concluding that the armed robbery conviction unconstitutionally deprived Thompson of his right to testify in his own defense at the murder trial. State v. Thompson, 2002\xe2\x80\x930361 (La. App. 7/17/02), 825 So.\xc2\xa02d 552. In 2003, the district attorney\xe2\x80\x99s office retried Thompson for Liuzza\xe2\x80\x99s murder.[Footnote 2] The jury found him not guilty.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thompson then brought this action against the district attorney\xe2\x80\x99s office, Connick, Williams, and others, alleging that their conduct caused him to be wrongfully convicted, incarcerated for 18 years, and nearly executed. The only claim that proceeded to trial was Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s claim under \xc2\xa71983 that the district attorney\xe2\x80\x99s office had violated Brady by failing to disclose the crime lab report in his armed robbery trial. See Brady, 373 U. S. 83. Thompson alleged liability under two theories: (1)\xc2\xa0the Brady violation was caused by an unconstitutional policy of the district attorney\xe2\x80\x99s office; and (2)\xc2\xa0the violation was caused by Connick\xe2\x80\x99s deliberate indifference to an obvious need to train the prosecutors in his office in order to avoid such constitutional violations.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Before trial, Connick conceded that the failure to produce the crime lab report constituted a Brady violation.[Footnote 3] See Record EX608, EX880. Accordingly, the District Court instructed the jury that the \xe2\x80\x9conly issue\xe2\x80\x9d was whether the nondisclosure was caused by either a policy, practice, or custom of the district attorney\xe2\x80\x99s office or a deliberately indifferent failure to train the office\xe2\x80\x99s prosecutors. Record 1615.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although no prosecutor remembered any specific training session regarding Brady prior to 1985, it was undisputed at trial that the prosecutors were familiar with the general Brady requirement that the State disclose to the defense evidence in its possession that is favorable to \nthe accused. Prosecutors testified that office policy was to turn crime lab reports and other scientific evidence over \nto the defense. They also testified that, after the discovery of the undisclosed crime lab report in 1999, prosecutors disagreed about whether it had to be disclosed under Brady absent knowledge of Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s blood type.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The jury rejected Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s claim that an unconstitutional office policy caused the Brady violation, but found the district attorney\xe2\x80\x99s office liable for failing to train the prosecutors. The jury awarded Thompson $14 million in damages, and the District Court added more than $1 million in attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees and costs.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After the verdict, Connick renewed his objection\xe2\x80\x94which he had raised on summary judgment\xe2\x80\x94that he could not have been deliberately indifferent to an obvious need for more or different Brady training because there was no evidence that he was aware of a pattern of similar Brady violations. The District Court rejected this argument for the reasons that it had given in the summary judgment order. In that order, the court had concluded that a pattern of violations is not necessary to prove deliberate indifference when the need for training is \xe2\x80\x9cso obvious.\xe2\x80\x9d No. Civ. A. 03\xe2\x80\x932045 (ED La., Nov. 15, 2005), App. to Pet. for Cert. 141a, 2005 WL 3541035, *13. Relying on Canton v. Harris, 489 U. S. 378 (1989), the court had held that Thompson could demonstrate deliberate indifference by proving that \xe2\x80\x9cthe DA\xe2\x80\x99s office knew to a moral certainty that assistan[t] [district attorneys] would acquire Brady material, that without training it is not always obvious what Brady requires, and that withholding Brady material will virtually always lead to a substantial violation of constitutional rights.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 4] App. to Pet. for Cert. 141a, 2005 WL 3541035, *13.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A panel of the Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit affirmed. The panel acknowledged that Thompson did not present evidence of a pattern of similar Brady violations, 553 F.\xc2\xa03d 836, 851 (2008), but held that Thompson did not need to prove a pattern, id., at 854. According to the panel, Thompson demonstrated that Connick was on notice of an obvious need for Brady training by presenting evidence \xe2\x80\x9cthat attorneys, often fresh out of law school, would undoubtedly be required to confront Brady issues while at the DA\xe2\x80\x99s Office, that erroneous decisions regarding Brady evidence would result in serious constitutional violations, that resolution of Brady issues was often unclear, and that training in Brady would have been helpful.\xe2\x80\x9d 553 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 854.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals sitting en banc vacated the panel opinion, granted rehearing, and divided evenly, thereby affirming the District Court. 578 F.\xc2\xa03d 293 (CA5 2009) (per curiam). In four opinions, the divided en banc court disputed whether Thompson could establish municipal liability for failure to train the prosecutors based on the single Brady violation without proving a prior pattern of similar violations, and, if so, what evidence would make that showing. We granted certiorari. 559 U. S. ___ (2010).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Brady violation conceded in this case occurred when one or more of the four prosecutors involved with Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s armed robbery prosecution failed to disclose the crime lab report to Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s counsel. Under Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s failure-to-train theory, he bore the burden of proving both (1) that Connick, the policymaker for the district attorney\xe2\x80\x99s office, was deliberately indifferent to the need to train the prosecutors about their Brady disclosure obligation with respect to evidence of this type and (2) that the lack of training actually caused the Brady violation in this case. Connick argues that he was entitled to judgment as a matter of law because Thompson did not prove that he was on actual or constructive notice of, and therefore deliberately indifferent to, a need for more or different Brady training. We agree.[Footnote 5]

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Title 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983 provides in relevant part:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cEvery person who, under color of any statute, ordinance, regulation, custom, or usage, of any State \xe2\x80\xa6 subjects, or causes to be subjected, any citizen of the United States or other person within the jurisdiction thereof to the deprivation of any rights, privileges, or immunities secured by the Constitution and laws, shall be liable to the party injured in an action at law, suit in equity, or other proper proceeding for \nredress \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\nA municipality or other local government may be liable under this section if the governmental body itself \xe2\x80\x9csubjects\xe2\x80\x9d a person to a deprivation of rights or \xe2\x80\x9ccauses\xe2\x80\x9d a person \xe2\x80\x9cto be subjected\xe2\x80\x9d to such deprivation. See Monell v. New York City Dept. of Social Servs., 436 U. S. 658, 692 (1978). But, under \xc2\xa71983, local governments are responsible only for \xe2\x80\x9ctheir own illegal acts.\xe2\x80\x9d Pembaur v. Cincinnati, 475 U. S. 469, 479 (1986) (citing Monell, 436 U. S., at 665\xe2\x80\x93683). They are not vicariously liable under \xc2\xa71983 for their employees\xe2\x80\x99 actions. See id., at 691; Canton, 489 U. S., at 392; Board of Comm\xe2\x80\x99rs of Bryan Cty. v. Brown, 520 U. S. 397, 403 (1997) (collecting cases).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Plaintiffs who seek to impose liability on local governments under \xc2\xa71983 must prove that \xe2\x80\x9caction pursuant to official municipal policy\xe2\x80\x9d caused their injury. Monell, 436 U. S., at 691; see id., at 694. Official municipal policy includes the decisions of a government\xe2\x80\x99s lawmakers, the acts of its policymaking officials, and practices so persistent and widespread as to practically have the force of law. See ibid.; Pembaur, supra, at 480\xe2\x80\x93481; Adickes v. S. H. Kress & Co., 398 U. S. 144, 167\xe2\x80\x93168 (1970). These are \xe2\x80\x9caction[s] for which the municipality is actually responsible.\xe2\x80\x9d Pembaur, supra, at 479\xe2\x80\x93480.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In limited circumstances, a local government\xe2\x80\x99s decision not to train certain employees about their legal duty to avoid violating citizens\xe2\x80\x99 rights may rise to the level of an official government policy for purposes of \xc2\xa71983. A municipality\xe2\x80\x99s culpability for a deprivation of rights is at its most tenuous where a claim turns on a failure to train. See Oklahoma City v. Tuttle, 471 U. S. 808, 822\xe2\x80\x93823 (1985) (plurality opinion) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] \xe2\x80\x98policy\xe2\x80\x99 of \xe2\x80\x98inadequate training\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cfar more nebulous, and a good deal further removed from the constitutional violation, than was the policy in Monell\xe2\x80\x9d). To satisfy the statute, a municipality\xe2\x80\x99s failure to train its employees in a relevant respect must amount to \xe2\x80\x9cdeliberate indifference to the rights of persons with whom the [untrained employees] come into contact.\xe2\x80\x9d Canton, 489 U. S., at 388. Only then \xe2\x80\x9ccan such a shortcoming be properly thought of as a city \xe2\x80\x98policy or custom\xe2\x80\x99 that is actionable under \xc2\xa71983.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 389.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[D]eliberate indifference\xe2\x80\x99 is a stringent standard of fault, requiring proof that a municipal actor disregarded a known or obvious consequence of his action.\xe2\x80\x9d Bryan Cty., 520 U. S., at 410. Thus, when city policymakers are on actual or constructive notice that a particular omission in their training program causes city employees to violate citizens\xe2\x80\x99 constitutional rights, the city may be deemed deliberately indifferent if the policymakers choose to retain that program. Id., at 407. The city\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cpolicy of inaction\xe2\x80\x9d in light of notice that its program will cause constitutional violations \xe2\x80\x9cis the functional equivalent of a decision by the city itself to violate the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Canton, 489 U. S., at 395 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part). A less stringent standard of fault for a failure-to-train claim \xe2\x80\x9cwould result in de facto respondeat superior liability on municipalities \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 392; see also Pembaur, supra, at 483 (opinion of Brennan, J.) (\xe2\x80\x9c[M]unicipal liability under \xc2\xa71983 attaches where\xe2\x80\x94and only where\xe2\x80\x94a deliberate choice to follow a course of action is made from among various alternatives by [the relevant] officials \xe2\x80\xa6\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A pattern of similar constitutional violations by untrained employees is \xe2\x80\x9cordinarily necessary\xe2\x80\x9d to demonstrate deliberate indifference for purposes of failure to train. Bryan Cty., 520 U. S., at 409. Policymakers\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9ccontinued adherence to an approach that they know or should know has failed to prevent tortious conduct by employees may establish the conscious disregard for the consequences of their action\xe2\x80\x94the \xe2\x80\x98deliberate indifference\xe2\x80\x99\xe2\x80\x94necessary to trigger municipal liability.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 407. Without notice that a course of training is deficient in a particular respect, decisionmakers can hardly be said to have deliberately chosen a training program that will cause violations of constitutional rights.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although Thompson does not contend that he proved a pattern of similar Brady violations, 553 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 851, vacated, 578 F.\xc2\xa03d 293 (en banc), he points out that, during the ten years preceding his armed robbery trial, Louisiana courts had overturned four convictions because of Brady violations by prosecutors in Connick\xe2\x80\x99s office.[Footnote 6] Those four reversals could not have put Connick on notice that the office\xe2\x80\x99s Brady training was inadequate with respect to the sort of Brady violation at issue here. None of those cases involved failure to disclose blood evidence, a crime lab report, or physical or scientific evidence of any kind. Because those incidents are not similar to the violation at issue here, they could not have put Connick on notice that specific training was necessary to avoid this constitutional violation.[Footnote 7]

\n

C

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Instead of relying on a pattern of similar Brady violations, Thompson relies on the \xe2\x80\x9csingle-incident\xe2\x80\x9d liability that this Court hypothesized in Canton. He contends that the Brady violation in his case was the \xe2\x80\x9cobvious\xe2\x80\x9d consequence of failing to provide specific Brady training, and that this showing of \xe2\x80\x9cobviousness\xe2\x80\x9d can substitute for the pattern of violations ordinarily necessary to establish municipal culpability.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Canton, the Court left open the possibility that, \xe2\x80\x9cin a narrow range of circumstances,\xe2\x80\x9d a pattern of similar violations might not be necessary to show deliberate indifference. Bryan Cty., supra, at 409. The Court posed the hypothetical example of a city that arms its police force with firearms and deploys the armed officers into the public to capture fleeing felons without training the officers in the constitutional limitation on the use of deadly force. Canton, supra, at 390, n.\xc2\xa010. Given the known frequency with which police attempt to arrest fleeing felons and the \xe2\x80\x9cpredictability that an officer lacking specific tools to handle that situation will violate citizens\xe2\x80\x99 rights,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court theorized that a city\xe2\x80\x99s decision not to train the officers about constitutional limits on the use of deadly force could reflect the city\xe2\x80\x99s deliberate indifference to the \xe2\x80\x9chighly predictable consequence,\xe2\x80\x9d namely, violations of constitutional rights. Bryan Cty., supra, at 409. The Court sought not to foreclose the possibility, however rare, that the unconstitutional consequences of failing to train could be so patently obvious that a city could be liable under \xc2\xa71983 without proof of a pre-existing pattern of violations.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Failure to train prosecutors in their Brady obligations does not fall within the narrow range of Canton\xe2\x80\x99s hypothesized single-incident liability. The obvious need for specific legal training that was present in the Canton scenario is absent here. Armed police must sometimes make split-second decisions with life-or-death consequences. There is no reason to assume that police academy applicants are familiar with the constitutional constraints on the use of deadly force. And, in the absence of training, there is no way for novice officers to obtain the legal knowledge they require. Under those circumstances there is an obvious need for some form of training. In stark contrast, legal \xe2\x80\x9c[t]raining is what differentiates attorneys from average public employees.\xe2\x80\x9d 578 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 304\xe2\x80\x93305 (opinion of Clement, J.).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Attorneys are trained in the law and equipped with the tools to interpret and apply legal principles, understand constitutional limits, and exercise legal judgment. Before they may enter the profession and receive a law license, all attorneys must graduate from law school or pass a substantive examination; attorneys in the vast majority of jurisdictions must do both. See, e.g., La. State Bar Assn. (LSBA), Articles of Incorporation, La. Rev. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa737, ch. 4, App., Art. 14, \xc2\xa77 (1988 West Supp.) (as amended through 1985). These threshold requirements are designed to ensure that all new attorneys have learned how to find, understand, and apply legal rules. Cf. United States v. Cronic, 466 U. S. 648, 658, 664 (1984) (noting that the presumption \xe2\x80\x9cthat the lawyer is competent to provide the guiding hand that the defendant needs\xe2\x80\x9d applies even to young and inexperienced lawyers in their first jury trial and even when the case is complex).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor does professional training end at graduation. Most jurisdictions require attorneys to satisfy continuing-education requirements. See, e.g., LSBA, Articles of Incorporation, Art. 16, Rule 1.1(b) (effective 1987); La. Sup. Ct. Rule XXX (effective 1988). Even those few jurisdictions that do not impose mandatory continuing-education requirements mandate that attorneys represent their clients competently and encourage attorneys to engage in continuing study and education. See, e.g., Mass. Rule Prof. Conduct 1.1 and comment 6 (West 2006). Before Louisiana adopted continuing-education requirements, it imposed similar general competency requirements on its state bar. LSBA, Articles of Incorporation, Art. 16, EC 1\xe2\x80\x931, 1\xe2\x80\x932, DR 6\xe2\x80\x93101 (West 1974) (effective 1971).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Attorneys who practice with other attorneys, such as in district attorney\xe2\x80\x99s offices, also train on the job as they learn from more experienced attorneys. For instance, here in the Orleans Parish District Attorney\xe2\x80\x99s Office, junior prosecutors were trained by senior prosecutors who supervised them as they worked together to prepare cases for trial, and trial chiefs oversaw the preparation of the cases. Senior attorneys also circulated court decisions and instructional memoranda to keep the prosecutors abreast of relevant legal developments.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In addition, attorneys in all jurisdictions must satisfy character and fitness standards to receive a law license and are personally subject to an ethical regime designed to reinforce the profession\xe2\x80\x99s standards. See, e.g., LSBA, Articles of Incorporation, Art. 14, \xc2\xa77 (1985); see generally id., Art. 16 (1971) (Code of Professional Responsibility). Trial lawyers have a \xe2\x80\x9cduty to bring to bear such skill and knowledge as will render the trial a reliable adversarial testing process.\xe2\x80\x9d Strickland v. Washington, 466 U. S. 668, 688 (1984). Prosecutors have a special \xe2\x80\x9cduty to seek justice, not merely to convict.\xe2\x80\x9d LSBA, Articles of Incorporation, Art. 16, EC 7\xe2\x80\x9313 (1971); ABA Standards for Criminal Justice 3\xe2\x80\x931.1(c) (2d ed. 1980). Among prosecutors\xe2\x80\x99 unique ethical obligations is the duty to produce Brady evidence to the defense. See, e.g., LSBA, Articles of Incorporation, Art. 16, EC 7\xe2\x80\x9313 (1971); ABA Model Rule of Prof. Conduct 3.8(d) (1984).[Footnote 8] An attorney who violates his or her ethical obligations is subject to professional discipline, including sanctions, suspension, and disbarment. See, e.g., LSBA, Articles of Incorporation, Art. 15, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa75, 6 (1971); id., Art. 16, DR 1\xe2\x80\x93102; ABA Model Rule of Prof. Conduct 8.4 (1984).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In light of this regime of legal training and professional responsibility, recurring constitutional violations are not the \xe2\x80\x9cobvious consequence\xe2\x80\x9d of failing to provide prosecutors with formal in-house training about how to obey the law. Bryan Cty., 520 U. S., at 409. Prosecutors are not only equipped but are also ethically bound to know what Brady entails and to perform legal research when they are uncertain. A district attorney is entitled to rely on prosecutors\xe2\x80\x99 professional training and ethical obligations in the absence of specific reason, such as a pattern of violations, to believe that those tools are insufficient to prevent future constitutional violations in \xe2\x80\x9cthe usual and recurring situations with which [the prosecutors] must deal.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 9] Canton, 489 U. S., at 391. A licensed attorney making legal judgments, in his capacity as a prosecutor, about Brady material simply does not present the same \xe2\x80\x9chighly predictable\xe2\x80\x9d constitutional danger as Canton\xe2\x80\x99s untrained officer.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A second significant difference between this case and the example in Canton is the nuance of the allegedly necessary training. The Canton hypothetical assumes that the armed police officers have no knowledge at all of the constitutional limits on the use of deadly force. But it is undisputed here that the prosecutors in Connick\xe2\x80\x99s office were familiar with the general Brady rule. Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s complaint therefore cannot rely on the utter lack of an ability to cope with constitutional situations that underlies the Canton hypothetical, but rather must assert that prosecutors were not trained about particular Brady evidence or the specific scenario related to the violation in his case. That sort of nuance simply cannot support an inference of deliberate indifference here. As the Court said in Canton, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n virtually every instance where a person has had his or her constitutional rights violated by a city employee, a \xc2\xa71983 plaintiff will be able to point to something the city \xe2\x80\x98could have done\xe2\x80\x99 to prevent the unfortunate incident.\xe2\x80\x9d 489 U. S., at 392 (citing Tuttle, 471 U. S., at 823 (plurality opinion)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thompson suggests that the absence of any formal training sessions about Brady is equivalent to the complete absence of legal training that the Court imagined in Canton. But failure-to-train liability is concerned with the substance of the training, not the particular instructional format. The statute does not provide plaintiffs or courts carte blanche to micromanage local governments throughout the United States.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We do not assume that prosecutors will always make correct Brady decisions or that guidance regarding specific Brady questions would not assist prosecutors. But showing merely that additional training would have been \nhelpful in making difficult decisions does not establish municipal liability. \xe2\x80\x9c[P]rov[ing] that an injury or accident could have been avoided if an [employee] had had better or more training, sufficient to equip him to avoid the particular injury-causing conduct\xe2\x80\x9d will not suffice. Canton, supra, at 391. The possibility of single-incident liability that the Court left open in Canton is not this case.[Footnote 10]

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dissent rejects our holding that Canton\xe2\x80\x99s hypothesized single-incident liability does not, as a legal matter, encompass failure to train prosecutors in their Brady obligation. It would instead apply the Canton hypothetical to this case, and thus devotes almost all of its opinion to explaining why the evidence supports liability under that theory.[Footnote 11] But the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s attempt to address our holding\xe2\x80\x94by pointing out that not all prosecutors will necessarily have enrolled in criminal procedure class\xe2\x80\x94misses the point. See post, at 29\xe2\x80\x9330. The reason why the Canton hypothetical is inapplicable is that attorneys, unlike police officers, are equipped with the tools to find, interpret, and apply legal principles.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0By the end of its opinion, however, the dissent finally reveals that its real disagreement is not with our holding today, but with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent. The dissent does not see \xe2\x80\x9cany reason,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 31, for the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion in Bryan County that a pattern of violations is \xe2\x80\x9cordinarily necessary\xe2\x80\x9d to demonstrate deliberate indifference for purposes of failure to train, 520 U. S., at 409. Cf. id., at 406\xe2\x80\x93408 (explaining why a pattern of violations is ordinarily necessary). But cf. post, at 30\xe2\x80\x9331 (describing our reliance on Bryan County as \xe2\x80\x9cimply[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d a new \xe2\x80\x9climitation\xe2\x80\x9d on \xc2\xa71983). As our precedent makes clear, proving that a municipality itself actually caused a constitutional violation by failing to train the offending employee presents \xe2\x80\x9cdifficult problems of proof,\xe2\x80\x9d and we must adhere to a \xe2\x80\x9cstringent standard of fault,\xe2\x80\x9d lest municipal liability under \xc2\xa71983 collapse into respondeat superior.[Footnote 12] Bryan County, 520 U. S., at 406, 410; see Canton, 489 U. S., at 391\xe2\x80\x93392.

\n

3

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court and the Court of Appeals panel erroneously believed that Thompson had proved deliberate indifference by showing the \xe2\x80\x9cobviousness\xe2\x80\x9d of a need for additional training. They based this conclusion on Connick\xe2\x80\x99s awareness that (1)\xc2\xa0prosecutors would confront Brady issues while at the district attorney\xe2\x80\x99s office; (2)\xc2\xa0inexperienced prosecutors were expected to understand Brady\xe2\x80\x99s requirements; (3)\xc2\xa0Brady has gray areas that make for difficult choices; and (4)\xc2\xa0erroneous decisions regarding Brady evidence would result in constitutional violations. 553 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 854; App. to Pet. for Cert. 141a, 2005 WL 3541035, *13. This is insufficient.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It does not follow that, because Brady has gray areas and some Brady decisions are difficult, prosecutors will so obviously make wrong decisions that failing to train them amounts to \xe2\x80\x9ca decision by the city itself to violate the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Canton, 489 U. S., at 395 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part). To prove deliberate indifference, Thompson needed to show that Connick was on notice that, absent additional specified training, it was \xe2\x80\x9chighly predictable\xe2\x80\x9d that the prosecutors in his office would be confounded by those gray areas and make incorrect Brady decisions as a result. In fact, Thompson had to show that it was so predictable that failing to train the prosecutors amounted to conscious disregard for defendants\xe2\x80\x99 Brady rights. See Bryan Cty., 520 U. S., at 409; Canton, supra, at 389. He did not do so.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The role of a prosecutor is to see that justice is done. Berger v. United States, 295 U. S. 78, 88 (1935). \xe2\x80\x9cIt is as much [a prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s] duty to refrain from improper methods calculated to produce a wrongful conviction as it is to use every legitimate means to bring about a just one.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. By their own admission, the prosecutors who tried Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s armed robbery case failed to carry out that responsibility. But the only issue before us is whether Connick, as the policymaker for the district attorney\xe2\x80\x99s office, was deliberately indifferent to the need to train the attorneys under his authority.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We conclude that this case does not fall within the narrow range of \xe2\x80\x9csingle-incident\xe2\x80\x9d liability hypothesized in Canton as a possible exception to the pattern of violations necessary to prove deliberate indifference in \xc2\xa71983 actions alleging failure to train. The District Court should have granted Connick judgment as a matter of law on the failure-to-train claim because Thompson did not prove a pattern of similar violations that would \xe2\x80\x9cestablish that the \xe2\x80\x98policy of inaction\xe2\x80\x99 [was] the functional equivalent of a decision by the city itself to violate the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Canton, supra, at 395 (opinion of O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J.).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment of the United States Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit is reversed.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in full. I write separately only to address several aspects of the dissent.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01. The dissent\xe2\x80\x99s lengthy excavation of the trial record is a puzzling exertion. The question presented for our review is whether a municipality is liable for a single Brady violation by one of its prosecutors, even though no pattern or practice of prior violations put the municipality on notice of a need for specific training that would have prevented it. See Brady v. Maryland, 373 U. S. 83 (1963). That question is a legal one: whether a Brady violation presents one of those rare circumstances we hypothesized in Canton\xe2\x80\x99s footnote 10, in which the need for training in constitutional requirements is so obvious ex ante that the municipality\xe2\x80\x99s failure to provide that training amounts \nto deliberate indifference to constitutional violations. See Canton v. Harris, 489 U. S. 378, 390, n.\xc2\xa010 (1989).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dissent defers consideration of this question until page 23 of its opinion. It first devotes considerable space to allegations that Connick\xe2\x80\x99s prosecutors misunderstood Brady when asked about it at trial, see post, at 16\xe2\x80\x9318 (opinion of Ginsburg, J.), and to supposed gaps in the Brady guidance provided by Connick\xe2\x80\x99s office to prosecutors, including deficiencies (unrelated to the specific Brady violation at issue in this case) in a policy manual published by Connick\xe2\x80\x99s office three years after Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s trial, see post, at 18\xe2\x80\x9321. None of that is relevant. Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s failure-to-train theory at trial was not based on a pervasive culture of indifference to Brady, but rather on the inevitability of mistakes over enough iterations of criminal trials. The District Court instructed the jury it could find Connick deliberately indifferent if:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cFirst: The District Attorney was certain that prosecutors would confront the situation where they would have to decide which evidence was required by the constitution to be provided to an accused[;]

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9cSecond: The situation involved a difficult choice, or one that prosecutors had a history of mishandling, such that additional training, supervision, or monitoring was clearly needed[; and]

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9cThird: The wrong choice by a prosecutor in that situation will frequently cause a deprivation of an accused\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional rights.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 828.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That theory of deliberate indifference would repeal the law of Monell[Footnote 1] in favor of the Law of Large Numbers. Brady mistakes are inevitable. So are all species of error routinely confronted by prosecutors: authorizing a bad warrant; losing a Batson[Footnote 2] claim; crossing the line in closing argument; or eliciting hearsay that violates the Confrontation Clause. Nevertheless, we do not have \xe2\x80\x9cde facto respondeat superior liability,\xe2\x80\x9d Canton, 489 U. S., at 392, for each such violation under the rubric of failure-to-train simply because the municipality does not have a professional educational program covering the specific violation in sufficient depth.[Footnote 3] Were Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s theory the law, there would have been no need for Canton\xe2\x80\x99s footnote to confine its hypothetical to the extreme circumstance of arming police officers with guns without telling them about the constitutional limitations upon shooting fleeing felons; the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s instructions cover every recurring situation in which citizens\xe2\x80\x99 rights can be violated.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That result cannot be squared with our admonition that failure-to-train liability is available only in \xe2\x80\x9climited circumstances,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 387, and that a pattern of consti-tutional violations is \xe2\x80\x9cordinarily necessary to establish municipal culpability and causation,\xe2\x80\x9d Board of Comm\xe2\x80\x99rs of Bryan Cty. v. Brown, 520 U. S. 397, 409 (1997). These restrictions are indispensable because without them, \xe2\x80\x9cfailure to train\xe2\x80\x9d would become a talismanic incantation producing municipal liability \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n virtually every instance where a person has had his or her constitutional rights violated by a city employee\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94which is what Monell rejects. Canton, 489 U. S., at 392. Worse, it would \xe2\x80\x9cengage the federal courts in an endless exercise of second-guessing municipal employee-training programs,\xe2\x80\x9d thereby diminishing the autonomy of state and local governments. Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02. Perhaps for that reason, the dissent does not seriously contend that Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s theory of recovery was proper. Rather, it accuses Connick of acquiescing in that theory at trial. See post, at 25. The accusation is false. Connick\xe2\x80\x99s central claim was and is that failure-to-train liability for a Brady violation cannot be premised on a single incident, but requires a pattern or practice of previous violations. He pressed that argument at the summary judgment stage but was rebuffed. At trial, when Connick offered a jury instruction to the same effect, the trial judge effectively told him to stop bringing up the subject:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c[Connick\xe2\x80\x99s counsel]: Also, as part of that definition in that same location, Your Honor, we would like to include language that says that deliberate indifference to training requires a pattern of similar violations and proof of deliberate indifference requires more than a single isolated act.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c[Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s counsel]: That\xe2\x80\x99s not the law, Your Honor.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cTHE COURT: No, I\xe2\x80\x99m not giving that. That was in your motion for summary judgment that I denied.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. 1013.

\n

\nNothing more is required to preserve a claim of error. See Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 51(d)(1)(B).[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa03. But in any event, to recover from a municipality under 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983, a plaintiff must satisfy a \xe2\x80\x9crigorous\xe2\x80\x9d standard of causation, Bryan Cty., 520 U. S., at 405; he must \xe2\x80\x9cdemonstrate a direct causal link between the municipal action and the deprivation of federal rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 404. Thompson cannot meet that standard. The withholding of evidence in his case was almost certainly caused not by a failure to give prosecutors specific training, but by miscreant prosecutor Gerry Deegan\xe2\x80\x99s willful suppression of evidence he believed to be exculpatory, in an effort to railroad Thompson. According to Deegan\xe2\x80\x99s colleague Michael Riehlmann, in 1994 Deegan confessed to him\xe2\x80\x94in the same conversation in which Deegan revealed he had only a few months to live\xe2\x80\x94that he had \xe2\x80\x9csuppressed blood evidence in the armed robbery trial of John Thompson that in some way exculpated the defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 367; see also id., at 362 (\xe2\x80\x9c[Deegan] told me \xe2\x80\xa6 that he had failed to inform the defense of exculpatory information\xe2\x80\x9d). I have no reason to disbelieve that account, particularly since Riehlmann\xe2\x80\x99s testimony hardly paints a flattering picture of himself: Riehlmann kept silent about Deegan\xe2\x80\x99s misconduct for another five years, as a result of which he incurred professional sanctions. See In\xc2\xa0re Riehlmann, 2004\xe2\x80\x930680 (La. 1/19/05), 891 So.\xc2\xa02d 1239. And if Riehlmann\xe2\x80\x99s story is true, then the \xe2\x80\x9cmoving force,\xe2\x80\x9d Bryan Cty., supra, at 404 (internal quotation marks omitted), behind the suppression of evidence was Deegan, not a failure of continuing legal education.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa04. The dissent suspends disbelief about this, insisting that with proper Brady training, \xe2\x80\x9csurely at least one\xe2\x80\x9d of the prosecutors in Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s trial would have turned over the lab report and blood swatch. Post, at 21. But training must consist of more than mere broad encomiums of Brady: We have made clear that \xe2\x80\x9cthe identified deficiency in a city\xe2\x80\x99s training program [must be] closely related to the ultimate injury.\xe2\x80\x9d Canton, supra, at 391. So even indulging the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s assumption that Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s prosecutors failed to disclose the lab report in good faith\xe2\x80\x94in a way that could be prevented by training\xe2\x80\x94what sort of training would have prevented the good-faith nondisclosure of a blood report not known to be exculpatory?

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Perhaps a better question to ask is what legally accurate training would have prevented it. The dissent\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion is to instruct prosecutors to ignore the portion of Brady limiting prosecutors\xe2\x80\x99 disclosure obligations to evidence that is \xe2\x80\x9cfavorable to an accused,\xe2\x80\x9d 373 U. S., at 87. Instead, the dissent proposes that \xe2\x80\x9cConnick could have communicated to Orleans Parish prosecutors, in no uncertain terms, that, \xe2\x80\x98[i]f you have physical evidence that, if tested, can establish the innocence of the person who is charged, you have to turn it over.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 20, n.\xc2\xa013 (quoting Tr. of Oral Arg. 34). Though labeled a training suggestion, the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s proposal is better described as a sub silentio expansion of the substantive law of Brady. If any of our cases establishes such an obligation, I have never read it, and the dissent does not cite it.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Since Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s trial, however, we have decided a case that appears to say just the opposite of the training the dissent would require: In Arizona v. Youngblood, 488 U. S. 51, 58 (1988), we held that \xe2\x80\x9cunless a criminal defendant can show bad faith on the part of the police, failure to preserve potentially useful evidence does not constitute a denial of due process of law.\xe2\x80\x9d We acknowledged that \xe2\x80\x9cBrady \xe2\x80\xa6 makes the good or bad faith of the State irrelevant when the State fails to disclose to the defendant material exculpatory evidence,\xe2\x80\x9d but concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Due Process Clause requires a different result when we deal with the failure of the State to preserve evidentiary material of which no more can be said than that it could have been subjected to tests, the results of which might have exonerated the defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 57. Perhaps one day we will recognize a distinction between good-faith failures to preserve from destruction evidence whose inculpatory or exculpatory character is unknown, and good-faith failures to turn such evidence over to the defense. But until we do so, a failure to train prosecutors to observe that distinction cannot constitute deliberate indifference.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa05. By now the reader has doubtless guessed the best-kept secret of this case: There was probably no Brady violation at all\xe2\x80\x94except for Deegan\xe2\x80\x99s (which, since it was a bad-faith, knowing violation, could not possibly be attributed to lack of training).[Footnote 6] The dissent surely knows this, which is why it leans heavily on the fact that Connick conceded that Brady was violated. I can honor that concession in my analysis of the case because even if it extends beyond Deegan\xe2\x80\x99s deliberate actions, it remains irrelevant to Connick\xe2\x80\x99s training obligations. For any Brady violation apart from Deegan\xe2\x80\x99s was surely on the very frontier of our Brady jurisprudence; Connick could not possibly have been on notice decades ago that he was required to instruct his prosecutors to respect a right to untested evidence that we had not (and still have not) recognized. As a consequence, even if I accepted the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that failure-to-train liability could be premised on a single Brady error, I could not agree that the lack of an accurate training regimen caused the violation Connick has conceded.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Brady v. Maryland, 373 U. S. 83, 87 (1963), this Court held that due process requires the prosecution to turn over evidence favorable to the accused and material to his guilt or punishment. That obligation, the parties have stipulated, was dishonored in this case; consequently, John Thompson spent 18 years in prison, 14 of them isolated on death row, before the truth came to light: He was innocent of the charge of attempted armed robbery, and his subsequent trial on a murder charge, by prosecutorial design, was fundamentally unfair.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court holds that the Orleans Parish District Attorney\xe2\x80\x99s Office (District Attorney\xe2\x80\x99s Office or Office) cannot be held liable, in a civil rights action under 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983, for the grave injustice Thompson suffered. That is so, the Court tells us, because Thompson has shown only an aberrant Brady violation, not a routine practice of giving short shrift to Brady\xe2\x80\x99s requirements. The evidence presented to the jury that awarded compensation to Thompson, however, points distinctly away from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s assessment. As the trial record in the \xc2\xa71983 action reveals, the conceded, long-concealed prosecutorial transgressions were neither isolated nor atypical.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0From the top down, the evidence showed, members of the District Attorney\xe2\x80\x99s Office, including the District At-torney himself, misperceived Brady\xe2\x80\x99s compass and therefore inadequately attended to their disclosure obligations. Throughout the pretrial and trial proceedings against Thompson, the team of four engaged in prosecuting him for armed robbery and murder hid from the defense and the court exculpatory information Thompson requested and had a constitutional right to receive. The prosecutors did so despite multiple opportunities, spanning nearly two decades, to set the record straight. Based on the prosecutors\xe2\x80\x99 conduct relating to Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s trials, a fact trier could reasonably conclude that inattention to Brady was standard operating procedure at the District Attorney\xe2\x80\x99s Office.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0What happened here, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion obscures, was no momentary oversight, no single incident of a lone officer\xe2\x80\x99s misconduct. Instead, the evidence demonstrated that misperception and disregard of Brady\xe2\x80\x99s disclosure requirements were pervasive in Orleans Parish. That evidence, I would hold, established persistent, deliberately indifferent conduct for which the District Attorney\xe2\x80\x99s Office bears responsibility under \xc2\xa71983.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I dissent from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment mindful that Brady violations, as this case illustrates, are not easily detected. But for a chance discovery made by a defense team investigator weeks before Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s scheduled execution, the evidence that led to his exoneration might have remained under wraps. The prosecutorial concealment Thompson encountered, however, is bound to be repeated unless municipal agencies bear responsibility\xe2\x80\x94made tangible by \xc2\xa71983 liability\xe2\x80\x94for adequately conveying what Brady requires and for monitoring staff compliance. Failure to train, this Court has said, can give rise to municipal liability under \xc2\xa71983 \xe2\x80\x9cwhere the failure \xe2\x80\xa6 amounts to deliberate indifference to the rights of persons with whom the [untrained employees] come into contact.\xe2\x80\x9d Canton v. Harris, 489 U. S. 378, 388 (1989). That standard is well met in this case.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I turn first to a contextual account of the Brady violations that infected Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s trials.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the early morning hours of December 6, 1984, an assailant shot and killed Raymond T. Liuzza, Jr., son of a prominent New Orleans business executive, on the street fronting the victim\xe2\x80\x99s home. Only one witness saw the assailant. As recorded in two contemporaneous police reports, that eyewitness initially described the assailant as African-American, six feet tall, with \xe2\x80\x9cclose cut hair.\xe2\x80\x9d Record EX2\xe2\x80\x93EX3, EX9.[Footnote 1] Thompson is five feet eight inches tall and, at the time of the murder, styled his hair in a large \xe2\x80\x9cAfro.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at EX13. The police reports of the witness\xe2\x80\x99 immediate identification were not disclosed to Thompson or to the court.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While engaged in the murder investigation, the Orleans Parish prosecutors linked Thompson to another violent crime committed three weeks later. On December 28, an assailant attempted to rob three siblings at gunpoint. During the struggle, the perpetrator\xe2\x80\x99s blood stained the oldest child\xe2\x80\x99s pant leg. That blood, preserved on a swatch of fabric cut from the pant leg by a crime scene analyst, was eventually tested. The test conclusively established that the perpetrator\xe2\x80\x99s blood was type B. Id., at EX151. Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s blood is type O. His prosecutors failed to disclose the existence of the swatch or the test results.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0One month after the Liuzza murder, Richard Perkins, a man who knew Thompson, approached the Liuzza family. Perkins did so after the family\xe2\x80\x99s announcement of a $15,000 reward for information leading to the murderer\xe2\x80\x99s conviction. Police officers surreptitiously recorded the Perkins-Liuzza conversations.[Footnote 2] As documented on tape, Perkins told the family, \xe2\x80\x9cI don\xe2\x80\x99t mind helping [you] catch [the perpetrator], \xe2\x80\xa6 but I would like [you] to help me and, you know, I\xe2\x80\x99ll help [you].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at EX479, EX481. Once the family assured Perkins, \xe2\x80\x9cwe\xe2\x80\x99re on your side, we want to try and help you,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at EX481, Perkins intimated that Thompson and another man, Kevin Freeman, had been involved in Liuzza\xe2\x80\x99s murder. Perkins thereafter told the police what he had learned from Freeman about the murder, and that information was recorded in a police report. Based on Perkins\xe2\x80\x99 account, Thompson and Freeman were arrested on murder charges.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Freeman was six feet tall and went by the name \xe2\x80\x9cKojak\xe2\x80\x9d because he kept his hair so closely trimmed that his scalp was visible. Unlike Thompson, Freeman fit the eyewitness\xe2\x80\x99 initial description of the Liuzza assailant\xe2\x80\x99s height and hair style. As the Court notes, ante, at 4, n.\xc2\xa02, Freeman became the key witness for the prosecution at Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s trial for the murder of Liuzza.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s arrest for the Liuzza murder, the father of the armed robbery victims saw a newspaper photo of Thompson with a large Afro hairstyle and showed it to his children. He reported to the District Attorney\xe2\x80\x99s Office that the children had identified Thompson as their attacker, and the children then picked that same photo out of a \xe2\x80\x9cphotographic lineup.\xe2\x80\x9d Record EX120, EX642\xe2\x80\x93EX643. Indicting Thompson on the basis of these questionable identifications, the District Attorney\xe2\x80\x99s Office did not pause to test the pant leg swatch dyed by the perpetrator\xe2\x80\x99s blood. This lapse ignored or overlooked a prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s notation that the Office \xe2\x80\x9cmay wish to do [a] blood test.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at EX122.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The murder trial was scheduled to begin in mid-March 1985. Armed with the later indictment against Thompson for robbery, however, the prosecutors made a strategic choice: They switched the order of the two trials, proceeding first on the robbery indictment. Id., at EX128\xe2\x80\x93EX129. Their aim was twofold. A robbery conviction gained first would serve to inhibit Thompson from testifying in his own defense at the murder trial, for the prior conviction could be used to impeach his credibility. In addition, an armed robbery conviction could be invoked at the penalty phase of the murder trial in support of the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s plea for the death penalty. Id., at 682.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Recognizing the need for an effective prosecution team, petitioner Harry F. Connick, District Attorney for the Parish of Orleans, appointed his third-in-command, Eric Dubelier, as special prosecutor in both cases. Dubelier enlisted Jim Williams to try the armed robbery case and to assist him in the murder case. Gerry Deegan assisted Williams in the armed robbery case. Bruce Whittaker, the fourth prosecutor involved in the cases, had approved Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s armed robbery indictment.[Footnote 3]

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0During pretrial proceedings in the armed robbery case, Thompson filed a motion requesting access to all materials and information \xe2\x80\x9cfavorable to the defendant\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cmaterial and relevant to the issue of guilt or punishment,\xe2\x80\x9d as well as \xe2\x80\x9cany results or reports\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9cscientific tests or experiments.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at EX144, EX145. Prosecutorial responses to this motion fell far short of Brady compliance.[Footnote 4]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, prosecutors blocked defense counsel\xe2\x80\x99s inspection of the pant leg swatch stained by the robber\xe2\x80\x99s blood. Although Dubelier\xe2\x80\x99s April 3 response stated, \xe2\x80\x9cInspection to be permitted,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at EX149, the swatch was signed out from the property room at 10:05 a.m. the next day, and was not returned until noon on April 10, the day before trial, id., at EX43, EX670. Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s attorney inspected the evidence made available to him and found no blood evidence. No one told defense counsel about the swatch and its recent removal from the property room. Id., at EX701\xe2\x80\x93EX702; Tr. 400\xe2\x80\x93402. But cf. ante, at 17, n.\xc2\xa011 (Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s attorney had \xe2\x80\x9caccess to the evidence locker where the swatch was recorded as evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, Dubelier or Whittaker ordered the crime laboratory to rush a pretrial test of the swatch. Tr. 952\xe2\x80\x93954. Whittaker received the lab report, addressed to his attention, two days before trial commenced. Immediately thereafter, he placed the lab report on Williams\xe2\x80\x99 desk. Record EX151, EX589. Although the lab report conclusively identified the perpetrator\xe2\x80\x99s blood type, id., at EX151, the District Attorney\xe2\x80\x99s Office never revealed the report to the defense.[Footnote 6]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Third, Deegan checked the swatch out of the property room on the morning of the first day of trial, but the prosecution did not produce the swatch at trial. Id., at EX43. Deegan did not return the swatch to the property room after trial, and the swatch has never been found. Tr. of Oral Arg. 37.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c[B]ased solely on the descriptions\xe2\x80\x9d provided by the three victims, Record 683, the jury convicted Thompson of attempted armed robbery. The court sentenced him to 49.5 years without possibility of parole\xe2\x80\x94the maximum available sentence.

\n

D

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Prosecutors continued to disregard Brady during the murder trial, held in May 1985, at which the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s order-of-trial strategy achieved its aim.[Footnote 7] By prosecuting Thompson for armed robbery first\xe2\x80\x94and withholding blood evidence that might have exonerated Thompson of that charge\xe2\x80\x94the District Attorney\xe2\x80\x99s Office disabled Thompson from testifying in his own defense at the murder trial.[Footnote 8] As earlier observed, see supra, at 5, impeaching use of the prior conviction would have severely undermined Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s credibility. And because Thompson was effectively stopped from testifying in his own defense, the testimony of the witnesses against him gained force. The prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s failure to reveal evidence that could have impeached those witnesses helped to seal Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s fate.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, the prosecution undermined Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s efforts to impeach Perkins. Perkins testified that he volunteered information to the police with no knowledge of reward money. Record EX366, EX372\xe2\x80\x93EX373. Because prosecutors had not produced the audiotapes of Perkins\xe2\x80\x99 conversations with the Liuzza family (or a police summary of the tapes), Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s attorneys could do little to cast doubt on Perkins\xe2\x80\x99 credibility. In closing argument, the prosecution emphasized that Thompson presented no \xe2\x80\x9cdirect evidence\xe2\x80\x9d that reward money had motivated any of the witnesses. Id., at EX3171\xe2\x80\x93EX3172.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, the prosecution impeded Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s impeachment of key witness Kevin Freeman. It did so by failing to disclose a police report containing Perkins\xe2\x80\x99 account of\nwhat he had learned from Freeman about the murder. See supra, at 4. Freeman\xe2\x80\x99s trial testimony was materially inconsistent with that report. Tr. 382\xe2\x80\x93384, 612\xe2\x80\x93614;\nRecord EX270\xe2\x80\x93EX274. Lacking any knowledge of the police report, Thompson could not point to the inconsistencies.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Third, and most vital, the eyewitness\xe2\x80\x99 initial description of the assailant\xe2\x80\x99s hair, see supra, at 3, was of prime relevance, for it suggested that Freeman, not Thompson, murdered Liuzza, see supra, at 4. The materiality of the eyewitness\xe2\x80\x99 contemporaneous description of the murderer should have been altogether apparent to the prosecution. Failure to produce the police reports setting out what the eyewitness first said not only undermined efforts to impeach that witness and the police officer who initially interviewed him. The omission left defense counsel without knowledge that the prosecutors were restyling the killer\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cclose cut hair\xe2\x80\x9d into an \xe2\x80\x9cAfro.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Prosecutors finessed the discrepancy between the eyewitness\xe2\x80\x99 initial description and Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s appearance. They asked leading questions prompting the eyewitness to agree on the stand that the perpetrator\xe2\x80\x99s hair was \xe2\x80\x9cafro type,\xe2\x80\x9d yet \xe2\x80\x9cstraight back.\xe2\x80\x9d Record EX322\xe2\x80\x93EX323. Corroboratively, the police officer\xe2\x80\x94after refreshing his recollection by reviewing material at the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s table\xe2\x80\x94gave artful testimony. He characterized the witness\xe2\x80\x99 initial description of the perpetrator\xe2\x80\x99s hair as \xe2\x80\x9cblack and short, afro style.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at EX265 (emphasis added). As prosecutors well knew, nothing in the withheld police reports, which described the murderer\xe2\x80\x99s hair simply as \xe2\x80\x9cclose cut,\xe2\x80\x9d portrayed a perpetrator with an Afro or Afro-style hair.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The jury found Thompson guilty of first-degree murder. Having prevented Thompson from testifying that Freeman was the killer, the prosecution delivered its ultimate argument. Because Thompson was already serving a near-life sentence for attempted armed robbery, the prosecution urged, the only way to punish him for murder was to execute him. The strategy worked as planned; Thompson was sentenced to death.

Id., at EX712\xe2\x80\x93EX713.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On April 16, 1999, the State of Louisiana scheduled Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s execution. Id., at EX1366\xe2\x80\x93EX1367. \n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s lawyers presented to the trial court the crime lab report showing that the robber\xe2\x80\x99s blood type was B, and a report identifying Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s blood type as O. \n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Connick sought an abbreviated hearing. A full hearing was unnecessary, he urged, because the Office had confessed error and had moved to dismiss the armed robbery charges. See, e.g., id., at EX617. The court insisted on a public hearing. Given \xe2\x80\x9cthe history of this case,\xe2\x80\x9d the court said, it \xe2\x80\x9cwas not willing to accept the representations that [Connick] and [his] office made [in their motion to dismiss].\xe2\x80\x9d id., at EX882. After a full day\xe2\x80\x99s hearing, the court vacated Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s attempted armed robbery conviction and dismissed the charges. Before doing so, the court admonished:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[A]ll day long there have been a number of young Assistant D.\xc2\xa0A.\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\xa6 sitting in this courtroom watching this, and I hope they take home \xe2\x80\xa6 and take to heart the message that this kind of conduct cannot go on in this Parish if this Criminal Justice System is going to work.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at EX883.

Tr. 986; cf. supra, at 7, n.\xc2\xa06. And he told the investigating prosecutor that the grand jury \xe2\x80\x9cw[ould] make [his] job more difficult.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. 978\xe2\x80\x93979. \n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On July 16, 2003, Thompson commenced a civil action under 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983 alleging that Connick, other officials of the Orleans Parish District Attorney\xe2\x80\x99s Office, and the Office itself, had violated his constitutional rights by wrongfully withholding Brady evidence. Thompson sought to hold Connick and the District Attorney\xe2\x80\x99s Office liable for failure adequately to train prosecutors concerning their Brady obligations. Such liability attaches, I agree with the Court, only when the failure \xe2\x80\x9camount[s] to \xe2\x80\x98deliberate indifference to the rights of persons with whom the [untrained employees] come into contact.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9 (quoting Canton v. Harris, 489 U. S. 378, 388 (1989)). I disagree, however, with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that Thompson failed to prove deliberate indifference.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Having weighed all the evidence, the jury in the \xc2\xa71983 case found for Thompson, concluding that the District Attorney\xe2\x80\x99s Office had been deliberately indifferent to Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s Brady rights and to the need for training and supervision to safeguard those rights. \xe2\x80\x9cViewing the evidence in the light most favorable to [Thompson], as appropriate in light of the verdic[t] rendered by the jury,\xe2\x80\x9d Patrick v. Burget, 486 U. S. 94, 98, n. 3 (1988), I see no cause to upset the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s determination, affirmed by the Fifth Circuit, that \xe2\x80\x9cample evidence \xe2\x80\xa6 adduced at trial\xe2\x80\x9d supported the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict. Record 1917.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Over 20 years ago, we observed that a municipality\xe2\x80\x99s failure to provide training may be so egregious that, even without notice of prior constitutional violations, the failure \xe2\x80\x9ccould properly be characterized as \xe2\x80\x98deliberate indifference\xe2\x80\x99 to constitutional rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Canton, 489 U. S., at 390, n. 10. \xe2\x80\x9c[I]n light of the duties assigned to specific officers or employees,\xe2\x80\x9d Canton recognized, \xe2\x80\x9cit may happen that \xe2\x80\xa6 the need for more or different training is so obvious, and the inadequacy so likely to result in the violation of constitutional rights, that the policymakers \xe2\x80\xa6 can reasonably be said to have been deliberately indifferent to the need.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 390. Thompson presented convincing evidence to satisfy this standard.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s \xc2\xa71983 suit proceeded to a jury trial on two theories of liability: First, the Orleans Parish Office\xe2\x80\x99s official Brady policy was unconstitutional; and second, Connick was deliberately indifferent to an obvious need to train his prosecutors about their Brady obligations. Connick\xe2\x80\x99s Brady policy directed prosecutors to \xe2\x80\x9cturn over what was required by state and federal law, but no more.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 6\xe2\x80\x937. The jury thus understandably rejected Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s claim that the official policy itself was unconstitutional. Ante, at 5.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The jury found, however, that Connick was deliberately indifferent to the need to train prosecutors about Brady\xe2\x80\x99s command. On the special verdict form, the jury answered yes to the following question:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cWas the Brady violation in the armed robbery case or any infringements of John Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s rights in the murder trial substantially caused by [Connick\xe2\x80\x99s] failure, through deliberate indifference, to establish policies and procedures to protect one accused of a crime from these constitutional violations?\xe2\x80\x9d Record 1585.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Consistent with the question put to the jury, and without objection, the court instructed the jurors: \xe2\x80\x9c[Y]ou are not limited to the nonproduced blood evidence and the resulting infringement of Mr. Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s right to testify at the murder trial. You may consider all of the evidence presented during this trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. 1099; Record 1620.[Footnote 11] But cf. ante, at 2, 6, 10, n.\xc2\xa07, 16; ante, at 1 (Scalia, J., concurring) (maintaining that the case involves a single Brady violation). That evidence included a stipulation that in his retrial for the Liuzza murder, Thompson had introduced ten exhibits containing relevant information withheld by the prosecution in 1985. See supra, at 13.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Abundant evidence supported the jury\xe2\x80\x99s finding that additional Brady training was obviously necessary to ensure that Brady violations would not occur: (1) Connick, the Office\xe2\x80\x99s sole policymaker, misunderstood Brady. (2) Other leaders in the Office, who bore direct responsibility for training less experienced prosecutors, were similarly uninformed about Brady. (3) Prosecutors in the Office received no Brady training. (4) The Office shirked its responsibility to keep prosecutors abreast of relevant legal developments concerning Brady requirements. As a result of these multiple shortfalls, it was hardly surprising that Brady violations in fact occurred, severely undermining the integrity of Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s trials.

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Connick was the Office\xe2\x80\x99s sole policymaker, and his testimony exposed a flawed understanding of a prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s Brady obligations. First, Connick admitted to the jury that his earlier understanding of Brady, conveyed in prior sworn testimony, had been too narrow. Tr. 181\xe2\x80\x93182. Second, Connick confessed to having withheld a crime lab report \xe2\x80\x9cone time as a prosecutor and I got indicted by the U. S. Attorney over here for doing it.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 872. Third, even at trial Connick persisted in misstating Brady\xe2\x80\x99s requirements. For example, Connick urged that there could be no Brady violation arising out of \xe2\x80\x9cthe inadvertent conduct of [an] assistant under pressure with a lot of case load.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. 188\xe2\x80\x93189. The court, however, correctly instructed the jury that, in determining whether there has been a Brady violation, the \xe2\x80\x9cgood or bad faith of the prosecution does not matter.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. 1094\xe2\x80\x931095.

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The testimony of other leaders in the District Attorney\xe2\x80\x99s Office revealed similar misunderstandings. Those misunderstandings, the jury could find, were in large part responsible for the gross disregard of Brady rights Thompson experienced. Dubelier admitted that he never reviewed police files, but simply relied on the police to flag any potential Brady information. Tr. 542. The court, however, instructed the jury that an individual prosecutor has a \xe2\x80\x9cduty \xe2\x80\xa6 to learn of any favorable evidence known to others acting on the government\xe2\x80\x99s behalf in the case, including the police.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1095; Record 1614. Williams was asked whether \xe2\x80\x9cBrady material includes documents in the possession of the district attorney that could be used to impeach a witness, to show that he\xe2\x80\x99s lying\xe2\x80\x9d; he responded simply, and mistakenly, \xe2\x80\x9cNo.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. 381. The testimony of \xe2\x80\x9chigh-ranking individuals in the Orleans Parish District Attorney\xe2\x80\x99s Office,\xe2\x80\x9d Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s expert explained,[Footnote 12] exposed \xe2\x80\x9ccomplete errors \xe2\x80\xa6 as to what Brady required [prosecutors] to do.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 427, 434. \xe2\x80\x9cDubelier had no understanding of his obligations under Brady whatsoever,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 458, the expert observed, and Williams \xe2\x80\x9cis still not sure what his obligations were under Brady,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 448. But cf. ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x935 (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t was undisputed at trial that the prosecutors were familiar with the general Brady requirement that the State disclose to the defense evidence in its possession that is favorable to the accused.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The jury could attribute the violations of Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s rights directly to prosecutors\xe2\x80\x99 misapprehension of Brady. The prosecution had no obligation to produce the \xe2\x80\x9cclose-cut hair\xe2\x80\x9d police reports, Williams maintained, because newspaper reports had suggested that witness descrip-\ntions were not consistent with Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s appearance. Therefore, Williams urged, the defense already \xe2\x80\x9chad everything.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. 139. Dubelier tendered an alternative ex-\nplanation for the nondisclosure. In Dubelier\xe2\x80\x99s view, the descriptions were not \xe2\x80\x9cinconsistent with [Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s] appearance,\xe2\x80\x9d as portrayed in a police photograph showing Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s hair extending at least three inches above his forehead. Id., at 171\xe2\x80\x93172; Record EX73. Williams insisted that he had discharged the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s duty to disclose the blood evidence by mentioning, in a motion hearing, that the prosecution intended to obtain a blood sample from Thompson. Tr. 393\xe2\x80\x93394. During the armed robbery trial, Williams told one of the victims that the results of the blood test made on the swatch had been \xe2\x80\x9cinconclusive.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 962. And he testified in the \xc2\xa71983 action that the lab report was not Brady material \xe2\x80\x9cbecause I didn\xe2\x80\x99t know what the blood type of Mr. Thompson was.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. 393. But see supra, at 6\xe2\x80\x937, n.\xc2\xa05 (District Court instructed the jury that the lab report was Brady material).

\n

3

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Connick should have comprehended that Orleans Parish prosecutors lacked essential guidance on Brady and its application. In fact, Connick has effectively conceded that Brady training in his Office was inadequate. Tr. of Oral Arg. 60. Connick explained to the jury that prosecutors\xe2\x80\x99 offices must \xe2\x80\x9cmake \xe2\x80\xa6 very clear to [new prosecutors] what their responsibility [i]s\xe2\x80\x9d under Brady and must not \xe2\x80\x9cgiv[e] them a lot of leeway.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. 834\xe2\x80\x93835. But the jury heard ample evidence that Connick\xe2\x80\x99s Office gave prosecutors no Brady guidance, and had installed no procedures to monitor Brady compliance.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1985, Connick acknowledged, many of his prosecutors \xe2\x80\x9cwere coming fresh out of law school,\xe2\x80\x9d and the Office\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9c[h]uge turnover\xe2\x80\x9d allowed attorneys with little experience to advance quickly to supervisory positions. See Tr. 853\xe2\x80\x93854, 832. By 1985, Dubelier and Williams were two of the highest ranking attorneys in the Office, id., at 342, 356\xe2\x80\x93357, yet neither man had even five years of experience as a prosecutor, see supra, at 5, n.\xc2\xa03; Record EX746; Tr. 55, 571\xe2\x80\x93576.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Dubelier and Williams learned the prosecutorial craft in Connick\xe2\x80\x99s Office, and, as earlier observed, see supra, at 17\xe2\x80\x9318, their testimony manifested a woefully deficient understanding of Brady. Dubelier and Williams told the jury that they did not recall any Brady training in the Office. Tr. 170\xe2\x80\x93171, 364.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Connick testified that he relied on supervisors, including Dubelier and Williams, to ensure prosecutors were familiar with their Brady obligations. Tr. 805\xe2\x80\x93806. Yet Connick did not inquire whether the supervisors themselves understood the importance of teaching newer prosecutors about Brady. Riehlmann could not \xe2\x80\x9crecall that [he] was ever trained or instructed by anybody about [his] Brady obligations,\xe2\x80\x9d on the job or otherwise. Tr. 728\xe2\x80\x93729. Whittaker agreed it was possible for \xe2\x80\x9cinexperienced lawyers, just a few weeks out of law school with no training,\xe2\x80\x9d to bear responsibility for \xe2\x80\x9cdecisions on \xe2\x80\xa6 whether material was Brady material and had to be produced.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 319.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s expert characterized Connick\xe2\x80\x99s supervision regarding Brady as \xe2\x80\x9cthe blind leading the blind.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. 458. For example, in 1985 trial attorneys \xe2\x80\x9csometimes \xe2\x80\xa6 went to Mr. Connick\xe2\x80\x9d with Brady questions, \xe2\x80\x9cand he would tell them\xe2\x80\x9d how to proceed. Tr. 892. But Connick acknowledged that he had \xe2\x80\x9cstopped reading law books \xe2\x80\xa6 and looking at opinions\xe2\x80\x9d when he was first elected District Attorney in 1974. Id., at 175\xe2\x80\x93176.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As part of their training, prosecutors purportedly attended a pretrial conference with the Office\xe2\x80\x99s chief of trials before taking a case to trial. Connick intended the practice to provide both training and accountability. But it achieved neither aim in Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s prosecutions, for Dubelier and Williams, as senior prosecutors in the Office, were free to take cases to trial without pretrying them, and that is just how they proceeded in Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s prosecutions. Id., at 901\xe2\x80\x93902; Record 685. But cf. ante, at 13 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]rial chiefs oversaw the preparation of the cases.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Prosecutors confirmed that training in the District Attorney\xe2\x80\x99s Office, overall, was deficient. Soon after Connick retired, a survey of assistant district attorneys in the Office revealed that more than half felt that they had not received the training they needed to do their jobs. Tr. 178.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thompson, it bears emphasis, is not complaining about the absence of formal training sessions. Tr. of Oral Arg. 55. But cf. ante, at 15\xe2\x80\x9316. His complaint does not demand that Brady compliance be enforced in any particular way. He asks only that Brady obligations be communicated accurately and genuinely enforced.[Footnote 13] Because that did not happen in the District Attorney\xe2\x80\x99s Office, it was inevitable that prosecutors would misapprehend Brady. Had Brady\xe2\x80\x99s importance been brought home to prosecutors, surely at least one of the four officers who knew of the swatch and lab report would have revealed their existence to defense counsel and the court.[Footnote 14]

\n

4

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Louisiana did not require continuing legal education at the time of Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s trials. Tr. 361. But cf. ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313. Primary responsibility for keeping prosecutors au courant with developments in the law, therefore, resided in the District Attorney\xe2\x80\x99s Office. Over the course of Connick\xe2\x80\x99s tenure as District Attorney, the jury learned, the Office\xe2\x80\x99s chief of appeals circulated memoranda when appellate courts issued important opinions. Tr. 751\xe2\x80\x93754, 798.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The 1987 Office policy manual was a compilation of memoranda on criminal law and practice circulated to prosecutors from 1974, when Connick became District Attorney, through 1987. Id., at 798. The manual contained four sentences, nothing more, on Brady.[Footnote 15] This slim instruction, the jury learned, was notably inaccurate, incomplete, and dated. Tr. 798\xe2\x80\x93804, 911\xe2\x80\x93918. But cf. ante, at 13 (\xe2\x80\x9cSenior attorneys also circulated court decisions and instructional memoranda to keep the prose-cutors abreast of relevant legal developments.\xe2\x80\x9d). For example, the manual did not acknowledge what Giglio v. United States, 405 U. S. 150 (1972), made plain: Impeachment evidence is Brady material prosecutors are obligated to disclose.[Footnote 16]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, the evidence permitted the jury to reach the following conclusions. First, Connick did not ensure that prosecutors in his Office knew their Brady obligations; he neither confirmed their familiarity with Brady when he hired them, nor saw to it that training took place on his watch. Second, the need for Brady training and monitoring was obvious to Connick. Indeed he so testified. Third, Connick\xe2\x80\x99s cavalier approach to his staff\xe2\x80\x99s knowledge and observation of Brady requirements contributed to a culture of inattention to Brady in Orleans Parish.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As earlier noted, see supra, at 11, Connick resisted an effort to hold prosecutors accountable for Brady compliance because he felt the effort would \xe2\x80\x9cmake [his] job more difficult.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. 978. He never disciplined or fired a single prosecutor for violating Brady. Tr. 182\xe2\x80\x93183. The jury was told of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Kyles v. Whitley, 514 U. S. 419 (1995), a capital case prosecuted by Connick\xe2\x80\x99s Office that garnered attention because it featured \xe2\x80\x9cso many instances of the state\xe2\x80\x99s failure to disclose exculpatory evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 455 (Stevens, J., concurring). When questioned about Kyles, Connick told the jury he was satisfied with his Office\xe2\x80\x99s practices and saw no need, occasioned by Kyles, to make any changes. Tr. 184\xe2\x80\x93185. In both quantity and quality, then, the evidence canvassed here was more than sufficient to warrant a jury determination that Connick and the prosecutors who served under him were not merely negligent regarding Brady. Rather, they were deliberately indifferent to what the law\nrequires.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Canton, this Court spoke of circumstances in which the need for training may be \xe2\x80\x9cso obvious,\xe2\x80\x9d and the lack of training \xe2\x80\x9cso likely\xe2\x80\x9d to result in constitutional violations, that policymakers who do not provide for the requisite training \xe2\x80\x9ccan reasonably be said to have been deliberately indifferent to the need\xe2\x80\x9d for such training. 489 U. S., at 390. This case, I am convinced, belongs in the category Canton marked out.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Canton offered an often-cited illustration. \xe2\x80\x9c[C]ity policymakers know to a moral certainty that their police officers will be required to arrest fleeing felons.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid., n.\xc2\xa010. Those policymakers, Canton observed, equip police officers with firearms to facilitate such arrests. Ibid. The need to instruct armed officers about \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutional limitations on the use of deadly force,\xe2\x80\x9d Canton said, is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98so obvious,\xe2\x80\x99 that failure to [train the officers] could properly be characterized as \xe2\x80\x98deliberate indifference\xe2\x80\x99 to constitutional rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court, tracking Canton\xe2\x80\x99s language, instructed the jury that Thompson could prevail on his \xe2\x80\x9cdeliberate indifference\xe2\x80\x9d claim only if the evidence persuaded the jury on three points. First, Connick \xe2\x80\x9cwas certain that prosecutors would confront the situation where they would have to decide which evidence was required by the Constitution to be provided to the accused.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. 1099. Second, \xe2\x80\x9cthe situation involved a difficult choice[,] or one that prosecutors had a history of mishandling, such that additional training, supervision or monitoring was clearly needed.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Third, \xe2\x80\x9cthe wrong choice by a prosecutor in that situation would frequently cause a deprivation of an accused\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; Record 1619\xe2\x80\x931620; see Canton, 489 U. S., at 390, and n.\xc2\xa010; Walker v. New York, 974 F.\xc2\xa02d 293, 297\xe2\x80\x93298 (CA2 1992).[Footnote 17]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners used this formulation of the failure to train standard in pretrial and post-trial submissions, Record 1256\xe2\x80\x931257, 1662, and in their own proposed jury instruction on deliberate indifference.[Footnote 18] Nor do petitioners dispute that Connick \xe2\x80\x9ckn[e]w to a moral certainty that\xe2\x80\x9d his prosecutors would regularly face Brady decisions. See Canton, 489 U. S., at 390, n.\xc2\xa010.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The jury, furthermore, could reasonably find that Brady rights may involve choices so difficult that Connick obviously knew or should have known prosecutors needed more than perfunctory training to make the correct choices. See Canton, 489 U. S., at 390, and n. 10.[Footnote 19] As demonstrated earlier, see supra, at 16\xe2\x80\x9318, even at trial prosecutors failed to give an accurate account of their Brady obligations. And, again as emphasized earlier, see supra, at 18\xe2\x80\x9320, the evidence permitted the jury to conclude that Connick should have known Brady training in his office bordered on \xe2\x80\x9czero.\xe2\x80\x9d See Tr. of Oral Arg. 41. Moreover, Connick understood that newer prosecutors needed \xe2\x80\x9cvery clear\xe2\x80\x9d guidance and should not be left to grapple with Brady on their own. Tr. 834\xe2\x80\x93835. It was thus \xe2\x80\x9cobvious\xe2\x80\x9d to him, the jury could find, that constitutional rights would be in jeopardy if prosecutors received slim to no Brady training.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Based on the evidence presented, the jury could conclude that Brady errors by untrained prosecutors would frequently cause deprivations of defendants\xe2\x80\x99 constitutional rights. The jury learned of several Brady oversights in Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s trials and heard testimony that Connick\xe2\x80\x99s Office had one of the worst Brady records in the country. Tr. 163. Because prosecutors faced considerable pressure to get convictions, id., at 317, 341, and were instructed to \xe2\x80\x9cturn over what was required by state and federal law, but no more,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 6\xe2\x80\x937, the risk was all too real that they would err by withholding rather than revealing information favorable to the defense.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, despite Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s protestations to the contrary, ante, at 1, 5, the Brady violations in Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s prosecutions were not singular and they were not aberrational. They were just what one would expect given the attitude toward Brady pervasive in the District Attorney\xe2\x80\x99s Office. Thompson demonstrated that no fewer than five prosecutors\xe2\x80\x94the four trial prosecutors and Riehlmann\xe2\x80\x94disregarded his Brady rights. He established that they kept from him, year upon year, evidence vital to his defense. Their conduct, he showed with equal force, was a foreseeable consequence of lax training in, and absence of monitoring of, a legal requirement fundamental to a fair trial.[Footnote 20]

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Unquestionably, a municipality that leaves police officers untrained in constitutional limits on the use of deadly weapons places lives in jeopardy. Canton, 489 U. S., at 390, n.\xc2\xa010. But as this case so vividly shows, a municipality that empowers prosecutors to press for a death sentence without ensuring that those prosecutors know and honor Brady rights may be no less \xe2\x80\x9cdeliberately indifferent\xe2\x80\x9d to the risk to innocent lives.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Brady, this Court has long recognized, is among the most basic safeguards brigading a criminal defendant\xe2\x80\x99s fair trial right. See Cone v. Bell, 556 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (slip op., at 1). See also United States v. Bagley, 473 U. S. 667, 695 (1985) (Marshall, J., dissenting). Vigilance in superintending prosecutors\xe2\x80\x99 attention to Brady\xe2\x80\x99s requirement is all the more important for this reason: A Brady violation, by its nature, causes suppression of evidence beyond the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s capacity to ferret out. Because the absence of the withheld evidence may result in the conviction of an innocent defendant, it is unconscionable not to impose reasonable controls impelling prosecutors to bring the information to light.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court nevertheless holds Canton\xe2\x80\x99s example inapposite. It maintains that professional obligations, ethics rules, and training\xe2\x80\x94including on-the-job training\xe2\x80\x94set attorneys apart from other municipal employees, including rookie police officers. Ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9315. Connick \xe2\x80\x9chad every incentive at trial to attempt to establish\xe2\x80\x9d that he could reasonably rely on the professional education and status of his staff. Cf. ante, at 10, n.\xc2\xa06. But the jury heard and rejected his argument to that effect. Tr. 364, 576\xe2\x80\x93577, 834\xe2\x80\x93835.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court advances Connick\xe2\x80\x99s argument with greater clarity, but with no greater support. On what basis can one be confident that law schools acquaint students with prosecutors\xe2\x80\x99 unique obligation under Brady? Whittaker told the jury he did not recall covering Brady in his criminal procedure class in law school. Tr. 335. Dubelier\xe2\x80\x99s alma mater, like most other law faculties, does not make criminal procedure a required course.[Footnote 21]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Connick suggested that the bar examination ensures that new attorneys will know what Brady demands. Tr. 835. Research indicates, however, that from 1980 to the present, Brady questions have not accounted for even\n10% of the total points in the criminal law and procedure section of any administration of the Louisiana Bar Examination.[Footnote 22] A person sitting for the Louisiana Bar Examination, moreover, need pass only five of the exam\xe2\x80\x99s nine sections.[Footnote 23] One can qualify for admission to the profession with no showing of even passing knowledge of criminal law and procedure.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion that lawyers do not need Brady training because they \xe2\x80\x9care equipped with the tools to find, interpret, and apply legal principles,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 17\xe2\x80\x9318, \xe2\x80\x9cblinks reality\xe2\x80\x9d and is belied by the facts of this case. See Brief for Former Federal Civil Rights Officials and Prosecutors as Amici Curiae 13. Connick himself recognized that his prosecutors, because of their inexperience, were not so equipped. Indeed, \xe2\x80\x9cunderstanding and complying with Brady obligations are not easy tasks, and the appropriate way to resolve Brady issues is not always self-evident.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Former Federal Civil Rights Officials and Prosecutors as Amici Curiae 6. \xe2\x80\x9cBrady compliance,\xe2\x80\x9d therefore, \xe2\x80\x9cis too much at risk, and too fundamental to the fairness of our criminal justice system, to be taken for granted,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ctraining remains critical.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 3, 7.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority further suggests that a prior pattern of similar violations is necessary to show deliberate indifference to defendants\xe2\x80\x99 Brady rights. See ante, at 5\xe2\x80\x936, and n.\xc2\xa04, 11\xe2\x80\x9312.[Footnote 24] The text of \xc2\xa71983 contains no such limitation.[Footnote 25] Nor is there any reason to imply such a limitation.[Footnote 26] A district attorney\xe2\x80\x99s deliberate indifference might be shown in several ways short of a prior pattern.[Footnote 27] This case is one such instance. Connick, who himself had been indicted for suppression of evidence, created a tinderbox in Orleans Parish in which Brady violations were nigh inevitable. And when they did occur, Connick insisted there was no need to change anything, and opposed efforts to hold prosecutors accountable on the ground that doing so would make his job more difficult.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A District Attorney aware of his office\xe2\x80\x99s high turnover rate, who recruits prosecutors fresh out of law school and promotes them rapidly through the ranks, bears responsibility for ensuring that on-the-job training takes place. In short, the buck stops with him.[Footnote 28] As the Court recognizes, \xe2\x80\x9cthe duty to produce Brady evidence to the defense\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9c[a]mong prosecutors\xe2\x80\x99 unique ethical obligations.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13. The evidence in this case presents overwhelming support for the conclusion that the Orleans Parish Office slighted its responsibility to the profession and to the State\xe2\x80\x99s system of justice by providing no on-the-job Brady training. Connick was not \xe2\x80\x9centitled to rely on prosecutors\xe2\x80\x99 professional training,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 14, for Connick himself should have been the principal insurer of that training.
