\nWAGNON V. PRAIRIE BAND POTAWATOMI NATION
\n
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\n
\n

WAGNON, SECRETARY, KANSAS DEPARTMENT OF REVENUE v. PRAIRIE BAND POTAWATOMI NATION

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the tenth circuit

\n

No. 04\xe2\x80\x93631.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 3, 2005\xe2\x80\x94Decided December 6, 2005

\n

Kansas\xe2\x80\x99 motor fuel tax applies to the receipt of fuel by off-reservation non-Indian distributors who subsequently deliver it to the gas station owned by, and located on the Reservation of, the Prairie Band Potawatomi Nation (Nation). The station is meant to accommodate reservation traffic, including patrons driving to the casino the Nation owns and operates there. Most of the station\xe2\x80\x99s fuel is sold to such patrons, but some sales are made to persons living or working on the reservation. The Nation\xe2\x80\x99s own tax on the station\xe2\x80\x99s fuel sales generates revenue for reservation infrastructure. The Nation sued for declaratory judgment and injunctive relief from the State\xe2\x80\x99s collection of its tax from distributors delivering fuel to the reservation. Granting the State summary judgment, the District Court determined that the balance of state, federal, and tribal interests tilted in favor of the State under the test set forth in White Mountain Apache Tribe v. Bracker, 448 U. S. 136. The Tenth Circuit reversed, agreeing with the Nation that the Kansas tax is an impermissible affront to its sovereignty. The court reasoned that the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s fuel revenues were derived from value generated primarily on its reservation\xe2\x80\x94i.e., the creation of a new fuel market by virtue of the casino\xe2\x80\x94and that the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s interests in taxing this reservation-created value to raise revenue for reservation infrastructure outweighed the State\xe2\x80\x99s general interest in raising revenues.

\n

Held: Because Kansas\xe2\x80\x99 motor fuel tax is a nondiscriminatory tax imposed on an off-reservation transaction between non-Indians, the tax is valid and poses no affront to the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s sovereignty. The Bracker interest-balancing test does not apply to a tax that results from an off-reservation transaction between non-Indians. Pp. 4\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0The Kansas tax is imposed on non-Indian distributors based upon their off-reservation receipt of motor fuel, not on the on-reservation sale and delivery of that fuel. Pp. 4\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Under this Court\xe2\x80\x99s Indian tax immunity cases, the \xe2\x80\x9cwho\xe2\x80\x9d and the \xe2\x80\x9cwhere\xe2\x80\x9d of a challenged tax have significant consequences. \xe2\x80\x9cThe initial and frequently dispositive question \xe2\x80\xa6 is who bears [a tax\xe2\x80\x99s] legal incidence,\xe2\x80\x9d Oklahoma Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Chickasaw Nation, 515 U. S. 450, 458 (emphasis added). Moreover, the States are categorically barred from placing a tax\xe2\x80\x99s legal incidence \xe2\x80\x9con a tribe or on tribal members for sales made inside Indian country\xe2\x80\x9d without congressional authorization. Id., at 459 (emphasis added). Even when a State imposes a tax\xe2\x80\x99s legal incidence on a non-Indian seller, the tax may nonetheless be pre-empted if the transaction giving rise to tax liability occurs on the reservation and the imposition of the tax fails to satisfy the Bracker interest-balancing test. See, e.g., Central Machinery Co. v. Arizona Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 448 U. S. 160. Pp. 4\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The Court rejects the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s argument that it is entitled to prevail under Chickasaw\xe2\x80\x99s categorical bar because the fairest reading of the Kansas statute is that the tax\xe2\x80\x99s legal incidence actually falls on the Tribe on the reservation. Under the statute, the tax\xe2\x80\x99s incidence is expressly imposed on the distributor that first receives the fuel. Such \xe2\x80\x9cdispositive language\xe2\x80\x9d from the state legislature is determinative of who bears a state excise tax\xe2\x80\x99s legal incidence. Chickasaw, supra, at 461. Even absent such \xe2\x80\x9cdispositive language,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court would nonetheless conclude that the tax\xe2\x80\x99s legal incidence is on the distributor because Kansas law makes clear that it is the distributor, not the retailer, that is liable for the tax. The lower courts and the Kansas agency charged with administering the motor fuel tax reached the same conclusion. Kaul v. Kansas Dept. of Revenue, 266 Kan. 464, 970 P.\xc2\xa02d 60, distinguished. Pp. 5\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Also rejected is the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s alternative argument that the Bracker test must be applied irrespective of who bears the Kansas tax\xe2\x80\x99s legal incidence because the tax arises as a result of the on-reservation sale and delivery of fuel. The Nation presented a starkly different, and correct, interpretation of the statute in the Tenth Circuit, arguing that the balancing test is appropriate even though the tax\xe2\x80\x99s legal incidence is imposed on the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s non-Indian distributor and is triggered by the distributor\xe2\x80\x99s receipt of fuel outside the reservation. The Nation\xe2\x80\x99s argument here is rebutted by provisions of the Kansas statute demonstrating that the only taxable event occurs when the distributor first receives the fuel and by a final determination by the State reaching the same conclusion. The Nation\xe2\x80\x99s theory that the existence of statutory deductions for certain postreceipt transactions make it impossible for a distributor to calculate its ultimate tax liability without knowing whether, where, and to whom the fuel is ultimately sold or delivered suffers from several conceptual defects. For example, availability of the deductions does not change the nature of the taxable event, the distributor\xe2\x80\x99s receipt of the fuel. Pp. 8\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0The Tenth Circuit erred in concluding that the Kansas tax is nevertheless subject to Bracker\xe2\x80\x99s test. That test applies only where \xe2\x80\x9ca State asserts authority over the conduct of non-Indians engaging in activity on the reservation.\xe2\x80\x9d 448 U. S., at 144. It has never been applied where, as here, a state tax imposed on a non-Indian arises from a transaction occurring off the reservation. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s Indian tax immunity cases counsel against such an application. Pp. 12\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Limiting the Bracker test exclusively to on-reservation transactions between a nontribal entity and a tribe or tribal member is consistent with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s unique Indian tax immunity jurisprudence, which relies \xe2\x80\x9cheavily on the doctrine of tribal sovereignty [giving] state law \xe2\x80\x98no role to play\xe2\x80\x99 within a tribe\xe2\x80\x99s territorial boundaries,\xe2\x80\x9d Oklahoma Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Sac and Fox Nation, 508 U. S. 114, 123\xe2\x80\x93124. The Court has taken an altogether different course, by contrast, when a State asserts its taxing authority outside of Indian Country. E.g., Chickasaw, 515 U. S. 450. In such cases, \xe2\x80\x9c[a]bsent express federal law to the contrary, Indians going beyond reservation boundaries have generally been held subject to non-discriminatory state law otherwise applicable to all citizens of the State.\xe2\x80\x9d Mescalero Apache Tribe v. Jones, 411 U. S. 145, 148\xe2\x80\x93149. If a State may apply a nondiscriminatory tax to Indians who have gone beyond the reservation\xe2\x80\x99s boundaries, it may also apply a nondiscriminatory tax where, as here, the tax is imposed on non-Indians as a result of an off-reservation transaction. In these circumstances, Bracker is inapplicable. Cf. Arizona Dept. of Revenue v. Blaze Constr. Co., 526 U. S. 32, 37. The application of the test here is also inconsistent with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s efforts to establish \xe2\x80\x9cbright line standard[s]\xe2\x80\x9d in the tax administration context. Ibid. The Nation is not entitled to interest balancing by virtue of its claim that the Kansas tax interferes with the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s own motor fuel tax. This is ultimately a complaint about the state tax\xe2\x80\x99s downstream economic consequences. The Nation cannot invalidate that tax by complaining about a decrease in its revenues. See, e.g., Washington v. Confederated Tribes of Colville Reservation, 447 U. S. 134, 156. Nor would the Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis change if legal significance were accorded the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s decision to label a portion of its gas station\xe2\x80\x99s revenues as tax proceeds. See id., at 184, n.\xc2\xa09. Pp. 12\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0This Court rejects the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s contention that the Kansas tax is invalid notwithstanding the Bracker test\xe2\x80\x99s inapplicability because it exempts from taxation fuel sold or delivered to state and federal sovereigns and is therefore impermissibly discriminatory. The Nation is not similarly situated to the exempted sovereigns. While Kansas\xe2\x80\x99 tax pays for roads and bridges on the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s reservation, including the main highway used by casino patrons, Kansas offers no such services to the several States or the Federal Government. Moreover, to the extent Kansas retailers bear the tax\xe2\x80\x99s cost, that burden applies equally to all retailers within the State regardless of whether they are located on a reservation. P. 18.

\n

379 F.\xc2\xa03d 979, reversed.

JOAN WAGNON, SECRETARY, KANSAS DEPART-\nMENT OF REVENUE, PETITIONER v. PRAIRIE\nBAND POTAWATOMI NATION

\n

\n

[December 6, 2005]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Thomas delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The State of Kansas imposes a tax on the receipt of motor fuel by fuel distributors within its boundaries. Kansas applies that tax to motor fuel received by non-Indian fuel distributors who subsequently deliver that fuel to a gas station owned by, and located on, the Reservation of the Prairie Band Potawatomi Nation (Nation). The Nation maintains that this application of the Kansas motor fuel tax is an impermissible affront to its sovereignty. The Court of Appeals agreed, holding that the application of the Kansas tax to fuel received by a non-Indian distributor, but subsequently delivered to the Nation, was invalid under the interest-balancing test set forth in White Mountain Apache Tribe v. Bracker, 448 U. S. 136 (1980). But the Bracker interest-balancing test applies only where \xe2\x80\x9ca State asserts authority over the conduct of non-Indians engaging in activity on the reservation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 144. It does not apply where, as here, a state tax is imposed on a non-Indian and arises as a result of a transaction that occurs off the reservation. Accordingly, we reverse.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Nation is a federally recognized Indian Tribe whose reservation is on United States trust land in Jackson County, Kansas. The Nation owns and operates a casino on its reservation. In order to accommodate casino patrons and other reservation-related traffic, the Nation constructed, and now owns and operates, a gas station on its reservation next to the casino. Seventy-three percent of the station\xe2\x80\x99s fuel sales are made to casino patrons, while 11 percent of the station\xe2\x80\x99s fuel sales are made to persons who live or work on the reservation. The Nation purchases fuel for its gas station from non-Indian distributors located off its reservation. Those distributors pay a state fuel tax on their initial receipt of motor fuel, Kan. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa779\xe2\x80\x933408 (2003 Cum. Supp.),[Footnote 1] and pass along the cost of that tax to their customers, including the Nation.[Footnote 2]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Nation sells its fuel within 2 cents per gallon of the prevailing market price. Prairie Band Potawatomi Nation v. Richards, 379 F.\xc2\xa03d 979, 982 (CA10 2004). It does so notwithstanding the distributor\xe2\x80\x99s decision to pass along the cost of the State\xe2\x80\x99s fuel tax to the Nation, and the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s decision to impose its own tax on the station\xe2\x80\x99s fuel sales in the amount of 16 cents per gallon of gasoline and 18 cents per gallon of diesel (increased to 20 cents for gasoline and 22 cents for diesel in January 2003). Id., at 982. The Nation\xe2\x80\x99s fuel tax generates approximately $300,000 annually, funds that the Nation uses for \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98constructing and maintaining roads, bridges and rights-of-way located on or near the Reservation,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d including the access road between the state-funded highway and the casino. Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Nation brought an action in Federal District Court for declaratory judgment and injunctive relief from the State\xe2\x80\x99s collection of motor fuel tax from distributors who deliver fuel to the reservation. The District Court granted summary judgment in favor of the State. Applying the Bracker interest-balancing test, it determined that the balance of state, federal, and tribal interests tilted in favor of the State. The court reached this determination because \xe2\x80\x9cit is undisputed that the legal incidence of the tax is directed off-reservation at the fuel distributors,\xe2\x80\x9d Prairie Band Potawatomi Nation v. Richards, 241 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1295, 1311 (Kan. 2003), and because the ultimate purchasers of the fuel, non-Indian casino patrons, receive the bulk of their governmental services from the State, id., at 1309. The court held that the State\xe2\x80\x99s tax did not interfere with the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s right of self-government, adding that \xe2\x80\x9ca tribe cannot oust a state from any power to tax on-reservation purchases by nonmembers of the tribe by simply imposing its own tax on the transactions or by otherwise earning its revenues from the tribal business.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals for the Tenth Circuit reversed. 379 F.\xc2\xa03d 979 (2004). It determined that, under Bracker, the balance of state, federal, and tribal interests favored the Tribe. The Tenth Circuit reasoned that the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s fuel revenues were \xe2\x80\x9cderived from value generated primarily on its reservation,\xe2\x80\x9d 379 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 984\xe2\x80\x94namely, the creation of a new fuel market by virtue of the presence of the casino\xe2\x80\x94and that the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s interests in taxing this reservation-created value to raise revenue for reservation infrastructure outweighed the State\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral interest in raising revenues,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 986. We granted certiorari, 543 U. S. 1186 (2005), and now reverse.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although we granted certiorari to determine whether Kansas may tax a non-Indian distributor\xe2\x80\x99s off-reservation receipt of fuel without being subject to the Bracker interest-balancing test, Pet. for Cert. i, the Nation maintains that Kansas\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9ctax is imposed not on the off-reservation receipt of fuel, but on its on-reservation sale and delivery,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 11 (emphasis in original). As the Nation recognizes, under our Indian tax immunity cases, the \xe2\x80\x9cwho\xe2\x80\x9d and the \xe2\x80\x9cwhere\xe2\x80\x9d of the challenged tax have significant consequences. We have determined that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he initial and frequently dispositive question in Indian tax cases . . . is who bears the legal incidence of [the] tax,\xe2\x80\x9d Oklahoma Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Chickasaw Nation, 515 U. S. 450, 458 (1995) (emphasis added), and that the States are categorically barred from placing the legal incidence of an excise tax \xe2\x80\x9con a tribe or on tribal members for sales made inside Indian country\xe2\x80\x9d without congressional authorization, id., at 459 (emphasis added). We have further determined that, even when a State imposes the legal incidence of its tax on a non-Indian seller, the tax may nonetheless be pre-empted if the transaction giving rise to tax liability occurs on the reservation and the imposition of the tax fails to satisfy the Bracker interest-balancing test. See 448 U. S. 136 (holding that state taxes imposed on on-reservation logging and hauling operations by non-Indian contractor are invalid under the interest-balancing test); cf. Central Machinery Co. v. Arizona Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 448 U. S. 160 (1980) (holding that the Indian trader statutes pre-empted Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s tax on a non-Indian seller\xe2\x80\x99s on-reservation sales).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Nation maintains that it is entitled to prevail under the categorical bar articulated in Chickasaw because \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he fairest reading of the statute is that the legal incidence of the tax actually falls on the Tribe [on the reservation].\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 17, n.\xc2\xa05. The Nation alternatively maintains it is entitled to prevail even if the legal incidence of the tax is on the non-Indian distributor because, according to the Nation, the tax arises out of a distributor\xe2\x80\x99s on-reservation transaction with the Tribe and is therefore subject to the Bracker balancing test. Brief for Respondent 15. We address the \xe2\x80\x9cwho\xe2\x80\x9d and the \xe2\x80\x9cwhere\xe2\x80\x9d of Kansas\xe2\x80\x99 motor fuel tax in turn.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Kansas law specifies that \xe2\x80\x9cthe incidence of [the motor fuel] tax is imposed on the distributor of the first receipt of the motor fuel.\xe2\x80\x9d Kan. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa779\xe2\x80\x933408(c) (2003 Cum. Supp.). We have suggested that such \xe2\x80\x9cdispositive language\xe2\x80\x9d from the state legislature is determinative of who bears the legal incidence of a state excise tax. Chickasaw, supra, at 461. But even if the state legislature had not employed such \xe2\x80\x9cdispositive language,\xe2\x80\x9d thereby requiring us instead to look to a \xe2\x80\x9cfair interpretation of the taxing statute as written and applied,\xe2\x80\x9d California Bd. of Equalization v. Chemehuevi Tribe, 474 U. S. 9, 11 (1985) (per curiam), we would nonetheless conclude that the legal incidence of the tax is on the distributor.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Kansas law makes clear that it is the distributor, rather than the retailer, that is liable to pay the motor fuel tax. Section 79\xe2\x80\x933410(a) (1997) provides, in relevant part, that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]very distributor . . . shall compute and shall pay to the director . . . the amount of [motor fuel] taxes due to the state.\xe2\x80\x9d While the distributors are \xe2\x80\x9centitled\xe2\x80\x9d to pass along the cost of the tax to downstream purchasers, see \xc2\xa779\xe2\x80\x933409 (2003 Cum. Supp.), they are not required to do so. In sum, the legal incidence of the Kansas motor fuel tax is on the distributor. The lower courts reached the same conclusion. 379 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 982 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Kansas legislature structured the tax so that its legal incidence is placed on non-Indian distributors\xe2\x80\x9d); 241 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1311 (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is undisputed that the legal incidence of the tax is directed off-reservation at the fuel distributors\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Sac and Fox Nation of Missouri v. Pierce, 213 F. 3d 566, 578 (CA10 2000) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he legal incidence of the [Kansas] tax law as presently written falls on the fuel distributors rather than on the Tribes\xe2\x80\x9d); Winnebago Tribe of Nebraska v. Kline, 297 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1291, 1294 (Kan. 2004) (\xe2\x80\x9cUnder the Kansas statutory scheme, the legal incidence of the state\xe2\x80\x99s fuel tax falls on the \xe2\x80\x98distributor of first receipt\xe2\x80\x99 of such fuel\xe2\x80\x9d); Sac and Fox Nation of Missouri v. LaFaver, 31 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1298, 1307 (Kan. 1998) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he statutes are extremely clear in providing that the tax in question is imposed upon the distributor\xe2\x80\x9d). And the Kansas Department of Revenue, the state agency charged with administering the motor fuel tax, has concluded likewise. See Letter from David J. Heinemann, Office of Administrative Appeals, to Mark Burghart, Written Final Determination in Request for Informal Conference for Reconsideration of Agency Action, Davies Oil Co., Inc., Docket No. 01\xe2\x80\x93970 (Jan. 3, 2002) (hereinafter Kansas Dept. of Revenue Letter) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe legal incidence of the Kansas fuel tax rests with Davies, the distributor, who is up-stream from Nation, the\nretailer\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The United States, as amicus, contends that this conclusion is foreclosed by the Kansas Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Kaul v. Kansas Dept. of Revenue, 266 Kan. 464, 970 P.\xc2\xa02d 60 (1998). The United States reads Kaul as holding that the legal incidence of Kansas\xe2\x80\x99 motor fuel tax rests on the Indian retailers, rather than on the non-Indian distributors. And, under the United States\xe2\x80\x99 view, so long as the Kansas Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98definitive determination as to the operating incidence\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of its fuel tax is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98consistent with the statute\xe2\x80\x99s reasonable interpretation,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d it should be \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98deemed conclusive.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 10 (quoting Gurley v. Rhoden, 421 U. S. 200, 208 (1975)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We disagree with the United States\xe2\x80\x99 interpretation of Kaul. In Kaul, two members of the Citizen Band Potawatomi Tribe of Oklahoma sought to enjoin the enforcement of Kansas\xe2\x80\x99 fuel tax on fuel delivered to their gas station located on the Prairie Band Potawatomi Tribe of Kansas\xe2\x80\x99 Reservation. The Kansas Supreme Court determined that the station owners had standing to challenge the tax because the statute provided that the distributor was entitled to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98charge and collect such tax . . . as a part of the selling price.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Kaul, supra, at 474, 970 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 67 (quoting Kan. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa779\xe2\x80\x933409 (1995); emphasis deleted). The court determined that the station owners were not entitled to an injunction, however, because they were not members of a Kansas Tribe and thus there had \xe2\x80\x9cbeen no showing by Retailers that payment of fuel tax to Kansas interferes with the self-government of a Kansas tribe or a Kansas tribal member.\xe2\x80\x9d 266 Kan., at 464, 970 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 69. The court then noted that \xe2\x80\x9cthe legal incidence of the tax on motor fuel rests on nontribal members and does not affect the Potawatomi Indian reservation within the state of Kansas.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Kaul does not foreclose our determination that the distributor bears the legal incidence of the Kansas motor fuel tax. As an initial matter, it is unclear whether the court\xe2\x80\x99s reference to \xe2\x80\x9cnontribal members\xe2\x80\x9d is a reference to the non-tribal-member retailers or the non-tribal-member distributors. At the very least, Kaul\xe2\x80\x99s imprecise language cannot be characterized as a definitive determination. Moreover, the 1998 amendments to the Kansas fuel provisions, including the amendment to \xc2\xa779\xe2\x80\x933408(c) that provides that \xe2\x80\x9cthe incidence of this tax is imposed on the distributor,\xe2\x80\x9d were not applied in Kaul. Id., at 473, 970 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 66 (identifying provisions that were repealed in 1998 as being \xe2\x80\x9cin effect during the period relevant to this case\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 474, 970 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 67 (noting that a \xe2\x80\x9ccritical statute\xe2\x80\x9d to its holding was the 1995 version of \xc2\xa779\xe2\x80\x933409, which was amended in 1998). Accordingly, Kaul did not speak authoritatively on the provisions before us today.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Nation maintains that we must apply the Bracker interest-balancing test, irrespective of the identity of the taxpayer (i.e., the party bearing the legal incidence), because the Kansas fuel tax arises as a result of the on-reservation sale and delivery of the motor fuel. See Brief for Respondent 15. Notably, however, the Nation presented a starkly different interpretation of the statute in the proceedings before the Court of Appeals, arguing that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he balancing test is appropriate even though the legal incidence of the tax is imposed on the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s non-Indian distributor and is triggered by the distributor\xe2\x80\x99s receipt of fuel outside the reservation.\xe2\x80\x9d Appellant\xe2\x80\x99s Reply Brief in No. 03\xe2\x80\x933218 (CA10), p. 3 (emphasis added); see also 241 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1311 (District Court observing that \xe2\x80\x9cit is undisputed that the legal incidence of the tax is directed off-reservation at the fuel distributors\xe2\x80\x9d). A \xe2\x80\x9cfair interpretation of the taxing statute as written and applied,\xe2\x80\x9d Chemehuevi Tribe, 474 U. S., at 11, confirms that the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the statute before the Court of Appeals was correct.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As written, the Kansas fuel tax provisions state that \xe2\x80\x9cthe incidence of this tax is imposed on the distributor of the first receipt of the motor fuel and such taxes shall be paid but once. Such tax shall be computed on all motor-vehicle fuels or special fuels received by each distributor, manufacturer or importer in this state and paid in the manner provided for herein . . . .\xe2\x80\x9d Kan. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa779\xe2\x80\x933408(c) (2003 Cum. Supp.). Under this provision, the distributor who initially receives the motor fuel is liable for payment of the fuel tax, and the distributor\xe2\x80\x99s tax liability is determined by calculating the amount of fuel received by the distributor.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 79\xe2\x80\x933410(a) (1997) confirms that it is the distributor\xe2\x80\x99s off-reservation receipt of the motor fuel, and not any subsequent event, that establishes tax liability. That section provides:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[E]very distributor, manufacturer, importer, exporter or retailer of motor-vehicle fuels or special fuels, on or before the 25th day of each month, shall render to the director . . . a report certified to be true and correct showing the number of gallons of motor-vehicle fuels or special fuels received by such distributor, manufacturer, importer, exporter or retailer during the preceding calendar month . . . . Every distributor, manufacturer or importer within the time herein fixed for the rendering of such reports, shall compute and shall pay to the director at the director\'s office the amount of taxes due to the state on all motor-vehicle fuels or special fuels received by such distributor, manufacturer or importer during the preceding calendar month.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\nThus, Kansas law expressly provides that a distributor\xe2\x80\x99s monthly tax obligations are determined by the amount of fuel received by the distributor during the preceding month. See Kline, 297 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1294 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe distributor must compute and remit the tax each month for the fuel received by the distributor in the State of Kansas\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Nation disagrees. It contends that what is taxed is not the distributors\xe2\x80\x99 (off-reservation) receipt of the fuel, but rather the distributors\xe2\x80\x99 use, sale, or delivery of the motor fuel\xe2\x80\x94in this case, the distributors\xe2\x80\x99 (on-reservation) sale or delivery to the Nation. The Nation grounds support for this proposition in \xc2\xa779\xe2\x80\x933408(a) (2003 Cum. Supp.). That section provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] tax . . . is hereby imposed on the use, sale or delivery of all motor vehicle fuels or special fuels which are used, sold or delivered in this state for any purpose whatsoever.\xe2\x80\x9d But this section cannot be read in isolation. If it were, it would permit Kansas to tax the same fuel multiple times\xe2\x80\x94namely, every time fuel is sold, delivered, or used. Section 79\xe2\x80\x933408(a) must be read in conjunction with subsection (c), which specifies that \xe2\x80\x9cthe incidence of this tax is imposed on the distributor of the first receipt of the motor fuel and such taxes shall be paid but once.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.) The identity of the single, taxable event is revealed in the very next sentence of subsection (c), which provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[s]uch tax shall be computed on all . . . fuels received by each distributor.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.) In short, the \xe2\x80\x9cuse, sale or delivery\xe2\x80\x9d that triggers tax liability is the sale or delivery of the fuel to the distributor. The Kansas Department of Revenue has issued a final determination reaching the same conclusion. See Kansas Dept. of Revenue Letter (\xe2\x80\x9c[P]ursuant to the Kansas Motor Fuel Tax Act \xe2\x80\xa6 the state fuel tax was imposed on Davies, a distributor, when Davies first received the fuel at its business, a site located off of Nation\xe2\x80\x99s reservation\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Nation claims further support for its interpretation of the statute in \xc2\xa779\xe2\x80\x933408(d) (2003 Cum. Supp.). Section 79\xe2\x80\x933408(d) permits distributors to obtain deductions from the Kansas motor fuel tax for certain postreceipt transactions, such as sale or delivery of fuel for export from the State and sale or delivery of fuel to the United States. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa779\xe2\x80\x933408(d)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(2). The Nation argues that these exemptions make it impossible for a distributor to calculate its \xe2\x80\x9cultimate tax liability\xe2\x80\x9d without knowing \xe2\x80\x9cwhether, where, and to whom the fuel is ultimately sold or delivered.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 15. The Nation infers from these provisions that the taxable event is actually the distributors\xe2\x80\x99 postreceipt delivery of fuel to retailers such as the Nation, rather than the distributors\xe2\x80\x99 initial receipt of the fuel.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Nation\xe2\x80\x99s theory suffers from a number of conceptual defects. First, under Kansas law, a distributor must pay the tax even for fuel that sits in its inventory\xe2\x80\x94fuel that is not (or at least has not yet been) used, sold, or delivered by the distributor.[Footnote 3] But the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation presumes that the tax is owed only on a distributor\xe2\x80\x99s postreceipt use, sale, or delivery of fuel. As this interpretation cannot be reconciled with the manner in which the Kansas motor fuel tax is actually applied, it must be rejected.[Footnote 4] Second, the availability of tax deductions does not change the nature of the taxable event, here the distributor\xe2\x80\x99s receipt of the fuel. By analogy, an individual federal income taxpayer may reduce his tax liability by paying home mortgage interest. But that entitlement does not render the taxable event anything other than the receipt of income by the taxpayer. See 26 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71 (2000 ed. and Supp. II), \xc2\xa7163(h) (2000 ed.); cf. North American Oil Consol. v. Burnet, 286 U. S. 417, 424 (1932) (federal income tax liability arises when \xe2\x80\x9ca taxpayer \xe2\x80\xa6 has received income\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, the Nation contends that its interpretation of the statute is supported by Kan. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa779\xe2\x80\x933417 (1997), which permits a refund\xe2\x80\x94in certain circumstances\xe2\x80\x94for destroyed fuel. However, the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation is actually foreclosed by that section. Section 79\xe2\x80\x933417 entitles a distributor to a \xe2\x80\x9crefund from the state of the amount of motor-vehicle fuels or special fuels tax paid on any . . . fuels of 100 gallons or more in quantity, which are lost or destroyed at any one time while such distributor is the owner thereof,\xe2\x80\x9d provided the distributor supplies the required notification and documentation to the State. This section illustrates that a distributor pays taxes for fuel in its possession that it has not delivered or sold, and is only entitled to the refund described in this section for tax it has already paid on fuel that is subsequently destroyed. While this section does not specify the event that gives rise to the distributor\xe2\x80\x99s tax liability, it forecloses the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s contention that such liability does not arise until fuel is sold or delivered to a nonexempt entity.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although Kansas\xe2\x80\x99 fuel tax is imposed on non-Indian distributors based upon those distributors\xe2\x80\x99 off-reservation receipt of motor fuel, the Tenth Circuit concluded that the tax was nevertheless still subject to the interest-balancing test this Court set forth in Bracker, 448 U. S. 136. As Bracker itself explained, however, we formulated the balancing test to address the \xe2\x80\x9cdifficult questio[n]\xe2\x80\x9d that arises when \xe2\x80\x9ca State asserts authority over the conduct of non-Indians engaging in activity on the reservation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 144\xe2\x80\x93145 (emphasis added). The Bracker interest-balancing test has never been applied where, as here, the State asserts \nits taxing authority over non-Indians off the reservation. And although we have never addressed this precise issue, our Indian tax immunity cases counsel against such an application.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have applied the balancing test articulated in Bracker only where \xe2\x80\x9cthe legal incidence of the tax fell on a nontribal entity engaged in a transaction with tribes or tribal members,\xe2\x80\x9d Arizona Dept. of Revenue v. Blaze Constr. Co., 526 U. S. 32, 37 (1999), on the reservation. See Bracker, supra (motor carrier license and use fuel taxes imposed on on-reservation logging and hauling operations by non-Indian contractor); Department of Taxation and Finance of N. Y. v. Milhelm Attea & Bros., 512 U. S. 61 (1994) (various taxes imposed on non-Indian purchasers of goods retailed on-reservation); Cotton Petroleum Corp. v. New Mexico, 490 U. S. 163 (1989) (state severance tax imposed on non-Indian lessee\xe2\x80\x99s on-reservation production of oil and gas); Ramah Navajo School Bd., Inc. v. Bureau of Revenue of N. M., 458 U. S. 832 (1982) (state gross receipts tax imposed on private contractor\xe2\x80\x99s proceeds from the construction of a school on the reservation); Washington v. Confederated Tribes of Colville Reservation, 447 U. S. 134 (1980) (cigarette and sales taxes imposed on on-reservation purchases by nonmembers); Central Machinery Co., 448 U. S. 160 (tax imposed on on-reservation sale of farm machinery to Tribe). Similarly, the cases identified in Bracker as supportive of the balancing test were exclusively concerned with the on-reservation conduct of non-Indians. See Warren Trading Post Co. v. Arizona Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 380 U. S. 685 (1965) (gross proceeds tax imposed on non-Indian retailer on Navajo Indian Reservation); Thomas v. Gay, 169 U. S. 264 (1898) (state property tax imposed on cattle owned by non-Indian lessees of tribal land); Williams v. Lee, 358 U. S. 217 (1959) (holding the state courts lacked jurisdiction over dispute between non-Indian, on-reservation retailer and Indian debtors).[Footnote 5]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Limiting the interest-balancing test exclusively to on-reservation transactions between a nontribal entity and a tribe or tribal member is consistent with our unique Indian tax immunity jurisprudence. We have explained that this jurisprudence relies \xe2\x80\x9cheavily on the doctrine of tribal sovereignty . . . which historically gave state law \xe2\x80\x98no role to play\xe2\x80\x99 within a tribe\xe2\x80\x99s territorial boundaries.\xe2\x80\x9d Oklahoma Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Sac and Fox Nation, 508 U. S. 114, 123\xe2\x80\x93124 (1993) (quoting McClanahan v. Arizona Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 411 U. S. 164, 168 (1973)). We have further explained that the doctrine of tribal sovereignty, which has a \xe2\x80\x9csignificant geographical component,\xe2\x80\x9d Bracker, supra, at 151, requires us to \xe2\x80\x9crevers[e]\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98general rule\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98exemptions from tax laws should . . . be clearly expressed.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Sac and Fox, supra, at 124 (quoting McClanahan, supra, at 176). And we have determined that the geographical component of tribal sovereignty \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98provide[s] a backdrop against which the applicable treaties and federal statutes must be read.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Sac and Fox, supra, at 124 (quoting McClanahan, supra, at 172). Indeed, the particularized inquiry we set forth in Bracker relied specifically on that backdrop. See 448 U. S., at 144\xe2\x80\x93145 (noting that where \xe2\x80\x9ca State asserts authority over the conduct of non-Indians engaging in activity on the reservation . . . we have examined the language of the relevant federal treaties and statutes in terms of both the broad policies that underlie them and the notions of sovereignty that have developed from historical traditions of tribal independence\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have taken an altogether different course, by contrast, when a State asserts its taxing authority outside of Indian Country. Without applying the interest-balancing test, we have permitted the taxation of the gross receipts of an off-reservation, Indian-owned ski resort, Mescalero Apache Tribe v. Jones, 411 U. S. 145 (1973), and the taxation of income earned by Indians working on-reservation but living off-reservation, Chickasaw, 515 U. S. 450. In these cases, we have concluded that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]bsent express federal law to the contrary, Indians going beyond reservation boundaries have generally been held subject to nondiscriminatory state law otherwise applicable to all citizens of the State.\xe2\x80\x9d Mescalero Apache, supra, at 148\xe2\x80\x93149; Chickasaw, supra, at 465 (quoting Mescalero Apache, supra, at 148\xe2\x80\x93149). If a State may apply a nondiscriminatory tax to Indians who have gone beyond the boundaries of the reservation, then it follows that it may apply a nondiscriminatory tax where, as here, the tax is imposed on non-Indians as a result of an off-reservation transaction. In these circumstances, the interest-balancing test set forth in Bracker is inapplicable. Cf. Blaze Constr., 526 U. S., at 37 (declining to apply the Bracker interest-balancing test \xe2\x80\x9cwhere a State seeks to tax a transaction [on-reservation] between the Federal Government and its non-Indian private contractor\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The application of the interest-balancing test to the Kansas motor fuel tax is not only inconsistent with the special geographic sovereignty concerns that gave rise to that test, but also with our efforts to establish \xe2\x80\x9cbright-line standard[s]\xe2\x80\x9d in the context of tax administration. Ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9cThe need to avoid litigation and to ensure efficient tax administration counsels in favor of a bright-line standard for taxation of federal contracts, regardless of whether the contracted-for activity takes place on Indian reservations\xe2\x80\x9d); cf. Chickasaw, supra, at 460 (noting that the legal incidence test \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98provide[s] a reasonably bright-line standard\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d); County of Yakima v. Confederated Tribes and Bands of Yakima Nation, 502 U. S. 251, 267\xe2\x80\x93268 (1992). Indeed, we have recognized that the Bracker interest-balancing test \xe2\x80\x9conly cloud[s]\xe2\x80\x9d our efforts to establish such standards. Blaze Constr., supra, at 37. Under the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s view, however, any off-reservation tax imposed on the manufacture or sale of any good imported by the Nation or one of its members would be subject to interest balancing. Such an expansion of the application of the Bracker test is not supported by our cases.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor is the Nation entitled to interest balancing by virtue of its claim that the Kansas motor fuel tax interferes with its own motor fuel tax. As an initial matter, this is ultimately a complaint about the downstream economic consequences of the Kansas tax. As the owner of the station, the Nation will keep every dollar it collects above its operating costs. Given that the Nation sells gas at prevailing market rates, its decision to impose a tax should have no effect on its net revenues from the operation of the station; it should not matter whether those revenues are labeled \xe2\x80\x9cprofits\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ctax proceeds.\xe2\x80\x9d The Nation merely seeks to increase those revenues by purchasing untaxed fuel. But the Nation cannot invalidate the Kansas tax by complaining about a decrease in revenues. See Colville, 447 U. S., at 156 (\xe2\x80\x9cWashington does not infringe the right of reservation Indians to \xe2\x80\x98make their own laws and be ruled by them,\xe2\x80\x99 Williams v. Lee, 358 U. S. 217, 220 (1959), merely because the result of imposing its taxes will be to deprive the Tribes of revenues which they currently are receiving\xe2\x80\x9d). Nor would our analysis change if we accorded legal significance to the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s decision to label a portion of the station\xe2\x80\x99s revenues as tax proceeds. See id., at 184, n.\xc2\xa09 (Rehnquist, J., concurring in part, concuring in result in part, and dissenting in part) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhen two sovereigns have legitimate authority to tax the same transaction, exercise of that authority by one sovereign does not oust the jurisdiction of the other. If it were otherwise, we would not be obligated to pay federal as well as state taxes on our income or gasoline purchases. Economic burdens on the competing sovereign . . . do not alter the concurrent nature of the taxing authority\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 6]

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, the Nation contends that the Kansas motor fuel tax is invalid notwithstanding the inapplicability of the interest-balancing test, because it \xe2\x80\x9cexempts from taxation fuel sold or delivered to all other sovereigns,\xe2\x80\x9d and is therefore impermissibly discriminatory. Brief for Respondent 17\xe2\x80\x9320 (emphasis deleted); Kan. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa779\xe2\x80\x933408(d)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(2) (2003 Cum. Supp.). But the Nation is not similarly situated to the sovereigns exempted from the Kansas fuel tax. While Kansas uses the proceeds from its fuel tax to pay for a significant portion of the costs of maintaining the roads and bridges on the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s reservation, including the main highway used by the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s casino patrons, Kansas offers no such services to the several States or the Federal Government. Moreover, to the extent Kansas fuel retailers bear the cost of the fuel tax, that burden falls equally upon all retailers within the State regardless of whether those retailers are located on an Indian reservation. Accordingly, the Kansas motor fuel tax is not impermissibly discriminatory.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For the foregoing reasons, we hold that the Kansas motor fuel tax is a nondiscriminatory tax imposed on an off-reservation transaction between non-Indians. Accordingly, the tax is valid and poses no affront to the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s sovereignty. The judgment of the Court of Appeals is reversed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Both the Nation and the State have authority to tax fuel sales at the Nation Station. See Merrion v. Jicarilla Apache Tribe, 455 U. S. 130, 137 (1982) (describing \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he power to tax [as] an essential attribute of Indian sovereignty[,] \xe2\x80\xa6 a necessary instrument of self-government and territorial management,\xe2\x80\x9d which \xe2\x80\x9cenables a tribal government to raise revenues for its essential services\xe2\x80\x9d). As a practical matter, however, the two tolls cannot coexist. 379 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 986. If the Nation imposes its tax on top of Kansas\xe2\x80\x99 tax, then unless the Nation operates the Nation Station at a substantial loss, scarcely anyone will fill up at its pumps. Effectively double-taxed, the Nation Station must operate as an unprofitable venture, or not at all. In these circumstances, which tax is paramount? Applying the interest-balancing approach described in White Mountain Apache Tribe v. Bracker, 448 U. S. 136 (1980), the Court of Appeals for the Tenth Circuit held that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Kansas tax, as applied here, is preempted because it is incompatible with and outweighed by the strong tribal and federal interests against the tax.\xe2\x80\x9d 379 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 983. I agree and would affirm the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 judgment.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Understanding Bracker is key to the inquiry here. Bracker addressed the question whether a State should be preempted from collecting otherwise lawful taxes from non-Indians in view of the burden consequently imposed upon a Tribe or its members. In that case, Arizona sought to enforce its fuel-use and vehicle-license taxes against a non-Indian enterprise that contracted with the White Mountain Apache Tribe to harvest timber from reservation forests. 448 U. S., at 138\xe2\x80\x93140. The Court recognized that Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s levies raised difficult questions concerning \xe2\x80\x9cthe boundaries between state regulatory authority and tribal self-government.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 141. Determining whether taxes formally imposed on non-Indians are preempted, the Court instructed, should not turn \xe2\x80\x9con mechanical or absolute conceptions of state or tribal sovereignty, but [calls] for a particularized inquiry into the nature of the state, federal, and tribal interests at stake.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 145. This inquiry is \xe2\x80\x9cdesigned to determine whether, in the specific context, the exercise of state authority would violate federal law,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., or \xe2\x80\x9cunlawfully infringe \xe2\x80\x98on the right of reservation Indians to make their own laws and be ruled by them,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 142 (quoting Williams v. Lee, 358 U. S. 217, 220 (1959)). Applying the interest-balancing approach, the Court concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthe proposed exercise of state authority [was] impermissible\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9cit [was] undisputed that the economic burden of the asserted taxes will ultimately fall on the Tribe,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cthe Federal Government has undertaken comprehensive regulation of the harvesting and sale of tribal timber,\xe2\x80\x9d and the state officials were \xe2\x80\x9cunable to justify the taxes except in terms of a generalized interest in raising revenue.\xe2\x80\x9d 448 U. S., at 151.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court has repeatedly applied the interest-balancing approach described in Bracker in evaluating claims that state taxes levied on non-Indians should be preempted because they undermine tribal and federal interests.[Footnote 2] In many cases, both pre- and post-Bracker, a balancing analysis has yielded a decision upholding application of the state tax in question. See, e.g., Cotton Petroleum Corp. v. New Mexico, 490 U. S. 163, 183\xe2\x80\x93187 (1989) (State permitted to impose a severance tax on a non-Indian company that leased tribal land for oil and gas production); Washington v. Confederated Tribes of Colville Reservation, 447 U. S. 134, 154\xe2\x80\x93159 (1980) (State permitted to tax non-Indians\xe2\x80\x99 purchases of cigarettes from on-reservation tribal retailers); Moe v. Confederated Salish and Kootenai Tribes of Flathead Reservation, 425 U. S. 463, 481\xe2\x80\x93483 (1976) (same). Sometimes, however, particularized inquiry has resulted in a holding that federal or tribal interests are superior. See, e.g., Ramah Navajo School Bd., Inc. v. Bureau of Revenue of N. M., 458 U. S. 832, 843\xe2\x80\x93846 (1982) (State prohibited from imposing gross-receipts tax on a non-Indian contractor constructing an on-reservation tribal school).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Kansas contends that the interest-balancing approach is not suitably employed to assess its fuel tax for these reasons: (1) the Kansas Legislature imposed the legal incidence of the tax on the distributor\xe2\x80\x94here, a non-Indian enterprise\xe2\x80\x94not on retailers or their customers; and (2) the distributor\xe2\x80\x99s liability is triggered when it receives fuel from its supplier\xe2\x80\x94a transaction that occurs off-reservation. Reply Brief 2\xe2\x80\x936. Given these circumstances, Kansas urges and the Court accepts, no balancing is in order. See ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313; Brief for Petitioner 6, 14\xe2\x80\x9321. It is irrelevant in the State\xe2\x80\x99s calculus that its approach would effectively nullify the tribal fuel tax.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I note first that Kansas\xe2\x80\x99 placement of the legal incidence of the fuel tax is not as clear and certain as the State suggests and the Court holds. True, the statute states that \xe2\x80\x9cthe incidence of this tax is imposed on the distributor of the first receipt of the motor fuel.\xe2\x80\x9d Kan. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa779\xe2\x80\x933408(c) (2003 Cum. Supp.). But the statute declares initially that the tax \xe2\x80\x9cis hereby imposed on the use, sale or delivery of all motor vehicle fuels \xe2\x80\xa6 used, sold or delivered in this state for any purpose whatsoever,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa779\xe2\x80\x933408(a), and it authorizes distributors to pass on the tax to retailers, \xc2\xa779\xe2\x80\x933409. Notably, the statute excludes from taxation several \xe2\x80\x9ctransactions,\xe2\x80\x9d including the \xe2\x80\x9csale or delivery of motor-vehicle fuel \xe2\x80\xa6 for export from the state of Kansas to any other state or territory or to any foreign country\xe2\x80\x9d; \xe2\x80\x9csale or delivery \xe2\x80\xa6 to the United States\xe2\x80\x9d; \xe2\x80\x9csale or delivery \xe2\x80\xa6 to a contractor for use in performing work for the United States\xe2\x80\x9d; and \xe2\x80\x9csale or delivery \xe2\x80\xa6 to another duly licensed distributor.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa779\xe2\x80\x933408(d). Kansas also excludes from taxation \xe2\x80\x9clost or destroyed\xe2\x80\x9d fuel, which is never sold by the distributor. \xc2\xa779\xe2\x80\x933417 (1997). These provisions indicate not only that the Kansas Legislature anticipated that distributors would shift the tax burden further downstream. They reveal as well where the Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis of the fuel tax goes awry.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When all the exclusions are netted out, the Kansas tax is imposed not on all the distributor\xe2\x80\x99s receipts, but effectively only on fuel actually resold by the distributor to an in-state nonexempt purchaser. To illustrate: Suppose in January a distributor acquires 100,000 gallons of fuel and promptly sells 80,000 to in-state nonexempt purchasers and 20,000 to exempt purchasers, for example, the United States or a U. S. contractor. The distributor would compute its tax liability by \xe2\x80\x9cdeducting\xe2\x80\x9d the 20,000 gallons, see ante, at 11, n.\xc2\xa03, but would remit tax only on the 80,000 gallons bought by in-state nonexempt retailers.[Footnote 3] If the distributor elected to build inventory in January by holding an additional 10,000 gallons for resale in February, Kansas would tax in January, but the distributor would effectively offset in February the tax paid in January on the inventory buildup. Again, in the end, only fuel actually sold to in-state nonexempt buyers would be burdened by Kansas\xe2\x80\x99 fuel tax.[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Kansas\xe2\x80\x99 attribution of controlling effect to the formal legal incidence of the tax rests in part on the State\xe2\x80\x99s misreading of Oklahoma Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Chickasaw Nation, 515 U. S. 450 (1995). See Brief for Petitioner 8, 16\xe2\x80\x9320. The Court in that case distinguished instances in which the legal incidence of a State\xe2\x80\x99s excise tax rests on a Tribe or tribal members, from instances in which the legal incidence rests on non-Indians. When \xe2\x80\x9cthe legal incidence \xe2\x80\xa6 rests on a tribe or on tribal members for sales made inside Indian country,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court said, \xe2\x80\x9cthe tax cannot be enforced absent clear congressional authorization.\xe2\x80\x9d 515 U. S., at 459. This \xe2\x80\x9cbright-line standard,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 460, is sensitive to the sovereign status of Indian Tribes, and reflects the Court\xe2\x80\x99s recognition that \xe2\x80\x9ctribal sovereignty is dependent on, and subordinate to, only the Federal Government, not the States.\xe2\x80\x9d Colville, 447 U. S., at 154.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When a State places the legal incidence of its tax on non-Indians, however, no similarly overt disrespect for a Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s independence and dignity is displayed. In cases of this genre, Chickasaw Nation recognized, the Court has resisted adoption of a categorical rule. In lieu of attributing dispositive significance to the legal incidence, the Court has focused on the particular levy, and has evaluated the federal, state, and tribal interests at stake. 515 U. S., at 459; see Cotton Petroleum, 490 U. S., at 176 (Instead of a \xe2\x80\x9cmechanical or absolute\xe2\x80\x9d test, the Court has \xe2\x80\x9capplied a flexible pre-emption analysis sensitive to the particular facts and legislation involved. Each case \xe2\x80\x98requires a particularized examination of the relevant state, federal, and tribal interests.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Ramah, 458 U. S., at 838)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Chickasaw Nation did observe that \xe2\x80\x9cif a State is unable to enforce a tax because the legal incidence of the impost is on Indians or Indian tribes, the State generally is free to amend its law to shift the tax\xe2\x80\x99s legal incidence.\xe2\x80\x9d 515 U. S., at 460. Kansas took the cue. After our decision in Chickasaw Nation, Kansas amended its fuel tax statute to state that \xe2\x80\x9cthe incidence of this tax is imposed on the distributor.\xe2\x80\x9d Kan. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa779\xe2\x80\x933408(c) (2003 Cum. Supp.); see 1998 Kan. Sess. Laws, ch. 96, \xc2\xa72, pp. 450\xe2\x80\x93451; see also Kaul v. Kansas Dept. of Revenue, 266 Kan. 464, 474, 970 P. 2d 60, 67 (1998).[Footnote 6]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Kansas is mistaken, however, regarding the legal significance of this shift. Chickasaw Nation clarified only that a State could shift the legal incidence to non-Indians so as to avoid the categorical bar applicable when a state excise tax is imposed directly on a Tribe or tribal members for on-reservation activity. 515 U. S., at 460. At the same time, Chickasaw Nation indicated that a shift in the legal incidence of the kind Kansas has legislated would trigger\xe2\x80\x94not foreclose\xe2\x80\x94interest balancing. Ibid.[Footnote 7]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Kansas and the Court heavily rely upon Mescalero Apache Tribe v. Jones, 411 U. S. 145 (1973) (Mescalero I). That case involved a ski resort operated by the Mescalero Apache Tribe on off-reservation land leased from the Federal Government. This Court upheld New Mexico\xe2\x80\x99s imposition of a tax on the gross receipts of the resort. Balancing was not in order, the Court explained, because the Tribe had ventured outside its own domain, and was fairly treated, for gross receipts purposes, just as a non-Indian enterprise would be. In such cases, the Court observed, an express-preemption standard is appropriately applied. As the Court put it: \xe2\x80\x9cAbsent express federal law to the contrary, Indians going beyond reservation boundaries have generally been held subject to nondiscriminatory state law otherwise applicable to all citizens of the State.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 148\xe2\x80\x93149. Accord Chickasaw Nation, 515 U. S., at 462\xe2\x80\x93465 (State permitted to tax income of tribal members residing outside Indian Country). Cases of the Mescalero I kind, however, do not touch and concern what is at issue in the instant case: taxes formally imposed on nonmembers that nonetheless burden on-reservation tribal activity.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Conceding that \xe2\x80\x9cwe have never addressed th[e] precise issue\xe2\x80\x9d this case poses, the Court asserts that \xe2\x80\x9cour Indian tax immunity cases counsel against\xe2\x80\x9d application of the Bracker interest-balancing test to Kansas\xe2\x80\x99 fuel tax as it impacts on the Nation Station. Ante, at 13. The Court so maintains on the ground that the Kansas fuel tax is imposed on a non-Indian and is unrelated to activity \xe2\x80\x9con the reservation.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9316. As earlier explained, see supra, at 6\xe2\x80\x937, one can demur to the assertion that the legal incidence of the tax falls on the distributor, a nontribal entity. With respect to sales and deliveries to the Nation Station, however, the nontribal entity can indeed be described as \xe2\x80\x9cengaged in [an on-reservation] transaction with [a Tribe].\xe2\x80\x9d Arizona Dept. of Revenue v. Blaze Constr. Co., 526 U. S. 32, 37 (1999).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The reservation destination of fuel purchased by the Nation Station does not show the requisite engagement, in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s view, but I do not comprehend why. The destination of the fuel counts not only under \xc2\xa779\xe2\x80\x933408(a) (2003 Cum. Supp.) (fuel tax \xe2\x80\x9cis hereby imposed on \xe2\x80\xa6 all motor vehicle fuels \xe2\x80\xa6 used, sold or delivered in this state\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 8] To whom and where the distributor sells are the criteria that determine the \xe2\x80\x9ctransactions\xe2\x80\x9d on which \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o tax is \xe2\x80\xa6 imposed,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa779\xe2\x80\x933408(d), and, correspondingly, the transactions on which the tax is imposed. As earlier explained, see supra, at 4\xe2\x80\x936, the tax is in reality imposed only on fuel actually resold by the distributor to an in-state nonexempt purchaser. Here, that purchaser is the Nation Station, plainly an on-reservation venture.[Footnote 9]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Balancing tests have been criticized as rudderless, affording insufficient guidance to decisionmakers. See Colville, 447 U. S., at 176 (Rehnquist, J., concurring in part, concurring in result in part, and dissenting in part) (criticizing the \xe2\x80\x9ccase-by-case litigation which has plagued this area of law\xe2\x80\x9d); Brief for Petitioner 30\xe2\x80\x9332. Pointed as the criticism may be, one must ask, as in life\xe2\x80\x99s choices generally, what is the alternative. \xe2\x80\x9cThe principle of tribal self-government, grounded in notions of inherent sovereignty and in congressional policies, seeks an accommodation between the interests of the Tribes and the Federal Government, on the one hand, and those of the State, on the other.\xe2\x80\x9d Colville, 447 U. S., at 156. No \xe2\x80\x9cbright-line\xe2\x80\x9d test is capable of achieving such an accommodation with respect to state taxes formally imposed on non-Indians, but impacting on-reservation ventures. The one the Court adopts inevitably means, so long as the State officially places the burden on the non-Indian distributor in cases of this order, the Tribe loses. Faute de mieux and absent congressional instruction otherwise, I would adhere to precedent calling for \xe2\x80\x9ca particularized inquiry into the nature of the state, federal, and tribal interests at stake.\xe2\x80\x9d Bracker, 448 U. S., at 145.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I turn to the question whether the Court of Appeals correctly balanced the competing interests in this case. Kansas and the Nation both assert a substantial interest in using their respective fuel taxes to raise revenue for road maintenance. Weighing competing state and tribal interests in raising revenue for public works, Colville observed:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cWhile the Tribes do have an interest in raising revenues for essential governmental programs, that interest is strongest when the revenues are derived from value generated on the reservation by activities involving the Tribes and when the taxpayer is the recipient of tribal services. The State also has a legitimate governmental interest in raising revenues, and that interest is likewise strongest when the tax is directed at off-reservation value and when the taxpayer is the recipient of state services.\xe2\x80\x9d 447 U. S., at 156\xe2\x80\x93157.

\n

\nIn Colville, it was \xe2\x80\x9cpainfully apparent\xe2\x80\x9d that outsiders had no reason to travel to Indian reservations to buy cigarettes other than the bargain prices tribal smokeshops charged by virtue of their claimed exemption from state taxation. Id., at 154\xe2\x80\x93155. The Court upheld the State of Washington\xe2\x80\x99s taxes on cigarette purchases by nonmembers at tribal smokeshops. No \xe2\x80\x9cprincipl[e] of federal Indian law,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court said, \xe2\x80\x9cauthorize[s] Indian tribes \xe2\x80\xa6 to market an exception from state taxation to persons who would normally do their business elewhere.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 155.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case, as the Court of Appeals recognized, bears scant resemblance to Colville. \xe2\x80\x9c[I]n stark contrast to the smokeshops in Colville,\xe2\x80\x9d the Nation here is not using its asserted exemption from state taxation to lure non-Indians onto its reservation. 379 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 985. The Nation Station is not visible from the state highway, and it advertises no exemption from the State\xe2\x80\x99s fuel tax. Including the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s tax, the Nation Station sells fuel \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98within 2\xcb\x98 per gallon of the price prevailing in the local market.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 982 (quoting the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s expert\xe2\x80\x99s report); see also App. 36\xe2\x80\x9340.[Footnote 10] The Nation Station\xe2\x80\x99s draw, therefore, is neither price nor proximity to the highway; rather, the Nation Station operates almost exclusively as an amenity for people driving to and from the casino.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Tenth Circuit regarded as valuable to its assessment the opinion of the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s expert, which concluded: \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[T]he Tribal and State taxes are mutually exclusive and only one can be collected without reducing the [Nation Station\xe2\x80\x99s] fuel business to virtually zero.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 379 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 986. Kansas \xe2\x80\x9csubmitted [no] contradictory evidence\xe2\x80\x9d and did not argue that the expert opinion offered by the Nation was \xe2\x80\x9ceither incorrect or exaggerated.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.[Footnote 11] In this respect, the case is indeed novel. It is the first case in which a Tribe demonstrated below that the imposition of a state tax would prevent the Tribe from imposing its own tax. Cf. Cotton Petroleum, 490 U. S., at 185 (state and tribal taxes were not mutually exclusive because \xe2\x80\x9cthe Tribe could, in fact, increase its taxes without adversely affecting on-reservation oil and gas development\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals considered instructive this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in California v. Cabazon Band of Mission Indians, 480 U. S. 202 (1987). See 379 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 985. The Court there held that tribal and federal interests outweighed state interests in regulating tribe-operated facilities for bingo and other games. Cabazon, 480 U. S., at 219\xe2\x80\x93220. Distinguishing Colville, the Court pointed out that the Tribes in Cabazon \xe2\x80\x9c[were] not merely importing a product onto the reservatio[n] for immediate resale to non-Indians\xe2\x80\x9d; they had \xe2\x80\x9cbuilt modern facilities\xe2\x80\x9d and provided \xe2\x80\x9cancillary services\xe2\x80\x9d so that customers would come in increasing numbers and \xe2\x80\x9cspend extended periods of time\xe2\x80\x9d playing their \xe2\x80\x9cwell-run games.\xe2\x80\x9d 480 U. S., at 219; see also New Mexico v. Mescalero Apache Tribe, 462 U. S. 324, 327, 341 (1983) (Mescalero II) (State barred from regulating hunting and fishing on-reservation where the Tribe had constructed a \xe2\x80\x9cresort complex\xe2\x80\x9d and developed wildlife and land resources).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As in Cabazon, so here, the Nation Station is not \xe2\x80\x9cmerely importing a product onto the reservatio[n] for immediate resale to non-Indians\xe2\x80\x9d at a stand-alone retail outlet. 480 U. S., at 219. Fuel sales at the Nation Station are \xe2\x80\x9can integral and essential part of the [Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s] on-reservation gaming enterprise.\xe2\x80\x9d 379 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 984. The Nation built the Nation Station as a convenience for its casino patrons and, but for the casino, there would be no market for fuel in this otherwise remote area. Id., at 982.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals further emphasized that the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cinterests here are strengthened because of its need to raise fuel revenues to construct and maintain reservation roads, bridges, and related infrastructure without state assistance.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 985. The Nation\xe2\x80\x99s fuel revenue comes exclusively from the Nation Station, and that revenue (approximately $300,000 annually) may be used only for \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98constructing and maintaining roads, bridges and rights-of-way located on or near the reservation.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 985\xe2\x80\x93986 (quoting Prairie Band Potawatomi Law and Order Code \xc2\xa710\xe2\x80\x936\xe2\x80\x937 (2003)).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Nation\xe2\x80\x99s interests coincide with \xe2\x80\x9cstrong federal interests in promoting tribal economic development, tribal self-sufficiency, and strong tribal governments.\xe2\x80\x9d 379 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 986. The United States points to the poor condition of Indian reservation roads, documented in federal reports, conditions that affect not only driving safety, but also the ability to furnish emergency medical, fire, and police services on an expedited basis, transportation to schools and jobs, and the advancement of economic activity critical to tribal self-sufficiency. Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 26; see, e.g., Dept. of Commerce, Bureau of Indian Affairs, TEA\xe2\x80\x9321 Reauthorization Resource Paper: Transportation Serving Native American Lands (May 2003). The shared interest of the Federal Government and the Nation in improving reservation roads is reflected in Department of the Interior regulations implementing the Indian Reservation Roads Program. See 69 Fed. Reg. 43090 (2004); 25 CFR \xc2\xa7170 et seq. (2005). The regulations aim at enhancing the ability of tribal governments to promote road construction and maintenance. They anticipate that Tribes will supplement federal funds with their own revenues, including funds gained from a \xe2\x80\x9c[t]ribal fuel tax.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7170.932(d). Because the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s roads are integrally related to its casino enterprise, they also further federal interests in tribal economic development advanced by the Indian Gaming Regulatory Act, Pub. L. 100\xe2\x80\x93497, 102 Stat. 2467, 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72701 et seq.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Against these strong tribal and federal interests, Kansas asserts only its \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral interest in raising revenues.\xe2\x80\x9d 379 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 986. \xe2\x80\x9cKansas\xe2\x80\x99 interest,\xe2\x80\x9d as the Court of Appeals observed, \xe2\x80\x9cis not at its strongest.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 987. By effectively taxing the Nation Station, Kansas would be deriving revenue \xe2\x80\x9cprimarily from value generated on the reservation\xe2\x80\x9d by the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s casino. Ibid. Moreover, the revenue Kansas would gain from applying its tax to fuel destined for the Nation Station appears insubstantial when compared with the total revenue ($6.1 billion in 2004) the State annually collects through the tax. See id., at 982; Brief for Respondent 12 (observing that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he tax revenues at issue\xe2\x80\x94roughly $300,000 annually\xe2\x80\x94are less than one-tenth of one percent of the total state fuel tax revenues\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court asserts that \xe2\x80\x9cKansas uses the proceeds from its fuel tax to pay for a significant portion of the costs of maintaining the roads and bridges on the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s reservation.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 18. The record reveals a different reality. According to the affidavit of the Director of the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s Road and Bridge Department, Kansas and its subdivisions have failed to provide proper maintenance even on their own roads running through the reservation. App. 79. As a result, the Nation has had to assume responsibility for a steadily growing number of road miles within the reservation (roughly 118 of the 212 total miles in 2000). Ibid.; see also Brief for Respondent 3, 40, 44\xe2\x80\x9345. Of greater significance, Kansas expends none of its fuel tax revenue on the upkeep or improvement of tribally owned reservation roads. 379 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 986\xe2\x80\x93987; cf. Ramah, 458 U. S., at 843, n.\xc2\xa07 (\xe2\x80\x9cThis case would be different if the State were actively seeking tax revenues for the purpose of constructing, or assisting in the efforts to provide, adequate [tribal services].\xe2\x80\x9d). In contrast, Kansas sets aside a significant percentage of its fuel tax revenues (over 40% in 1999) for counties and localities. Kan. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa779\xe2\x80\x933425 (2003 Cum. Supp.); see also \xc2\xa779\xe2\x80\x9334,142 (1997) (prescribing allocation formula); 1999 Kan. Sess. Laws, ch. 137, \xc2\xa737. And, as indicated earlier, supra, at 4\xe2\x80\x935, Kansas accords the Nation no dispensation based on the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign status. The Nation thus receives neither a state exemption so that it can impose its own fuel tax, nor a share of the State\xe2\x80\x99s fuel tax revenues. Accordingly, the net result of invalidating Kansas\xe2\x80\x99 tax as applied to fuel distributed to the Nation Station would be a somewhat more equitable distribution of road maintenance revenues in Kansas.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Kansas argues that, were the Nation to prevail in this case, nothing would stop the Nation from reducing its tax in order to sell gas below the market price. Brief for Petitioner 30. Colville should quell the State\xe2\x80\x99s fears in this regard. Were the Nation to pursue such a course, it would be marketing an exemption, much as the smokeshops did in Colville, and hence, interest balancing would likely yield a judgment for the State. See 447 U. S., at 155\xe2\x80\x93157. In any event, as the Nation points out, the State could guard against the risk that \xe2\x80\x9cTribes will impose a \xe2\x80\x98nominal tax\xe2\x80\x99 and sell goods at a deep discount on the reservation.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 34\xe2\x80\x9335. The State could provide a credit for any tribal tax imposed or enact a state tax that applies only to the extent that the Nation fails to impose an equivalent tribal tax. Id., at 35.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision is particularly troubling because of the cloud it casts over the most beneficial means to resolve conflicts of this order. In Oklahoma Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Citizen Band Potawatomi Tribe of Okla., 498 U. S. 505 (1991), the Court counseled that States and Tribes may enter into agreements establishing \xe2\x80\x9ca mutually satisfactory regime for the collection of this sort of tax.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 514; see also Nevada v. Hicks, 533 U. S. 353, 393 (2001) (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment) (describing various state-tribal agreements); Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 28\xe2\x80\x9329, and n.\xc2\xa012; Brief for National Intertribal Tax Alliance et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae; Ansson, State Taxation of Non-Indians Who Do Business With Indian Tribes: Why Several Recent Ninth Circuit Holdings Reemphasize the Need for Indian Tribes to Enter Into Taxation Compacts With Their Respective States, 78 Ore. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 501, 546 (1999) (\xe2\x80\x9cMore than 200 Tribes in eighteen states have resolved their taxation disputes by entering into intergovernmental agreements.\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 12] By truncating the balancing-of-interests approach, the Court has diminished prospects for cooperative efforts to achieve resolution of taxation issues through constructive intergovernmental agreements.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, the Nation operates the Nation Station in order to provide a service for patrons at its casino without, in any way, seeking to attract bargain hunters on the lookout for cheap gas. Kansas\xe2\x80\x99 collection of its tax on fuel destined for the Nation Station will effectively nullify the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s tax, which funds critical reservation road-building programs, endeavors not aided by state funds. I resist that unbalanced judgment.
