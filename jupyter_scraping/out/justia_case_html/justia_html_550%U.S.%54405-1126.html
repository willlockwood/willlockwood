BELL ATLANTIC CORP. et\xc2\xa0al. v. TWOMBLY et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the second circuit

\n

No. 05\xe2\x80\x931126.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 27, 2006\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 21, 2007

\n

The 1984 divestiture of the American Telephone & Telegraph Company\xe2\x80\x99s (AT&T) local telephone business left a system of regional service monopolies, sometimes called Incumbent Local Exchange Carriers (ILECs), and a separate long-distance market from which the ILECs were excluded. The Telecommunications Act of 1996 withdrew approval of the ILECs\xe2\x80\x99 monopolies, \xe2\x80\x9cfundamentally restructur[ing] local telephone markets\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9csubject[ing] [ILECs] to a host of duties intended to facilitate market entry.\xe2\x80\x9d AT&T Corp. v. Iowa Utilities Bd., 525 U. S. 366, 371. It also authorized them to enter the long-distance market. \xe2\x80\x9cCentral to the [new] scheme [was each ILEC\xe2\x80\x99s] obligation \xe2\x80\xa6 to share its network with\xe2\x80\x9d competitive local exchange carriers (CLECs).\xe2\x80\x9d Verizon Communications Inc. v. Law Offices of Curtis V. Trinko, LLP, 540 U. S. 398, 402.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents (hereinafter plaintiffs) represent a class of subscribers of local telephone and/or high speed Internet services in this action against petitioner ILECs for claimed violations of \xc2\xa71 of the Sherman Act, which prohibits \xe2\x80\x9c[e]very contract, combination in the form of trust or otherwise, or conspiracy, in restraint of trade or commerce among the several States, or with foreign nations.\xe2\x80\x9d The complaint alleges that the ILECs conspired to restrain trade (1) by engaging in parallel conduct in their respective service areas to inhibit the growth of upstart CLECs; and (2) by agreeing to refrain from competing against one another, as indicated by their common failure to pursue attractive business opportunities in contiguous markets and by a statement by one ILEC\xe2\x80\x99s chief executive officer that competing in another ILEC\xe2\x80\x99s territory did not seem right. The District Court dismissed the complaint, concluding that parallel business conduct allegations, taken alone, do not state a claim under \xc2\xa71; plaintiffs must allege additional facts tending to exclude independent self-interested conduct as an explanation for the parallel actions. Reversing, the Second Circuit held that plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 parallel conduct allegations were sufficient to withstand a motion to dismiss because the ILECs failed to show that there is no set of facts that would permit plaintiffs to demonstrate that the particular parallelism asserted was the product of collusion rather than coincidence.

\n

Held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0Stating a \xc2\xa71 claim requires a complaint with enough factual matter (taken as true) to suggest that an agreement was made. An allegation of parallel conduct and a bare assertion of conspiracy will not suffice. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Because \xc2\xa71 prohibits \xe2\x80\x9conly restraints effected by a contract, combination, or conspiracy,\xe2\x80\x9d Copperweld Corp. v. Independence Tube Corp., 467 U. S. 752, 775, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he crucial question\xe2\x80\x9d is whether the challenged anticompetitive conduct \xe2\x80\x9cstem[s] from independent decision or from an agreement,\xe2\x80\x9d Theatre Enterprises, Inc. v. Paramount Film Distributing Corp., 346 U. S. 537, 540. While a showing of parallel \xe2\x80\x9cbusiness behavior is admissible circumstantial evidence from which\xe2\x80\x9d agreement may be inferred, it falls short of \xe2\x80\x9cconclusively establish[ing] agreement or \xe2\x80\xa6 itself constitut[ing] a Sherman Act offense.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 540\xe2\x80\x93541. The inadequacy of showing parallel conduct or interdependence, without more, mirrors the behavior\xe2\x80\x99s ambiguity: consistent with conspiracy, but just as much in line with a wide swath of rational and competitive business strategy unilaterally prompted by common perceptions of the market. Thus, this Court has hedged against false inferences from identical behavior at a number of points in the trial sequence, e.g., at the summary judgment stage, see Matsushita Elec. Industrial Co. v. Zenith Radio Corp., 475 U. S. 574. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0This case presents the antecedent question of what a plaintiff must plead in order to state a \xc2\xa71 claim. Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 8(a)(2) requires only \xe2\x80\x9ca short and plain statement of the claim showing that the pleader is entitled to relief,\xe2\x80\x9d in order to \xe2\x80\x9cgive the defendant fair notice of what the \xe2\x80\xa6 claim is and the grounds upon which it rests,\xe2\x80\x9d Conley v. Gibson, 355 U. S. 41, 47. While a complaint attacked by a Rule 12(b)(6) motion to dismiss does not need detailed factual allegations, ibid., a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s obligation to provide the \xe2\x80\x9cgrounds\xe2\x80\x9d of his \xe2\x80\x9centitle[ment] to relief\xe2\x80\x9d requires more than labels and conclusions, and a formulaic recitation of a cause of action\xe2\x80\x99s elements will not do. Factual allegations must be enough to raise a right to relief above the speculative level on the assumption that all of the complaint\xe2\x80\x99s allegations are true. Applying these general standards to a \xc2\xa71 claim, stating a claim requires a complaint with enough factual matter to suggest an agreement. Asking for plausible grounds does not impose a probability requirement at the pleading stage; it simply calls for enough fact to raise a reasonable expectation that discovery will reveal evidence of illegal agreement. The need at the pleading stage for allegations plausibly suggesting (not merely consistent with) agreement reflects Rule 8(a)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s threshold requirement that the \xe2\x80\x9cplain statement\xe2\x80\x9d possess enough heft to \xe2\x80\x9csho[w] that the pleader is entitled to relief.\xe2\x80\x9d A parallel conduct allegation gets the \xc2\xa71 complaint close to stating a claim, but without further factual enhancement it stops short of the line between possibility and plausibility. The requirement of allegations suggesting an agreement serves the practical purpose of preventing a plaintiff with \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a largely groundless claim\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d from \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98tak[ing] up the time of a number of other people, with the right to do so representing an in terrorem increment of the settlement value.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Dura Pharmaceuticals, Inc. v. Broudo, 544 U. S. 336, 347. It is one thing to be cautious before dismissing an antitrust complaint in advance of discovery, but quite another to forget that proceeding to antitrust discovery can be expensive. That potential expense is obvious here, where plaintiffs represent a putative class of at least 90 percent of subscribers to local telephone or high-speed Internet service in an action against America\xe2\x80\x99s largest telecommunications firms for unspecified instances of antitrust violations that allegedly occurred over a 7-year period. It is no answer to say that a claim just shy of plausible entitlement can be weeded out early in the discovery process, given the common lament that the success of judicial supervision in checking discovery abuse has been modest. Plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 main argument against the plausibility standard at the pleading stage is its ostensible conflict with a literal reading of Conley\xe2\x80\x99s statement construing Rule 8: \xe2\x80\x9ca complaint should not be dismissed for failure to state a claim unless it appears beyond doubt that the plaintiff can prove no set of facts in support of his claim which would entitle him to relief.\xe2\x80\x9d 355 U. S., at 45\xe2\x80\x9346. The \xe2\x80\x9cno set of facts\xe2\x80\x9d language has been questioned, criticized, and explained away long enough by courts and commentators, and is best forgotten as an incomplete, negative gloss on an accepted pleading standard: once a claim has been stated adequately, it may be supported by showing any set of facts consistent with the allegations in the complaint. Conley described the breadth of opportunity to prove what an adequate complaint claims, not the minimum standard of adequate pleading to govern a complaint\xe2\x80\x99s survival. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0Under the plausibility standard, plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 claim of conspiracy in restraint of trade comes up short. First, the complaint leaves no doubt that plaintiffs rest their \xc2\xa71 claim on descriptions of parallel conduct, not on any independent allegation of actual agreement among the ILECs. The nub of the complaint is the ILECs\xe2\x80\x99 parallel behavior, and its sufficiency turns on the suggestions raised by this conduct when viewed in light of common economic experience. Nothing in the complaint invests either the action or inaction alleged with a plausible conspiracy suggestion. As to the ILECs\xe2\x80\x99 supposed agreement to disobey the 1996 Act and thwart the CLECs\xe2\x80\x99 attempts to compete, the District Court correctly found that nothing in the complaint intimates that resisting the upstarts was anything more than the natural, unilateral reaction of each ILEC intent on preserving its regional dominance. The complaint\xe2\x80\x99s general collusion premise fails to answer the point that there was no need for joint encouragement to resist the 1996 Act, since each ILEC had reason to try and avoid dealing with CLECs and would have tried to keep them out, regardless of the other ILECs\xe2\x80\x99 actions. Plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 second conspiracy theory rests on the competitive reticence among the ILECs themselves in the wake of the 1996 Act to enter into their competitors\xe2\x80\x99 territories, leaving the relevant market highly compartmentalized geographically, with minimal competition. This parallel conduct did not suggest conspiracy, not if history teaches anything. Monopoly was the norm in telecommunications, not the exception. Because the ILECs were born in that world, doubtless liked it, and surely knew the adage about him who lives by the sword, a natural explanation for the noncompetition is that the former Government-sanctioned monopolists were sitting tight, expecting their neighbors to do the same. Antitrust conspiracy was not suggested by the facts adduced under either theory of the complaint, which thus fails to state a valid \xc2\xa71 claim. This analysis does not run counter to Swierkiewicz v. Sorema N.\xc2\xa0A., 534 U. S. 506, 508, which held that \xe2\x80\x9ca complaint in an employment discrimination lawsuit [need] not contain specific facts establishing a prima facie case of discrimination.\xe2\x80\x9d Here, the Court is not requiring heightened fact pleading of specifics, but only enough facts to state a claim to relief that is plausible on its face. Because the plaintiffs here have not nudged their claims across the line from conceivable to plausible, their complaint must be dismissed. Pp.\xc2\xa018\xe2\x80\x9324.

\n

425 F.\xc2\xa03d 99, reversed and remanded.

BELL ATLANTIC CORPORATION, et\xc2\xa0al., PETI-\nTIONERS v. WILLIAM TWOMBLY et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the second circuit

\n

[May 21, 2007]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Souter delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Liability under \xc2\xa71 of the Sherman Act, 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71, requires a \xe2\x80\x9ccontract, combination\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xe2\x80\x82, or conspiracy, in restraint of trade or commerce.\xe2\x80\x9d The question in this putative class action is whether a \xc2\xa71 complaint can survive a motion to dismiss when it alleges that major telecommunications providers engaged in certain parallel conduct unfavorable to competition, absent some factual context suggesting agreement, as distinct from identical, independent action. We hold that such a complaint should be dismissed.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The upshot of the 1984 divestiture of the American Telephone & Telegraph Company\xe2\x80\x99s (AT&T) local telephone business was a system of regional service monopolies (variously called \xe2\x80\x9cRegional Bell Operating Companies,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cBaby Bells,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cIncumbent Local Exchange Carriers\xe2\x80\x9d (ILECs)), and a separate, competitive market for long-distance service from which the ILECs were excluded. More than a decade later, Congress withdrew approval of the ILECs\xe2\x80\x99 monopolies by enacting the Telecommunications Act of 1996 (1996 Act), 110 Stat. 56, which \xe2\x80\x9cfundamentally restructure[d] local telephone markets\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9csubject[ed] [ILECs] to a host of duties intended to facilitate market entry.\xe2\x80\x9d AT&T Corp. v. Iowa Utilities Bd., 525 U. S. 366, 371 (1999). In recompense, the 1996 Act set conditions for authorizing ILECs to enter the long-distance market. See 47 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7271.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cCentral to the [new] scheme [was each ILEC\xe2\x80\x99s] obligation \xe2\x80\xa6 to share its network with competitors,\xe2\x80\x9d Verizon Communications Inc. v. Law Offices of Curtis V. Trinko, LLP, 540 U. S. 398, 402 (2004), which came to be known as \xe2\x80\x9ccompetitive local exchange carriers\xe2\x80\x9d (CLECs), Pet. for Cert. 6, n.\xe2\x80\x821. A CLEC could make use of an ILEC\xe2\x80\x99s network in any of three ways: by (1) \xe2\x80\x9cpurchas[ing] local telephone services at wholesale rates for resale to end users,\xe2\x80\x9d (2) \xe2\x80\x9cleas[ing] elements of the [ILEC\xe2\x80\x99s] network \xe2\x80\x98on an unbundled basis,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d or (3) \xe2\x80\x9cinterconnect[ing] its own facilities with the [ILEC\xe2\x80\x99s] network.\xe2\x80\x9d Iowa Utilities Bd., supra, at 371 (quoting 47 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7251(c)). Owing to the \xe2\x80\x9cconsiderable expense and effort\xe2\x80\x9d required to make unbundled network elements available to rivals at wholesale prices, Trinko, supra, at 410, the ILECs vigorously litigated the scope of the sharing obligation imposed by the 1996 Act, with the result that the Federal Communications Commission (FCC) three times revised its regulations to narrow the range of network elements to be shared with the CLECs. See Covad Communications Co. v. FCC, 450 F.\xc2\xa03d 528, 533\xe2\x80\x93534 (CADC 2006) (summarizing the 10-year-long regulatory struggle between the ILECs and CLECs).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents William Twombly and Lawrence Marcus (hereinafter plaintiffs) represent a putative class consisting of all \xe2\x80\x9csubscribers of local telephone and/or high speed internet services \xe2\x80\xa6 from February 8, 1996 to present.\xe2\x80\x9d Amended Complaint in No. 02 CIV. 10220 (GEL) (SDNY) \xc2\xb653, App. 28 (hereinafter Complaint). In this action against petitioners, a group of ILECs,[Footnote 1] plaintiffs seek treble damages and declaratory and injunctive relief for claimed violations of \xc2\xa71 of the Sherman Act, ch. 647, 26 Stat. 209, as amended, 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71, which prohibits \xe2\x80\x9c[e]very contract, combination in the form of trust or otherwise, or conspiracy, in restraint of trade or commerce among the several States, or with foreign nations.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The complaint alleges that the ILECs conspired to restrain trade in two ways, each supposedly inflating charges for local telephone and high-speed Internet services. Plaintiffs say, first, that the ILECs \xe2\x80\x9cengaged in parallel conduct\xe2\x80\x9d in their respective service areas to inhibit the growth of upstart CLECs. Complaint \xc2\xb647, App. 23\xe2\x80\x9326. Their actions allegedly included making unfair agreements with the CLECs for access to ILEC networks, providing inferior connections to the networks, overcharging, and billing in ways designed to sabotage the CLECs\xe2\x80\x99 relations with their own customers. Ibid. According to the complaint, the ILECs\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9ccompelling common motivatio[n]\xe2\x80\x9d to thwart the CLECs\xe2\x80\x99 competitive efforts naturally led them to form a conspiracy; \xe2\x80\x9c[h]ad any one [ILEC] not sought to prevent CLECs \xe2\x80\xa6 from competing effectively\xe2\x80\x82.\xe2\x80\x82.\xe2\x80\x82.\xe2\x80\x82, the resulting greater competitive inroads into that [ILEC\xe2\x80\x99s] territory would have revealed the degree to which competitive entry by CLECs would have been successful in the other territories in the absence of such conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., \xc2\xb650, App. 26\xe2\x80\x9327.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, the complaint charges agreements by the ILECs to refrain from competing against one another. These are to be inferred from the ILECs\xe2\x80\x99 common failure \xe2\x80\x9cmeaningfully [to] pursu[e]\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cattractive business opportunit[ies]\xe2\x80\x9d in contiguous markets where they possessed \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial competitive advantages,\xe2\x80\x9d id., \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb640\xe2\x80\x9341, App. 21\xe2\x80\x9322, and from a statement of Richard Notebaert, chief executive officer (CEO) of the ILEC Qwest, that competing in the territory of another ILEC \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98might be a good way to turn a quick dollar but that doesn\xe2\x80\x99t make it right,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d id., \xc2\xb642, App. 22.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The complaint couches its ultimate allegations this way:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIn the absence of any meaningful competition between the [ILECs] in one another\xe2\x80\x99s markets, and in light of the parallel course of conduct that each engaged in to prevent competition from CLECs within their respective local telephone and/or high speed internet services markets and the other facts and market circumstances alleged above, Plaintiffs allege upon information and belief that [the ILECs] have entered into a contract, combination or conspiracy to prevent competitive entry in their respective local telephone and/or high speed internet services markets and have agreed not to compete with one another and otherwise allocated customers and markets to one another.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., \xc2\xb651, App. 27.[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The United States District Court for the Southern District of New York dismissed the complaint for failure to state a claim upon which relief can be granted. The District Court acknowledged that \xe2\x80\x9cplaintiffs may allege a conspiracy by citing instances of parallel business behavior that suggest an agreement,\xe2\x80\x9d but emphasized that \xe2\x80\x9cwhile \xe2\x80\x98[c]ircumstantial evidence of consciously parallel behavior may have made heavy inroads into the traditional judicial attitude toward conspiracy[,\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6] \xe2\x80\x9cconscious parallelism\xe2\x80\x9d has not yet read conspiracy out of the Sherman Act entirely.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 313 F. Supp. 2d 174, 179 (2003) (quoting Theatre Enterprises, Inc. v. Paramount Film Distributing Corp., 346 U. S. 537, 541 (1954); alterations in original). Thus, the District Court understood that allegations of parallel business conduct, taken alone, do not state a claim under \xc2\xa71; plaintiffs must allege additional facts that \xe2\x80\x9cten[d] to exclude independent self-interested conduct as an explanation for defendants\xe2\x80\x99 parallel behavior.\xe2\x80\x9d 313 F. Supp. 2d, at 179. The District Court found plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 allegations of parallel ILEC actions to discourage competition inadequate because \xe2\x80\x9cthe behavior of each ILEC in resisting the incursion of CLECs is fully explained by the ILEC\xe2\x80\x99s own interests in defending its individual territory.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 183. As to the ILECs\xe2\x80\x99 supposed agreement against competing with each other, the District Court found that the complaint does not \xe2\x80\x9calleg[e] facts \xe2\x80\xa6 suggesting that refraining from competing in other territories as CLECs was contrary to [the ILECs\xe2\x80\x99] apparent economic interests, and consequently [does] not rais[e] an inference that [the ILECs\xe2\x80\x99] actions were the result of a conspiracy.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 188.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit reversed, holding that the District Court tested the complaint by the wrong standard. It held that \xe2\x80\x9cplus factors are not required to be pleaded to permit an antitrust claim based on parallel conduct to survive dismissal.\xe2\x80\x9d 425 F.\xc2\xa03d 99, 114 (2005) (emphasis in original). Although the Court of Appeals took the view that plaintiffs must plead facts that \xe2\x80\x9cinclude conspiracy among the realm of \xe2\x80\x98plausible\xe2\x80\x99 possibilities in order to survive a motion to dismiss,\xe2\x80\x9d it then said that \xe2\x80\x9cto rule that allegations of parallel anticompetitive conduct fail to support a plausible conspiracy claim, a court would have to conclude that there is no set of facts that would permit a plaintiff to demonstrate that the particular parallelism asserted was the product of collusion rather than coincidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari to address the proper standard for pleading an antitrust conspiracy through allegations of parallel conduct, 547 U. S. ___ (2006), and now reverse.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because \xc2\xa71 of the Sherman Act \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not prohibit [all] unreasonable restraints of trade \xe2\x80\xa6 but only restraints effected by a contract, combination, or conspiracy,\xe2\x80\x9d Copperweld Corp. v. Independence Tube Corp., 467 U. S. 752, 775 (1984), \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he crucial question\xe2\x80\x9d is whether the challenged anticompetitive conduct \xe2\x80\x9cstem[s] from independent decision or from an agreement, tacit or express,\xe2\x80\x9d Theatre Enterprises, 346 U. S., at 540. While a showing of parallel \xe2\x80\x9cbusiness behavior is admissible circumstantial evidence from which the fact finder may infer agreement,\xe2\x80\x9d it falls short of \xe2\x80\x9cconclusively establish[ing] agreement or \xe2\x80\xa6 itself constitut[ing] a Sherman Act offense.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 540\xe2\x80\x93541. Even \xe2\x80\x9cconscious parallelism,\xe2\x80\x9d a common reaction of \xe2\x80\x9cfirms in a concentrated market [that] recogniz[e] their shared economic interests and their interdependence with respect to price and output decisions\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cnot in itself unlawful.\xe2\x80\x9d Brooke Group Ltd. v. Brown & Williamson Tobacco Corp., 509 U. S. 209, 227 (1993); see 6 P. Areeda & H. Hovenkamp, Antitrust Law \xc2\xb61433a, p. 236 (2d ed. 2003) (hereinafter Areeda & Hovenkamp) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe courts are nearly unanimous in saying that mere interdependent parallelism does not establish the contract, combination, or conspiracy required by Sherman Act \xc2\xa71\xe2\x80\x9d); Turner, The Definition of Agreement Under the Sherman Act: Conscious Parallelism and Refusals to Deal, 75 Harv. L. Rev. 655, 672 (1962) (\xe2\x80\x9c[M]ere interdependence of basic price decisions is not conspiracy\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The inadequacy of showing parallel conduct or interdependence, without more, mirrors the ambiguity of the behavior: consistent with conspiracy, but just as much in line with a wide swath of rational and competitive business strategy unilaterally prompted by common perceptions of the market. See, e.g., AEI-Brookings Joint Center for Regulatory Studies, Epstein, Motions to Dismiss Antitrust Cases: Separating Fact from Fantasy, Related Publication 06\xe2\x80\x9308, pp.\xe2\x80\x823\xe2\x80\x934 (2006) (discussing problem of \xe2\x80\x9cfalse positives\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa71 suits). Accordingly, we have previously hedged against false inferences from identical behavior at a number of points in the trial sequence. An antitrust conspiracy plaintiff with evidence showing nothing beyond parallel conduct is not entitled to a directed verdict, see Theatre Enterprises, supra; proof of a \xc2\xa71 conspiracy must include evidence tending to exclude the possibility of independent action, see Monsanto Co. v. Spray-Rite Service Corp., 465 U.\xe2\x80\x82S. 752 (1984); and at the summary judgment stage a \xc2\xa71 plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s offer of conspiracy evidence must tend to rule out the possibility that the defendants were acting independently, see Matsushita Elec. Industrial Co. v. Zenith Radio Corp., 475 U. S. 574 (1986).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case presents the antecedent question of what a plaintiff must plead in order to state a claim under \xc2\xa71 of the Sherman Act. Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 8(a)(2) requires only \xe2\x80\x9ca short and plain statement of the claim showing that the pleader is entitled to relief,\xe2\x80\x9d in order to \xe2\x80\x9cgive the defendant fair notice of what the \xe2\x80\xa6 claim is and the grounds upon which it rests,\xe2\x80\x9d Conley v. Gibson, 355 U. S. 41, 47 (1957). While a complaint attacked by a Rule 12(b)(6) motion to dismiss does not need detailed factual allegations, ibid.; Sanjuan v. American Bd. of Psychiatry and Neurology, Inc., 40 F.\xc2\xa03d 247, 251 (CA7 1994), a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s obligation to provide the \xe2\x80\x9cgrounds\xe2\x80\x9d of his \xe2\x80\x9centitle[ment] to relief\xe2\x80\x9d requires more than labels and conclusions, and a formulaic recitation of the elements of a cause of action will not do, see Papasan v. Allain, 478 U. S. 265, 286 (1986) (on a motion to dismiss, courts \xe2\x80\x9care not bound to accept as true a legal conclusion couched as a factual allegation\xe2\x80\x9d). Factual allegations must be enough to raise a right to relief above the speculative level, see 5 C. Wright & A. Miller, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa71216, pp. 235\xe2\x80\x93236 (3d ed. 2004) (hereinafter Wright & Miller) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he pleading must contain something more \xe2\x80\xa6 than \xe2\x80\xa6 a statement of facts that merely creates a suspicion [of] a legally cognizable right of action\xe2\x80\x9d),[Footnote 3] on the assumption that all the allegations in the complaint are true (even if doubtful in fact), see, e.g., Swierkiewicz v. Sorema N.\xc2\xa0A., 534 U. S. 506, 508, n. 1 (2002); Neitzke v. Williams, 490 U. S. 319, 327 (1989) (\xe2\x80\x9cRule 12(b)(6) does not countenance \xe2\x80\xa6 dismissals based on a judge\xe2\x80\x99s disbelief of a complaint\xe2\x80\x99s factual allegations\xe2\x80\x9d); Scheuer v. Rhodes, 416 U. S. 232, 236 (1974) (a well-pleaded complaint may proceed even if it appears \xe2\x80\x9cthat a recovery is very remote and unlikely\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In applying these general standards to a \xc2\xa71 claim, we hold that stating such a claim requires a complaint with enough factual matter (taken as true) to suggest that an agreement was made. Asking for plausible grounds to infer an agreement does not impose a probability requirement at the pleading stage; it simply calls for enough fact to raise a reasonable expectation that discovery will reveal evidence of illegal agreement.[Footnote 4] And, of course, a well-pleaded complaint may proceed even if it strikes a savvy judge that actual proof of those facts is improbable, and \xe2\x80\x9cthat a recovery is very remote and unlikely.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. In identifying facts that are suggestive enough to render a \xc2\xa71 conspiracy plausible, we have the benefit of the prior rulings and considered views of leading commentators, already quoted, that lawful parallel conduct fails to bespeak unlawful agreement. It makes sense to say, therefore, that an allegation of parallel conduct and a bare assertion of conspiracy will not suffice. Without more, parallel conduct does not suggest conspiracy, and a conclusory allegation of agreement at some unidentified point does not supply facts adequate to show illegality. Hence, when allegations of parallel conduct are set out in order to make a \xc2\xa71 claim, they must be placed in a context that raises a suggestion of a preceding agreement, not merely parallel conduct that could just as well be independent action.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The need at the pleading stage for allegations plausibly suggesting (not merely consistent with) agreement reflects the threshold requirement of Rule 8(a)(2) that the \xe2\x80\x9cplain statement\xe2\x80\x9d possess enough heft to \xe2\x80\x9csho[w] that the pleader is entitled to relief.\xe2\x80\x9d A statement of parallel conduct, even conduct consciously undertaken, needs some setting suggesting the agreement necessary to make out a \xc2\xa71 claim; without that further circumstance pointing toward a meeting of the minds, an account of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s commercial efforts stays in neutral territory. An allegation of parallel conduct is thus much like a naked assertion of conspiracy in a \xc2\xa71 complaint: it gets the complaint close to stating a claim, but without some further factual enhancement it stops short of the line between possibility and plausibility of \xe2\x80\x9centitle[ment] to relief.\xe2\x80\x9d Cf. DM Research, Inc. v. College of Am. Pathologists, 170 F.\xc2\xa03d 53, 56 (CA1 1999) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]erms like \xe2\x80\x98conspiracy,\xe2\x80\x99 or even \xe2\x80\x98agreement,\xe2\x80\x99 are border-line: they might well be sufficient in conjunction with a more specific allegation\xe2\x80\x94for example, identifying a written agreement or even a basis for inferring a tacit agreement, \xe2\x80\xa6 but a court is not required to accept such terms as a sufficient basis for a complaint\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We alluded to the practical significance of the Rule 8 entitlement requirement in Dura Pharmaceuticals, Inc. v. Broudo, 544 U. S. 336 (2005), when we explained that something beyond the mere possibility of loss causation must be alleged, lest a plaintiff with \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a largely groundless claim\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d be allowed to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98take up the time of a number of other people, with the right to do so representing an in terrorem increment of the settlement value.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 347 (quoting Blue Chip Stamps v. Manor Drug Stores, 421 U. S. 723, 741 (1975)). So, when the allegations in a complaint, however true, could not raise a claim of entitlement to relief, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98this basic deficiency should . . . be exposed at the point of minimum expenditure of time and money by the parties and the court.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 5 Wright & Miller \xc2\xa71216, at 233\xe2\x80\x93234 (quoting Daves v. Hawaiian Dredging Co., 114 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 643, 645 (Haw. 1953)); see also Dura, supra, at 346; Asahi Glass Co. v. Pentech Pharmaceuticals, Inc., 289 F.\xc2\xa0Supp.\xc2\xa02d 986, 995 (ND Ill. 2003) (Posner, J., sitting by designation) (\xe2\x80\x9c[S]ome threshold of plausibility must be crossed at the outset before a patent antitrust case should be permitted to go into its inevitably costly and protracted discovery phase\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thus, it is one thing to be cautious before dismissing an antitrust complaint in advance of discovery, cf. Poller v. Columbia Broadcasting System, Inc., 368 U. S. 464, 473 (1962), but quite another to forget that proceeding to antitrust discovery can be expensive. As we indicated over 20 years ago in Associated Gen. Contractors of Cal., Inc. v. Carpenters, 459 U. S. 519, 528, n. 17 (1983), \xe2\x80\x9ca district court must retain the power to insist upon some specificity in pleading before allowing a potentially massive factual controversy to proceed.\xe2\x80\x9d See also Car Carriers, Inc. v. Ford Motor Co., 745 F.\xc2\xa02d 1101, 1106 (CA7 1984) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he costs of modern federal antitrust litigation and the increasing caseload of the federal courts counsel against sending the parties into discovery when there is no reasonable likelihood that the plaintiffs can construct a claim from the events related in the complaint\xe2\x80\x9d); Note, Modeling the Effect of One-Way Fee Shifting on Discovery Abuse in Private Antitrust Litigation, 78 N.\xe2\x80\x82Y.\xe2\x80\x82U.\xe2\x80\x82L. Rev. 1887, 1898\xe2\x80\x931899 (2003) (discussing the unusually high cost of discovery in antitrust cases); Manual for Complex Litigation, Fourth, \xc2\xa730, p.\xe2\x80\x82519 (2004) (describing extensive scope of discovery in antitrust cases); Memorandum from Paul V. Niemeyer, Chair, Advisory Committee on Civil Rules, to Hon. Anthony J. Scirica, Chair, Committee on Rules of Practice and Procedure (May 11, 1999), 192 F.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0D. 354, 357 (2000) (reporting that discovery accounts for as much as 90 percent of litigation costs when discovery is actively employed). That potential expense is obvious enough in the present case: plaintiffs represent a putative class of at least 90 percent of all subscribers to local telephone or high-speed Internet service in the continental United States, in an action against America\xe2\x80\x99s largest telecommunications firms (with many thousands of employees generating reams and gigabytes of business records) for unspecified (if any) instances of antitrust violations that allegedly occurred over a period of seven years.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is no answer to say that a claim just shy of a plausible entitlement to relief can, if groundless, be weeded out early in the discovery process through \xe2\x80\x9ccareful case management,\xe2\x80\x9d post at 4, given the common lament that the success of judicial supervision in checking discovery abuse has been on the modest side. See, e.g., Easterbrook, Discovery as Abuse, 69 B. U. L. Rev. 635, 638 (1989) (\xe2\x80\x9cJudges can do little about impositional discovery when parties control the legal claims to be presented and conduct the discovery themselves\xe2\x80\x9d). And it is self-evident that the problem of discovery abuse cannot be solved by \xe2\x80\x9ccareful scrutiny of evidence at the summary judgment stage,\xe2\x80\x9d much less \xe2\x80\x9clucid instructions to juries,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 4; the threat of discovery expense will push cost-conscious defendants to settle even anemic cases before reaching those proceedings. Probably, then, it is only by taking care to require allegations that reach the level suggesting conspiracy that we can hope to avoid the potentially enormous expense of discovery in cases with no \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98reasonably founded hope that the [discovery] process will reveal relevant evidence\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to support a \xc2\xa71 claim. Dura, 544 U. S., at 347 (quoting Blue Chip Stamps, supra, at 741; alteration in Dura).[Footnote 6]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Plaintiffs do not, of course, dispute the requirement of plausibility and the need for something more than merely parallel behavior explained in Theatre Enterprises, Monsanto, and Matsushita, and their main argument against the plausibility standard at the pleading stage is its ostensible conflict with an early statement of ours construing Rule 8. Justice Black\xe2\x80\x99s opinion for the Court in Conley v. Gibson spoke not only of the need for fair notice of the grounds for entitlement to relief but of \xe2\x80\x9cthe accepted rule that a complaint should not be dismissed for failure to state a claim unless it appears beyond doubt that the plaintiff can prove no set of facts in support of his claim which would entitle him to relief.\xe2\x80\x9d 355 U. S., at 45\xe2\x80\x9346. This \xe2\x80\x9cno set of facts\xe2\x80\x9d language can be read in isolation as saying that any statement revealing the theory of the claim will suffice unless its factual impossibility may be shown from the face of the pleadings; and the Court of Appeals appears to have read Conley in some such way when formulating its understanding of the proper pleading standard, see 425 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 106, 114 (invoking Conley\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cno set of facts\xe2\x80\x9d language in describing the standard for dismissal).[Footnote 7]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On such a focused and literal reading of Conley\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cno set of facts,\xe2\x80\x9d a wholly conclusory statement of claim would survive a motion to dismiss whenever the pleadings left open the possibility that a plaintiff might later establish some \xe2\x80\x9cset of [undisclosed] facts\xe2\x80\x9d to support recovery. So here, the Court of Appeals specifically found the prospect of unearthing direct evidence of conspiracy sufficient to preclude dismissal, even though the complaint does not set forth a single fact in a context that suggests an agreement. 425 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 106, 114. It seems fair to say that this approach to pleading would dispense with any showing of a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98reasonably founded hope\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d that a plaintiff would be able to make a case, see Dura, 544 U. S., at 347 (quoting Blue Chip Stamps, 421 U. S., at 741); Mr. Micawber\xe2\x80\x99s optimism would be enough.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Seeing this, a good many judges and commentators have balked at taking the literal terms of the Conley passage as a pleading standard. See, e.g., Car Carriers, 745 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 1106 (\xe2\x80\x9cConley has never been interpreted literally\xe2\x80\x9d and, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n practice, a complaint \xe2\x80\xa6 must contain either direct or inferential allegations respecting all the material elements necessary to sustain recovery under some viable legal theory\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted; emphasis and omission in original); Ascon Properties, Inc. v. Mobil Oil Co., 866 F.\xc2\xa02d 1149, 1155 (CA9 1989) (tension between Conley\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cno set of facts\xe2\x80\x9d language and its acknowledgment that a plaintiff must provide the \xe2\x80\x9cgrounds\xe2\x80\x9d on which his claim rests); O\xe2\x80\x99Brien v. DiGrazia, 544 F.\xc2\xa02d 543, 546, n. 3 (CA1 1976) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hen a plaintiff \xe2\x80\xa6 supplies facts to support his claim, we do not think that Conley imposes a duty on the courts to conjure up unpleaded facts that might turn a frivolous claim of unconstitutional \xe2\x80\xa6 action into a substantial one\xe2\x80\x9d); McGregor v. Industrial Excess Landfill, Inc., 856 F.\xc2\xa02d 39, 42\xe2\x80\x9343 (CA6 1988) (quoting O\xe2\x80\x99Brien\xe2\x80\x99s analysis); Hazard, From Whom No Secrets Are Hid, 76 Tex. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1665, 1685 (1998) (describing Conley as having \xe2\x80\x9cturned Rule 8 on its head\xe2\x80\x9d); Marcus, The Revival of Fact Pleading Under the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, 86 Colum. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 433, 463\xe2\x80\x93465 (1986) (noting tension between Conley and subsequent understandings of Rule 8).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We could go on, but there is no need to pile up further citations to show that Conley\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cno set of facts\xe2\x80\x9d language has been questioned, criticized, and explained away long enough. To be fair to the Conley Court, the passage should be understood in light of the opinion\xe2\x80\x99s preceding summary of the complaint\xe2\x80\x99s concrete allegations, which the Court quite reasonably understood as amply stating a claim for relief. But the passage so often quoted fails to mention this understanding on the part of the Court, and after puzzling the profession for 50 years, this famous observation has earned its retirement. The phrase is best forgotten as an incomplete, negative gloss on an accepted pleading standard: once a claim has been stated adequately, it may be supported by showing any set of facts consistent with the allegations in the complaint. See Sanjuan, 40 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 251 (once a claim for relief has been stated, a plaintiff \xe2\x80\x9creceives the benefit of imagination, so long as the hypotheses are consistent with the complaint\xe2\x80\x9d); accord, Swierkiewicz, 534 U. S., at 514; National Organization for Women, Inc. v. Scheidler, 510 U. S. 249, 256 (1994); H. J. Inc. v. Northwestern Bell Telephone Co., 492 U. S. 229, 249\xe2\x80\x93250 (1989); Hishon v. King & Spalding, 467 U. S. 69, 73 (1984). Conley, then, described the breadth of opportunity to prove what an adequate complaint claims, not the minimum standard of adequate pleading to govern a complaint\xe2\x80\x99s survival.[Footnote 8]

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When we look for plausibility in this complaint, we agree with the District Court that plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 claim of conspiracy in restraint of trade comes up short. To begin with, the complaint leaves no doubt that plaintiffs rest their \xc2\xa71 claim on descriptions of parallel conduct and not on any independent allegation of actual agreement among the ILECs. Supra, at 4. Although in form a few stray statements speak directly of agreement,[Footnote 9] on fair reading these are merely legal conclusions resting on the prior allegations. Thus, the complaint first takes account of the alleged \xe2\x80\x9cabsence of any meaningful competition between [the ILECs] in one another\xe2\x80\x99s markets,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cthe parallel course of conduct that each [ILEC] engaged in to prevent competition from CLECs,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cand the other facts and market circumstances alleged [earlier]\xe2\x80\x9d; \xe2\x80\x9cin light of\xe2\x80\x9d these, the complaint concludes \xe2\x80\x9cthat [the ILECs] have entered into a contract, combination or conspiracy to prevent competitive entry into their \xe2\x80\xa6 markets and have agreed not to compete with one another.\xe2\x80\x9d Complaint \xc2\xb651, App. 27.[Footnote 10] The nub of the complaint, then, is the ILECs\xe2\x80\x99 parallel behavior, consisting of steps to keep the CLECs out and manifest disinterest in becoming CLECs themselves, and its sufficiency turns on the suggestions raised by this conduct when viewed in light of common economic experience.[Footnote 11]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We think that nothing contained in the complaint invests either the action or inaction alleged with a plausible suggestion of conspiracy. As to the ILECs\xe2\x80\x99 supposed agreement to disobey the 1996 Act and thwart the CLECs\xe2\x80\x99 attempts to compete, we agree with the District Court that nothing in the complaint intimates that the resistance to the upstarts was anything more than the natural, unilateral reaction of each ILEC intent on keeping its regional dominance. The 1996 Act did more than just subject the ILECs to competition; it obliged them to subsidize their competitors with their own equipment at wholesale rates. The economic incentive to resist was powerful, but resisting competition is routine market conduct, and even if the ILECs flouted the 1996 Act in all the ways the plaintiffs allege, see id., \xc2\xb647, App. 23\xe2\x80\x9324, there is no reason to infer that the companies had agreed among themselves to do what was only natural anyway; so natural, in fact, that if alleging parallel decisions to resist competition were enough to imply an antitrust conspiracy, pleading a \xc2\xa71 violation against almost any group of competing businesses would be a sure thing.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The complaint makes its closest pass at a predicate for conspiracy with the claim that collusion was necessary because success by even one CLEC in an ILEC\xe2\x80\x99s territory \xe2\x80\x9cwould have revealed the degree to which competitive entry by CLECs would have been successful in the other territories.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., \xc2\xb650, App. 26\xe2\x80\x9327. But, its logic aside, this general premise still fails to answer the point that there was just no need for joint encouragement to resist the 1996 Act; as the District Court said, \xe2\x80\x9ceach ILEC has reason to want to avoid dealing with CLECs\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ceach ILEC would attempt to keep CLECs out, regardless of the actions of the other ILECs.\xe2\x80\x9d 313 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 184; cf. Kramer v. Pollock-Krasner Foundation, 890 F. Supp. 250, 256 (SDNY 1995) (while the plaintiff \xe2\x80\x9cmay believe the defendants conspired\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xe2\x80\x82, the defendants\xe2\x80\x99 allegedly conspiratorial actions could equally have been prompted by lawful, independent goals which do not constitute a conspiracy\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 12]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 second conspiracy theory rests on the competitive reticence among the ILECs themselves in the wake of the 1996 Act, which was supposedly passed in the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98hop[e] that the large incumbent local monopoly companies \xe2\x80\xa6 might attack their neighbors\xe2\x80\x99 service areas, as they are the best situated to do so.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Complaint \xc2\xb638, App. 20 (quoting Consumer Federation of America, Lessons from 1996 Telecommunications Act: Deregulation Before Meaningful Competition Spells Consumer Disaster, p.\xe2\x80\x8212 (Feb. 2000). Contrary to hope, the ILECs declined \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98to enter each other\xe2\x80\x99s service territories in any significant way,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Complaint \xc2\xb638, App. 20, and the local telephone and high speed Internet market remains highly compartmentalized geographically, with minimal competition. Based on this state of affairs, and perceiving the ILECs to be blessed with \xe2\x80\x9cespecially attractive business opportunities\xe2\x80\x9d in surrounding markets dominated by other ILECs, the plaintiffs assert that the ILECs\xe2\x80\x99 parallel conduct was \xe2\x80\x9cstrongly suggestive of conspiracy.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., \xc2\xb640, App. 21.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But it was not suggestive of conspiracy, not if history teaches anything. In a traditionally unregulated industry with low barriers to entry, sparse competition among large firms dominating separate geographical segments of the market could very well signify illegal agreement, but here we have an obvious alternative explanation. In the decade preceding the 1996 Act and well before that, monopoly was the norm in telecommunications, not the exception. See Verizon Communications Inc. v. FCC, 535 U. S. 467, 477\xe2\x80\x93478 (2002) (describing telephone service providers as traditional public monopolies). The ILECs were born in that world, doubtless liked the world the way it was, and surely knew the adage about him who lives by the sword. Hence, a natural explanation for the noncompetition alleged is that the former Government-sanctioned monopolists were sitting tight, expecting their neighbors to do the same thing.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In fact, the complaint itself gives reasons to believe that the ILECs would see their best interests in keeping to their old turf. Although the complaint says generally that the ILECs passed up \xe2\x80\x9cespecially attractive business opportunit[ies]\xe2\x80\x9d by declining to compete as CLECs against other ILECs, Complaint \xc2\xb640, App. 21, it does not allege that competition as CLECs was potentially any more lucrative than other opportunities being pursued by the ILECs during the same period,[Footnote 13] and the complaint is replete with indications that any CLEC faced nearly insurmountable barriers to profitability owing to the ILECs\xe2\x80\x99 flagrant resistance to the network sharing requirements of the 1996 Act, id., \xc2\xb647; App. 23\xe2\x80\x9326. Not only that, but even without a monopolistic tradition and the peculiar difficulty of mandating shared networks, \xe2\x80\x9c[f]irms do not expand without limit and none of them enters every market that an outside observer might regard as profitable, or even a small portion of such markets.\xe2\x80\x9d Areeda & Hovenkamp \xc2\xb6307d, at 155 (Supp. 2006) (commenting on the case at bar). The upshot is that Congress may have expected some ILECs to become CLECs in the legacy territories of other ILECs, but the disappointment does not make conspiracy plausible. We agree with the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s assessment that antitrust conspiracy was not suggested by the facts adduced under either theory of the complaint, which thus fails to state a valid \xc2\xa71 claim.[Footnote 14]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Plaintiffs say that our analysis runs counter to Swierkiewicz v. Sorema N.\xc2\xa0A., 534 U. S. 506, 508 (2002), which held that \xe2\x80\x9ca complaint in an employment discrimination lawsuit [need] not contain specific facts establishing a prima facie case of discrimination under the framework set forth in McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green, 411 U. S. 792 (1973).\xe2\x80\x9d They argue that just as the prima facie case is a \xe2\x80\x9cflexible evidentiary standard\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cshould not be transposed into a rigid pleading standard for discrimination cases,\xe2\x80\x9d Swierkiewicz, supra, at 512, \xe2\x80\x9ctranspos[ing] \xe2\x80\x98plus factor\xe2\x80\x99 summary judgment analysis woodenly into a rigid Rule 12(b)(6) pleading standard \xe2\x80\xa6 would be unwise,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 39. As the District Court correctly understood, however, \xe2\x80\x9cSwierkiewicz did not change the law of pleading, but simply re-emphasized \xe2\x80\xa6 that the Second Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s use of a heightened pleading standard for Title VII cases was contrary to the Federal Rules\xe2\x80\x99 structure of liberal pleading requirements.\xe2\x80\x9d 313 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 181 (citation and footnote omitted). Even though Swierkiewicz\xe2\x80\x99s pleadings \xe2\x80\x9cdetailed the events leading to his termination, provided relevant dates, and included the ages and nationalities of at least some of the relevant persons involved with his termination,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court of Appeals dismissed his complaint for failing to allege certain additional facts that Swierkiewicz would need at the trial stage to support his claim in the absence of direct evidence of discrimination. Swierkiewicz, 534 U. S., at 514. We reversed on the ground that the Court of Appeals had impermissibly applied what amounted to a heightened pleading requirement by insisting that Swierkiewicz allege \xe2\x80\x9cspecific facts\xe2\x80\x9d beyond those necessary to state his claim and the grounds showing entitlement to relief. Id., at 508.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Here, in contrast, we do not require heightened fact pleading of specifics, but only enough facts to state a claim to relief that is plausible on its face. Because the plaintiffs here have not nudged their claims across the line from conceivable to plausible, their complaint must be dismissed.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the first paragraph of its 24-page opinion the Court states that the question to be decided is whether allegations that \xe2\x80\x9cmajor telecommunications providers engaged in certain parallel conduct unfavorable to competition\xe2\x80\x9d suffice to state a violation of \xc2\xa71 of the Sherman Act. Ante, at 1. The answer to that question has been settled for more than 50 years. If that were indeed the issue, a summary reversal citing Theatre Enterprises, Inc. v. Paramount Film Distributing Corp., 346 U. S. 537 (1954), would adequately resolve this case. As Theatre Enterprises held, parallel conduct is circumstantial evidence admissible on the issue of conspiracy, but it is not itself illegal. Id., at 540\xe2\x80\x93542.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thus, this is a case in which there is no dispute about the substantive law. If the defendants acted independently, their conduct was perfectly lawful. If, however, that conduct is the product of a horizontal agreement among potential competitors, it was unlawful. Plaintiffs have alleged such an agreement and, because the complaint was dismissed in advance of answer, the allegation has not even been denied. Why, then, does the case not proceed? Does a judicial opinion that the charge is not \xe2\x80\x9cplausible\xe2\x80\x9d provide a legally acceptable reason for dismissing the complaint? I think not.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 amended complaint describes a variety of circumstantial evidence and makes the straightforward allegation that petitioners

\n

\xe2\x80\x9centered into a contract, combination or conspiracy to prevent competitive entry in their respective local telephone and/or high speed internet services markets and have agreed not to compete with one another and otherwise allocated customers and markets to one another.\xe2\x80\x9d Amended Complaint in No. 02 CIV. 10220 (GEL) (SDNY) \xc2\xb651, App. 27 (hereinafter Complaint).

\n

\nThe complaint explains that, contrary to Congress\xe2\x80\x99 expectation when it enacted the 1996 Telecommunications Act, and consistent with their own economic self-interests, petitioner Incumbent Local Exchange Carriers (ILECs) have assiduously avoided infringing upon each other\xe2\x80\x99s markets and have refused to permit nonincumbent competitors to access their networks. The complaint quotes Richard Notebaert, the former CEO of one such ILEC, as saying that competing in a neighboring ILEC\xe2\x80\x99s territory \xe2\x80\x9cmight be a good way to turn a quick dollar but that doesn\xe2\x80\x99t make it right.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., \xc2\xb642, App. 22. Moreover, respondents allege that petitioners \xe2\x80\x9ccommunicate amongst themselves\xe2\x80\x9d through numerous industry associations. Id., \xc2\xb646, App. 23. In sum, respondents allege that petitioners entered into an agreement that has long been recognized as a classic per se violation of the Sherman Act. See Report of the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s National Committee to Study the Antitrust Laws 26 (1955).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under rules of procedure that have been well settled since well before our decision in Theatre Enterprises, a judge ruling on a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s motion to dismiss a complaint, \xe2\x80\x9cmust accept as true all of the factual allegations contained in the complaint.\xe2\x80\x9d Swierkiewicz v. Sorema N.\xc2\xa0A., 534 U. S. 506, 508, n.\xc2\xa01 (2002); see Overstreet v. North Shore Corp., 318 U. S. 125, 127 (1943). But instead of requiring knowledgeable executives such as Notebaert to respond to these allegations by way of sworn depositions or other limited discovery\xe2\x80\x94and indeed without so much as requiring petitioners to file an answer denying that they entered into any agreement\xe2\x80\x94the majority permits immediate dismissal based on the assurances of company lawyers that nothing untoward was afoot. The Court embraces the argument of those lawyers that \xe2\x80\x9cthere is no reason to infer that the companies had agreed among themselves to do what was only natural anyway,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 19; that \xe2\x80\x9cthere was just no need for joint encouragement to resist the 1996 Act,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 20; and that the \xe2\x80\x9cnatural explanation for the noncompetition alleged is that the former Government-sanctioned monopolists were sitting tight, expecting their neighbors to do the same thing,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 21.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court and petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 legal team are no doubt correct that the parallel conduct alleged is consistent with the absence of any contract, combination, or conspiracy. But that conduct is also entirely consistent with the presence of the illegal agreement alleged in the complaint. And the charge that petitioners \xe2\x80\x9cagreed not to compete with one another\xe2\x80\x9d is not just one of \xe2\x80\x9ca few stray statements,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 18; it is an allegation describing unlawful conduct. As such, the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, our longstanding precedent, and sound practice mandate that the District Court at least require some sort of response from petitioners before dismissing the case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Two practical concerns presumably explain the Court\xe2\x80\x99s dramatic departure from settled procedural law. Private antitrust litigation can be enormously expensive, and there is a risk that jurors may mistakenly conclude that evidence of parallel conduct has proved that the parties acted pursuant to an agreement when they in fact merely made similar independent decisions. Those concerns merit careful case management, including strict control of discovery, careful scrutiny of evidence at the summary judgment stage, and lucid instructions to juries; they do not, however, justify the dismissal of an adequately pleaded complaint without even requiring the defendants to file answers denying a charge that they in fact engaged in collective decisionmaking. More importantly, they do not justify an interpretation of Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 12(b)(6) that seems to be driven by the majority\xe2\x80\x99s appraisal of the plausibility of the ultimate factual allegation rather than its legal sufficiency.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Rule 8(a)(2) of the Federal Rules requires that a complaint contain \xe2\x80\x9ca short and plain statement of the claim showing that the pleader is entitled to relief.\xe2\x80\x9d The rule did not come about by happenstance and its language is not inadvertent. The English experience with Byzantine special pleading rules\xe2\x80\x94illustrated by the hypertechnical Hilary rules of 1834[Footnote 1]\xe2\x80\x94made obvious the appeal of a pleading standard that was easy for the common litigant to understand and sufficed to put the defendant on notice as to the nature of the claim against him and the relief sought. Stateside, David Dudley Field developed the highly influential New York Code of 1848, which required \xe2\x80\x9c[a] statement of the facts constituting the cause of action, in ordinary and concise language, without repetition, and in such a manner as to enable a person of common understanding to know what is intended.\xe2\x80\x9d An Act to Simplify and Abridge the Practice, Pleadings and Proceedings of the Courts of this State, ch. 379, \xc2\xa7120(2), 1848 N.\xc2\xa0Y. Laws pp. 497, 521. Substantially similar language appeared in the Federal Equity Rules adopted in 1912. See Fed. Equity Rule 25 (requiring \xe2\x80\x9ca short and simple statement of the ultimate facts upon which the plaintiff asks relief, omitting any mere statement of evidence\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A difficulty arose, however, in that the Field Code and its progeny required a plaintiff to plead \xe2\x80\x9cfacts\xe2\x80\x9d rather than \xe2\x80\x9cconclusions,\xe2\x80\x9d a distinction that proved far easier to say than to apply. As commentators have noted,

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cit is virtually impossible logically to distinguish among \xe2\x80\x98ultimate facts,\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98evidence,\xe2\x80\x99 and \xe2\x80\x98conclusions.\xe2\x80\x99 Essentially any allegation in a pleading must be an assertion that certain occurrences took place. The pleading spectrum, passing from evidence through ultimate facts to conclusions, is largely a continuum varying only in the degree of particularity with which the occurrences are described.\xe2\x80\x9d Weinstein & Distler, Comments on Procedural Reform: Drafting Pleading Rules, 57 Colum. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 518, 520\xe2\x80\x93521 (1957).

\n

\nSee also Cook, Statements of Fact in Pleading Under the Codes, 21 Colum. L. Rev. 416, 417 (1921) (hereinafter Cook) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]here is no logical distinction between statements which are grouped by the courts under the phrases \xe2\x80\x98statements of fact\xe2\x80\x99 and \xe2\x80\x98conclusions of law\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). Rule 8 was directly responsive to this difficulty. Its drafters intentionally avoided any reference to \xe2\x80\x9cfacts\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cevidence\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cconclusions.\xe2\x80\x9d See 5 C. Wright & A. \n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under the relaxed pleading standards of the Federal Rules, the idea was not to keep litigants out of court but rather to keep them in. The merits of a claim would be sorted out during a flexible pretrial process and, as appropriate, through the crucible of trial. See Swierkiewicz, 534 U. S., at 514 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe liberal notice pleading of Rule 8(a) is the starting point of a simplified pleading system, which was adopted to focus litigation on the merits of a claim\xe2\x80\x9d). Charles E. Clark, the \xe2\x80\x9cprincipal draftsman\xe2\x80\x9d of the Federal Rules,[Footnote 2] put it thus:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cExperience has shown \xe2\x80\xa6 that we cannot expect the proof of the case to be made through the pleadings, and that such proof is really not their function. We can expect a general statement distinguishing the case from all others, so that the manner and form of trial and remedy expected are clear, and so that a permanent judgment will result.\xe2\x80\x9d The New Federal Rules of Civil Procedure: The Last Phase\xe2\x80\x94Underlying Philosophy Embodied in Some of the Basic Provisions of the New Procedure, 23 A.\xc2\xa0B.\xc2\xa0A. J. 976, 977 (1937) (hereinafter Clark, New Federal Rules).

\n

\nThe pleading paradigm under the new Federal Rules was well illustrated by the inclusion in the appendix of Form 9, a complaint for negligence. As relevant, the Form 9 complaint states only: \xe2\x80\x9cOn June 1, 1936, in a public highway called Boylston Street in Boston, Massachusetts, defendant negligently drove a motor vehicle against plaintiff who was then crossing said highway.\xe2\x80\x9d Form 9, Complaint for Negligence, Forms App., Fed. Rules Civ. Proc., 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. App., p. 829 (hereinafter Form 9). The complaint then describes the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s injuries and demands judgment. The asserted ground for relief\xe2\x80\x94namely, the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s negligent driving\xe2\x80\x94would have been called a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98conclusion of law\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d under the code pleading of old. See, e.g., Cook 419. But that bare allegation suffices under a system that \xe2\x80\x9crestrict[s] the pleadings to the task of general notice-giving and invest[s] the deposition-discovery process with a vital role in the preparation for trial.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 3] Hickman v. Taylor, 329 U. S. 495, 501 (1947); see also Swierkiewicz, 534 U. S., at 513, n.\xc2\xa04 (citing Form 9 as an example of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the simplicity and brevity of statement which the rules contemplate\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d); Thomson v. Washington, 362 F.\xc2\xa03d 969, 970 (CA7 2004) (Posner, J.) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe federal rules replaced fact pleading with notice pleading\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is in the context of this history that Conley v. Gibson, 355 U. S. 41 (1957), must be understood. The Conley plaintiffs were black railroad workers who alleged that their union local had refused to protect them against discriminatory discharges, in violation of the National Railway Labor Act. The union sought to dismiss the complaint on the ground that its general allegations of discriminatory treatment by the defendants lacked sufficient specificity. Writing for a unanimous Court, Justice Black rejected the union\xe2\x80\x99s claim as foreclosed by the language of Rule 8. Id., at 47\xe2\x80\x9348. In the course of doing so, he articulated the formulation the Court rejects today: \xe2\x80\x9cIn appraising the sufficiency of the complaint we follow, of course, the accepted rule that a complaint should not be dismissed for failure to state a claim unless it appears beyond doubt that the plaintiff can prove no set of facts in support of his claim which would entitle him to relief.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 45\xe2\x80\x9346.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Consistent with the design of the Federal Rules, Conley\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cno set of facts\xe2\x80\x9d formulation permits outright dismissal only when proceeding to discovery or beyond would be futile. Once it is clear that a plaintiff has stated a claim that, if true, would entitle him to relief, matters of proof are appropriately relegated to other stages of the trial process. Today, however, in its explanation of a decision to dismiss a complaint that it regards as a fishing expedition, the Court scraps Conley\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cno set of facts\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d language. Concluding that the phrase has been \xe2\x80\x9cquestioned, criticized, and explained away long enough,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 16, the Court dismisses it as careless composition.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If Conley\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cno set of facts\xe2\x80\x9d language is to be interred, let it not be without a eulogy. That exact language, which the majority says has \xe2\x80\x9cpuzzl[ed] the profession for 50 years,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., has been cited as authority in a dozen opinions of this Court and four separate writings.[Footnote 4] In not one of those 16 opinions was the language \xe2\x80\x9cquestioned,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ccriticized,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cexplained away.\xe2\x80\x9d Indeed, today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion is the first by any Member of this Court to express any doubt as to the adequacy of the Conley formulation. Taking their cues from the federal courts, 26 States and the District of Columbia utilize as their standard for dismissal of a complaint the very language the majority repudiates: whether it appears \xe2\x80\x9cbeyond doubt\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cno set of facts\xe2\x80\x9d in support of the claim would entitle the plaintiff to relief.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners have not requested that the Conley formulation be retired, nor have any of the six amici who filed briefs in support of petitioners. I would not rewrite the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s civil procedure textbooks and call into doubt the pleading rules of most of its States without far more informed deliberation as to the costs of doing so. Congress has established a process\xe2\x80\x94a rulemaking process\xe2\x80\x94for revisions of that order. See 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72072\xe2\x80\x932074 (2000 ed. and Supp. IV).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Today\xe2\x80\x99s majority calls Conley\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98no set of facts\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d language \xe2\x80\x9can incomplete, negative gloss on an accepted pleading standard: once a claim has been stated adequately, it may be supported by showing any set of facts consistent with the allegations in the complaint.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16. This is not and cannot be what the Conley Court meant. First, as I have explained, and as the Conley Court well knew, the pleading standard the Federal Rules meant to codify does not require, or even invite, the pleading of facts.[Footnote 6] The \xe2\x80\x9cpleading standard\xe2\x80\x9d label the majority gives to what it reads into the Conley opinion\xe2\x80\x94a statement of the permissible factual support for an adequately pleaded complaint\xe2\x80\x94would not, therefore, have impressed the Conley Court itself. Rather, that Court would have understood the majority\xe2\x80\x99s remodeling of its language to express an evidentiary standard, which the Conley Court had neither need nor want to explicate. Second, it is pellucidly clear that the Conley Court was interested in what a complaint must contain, not what it may contain. In fact, the Court said without qualification that it was \xe2\x80\x9cappraising the sufficiency of the complaint.\xe2\x80\x9d 355 U. S., at 45 (emphasis added). It was, to paraphrase today\xe2\x80\x99s majority, describing \xe2\x80\x9cthe minimum standard of adequate pleading to govern a complaint\xe2\x80\x99s survival,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 16\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We can be triply sure as to Conley\xe2\x80\x99s meaning by examining the three Court of Appeals cases the Conley Court cited as support for the \xe2\x80\x9caccepted rule\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9ca complaint should not be dismissed for failure to state a claim unless it appears beyond doubt that the plaintiff can prove no set of facts in support of his claim which would entitle him to relief.\xe2\x80\x9d 355 U. S., at 45\xe2\x80\x9346. In the first case, Leimer v. State Mut. Life Assur. Co. of Worcester, Mass., 108 F.\xc2\xa02d 302 (CA8 1940), the plaintiff alleged that she was the beneficiary of a life insurance plan and that the insurance company was wrongfully withholding proceeds from her. In reversing the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s grant of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s motion to dismiss, the Eighth Circuit noted that court\xe2\x80\x99s own longstanding rule that, to warrant dismissal, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98it should appear from the allegations that a cause of action does not exist, rather than that a cause of action has been defectively stated.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 305 (quoting Winget v. Rockwood, 69 F.\xc2\xa02d 326, 329 (CA8 1934)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Leimer court viewed the Federal Rules\xe2\x80\x94specifically Rules 8(a)(2), 12(b)(6), 12(e) (motion for a more definite statement), and 56 (motion for summary judgment)\xe2\x80\x94as reinforcing the notion that \xe2\x80\x9cthere is no justification for dismissing a complaint for insufficiency of statement, except where it appears to a certainty that the plaintiff would be entitled to no relief under any state of facts which could be proved in support of the claim.\xe2\x80\x9d 108 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 306. The court refuted in the strongest terms any suggestion that the unlikelihood of recovery should determine the fate of a complaint: \xe2\x80\x9cNo matter how improbable it may be that she can prove her claim, she is entitled to an opportunity to make the attempt, and is not required to accept as final a determination of her rights based upon inferences drawn in favor of the defendant from her amended complaint.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Third Circuit relied on Leimer\xe2\x80\x99s admonition in Continental Collieries, Inc. v. Shober, 130 F.\xc2\xa02d 631 (1942), which the Conley Court also cited in support of its \xe2\x80\x9cno set of facts\xe2\x80\x9d formulation. In a diversity action the plaintiff alleged breach of contract, but the District Court dismissed the complaint on the ground that the contract appeared to be unenforceable under state law. The Court of Appeals reversed, concluding that there were facts in dispute that went to the enforceability of the contract, and that the rule at the pleading stage was as in Leimer: \xe2\x80\x9cNo matter how likely it may seem that the pleader will be unable to prove his case, he is entitled, upon averring a claim, to an opportunity to try to prove it.\xe2\x80\x9d 130 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 635.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The third case the Conley Court cited approvingly was written by Judge Clark himself. In Dioguardi v. Durning, 139 F.\xc2\xa02d 774 (CA2 1944), the pro se plaintiff, an importer of \xe2\x80\x9ctonics,\xe2\x80\x9d charged the customs inspector with auctioning off the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s former merchandise for less than was bid for it\xe2\x80\x94and indeed for an amount equal to the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s own bid\xe2\x80\x94and complained that two cases of tonics went missing three weeks before the sale. The inference, hinted at by the averments but never stated in so many words, was that the defendant fraudulently denied the plaintiff his rightful claim to the tonics, which, if true, would have violated federal law. Writing six years after the adoption of the Federal Rules he held the lead rein in drafting, Judge Clark said that the defendant

\n

\xe2\x80\x9ccould have disclosed the facts from his point of view, in advance of a trial if he chose, by asking for a pre-trial hearing or by moving for a summary judgment with supporting affidavits. But, as it stands, we do not see how the plaintiff may properly be deprived of his day in court to show what he obviously so firmly believes and what for present purposes defendant must be taken as admitting.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 775.

\n

\nAs any civil procedure student knows, Judge Clark\xe2\x80\x99s opinion disquieted the defense bar and gave rise to a movement to revise Rule 8 to require a plaintiff to plead a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98cause of action.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d See 5 Wright & Miller \xc2\xa71201, at 86\xe2\x80\x9387. The movement failed, see ibid.; Dioguardi was explicitly approved in Conley; and \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n retrospect the case itself seems to be a routine application of principles that are universally accepted,\xe2\x80\x9d 5 Wright & Miller \xc2\xa71220, at 284\xe2\x80\x93285.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In light of Leimer, Continental Collieries, and Dioguardi, Conley\xe2\x80\x99s statement that a complaint is not to be dismissed unless \xe2\x80\x9cno set of facts\xe2\x80\x9d in support thereof would entitle the plaintiff to relief is hardly \xe2\x80\x9cpuzzling,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 16. It reflects a philosophy that, unlike in the days of code pleading, separating the wheat from the chaff is a task assigned to the pretrial and trial process. Conley\xe2\x80\x99s language, in short, captures the policy choice embodied in the Federal Rules and binding on the federal courts.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have consistently reaffirmed that basic understanding of the Federal Rules in the half century since Conley. For example, in Scheuer v. Rhodes, 416 U. S. 232 (1974), we reversed the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 dismissal on the pleadings when the respondents, the Governor and other officials of the State of Ohio, argued that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims were barred by sovereign immunity. In a unanimous opinion by then-Justice Rehnquist, we emphasized that

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen a federal court reviews the sufficiency of a complaint, before the reception of any evidence either by affidavit or admissions, its task is necessarily a limited one. The issue is not whether a plaintiff will ultimately prevail but whether the claimant is entitled to offer evidence to support the claims. Indeed it may appear on the face of the pleadings that a recovery is very remote and unlikely but that is not the test.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 236 (emphasis added).

\n

\nThe Rhodes plaintiffs had \xe2\x80\x9calleged generally and in conclusory terms\xe2\x80\x9d that the defendants, by calling out the National Guard to suppress the Kent State University student protests, \xe2\x80\x9cwere guilty of wanton, wilful and negligent conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d Krause v. Rhodes, 471 F.\xc2\xa02d 430, 433 (CA6 1972). We reversed the Court of Appeals on the ground that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hatever the plaintiffs may or may not be able to establish as to the merits of their allegations, their claims, as stated in the complaints, given the favorable reading required by the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure,\xe2\x80\x9d were not barred by the Eleventh Amendment because they were styled as suits against the defendants in their individual capacities. 416 U. S., at 238.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We again spoke with one voice against efforts to expand pleading requirements beyond their appointed limits in Leatherman v. Tarrant County Narcotics Intelligence and Coordination Unit, 507 U. S. 163 (1993). Writing for the unanimous Court, Chief Justice Rehnquist rebuffed the Fifth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s effort to craft a standard for pleading municipal liability that accounted for \xe2\x80\x9cthe enormous expense involved today in litigation,\xe2\x80\x9d Leatherman v. Tarrant Cty. Narcotics Intelligence and Coordination Unit, 954 F.\xc2\xa02d 1054, 1057 (1992) (internal quotation marks omitted), by requiring a plaintiff to \xe2\x80\x9cstate with factual detail and particularity the basis for the claim which necessarily includes why the defendant-official cannot successfully maintain the defense of immunity.\xe2\x80\x9d Leatherman, 507 U. S., at 167 (internal quotation marks omitted). We found this language inconsistent with Rules 8(a)(2) and 9(b) and emphasized that motions to dismiss were not the place to combat discovery abuse: \xe2\x80\x9cIn the absence of [an amendment to Rule 9(b)], federal courts and litigants must rely on summary judgment and control of discovery to weed out unmeritorious claims sooner rather than later.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 168\xe2\x80\x93169.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Most recently, in Swierkiewicz, 534 U. S. 506, we were faced with a case more similar to the present one than the majority will allow. In discrimination cases, our precedents require a plaintiff at the summary judgment stage to produce either direct evidence of discrimination or, if the claim is based primarily on circumstantial evidence, to meet the shifting evidentiary burdens imposed under the framework articulated in McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green, 411 U. S. 792 (1973). See, e.g., Trans World Airlines, Inc. v. Thurston, 469 U. S. 111, 121 (1985). Swierkiewicz alleged that he had been terminated on account of national origin in violation of Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964. The Second Circuit dismissed the suit on the pleadings because he had not pleaded a prima facie case of discrimination under the McDonnell Douglas standard.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We reversed in another unanimous opinion, holding that \xe2\x80\x9cunder a notice pleading system, it is not appropriate to require a plaintiff to plead facts establishing a prima facie case because the McDonnell Douglas framework does not apply in every employment discrimination case.\xe2\x80\x9d Swierkiewicz, 534 U. S., at 511. We also observed that Rule 8(a)(2) does not contemplate a court\xe2\x80\x99s passing on the merits of a litigant\xe2\x80\x99s claim at the pleading stage. Rather, the \xe2\x80\x9csimplified notice pleading standard\xe2\x80\x9d of the Federal Rules \xe2\x80\x9crelies on liberal discovery rules and summary judgment motions to define disputed facts and issues and to dispose of unmeritorious claims.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 512; see Brief for United States et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae in Swierkiewicz v. Sorema N.\xc2\xa0A., O. T. 2001, No. 00\xe2\x80\x931853, p. 10 (stating that a Rule 12(b)(6) motion is not \xe2\x80\x9can appropriate device for testing the truth of what is asserted or for determining whether a plaintiff has any evidence to back up what is in the complaint\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).[Footnote 7]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As in the discrimination context, we have developed an evidentiary framework for evaluating claims under \xc2\xa71 of the Sherman Act when those claims rest on entirely circumstantial evidence of conspiracy. See Matsushita Elec. Industrial Co. v. Zenith Radio Corp., 475 U. S. 574 (1986). Under Matsushita, a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s allegations of an illegal conspiracy may not, at the summary judgment stage, rest solely on the inferences that may be drawn from the parallel conduct of the defendants. In order to survive a Rule 56 motion, a \xc2\xa71 plaintiff \xe2\x80\x9cmust present evidence \xe2\x80\x98that tends to exclude the possibility\xe2\x80\x99 that the alleged conspirators acted independently.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 588 (quoting Monsanto Co. v. Spray-Rite Service Corp., 465 U. S. 752, 764 (1984)). That is, the plaintiff \xe2\x80\x9cmust show that the inference of conspiracy is reasonable in light of the competing inferences of independent action or collusive action.\xe2\x80\x9d 475 U. S., at 588.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Everything today\xe2\x80\x99s majority says would therefore make perfect sense if it were ruling on a Rule 56 motion for summary judgment and the evidence included nothing more than the Court has described. But it should go without saying in the wake of Swierkiewicz that a heightened production burden at the summary judgment stage does not translate into a heightened pleading burden at the complaint stage. The majority rejects the complaint in this case because\xe2\x80\x94in light of the fact that the parallel conduct alleged is consistent with ordinary market behavior\xe2\x80\x94the claimed conspiracy is \xe2\x80\x9cconceivable\xe2\x80\x9d but not \xe2\x80\x9cplausible,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 24. I have my doubts about the majority\xe2\x80\x99s assessment of the plausibility of this alleged conspiracy. See Part III, infra. But even if the majority\xe2\x80\x99s speculation is correct, its \xe2\x80\x9cplausibility\xe2\x80\x9d standard is irreconcilable with Rule 8 and with our governing precedents. As we made clear in Swierkiewicz and Leatherman, fear of the burdens of litigation does not justify factual conclusions supported only by lawyers\xe2\x80\x99 arguments rather than sworn denials or admissible evidence.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case is a poor vehicle for the Court\xe2\x80\x99s new pleading rule, for we have observed that \xe2\x80\x9cin antitrust cases, where \xe2\x80\x98the proof is largely in the hands of the alleged conspirators,\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\xa6 dismissals prior to giving the plaintiff ample opportunity for discovery should be granted very sparingly.\xe2\x80\x9d Hospital Building Co. v. Trustees of Rex Hospital, 425 U. S. 738, 746 (1976) (quoting Poller v. Columbia Broadcasting System, Inc., 368 U. S. 464, 473 (1962)); see also Knuth v. Erie-Crawford Dairy Cooperative Assn., 395 F.\xc2\xa02d 420, 423 (CA3 1968) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe \xe2\x80\x98liberal\xe2\x80\x99 approach to the consideration of antitrust complaints is important because inherent in such an action is the fact that all the details and specific facts relied upon cannot properly be set forth as part of the pleadings\xe2\x80\x9d). Moreover, the fact that the Sherman Act authorizes the recovery of treble damages and attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees for successful plaintiffs indicates that Congress intended to encourage, rather than discourage, private enforcement of the law. See Radovich v. National Football League, 352 U. S. 445, 454 (1957) (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress itself has placed the private antitrust litigant in a most favorable position \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. In the face of such a policy this Court should not add requirements to burden the private litigant beyond what is specifically set forth by Congress in those laws\xe2\x80\x9d). It is therefore more, not less, important in antitrust cases to resist the urge to engage in armchair economics at the pleading stage.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The same year we decided Conley, Judge Clark wrote, presciently,

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cI fear that every age must learn its lesson that special pleading cannot be made to do the service of trial and that live issues between active litigants are not to be disposed of or evaded on the paper pleadings, i.e., the formalistic claims of the parties. Experience has found no quick and easy short cut for trials in cases generally and antitrust cases in particular.\xe2\x80\x9d Special Pleading in the \xe2\x80\x9cBig Case\xe2\x80\x9d? in Procedure\xe2\x80\x94The Handmaid of Justice 147, 148 (C. Wright & H. Reasoner eds. 1965) (hereinafter Clark, Special Pleading in the Big Case) (emphasis added).

\n

\nIn this \xe2\x80\x9cBig Case,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court succumbs to the temptation that previous Courts have steadfastly resisted.[Footnote 8] While the majority assures us that it is not applying any \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98heightened\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d pleading standard, see ante, at 23, n.\xc2\xa014, I shall now explain why I have a difficult time understanding its opinion any other way.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court does not suggest that an agreement to do what the plaintiffs allege would be permissible under the antitrust laws, see, e.g., Associated Gen. Contractors of Cal., Inc. v. Carpenters, 459 U. S. 519, 526\xe2\x80\x93527 (1983). Nor does the Court hold that these plaintiffs have failed to allege an injury entitling them to sue for damages under those laws, see Brunswick Corp. v. Pueblo Bowl-O-Mat, Inc., 429 U. S. 477, 489\xe2\x80\x93490 (1977). Rather, the theory on which the Court permits dismissal is that, so far as the Federal Rules are concerned, no agreement has been alleged at all. This is a mind-boggling conclusion.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the Court explains, prior to the enactment of the Telecommunications Act of 1996 the law prohibited the defendants from competing with each other. The new statute was enacted to replace a monopolistic market with a competitive one. The Act did not merely require the regional monopolists to take affirmative steps to facilitate entry to new competitors, see Verizon Communications Inc. v. Law Offices of Curtis V. Trinko, LLP, 540 U. S. 398, 402 (2004); it also permitted the existing firms to compete with each other and to expand their operations into previously forbidden territory. See 47 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7271. Each of the defendants decided not to take the latter step. That was obviously an extremely important business decision, and I am willing to presume that each company acted entirely independently in reaching that decision. I am even willing to entertain the majority\xe2\x80\x99s belief that any agreement among the companies was unlikely. But the plaintiffs allege in three places in their complaint, \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb6 4, 51, 64, App. 11, 27, 30, that the ILECs did in fact agree both to prevent competitors from entering into their local markets and to forgo competition with each other. And as the Court recognizes, at the motion to dismiss stage, a judge assumes \xe2\x80\x9cthat all the allegations in the complaint are true (even if doubtful in fact).\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority circumvents this obvious obstacle to dismissal by pretending that it does not exist. The Court admits that \xe2\x80\x9cin form a few stray statements in the complaint speak directly of agreement,\xe2\x80\x9d but disregards those allegations by saying that \xe2\x80\x9con fair reading these are merely legal conclusions resting on the prior allegations\xe2\x80\x9d of parallel conduct. Ante, at 18. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s dichotomy between factual allegations and \xe2\x80\x9clegal conclusions\xe2\x80\x9d is the stuff of a bygone era, supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x937. That distinction was a defining feature of code pleading, see generally Clark, The Complaint in Code Pleading, 35 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J. 259 (1925\xe2\x80\x931926), but was conspicuously abolished when the Federal Rules were enacted in 1938. See United States v. Employing Plasterers Assn. of Chicago, 347 U. S. 186, 188 (1954) (holding, in an antitrust case, that the Government\xe2\x80\x99s allegations of effects on interstate commerce must be taken into account in deciding whether to dismiss the complaint \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hether these charges be called \xe2\x80\x98allegations of fact\xe2\x80\x99 or \xe2\x80\x98mere conclusions of the pleader\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d); Brownlee v. Conine, 957 F.\xc2\xa02d 353, 354 (CA7 1992) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Federal Rules of Civil Procedure establish a system of notice pleading rather than of fact pleading, \xe2\x80\xa6 so the happenstance that a complaint is \xe2\x80\x98conclusory,\xe2\x80\x99 whatever exactly that overused lawyers\xe2\x80\x99 clich\xc3\xa9 means, does not automatically condemn it\xe2\x80\x9d); Walker Distributing Co. v. Lucky Lager Brewing Co., 323 F.\xc2\xa02d 1, 3\xe2\x80\x934 (CA9 1963) (\xe2\x80\x9c[O]ne purpose of Rule 8 was to get away from the highly technical distinction between statements of fact and conclusions of law \xe2\x80\xa6\xe2\x80\x9d); Oil, Chemical & Atomic Workers Int\xe2\x80\x99l Union v. Indeed it is less of one.[Footnote 9]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even if I were inclined to accept the Court\xe2\x80\x99s anachronistic dichotomy and ignore the complaint\xe2\x80\x99s actual allegations, I would dispute the Court\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion that any inference of agreement from petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 parallel conduct is \xe2\x80\x9cimplausible.\xe2\x80\x9d Many years ago a truly great economist perceptively observed that \xe2\x80\x9c[p]eople of the same trade seldom meet together, even for merriment and diversion, but the conversation ends in a conspiracy against the public, or in some contrivance to raise prices.\xe2\x80\x9d A. Smith, An Inquiry Into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations, in 39 Great Books of the Western World 55 (R. Hutchins & M. Adler eds. 1952). I am not so cynical as to accept that sentiment at face value, but I need not do so here. Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 complaint points not only to petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 numerous opportunities to meet with each other, Complaint \xc2\xb646, App. 23,[Footnote 10] but also to Notebaert\xe2\x80\x99s curious statement that encroaching on a fellow incumbent\xe2\x80\x99s territory \xe2\x80\x9cmight be a good way to turn a quick dollar but that doesn\xe2\x80\x99t make it right,\xe2\x80\x9d id., \xc2\xb642, App. 22. What did he mean by that? One possible (indeed plausible) inference is that he meant that while it would be in his company\xe2\x80\x99s economic self-interest to compete with its brethren, he had agreed with his competitors not to do so. According to the complaint, that is how the Illinois Coalition for Competitive Telecom construed Notebaert\xe2\x80\x99s statement, id., \xc2\xb644, App. 22 (calling the statement \xe2\x80\x9cevidence of potential collusion among regional Bell phone monopolies to not compete against one another and kill off potential competitors in local phone service\xe2\x80\x9d), and that is how Members of Congress construed his company\xe2\x80\x99s behavior, id., \xc2\xb645, App. 23 (describing a letter to the Justice Department requesting an investigation into the possibility that the ILECs\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cvery apparent non-competition policy\xe2\x80\x9d was coordinated).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Perhaps Notebaert meant instead that competition would be sensible in the short term but not in the long run. That\xe2\x80\x99s what his lawyers tell us anyway. See Brief for Petitioners 36. But I would think that no one would know better what Notebaert meant than Notebaert himself. Instead of permitting respondents to ask Notebaert, however, the Court looks to other quotes from that and other articles and decides that what he meant was that entering new markets as a CLEC would not be a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98sustainable economic model.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 22, n.\xc2\xa013. Never mind that\xe2\x80\x94as anyone ever interviewed knows\xe2\x80\x94a newspaper article is hardly a verbatim transcript; the writer selects quotes to package his story, not to record a subject\xe2\x80\x99s views for posterity. But more importantly the District Court was required at this stage of the proceedings to construe Notebaert\xe2\x80\x99s ambiguous statement in the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 favor.[Footnote 11] See Allen v. Wright, 468 U. S. 737, 768, n.\xc2\xa01 (1984). The inference the statement supports\xe2\x80\x94that simultaneous decisions by ILECs not even to attempt to poach customers from one another once the law authorized them to do so were the product of an agreement\xe2\x80\x94sits comfortably within the realm of possibility. That is all the Rules require.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To be clear, if I had been the trial judge in this case, I would not have permitted the plaintiffs to engage in massive discovery based solely on the allegations in this complaint. On the other hand, I surely would not have dismissed the complaint without requiring the defendants to answer the charge that they \xe2\x80\x9chave agreed not to compete with one another and otherwise allocated customers and markets to one another.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 12] \xc2\xb651, App. 27. Even a sworn denial of that charge would not justify a summary dismissal without giving the plaintiffs the opportunity to take depositions from Notebaert and at least one responsible executive representing each of the other defendants.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents in this case proposed a plan of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98phased discovery\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d limited to the existence of the alleged conspiracy and class certification. Brief for Respondents 25\xe2\x80\x9326. Two petitioners rejected the plan. Ibid. Whether or not respondents\xe2\x80\x99 proposed plan was sensible, it was an appropriate subject for negotiation.[Footnote 13] Given the charge in the complaint\xe2\x80\x94buttressed by the common sense of Adam Smith\xe2\x80\x94I cannot say that the possibility that joint discussions and perhaps some agreements played a role in petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 decisionmaking process is so implausible that dismissing the complaint before any defendant has denied the charge is preferable to granting respondents even a minimal opportunity to prove their claims. See Clark, New Federal Rules 977 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]hrough the weapons of discovery and summary judgment we have developed new devices, with more appropriate penalties to aid in matters of proof, and do not need to force the pleadings to their less appropriate function\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I fear that the unfortunate result of the majority\xe2\x80\x99s new pleading rule will be to invite lawyers\xe2\x80\x99 debates over economic theory to conclusively resolve antitrust suits in the absence of any evidence. It is no surprise that the antitrust defense bar\xe2\x80\x94among whom \xe2\x80\x9clament\xe2\x80\x9d as to inadequate judicial supervision of discovery is most \xe2\x80\x9ccommon,\xe2\x80\x9d see ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x94should lobby for this state of affairs. But \xe2\x80\x9cwe must recall that their primary responsibility is to win cases for their clients, not to improve law administration for the public.\xe2\x80\x9d Clark, Special Pleading in the Big Case 152. As we did in our prior decisions, we should have instructed them that their remedy was to seek to amend the Federal Rules\xe2\x80\x94not our interpretation of them.[Footnote 14] See Swierkiewicz, 534 U. S., at 515; Crawford-El v. Britton, 523 U. S. 574, 595 (1998); Leatherman, 507 U. S., at 168.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Just a few weeks ago some of my colleagues explained that a strict interpretation of the literal text of statutory language is essential to avoid judicial decisions that are not faithful to the intent of Congress. Zuni Public School Dist. No. 89 v. Department of Education, 550 U. S. ___, ___ (2007) (Scalia, J., dissenting). I happen to believe that there are cases in which other tools of construction are more reliable than text, but I agree of course that congressional intent should guide us in matters of statutory interpretation. Id., at ___ (Stevens, J., concurring). This is a case in which the intentions of the drafters of three important sources of law\xe2\x80\x94the Sherman Act, the Telecommunications Act of 1996, and the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure\xe2\x80\x94all point unmistakably in the same direction, yet the Court marches resolutely the other way. Whether the Court\xe2\x80\x99s actions will benefit only defendants in antitrust treble-damages cases, or whether its test for the sufficiency of a complaint will inure to the benefit of all civil defendants, is a question that the future will answer. But that the Court has announced a significant new rule that does not even purport to respond to any congressional command is glaringly obvious.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The transparent policy concern that drives the decision is the interest in protecting antitrust defendants\xe2\x80\x94who in this case are some of the wealthiest corporations in our economy\xe2\x80\x94from the burdens of pretrial discovery. Ante, at 11\xe2\x80\x9313. Even if it were not apparent that the legal fees petitioners have incurred in arguing the merits of their Rule 12(b) motion have far exceeded the cost of limited discovery, or that those discovery costs would burden respondents as well as petitioners,[Footnote 15] that concern would not provide an adequate justification for this law-changing decision. For in the final analysis it is only a lack of confidence in the ability of trial judges to control discovery, buttressed by appellate judges\xe2\x80\x99 independent appraisal of the plausibility of profoundly serious factual allegations, that could account for this stark break from precedent.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If the allegation of conspiracy happens to be true, today\xe2\x80\x99s decision obstructs the congressional policy favoring competition that undergirds both the Telecommunications Act of 1996 and the Sherman Act itself. More importantly, even if there is abundant evidence that the allegation is untrue, directing that the case be dismissed without even looking at any of that evidence marks a fundamental\xe2\x80\x94and unjustified\xe2\x80\x94change in the character of pretrial practice.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, I respectfully dissent.

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In short, the Federal Rules contemplate that pretrial matters will be settled through a flexible process of give and take, of proffers, stipulations, and stonewalls, not by having trial judges screen allegations for their plausibility vel non without requiring an answer from the defendant. See Societe Internationale pour Participations Industrielles et Commerciales, S. A. v. Rogers, 357 U. S. 197, 206 (1958) (\xe2\x80\x9cRule 34 is sufficiently flexible to be adapted to the exigencies of particular litigation\xe2\x80\x9d). And should it become apparent over the course of litigation that a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s filings bespeak an in terrorem suit, the district court has at its call its own in terrorem device, in the form of a wide array of Rule 11 sanctions. See Rules 11(b), (c) (authorizing sanctions if a suit is presented \xe2\x80\x9cfor any improper purpose, such as to harass or to cause unnecessary delay or needless increase in the cost of litigation\xe2\x80\x9d); see Business Guides, Inc. v. Chromatic Communications Enterprises, Inc., 498 U. S. 533 (1991) (holding that Rule 11 applies to a represented party who signs a pleading, motion, or other papers, as well as to attorneys); Atkins v. Fischer, 232 F.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0D. 116, 126 (DC 2005) (\xe2\x80\x9cAs possible sanctions pursuant to Rule 11, the court has an arsenal of options at its disposal\xe2\x80\x9d).
