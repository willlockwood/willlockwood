SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

Syllabus

\n

MASLENJAK v. UNITED STATES

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

No. 16\xe2\x80\x93309.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 26, 2017\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 22, 2017

\n

Petitioner Divna Maslenjak is an ethnic Serb who resided in Bosnia during the 1990\xe2\x80\x99s, when a civil war divided the new country. In 1998, she and her family sought refugee status in the United States. Interviewed under oath, Maslenjak explained that the family feared persecution from both sides of the national rift: Muslims would mistreat them because of their ethnicity, and Serbs would abuse them because Maslenjak\xe2\x80\x99s husband had evaded service in the Bosnian Serb Army by absconding to Serbia. Persuaded of the Maslenjaks\xe2\x80\x99 plight, American officials granted them refugee status. Years later, Maslenjak applied for U.\xc2\xa0S. citizenship. In the application process, she swore that she had never given false information to a government official while applying for an immigration benefit or lied to an official to gain entry into the United States. She was naturalized as a U.\xc2\xa0S. citizen. But it soon emerged that her professions of honesty were false: Maslenjak had known all along that her husband spent the war years not secreted in Serbia, but serving as an officer in the Bosnian Serb Army.

\n

The Government charged Maslenjak with knowingly \xe2\x80\x9cprocur[ing], contrary to law, [her] naturalization,\xe2\x80\x9d in violation of\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71425(a). According to the Government\xe2\x80\x99s theory, Maslenjak violated \xc2\xa71425(a) because, in the course of procuring her naturalization, she broke another law:\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71015(a), which prohibits knowingly making a false statement under oath in a naturalization proceeding. The District Court instructed the jury that, to secure a conviction under \xc2\xa71425(a), the Government need not prove that Maslenjak\xe2\x80\x99s false statements were material to, or influenced, the decision to approve her citizenship application. The Sixth Circuit affirmed the conviction, holding that if Maslenjak made false statements violating \xc2\xa71015(a) and procured naturalization, then she also violated \xc2\xa71425(a).

\n

Held:

\n

1.\xc2\xa0The text of \xc2\xa71425(a) makes clear that, to secure a conviction, the Government must establish that the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s illegal act played a role in her acquisition of citizenship. To \xe2\x80\x9cprocure .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. naturalization\xe2\x80\x9d means to obtain it. And the adverbial phrase \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary to law\xe2\x80\x9d specifies how a person must procure naturalization so as to run afoul of the statute: illegally. Thus, someone \xe2\x80\x9cprocure[s], contrary to law, naturalization\xe2\x80\x9d when she obtains citizenship illegally. As ordinary usage demonstrates, the most natural understanding of that phrase is that the illegal act must have somehow contributed to the obtaining of citizenship. To get citizenship unlawfully is to get it through an unlawful means\xe2\x80\x94and that is just to say that an illegality played some role in its acquisition.

\n

The Government\xe2\x80\x99s contrary view\xe2\x80\x94that \xc2\xa71425(a) requires only a violation in the course of procuring naturalization\xe2\x80\x94falters on the way language naturally works. Suppose that an applicant for citizenship fills out the paperwork in a government office with a knife tucked away in her handbag. She has violated the law against possessing a weapon in a federal building, and she has done so in the course of procuring citizenship, but nobody would say she has \xe2\x80\x9cprocure[d]\xe2\x80\x9d her citizenship \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary to law.\xe2\x80\x9d That is because the violation of law and the acquisition of citizenship in that example are merely coincidental: The one has no causal relation to the other. Although the Government attempts to define such examples out of the statute, that effort falls short for multiple reasons. Most important, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s attempted carve-out does nothing to alter the linguistic understanding that gives force to the examples the Government would exclude. Under ordinary rules of language usage, \xc2\xa71425(a) demands a causal or means-end connection between a legal violation and naturalization.

\n

The broader statutory context reinforces the point, because the Government\xe2\x80\x99s reading would create a profound mismatch between the requirements for naturalization and those for denaturalization: Some legal violations that do not justify denying citizenship would nonetheless justify revoking it later. For example, lies told out of \xe2\x80\x9cembarrassment, fear, or a desire for privacy\xe2\x80\x9d (rather than \xe2\x80\x9cfor the purpose of obtaining [immigration] benefits\xe2\x80\x9d) are not generally disqualifying under the statutory requirement of \xe2\x80\x9cgood moral character.\xe2\x80\x9d Kungys v. United States,\n\n485 U.\xc2\xa0S. 759\n;\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71101(f)(6). But under the Government\xe2\x80\x99s reading of \xc2\xa71425(a), any lie told in the naturalization process would provide a basis for rescinding citizenship. The Government could thus take away on one day what it was required to give the day before. And by so unmooring the revocation of citizenship from its award, the Government opens the door to a world of disquieting consequences\xe2\x80\x94which this Court would need far stronger textual support to believe Congress intended. The statute Congress passed, most naturally read, strips a person of citizenship not when she committed any illegal act during the naturalization process, but only when that act played some role in her naturalization. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

2.\xc2\xa0When the underlying illegality alleged in a \xc2\xa71425(a) prosecution is a false statement to government officials, a jury must decide whether the false statement so altered the naturalization process as to have influenced an award of citizenship. Because the entire naturalization process is set up to provide little room for subjective preferences or personal whims, that inquiry is properly framed in objective terms: To decide whether a defendant acquired citizenship by means of a lie, a jury must evaluate how knowledge of the real facts would have affected a reasonable government official properly applying naturalization law.

\n

If the facts the defendant misrepresented are themselves legally disqualifying for citizenship, the jury can make quick work of that inquiry. In such a case, the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s lie must have played a role in her naturalization. But that is not the only time a jury can find that a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s lies had the requisite bearing on a naturalization decision, because lies can also throw investigators off a trail leading to disqualifying facts. When relying on such an investigation-based theory, the Government must make a two-part showing. Initially, the Government must prove that the misrepresented fact was sufficiently relevant to a naturalization criterion that it would have prompted reasonable officials, \xe2\x80\x9cseeking only evidence concerning citizenship qualifications,\xe2\x80\x9d to undertake further investigation. Kungys, 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 774, n.\xc2\xa09. If that much is true, the inquiry turns to the prospect that such an investigation would have borne disqualifying fruit. The Government need not show definitively that its investigation would have unearthed a disqualifying fact. It need only establish that the investigation \xe2\x80\x9cwould predictably have disclosed\xe2\x80\x9d some legal disqualification. Id., at 774. If that is so, the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s misrepresentation contributed to the citizenship award in the way \xc2\xa71425(a) requires. This demanding but still practicable causal standard reflects the real-world attributes of cases premised on what an unhindered investigation would have found.

\n

When the Government can make its two-part showing, the defendant may overcome it by establishing that she was qualified for citizenship (even though she misrepresented facts that suggested the opposite). Thus, whatever the Government shows with respect to a thwarted investigation, qualification for citizenship is a complete defense to a prosecution under \xc2\xa71425(a). Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

3. Measured against this analysis, the jury instructions in this case were in error. The jury needed to find more than an unlawful false statement. However, it was not asked to\xe2\x80\x94and so did not\xe2\x80\x94make any of the necessary determinations. The Government\xe2\x80\x99s assertion that any instructional error was harmless is left for resolution on remand. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

821 F.\xc2\xa03d 675, vacated and remanded.

DIVNA MASLENJAK, PETITIONER v.UNITED STATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

[June 22, 2017]

\n

\n

Justice Kagan delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

A federal statute,\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71425(a), makes it a crime to \xe2\x80\x9cknowingly procure[\xc2\xa0], contrary to law, the naturalization of any person.\xe2\x80\x9d And when someone is convicted under \xc2\xa71425(a) of unlawfully procuring her own naturalization, her citizenship is automatically revoked. See\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71451(e). In this case, we consider what the Government must prove to obtain such a conviction. We hold that the Government must establish that an illegal act by the defendant played some role in her acquisition of citizenship. When the illegal act is a false statement, that means demonstrating that the defendant lied about facts that would have mattered to an immigration official, because they would have justified denying naturalization or would predictably have led to other facts warranting that result.

\n

I

\n

Petitioner Divna Maslenjak is an ethnic Serb who re-sided in Bosnia during the 1990\xe2\x80\x99s, when a civil war between Serbs and Muslims divided the new country. In 1998, she and her family (her husband Ratko Maslenjak and their two children) met with an American immigration official to seek refugee status in the United States. Interviewed under oath, Maslenjak explained that the family feared persecution in Bosnia from both sides of the national rift. Muslims, she said, would mistreat them because of their ethnicity. And Serbs, she testified, would abuse them because her husband had evaded service in the Bosnian Serb Army by absconding to Serbia\xe2\x80\x94where he remained hidden, apart from the family, for some five years. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 58a\xe2\x80\x9360a. Persuaded of the Maslenjaks\xe2\x80\x99 plight, American officials granted them refugee status, and they immigrated to the United States in 2000.

\n

Six years later, Maslenjak applied for naturalization. Question 23 on the application form asked whether she had ever given \xe2\x80\x9cfalse or misleading information\xe2\x80\x9d to a government official while applying for an immigration benefit; question 24 similarly asked whether she had ever \xe2\x80\x9clied to a[\xc2\xa0] government official to gain entry or admission into the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 72a. Maslenjak answered \xe2\x80\x9cno\xe2\x80\x9d to both questions, while swearing under oath that her replies were true. Id., at 72a, 74a. She also swore that all her written answers were true during a subsequent interview with an immigration official. In August 2007, Maslenjak was naturalized as a U.\xc2\xa0S. citizen.

\n

But Maslenjak\xe2\x80\x99s professions of honesty were false: In fact, she had made up much of the story she told to immigration officials when seeking refuge in this country. Her fiction began to unravel at around the same time she applied for citizenship. In 2006, immigration officials confronted Maslenjak\xe2\x80\x99s husband Ratko with records showing that he had not fled conscription during the Bosnian civil war; rather, he had served as an officer in the Bos-nian Serb Army. And not only that: He had served in a brigade that participated in the Srebrenica massacre\xe2\x80\x94a slaughter of some 8,000 Bosnian Muslim civilians. Within a year, the Government convicted Ratko on charges of making false statements on immigration documents. The newly naturalized Maslenjak attempted to prevent Ratko\xe2\x80\x99s deportation. During proceedings on that matter, Maslenjak admitted she had known all along that Ratko spent the war years not secreted in Serbia but fighting in Bosnia.

\n

As a result, the Government charged Maslenjak with knowingly \xe2\x80\x9cprocur[ing], contrary to law, [her] naturalization,\xe2\x80\x9d in violation of\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71425(a). According to the Government\xe2\x80\x99s theory, Maslenjak violated \xc2\xa71425(a) because, in the course of procuring her naturalization, she broke another law:\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71015(a), which prohibits knowingly making a false statement under oath in a naturalization proceeding. The false statements the Government invoked were Maslenjak\xe2\x80\x99s answers to questions 23 and 24 on the citizenship application (stating that she had not lied in seeking refugee status) and her corresponding statements in the citizenship interview. Those statements, the Government argued to the District Court, need not have affected the naturalization decision to support a conviction under \xc2\xa71425(a). The court agreed: Over Maslenjak\xe2\x80\x99s objection, it instructed the jury that a conviction was proper so long as the Government \xe2\x80\x9cprove[d] that one of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s statements was false\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94even if the statement was not \xe2\x80\x9cmaterial\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cdid not influence the decision to approve [her] naturalization.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 86a. The jury returned a guilty verdict; and the District Court, based on that finding, stripped Maslenjak of her citizenship. See\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71451(e).

\n

The United States Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit affirmed the conviction. As relevant here, the Sixth Circuit upheld the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s instructions that Maslenjak\xe2\x80\x99s false statements need not have influenced the naturalization decision. If, the Court of Appeals held, Maslenjak made false statements violating \xc2\xa71015(a) and she procured naturalization, then she also violated \xc2\xa71425(a)\xe2\x80\x94irrespective of whether the false statements played any role in her obtaining citizenship. See 821 F.\xc2\xa03d 675, 685\xe2\x80\x93686 (2016). That decision created a conflict in the Circuit Courts.[1] We granted certiorari to resolve it, 580 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2017), and we now vacate the Sixth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

Section 1425(a), the parties agree, makes it a crime to commit some other illegal act in connection with naturalization. But the parties dispute the nature of the required connection. Maslenjak argues that the relationship must be \xe2\x80\x9ccausal\xe2\x80\x9d in kind: A person \xe2\x80\x9cprocures\xe2\x80\x9d her naturalization \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary to law,\xe2\x80\x9d she contends, only if a predicate crime in some way \xe2\x80\x9ccontribut[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d to her gaining citizenship. Brief for Petitioner 21. By contrast, the Government proposes a basically chronological link: Section 1425(a), it urges, \xe2\x80\x9cpunishes the commission of other violations of law in the course of procuring naturalization\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94even if the illegality could not have had any effect on the naturalization decision. Brief for United States 14 (emphasis added). We conclude that Maslenjak has the better of this argument.

\n

We begin, as usual, with the statutory text. In ordinary usage, \xe2\x80\x9cto procure\xe2\x80\x9d something is \xe2\x80\x9cto get possession of\xe2\x80\x9d it. Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third New International Dictionary 1809 (2002); accord, Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 1401 (10th ed. 2014) (defining \xe2\x80\x9cprocure\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o obtain (something), esp. by special effort or means\xe2\x80\x9d). So to \xe2\x80\x9cprocure .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. naturalization\xe2\x80\x9d means to obtain naturalization (or, to use another word, citizenship). The adverbial phrase \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary to law,\xe2\x80\x9d wedged in between \xe2\x80\x9cprocure\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cnaturalization,\xe2\x80\x9d then specifies how a person must procure naturalization so as to run afoul of the statute: in contravention of the law\xe2\x80\x94or, in a word, illegally. Putting the pieces together, someone \xe2\x80\x9cprocure[s], contrary to law, naturalization\xe2\x80\x9d when she obtains citizenship illegally.

\n

What, then, does that whole phrase mean? The most natural understanding is that the illegal act must have somehow contributed to the obtaining of citizenship. Consider if someone said to you: \xe2\x80\x9cJohn obtained that painting illegally.\xe2\x80\x9d You might imagine that he stole it off the walls of a museum. Or that he paid for it with a forged check. Or that he impersonated the true buyer when the auction house delivered it. But in all events, you would imagine illegal acts in some kind of means-end relation\xe2\x80\x94or otherwise said, in some kind of causal relation\xe2\x80\x94to the painting\xe2\x80\x99s acquisition. If someone said to you, \xe2\x80\x9cJohn obtained that painting illegally, but his unlawful acts did not play any role in his obtaining it,\xe2\x80\x9d you would not have a clue what the statement meant. You would think it nonsense\xe2\x80\x94or perhaps the opening of a riddle. That is because if no illegal act contributed at all to getting the painting, then the painting would not have been gotten illegally. And the same goes for naturalization. If whatever illegal conduct occurring within the naturalization process was a causal dead-end\xe2\x80\x94if, so to speak, the ripples from that act could not have reached the decision to award citizenship\xe2\x80\x94then the act cannot support a charge that the applicant obtained naturalization illegally. The conduct, though itself illegal, would not also make the obtaining of citizenship so. To get citizenship unlawfully, we understand, is to get it through an unlawful means\xe2\x80\x94and that is just to say that an illegality played some role in its acquisition.[2]

\n

The Government\xe2\x80\x99s contrary view\xe2\x80\x94that \xc2\xa71425(a) requires only a \xe2\x80\x9cviolation[\xc2\xa0] of law in the course of procuring naturalization\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94falters on the way language naturally works. Brief for United States 14. Return for a moment to our artwork example. Imagine this time that John made an illegal turn while driving to the auction house to purchase a painting. Would you say that he had \xe2\x80\x9cprocured the painting illegally\xe2\x80\x9d because he happened to violate the law in the course of obtaining it? Not likely. And again, the same is true with respect to naturalization. Suppose that an applicant for citizenship fills out the necessary paperwork in a government office with a knife tucked away in her handbag (but never mentioned or used). She has violated the law\xe2\x80\x94specifically, a statute criminalizing the possession of a weapon in a federal building. See\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7930. And she has surely done so \xe2\x80\x9cin the course of\xe2\x80\x9d procuring citizenship. But would you say, using English as you ordinarily would, that she has \xe2\x80\x9cprocure[d]\xe2\x80\x9d her citizenship \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary to law\xe2\x80\x9d (or, as you would really speak, \xe2\x80\x9cillegally\xe2\x80\x9d)? Once again, no. That is because the violation of law and the acquisition of citizenship are in that example merely coincidental: The one has no causal relation to the other.

\n

The Government responds to such examples by seeking to define them out of the statute, but that effort falls short for multiple reasons. According to the Government, the laws to which \xc2\xa71425(a) speaks are only laws \xe2\x80\x9cpertaining to naturalization.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 20. But to begin with, that claim fails on its own terms. The Government\xe2\x80\x99s proposed limitation has no basis in \xc2\xa71425(a)\xe2\x80\x99s text (which refers to \xe2\x80\x9claw\xe2\x80\x9d generally); it is a deus ex machina\xe2\x80\x94rationalized only by calling it \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary,\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 39, and serving only to get the Government out of a tight interpretive spot. Indeed, the Government does not really buy its own argument: At another point, it asserts that an applicant for citizenship can violate \xc2\xa71425(a) by bribing a government official, see Brief for United States 16\xe2\x80\x94even though the law against that conduct has nothing in particular to do with naturalization. See\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7201(b)(1). And still more important, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s (sometime) carve-out does nothing to alter the linguistic understanding that gives force to the examples the Government would exclude\xe2\x80\x94and that applies just as well to every application that would remain. Laws pertaining to naturalization, in other words, are subject to the same rules of language usage as laws concerning other subjects. And under those rules, as we have shown, \xc2\xa71425(a) demands a means-end connection between a legal violation and naturalization. See supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x936. Take \xc2\xa71015(a)\xe2\x80\x99s bar on making false statements in connection with naturalization\xe2\x80\x94the prototypical \xc2\xa71425(a) predicate, and the one at issue here. If such a statement (in an interview, say) has no bearing at all on the decision to award citizenship, then it cannot render that award\xe2\x80\x94as \xc2\xa71425(a) requires\xe2\x80\x94illegally gained.

\n

The broader statutory context reinforces that point, because the Government\xe2\x80\x99s reading would create a profound mismatch between the requirements for naturalization on the one hand and those for denaturalization on the other. See West Virginia Univ. Hospitals, Inc. v. Casey,\n\n499 U.\xc2\xa0S. 83,\n101 (1991)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is our role to make sense rather than nonsense out of the corpus juris\xe2\x80\x9d). The immigration statute requires all applicants for citizenship to have \xe2\x80\x9cgood moral character,\xe2\x80\x9d and largely defines that term through a list of unlawful or unethical behaviors. 8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71427(a)(3), 1101(f\xc2\xa0).[3] On the Government\xe2\x80\x99s theory, some legal violations that do not justify denying citizenship under that definition would nonetheless justify revoking it later. Again, false statements under \xc2\xa71015(a) offer an apt illustration. The statute\xe2\x80\x99s description of \xe2\x80\x9cgood moral character\xe2\x80\x9d singles out a specific class of lies\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cfalse testimony for the purpose of obtaining [immigration] benefits\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94as a reason to deny naturalization.\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71101(f\xc2\xa0)(6). By contrast, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]illful misrepresentations made for other reasons, such as embarrassment, fear, or a desire for privacy, were not deemed sufficiently culpable to brand the applicant as someone who lacks good moral character\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94and so are not generally disqualifying. Kungys v. United States,\n\n485 U.\xc2\xa0S. 759,\n780 (1988)\n (quoting Supplemental Brief for United States 12). But under the Government\xe2\x80\x99s reading of \xc2\xa71425(a), a lie told in the naturalization process\xe2\x80\x94even out of embarrassment, fear, or a desire for privacy\xe2\x80\x94would always provide a basis for rescinding citizenship. The Government could thus take away on one day what it was required to give the day before.

\n

And by so wholly unmooring the revocation of citizenship from its award, the Government opens the door to a world of disquieting consequences\xe2\x80\x94which we would need far stronger textual support to believe Congress intended. Consider the kinds of questions a person seeking citizenship confronts on the standard application form. Says one: \xe2\x80\x9cHave you EVER been .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in any way associated with[\xc2\xa0] any organization, association, fund, foundation, party, club, society, or similar group[?]\xe2\x80\x9d Form N\xe2\x80\x93400, Application for Naturalization 12 (2016), online at http://www.uscis.gov/n-400 (as last visited June 20, 2017) (bold in original). Asks another: \xe2\x80\x9cHave you EVER committed .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a crime or offense for which you were NOT arrested?\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 14. Suppose, for reasons of embarrassment or what-have-you, a person concealed her membership in an online support group or failed to disclose a prior speeding violation. Under the Government\xe2\x80\x99s view, a prosecutor could scour her paperwork and bring a \xc2\xa71425(a) charge on that meager basis, even many years after she became a citizen. That would give prosecutors nearly limitless leverage\xe2\x80\x94and afford newly naturalized Americans precious little security. Small wonder that Congress, in enacting \xc2\xa71425(a), did not go so far as the Government claims. The statute it passed, most naturally read, strips a person of citizenship not when she committed any illegal act during the naturalization process, but only when that act played some role in her naturalization.

\n

B

\n

That conclusion leaves us with a more operational question: How should \xc2\xa71425(a)\xe2\x80\x99s requirement of causal influence apply in practice, when charges are brought under that law?[4] Because the proper analysis may vary with the nature of the predicate crime, we confine our discussion of that issue to the kind of underlying illegality alleged here: a false statement made to government officials. Such conduct can affect a naturalization decision in a single, significant way\xe2\x80\x94by distorting the Government\xe2\x80\x99s understanding of the facts when it investigates, and then adjudicates, an application. So the issue a jury must decide in a case like this one is whether a false statement sufficiently altered those processes as to have influenced an award of citizenship.

\n

The answer to that question, like the naturalization decision itself, turns on objective legal criteria. Congress has prescribed specific eligibility standards for new citizens, respecting such matters as length of residency and \xe2\x80\x9cphysical[\xc2\xa0] presen[ce],\xe2\x80\x9d understanding of English and American government, and (as previously mentioned) \xe2\x80\x9cgood moral character,\xe2\x80\x9d with all its many specific components. See 8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71423(a), 1427(a); supra, at 8. Government officials are obligated to apply that body of law faithfully\xe2\x80\x94granting naturalization when the appli-cable criteria are satisfied, and denying it when they are not. See Kungys, 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 774, n.\xc2\xa09 (opinion of Scalia, J.); id., at 787 (Stevens, J., concurring in judgment). And to ensure right results are reached, a court can reverse such a determination, at an applicant\xe2\x80\x99s request, based on its \xe2\x80\x9cown findings of fact and conclusions of law.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71421(c). The entire system, in other words, is set up to provide little or no room for subjective preferences or personal whims. Because that is so, the question of what any individual decisionmaker might have done with accurate information is beside the point: The defendant in a \xc2\xa71425(a) case should neither benefit nor suffer from a wayward official\xe2\x80\x99s deviations from legal requirements. Accordingly, the proper causal inquiry under \xc2\xa71425(a) is framed in objective terms: To decide whether a defendant acquired citizenship by means of a lie, a jury must evaluate how knowledge of the real facts would have affected a reasonable government official properly applying naturalization law.

\n

If the facts the defendant misrepresented are themselves disqualifying, the jury can make quick work of that inquiry. In such a case, there is an obvious causal link between the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s lie and her procurement of citizenship. To take an example: An applicant for citizenship must be physically present in the United States for more than half of the five-year period preceding her application. See\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71427(a)(1). Suppose a defendant misrepresented her travel history to convey she had met that requirement, when in fact she had not. The Government need only expose that lie to establish that she obtained naturalization illegally\xe2\x80\x94for had she told the truth instead, the official would have promptly denied her application. Or consider another, perhaps more common case stemming from the \xe2\x80\x9cgood moral character\xe2\x80\x9d criterion. See \xc2\xa71427(a)(3); supra, at 8. That phrase is defined to exclude any person who has been convicted of an aggravated fel-ony. See \xc2\xa71101(f\xc2\xa0)(8). If a defendant falsely denied such a conviction, she too would have gotten her citizenship by means of a lie\xe2\x80\x94for otherwise the outcome would have been different. In short, when the defendant misrepresents facts that the law deems incompatible with citizenship, her lie must have played a role in her naturalization.

\n

But that is not the only time a jury can find that a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s lie had the requisite bearing on a naturalization decision. For even if the true facts lying behind a false statement would not \xe2\x80\x9cin and of themselves justify denial of citizenship,\xe2\x80\x9d they could have \xe2\x80\x9cled to the discovery of other facts which would\xe2\x80\x9d do so. Chaunt v. United States,\n\n364 U.\xc2\xa0S. 350\n\xe2\x80\x93353 (1960). We previously addressed that possibility when considering the civil statute that authorizes the Government to revoke naturalization. See Kungys, 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 774\xe2\x80\x93777 (opinion of Scalia, J.) (interpreting\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71451(a)).[5] As we explained in that context, a person whose lies throw investigators off a trail leading to disqualifying facts gets her citizenship by means of those lies\xe2\x80\x94no less than if she had denied the damning facts at the very end of the trail. See ibid.

\n

When relying on such an investigation-based theory, the Government must make a two-part showing to meet its burden. As an initial matter, the Government has to prove that the misrepresented fact was sufficiently relevant to one or another naturalization criterion that it would have prompted reasonable officials, \xe2\x80\x9cseeking only evidence concerning citizenship qualifications,\xe2\x80\x9d to undertake further investigation. Id., at 774, n.\xc2\xa09. If that much is true, the inquiry turns to the prospect that such an investigation would have borne disqualifying fruit. As to that second link in the causal chain, the Government need not show definitively that its investigation would have unearthed a disqualifying fact (though, of course, it may). Rather, the Government need only establish that the investigation \xe2\x80\x9cwould predictably have disclosed\xe2\x80\x9d some legal disqualification. Id., at 774; see id., at 783 (Brennan, J., concurring). If that is so, the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s misrepresentation contributed to the citizenship award in the way we think \xc2\xa71425(a) requires.

\n

That standard reflects two real-world attributes of cases premised on what an unhindered investigation would have found. First is the difficulty of proving that a hypothetical inquiry would have led to some disqualifying discovery, often several years after the defendant told her lies. As witnesses and other evidence disappear, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s effort to reconstruct the course of a \xe2\x80\x9ccould have been\xe2\x80\x9d investigation confronts ever-mounting obstacles. See id., at 779 (opinion of Scalia, J.). Second, and critical to our analysis, is that the defendant\xe2\x80\x94not the Government\xe2\x80\x94bears the blame for that evidentiary predicament. After all, the inquiry cannot get this far unless the defendant made an unlawful false statement and, by so doing, obstructed the normal course of an investigation. See id., at 783 (Brennan, J., concurring) (emphasizing that \xe2\x80\x9cthe citizen\xe2\x80\x99s misrepresentation [in a naturalization proceeding] necessarily frustrated the Government\xe2\x80\x99s investigative efforts\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Bigelow v. RKO Radio Pictures, Inc., 327 U.\xc2\xa0S. 251, 265 (1946) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe most elementary conceptions of justice and public policy require that the wrongdoer shall bear the risk of the uncertainty which his own wrong has created\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Section 1425(a) is best read to take those exigencies and equities into account, by enabling the Government (as just described) to rest on disqualifications that a thwarted investigation predictably would have uncovered. A yet-stricter causal requirement, demanding proof positive that a disqualifying fact would have been found, sets the bar so high that \xe2\x80\x9cwe cannot conceive that Congress intended\xe2\x80\x9d that result. Kungys, 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 777 (opinion of Scalia, J.). And nothing in the statutory text requires that approach. While \xc2\xa71425(a) clearly imports some kind of causal or means-end relation, see supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x939, Congress left that relation\xe2\x80\x99s precise character unspecified. Cf. Burrage v. United States, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 10) (noting that courts have not always construed criminal statutes to \xe2\x80\x9crequire[\xc2\xa0] strict but-for causality,\xe2\x80\x9d and have greater reason to reject such a reading when the laws do not use language like \xe2\x80\x9cresults from\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cbecause of\xe2\x80\x9d). The open-endedness of the statutory language allows, indeed supports, our adoption of a demanding but still practicable causal standard.

\n

Even when the Government can make its two-part showing, however, the defendant may be able to overcome it. Section 1425(a) is not a tool for denaturalizing people who, the available evidence indicates, were actually qualified for the citizenship they obtained. When addressing the civil denaturalization statute, this Court insisted on a similar point: We provided the defendant with an opportunity to rebut the Government\xe2\x80\x99s case \xe2\x80\x9cby showing, through a preponderance of the evidence, that the statutory requirement as to which [a lie] had a natural tendency to produce a favorable decision was in fact met.\xe2\x80\x9d Kungys, 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 777 (opinion of Scalia, J.) (emphasis deleted); accord, id., at 783\xe2\x80\x93784 (Brennan, J., concurring). Or said otherwise, we gave the defendant a chance to establish that she was qualified for citizenship, and held that she could not be denaturalized if she did so\xe2\x80\x94even though she concealed or misrepresented facts that suggested the opposite. And indeed, all our denaturalization decisions share this crucial feature: We have never read a statute to strip citizenship from someone who met the legal criteria for acquiring it. See, e.g., Fedorenko v. United States,\n\n449 U.\xc2\xa0S. 490\n\xe2\x80\x93507 (1981); Costello v. United States,\n\n365 U.\xc2\xa0S. 265\n\xe2\x80\x93272 (1961); Schneiderman v. United States,\n\n320 U.\xc2\xa0S. 118\n\xe2\x80\x93123 (1943). We will not start now. Whatever the Government shows with respect to a thwarted investigation, qualification for citizenship is a complete defense to a prosecution brought under \xc2\xa71425(a).

\n

III

\n

Measured against all we have said, the jury instructions in this case were in error. As earlier noted, the District Court told the jury that it could convict based on any false statement in the naturalization process (i.e., any violation of \xc2\xa71015(a)), no matter how inconsequential to the ultimate decision. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 86a; supra, at 3. But as we have shown, the jury needed to find more than an unlawful false statement. Recall that Maslenjak\xe2\x80\x99s lie in the naturalization process concerned her prior statements to immigration officials: She swore that she had been honest when applying for admission as a refugee, but in fact she had not. See supra, at 2\xe2\x80\x933. The jury could have convicted if that earlier dishonesty (i.e., the thing she misrepresented when seeking citizenship) were itself a reason to deny naturalization\xe2\x80\x94say, because it counted as \xe2\x80\x9cfalse testimony for the purpose of obtaining [immigration] benefits\xe2\x80\x9d and thus demonstrated bad moral character. See supra, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312. Or else, the jury could have convicted if (1) knowledge of that prior dishonesty would have led a reasonable official to make some further investigation (say, into the circumstances of her admission), (2) that inquiry would predictably have yielded a legal basis for rejecting her citizenship application, and (3) Maslenjak failed to show that (notwithstanding such an objective likelihood) she was in fact qualified to become a U.\xc2\xa0S. citizen. See supra, at 12\xe2\x80\x9315. This jury, however, was not asked to\xe2\x80\x94and so did not\xe2\x80\x94make any of those determinations. Accordingly, Maslenjak was not convicted by a properly instructed jury of \xe2\x80\x9cprocur[ing], contrary to law, [her] naturalization.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

The Government asserts that any instructional error in this case was harmless. \xe2\x80\x9cHad officials known the truth,\xe2\x80\x9d the Government asserts, \xe2\x80\x9cit would have affected their decision to grant [Maslenjak] citizenship.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 12. Unsurprisingly, Maslenjak disagrees. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 6\xe2\x80\x938; Reply to Brief in Opposition 9\xe2\x80\x9310. In keeping with our usual practice, we leave that dispute for resolution on remand. See, e.g., Skilling v. United States,\n\n561 U.\xc2\xa0S. 358,\n414 (2010)\n.

\n

For the reasons stated, we vacate the judgment of the Court of Appeals and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

We granted review in this case to decide whether \xe2\x80\x9ca naturalized American citizen can be stripped of her citizenship in a criminal proceeding based on an immaterial false statement.\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Cert. i. The answer to that question is \xe2\x80\x9cno.\xe2\x80\x9d Although the relevant criminal statute,\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71425(a), does not expressly refer to the concept of materiality, the critical statutory language effectively requires proof of materiality in a case involving false statements. The statute makes it a crime for a person to \xe2\x80\x9cprocure\xe2\x80\x9d naturalization \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary to law.\xe2\x80\x9d In false statement cases, then, the statute essentially imposes the familiar materiality requirement that applies in other contexts. That is, a person violates the statute by procuring naturalization through an illegal false statement which has a \xe2\x80\x9cnatural tendency to influence\xe2\x80\x9d the outcome\xe2\x80\x94that is, the obtaining of naturalization. Kungys v. United States,\n\n485 U.\xc2\xa0S. 759,\n772 (1988)\n.

\n

Understood in this way, Section 1425(a) does not require proof that a false statement actually had some effect on the naturalization decision. The operative statutory language\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cprocure\xe2\x80\x9d naturalization \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary to law\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94imposes no such requirement.

\n

Here is an example. Eight co-workers jointly buy two season tickets to see their favorite football team play. They all write their names on a piece of paper and place the slips in a hat to see who will get the tickets for the big game with their team\xe2\x80\x99s traditional rival. One of the friends puts his name in twice, and his name is drawn. I would say that he \xe2\x80\x9cprocured\xe2\x80\x9d the tickets \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary to\xe2\x80\x9d the rules of the drawing even though he might have won if he had put his name in only once.

\n

Here is another example. A runner who holds the world\xe2\x80\x99s record in an event wants to make sure she wins the gold medal at the Olympics, so she takes a performance enhancing drug. She wins the race but fails a drug test and is disqualified. The second-place time is slow, and sportswriters speculate that she would have won without taking the drug. But it would be entirely consistent with standard English usage for the race officials to say that she \xe2\x80\x9cprocured\xe2\x80\x9d her first-place finish \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary to\xe2\x80\x9d the governing rules.

\n

As these examples illustrate\xe2\x80\x94and others could be added\xe2\x80\x94the language of\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71425(a) does not require that an illegal false statement have a demonstrable effect on the naturalization decision. Instead, the statute applies when a person makes an illegal false statement to obtain naturalization, and that false statement is material to the outcome. I see no indication that Congress meant to require more.

\n

One additional point is worth mentioning. Section 1425(a) not only makes it a crime to procure naturalization contrary to law; it applies equally to any person who \xe2\x80\x9cattempts to procure, contrary to law .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. naturalization.\xe2\x80\x9d Therefore, if a defendant knowingly performs a substantial act that he or she thinks will procure naturalization, that is sufficient for conviction. See United States v. Resendiz-Ponce,\n\n549 U.\xc2\xa0S. 102\n\xe2\x80\x93108 (2007).

The Court holds that the plain text and structure of the statute before us require the Government to prove causation as an element of conviction: The defendant\xe2\x80\x99s illegal conduct must, in some manner, cause her naturalization. I agree with this much and concur in Part II\xe2\x80\x93A of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion to the extent it so holds. And because the jury wasn\xe2\x80\x99t instructed at all about causation, I agree too that reversal is required.

\n

But, respectfully, there I would stop. In an effort to \xe2\x80\x9coperational[ize]\xe2\x80\x9d the statute\xe2\x80\x99s causation requirement, the Court says a great deal more, offering, for example, two newly announced tests, the second with two more subparts, and a new affirmative defense\xe2\x80\x94all while indicating that some of these new tests and defenses may apply only in some but not all cases. See, e.g., ante, at 10\xe2\x80\x9315. The work here is surely thoughtful and may prove entirely sound. But the question presented and the briefing before us focused primarily on whether the statute contains a materiality element, not on the contours of a causation requirement. So the parties have not had the chance to join issue fully on the matters now decided. Compare ante, at 10, n.\xc2\xa04, with Brief for Petitioner, pp. i, 18\xe2\x80\x9338; Brief for United States, pp. i, 12\xe2\x80\x9351. And, of course, the lower courts have not had a chance to pass on any of these questions in the first instance. Most cited by the Court have (again) focused only on the materiality (not causation) question; none has tested the elaborate operational details advanced today; and at least one has found our prior unilateral and fractured foray into a related statute in Kungys v. United States,\n\n485 U.\xc2\xa0S. 759 (1988)\n, \xe2\x80\x9cmaddening[\xc2\xa0].\xe2\x80\x9d See ante, at 10, n.\xc2\xa04 (collecting cases).

\n

Respectfully, it seems to me at least reasonably possible that the crucible of adversarial testing on which we usually depend, along with the experience of our thoughtful colleagues on the district and circuit benches, could yield insights (or reveal pitfalls) we cannot muster guided only by our own lights. So while I agree with the Court that the parties will need guidance about the details of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s causation requirement, see ibid., I have no doubt that the Court of Appeals, with aid of briefing from the parties, can supply that on remand. Other circuits may improve that guidance over time too. And eventually we can bless the best of it. For my part, I believe it is work enough for the day to recognize that the statute requires some proof of causation, that the jury instructions here did not, and to allow the parties and courts of appeals to take it from there as they usually do. This Court often speaks most wisely when it speaks last.
