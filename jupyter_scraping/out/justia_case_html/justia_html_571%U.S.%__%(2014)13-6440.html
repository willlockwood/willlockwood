SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

\n

ANTHONY RAY HINTON v. ALABAMA

\n

on petition for writ of certiorari to the court of criminal appeals of alabama

\n

No. 13\xe2\x80\x936440\xe2\x80\x83Decided February 24, 2014

\n

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Per Curiam.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In Strickland v. Washington,\n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 668 (1984)\n, we held that a criminal defendant\xe2\x80\x99s\n \nSixth Amendment right to counsel is violated if his trial attorney\xe2\x80\x99s performance falls below an objective standard of reasonableness and if there is a reasonable probability that the result of the trial would have been different absent the deficient act or omission. Id., at 687\xe2\x80\x93688, 694. Anthony Ray Hinton, an inmate on Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s death row, asks us to decide whether the Alabama courts correctly applied Strickland to his case. We conclude that they did not and hold that Hinton\xe2\x80\x99s trial attorney rendered constitutionally deficient performance. We vacate the lower court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment and remand the case for reconsideration of whether the attorney\xe2\x80\x99s deficient performance was prejudicial.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In February 1985, a restaurant manager in Birming-ham was shot to death in the course of an after-hours rob-bery of his restaurant. A second manager was murderedduraing a very similar robbery of another restaurant inJuly. Then, later in July, a restaurant manager named Smotherman survived another similar robbery-shooting. During each crime, the robber fired two .38 caliber bullets; all six bullets were recovered by police investigators. Smotherman described his assailant to the police, and when the police showed him a photographic array, he picked out Hinton\xe2\x80\x99s picture.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The police arrested Hinton and recovered from his house a .38 caliber revolver belonging to his mother, who shared the house with him. After analyzing the six bullets fired during the three crimes and test-firing the revolver, examiners at the State\xe2\x80\x99s Department of Forensic Sciences concluded that the six bullets had all been fired fromthe same gun: the revolver found at Hinton\xe2\x80\x99s house. Hinton was charged with two counts of capital murder forthe killings during the first two robberies. He was not charged in connection with the third robbery (that is, the Smotherman robbery).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n At trial, the State\xe2\x80\x99s strategy was to link Hinton to the Smotherman robbery through eyewitness testimony and forensic evidence about the bullets fired at Smotherman and then to persuade the jury that, in light of the similar-ity of the three crimes and forensic analysis of the bullets and the Hinton revolver, Hinton must also have committed the two murders. Smotherman identified Hinton as the man who robbed his restaurant and tried to kill him, and two other witnesses provided testimony that tended to link Hinton to the Smotherman robbery. Hinton maintained that he was innocent and that Smotherman had misidentified him. In support of that defense, Hinton presented witnesses who testified in support of his alibi that he was at work at a warehouse at the time of the Smotherman robbery. See 548 So.\xc2\xa02d 562, 568\xe2\x80\x93569 (Ala. 1989) (summarizing the evidence on each side of the case).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The six bullets and the revolver were the only physical evidence. Besides those items, the police found no evidence at the crime scenes that could be used to identify the perpetrator (such as fingerprints) and no incriminating evidence at Hinton\xe2\x80\x99s home or in his car. The State\xe2\x80\x99s case turned on whether its expert witnesses could convince the jury that the six recovered bullets had indeed been fired from the Hinton revolver. According to the Alabama Supreme Court, \xe2\x80\x9cthe only evidence linking Hinton to the two murders were forensic comparisons of the bullets recovered from those crime scenes to the Hinton revolver.\xe2\x80\x9d 2008 WL 4603723, *2 (Oct. 17, 2008).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The category of forensic evidence at issue in this case is \xe2\x80\x9cfirearms and toolmark\xe2\x80\x9d evidence. Toolmark examiners attempt to determine whether a bullet recovered from a crime scene was fired from a particular gun by comparing microscopic markings (toolmarks) on the recovered bullet to the markings on a bullet known to have been fired from that gun. The theory is that minor differences even between guns of the same model will leave discernible traces on bullets that are unique enough for an examiner to conclude that the recovered bullet was or was not fired from a given weapon. See generally National Research Council, Strengthening Forensic Science in the United States: A Path Forward 150\xe2\x80\x93155 (2009).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Recognizing that Hinton\xe2\x80\x99s defense called for an effective rebuttal of the State\xe2\x80\x99s expert witnesses, Hinton\xe2\x80\x99s attorney filed a motion for funding to hire an expert witness of his own. In response, the trial judge granted $1,000 with this statement:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98I don\xe2\x80\x99t know as to what my limitations are as for how much I can grant, but I can grant up to $500.00 in each case [that is, for each of the two murder charges, which were tried together] as far as I know right now and I\xe2\x80\x99m granting up to $500.00 in each of these two cases for this. So if you need additional experts I would go ahead and file on a separate form and I\xe2\x80\x99ll have to see if I can grant additional experts, but I am granting up to $500.00, which is the statutory maximum as far as I know on this and if it\xe2\x80\x99s necessary that we go beyond that then I may check to see if we can, but this one\xe2\x80\x99s granted.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 2006 WL 1125605, *59 (Ala. Crim. App., Apr. 28, 2006) (Cobb, J., dissenting) (quoting Tr. 10).

\n

Hinton\xe2\x80\x99s attorney did not take the judge up on his invitation to file a request for more funding.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In fact, $500 per case ($1,000 total) was not the statu-tory maximum at the time of Hinton\xe2\x80\x99s trial. An earlier version of the statute had limited state reimbursement of expenses to one half of the $1,000 statutory cap on attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees, which explains why the judge believed that Hinton was entitled to up to $500 for each of the two murder charges. See Smelley v. State, 564 So.\xc2\xa02d 74, 88 (Ala. Crim. App. 1990). But the relevant statute had been amended to provide: \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Counsel shall also be entitled to be reimbursed for any expenses reasonably incurred in such defense to be approved in advance by the trial court.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d See Dubose v. State, 662 So.\xc2\xa02d 1156, 1177, n.\xc2\xa05 (Ala. Crim. App. 1993) (quoting Ala. Code \xc2\xa715\xe2\x80\x9312\xe2\x80\x9321(d) (1984)), aff\xe2\x80\x99d 662 So.\xc2\xa02d 1189 (Ala. 1995). That amendment went into effect on June 13, 1984, Dubose, supra, at 1177, n. 5, which was over a year before Hinton was arrested, so Hinton\xe2\x80\x99s trial attorney could have corrected the trial judge\xe2\x80\x99s mistaken belief that a $1,000 limit applied and accepted his invitation to file a motion for additional funds.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The attorney failed to do so because he was himself unaware that Alabama law no longer imposed a specific limit and instead allowed reimbursement for \xe2\x80\x9cany expenses reasonably incurred.\xe2\x80\x9d At an evidentiary hearing held on Hinton\xe2\x80\x99s postconviction petition, the following conversation occurred between a state attorney and Hinton\xe2\x80\x99s trial attorney:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cQ. You did an awful lot of work to try and find what you believed to be a qualified expert in this case, didn\xe2\x80\x99t you?

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cA. Yes, sir, I did.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cQ. Would you characterize it that you did everything that you knew to do?

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cA. Yes, sir, I think so.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cQ. And this case, did it come down to an unwillingness of experts to work for the price that you were able to pay?

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cA. Yes, sir, I think it did.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cQ. So your failure to get an expert that you would have been let\xe2\x80\x99s say a hundred percent satisfied with was not a failure on your part to go out and do some act, it was a failure of the court to approve what you believed would have been sufficient funds?

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cA. Well, putting it a little differently, yes, sir, it was a failure\xe2\x80\x94it was my failure, my inability under the statute to obtain any more funding for the purpose of hiring qualified experts.\xe2\x80\x9d Reporter\xe2\x80\x99s Official Tr. 206\xe2\x80\x93207 (emphasis added).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Operating under the mistaken belief that he could pay no more than $1,000, Hinton\xe2\x80\x99s attorney went looking for an expert witness. According to his postconviction testimony, he made an extensive search for a well-regarded expert, but found only one person who was willing to take the case for the pay he could offer: Andrew Payne. Hinton\xe2\x80\x99s attorney \xe2\x80\x9ctestified that Payne did not have the expertise he thought he needed and that he did not consider Payne\xe2\x80\x99s testimony to be effective.\xe2\x80\x9d 2006 WL 1125605, *27. As he told the trial judge during a pretrial hearing:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cI made an effort to get somebody that I thought would be useable. And I\xe2\x80\x99ll have to tell you what I did [about] Payne. I called a couple of other lawyers in town .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to ask if they knew of anybody. One of them knew him; one of them knew him. The reason I didn\xe2\x80\x99t contact him was because he wasn\xe2\x80\x99t recommended by the lawyer. So now I\xe2\x80\x99m stuck that he\xe2\x80\x99s the only guy I could possibly produce.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at *30 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n At trial, Payne testified that the toolmarks in the barrel of the Hinton revolver had been corroded away so that it would be impossible to say with certainty whether a particular bullet had been fired from that gun. He also testified that the bullets from the three crime scenes did not match one another. The State\xe2\x80\x99s two experts, by contrast, maintained that all six bullets had indeed been fired from the Hinton revolver.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n On cross-examination, the prosecutor badly discredited Payne. Payne admitted that he\xe2\x80\x99d testified as an expert on firearms and toolmark identification just twice in the preceding eight years and that one of the two cases involved a shotgun rather than a handgun. Payne also conceded that he had had difficulty operating the microscope at the state forensic laboratory and had asked for help from one of the state experts. The prosecutor ended the cross-examination with this colloquy:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cQ. Mr. Payne, do you have some problem with your vision?

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cA. Why, yes.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cQ. How many eyes do you have?

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cA. One.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. 1667.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s closing argument highlighted the fact that Payne\xe2\x80\x99s expertise was in military ordnance, not firearms and toolmark identification, and that Payne had graduated in 1933 (more than half a century before the trial) with a degree in civil engineering, whereas the State\xe2\x80\x99s experts had years of training and experience in the field of firearms and toolmark examination. The prosecutor said:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98I ask you to reject [Payne\xe2\x80\x99s] testimony and you have that option because you are the judges of the facts and whose testimony, Mr. Yates\xe2\x80\x99 or Mr. Payne\xe2\x80\x99s, you will give credence to, and I submit to you that as between these two men there is no match between them. There is no comparison. One man just doesn\xe2\x80\x99t have it and the other does it day in and day out, month in and month out, year in and year out, and is recognized across the state as an expert.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 2006 WL 1125605, *64 (Cobb, J., dissenting) (quoting Tr. 1733\xe2\x80\x931734).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The jury convicted Hinton and recommended by a 10-to-2 vote that he be sentenced to death. The trialjudge accepted that recommendation and imposed a death sentence.

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In his state postconviction petition, Hinton contended that his trial attorney was \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98ineffective to not seek additional funds when it became obvious that the individual willing to examine the evidence in the case for the $1,000 allotted by the court was incompetent and unqualified. Indeed, this failure to seek additional, sufficient funds is rendered all the more inexplicable by the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s express invitation to counsel to seek more funds if such funds were necessary.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 2006 WL 1125605, *28.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n To show that he had been prejudiced by Payne\xe2\x80\x99s ineffective testimony, Hinton produced three new experts on toolmark evidence. One of the three, a forensic consultant named John Dillon, had worked on toolmark identification at the Federal Bureau of Investigation\xe2\x80\x99s forensics laboratory and, from 1988 until he retired in 1994, had served as chief of the firearms and toolmark unit at the FBI\xe2\x80\x99s headquarters. The other two postconviction experts had worked for many years as firearms and toolmark examiners at the Dallas County Crime Laboratory and had each testified as toolmark experts in several hundred cases.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n All three experts examined the physical evidence and testified that they could not conclude that any of the six bullets had been fired from the Hinton revolver. The State did not submit rebuttal evidence during the postconviction hearing, and one of Hinton\xe2\x80\x99s experts testified that, pursuant to the ethics code of his trade organization, the Association of Firearm and Tool Mark Examiners, he had asked the State\xe2\x80\x99s expert, Yates, to show him how he had determined that the recovered bullets had been fired from the Hinton revolver. Yates refused to cooperate.

\n

C

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The circuit court denied Hinton\xe2\x80\x99s postconviction petition on the ground that Hinton had not been prejudiced by Payne\xe2\x80\x99s allegedly poor performance because Payne\xe2\x80\x99s testimony did not depart from what Hinton\xe2\x80\x99s postconviction experts had said: The bullets could not be affirmatively matched either to one another or to the Hinton revolver.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Alabama Court of Criminal Appeals affirmed by a 3-to-2 vote. 2006 WL 1125605. The court agreed with the circuit court that Hinton had not been prejudiced because Payne\xe2\x80\x99s testimony, if believed by the jury, strongly supported the inference that Hinton was innocent. Id., at *31. Then-Judge Cobb (who later became chief justice of the Alabama Supreme Court) dissented. In her view, Hinton\xe2\x80\x99s attorney had been ineffective in failing to seek additional funds to hire a better expert and Hinton had been prejudiced by that failure, meaning that he was entitled to a new trial. Then-Judge Shaw (who is now a justice of the Alabama Supreme Court) also dissented. He would have remanded the case to the circuit court to make a finding as to whether or not Payne was qualified to act as an expert on toolmark evidence. He stated that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t goes without saying that, with knowledge that sufficient funds were available to have a qualified firearms and toolmarks expert, no reasonable criminal defense lawyer would seek out and hire an unqualified firearms witness.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at *73.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Supreme Court of Alabama reversed and remanded. 2008 WL 4603723. After quoting at length from Judge Shaw\xe2\x80\x99s dissent, the Court stated, \xe2\x80\x9cWe agree with Judge Shaw that \xe2\x80\x98the dispositive issue is whether Payne was a qualified firearms and toolmarks expert\xe2\x80\x99 and that in denying Hinton\xe2\x80\x99s [postconviction] petition the trial court did not directly rule on \xe2\x80\x98the issue whether Payne was qualified to be testifying in the first place.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at *4 (quoting 2006 WL 1125605, *70, *72 (Shaw, J., dissenting)). The Supreme Court was thus focused on Payne\xe2\x80\x99s own qualifications, rather than on whether a better expert\xe2\x80\x94one who could have been hired had the attorney learned that there was no funding cap and requested additional funds\xe2\x80\x94would have made a more compelling case for Hinton.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n On remand, the circuit court held that Payne was indeed qualified to testify as a firearms and toolmark expert witness under the Alabama evidentiary standard in place at the time of the trial, which required only that Payne have had \xe2\x80\x9cknowledge of firearms and toolmarks exam-ination beyond that of an average layperson.\xe2\x80\x9d 2008 WL 5517591, *5 (Ala. Crim. App., Dec. 19, 2008); see also Charles v. State, 350 So. 2d 730, 733 (Ala. Crim. App. 1977) (\xe2\x80\x9cAn \xe2\x80\x98expert witness\xe2\x80\x99 is one who can enlighten a jury more than the average man in the street.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. An expert witness, by definition, is any person whose opportunity or means of knowledge in a specialized art or science is to some degree better than that found in the average juror or witness\xe2\x80\x9d). The appellate court affirmed the circuit court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling that Payne was qualified under the applicable standard. 2013 WL 598122 (Ala. Crim. App., Feb. 15, 2013). The Alabama Supreme Court denied review by a 4-to-3 vote, with two justices recused. Hinton then filed this petition for a writ of certiorari.

\n

II

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This case calls for a straightforward application of our ineffective-assistance-of-counsel precedents, beginning with Strickland v. Washington,\n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 668\n. Strickland recognized that the\n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s guarantee that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n all criminal prosecutions, the accused shall enjoy the right .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to have the Assistance of Counsel for his defence\xe2\x80\x9d entails that defendants are entitled to be represented by an attorney who meets at least a minimal standard of competence. Id., at 685\xe2\x80\x93687. \xe2\x80\x9cUnder Strickland, we first determine whether counsel\xe2\x80\x99s representation \xe2\x80\x98fell below an objective standard of reasonableness.\xe2\x80\x99 Then we ask whether \xe2\x80\x98there is a reasonable probability that, but for counsel\xe2\x80\x99s unprofessional errors, the result of the proceeding would have been different.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Padilla v. Kentucky,\n\n559 U.\xc2\xa0S. 356,\n366 (2010)\n (quoting Strickland, supra, at 688, 694).

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \xe2\x80\x9cThe first prong\xe2\x80\x94constitutional deficiency\xe2\x80\x94is necessarily linked to the practice and expectations of the legal community: \xe2\x80\x98The proper measure of attorney performance remains simply reasonableness under prevailing professional norms.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Padilla, supra, at 366 (quoting Strickland, supra, at 688). \xe2\x80\x9cIn any case presenting an ineffectiveness claim, the performance inquiry must be whether counsel\xe2\x80\x99s assistance was reasonable considering all the circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Strickland, supra, at 688. Under that standard, it was unreasonable for Hinton\xe2\x80\x99s lawyer to fail to seek additional funds to hire an expert where that failure was based not on any strategic choice but on a mistaken belief that available funding was capped at $1,000.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \xe2\x80\x9cCriminal cases will arise where the only reasonable and available defense strategy requires consultation with experts or introduction of expert evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Harrington v. Richter, 562 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 16). This was such a case. As Hinton\xe2\x80\x99s trial attorney recognized, the core of the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s case was the state experts\xe2\x80\x99 conclusion that the six bullets had been fired from the Hinton revolver, and effectively rebutting that case required a competent expert on the defense side. Hinton\xe2\x80\x99s attorney also recognized that Payne was not a good expert, at least with respect to toolmark evidence. Nonetheless, he felt he was \xe2\x80\x9cstuck\xe2\x80\x9d with Payne because he could not find a better expert willing to work for $1,000 and he believed that he was unable to obtain more than $1,000 to cover expert fees.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n As discussed above, that belief was wrong: Alabama law in effect beginning more than a year before Hinton was arrested provided for state reimbursement of \xe2\x80\x9cany expenses reasonably incurred in such defense to be approved in advance by the trial court.\xe2\x80\x9d Ala. Code \xc2\xa715\xe2\x80\x9312\xe2\x80\x9321(d). And the trial judge expressly invited Hinton\xe2\x80\x99s attorney to file a request for further funds if he felt that more funding was necessary. Yet the attorney did not seek further funding.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The trial attorney\xe2\x80\x99s failure to request additional funding in order to replace an expert he knew to be inadequate because he mistakenly believed that he had received all he could get under Alabama law constituted deficient performance. Under Strickland, \xe2\x80\x9cstrategic choices made after thorough investigation of law and facts relevant to plausible options are virtually unchallengeable; and strategic choices made after less than complete investigation are reasonable precisely to the extent that reasonable professional judgments support the limitations on investigation. In other words, counsel has a duty to make reasonable investigations or to make a reasonable decision that makes particular investigations unnecessary.\xe2\x80\x9d 466 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 690\xe2\x80\x93691. Hinton\xe2\x80\x99s attorney knew that he needed more funding to present an effective defense, yet he failed to make even the cursory investigation of the state statute providing for defense funding for indigent defendants that would have revealed to him that he could receive reimbursement not just for $1,000 but for \xe2\x80\x9cany expenses reasonably incurred.\xe2\x80\x9d An attorney\xe2\x80\x99s ignorance of a point of law that is fundamental to his case combined with his failure to perform basic research on that point is a quintessential example of unreasonable performance under Strickland. See, e.g., Williams v. Taylor,\n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 362\n, 395 (2000) (finding deficient performance where counsel \xe2\x80\x9cfailed to conduct an investigation that would have uncovered extensive records [that could be used for death penalty mitigation purposes], not because of any strategic calcu-lation but because they incorrectly thought that state law barred access to such records\xe2\x80\x9d); Kimmelman v. Morrison,\n\n477 U.\xc2\xa0S. 365,\n385 (1986)\n (finding deficient performance where counsel failed to conduct pretrial discovery and that failure \xe2\x80\x9cwas not based on \xe2\x80\x98strategy,\xe2\x80\x99 but on counsel\xe2\x80\x99s mistaken belie[f\xc2\xa0] that the State was obliged to take the initiative and turn over all of its inculpatory evidence to the defense\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We wish to be clear that the inadequate assistance of counsel we find in this case does not consist of the hiring of an expert who, though qualified, was not qualified enough. The selection of an expert witness is a paradigmatic example of the type of \xe2\x80\x9cstrategic choic[e]\xe2\x80\x9d that, when made \xe2\x80\x9cafter thorough investigation of [the] law and facts,\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cvirtually unchallengeable.\xe2\x80\x9d Strickland, 466 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 690. We do not today launch federal courts into examination of the relative qualifications of experts hired and experts that might have been hired. The only inadequate assistance of counsel here was the inexcusable mistake of law\xe2\x80\x94the unreasonable failure to understand the resources that state law made available to him\xe2\x80\x94that caused counsel to employ an expert that he himself deemed inadequate.

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Having established deficient performance, Hinton must also \xe2\x80\x9cshow that there is a reasonable probability that, but for counsel\xe2\x80\x99s unprofessional errors, the result of the proceeding would have been different. A reasonable probability is a probability sufficient to undermine confidence in the outcome.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 694. \xe2\x80\x9cWhen a defendant challenges a conviction, the question is whether there is a reasonable probability that, absent the errors, the factfinder would have had a reasonable doubt respecting guilt.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 695.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court of Criminal Appeals held, and the State contends in its brief in opposition to certiorari, that Hinton could not have been prejudiced by his attorney\xe2\x80\x99s use of Payne rather than a more qualified expert because Payne said all that Hinton could have hoped for from a toolmark expert: that the bullets used in the crimes could nothave been fired from the Hinton revolver. See 2006 WL 1125605, *31 (\xe2\x80\x9c[E]ven assuming that counsel\xe2\x80\x99s apparent ignorance that the cap on expert expenses had been lifted constituted deficient performance .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0, the appellant has not shown that he was prejudiced by that deficient performance\xe2\x80\x9d). It is true that Payne\xe2\x80\x99s testimony would have done Hinton a lot of good if the jury had believed it. But the jury did not believe Payne. And if there is a reasonable probability that Hinton\xe2\x80\x99s attorney would have hired an expert who would have instilled in the jury a reasonable doubt as to Hinton\xe2\x80\x99s guilt had the attorney known that the statutory funding limit had been lifted, then Hinton was prejudiced by his lawyer\xe2\x80\x99s deficient performance and is entitled to a new trial.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n That the State presented testimony from two experienced expert witnesses that tended to inculpate Hinton does not, taken alone, demonstrate that Hinton is guilty. Prosecution experts, of course, can sometimes make mistakes. Indeed, we have recognized the threat to fair criminal trials posed by the potential for incompetent or fraudulent prosecution forensics experts, noting that \xe2\x80\x9c[s]erious deficiencies have been found in the forensic evidence used in criminal trials.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. One study of cases in which exon-erating evidence resulted in the overturning of criminal convictions concluded that invalid forensic testimony contributed to the convictions in 60% of the cases.\xe2\x80\x9d Melendez-Diaz v. Massachusetts,\n\n557 U.\xc2\xa0S. 305,\n319 (2009)\n (citing Garrett & Neufeld, Invalid Forensic Science Testimony and Wrongful Convictions, 95 Va. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1, 14 (2009)). This threat is minimized when the defense retains a competent expert to counter the testimony of the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s expert witnesses; it is maximized when the defense instead fails to understand the resources available to it by law.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Because no court has yet evaluated the prejudice question by applying the proper inquiry to the facts of this case, we remand the case for reconsideration of whether Hinton\xe2\x80\x99s attorney\xe2\x80\x99s deficient performance was prejudicial under Strickland.

\n
