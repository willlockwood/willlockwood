ARIZONA STATE LEGISLATURE v. ARIZONA INDEPENDENT REDISTRICTING COMMISSION et\xc2\xa0al.

appeal from the united states district court for the district of arizona

No. 13\xe2\x80\x931314.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 2, 2015\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 29, 2015

Under Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s Constitution, the electorate shares lawmaking authority on equal footing with the Arizona Legislature. The voters may adopt laws and constitutional amendments by ballot initiative, and they may approve or disapprove, by referendum, measures passed by the Legislature. Ariz. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0IV, pt. 1, \xc2\xa71. \xe2\x80\x9cAny law which may be enacted by the Legislature .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. may be enacted by the people under the Initiative.\xe2\x80\x9d Art. XXII, \xc2\xa714.

In 2000, Arizona voters adopted Proposition 106, an initiative aimed at the problem of gerrymandering. Proposition 106 amended Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s Constitution, removing redistricting authority from the Arizona Legislature and vesting it in an independent commission, the Arizona Independent Redistricting Commission (AIRC). After the 2010 census, as after the 2000 census, the AIRC adopted redistricting maps for congressional as well as state legislative districts. The Arizona Legislature challenged the map the Commission adopted in 2012 for congressional districts, arguing that the AIRC and its map violated the \xe2\x80\x9cElections Clause\xe2\x80\x9d of the U.\xc2\xa0S. Constitution, which provides: \xe2\x80\x9cThe Times, Places and Manner of holding Elections for Senators and Representatives shall be prescribed in each State by the Legislature thereof; but the Congress may at any time by Law make or alter such Regulations.\xe2\x80\x9d Because \xe2\x80\x9cLegislature\xe2\x80\x9d means the State\xe2\x80\x99s representative assembly, the Arizona Legislature contended, the Clause precludes resort to an independent commission, created by initiative, to accomplish redistricting. A three-judge District Court held that the Arizona Legislature had standing to sue, but rejected its complaint on the merits.

Held:

1.\xc2\xa0The Arizona Legislature has standing to bring this suit. In claiming that Proposition 106 stripped it of its alleged constitutional prerogative to engage in redistricting and that its injury would be remedied by a court order enjoining the proposition\xe2\x80\x99s enforcement, the Legislature has shown injury that is \xe2\x80\x98concrete and particularized\xe2\x80\x99 and \xe2\x80\x98actual or imminent,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Arizonans for Official English v. Arizona, 520 U.\xc2\xa0S. 43, \xe2\x80\x9cfairly traceable to the challenged action,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9credressable by a favorable ruling,\xe2\x80\x9d Clapper v. Amnesty Int\xe2\x80\x99l USA, 568 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. Specifically, Proposition 106, together with the Arizona Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s ban on efforts by the Arizona Legislature to undermine the purposes of an initiative, would \xe2\x80\x9ccompletely nullif[y]\xe2\x80\x9d any vote by the Legislature, now or \xe2\x80\x9cin the future,\xe2\x80\x9d purporting to adopt a redistricting plan. Raines v. Byrd,521 U.\xc2\xa0S. 811\xe2\x80\x93824. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9315.

2.\xc2\xa0The Elections Clause and2 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72a(c) permit Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s use of a commission to adopt congressional districts. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9335.

(a)\xc2\xa0Redistricting is a legislative function to be performed in accordance with the State\xe2\x80\x99s prescriptions for lawmaking, which may include the referendum, Ohio ex rel. Davis v. Hildebrant,241 U.\xc2\xa0S. 565, and the Governor\xe2\x80\x99s veto, Smiley v. Holm,285 U.\xc2\xa0S. 355. While exercise of the initiative was not at issue in this Court\xe2\x80\x99s prior decisions, there is no constitutional barrier to a State\xe2\x80\x99s empowerment of its people by embracing that form of lawmaking. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9319.

(b)\xc2\xa0Title2 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72a(c)\xe2\x80\x94which provides that, \xe2\x80\x9c[u]ntil a State is redistricted in the manner provided by the law thereof after any apportionment,\xe2\x80\x9d it must follow federally prescribed redistricting procedures\xe2\x80\x94permits redistricting in accord with Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s initiative. From 1862 through 1901, apportionment Acts required a State to follow federal procedures unless \xe2\x80\x9cthe [state] legislature\xe2\x80\x9d drew district lines. In 1911, Congress, recognizing that States had supplemented the representative legislature mode of lawmaking with a direct lawmaking role for the people, replaced the reference to redistricting by the state \xe2\x80\x9clegislature\xe2\x80\x9d with a reference to redistricting of a State \xe2\x80\x9cin the manner provided by the laws thereof.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa74,37Stat.14. The Act\xe2\x80\x99s legislative history \xe2\x80\x9cleaves no .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. doubt,\xe2\x80\x9d Hildebrant, 241 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 568, that the change was made to safeguard to \xe2\x80\x9ceach state full authority to employ in the creation of congressional districts its own laws and regulations.\xe2\x80\x9d 47 Cong. Rec. 3437. \xe2\x80\x9cIf they include the initiative, it is included.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 3508. Congress used virtually identical language in enacting \xc2\xa72a(c) in 1941. This provision also accords full respect to the redistricting procedures adopted by the States. Thus, so long as a State has \xe2\x80\x9credistricted in the manner provided by the law thereof\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94as Arizona did by utilizing the independent commission procedure in its Constitution\xe2\x80\x94the resulting redistricting plan becomes the presumptively governing map.

Though four of \xc2\xa72a(c)\xe2\x80\x99s five default redistricting procedures\xe2\x80\x94operative only when a State is not \xe2\x80\x9credistricted in the manner provided by [state] law\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94have become obsolete as a result of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions embracing the one-person, one-vote principle, this infirmity does not bear on the question whether a State has been \xe2\x80\x9credistricted in the manner provided by [state] law.\xe2\x80\x9d Pp.\xc2\xa019\xe2\x80\x9323.

(c) \xc2\xa0The Elections Clause permits the people of Arizona to provide for redistricting by independent commission. The history and purpose of the Clause weigh heavily against precluding the people of Arizona from creating a commission operating independently of the state legislature to establish congressional districts. Such preclusion would also run up against the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s animating principle that the people themselves are the originating source of all the powers of government. Pp.\xc2\xa024\xe2\x80\x9335.

(1)\xc2\xa0The dominant purpose of the Elections Clause, the historical record bears out, was to empower Congress to override state election rules, not to restrict the way States enact legislation. See Inter Tribal Council of Ariz., 570 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___. Ratification arguments in support of congressional oversight focused on potential abuses by state politicians, but the legislative processes by which the States could exercise their initiating role in regulating congressional elections occasioned no debate. Pp. 25\xe2\x80\x9327.

(2)\xc2\xa0There is no suggestion that the Election Clause, by specifying \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature thereof,\xe2\x80\x9d required assignment of congressional redistricting authority to the State\xe2\x80\x99s representative body. It is characteristic of the federal system that States retain autonomy to establish their own governmental processes free from incursion by the Federal Government. See, e.g., Alden v. Maine,527 U.\xc2\xa0S. 706. \xe2\x80\x9cThrough the structure of its government, and the character of those who exercise government authority, a State defines itself as a sovereign.\xe2\x80\x9d Gregory v. Ashcroft,501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 452. Arizona engaged in definition of that kind when its people placed both the initiative power and the AIRC\xe2\x80\x99s redistricting authority in the portion of the Arizona Constitution delineating the State\xe2\x80\x99s legislative authority, Ariz. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0IV. The Elections Clause should not be read to single out federal elections as the one area in which States may not use citizen initiatives as an alternative legislative process. And reading the Clause to permit the use of the initiative to control state and local elections but not federal elections would \xe2\x80\x9cdeprive several States of the convenience of having the elections for their own governments and for the national government\xe2\x80\x9d held at the same times and places, and in the same manner. The Federalist No. 61, p. 374 (Hamilton). Pp.\xc2\xa027\xe2\x80\x9330.

(3)\xc2\xa0The Framers may not have imagined the modern initiative process in which the people\xe2\x80\x99s legislative power is coextensive with the state legislature\xe2\x80\x99s authority, but the invention of the initiative was in full harmony with the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s conception of the people as the font of governmental power. It would thus be perverse to interpret \xe2\x80\x9cLegislature\xe2\x80\x9d in the Elections Clause to exclude lawmaking by the people, particularly when such lawmaking is intended to advance the prospect that Members of Congress will in fact be \xe2\x80\x9cchosen .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. by the People of the several States,\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa72. Pp. 30\xe2\x80\x9333.

(4)\xc2\xa0Banning lawmaking by initiative to direct a State\xe2\x80\x99s method of apportioning congressional districts would not just stymie attempts to curb gerrymandering. It would also cast doubt on numerous other time, place, and manner regulations governing federal elections that States have adopted by the initiative method. As well, it could endanger election provisions in state constitutions adopted by conventions and ratified by voters at the ballot box, without involvement or approval by \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature.\xe2\x80\x9d Pp.\xc2\xa033\xe2\x80\x9335.

997 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1047, affirmed.

ARIZONA STATE LEGISLATURE, APPELLANT v. ARIZONA INDEPENDENT REDISTRICTING COMMISSION et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on appeal from the united states district court for the district of arizona

\n

[June 29, 2015]

\n

Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

This case concerns an endeavor by Arizona voters to address the problem of partisan gerrymandering\xe2\x80\x94the drawing of legislative district lines to subordinate adherents of one political party and entrench a rival party in power.[1] \xe2\x80\x9c[P]artisan gerrymanders,\xe2\x80\x9d this Court has recognized, \xe2\x80\x9c[are incompatible] with democratic principles.\xe2\x80\x9d Vieth v. Jubelirer,\n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 267,\n292 (2004)\n (plurality opinion); id., at 316 (Kennedy, J., concurring in judgment). Even so, the Court in Vieth did not grant relief on the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 partisan gerrymander claim. The plurality held the matter nonjusticiable. Id., at 281. Justice Kennedy found no standard workable in that case, but left open the possibility that a suitable standard might be identified in later litigation. Id., at 317.

\n

In 2000, Arizona voters adopted an initiative, Proposition 106, aimed at \xe2\x80\x9cending the practice of gerrymandering and improving voter and candidate participation in elections.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 50. Proposition 106 amended Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s Constitution to remove redistricting authority from the Ari-zona Legislature and vest that authority in an independent commission, the Arizona Independent Redistricting Commission (AIRC or Commission). After the 2010 census,as after the 2000 census, the AIRC adopted redistrict-ing maps for congressional as well as state legislative districts.

\n

The Arizona Legislature challenged the map the Commission adopted in January 2012 for congressional districts. Recognizing that the voters could control redistricting for state legislators, Brief for Appellant 42, 47; Tr. of Oral Arg. 3\xe2\x80\x934, the Arizona Legislature sued the AIRC in federal court seeking a declaration that the Commission and its map for congressional districts violated the \xe2\x80\x9cElections Clause\xe2\x80\x9d of the U.\xc2\xa0S. Constitution. That Clause, critical to the resolution of this case, provides:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe Times, Places and Manner of holding Elections for Senators and Representatives, shall be prescribed in each State by the Legislature thereof; but the Congress may at any time by Law make or alter such Regulations .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa74, cl.\xc2\xa01.

\n

The Arizona Legislature\xe2\x80\x99s complaint alleged that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he word \xe2\x80\x98Legislature\xe2\x80\x99 in the Elections Clause means [specifically and only] the representative body which makes the laws of the people,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 21, \xc2\xb637; so read, the Legislature urges, the Clause precludes resort to an independent commission, created by initiative, to accomplish redistricting. The AIRC responded that, for Elections Clause purposes, \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d is not confined to the elected representatives; rather, the term encompasses all legislative authority conferred by the State Constitution, including initiatives adopted by the people themselves.

\n

A three-judge District Court held, unanimously, that the Arizona Legislature had standing to sue; dividing two to one, the Court rejected the Legislature\xe2\x80\x99s complaint on the merits. We postponed jurisdiction and instructed the parties to address two questions: (1) Does the Arizona Legislature have standing to bring this suit? (2) Do the Elections Clause of the United States Constitution and\n\n2 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72a(c) permit Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s use of a commission to adopt congressional districts? 573 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014).

\n

We now affirm the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment. We hold, first, that the Arizona Legislature, having lost authority to draw congressional districts, has standing to contest the constitutionality of Proposition 106. Next, we hold that lawmaking power in Arizona includes the initiative proc-ess, and that both \xc2\xa72a(c) and the Elections Clause permit use of the AIRC in congressional districting in the same way the Commission is used in districting for Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s own Legislature.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

Direct lawmaking by the people was \xe2\x80\x9cvirtually unknown when the Constitution of 1787 was drafted.\xe2\x80\x9d Donovan & Bowler, An Overview of Direct Democracy in the American States, in Citizens as Legislators\xc2\xa01 (S. Bowler, T. Don-ovan, & C. Tolbert eds. 1998). There were obvious pre-cursors or analogues to the direct lawmaking operative today in several States, notably, New England\xe2\x80\x99s town hall meetings and the submission of early state constitutions to the people for ratification. See Lowell, The Referendum in the United States, in The Initiative, Referendum and Recall 126, 127 (W. Munro ed. 1912) (hereinafter IRR); W. Dodd, The Revision and Amendment of State Constitutions 64\xe2\x80\x9367 (1910).[2] But it was not until the turn of the 20th century, as part of the Progressive agenda of the era, that direct lawmaking by the electorate gained a foothold, largely in Western States. See generally Persily, The Peculiar Geography of Direct Democracy: Why the Initiative, Referendum and Recall Developed in the American West, 2 Mich L. & Pol\xe2\x80\x99y Rev. 11 (1997).

\n

The two main \xe2\x80\x9cagencies of direct legislation\xe2\x80\x9d are the initiative and the referendum. Munro, Introductory, in IRR 8. The initiative operates entirely outside the States\xe2\x80\x99 representative assemblies; it allows \xe2\x80\x9cvoters [to] petition to propose statutes or constitutional amendments to be adopted or rejected by the voters at the polls.\xe2\x80\x9d D. Magleby, Direct Legislation\xc2\xa01 (1984). While the initiative allowsthe electorate to adopt positive legislation, the referendum serves as a negative check. It allows \xe2\x80\x9cvoters [to] petition to refer a legislative action to the voters [for approval or disapproval] at the polls.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. \xe2\x80\x9cThe initiative [thus] corrects sins of omission\xe2\x80\x9d by representative bodies, while the \xe2\x80\x9creferendum corrects sins of commission.\xe2\x80\x9d Johnson, Direct Legislation as an Ally of Representative Government, in IRR 139, 142.

\n

In 1898, South Dakota took the pathmarking step of affirming in its Constitution the people\xe2\x80\x99s power \xe2\x80\x9cdirectly [to] control the making of all ordinary laws\xe2\x80\x9d by initiative and referendum. Introductory, id., at 9. In 1902, Oregon became the first State to adopt the initiative as a means, not only to enact ordinary laws, but also to amend the State\xe2\x80\x99s Constitution. J. Dinan, The American State Constitutional Tradition 62 (2006). By 1920, the people in 19 States had reserved for themselves the power to initiate ordinary lawmaking, and, in 13 States, the power to initiate amendments to the State\xe2\x80\x99s Constitution. Id., at 62, and n. 132, 94, and n. 151. Those numbers increased to 21 and 18, respectively, by the close of the 20th century. Ibid.[3]

\n

B

\n

For the delegates to Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional convention, direct lawmaking was a \xe2\x80\x9cprincipal issu[e].\xe2\x80\x9d J. Leshy, The Arizona State Constitution\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x939 (2d ed. 2013) (hereinafter Leshy). By a margin of more than three to one, the people of Arizona ratified the State\xe2\x80\x99s Constitution, which included, among lawmaking means, initiative and referendum pro-visions. Id., at 14\xe2\x80\x9316, 22. In the runup to Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s admission to the Union in 1912, those provisions generated no controversy. Id., at 22.

\n

In particular, the Arizona Constitution \xe2\x80\x9cestablishes the electorate [of Arizona] as a coordinate source of legislation\xe2\x80\x9d on equal footing with the representative legislative body. Queen Creek Land & Cattle Corp. v. Yavapai Cty. Bd. of Supervisors, 108 Ariz. 449, 451, 501 P.\xc2\xa02d 391, 393 (1972); Cave Creek Unified School Dist. v. Ducey, 233 Ariz. 1, 4, 308 P.\xc2\xa03d 1152, 1155 (2013) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe legislature and electorate share lawmaking power under Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s system of government.\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). The initiative, housed under the article of the Arizona Constitution concerning the \xe2\x80\x9cLegislative Department\xe2\x80\x9d and the section defining the State\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9clegislative authority,\xe2\x80\x9d reserves for the people \xe2\x80\x9cthe power to propose laws and amendments to the constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0IV, pt. 1, \xc2\xa71. The Arizona Constitution further states that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny law which may be enacted by the Legislature under this Constitution may be enacted by the people under the Initiative.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0XXII, \xc2\xa714. Accordingly, \xe2\x80\x9c[g]eneral references to the power of the \xe2\x80\x98legislature\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in the Arizona Constitution \xe2\x80\x9cinclude the people\xe2\x80\x99s right (specified in Article IV, part 1) to bypass their elected representatives and make laws directly through the initiative.\xe2\x80\x9d Leshy xxii.

\n

C

\n

Proposition 106, vesting redistricting authority in the AIRC, was adopted by citizen initiative in 2000 against a \xe2\x80\x9cbackground of recurring redistricting turmoil\xe2\x80\x9d in Arizona. Cain, Redistricting Commissions: A Better Political Buf-fer? 121 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J. 1808, 1831 (2012). Redistricting plans adopted by the Arizona Legislature sparked controversy in every redistricting cycle since the 1970\xe2\x80\x99s, and several of those plans were rejected by a federal court or refused preclearance by the Department of Justice under the Voting Rights Act of 1965. See id., at 1830\xe2\x80\x931832.[4]

\n

Aimed at \xe2\x80\x9cending the practice of gerrymandering and improving voter and candidate participation in elections,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 50, Proposition 106 amended the Arizona Constitution to remove congressional redistricting authority from the state legislature, lodging that authority, instead, in a new entity, the AIRC. Ariz. Const., Art. IV, pt. 2, \xc2\xa71, \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb63\xe2\x80\x9323. The AIRC convenes after each census, establishes final district boundaries, and certifies the new districts to the Arizona Secretary of State. \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb616\xe2\x80\x9317. The legislature may submit nonbinding recommendations to the AIRC, \xc2\xb616, and is required to make necessary appropriations for its operation, \xc2\xb618. The highest ranking officer and minority leader of each chamber of the legislature each select one member of the AIRC from a list compiled by Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s Commission on Appellate Court Appointments. \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb64\xe2\x80\x937. The four appointed members of the AIRC then choose, from the same list, the fifth member, who chairs the Commission. \xc2\xb68. A Commission\xe2\x80\x99s tenure is confined to one redistricting cycle; each member\xe2\x80\x99s time in office \xe2\x80\x9cexpire[s] upon the appointment of the first member of the next redistricting commission.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xb623.

\n

Holders of, or candidates for, public office may not serve on the AIRC, except candidates for or members of a school board. \xc2\xb63. No more than two members of the Commission may be members of the same political party, ibid., and the presiding fifth member cannot be registered with any party already represented on the Commission, \xc2\xb68. Subject to the concurrence of two-thirds of the Arizona Senate, AIRC members may be removed by the Arizona Governor for gross misconduct, substantial neglect of duty, or inability to discharge the duties of office. \xc2\xb610.[5]

\n

Several other States, as a means to curtail partisan gerrymandering, have also provided for the participation of commissions in redistricting. Some States, in common with Arizona, have given nonpartisan or bipartisan commissions binding authority over redistricting.[6] The California Redistricting Commission, established by popular initiative, develops redistricting plans which become effective if approved by public referendum.[7] Still other States have given commissions an auxiliary role, advising the legislatures on redistricting,[8] or serving as a \xe2\x80\x9cbackup\xe2\x80\x9d in the event the State\xe2\x80\x99s representative body fails to complete redistricting.[9] Studies report that nonpartisan and bipartisan commissions generally draw their maps in a timely fashion and create districts both more competitive and more likely to survive legal challenge. See Miller & Grofman, Redistricting Commissions in the Western United States, 3 U. C. Irvine L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 637, 661, 663\xe2\x80\x93664, 666 (2013).

\n

D

\n

On January 17, 2012, the AIRC approved final congressional and state legislative maps based on the 2010 census. See Arizona Independent Redistricting, Final Maps, http://azredistricting.org/Maps/Final-Maps/default.asp (all Internet materials as visited June 25, 2015, and included in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file). Less than four months later, on June 6, 2012, the Arizona Legislature filed suit in the United States District Court for the District of Arizona, naming as defendants the AIRC, its five members, and the Arizona Secretary of State. The Legislature sought both a declaration that Proposition 106 and congressional maps adopted by the AIRC are unconstitutional, and, as affirmative relief, an injunction against use of AIRC maps for any congressional election after the 2012 general election.

\n

A three-judge District Court, convened pursuant to\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72284(a), unanimously denied a motion by the AIRC to dismiss the suit for lack of standing. The Arizona Legislature, the court determined, had \xe2\x80\x9cdemonstrated that its loss of redistricting power constitute[d] a [sufficiently] concrete injury.\xe2\x80\x9d 997 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1047, 1050 (2014). On the merits, dividing two to one, the District Court granted the AIRC\xe2\x80\x99s motion to dismiss the complaint for failure to state a claim. Decisions of this Court, the majority concluded, \xe2\x80\x9cdemonstrate that the word \xe2\x80\x98Legislature\xe2\x80\x99 in the Elections Clause refers to the legislative process used in [a] state, determined by that state\xe2\x80\x99s own constitution and laws.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1054. As the \xe2\x80\x9clawmaking power\xe2\x80\x9d in Arizona \xe2\x80\x9cplainly includes the power to enact laws through initiative,\xe2\x80\x9d the District Court held, the \xe2\x80\x9cElections Clause permits [Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s] establishment and use\xe2\x80\x9d of the Commission. Id., at 1056. Judge Rosenblatt dissented in part. Proposition 106, in his view, unconstitutionally denied \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d of Arizona the \xe2\x80\x9cability to have any outcome-defining effect on the congressional redistricting process.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1058.

\n

We postponed jurisdiction, and now affirm.

\n

II

\n

We turn first to the threshold question: Does the Ari-zona Legislature have standing to bring this suit? Trained on \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the plaintiff is [a] proper party to bring [a particular lawsuit,]\xe2\x80\x9d standing is \xe2\x80\x9c[o]ne element\xe2\x80\x9d of the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s case-or-controversy limitation on federal judicial authority, expressed in Article III of the Constitution. Raines v. Byrd,\n\n521 U.\xc2\xa0S. 811,\n818 (1997)\n. \xe2\x80\x9cTo qual-ify as a party with standing to litigate,\xe2\x80\x9d the Arizona Legislature \xe2\x80\x9cmust show, first and foremost,\xe2\x80\x9d injury in the form of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98invasion of a legally protected interest\xe2\x80\x99 that is \xe2\x80\x98concrete and particularized\xe2\x80\x99 and \xe2\x80\x98actual or imminent.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Arizonans for Official English v. Arizona,\n\n520 U.\xc2\xa0S. 43,\n64 (1997)\n (quoting Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife,\n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 555,\n560 (1992)\n). The Legislature\xe2\x80\x99s injury also must be \xe2\x80\x9cfairly traceable to the challenged action\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9credressable by a favorable ruling.\xe2\x80\x9d Clapper v. Amnesty Int\xe2\x80\x99l USA, 568 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2013) (slip op., at 10) (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

The Arizona Legislature maintains that the Elections Clause vests in it \xe2\x80\x9cprimary responsibility\xe2\x80\x9d for redistricting. Brief for Appellant 51, 53. To exercise that responsibility, the Legislature urges, it must have at least the opportun-ity to engage (or decline to engage) in redistricting before the State may involve other actors in the redistricting process. See id., at 51\xe2\x80\x9353. Proposition 106, which gives the AIRC binding authority over redistricting, regardless of the Legislature\xe2\x80\x99s action or inaction, strips the Legislature of its alleged prerogative to initiate redistricting. That asserted deprivation would be remedied by a court order enjoining the enforcement of Proposition 106. Al-though we conclude that the Arizona Legislature does not have the exclusive, constitutionally guarded role it asserts, see infra, at 24\xe2\x80\x9335, one must not \xe2\x80\x9cconfus[e] weakness on the merits with absence of Article III standing.\xe2\x80\x9d Davis v. United States, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, n.\xc2\xa010 (2011) (slip op., at 19, n.\xc2\xa010); see Warth v. Seldin,\n\n422 U.\xc2\xa0S. 490,\n500 (1975)\n (standing \xe2\x80\x9coften turns on the nature and source of the claim asserted,\xe2\x80\x9d but it \xe2\x80\x9cin no way depends on the merits\xe2\x80\x9d of the claim).

\n

The AIRC argues that the Legislature\xe2\x80\x99s alleged injury is insufficiently concrete to meet the standing requirement absent some \xe2\x80\x9cspecific legislative act that would have taken effect but for Proposition 106.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Appellees 20. The United States, as amicus curiae, urges that even more is needed: the Legislature\xe2\x80\x99s injury will remain speculative, the United States contends, unless and until the Arizona Secretary of State refuses to implement a competing redistricting plan passed by the Legislature. Brief for United States 14\xe2\x80\x9317. In our view, the Arizona Legislature\xe2\x80\x99s suit is not premature, nor is its alleged injury too \xe2\x80\x9cconjectural\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9chypothetical\xe2\x80\x9d to establish standing. Defenders of Wildlife, 504 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 560 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

Two prescriptions of Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s Constitution would render the Legislature\xe2\x80\x99s passage of a competing plan and submission of that plan to the Secretary of State unavailing. Indeed, those actions would directly and immediately conflict with the regime Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s Constitution establishes. Cf. Sporhase v. Nebraska ex rel. Douglas, 458 U.\xc2\xa0S.941, 944, n.\xc2\xa02 (1982) (failure to apply for permit which \xe2\x80\x9cwould not have been granted\xe2\x80\x9d under existing law did not deprive plaintiffs of standing to challenge permitting regime). First, the Arizona Constitution instructs that the Legislature \xe2\x80\x9cshall not have the power to adopt any measure that supersedes [an initiative], in whole or in part, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. unless the superseding measure furthers the purposes\xe2\x80\x9d of the initiative. Art.\xc2\xa0IV, pt. 1, \xc2\xa71(14). Any redistricting map passed by the Legislature in an effort to supersede the AIRC\xe2\x80\x99s map surely would not \xe2\x80\x9cfurthe[r] the purposes\xe2\x80\x9d of Proposition 106. Second, once the AIRC certifies its redistricting plan to the Secretary of State, Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s Constitution requires the Secretary to implement that plan and no other. See Art. IV, pt. 2, \xc2\xa71(17); Arizona Minority Coalition for Fair Redistricting v. Arizona Independent Redistricting Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 211 Ariz. 337, 351, 121 P.\xc2\xa03d 843, 857 (App. 2005) (per curiam) (\xe2\x80\x9cOnce the Commission certifies [its] maps, the secretary of state must use them in conducting the next election.\xe2\x80\x9d). To establish standing, the Legislature need not violate the Arizona Constitution and show that the Secretary of State would similarly disregard the State\xe2\x80\x99s fundamental instrument of government.

\n

Raines v. Byrd,\n\n521 U.\xc2\xa0S. 811 (1997)\n, does not aid AIRC\xe2\x80\x99s argument that there is no standing here. In Raines, this Court held that six individual Members of Congress lacked standing to challenge the Line Item Veto Act. Id., at 813\xe2\x80\x93814, 829\xe2\x80\x93830 (holding specifically and only that \xe2\x80\x9cindividual members of Congress [lack] Article III standing\xe2\x80\x9d). The Act, which gave the President author-ity to cancel certain spending and tax benefit measures after signing them into law, allegedly diluted the efficacy of the Congressmembers\xe2\x80\x99 votes. Id., at 815\xe2\x80\x93817. The \xe2\x80\x9cinstitutional injury\xe2\x80\x9d at issue, we reasoned, scarcely zeroed in on any individual Member. Id., at 821. \xe2\x80\x9c[W]idely dispersed,\xe2\x80\x9d the alleged injury \xe2\x80\x9cnecessarily [impacted] all Members of Congress and both Houses .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. equally.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 829, 821. None of the plaintiffs, therefore, could tenably claim a \xe2\x80\x9cpersonal stake\xe2\x80\x9d in the suit. Id., at 830.

\n

In concluding that the individual Members lacked standing, the Court \xe2\x80\x9cattach[ed] some importance to the fact that [the Raines plaintiffs had] not been authorized to represent their respective Houses of Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 829. \xe2\x80\x9c[I]ndeed,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court observed, \xe2\x80\x9cboth houses actively oppose[d] their suit.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Having failed to prevail in their own Houses, the suitors could not repair to the Judiciary to complain. The Arizona Legislature, in contrast, is an institutional plaintiff asserting an institutional injury, and it commenced this action after authorizing votes in both of its chambers, App. 26\xe2\x80\x9327, 46. That \xe2\x80\x9cdifferent .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. circumstanc[e],\xe2\x80\x9d 521 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 830, was not sub judice in Raines.[10]

\n

Closer to the mark is this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Coleman v. Miller,\n\n307 U.\xc2\xa0S. 433 (1939)\n. There, plaintiffs were 20 (of 40) Kansas State Senators, whose votes \xe2\x80\x9cwould have been sufficient to defeat [a] resolution ratifying [a] proposed [federal] constitutional amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 446.[11] We held they had standing to challenge, as impermissible under Article V of the Federal Constitution, the State Lieutenant Governor\xe2\x80\x99s tie-breaking vote for the amendment. Ibid. Coleman, as we later explained in Raines, stood \xe2\x80\x9cfor the proposition that legislators whose votes would have been sufficient to defeat (or enact) a specific legislative Act have standing to sue if that legislative action goes into effect (or does not go into effect), on the ground that their votes have been completely nullified.\xe2\x80\x9d 521 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 823.[12] Our conclusion that the Arizona Legislature has standing fits that bill. Proposition 106, to-gether with the Arizona Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s ban on efforts to un-dermine the purposes of an initiative, see supra, at 11, would \xe2\x80\x9ccompletely nullif[y]\xe2\x80\x9d any vote by the Legislature, now or \xe2\x80\x9cin the future,\xe2\x80\x9d purporting to adopt a redistricting plan. Raines, 521 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 823\xe2\x80\x93824.[13]

\n

This dispute, in short, \xe2\x80\x9cwill be resolved .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in a concrete factual context conducive to a realistic appreciation of the consequences of judicial action.\xe2\x80\x9d Valley Forge Christian College v. Americans United for Separation of Church and State, Inc.,\n\n454 U.\xc2\xa0S. 464,\n472 (1982)\n.[14] Accordingly, we proceed to the merits.[15]

\n

III

\n

On the merits, we instructed the parties to address this question: Do the Elections Clause of the United States Constitution and\n\n2 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72a(c) permit Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s use of a commission to adopt congressional districts? The Elections Clause is set out at the start of this opinion, supra, at 2. Section 2a(c) provides:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cUntil a State is redistricted in the manner pro-vided by the law thereof after any apportionment, the Representatives to which such State is entitled under such apportionment shall be elected in the following manner: [setting out five federally prescribed redistricting procedures].\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Before focusing directly on the statute and constitutional prescriptions in point, we summarize this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent relating to appropriate state decisionmakers for redistricting purposes. Three decisions compose the relevant case law: Ohio ex rel. Davis v. Hildebrant,\n\n241 U.\xc2\xa0S. 565 (1916)\n; Hawke v. Smith (No. 1),\n\n253 U.\xc2\xa0S. 221 (1920)\n; and Smiley v. Holm,\n\n285 U.\xc2\xa0S. 355 (1932)\n.

\n

A

\n

Davis v. Hildebrant involved an amendment to the Constitution of Ohio vesting in the people the right, exercisable by referendum, to approve or disapprove by popular vote any law enacted by the State\xe2\x80\x99s legislature. A 1915 Act redistricting the State for the purpose of congressional elections had been submitted to a popular vote, resulting in disapproval of the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s measure. State election officials asked the State\xe2\x80\x99s Supreme Court to declare the referendum void. That court rejected the request, holding that the referendum authorized by Ohio\xe2\x80\x99s Constitution, \xe2\x80\x9cwas a part of the legislative power of the State,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cnothing in [federal statutory law] or in [the Elections Clause] operated to the contrary.\xe2\x80\x9d 241 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 567. This Court affirmed the Ohio Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment. In upholding the state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision, we recognized that the referendum was \xe2\x80\x9cpart of the legislative power\xe2\x80\x9d in Ohio, ibid., legitimately exercised by the people to disapprove the legislation creating congressional districts. For redistricting purposes, Hildebrant thus established, \xe2\x80\x9cthe Leg-islature\xe2\x80\x9d did not mean the representative body alone. Rather, the word encompassed a veto power lodged in the people. See id., at 569 (Elections Clause does not bar \xe2\x80\x9ctreating the referendum as part of the legislative power for the purpose of apportionment, where so ordained by the state constitutions and laws\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Hawke v. Smith involved the\n \nEighteenth Amendment to the Federal Constitution. Ohio\xe2\x80\x99s Legislature had ratified the Amendment, and a referendum on that ratification was at issue. Reversing the Ohio Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision upholding the referendum, we held that \xe2\x80\x9cratification by a State of a constitutional amendment is not an act of legislation within the proper sense of the word.\xe2\x80\x9d 253 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 229. Instead, Article V governing ratification had lodged in \xe2\x80\x9cthe legislatures of three-fourths of the several States\xe2\x80\x9d sole authority to assent to a proposed amendment. Id., at 226. The Court contrasted the ratifying function, exercisable exclusively by a State\xe2\x80\x99s legislature, with \xe2\x80\x9cthe ordinary business of legislation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 229. Davis v. Hildebrant, the Court explained, involved the enactment of legislation, i.e., a redistricting plan, and properly held that \xe2\x80\x9cthe referendum [was] part of the legislative author-ity of the State for [that] purpose.\xe2\x80\x9d 253 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 230.

\n

Smiley v. Holm raised the question whether legislation purporting to redistrict Minnesota for congressional elections was subject to the Governor\xe2\x80\x99s veto. The Minnesota Supreme Court had held that the Elections Clause placed redistricting authority exclusively in the hands of the State\xe2\x80\x99s legislature, leaving no role for the Governor. We reversed that determination and held, for the purpose at hand, Minnesota\xe2\x80\x99s legislative authority includes not just the two houses of the legislature; it includes, in addition, a make-or-break role for the Governor. In holding that the Governor\xe2\x80\x99s veto counted, we distinguished instances in which the Constitution calls upon state legislatures to exercise a function other than lawmaking. State legislatures, we pointed out, performed an \xe2\x80\x9celectoral\xe2\x80\x9d function \xe2\x80\x9cin the choice of United States Senators under Article I, section 3, prior to the adoption of the\n \nSeventeenth Amendment,\xe2\x80\x9d[16] a \xe2\x80\x9cratifying\xe2\x80\x9d function for \xe2\x80\x9cproposed amendments to the Constitution under Article V,\xe2\x80\x9d as explained in Hawke v. Smith, and a \xe2\x80\x9cconsenting\xe2\x80\x9d function \xe2\x80\x9cin relation to the acquisition of lands by the United States under Article I, section 8, paragraph 17.\xe2\x80\x9d 285 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 365\xe2\x80\x93366.

\n

In contrast to those other functions, we observed, redistricting \xe2\x80\x9cinvolves lawmaking in its essential features and most important aspect.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 366. Lawmaking, we further noted, ordinarily \xe2\x80\x9cmust be in accordance with the method which the State has prescribed for legislative enactments.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 367. In Minnesota, the State\xe2\x80\x99s Constitution had made the Governor \xe2\x80\x9cpart of the legislative process.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 369. And the Elections Clause, we explained, respected the State\xe2\x80\x99s choice to include the Governor in that process, although the Governor could play no part when the Constitution assigned to \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d a ratifying, electoral, or consenting function. Nothing in the Elections Clause, we said, \xe2\x80\x9cattempt[ed] to endow the legislature of the State with power to enact laws in any manner other than that in which the constitution of the State ha[d] provided that laws shall be enacted.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 368.

\n

The Chief Justice, in dissent, features, indeed trumpets repeatedly, the pre-\n \nSeventeenth Amendment regime in which Senators were \xe2\x80\x9cchosen [in each State] by the Legislature thereof.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa73; see post, at 1, 8\xe2\x80\x939, 19. If we are right, he asks, why did popular election proponents resort to the amending process instead of simply interpreting \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d to mean \xe2\x80\x9cthe people\xe2\x80\x9d? Post, at 1. Smiley, as just indicated, answers that question. Article I, \xc2\xa73, gave state legislatures \xe2\x80\x9ca function different from that of lawgiver,\xe2\x80\x9d 285 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 365; it made each of them \xe2\x80\x9can electoral body\xe2\x80\x9d charged to perform that function to the exclusion of other participants, ibid. So too, of the ratifying function. As we explained in Hawke, \xe2\x80\x9cthe power to legislate in the enactment of the laws of a State is derived from the people of the State.\xe2\x80\x9d 253 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 230. Ratification, however, \xe2\x80\x9chas its source in the Federal Constitution\xe2\x80\x9d and is not \xe2\x80\x9can act of legislation within the proper sense of the word.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 229\xe2\x80\x93230.

\n

Constantly resisted by The Chief Justice, but well understood in opinions that speak for the Court: \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he meaning of the word \xe2\x80\x98legislature,\xe2\x80\x99 used several times in the Federal Constitution, differs according to the connection in which it is employed, depend[ent] upon the character of the function which that body in each instance is called upon to exercise.\xe2\x80\x9d Atlantic Cleaners & Dyers, Inc. v. United States,\n\n286 U.\xc2\xa0S. 427,\n434 (1932)\n (citing Smiley, 285U.\xc2\xa0S. 355). Thus \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d comprises the referendum and the Governor\xe2\x80\x99s veto in the context of regulating congressional elections. Hildebrant, see supra, at 15\xe2\x80\x9316; Smiley, see supra, at 17\xe2\x80\x9318. In the context of ratifying constitutional amendments, in contrast, \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d has a different identity, one that excludes the referendum and the Governor\xe2\x80\x99s veto. Hawke, see supra, at 16.[17]

\n

In sum, our precedent teaches that redistricting is a legislative function, to be performed in accordance with the State\xe2\x80\x99s prescriptions for lawmaking, which may include the referendum and the Governor\xe2\x80\x99s veto. The exercise of the initiative, we acknowledge, was not at issue in our prior decisions. But as developed below, we see no constitutional barrier to a State\xe2\x80\x99s empowerment of its people by embracing that form of lawmaking.

\n

B

\n

We take up next the statute the Court asked the parties to address,\n\n2 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72a(c), a measure modeled on the Reapportionment Act Congress passed in 1911, Act of Aug. 8 (1911 Act), ch. 5, \xc2\xa74,\n\n37Stat.\n14. Section 2a(c), we hold, permits use of a commission to adopt Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s congressional districts. See supra, at 15.[18]

\n

From 1862 through 1901, the decennial congressional apportionment Acts provided that a State would be required to follow federally prescribed procedures for redistricting unless \xe2\x80\x9cthe legislature\xe2\x80\x9d of the State drew district lines. E.g., Act of July 14, 1862, ch. 170,\n\n12Stat.\n572; Act of Jan. 16, 1901, ch. 93, \xc2\xa74,\n\n31Stat.\n734. In drafting the 1911 Act, Congress focused on the fact that several States had supplemented the representative legislature mode of lawmaking with a direct lawmaking role for the people, through the processes of initiative (positive legislation by the electorate) and referendum (approval or disapproval of legislation by the electorate). 47 Cong. Rec. 3508 (statement of Sen. Burton); see supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x935. To accommodate that development, the 1911 Act eliminated the statutory reference to redistricting by the state \xe2\x80\x9clegislature\xe2\x80\x9d and instead directed that, if a State\xe2\x80\x99s apportionment of Representatives increased, the State should use the Act\xe2\x80\x99s default procedures for redistricting \xe2\x80\x9cuntil such State shall be redistricted in the manner provided by the laws thereof.\xe2\x80\x9d Ch. 5, \xc2\xa74,\n\n37Stat.\n14 (emphasis added).[19]

\n

Some Members of Congress questioned whether the language change was needed. In their view, existing apportionment legislation (referring to redistricting by a State\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9clegislature\xe2\x80\x9d) \xe2\x80\x9csuffic[ed] to allow, whatever the law of the State may be, the people of that State to control [redistricting].\xe2\x80\x9d 47 Cong. Rec. 3507 (statement of Sen. Shively); cf. Shiel v. Thayer, Bartlett Contested Election Cases, H.\xc2\xa0R. Misc. Doc. No. 57, 38th Cong., 2d Sess., 351 (1861) (view of House Committee of Elections Member Dawes that Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa74\xe2\x80\x99s reference to \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d meant simply the \xe2\x80\x9cconstituted authorities, through whom [the State] choose[s] to speak,\xe2\x80\x9d prime among them, the State\xe2\x80\x99s Constitution, \xe2\x80\x9cwhich rises above .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. all legislative action\xe2\x80\x9d). Others anticipated that retaining the reference to \xe2\x80\x9cthe legislature\xe2\x80\x9d would \xe2\x80\x9ccondem[n] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any [redistricting] legislation by referendum or by initiative.\xe2\x80\x9d 47 Cong. Rec. 3436 (statement of Sen. Burton). In any event, proponents of the change maintained, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n view of the very serious evils arising from gerrymanders,\xe2\x80\x9d Congress should not \xe2\x80\x9ctake any chances in [the] matter.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 3508 (same). \xe2\x80\x9c[D]ue respect to the rights, to the established methods, and to the laws of the respective States,\xe2\x80\x9d they urged, required Congress \xe2\x80\x9cto allow them to establish congressional districts in whatever way they may have provided by their constitution and by their statutes.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 3436; see id., at 3508 (statement of Sen. Works).

\n

As this Court observed in Hildebrant, \xe2\x80\x9cthe legislative history of th[e] [1911 Act] leaves no room for doubt [about why] the prior words were stricken out and the new words inserted.\xe2\x80\x9d 241 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 568. The change was made to safeguard to \xe2\x80\x9ceach State full authority to employ in the creation of congressional districts its own laws and regulations.\xe2\x80\x9d 47 Cong. Rec. 3437 (statement of Sen. Burton). The 1911 Act, in short, left the question of redistricting \xe2\x80\x9cto the laws and methods of the States. If they include initiative, it is included.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 3508.

\n

While the 1911 Act applied only to reapportionment following the 1910 census, see Wood v. Broom,\n\n287 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n\xe2\x80\x937 (1932), Congress used virtually identical language when it enacted \xc2\xa72a(c) in 1941. See Act of Nov. 15, 1941, ch. 470,\n\n55Stat.\n761\xe2\x80\x93762. Section 2a(c) sets forth congressional-redistricting procedures operative only if the State, \xe2\x80\x9cafter any apportionment,\xe2\x80\x9d had not redistricted \xe2\x80\x9cin the manner provided by the law thereof.\xe2\x80\x9d The 1941 provision, like the 1911 Act, thus accorded full respect to the redistricting procedures adopted by the States. So long as a State has \xe2\x80\x9credistricted in the manner provided by the law thereof\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94as Arizona did by utilizing the independent commission procedure called for by its Constitution\xe2\x80\x94the resulting redistricting plan becomes the presumptively governing map.[20]

\n

The Arizona Legislature characterizes \xc2\xa72a(c) as an \xe2\x80\x9cobscure provision, narrowed by subsequent developments to the brink of irrelevance.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Appellant 56. True, four of the five default redistricting procedures\xe2\x80\x94operative only when a State is not \xe2\x80\x9credistricted in the manner provided by [state] law\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94had \xe2\x80\x9cbecome (because of postenactment decisions of this Court) in virtually all situations plainly unconstitutional.\xe2\x80\x9d Branch v. Smith,\n\n538 U.\xc2\xa0S. 254\n\xe2\x80\x93274 (2003) (plurality opinion). Concretely, the default procedures specified in \xc2\xa72a(c)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(4) contemplate that a State would continue to use pre-existing districts following a new census. The one-person, one-vote principle announced in Wesberry v. Sanders,\n\n376 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1 (1964)\n, however, would bar those procedures, except in the \xe2\x80\x9cunlikely\xe2\x80\x9d event that \xe2\x80\x9cthe decennial census makes no districting change constitutionally necessary,\xe2\x80\x9d Branch, 538 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 273 (plurality opinion).

\n

Constitutional infirmity in \xc2\xa72a(c)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(4)\xe2\x80\x99s default procedures, however, does not bear on the question whether a State has been \xe2\x80\x9credistricted in the manner provided by [state] law.\xe2\x80\x9d[21] As just observed, Congress expressly directed that when a State has been \xe2\x80\x9credistricted in the manner provided by [state] law\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94whether by the legislature, court decree (see id., at 274), or a commission established by the people\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of the initiative\xe2\x80\x94the resulting districts are the ones that presumptively will be used to elect Representatives.[22]

\n

There can be no dispute that Congress itself may draw a State\xe2\x80\x99s congressional-district boundaries. See Vieth, 541 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 275 (plurality opinion) (stating that the Elections Clause \xe2\x80\x9cpermit[s] Congress to \xe2\x80\x98make or alter\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9cdistricts for federal elections\xe2\x80\x9d). The Arizona Legislature urges that the first part of the Elections Clause, vesting power to regulate congressional elections in State \xe2\x80\x9cLegislature[s],\xe2\x80\x9d precludes Congress from allowing a State to redistrict without the involvement of its representative body, even if Congress independently could enact the same redistricting plan under its plenary authority to \xe2\x80\x9cmake or alter\xe2\x80\x9d the State\xe2\x80\x99s plan. See Brief for Appellant 56\xe2\x80\x9357; Reply Brief 17. In other words, the Arizona Legislature regards \xc2\xa72a(c) as a futile exercise. The Congresses that passed \xc2\xa72a(c) and its forerunner, the 1911 Act, did not share that wooden interpretation of the Clause, nor do we. Any uncertainty about the import of \xc2\xa72a(c), however, is resolved by our holding that the Elections Clause permits regulation of congressional elections by initiative, see infra, at 24\xe2\x80\x9335, leaving no arguable conflict between \xc2\xa72a(c) and the first part of the Clause.

\n

C

\n

In accord with the District Court, see supra, at 9, we hold that the Elections Clause permits the people of Arizona to provide for redistricting by independent commission. To restate the key question in this case, the issue centrally debated by the parties: Absent congressional authorization, does the Elections Clause preclude the people of Arizona from creating a commission operating independently of the state legislature to establish congressional districts? The history and purpose of the Clause weigh heavily against such preclusion, as does the animating principle of our Constitution that the people themselves are the originating source of all the powers ofgovernment.

\n

We note, preliminarily, that dictionaries, even those in circulation during the founding era, capaciously define the word \xe2\x80\x9clegislature.\xe2\x80\x9d Samuel Johnson defined \xe2\x80\x9clegislature\xe2\x80\x9d simply as \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he power that makes laws.\xe2\x80\x9d 2 A Dictionary of the English Language (1st ed. 1755); ibid. (6th ed. 1785); ibid. (10th ed. 1792); ibid. (12th ed. 1802). Thomas Sheridan\xe2\x80\x99s dictionary defined \xe2\x80\x9clegislature\xe2\x80\x9d exactly as Dr. Johnson did: \xe2\x80\x9cThe power that makes laws.\xe2\x80\x9d 2 A Complete Dictionary of the English Language (4th ed. 1797). Noah Webster defined the term precisely that way as well. Compendious Dictionary of the English Language 174 (1806). And Nathan Bailey similarly defined \xe2\x80\x9clegislature\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cthe Authority of making Laws, or Power which makes them.\xe2\x80\x9d An Universal Etymological English Dictionary (20th ed. 1763).[23]

\n

As to the \xe2\x80\x9cpower that makes laws\xe2\x80\x9d in Arizona, initiatives adopted by the voters legislate for the State just as measures passed by the representative body do. See Ariz. Const., Art. IV, pt. 1, \xc2\xa71 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe legislative authority of the state shall be vested in the legislature, consisting of a senate and a house of representatives, but the people reserve the power to propose laws and amendments to the constitution and to enact or reject such laws and amendments at the polls, independently of the legislature.\xe2\x80\x9d). See also Eastlake v. Forest City Enterprises, Inc.,\n\n426 U.\xc2\xa0S. 668,\n672 (1976)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cIn establishing legislative bodies, the people can reserve to themselves power to deal directly with matters which might otherwise be assigned to the legislature.\xe2\x80\x9d). As well in Arizona, the people may delegate their legislative authority over redistricting to an independent commission just as the representative body may choose to do. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 15\xe2\x80\x9316 (answering the Court\xe2\x80\x99s question, may the Arizona Legislature itself establish a commission to attend to redistricting, counsel for appellant responded yes, state legislatures may delegate their authority to a commission, subject to their prerogative to reclaim the authority for themselves).

\n

1

\n

The dominant purpose of the Elections Clause, the historical record bears out, was to empower Congress to override state election rules, not to restrict the way States enact legislation. As this Court explained in Arizona v. Inter Tribal Council of Ariz., Inc.,\n\n570 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1 (2013)\n, the Clause \xe2\x80\x9cwas the Framers\xe2\x80\x99 insurance against the possibility that a State would refuse to provide for the election of representatives to the Federal Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 5) (citing The Federalist No. 59, pp. 362\xe2\x80\x93363 (C. Rossiter ed. 1961) (A. Hamilton)).

\n

The Clause was also intended to act as a safeguard against manipulation of electoral rules by politicians and factions in the States to entrench themselves or place their interests over those of the electorate. As Madison urged, without the Elections Clause, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]henever the State Legislatures had a favorite measure to carry, they would take care so to mould their regulations as to favor the candidates they wished to succeed.\xe2\x80\x9d 2 Records of the Federal Convention 241 (M. Farrand rev. 1966). Madison spoke in response to a motion by South Carolina\xe2\x80\x99s delegates to strike out the federal power. Those delegates so moved because South Carolina\xe2\x80\x99s coastal elite had malapportioned their legislature, and wanted to retain the ability to do so. See J. Rakove, Original Meanings: Politics and Ideas in the Making of the Constitution 223\xe2\x80\x93224 (1996). The problem Madison identified has hardly lessened over time. Conflict of interest is inherent when \xe2\x80\x9clegislators dra[w] district lines that they ultimately have to run in.\xe2\x80\x9d Cain, 121 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J., at 1817.

\n

Arguments in support of congressional control under the Elections Clause were reiterated in the public debate over ratification. Theophilus Parsons, a delegate at the Massachusetts ratifying convention, warned that \xe2\x80\x9cwhen faction and party spirit run high,\xe2\x80\x9d a legislature might take actions like \xe2\x80\x9cmak[ing] an unequal and partial division of the states into districts for the election of representatives.\xe2\x80\x9d Debate in Massachusetts Ratifying Convention (16\xe2\x80\x9317, 21 Jan. 1788), in 2 The Founders\xe2\x80\x99 Constitution 256 (P. Kurland & R. Lerner eds. 1987). Timothy Pickering of Massachusetts similarly urged that the Clause was necessary because \xe2\x80\x9cthe State governments may abuse their power, and regulate .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. elections in such manner as would be highly inconvenient to the people.\xe2\x80\x9d Letter to Charles Tillinghast (24 Dec. 1787), in id., at 253. He described the Clause as a way to \xe2\x80\x9censure to the people their rights of election.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

While attention focused on potential abuses by state-level politicians, and the consequent need for congres-sional oversight, the legislative processes by which the States could exercise their initiating role in regulating congressional elections occasioned no debate. That is hardly surprising. Recall that when the Constitution was composed in Philadelphia and later ratified, the people\xe2\x80\x99s legislative prerogatives\xe2\x80\x94the initiative and the referendum\xe2\x80\x94were not yet in our democracy\xe2\x80\x99s arsenal. See supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x935. The Elections Clause, however, is not reasonably read to disarm States from adopting modes of legislation that place the lead rein in the people\xe2\x80\x99s hands.[24]

\n

2

\n

The Arizona Legislature maintains that, by specifying \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature thereof,\xe2\x80\x9d the Elections Clause renders the State\xe2\x80\x99s representative body the sole \xe2\x80\x9ccomponent of state government authorized to prescribe .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. regulations .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. for congressional redistricting.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Appellant 30. The Chief Justice, in dissent, agrees. But it is characteristic of our federal system that States retain autonomy to establish their own governmental processes. See Alden v. Maine,\n\n527 U.\xc2\xa0S. 706,\n752 (1999)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cA State is entitled to order the processes of its own governance.\xe2\x80\x9d); The Federalist No. 43, at 272 (J. Madison) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhenever the States may choose to substitute other republican forms, they have a right to do so.\xe2\x80\x9d). \xe2\x80\x9cThrough the structure of its government, and the character of those who exercise government authority, a State defines itself as a sovereign.\xe2\x80\x9d Gregory v. Ashcroft,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 452,\n460 (1991)\n. Arizona engaged in definition of that kind when its people placed both the initiative power and the AIRC\xe2\x80\x99s redistricting authority in the portion of the Arizona Constitution delineating the State\xe2\x80\x99s legislative authority. See Ariz. Const., Art. IV; supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

This Court has \xe2\x80\x9clong recognized the role of the States as laboratories for devising solutions to difficult legal problems.\xe2\x80\x9d Oregon v. Ice,\n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 160,\n171 (2009)\n; see United States v. Lopez,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 549,\n581 (1995)\n (Kennedy, J., concurring) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he States may perform their role as lab-oratories for experimentation to devise various solutions where the best solution is far from clear.\xe2\x80\x9d); New State Ice Co. v. Liebmann,\n\n285 U.\xc2\xa0S. 262,\n311 (1932)\n (Brandeis, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is one of the happy incidents of the federal system that a single courageous State may, if its citizens choose, serve as a laboratory; and try novel social and economic experiments without risk to the rest of the country.\xe2\x80\x9d). Deference to state lawmaking \xe2\x80\x9callows local policies \xe2\x80\x98more sensitive to the diverse needs of a heterogeneous society,\xe2\x80\x99 permits \xe2\x80\x98innovation and experimentation,\xe2\x80\x99 enables greater citizen \xe2\x80\x98involvement in democratic processes,\xe2\x80\x99 and makes government \xe2\x80\x98more responsive by putting the States in competition for a mobile citizenry.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Bond v. United States, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 9) (quoting Gregory, 501 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 458).

\n

We resist reading the Elections Clause to single out federal elections as the one area in which States may not use citizen initiatives as an alternative legislative process. Nothing in that Clause instructs, nor has this Court ever held, that a state legislature may prescribe regulations on the time, place, and manner of holding federal elections in defiance of provisions of the State\xe2\x80\x99s constitution. See Shiel, H.\xc2\xa0R. Misc. Doc. No. 57, at 349\xe2\x80\x93352 (concluding that Oregon\xe2\x80\x99s Constitution prevailed over any conflicting leg-islative measure setting the date for a congressionalelection).

\n

The Chief Justice, in dissent, maintains that, under the Elections Clause, the state legislature can trump any initiative-introduced constitutional provision regulating federal elections. He extracts support for this position from Baldwin v. Trowbridge, 2 Bartlett Contested Election Cases, H.\xc2\xa0R. Misc. Doc. No. 152, 41st Cong., 2d Sess., 46\xe2\x80\x9347 (1866). See post, at 15\xe2\x80\x9316. There, Michigan voters had amended the State Constitution to require votes to be cast within a resident\xe2\x80\x99s township or ward. The Michigan Legislature, however, passed a law permitting soldiers to vote in other locations. One candidate would win if the State Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s requirement controlled; his opponent would prevail under the Michigan Legislature\xe2\x80\x99s prescription. The House Elections Committee, in a divided vote, ruled that, under the Elections Clause, the Michigan Legislature had the paramount power.

\n

As the minority report in Baldwin pointed out, however, the Supreme Court of Michigan had reached the opposite conclusion, holding, as courts generally do, that state legislation in direct conflict with the State\xe2\x80\x99s constitution is void. Baldwin, H.\xc2\xa0R. Misc. Doc. No. 152, at 50. The Baldwin majority\xe2\x80\x99s ruling, furthermore, appears in tension with the Election Committee\xe2\x80\x99s unanimous decision in Shiel just five years earlier. (The Committee, we repeat, \xe2\x80\x9cha[d] no doubt that the constitution of the State ha[d] fixed, beyond the control of the legislature, the time for holding [a congressional] election.\xe2\x80\x9d Shiel, H.\xc2\xa0R. Misc. Doc. No. 57, at 351.) Finally, it was perhaps not entirely accidental that the candidate the Committee declared winner in Baldwin belonged to the same political party as all but one member of the House Committee majority responsible for the decision. See U.\xc2\xa0S. House of Representatives Congress Profiles: 39th Congress (1865\xe2\x80\x931867), http://history\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0house\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0gov\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0Congressional-Overview\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0Profiles\xc2\xa0/39th/;Biographical Directory of the United States Cong-ress: Trowbridge, Rowland Ebenezer (1821\xe2\x80\x931881). Cf. Cain, 121 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J., at 1817 (identifying legislativeconflict of interest as the problem independent re-districting commissions aimed to check). In short, Baldwin is not a disposition that should attract this Court\xe2\x80\x99s reliance.

\n

We add, furthermore, that the Arizona Legislature does not question, nor could it, employment of the initiative to control state and local elections. In considering whether Article I, \xc2\xa74, really says \xe2\x80\x9cNo\xe2\x80\x9d to similar control of federal elections, we have looked to, and borrow from, Alexander Hamilton\xe2\x80\x99s counsel: \xe2\x80\x9c[I]t would have been hardly advisable .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to establish, as a fundamental point, what would deprive several States of the convenience of having the elections for their own governments and for the national government\xe2\x80\x9d held at the same times and places, and in the same manner. The Federalist No. 61, at 374. The Elections Clause is not sensibly read to subject States to that deprivation.[25]

\n

3

\n

The Framers may not have imagined the modern initiative process in which the people of a State exercise legislative power coextensive with the authority of an institutional legislature. But the invention of the initiative was in full harmony with the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s conception of the people as the font of governmental power. As Madison put it: \xe2\x80\x9cThe genius of republican liberty seems to demand .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. not only that all power should be derived from the people, but that those intrusted with it should be kept in dependence on the people.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., No. 37, at 223.

\n

The people\xe2\x80\x99s ultimate sovereignty had been expressed by John Locke in 1690, a near century before the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s formation:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he Legislative being only a Fiduciary Power to act for certain ends, there remains still in the People a Supream Power to remove or alter the Legislative, when they find the Legislative act contrary to the trust reposed in them. For all Power given with trust for the attaining an end, being limited by that end, whenever that end is manifestly neglected, or opposed, the trust must necessarily be forfeited, and the Power devolve into the hands of those that gave it, who may place it anew where they shall think best for their safety and security.\xe2\x80\x9d Two Treatises of Government \xc2\xa7149, p. 385 (P. Laslett ed. 1964).

\n

Our Declaration of Independence, \xc2\xb62, drew from Locke in stating: \xe2\x80\x9cGovernments are instituted among Men, deriving their just powers from the consent of the governed.\xe2\x80\x9d And our fundamental instrument of government derives its authority from \xe2\x80\x9cWe the People.\xe2\x80\x9d U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Preamble. As this Court stated, quoting Hamilton: \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he true principle of a republic is, that the people should choose whom they please to govern them.\xe2\x80\x9d Powell v. McCormack,\n\n395 U.\xc2\xa0S. 486\n\xe2\x80\x93541 (1969) (quoting 2 Debates on the Federal Constitution 257 (J. Elliot ed. 1876)). In this light, it would be perverse to interpret the term \xe2\x80\x9cLegislature\xe2\x80\x9d in the Elections Clause so as to exclude lawmaking by the people, particularly where such lawmaking is intended to check legislators\xe2\x80\x99 ability to choose the district lines they run in, thereby advancing the prospect that Members of Congress will in fact be \xe2\x80\x9cchosen .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. by the People of the several States,\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa72. See Cain, 121 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J., at 1817.

\n

The Chief Justice, in dissent, suggests that independent commissions established by initiative are a high-minded experiment that has failed. Post, at 26\xe2\x80\x9327. For this assessment, The Chief Justice cites a three-judge Federal District Court opinion, Harris v. Arizona Independent Redistricting Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 993 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1042 (Ariz. 2014). That opinion, he asserts, \xe2\x80\x9cdetail[s] the partisanship that has affected the Commission.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 26. No careful reader could so conclude.

\n

The report of the decision in Harris comprises a per curiam opinion, an opinion concurring in the judgment by Judge Silver, and a dissenting opinion by Judge Wake. The per curiam opinion found \xe2\x80\x9cin favor of the Commission.\xe2\x80\x9d 993 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1080. Deviations from the one-person, one-vote principle, the per curiam opinion explained at length, were \xe2\x80\x9csmall\xe2\x80\x9d and, in the main, could not be attributed to partisanship. Ibid. While partisanship \xe2\x80\x9cmay have played some role,\xe2\x80\x9d the per curiam opinion stated, deviations were \xe2\x80\x9cpredominantly a result of the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s good-faith efforts to achieve preclearance under the Voting Rights Act.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1060. Judge Silver, although she joined the per curiam opinion, made clear at the very outset of that opinion her finding that \xe2\x80\x9cpartisanship did not play a role.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1046, n.\xc2\xa01. In her concurring opinion, she repeated her finding that the evidence did not show partisanship at work, id., at 1087; instead, she found, the evidence \xe2\x80\x9c[was] overwhelming [that] the final map was a product of the commissioners\xe2\x80\x99s consideration of appropriate redistricting criteria.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1088. To describe Harris as a decision criticizing the Commission for pervasive partisanship, post, at 26, The Chief Justice could rely only upon the dissenting opinion, which expressed views the majority roundly rejected.

\n

Independent redistricting commissions, it is true, \xe2\x80\x9chave not eliminated the inevitable partisan suspicions associ-ated with political line-drawing.\xe2\x80\x9d Cain, 121 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J., at 1808. But \xe2\x80\x9cthey have succeeded to a great degree [in limiting the conflict of interest implicit in legislative control over redistricting].\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. They thus impede legislators from choosing their voters instead of facilitating the voters\xe2\x80\x99 choice of their representatives.

\n

4

\n

Banning lawmaking by initiative to direct a State\xe2\x80\x99s method of apportioning congressional districts would do more than stymie attempts to curb partisan gerrymandering, by which the majority in the legislature draws district lines to their party\xe2\x80\x99s advantage. It would also cast doubt on numerous other election laws adopted by the initiative method of legislating.

\n

The people, in several States, functioning as the lawmaking body for the purpose at hand, have used the initiative to install a host of regulations governing the \xe2\x80\x9cTimes, Places and Manner\xe2\x80\x9d of holding federal elections. Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa74. For example, the people of California provided for permanent voter registration, specifying that \xe2\x80\x9cno amendment by the Legislature shall provide for a general biennial or other periodic reregistration of voters.\xe2\x80\x9d Cal. Elec. Code Ann. \xc2\xa72123 (West 2003). The people of Ohio banned ballots providing for straight-ticket voting along party lines. Ohio Const., Art.\xc2\xa0V, \xc2\xa72a. The people of Oregon shortened the deadline for voter registration to 20 days prior to an election. Ore. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa72. None of those measures permit the state legislatures to override the people\xe2\x80\x99s prescriptions. The Arizona Legislature\xe2\x80\x99s theory\xe2\x80\x94that the lead role in regulating federal elections cannot be wrested from \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature,\xe2\x80\x9d and vested in commissions initiated by the people\xe2\x80\x94would endanger all of them.

\n

The list of endangered state elections laws, were we to sustain the position of the Arizona Legislature, would not stop with popular initiatives. Almost all state constitutions were adopted by conventions and ratified by voters at the ballot box, without involvement or approval by \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature.\xe2\x80\x9d[26] Core aspects of the electoral process regulated by state constitutions include voting by \xe2\x80\x9cballot\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9csecret ballot,\xe2\x80\x9d[27] voter registration,[28] absentee voting,[29] vote counting,[30] and victory thresholds.[31] Again, the States\xe2\x80\x99 legislatures had no hand in making these laws and may not alter or amend them.

\n

The importance of direct democracy as a means to control election regulations extends beyond the particular statutes and constitutional provisions installed by the people rather than the States\xe2\x80\x99 legislatures. The very prospect of lawmaking by the people may influence the legislature when it considers (or fails to consider) election-related measures. See Persily & Anderson, Regulating Democracy Through Democracy: The Use of Direct Legislation in Election Law Reform, 78 S. Cal. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 997, 1006\xe2\x80\x931008 (2005) (describing cases in which \xe2\x80\x9cindirect pressure of the initiative process .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. was sufficient to spur [state] legislature[s] to action\xe2\x80\x9d). Turning the coin, the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s responsiveness to the people its members represent is hardly heightened when the representative body can be confident that what it does will not be overturned or modified by the voters themselves.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

Invoking the Elections Clause, the Arizona Legislature instituted this lawsuit to disempower the State\xe2\x80\x99s voters from serving as the legislative power for redistricting purposes. But the Clause surely was not adopted to diminish a State\xe2\x80\x99s authority to determine its own lawmaking processes. Article I, \xc2\xa74, stems from a different view. Both parts of the Elections Clause are in line with the fundamental premise that all political power flows from the people. McCulloch v. Maryland, 4 Wheat. 316, 404\xe2\x80\x93405 (1819). So comprehended, the Clause doubly empowers the people. They may control the State\xe2\x80\x99s lawmaking processes in the first instance, as Arizona voters have done, and they may seek Congress\xe2\x80\x99 correction of regulations prescribed by state legislatures.

\n

The people of Arizona turned to the initiative to curb the practice of gerrymandering and, thereby, to ensure that Members of Congress would have \xe2\x80\x9can habitual recollection of their dependence on the people.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 57, at 350 (J. Madison). In so acting, Arizona voters sought to restore \xe2\x80\x9cthe core principle of republican government,\xe2\x80\x9d namely, \xe2\x80\x9cthat the voters should choose their representatives, not the other way around.\xe2\x80\x9d Berman, Managing Gerrymandering, 83 Texas L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 781 (2005). The Elections Clause does not hinder that endeavor.

\n

For the reasons stated, the judgment of the United States District Court for the District of Arizona is

\n

Affirmed.

ARIZONA STATE LEGISLATURE, APPELLANT v. ARIZONA INDEPENDENT REDISTRICTING COMMISSION et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on appeal from the united states district court for the district of arizona

\n

[June 29, 2015]

\n

\n

Justice Scalia, with whom Justice Thomas joins, dissenting.

\n

I do not believe that the question the Court answers is properly before us. Disputes between governmental branches or departments regarding the allocation of political power do not in my view constitute \xe2\x80\x9ccases\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ccontroversies\xe2\x80\x9d committed to our resolution by Art. III, \xc2\xa72, of the Constitution.

\n

What those who framed and ratified the Constitution had in mind when they entrusted the \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial Power\xe2\x80\x9d to a separate and coequal branch of the Federal Government was the judicial power they were familiar with\xe2\x80\x94that traditionally exercised by English and American courts. The \xe2\x80\x9ccases\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ccontroversies\xe2\x80\x9d that those courts entertained did not include suits between units of government regarding their legitimate powers. The job of the courts was, in Chief Justice Marshall\xe2\x80\x99s words, \xe2\x80\x9csolely, to decide on the rights of individuals,\xe2\x80\x9d Marbury v. Madison, 1 Cranch 137, 170 (1803). Tocqueville considered this one reason the new democracy could safely confer upon courts the immense power to hold legislation unconstitutional:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[B]y leaving it to private interest to censure the law, and by intimately uniting the trial of the law with the trial of an individual, legislation is protected from wanton assaults and from the daily aggressions of party spirit.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cI am inclined to believe this practice of the American courts to be at once most favorable to liberty and to public order. If the judge could only attack the legislator only openly and directly, he would sometimes be afraid to oppose him; and at other times party spirit might encourage him to brave it at every turn.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. But the American judge is brought into the political arena independently of his own will. He judges the law only because he is obliged to judge a case. The political question that he is called upon to resolve is connected with the interests of the parties, and he cannot refuse to decide it without a denial of justice.\xe2\x80\x9d A. de Tocqueville, Democracy in America 102-03 (P. Bradley ed. 1948).

\n

That doctrine of standing, that jurisdictional limitation upon our powers, does not have as its purpose (as the majority assumes) merely to assure that we will decide disputes in concrete factual contexts that enable \xe2\x80\x9crealistic appreciation of the consequences of judicial action,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 14. To the contrary. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he law of Art. III standing is built on a single basic idea\xe2\x80\x94the idea of separation of powers.\xe2\x80\x9d Allen v. Wright,\n\n468 U.\xc2\xa0S. 737,\n752 (1984)\n. It keeps us minding our own business.

\n

We consult history and judicial tradition to determine whether a given \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98disput[e is] appropriately resolved through the judicial process.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife,\n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 555,\n560 (1992)\n (internal quotation marks omitted). What history and judicial tradition show is that courts do not resolve direct disputes between two political branches of the same government regarding their respective powers. Nearly every separation-of-powers case presents questions like the ones in this case. But we have never passed on a separation-of-powers question raised directly by a governmental subunit\xe2\x80\x99s complaint. We have always resolved those questions in the context of a private lawsuit in which the claim or defense depends on the constitutional validity of action by one of the governmental subunits that has caused a private party concrete harm. That is why, for example, it took this Court over 50 years to rule upon the constitutionality of the Tenure of Office Act, passed in 1867. If the law of standing had been otherwise, \xe2\x80\x9cpresumably President Wilson, or Presidents Grant and Cleveland before him, would .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. have had standing, and could have challenged the law preventing the removal of a Presidential appointee without the consent of Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Raines v. Byrd,\n\n521 U.\xc2\xa0S. 811,\n828 (1997)\n.

\n

We do not have to look far back in the United States Reports to find other separation-of-powers cases which, if the Arizona Legislature\xe2\x80\x99s theory of standing is correct, took an awfully circuitous route to get here. In Zivotofsky v. Kerry, ante, p. ___, the President could have sued for an injunction against Congress\xe2\x80\x99s attempted \xe2\x80\x9cdirect usurpation\xe2\x80\x9d of his constitutionally-conferred authority to pronounce on foreign relations. Or in Wellness Int\xe2\x80\x99l Network, Ltd. v. Sharif, 575 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015), a Federal District Judge could have sought a declaratory judgment that a bankruptcy court\xe2\x80\x99s adjudicating a Stern claim improperly usurped his constitutionally conferred authority to decide cases and controversies. Or in NLRB v. Noel Canning, 573 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014), the Senate could have sued the President, claiming a direct usurpation of its prerogative to advise on and consent to Presidential appointments. Each of these cases involved the allocation of power to one or more branches of a government; and we surely would have dismissed suits arising in the hypothesized fashions.

\n

We have affirmatively rejected arguments for jurisdiction in cases like this one. For example, in Raines, 521 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 829\xe2\x80\x93830, we refused to allow Members of Congress to challenge the Line Item Veto Act, which they claimed \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98unconstitutionally expand[ed] the President\xe2\x80\x99s power\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98alter[ed] the constitutional balance of powers between the Legislative and Executive Branches.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 816. In Massachusetts v. Mellon,\n\n262 U.\xc2\xa0S. 447\n\xe2\x80\x93480 (1923), we refused to allow a State to pursue its claim that a conditional congressional appropriation \xe2\x80\x9cconstitute[d] an effective means of inducing the States to yield a portion of their sovereign rights.\xe2\x80\x9d (And Mellon involved a contention that one government infringed upon another government\xe2\x80\x99s power\xe2\x80\x94far closer to the traditional party-versus-party lawsuit than is an intragovernmental dispute.) We put it plainly: \xe2\x80\x9cIn the last analysis, the complaint of the plaintiff State is brought to the naked contention that Congress has usurped the reserved powers of the several States,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 483\xe2\x80\x94and because the State could not show a discrete harm except the alleged usurpation of its powers, we refused to allow the State\xe2\x80\x99s appeal.

\n

The sole precedent the Court relies upon is Coleman v. Miller,\n\n307 U.\xc2\xa0S. 433 (1939)\n. Coleman can be distinguished from the present case as readily as it was distinguished in Raines. In Raines, the accurate-in-fact (but inconsequential-in-principle) distinction was that the Senators in Coleman had their votes nullified, whereas the Members of Congress claimed that their votes could merely be rendered ineffective by a Presidential line-item veto. Raines, supra, at 823\xe2\x80\x93824. In the present case we could make the accurate-in-fact distinction that in Coleman individual legislators were found to have standing, whereas here it is the governmental body, the Arizona Legisla-ture, that seeks to bring suit. But the reality is that the supposed holding of Coleman stands out like a sore thumb from the rest of our jurisprudence, which denies standing for intragovernmental disputes.

\n

Coleman was a peculiar case that may well stand for nothing. The opinion discussing and finding standing, and going on to affirm the Kansas Supreme Court, was written by Chief Justice Hughes and announced by Justice Stone. Justice Frankfurter, joined by three other Justices, held there was no standing, and would have dismissed the petition (leaving the judgment of the Kansas Supreme Court in place). Justice Butler, joined by Justice McRey-nolds, dissented (neither joining Hughes\xe2\x80\x99s opinion nor separately discussing standing) and would have reversed the Kansas Supreme Court.

\n

That adds up to two votes to affirm on the merits, two to reverse on the merits (without discussing standing) and four to dismiss for lack of standing. Justice Stanley Reed, who was on the Court and apparently participated in the case, is not mentioned in any of the opinions recorded in the United States Reports. So, in order to find Coleman a binding precedent on standing, rather than a 4-to-4 standoff, one must assume that Justice Reed voted with Hughes. There is some reason to make that assumption: The four Justices rejecting standing went on to discuss the merits, because \xe2\x80\x9cthe ruling of the Court just announced removes from the case the question of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 standing to sue.\xe2\x80\x9d 307 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 456 (Black, J., concurring). But then again, if nine Justices participated, how could it be that on one of the two issues in the case the Court was \xe2\x80\x9cequally divided and therefore .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. expresse[d] no opinion\xe2\x80\x9d? Id., at 447.

\n

A pretty shaky foundation for a significant precedential ruling. Besides that, the two dissenters\xe2\x80\x99 mere assumption of standing\xe2\x80\x94neither saying anything about the subject nor joining Hughes\xe2\x80\x99s opinion on the point\xe2\x80\x94produces (if you assume Reed joined Hughes) a majority for standing but no majority opinion explaining why. And even under the most generous assumptions, since the Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment on the issue it resolved rested on the ground that that issue presented a political question\xe2\x80\x94which is itself a rejection of jurisdiction, Zivotofsky v. Clinton, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012) (slip op., at 5)\xe2\x80\x94Coleman\xe2\x80\x99s discussion of the additional jurisdictional issue of standing was quite superfluous and arguably nothing but dictum. The peculiar decision in Coleman should be charitably ignored.

\n

The Court asserts, quoting Raines, 521 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 819\xe2\x80\x93820, that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s standing analysis has been \xe2\x80\x9cespecially rigorous when reaching the merits of the dispute would force [the Court] to decide whether an action taken by one of the other two branches of the Federal Government was unconstitutional.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14, n.\xc2\xa012. The cases cited to support this dictum fail to do so; they are merely cases where a determination of unconstitutionality is avoided by applying what there is no reason to believe is anything other than normal standing requirements. It seems to me utterly implausible that the Framers wanted federal courts limited to traditional judicial cases only when they were pronouncing upon the rights of Congress and the President, and not when they were treading upon the powers of state legislatures and executives. Quite to the contrary, I think they would be all the more averse to unprecedented judicial meddling by federal courts with the branches of their state governments.

\n

I would dismiss this case for want of jurisdiction.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

Normally, having arrived at that conclusion, I would express no opinion on the merits unless my vote was necessary to enable the Court to produce a judgment. In the present case, however, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s resolution of the merits question (\xe2\x80\x9clegislature\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9cthe people\xe2\x80\x9d) is so outrageously wrong, so utterly devoid of textual or historic support, so flatly in contradiction of prior Supreme Court cases, so obviously the willful product of hostility to districting by state legislatures, that I cannot avoid adding my vote to the devastating dissent of the Chief Justice.

ARIZONA STATE LEGISLATURE, APPELLANT v. ARIZONA INDEPENDENT REDISTRICTING COMMISSION et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on appeal from the united states district court for the district of arizona

\n

[June 29, 2015]

\n

\n

Chief Justice Roberts, with whom Justice Scalia, Justice Thomas, and Justice Alito join, dissenting.

\n

Just over a century ago, Arizona became the second State in the Union to ratify the\n \nSeventeenth Amendment. That Amendment transferred power to choose United States Senators from \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d of each State, Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa73, to \xe2\x80\x9cthe people thereof.\xe2\x80\x9d The Amendment re-sulted from an arduous, decades-long campaign in which reformers across the country worked hard to garner approval from Congress and three-quarters of the States.

\n

What chumps! Didn\xe2\x80\x99t they realize that all they had to do was interpret the constitutional term \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d to mean \xe2\x80\x9cthe people\xe2\x80\x9d? The Court today performs just such a magic trick with the Elections Clause. Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa74. That Clause vests congressional redistricting authority in \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d of each State. An Arizona ballot initiative transferred that authority from \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d to an \xe2\x80\x9cIndependent Redistricting Commission.\xe2\x80\x9d The majority approves this deliberate constitutional evasion by doing what the proponents of the\n \nSeventeenth Amendment dared not: revising \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d to mean \xe2\x80\x9cthe people.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s position has no basis in the text, structure, or history of the Constitution, and it contradicts precedents from both Congress and this Court. The Constitution contains seventeen provisions referring to the \xe2\x80\x9cLegislature\xe2\x80\x9d of a State, many of which cannot possibly be read to mean \xe2\x80\x9cthe people.\xe2\x80\x9d See Appendix, infra. Indeed, several provisions expressly distinguish \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d from \xe2\x80\x9cthe People.\xe2\x80\x9d See Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa72; Amdt. 17. This Court has accordingly defined \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d in the Elections Clause as \xe2\x80\x9cthe representative body which ma[kes] the laws of the people.\xe2\x80\x9d Smiley v. Holm,\n\n285 U.\xc2\xa0S. 355,\n365 (1932)\n (quoting Hawke v. Smith (No. 1),\n\n253 U.\xc2\xa0S. 221,\n227 (1920)\n; emphasis added).

\n

The majority largely ignores this evidence, relying instead on disconnected observations about direct democracy, a contorted interpretation of an irrelevant statute, and naked appeals to public policy. Nowhere does the majority explain how a constitutional provision that vests redistricting authority in \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d permits a State to wholly exclude \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d from redistricting. Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s Commission might be a noble endeavor\xe2\x80\x94although it does not seem so \xe2\x80\x9cindependent\xe2\x80\x9d in practice\xe2\x80\x94but the \xe2\x80\x9cfact that a given law or procedure is efficient, convenient, and useful .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. will not save it if it is contrary to the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d INS v. Chadha,\n\n462 U.\xc2\xa0S. 919,\n944 (1983)\n. No matter how concerned we may be about partisanship in redistricting, this Court has no power to gerrymander the Constitution. I respectfully dissent.

\n

I

\n

The majority begins by discussing policy. I begin with the Constitution. The Elections Clause provides:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe Times, Places and Manner of holding Elections for Senators and Representatives, shall be prescribed in each State by the Legislature thereof; but the Congress may at any time by Law make or alter such Regulations, except as to the Places of chusing Senators.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa74, cl.\xc2\xa01.

\n

The Elections Clause both imposes a duty on States and assigns that duty to a particular state actor: In the absence of a valid congressional directive to the contrary, States must draw district lines for their federal representatives. And that duty \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d be carried out \xe2\x80\x9cin each State by the Legislature thereof.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

In Arizona, however, redistricting is not carried out by the legislature. Instead, as the result of a ballot initiative, an unelected body called the Independent Redistricting Commission draws the lines. See ante, at 6\xe2\x80\x937. The key question in the case is whether the Commission can conduct congressional districting consistent with the directive that such authority be exercised \xe2\x80\x9cby the Legislature.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

The majority concedes that the unelected Commission is not \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d of Arizona. The Court contends instead that the people of Arizona as a whole constitute \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d for purposes of the Elections Clause, and that they may delegate the congressional districting authority conferred by that Clause to the Commission. Ante, at 25. The majority provides no support for the delegation part of its theory, and I am not sure whether the major-ity\xe2\x80\x99s analysis is correct on that issue. But even giving the Court the benefit of the doubt in that regard, the Commission is still unconstitutional. Both the Constitution and our cases make clear that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d in the Elections Clause is the representative body which makes the laws of the people.

\n

A

\n

The majority devotes much of its analysis to establishing that the people of Arizona may exercise lawmaking power under their State Constitution. See ante, at 5\xe2\x80\x936, 25, 27\xe2\x80\x9328. Nobody doubts that. This case is governed, however, by the Federal Constitution. The States do not, in the majority\xe2\x80\x99s words, \xe2\x80\x9cretain autonomy to establish their own governmental processes,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 27, if those \xe2\x80\x9cprocesses\xe2\x80\x9d violate the United States Constitution. In a conflict between the Arizona Constitution and the Elections Clause, the State Constitution must give way. Art.\xc2\xa0VI, cl. 2; Cook v. Gralike,\n\n531 U.\xc2\xa0S. 510,\n523 (2001)\n. The majority opinion therefore largely misses the point.

\n

The relevant question in this case is how to define \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d under the Elections Clause. The majority opinion does not seriously turn to that question until page 24, and even then it fails to provide a coherent answer. The Court seems to conclude, based largely on its understanding of the \xe2\x80\x9chistory and purpose\xe2\x80\x9d of the Elections Clause, ante, at 24, that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d encompasses any entity in a State that exercises legislative power. That circular definition lacks any basis in the text of the Constitution or any other relevant legal source.

\n

The majority\xe2\x80\x99s textual analysis consists, in its entirety, of one paragraph citing founding era dictionaries. The majority points to various dictionaries that follow Samuel Johnson\xe2\x80\x99s definition of \xe2\x80\x9clegislature\xe2\x80\x9d as the \xe2\x80\x9cpower that makes laws.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted). The notion that this definition corresponds to the entire population of a State is strained to begin with, and largely discredited by the majority\xe2\x80\x99s own admission that \xe2\x80\x9c[d]irect lawmaking by the people was virtually unknown when the Constitution of 1787 was drafted.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 3 (internal quotation marks omitted); see ante, at 27. Moreover, Dr. Johnson\xe2\x80\x99s first example of the usage of \xe2\x80\x9clegislature\xe2\x80\x9d is this: \xe2\x80\x9cWithout the concurrent consent of all three parts of the legislature, no law is or can be made.\xe2\x80\x9d 2 A Dictionary of the English Language (1st ed. 1755) (emphasis deleted). Johnson borrowed that sentence from Matthew Hale, who defined the \xe2\x80\x9cThree Parts of the Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d of England as the King and the two houses of Parliament. History of the Common Law of England 2 (1713). (The contrary notion that the people as a whole make the laws would have cost you your head in England in 1713.) Thus, even under the majority\xe2\x80\x99s preferred definition, \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d referred to an institutional body of representatives, not the people at large.

\n

Any ambiguity about the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d is removed by other founding era sources. \xe2\x80\x9c[E]very state constitution from the Founding Era that used the term legislature defined it as a distinct multimember entity comprised of representatives.\xe2\x80\x9d Morley, The Intratextual Independent \xe2\x80\x9cLegislature\xe2\x80\x9d and the Elections Clause, 109 Nw. U. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. Online 131, 147, and n. 101 (2015) (citing eleven State Constitutions). The Federalist Papers are replete with references to \xe2\x80\x9clegislatures\xe2\x80\x9d that can only be understood as referring to representative institutions. E.g., The Federalist No. 27, pp. 174\xe2\x80\x93175 (C. Rossiter ed. 1961) (A. Hamilton) (describing \xe2\x80\x9cthe State legislatures\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cselect bodies of men\xe2\x80\x9d); id., No. 60, at 368 (contrasting \xe2\x80\x9cthe State legislatures\xe2\x80\x9d with \xe2\x80\x9cthe people\xe2\x80\x9d). Noah Webster\xe2\x80\x99s heralded American Dictionary of the English Language defines \xe2\x80\x9clegislature\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he body of men in a state or kingdom, invested with power to make and repeal laws.\xe2\x80\x9d 2 An American Dictionary of the English Language (1828). It continues, \xe2\x80\x9cThe legislatures of most of the states in America .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. consist of two houses or branches.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis deleted).

\n

I could go on, but the Court has said this before. As we put it nearly a century ago, \xe2\x80\x9cLegislature\xe2\x80\x9d was \xe2\x80\x9cnot a term of uncertain meaning when incorporated into the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Hawke, 253 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 227. \xe2\x80\x9cWhat it meant when adopted it still means for the purpose of interpretation.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. \xe2\x80\x9cA Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cthe representative body which ma[kes] the laws of the people.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; see Smiley, 285 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 365 (relying on this definition); Colorado Gen. Assembly v. Salazar,\n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1093,\n1095 (2004)\n (Rehnquist, C.\xc2\xa0J., dissenting from denial of certiorari) (same).

\n

B

\n

The unambiguous meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d in the Elections Clause as a representative body is confirmed by other provisions of the Constitution that use the same term in the same way. When seeking to discern the meaning of a word in the Constitution, there is no better dictionary than the rest of the Constitution itself. Our precedents new and old have employed this structural method of interpretation to read the Constitution in the manner it was drafted and ratified\xe2\x80\x94as a unified, coherent whole. See, e.g., NLRB v. Noel Canning, 573 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2014) (slip op., at 19\xe2\x80\x9320); id., at ___ (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment) (slip op., at 32); McCulloch v. Maryland, 4 Wheat. 316, 414\xe2\x80\x93415 (1819); Martin v. Hunter\xe2\x80\x99s Lessee, 1 Wheat. 304, 328\xe2\x80\x93330 (1816); Amar, Intratextualism, 112 Harv. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 747 (1999).

\n

The Constitution includes seventeen provisions referring to a State\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cLegislature.\xe2\x80\x9d See Appendix, infra. Every one of those references is consistent with the understanding of a legislature as a representative body. More importantly, many of them are only consistent with an institutional legislature\xe2\x80\x94and flatly incompatible with the majority\xe2\x80\x99s reading of \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d to refer to the people as a whole.

\n

Start with the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s first use of the term: \xe2\x80\x9cThe House of Representatives shall be composed of Members chosen every second Year by the People of the several States, and the Electors in each State shall have the Qualifications requisite for Electors of the most numerous Branch of the State Legislature.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa72, cl.\xc2\xa01. This reference to a \xe2\x80\x9cBranch of the State Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d can only be referring to an institutional body, and the explicit juxtaposition of \xe2\x80\x9cthe State Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d with \xe2\x80\x9cthe People of the several States\xe2\x80\x9d forecloses the majority\xe2\x80\x99s proposed reading.

\n

The next Section of Article I describes how to fill vacancies in the United States Senate: \xe2\x80\x9cif Vacancies happen by Resignation, or otherwise, during the Recess of the Legislature of any State, the Executive thereof may make temporary Appointments until the next Meeting of the Legislature, which shall then fill such Vacancies.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73, cl.\xc2\xa02.[1] The references to \xe2\x80\x9cthe Recess of the Legislature of any State\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cthe next Meeting of the Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d are only consistent with an institutional legislature, and make no sense under the majority\xe2\x80\x99s reading. The people as a whole (schoolchildren and a few unnamed others excepted) do not take a \xe2\x80\x9cRecess.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

The list goes on. Article IV provides that the \xe2\x80\x9cUnited States shall guarantee to every State in this Union a Republican Form of Government, and shall protect each of them against Invasion; and on Application of the Legislature, or of the Executive (when the Legislature cannot be convened), against domestic Violence.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa74. It is perhaps conceivable that all the people of a State could be \xe2\x80\x9cconvened\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94although this would seem difficult during an \xe2\x80\x9cInvasion\xe2\x80\x9d or outbreak of \xe2\x80\x9cdomestic Violence\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94but the only natural reading of the Clause is that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Executive\xe2\x80\x9d may submit a federal application when \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d as a representative body cannot be convened.

\n

Article VI provides that the \xe2\x80\x9cSenators and Representatives before mentioned, and the Members of the several State Legislatures, and all executive and judicial Officers, both of the United States and of the several States, shall be bound by Oath or Affirmation, to support this Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Cl.\xc2\xa03. Unless the majority is prepared to make all the people of every State swear an \xe2\x80\x9cOath or Affirmation, to support this Constitution,\xe2\x80\x9d this provision can only referto the \xe2\x80\x9cseveral State Legislatures\xe2\x80\x9d in their institutional capacity.

\n

Each of these provisions offers strong structural indications about what \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d must mean. But the most powerful evidence of all comes from the\n \nSeventeenth Amendment. Under the original Constitution, Senators were \xe2\x80\x9cchosen by the Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d of each State, Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa73, cl.\xc2\xa01, while Members of the House of Representatives were chosen \xe2\x80\x9cby the People,\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa72, cl.\xc2\xa01. That distinction was critical to the Framers. As James Madison explained, the Senate would \xe2\x80\x9cderive its powers from the States,\xe2\x80\x9d while the House would \xe2\x80\x9cderive its powers from the people of America.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 39, at 244. George Mason believed that the power of state legislatures to select Senators would \xe2\x80\x9cbe a reasonable guard\xe2\x80\x9d against \xe2\x80\x9cthe Danger .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that the national, will swallow up the State Legislatures.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 Records of the Federal Convention of 1787, p. 160 (M. Farrand ed. 1911). Not everyone agreed. James Wilson proposed allowing the people to elect Senators directly. His proposal was rejected ten to one. Debates in the Federal Convention of 1787, S. Doc. No. 404, 57th Cong., 1st Sess., 8 (1902).

\n

Before long, reformers took up Wilson\xe2\x80\x99s mantle and launched a protracted campaign to amend the Constitution. That effort began in 1826, when Representative Henry Storrs of New York proposed\xe2\x80\x94but then set aside\xe2\x80\x94a constitutional amendment transferring the power to elect Senators from the state legislatures to the people. 2 Cong. Deb. 1348\xe2\x80\x931349. Over the next three-quarters of a century, no fewer than 188 joint resolutions proposing similar reforms were introduced in both Houses of Congress. 1 W. Hall, The History and Effect of the\n \nSeventeenth Amendment 183\xe2\x80\x93184 (1936).

\n

At no point in this process did anyone suggest that a constitutional amendment was unnecessary because \xe2\x80\x9cLegislature\xe2\x80\x9d could simply be interpreted to mean \xe2\x80\x9cpeople.\xe2\x80\x9d See Hawke, 253 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 228 (\xe2\x80\x9cIt was never suggested, so far as we are aware, that the purpose of making the office of Senator elective by the people could be accomplished by a referendum vote. The necessity of the amendment to accomplish the purpose of popular election is shown in the adoption of the amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d). In fact, as the decades rolled by without an amendment, 28 of the 45 States settled for the next best thing by holding a popular vote on candidates for Senate, then pressuring state legislators into choosing the winner. See, e.g., Abstract of Laws Relating to the Election of United States Senators, S. Doc. No. 393, 59th Cong., 2d Sess. (1907). All agreed that cutting the state legislature out of senatorial selection entirely would require nothing less than to \xe2\x80\x9cStrike out\xe2\x80\x9d the original words in the Constitution and \xe2\x80\x9cinsert, \xe2\x80\x98elected by the people\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in its place. Cong. Globe, 31st Cong., 1st Sess., 88 (1849) (proposal of Sen. Jeremiah Clemens).

\n

Yet that is precisely what the majority does to the Elections Clause today\xe2\x80\x94amending the text not through the process provided by Article V, but by judicial decision. The majority\xe2\x80\x99s revision renders the\n \nSeventeenth Amendment an 86-year waste of time, and singles out the Elections Clause as the only one of the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s seventeen provisions referring to \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d that departs from the ordinary meaning of the term.

\n

The Commission had no answer to this point. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 42 (Justice Alito: \xe2\x80\x9cIs there any other provision where legislature means anything other than the conventional meaning?\xe2\x80\x9d Appellee: \xe2\x80\x9cI don\xe2\x80\x99t know the answer to that question.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s response is not much better. The majority observes that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d of a State may perform different functions under different provisions of the Constitution. Under Article I, \xc2\xa73, for example, \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d performed an \xe2\x80\x9celectoral\xe2\x80\x9d function by choosing Senators. The \xe2\x80\x9cLegislature\xe2\x80\x9d plays a \xe2\x80\x9cconsenting\xe2\x80\x9d function under Article I, \xc2\xa78, and Article IV, \xc2\xa73; a \xe2\x80\x9cratifying\xe2\x80\x9d function under Article V; and a \xe2\x80\x9clawmaking\xe2\x80\x9d function under the Elections Clause. Ante, at 19, and n.\xc2\xa017. All true. The majority, however, leaps from the premise that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d performs different functions under different provisions to the conclusion that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d assumes different identities under different provisions.

\n

As a matter of ordinary language and common sense, however, a difference in function does not imply a difference in meaning. A car, for example, generally serves a transportation function. But it can also fulfill a storage function. At a tailgate party or a drive-in movie, it may play an entertainment function. In the absence of vacancies at the roadside motel, it could provide a lodging function. To a neighbor with a dead battery, it offers an electricity generation function. And yet, a person describing a \xe2\x80\x9ccar\xe2\x80\x9d engaged in any of these varied functions would undoubtedly be referring to the same thing.

\n

The Constitution itself confirms this point. Articles I and II assign many different functions to the Senate: a lawmaking function, an impeachment trial function, a treaty ratification function, an appointee confirmation function, an officer selection function, a qualification judging function, and a recordkeeping function. Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa71; \xc2\xa73, cls. 5, 6; \xc2\xa75, cls.\xc2\xa01, 3; \xc2\xa77, cl. 2; Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa72, cl.\xc2\xa02. Yet the identity of the Senate remains the same as it discharges these various functions.

\n

Similarly, the House of Representatives performs different functions, including lawmaking, impeachment, and resolving Presidential elections in which no candidate wins a majority in the Electoral College. Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa71; \xc2\xa72, cl.\xc2\xa05; \xc2\xa77, cl.\xc2\xa02; Amdt. 12. The President is assigned not only executive functions, Art.\xc2\xa0II, but also legislative functions, such as approving or vetoing bills, convening both Houses of Congress, and recommending measures for their consideration, Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa77, cl. 2; Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa73. Courts not only exercise a judicial function, Art.\xc2\xa0III, \xc2\xa71, but may also perform an appointment function, Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa72, cl.\xc2\xa02. And so on. Neither the majority nor the Commission points to a single instance in which the identity of these actors changes as they exercise different functions.

\n

The majority attempts to draw support from precedent, but our cases only further undermine its position. In Hawke, this Court considered the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislatur[e]\xe2\x80\x9d in Article V, which outlines the process for ratifying constitutional amendments. The Court concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cLegislature\xe2\x80\x9d meant \xe2\x80\x9cthe representative body which ma[kes] the laws of the people.\xe2\x80\x9d 253 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 227. The Court then explained that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he term is often used in the Constitution with this evident meaning.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added). The Court proceeded to list other constitutional provisions that assign different functions to the \xe2\x80\x9cLegislature,\xe2\x80\x9d just as the majority does today. Id., at 227\xe2\x80\x93228; see ante, at 19, n.\xc2\xa017.

\n

Unlike the majority today, however, the Court in Hawke never hinted that the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cLegislature\xe2\x80\x9d varied across those different provisions because they assigned different functions. To the contrary, the Court drew inferences from the\n \nSeventeenth Amendment and its predecessor, Article I, \xc2\xa73\xe2\x80\x94in which \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d played an electoral function\xe2\x80\x94to define the \xe2\x80\x9cLegislature\xe2\x80\x9d in Article V, which assigned it a ratification function. See 253 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 228. The Court concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cLegislature\xe2\x80\x9d refers to a representative body, whatever its function. As the Court put it, \xe2\x80\x9cThere can be no question that the framers of the Constitution clearly understood and carefully used the terms in which that instrument referred to the action of the legislatures of the States. When they intended that direct action by the people should be had they were no less accurate in the use of apt phraseology to carry out such purpose.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (citing Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa72).

\n

Smiley, the leading precedent on the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d in the Elections Clause, reaffirmed the definition announced in Hawke. In Smiley, the petitioner argued\xe2\x80\x94as the Commission does here\xe2\x80\x94that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d referred not just to \xe2\x80\x9cthe two houses of the legislature\xe2\x80\x9d but to \xe2\x80\x9cthe entire legislative power of the state .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. however exercised.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner, O.\xc2\xa0T. 1931, No. 617, p.\xc2\xa022 (internal quotation marks omitted). The Court did not respond by holding, as the majority today suggests, that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the Legislature\xe2\x80\x99 comprises the referendum and the Governor\xe2\x80\x99s veto in the context of regulating congressional elections,\xe2\x80\x9d or that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the Legislature\xe2\x80\x99 has a different iden-tity\xe2\x80\x9d in the Elections Clause than it does in Article V. Ante, at 18\xe2\x80\x9319. Instead, the Court in Smiley said this:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cMuch that is urged in argument with regard to the meaning of the term \xe2\x80\x98Legislature\xe2\x80\x99 is beside the point. As this Court said in Hawke .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the term was not one \xe2\x80\x98of uncertain meaning when incorporated into the Constitution. What it meant when adopted it still means for purposes of interpretation. A Legislature was then the representative body which made the laws of the people.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 285 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 365 (quoting Hawke, 253 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 227).

\n

Remarkably, the majority refuses to even acknowledge the definition of \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d adopted in both Smiley and Hawke, and instead embraces the interpretation that this Court unanimously rejected more than 80 years ago.[2]

\n

C

\n

The history of the Elections Clause further supports the conclusion that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d is a representative body. The first known draft of the Clause to appear at the Constitutional Convention provided that \xe2\x80\x9cEach state shall prescribe the time and manner of holding elections.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 Debates on the Federal Constitution 146 (J. Elliot ed. 1836). After revision by the Committee of Detail, the Clause included the important limitation at issue here: \xe2\x80\x9cThe times and places, and the manner, of holding the elections of the members of each house, shall be prescribed by the legislature of each state; but their provisions concerning them may, at any time, be altered by the legislature of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 225 (emphasis added). The insertion of \xe2\x80\x9cthe legislature\xe2\x80\x9d indicates that the Framers thought carefully about which entity within the State was to perform congressional districting. And the parallel between \xe2\x80\x9cthe legislature of each state\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cthe legislature of the United States\xe2\x80\x9d further suggests that they meant \xe2\x80\x9cthe legislature\xe2\x80\x9d as a representative body.

\n

As the majority explains, the debate over the ratification of the Elections Clause centered on its second part, which empowers Congress to \xe2\x80\x9cmake or alter\xe2\x80\x9d regulations prescribed by \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d of a State. See ante, at 25\xe2\x80\x9327. Importantly for our purposes, however, both sides in this debate \xe2\x80\x9crecognized the distinction between the state legislature and the people themselves.\xe2\x80\x9d Brown v. Secretary of State of Florida, 668 F.\xc2\xa03d 1271, 1275\xe2\x80\x931276, n.\xc2\xa04 (CA11 2012).

\n

The Anti-Federalists, for example, supported vesting election regulation power solely in state legislatures because state \xe2\x80\x9clegislatures were more numerous bodies, usually elected annually, and thus more likely to be in sympathy with the interests of the people.\xe2\x80\x9d Natelson, The Original Scope of the Congressional Power to Regulate Elections, 13 U. Pa. J. Const. L. 1, 31 (2010) (emphasis added) (citing sources from ratification debates). Alexander Hamilton and others responded by raising the specter of state legislatures\xe2\x80\x94which he described as \xe2\x80\x9clocal administrations\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94deciding to \xe2\x80\x9cannihilate\xe2\x80\x9d the Federal Government by \xe2\x80\x9cneglecting to provide for the choice of persons to administer its affairs.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 59, at 363. As the majority acknowledges, the distinction between \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d and the people \xe2\x80\x9coccasioned no debate.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 27. That is because everybody understood what \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d meant.

\n

The majority contends that its counterintuitive reading of \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d is necessary to advance the \xe2\x80\x9canimating principle\xe2\x80\x9d of popular sovereignty. Ante, at 24. But the ratification of the Constitution was the ultimate act of popular sovereignty, and the people who ratified the Elections Clause did so knowing that it assigned authority to \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d as a representative body. The Elections Clause was not, as the majority suggests, an all-purpose \xe2\x80\x9csafeguard against manipulation of electoral rules by politicians.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 26. Like most provisions of the Constitution, the Elections Clause reflected a compromise\xe2\x80\x94a pragmatic recognition that the grand project of forging a Union required everyone to accept some things they did not like. See The Federalist No. 59, at 364 (describing the power allocated to state legislatures as \xe2\x80\x9can evil which could not have been avoided\xe2\x80\x9d). This Court has no power to upset such a compromise simply because we now think that it should have been struck differently. As we explained almost a century ago, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he framers of the Constitution might have adopted a different method,\xe2\x80\x9d but it \xe2\x80\x9cis not the function of courts .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to alter the method which the Constitution has fixed.\xe2\x80\x9d Hawke, 253 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 227.

\n

D

\n

In addition to text, structure, and history, several precedents interpreting the Elections Clause further reinforce that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d refers to a representative body.

\n

The first precedent comes not from this Court, but from Congress. Acting under its authority to serve as \xe2\x80\x9cthe Judge of the Elections, Returns and Qualifications of its own Members,\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa75, cl.\xc2\xa01, the House of Representatives in 1866 confronted a dispute about who should be seated as the Congressman from the Fifth District of Michigan. At a popular convention, Michigan voters had amended the State Constitution to require votes to be cast within a resident\xe2\x80\x99s township or ward. The Michigan Legislature, however, passed a law permitting soldiers to vote in alternative locations. If only the local votes counted, one candidate (Baldwin) would win; if the outside votes were included, the other candidate (Trowbridge) would be entitled to the seat. See Baldwin v. Trowbridge, 2 Bartlett Contested Election Cases, H.\xc2\xa0R. Misc. Doc. No. 152, 41st Cong., 2d Sess., 46\xe2\x80\x9347 (1866).

\n

The House Elections Committee explained that the Elections Clause conferred power on \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d of Michigan to prescribe election regulations. \xe2\x80\x9cBut,\xe2\x80\x9d the Committee asked, \xe2\x80\x9cwhat is meant by \xe2\x80\x98the legislature?\xe2\x80\x99 Does it mean the legislative power of the State, which would include a convention authorized to prescribe fundamental law; or does it mean the legislature eo nomine, as known in the political history of the country?\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 47. The Committee decided, and the full House agreed, that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d in the Elections Clause was the \xe2\x80\x9clegislature eo nomine\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94the legislature by that name, a representative body. Ibid. That conclusion followed both from the known meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d at the time of the framing and the many other uses of the word in the Constitution that would not be compatible with a popular convention. Thus, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]here there is a conflict of authority between the constitution and legislature of a State in regard to fixing place of elections, the power of the legislature is paramount.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 46; see California Democratic Party v. Jones,\n\n530 U.\xc2\xa0S. 567\n, and n. 11 (2000) (Stevens, J., dissenting) (relying on Baldwin for its conclusion that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Elections Clause\xe2\x80\x99s specific reference to \xe2\x80\x98the Legislature\xe2\x80\x99 is not so broad as to encompass the general \xe2\x80\x98legislative power of this State\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

The majority draws attention to the minority report in Baldwin. Ante, at 29. Under the present circumstances, I take some comfort in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s willingness to consider dissenting views. Still, the minority report does not diminish the force of Baldwin. The report cites a Michigan Supreme Court precedent that allegedly reached a con-trary result, but that case turned entirely on state constitu-tional questions arising from a state election\xe2\x80\x94not federal constitutional questions arising from a federal election. See People ex\xc2\xa0rel. Twitchell v. Blodgett, 13 Mich. 127 (1865). The majority also contends that Baldwin \xe2\x80\x9cappears in tension with\xe2\x80\x9d an earlier House Elections Committee precedent. Ante, at 29. By its own terms, however, that earlier precedent did not involve a conflict between a state legislative act and a state constitutional provision. See Shiel v. Thayer, 1 Bartlett Contested Election Cases, H.\xc2\xa0R. Misc. Doc. No. 57, 38th Cong., 2d Sess., 350 (1861) (\xe2\x80\x9cthe two branches of the legislature differed upon the question .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and so the bill never became a law\xe2\x80\x9d). In any event, to the degree that the two precedents are inconsistent, the later decision in Baldwin should govern.[3]

\n

The next relevant precedent is this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in McPherson v. Blacker,\n\n146 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1 (1892)\n. That case involved a constitutional provision with considerable similarity to the Elections Clause, the Presidential Electors Clause of Article II: \xe2\x80\x9cEach State shall appoint, in such Manner as the Legislature thereof may direct, a Number of Electors .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71, cl.\xc2\xa02 (emphasis added). The question was whether the state legislature, as a body of representatives, could divide authority to appoint electors across each of the State\xe2\x80\x99s congressional districts. The Court upheld the law and emphasized that the plain text of the Presidential Electors Clause vests the power to determine the manner of appointment in \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d of the State. That power, the Court explained, \xe2\x80\x9ccan neither be taken away nor abdicated.\xe2\x80\x9d 146 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 35 (emphasis added; internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

Against that backdrop, the Court decided two cases regarding the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d in the Elections Clause. In Ohio ex rel. Davis v. Hildebrant,\n\n241 U.\xc2\xa0S. 565 (1916)\n, the Ohio Legislature passed a congressional redistricting law. Under the Ohio Constitution, voters held a referendum on the law and rejected it. A supporter of the law sued on behalf of the State, contending that the referendum \xe2\x80\x9cwas not and could not be a part of the legislative authority of the State and therefore could have no influence on .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the law creating congressional districts\xe2\x80\x9d under the Elections Clause. Id., at 567.

\n

This Court rejected the challenger\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional argument as a nonjusticiable claim that the referendum \xe2\x80\x9ccauses a State .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to be not republican\xe2\x80\x9d in violation of the Guarantee Clause of the Constitution. Id., at 569 (citing Art. IV, \xc2\xa74). The Court also rejected an argument that Ohio\xe2\x80\x99s use of the referendum violated a federal statute, and held that Congress had the power to pass that statute under the Elections Clause. Id., at 568\xe2\x80\x93569. Hildebrant in no way suggested that the state legislature could be displaced from the redistricting process, and Hildebrant certainly did not hold\xe2\x80\x94as the majority today contends\xe2\x80\x94that \xe2\x80\x9cthe word [\xe2\x80\x98Legislature\xe2\x80\x99 in the Elections Clause] encompassed a veto power lodged in the people.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16. Hildebrant simply approved a State\xe2\x80\x99s decision to employ a referendum in addition to redistricting by the Legislature. See 241 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 569. The result of the decision was to send the Ohio Legislature back to the drawing board to do the redistricting.

\n

In Smiley, the Minnesota Legislature passed a law adopting new congressional districts, and the Governor exercised his veto power under the State Constitution. As noted above, the Minnesota secretary of state defended the veto on the ground that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d in the Elections Clause referred not just to \xe2\x80\x9cthe two houses of the legislature\xe2\x80\x9d but to \xe2\x80\x9cthe entire legislative power of the state .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. however exercised.\xe2\x80\x9d This Court rejected that argument, reiterating that the term \xe2\x80\x9cLegislature\xe2\x80\x9d meant \xe2\x80\x9cthe representative body which made the laws of the people.\xe2\x80\x9d 285 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 365 (quoting Hawke, 253 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 227). The Court nevertheless went on to hold that the Elections Clause did not prevent a State from applying the usual rules of its legislative process\xe2\x80\x94including a gubernatorial veto\xe2\x80\x94to election regulations prescribed by the legislature. 285 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 373. As in Hildebrant, the legislature was not displaced, nor was it redefined; it just had to start on a new redistricting plan.

\n

The majority initially describes Hildebrant and Smiley as holding that \xe2\x80\x9credistricting is a legislative function, to be performed in accordance with the State\xe2\x80\x99s prescriptions for lawmaking, which may include the referendum and the Governor\xe2\x80\x99s veto.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 19. That description is true, so far as it goes. But it hardly supports the result the majority reaches here. There is a critical difference between allowing a State to supplement the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s role in the legislative process and permitting the State to supplant the legislature altogether. See Salazar, 541 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 1095 (Rehnquist, C.\xc2\xa0J., dissenting from denial of certiorari) (\xe2\x80\x9cto be consistent with Article I, \xc2\xa74, there must be some limit on the State\xe2\x80\x99s ability to define lawmaking by excluding the legislature itself\xe2\x80\x9d). Nothing in Hildebrant, Smiley, or any other precedent supports the majority\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that imposing some constraints on the legislature justifies deposing it entirely.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

The constitutional text, structure, history, and precedent establish a straightforward rule: Under the Elections Clause, \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d is a representative body that, when it prescribes election regulations, may be required to do so within the ordinary lawmaking process, but may not be cut out of that process. Put simply, the state legislature need not be exclusive in congressional districting, but neither may it be excluded.

\n

The majority\xe2\x80\x99s contrary understanding requires it to accept a definition of \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d that contradicts the term\xe2\x80\x99s plain meaning, creates discord with the\n \nSeventeenth Amendment and the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s many other uses of the term, makes nonsense of the drafting and ratification of the Elections Clause, and breaks with the relevant precedents. In short, the effect of the majority\xe2\x80\x99s decision is to erase the words \xe2\x80\x9cby the Legislature thereof\xe2\x80\x9d from the Elections Clause. That is a judicial error of the most basic order. \xe2\x80\x9cIt cannot be presumed that any clause in the constitution is intended to be without effect; and therefore such a construction is inadmissible.\xe2\x80\x9d Marbury v. Madison, 1 Cranch 137, 174 (1803).

\n

II

\n

The Court also issues an alternative holding that a federal statute,\n\n2 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72a(c), permits Arizona to vest redistricting authority in the Commission. Ante, at 19\xe2\x80\x9323. The majority does not contend that this statutory holding resolves the constitutional question presented, see ante, at 23, so its reading of Section 2a(c) is largely beside the point. With respect, its statutory argument is also hard to take seriously. Section 2a(c) does not apply to this case. And even if it did, it would likely be unconstitutional.[4]

\n

A

\n

Section 2a(c) establishes a number of default rules that govern the States\xe2\x80\x99 manner of electing representatives \xe2\x80\x9c[u]ntil a State is redistricted in the manner provided by the law thereof.\xe2\x80\x9d Section 2a(c) is therefore \xe2\x80\x9cinapplicable unless the state legislature, and state and federal courts, have all failed to redistrict\xe2\x80\x9d the State. Branch v. Smith,\n\n538 U.\xc2\xa0S. 254,\n275 (2003)\n (plurality opinion); see id., at 298\xe2\x80\x93300 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part). Here, the Commission has redistricted the State \xe2\x80\x9cin the manner provided by the law thereof.\xe2\x80\x9d So by its terms, Section 2a(c) does not come into play in this case.

\n

The majority spends several pages discussing Section 2a(c), but it conspicuously declines to say that the statute actually applies to this case.[5] The majority notes that the pre-1911 versions of Section 2a(c) applied only until \xe2\x80\x9cthe legislature\xe2\x80\x9d redistricted the State, while the post-1911 versions applied only until the State is redistricted \xe2\x80\x9cin the manner provided by the law thereof.\xe2\x80\x9d The majority also describes in detail the legislative history that accompanied the 1911 amendment. But if Section 2a(c) does not apply, its legislative history is doubly irrelevant.

\n

The majority seems to suggest that Section 2a(c) somehow indicates federal approval for the district lines that the Commission has drawn. See ante, at 23. But the statute does nothing of the sort. Section 2a(c) explains what rules apply \xe2\x80\x9c[u]ntil a State is redistricted\xe2\x80\x9d; it says nothing about what rules apply after a State is redis-tricted. And it certainly does not say that the State\xe2\x80\x99s redistricting plan will by some alchemy become federal law. No legislative drafter remotely familiar with the English language would say that a State had to follow default rules \xe2\x80\x9c[u]ntil [it] is redistricted in the manner provided by the law thereof,\xe2\x80\x9d when what he meant was \xe2\x80\x9cany redistricting plan that the State adopts shall become federal law.\xe2\x80\x9d And if the drafter was doing something as significant as transforming state law into federal law, he presumably would have taken care to make that dramatic step \xe2\x80\x9cunmistakably clear.\xe2\x80\x9d Gregory v. Ashcroft,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 452,\n460 (1991)\n (internal quotation marks omitted). Tellingly, our most recent case on the meaning of Section 2a(c) seems not to have even considered the majority\xe2\x80\x99s position. See Branch,\n\n538 U.\xc2\xa0S. 254\n.

\n

Indeed, the majority does not even seem persuaded by its own argument. The majority quickly cautions, in discussing Section 2a(c), that \xe2\x80\x9ca State is required to comply with the Federal Constitution, the Voting Rights Act, and other federal laws when it draws and implements its district map.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 22, n.\xc2\xa020. The majority therefore concludes that \xe2\x80\x9cnothing in \xc2\xa72a(c) affects a challenge to a state district map on the ground that it violates one or more of those federal requirements.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. But here the Arizona Legislature has challenged \xe2\x80\x9ca state district map on the ground that it violates one .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of those federal requirements\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94the Elections Clause. If we take the majority at its word, nothing in Section 2a(c) should affect that challenge.

\n

B

\n

Not only is the majority\xe2\x80\x99s reading of Section 2a(c) implausible as a matter of statutory interpretation, it would also likely violate the Constitution in multiple ways.

\n

First, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s reading of Section 2a(c) as a statute approving the lines drawn by the Commission would seemingly authorize Congress to alter the Elections Clause. The first part of the Elections Clause gives state legislatures the power to prescribe regulations regarding the times, places, and manner of elections; the second part of the Clause gives Congress the power to \xe2\x80\x9cmake or alter such Regulations.\xe2\x80\x9d There is a difference between making or altering election regulations prescribed by the state legislature and authorizing an entity other than the state legislature to prescribe election regulations. In essence, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s proposed reading permits Congress to use the second part of the Elections Clause to nullify the first. Yet this Court has expressly held that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress ha[s] no power to alter Article I, section 4 [the Elections Clause].\xe2\x80\x9d Smiley, 285 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 372; see also Clinton v. City of New York,\n\n524 U.\xc2\xa0S. 417 (1998)\n (Congress may not circumvent Article I constraints on its lawmaking power); Chadha,\n\n462 U.\xc2\xa0S. 919\n (same).

\n

Second, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of Section 2a(c) would create a serious delegation problem. As a general matter, Congress may pass statutes that delegate some discretion to those who administer the laws. It is a well-accepted principle, however, that Congress may not delegate authority to one actor when the Constitution vests that authority in another actor. See Whitman v. American Trucking Assns., Inc.,\n\n531 U.\xc2\xa0S. 457,\n472 (2001)\n. The majority\xe2\x80\x99s reading of Section 2a(c) contradicts that rule by allowing Congress to delegate federal redistricting authority to a state entity other than the one in which the Elections Clause vests that authority: \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Third, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation conflicts with our most recent Elections Clause precedent, Arizona v. Inter Tribal Council of Ariz., Inc.,\n\n570 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1 (2013)\n. There we explained that when Congress legislates under the Elections Clause, it \xe2\x80\x9cnecessarily displaces some element of a pre-existing legal regime erected by the States.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 11). That is so because \xe2\x80\x9cthe power the Elections Clause confers [on Congress] is none other than the power to pre-empt.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___, (slip op., at 11\xe2\x80\x9312). Put differently, \xe2\x80\x9call action under the Elections Clause displaces some element of a pre-existing state regulatory regime, because the text of the Clause confers the power to do exactly (and only) that.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___, n.\xc2\xa06 (slip op., at 11, n. 6). Under the majority\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of Section 2a(c), however, Congress has done the opposite of preempting or displacing state law\xe2\x80\x94it has adopted state law.

\n

Normally, when \xe2\x80\x9ca serious doubt of constitutionality is raised, it is a cardinal principle that this Court will first ascertain whether a construction of the statute is fairly possible by which the question may be avoided.\xe2\x80\x9d Crowell v. Benson,\n\n285 U.\xc2\xa0S. 22,\n62 (1932)\n. The multiple serious constitutional doubts raised by the majority\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of Section 2a(c)\xe2\x80\x94in addition to the sheer weakness of its reading as a textual matter\xe2\x80\x94provide more than enough reason to reject the majority\xe2\x80\x99s construction. Section 2a(c) does not apply to this case.

\n

III

\n

Justice Jackson once wrote that the Constitution speaks in \xe2\x80\x9cmajestic generalities.\xe2\x80\x9d West Virginia Bd. of Ed. v. Barnette,\n\n319 U.\xc2\xa0S. 624,\n639 (1943)\n. In many places it does, and so we have cases expounding on \xe2\x80\x9cfreedom of speech\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cunreasonable searches and seizures.\xe2\x80\x9d Amdts. 1, 4. Yet the Constitution also speaks in some places with elegant specificity. A Member of the House of Representatives must be 25 years old. Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa72, cl.\xc2\xa02. Every State gets two Senators. Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa73, cl.\xc2\xa01. And the times, places, and manner of holding elections for those federal representatives \xe2\x80\x9cshall be prescribed in each State by the Legislature thereof.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa74, cl.\xc2\xa01.

\n

For the reasons I have explained, there is no real doubt about what \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d means. The Framers of the Constitution were \xe2\x80\x9cpractical men, dealing with the facts of political life as they understood them, putting into form the government they were creating, and prescribing in language clear and intelligible the powers that government was to take.\xe2\x80\x9d South Carolina v. United States, 199 U.\xc2\xa0S. 437, 449 (1905). We ought to give effect to the words they used.

\n

The majority today shows greater concern about redistricting practices than about the meaning of the Constitution. I recognize the difficulties that arise from trying to fashion judicial relief for partisan gerrymandering. See Vieth v. Jubelirer,\n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 267 (2004)\n; ante, at 1. But our inability to find a manageable standard in that area is no excuse to abandon a standard of meaningful interpretation in this area. This Court has stressed repeatedly that a law\xe2\x80\x99s virtues as a policy innovation cannot redeem its inconsistency with the Constitution. \xe2\x80\x9cFailure of political will does not justify unconstitutional remedies.\xe2\x80\x9d Clinton, 524 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 449 (Kennedy, J., concurring); see Stern v. Marshall, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2011); Free Enterprise Fund v. Public Company Accounting Oversight Bd.,\n\n561 U.\xc2\xa0S. 477 (2010)\n; Bowsher v. Synar,\n\n478 U.\xc2\xa0S. 714 (1986)\n; Chadha,\n\n462 U.\xc2\xa0S. 919\n; Myers v. United States,\n\n272 U.\xc2\xa0S. 52 (1926)\n.

\n

Indeed, the Court has enforced the text of the Constitution to invalidate state laws with policy objectives reminiscent of this one. Two of our precedents held that States could not use their constitutions to impose term limits on their federal representatives in violation of the United States Constitution. Cook,\n\n531 U.\xc2\xa0S. 510\n; U.\xc2\xa0S. Term Limits, Inc. v. Thornton,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 779 (1995)\n. The people of the States that enacted these reforms surely viewed them as measures that would \xe2\x80\x9cplace the lead rein in the people\xe2\x80\x99s hands.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 27. Yet the Court refused to accept \xe2\x80\x9cthat the Framers spent significant time and energy in debating and crafting Clauses that could be easily evaded.\xe2\x80\x9d Term Limits, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 831. The majority approves just such an evasion of the Constitution today.[6]

\n

The Court also overstates the effects of enforcing the plain meaning of the Constitution in this case. There is no dispute that Arizona may continue to use its Commission to draw lines for state legislative elections. The representatives chosen in those elections will then be responsible for congressional redistricting as members of the state legislature, so the work of the Commission will continue to influence Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s federal representation.

\n

Moreover, reading the Elections Clause to require the involvement of the legislature will not affect most other redistricting commissions. As the majority notes, many States have commissions that play an \xe2\x80\x9cauxiliary role\xe2\x80\x9d in congressional redistricting. Ante, at 8, and nn. 8\xe2\x80\x939. But in these States, unlike in Arizona, the legislature retains primary authority over congressional redistricting. See Brief for National Conference of State Legislatures as Amicus Curiae 3\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

The majority also points to a scattered array of election-related laws and constitutional provisions enacted via popular lawmaking that it claims would be \xe2\x80\x9cendangered\xe2\x80\x9d by interpreting the Elections Clause to mean what it says. Ante, at 33. Reviewing the constitutionality of these farflung provisions is well outside the scope of this case. Suffice it to say that none of them purports to do what the Arizona Constitution does here: set up an unelected, unaccountable institution that permanently and totally displaces the legislature from the redistricting process. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]his wolf comes as a wolf.\xe2\x80\x9d Morrison v. Olson,\n\n487 U.\xc2\xa0S. 654,\n699 (1988)\n (Scalia, J., dissenting).

\n

Absent from the majority\xe2\x80\x99s portrayal of the high motives that inspired the Arizona Commission is any discussion of how it has actually functioned. The facts described in a recent opinion by a three-judge District Court detail the partisanship that has affected the Commission on issues ranging from staffing decisions to drawing the district lines. See Harris v. Arizona Independent Redistricting Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 993 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1042 (Ariz. 2014). The per curiam opinion explained that \xe2\x80\x9cpartisanship played some rolein the design of the map,\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9csome of the commissioners were motivated in part in some of the linedrawing decisions by a desire to improve Democratic prospects in the affected districts,\xe2\x80\x9d and that the Commission retained a mapping consultant that \xe2\x80\x9chad worked for Democratic, independent, and nonpartisan campaigns, but no Republican campaigns.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1046, 1047, 1053. The hiring of the mapping consultant provoked sufficient controversy that the Governor of Arizona, supported by two-thirds of the Arizona Senate, attempted to remove the chairwoman of the Commission for \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial neglect of duty and gross misconduct in office.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1057; see Arizona Independent Redistricting Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Brewer, 229 Ariz. 347, 275 P.\xc2\xa03d 1267 (2012) (explaining the removal and concluding that the Governor exceeded her authority under the Arizona Constitution).

\n

Judge Silver\xe2\x80\x99s separate opinion noted that \xe2\x80\x9cthe very structure of Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s reformed redistricting process reflects that partisanship still plays a prominent role.\xe2\x80\x9d 993 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1083. Judge Wake\xe2\x80\x99s separate opinion described the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csystematic overpopulation of Republican plurality districts and underpopulation of Democratic plurality districts\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cold-fashioned partisan malapportionment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1091, 1108. In his words, the \xe2\x80\x9cCommission has been coin-clipping the currency of our democracy\xe2\x80\x94everyone\xe2\x80\x99s equal vote\xe2\x80\x94and giving all the shavings to one party, for no valid reason.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1092.

\n

The District Court concluded by a two-to-one margin that this partisanship did not rise to the level of a constitutional violation. The case is pending on appeal before this Court, and I take no position on the merits question. But a finding that the partisanship in the redistricting plan did not violate the Constitution hardly proves that the Commission is operating free of partisan influence\xe2\x80\x94and certainly not that it complies with the Elections Clause.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

The people of Arizona have concerns about the process of congressional redistricting in their State. For better or worse, the Elections Clause of the Constitution does not allow them to address those concerns by displacing their legislature. But it does allow them to seek relief from Congress, which can make or alter the regulations prescribed by the legislature. And the Constitution gives them another means of change. They can follow the lead of the reformers who won passage of the\n \nSeventeenth Amendment. Indeed, several constitutional amendments over the past century have involved modifications of the electoral process. Amdts. 19, 22, 24, 26. Unfortunately, today\xe2\x80\x99s decision will only discourage this democratic method of change. Why go through the hassle of writing a new provision into the Constitution when it is so much easier to write an old one out?

\n

I respectfully dissent.

ARIZONA STATE LEGISLATURE, APPELLANT v. ARIZONA INDEPENDENT REDISTRICTING COMMISSION et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on appeal from the united states district court for the district of arizona

\n

[June 29, 2015]

\n

\n

Justice Thomas, with whom Justice Scalia joins, dissenting.

\n

Reading today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, one would think the Court is a great defender of direct democracy in the States. As it reads \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature\xe2\x80\x9d out of the Times, Places and Manner Clause, U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Art. I, \xc2\xa74, the majority offers a paean to the ballot initiative. It speaks in glowing terms of the \xe2\x80\x9ccharacteristic of our federal system that States retain autonomy to establish their own governmental processes.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 27. And it urges \xe2\x80\x9c[d]eference to state lawmaking\xe2\x80\x9d so that States may perform their vital function as \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98laboratories\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9dof democracy. Ante, at 28.

\n

These sentiments are difficult to accept. The conduct of the Court in so many other cases reveals a different attitude toward the States in general and ballot initiatives in particular. Just last week, in the antithesis of deference to state lawmaking through direct democracy, the Court cast aside state laws across the country\xe2\x80\x94many of which were enacted through ballot initiative\xe2\x80\x94that reflected the traditional definition of marriage. See Obergefell v. Hodges, ante, p. ___.

\n

This Court\xe2\x80\x99s tradition of disdain for state ballot initiatives goes back quite a while. Two decades ago, it held unconstitutional an Arkansas ballot initiative imposing term limits on that State\xe2\x80\x99s Members of Congress, finding \xe2\x80\x9clittle significance\xe2\x80\x9d in the fact that such term limits were adopted by popular referendum. U.\xc2\xa0S. Term Limits, Inc. v. Thornton,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 779,\n822, n.\n32 (1995)\n. One year later, it held unconstitutional a ballot initiative that would have prevented the enactment of laws under which \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98homosexual, lesbian or bisexual orientation, conduct, practices or relationships [would] constitute or otherwise be the basis of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any minority status, quota preferences, protected status or claim of discrimination.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Romer v. Evans,\n\n517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 620,\n624 (1996)\n. The Court neither gave deference to state lawmaking nor said anything about the virtues of direct democracy. It instead declared that the result of the ballot initiative was an aberration\xe2\x80\x94that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t is not within our constitutional tradition to enact laws of this sort.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 633. But if \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutional tradition\xe2\x80\x9d is the measuring stick, then it is hard to understand how the Court condones a redistricting practice that was unheard of for nearly 200 years after the ratification of the Constitution and that conflicts with the express constitutional command that election laws \xe2\x80\x9cbe prescribed in each State by the Legislature thereof,\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa74.

\n

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s lack of respect for ballot initiatives is evident not only in what it has done, but in what it has failed to do. Just this Term, the Court repeatedly refused to review cases in which the Courts of Appeals had set aside state laws passed through ballot initiative. See, e.g., County of Maricopa v. Lopez-Valenzuela, 575 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015) (Thomas, J., dissenting from denial of certiorari) (state constitutional amendment denying bail for illegal aliens arrested in certain circumstances); Herbert v. Kitchen, 574 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014) (state constitutional amendment retaining traditional definition of marriage); Smith v. Bishop, 574 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014) (same); Rainey v. Bostic, 574 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014) (same); Walker v. Wolf, 574 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014) (same). It did so despite warnings that its indifference to such cases would \xe2\x80\x9conly embolden the lower courts to reject state laws on questionable constitutional grounds.\xe2\x80\x9d Lopez-Valenzuela, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 2). And it refused to grant a stay pending appeal of a decision purporting to require the State of Alabama to issue marriage licenses to same-sex couples, even though Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s licensing laws had not been challenged in that case. See Strange v. Searcy, 574 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015) (Thomas, J., dissenting from denial of application for stay). In each decision, the cheers for direct democracy were conspicuously absent.

\n

Sometimes disapproval of ballot initiatives has been even more blatant. Just last Term, one dissenting opinion castigated the product of a state ballot initiative as \xe2\x80\x9cstymieing the right of racial minorities to participate in the political process.\xe2\x80\x9d Schuette v. BAMN, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (Sotomayor, J., joined by Ginsburg, J., dissenting) (slip op., at 1). It did not hail the ballot initiative as the result of a \xe2\x80\x9cState\xe2\x80\x99s empowerment of its people,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 19, nor offer any deference to state lawmaking. Instead, it complained that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he majority of Michigan voters changed the rules in the middle of the game, reconfiguring the existing political process .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Schuette, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 4). And it criticized state ballot initiatives as biased against racial minorities because such minorities \xe2\x80\x9cface an especially uphill battle\xe2\x80\x9d in seeking the passage of such initiatives. Id., at ___ (slip op., at 20). How quickly the tune has changed.

\n

And how striking that it changed here. The ballot initiative in this case, unlike those that the Court has previously treated so dismissively, was unusually democracy-reducing. It did not ask the people to approve a particular redistricting plan through direct democracy, but instead to take districting away from the people\xe2\x80\x99s representatives and give it to an unelected committee, thereby reducing democratic control over the process in the future. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s characterization of this as direct democracy at its best is rather like praising a plebiscite in a \xe2\x80\x9cbanana republic\xe2\x80\x9d that installs a strongman as President for Life. And wrapping the analysis in a cloak of federalism does little to conceal the flaws in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning.

\n

I would dispense with the faux federalism and would instead treat the States in an evenhanded manner. That means applying the Constitution as written. Although the straightforward text of Article I, \xc2\xa74, prohibits redistricting by an unelected, independent commission, Article III limits our power to deciding cases or controversies. Because I agree with Justice Scalia that the Arizona Legislature lacks Article III standing to assert an institutional injury against another entity of state government, I would dismiss its suit. I respectfully dissent.
