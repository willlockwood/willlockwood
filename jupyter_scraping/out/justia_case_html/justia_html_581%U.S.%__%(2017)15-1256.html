Syllabus

\n

NELSON v. COLORADO

\n

certiorari to the colorado supreme court

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x931256.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 9, 2017\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 19, 2017[1]

\n

Petitioner Shannon Nelson was convicted by a Colorado jury of two felonies and three misdemeanors arising from the alleged sexual and physical abuse of her four children. The trial court imposed a prison term of 20 years to life and ordered her to pay $8,192.50 in court costs, fees, and restitution. On appeal, Nelson\xe2\x80\x99s conviction was reversed for trial error, and on retrial, she was acquitted of all charges.

\n

Petitioner Louis Alonzo Madden was convicted by a Colorado jury of attempting to patronize a prostituted child and attempted sexual assault. The trial court imposed an indeterminate prison sentence and ordered him to pay $4,413.00 in costs, fees, and restitution. After one of Madden\xe2\x80\x99s convictions was reversed on direct review and the other vacated on postconviction review, the State elected not to appeal or retry the case.

\n

The Colorado Department of Corrections withheld $702.10 from Nelson\xe2\x80\x99s inmate account between her conviction and acquittal, and Madden paid the State $1,977.75 after his conviction. In both cases, the funds were allocated to costs, fees, and restitution. Once their convictions were invalidated, both petitioners moved for return of the funds. Nelson\xe2\x80\x99s trial court denied her motion outright, and Madden\xe2\x80\x99s postconviction court allowed a refund of costs and fees, but not restitution. The Colorado Court of Appeals concluded that both petitioners were entitled to seek refunds of all they had paid, but the Colorado Supreme Court reversed. It reasoned that Colorado\xe2\x80\x99s Compensation for Certain Exonerated Persons statute (Exoneration Act or Act), Colo. Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa713\xe2\x80\x9365\xe2\x80\x93101, 13\xe2\x80\x9365\xe2\x80\x93102, 13\xe2\x80\x9365\xe2\x80\x93103, provided the exclusive authority for refunds and that, because neither Nelson nor Madden had filed a claim under that Act, the courts lacked authority to order refunds. The Colorado Supreme Court also held that there was no due process problem under the Act, which permits Colorado to retain conviction-related assessments unless and until the prevailing defendant institutes a discrete civil proceeding and proves her innocence by clear and convincing evidence.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The Exoneration Act\xe2\x80\x99s scheme does not comport with the\n \nFourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s guarantee of due process. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0The procedural due process inspection required by Mathews v. Eldridge,\n\n424 U.\xc2\xa0S. 319\n, governs these cases. Medina v. California,\n\n505 U.\xc2\xa0S. 437\n, controls when state procedural rules that are part of the criminal process are at issue. These cases, in contrast, concern the continuing deprivation of property after a conviction has been reversed or vacated, with no prospect of reprosecution. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0The three considerations balanced under Mathews\xe2\x80\x94the private interest affected; the risk of erroneous deprivation of that interest through the procedures used; and the governmental interest at stake\xe2\x80\x94weigh decisively against Colorado\xe2\x80\x99s scheme. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

(1)\xc2\xa0Nelson and Madden have an obvious interest in regaining the money they paid to Colorado. The State may not retain these funds simply because Nelson\xe2\x80\x99s and Madden\xe2\x80\x99s convictions were in place when the funds were taken, for once those convictions were erased, the presumption of innocence was restored. See, e.g., Johnson v. Mississippi,\n\n486 U.\xc2\xa0S. 578\n. And Colorado may not presume a person, adjudged guilty of no crime, nonetheless guilty enough for monetary exactions. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

(2)\xc2\xa0Colorado\xe2\x80\x99s scheme creates an unacceptable risk of the erroneous deprivation of defendants\xe2\x80\x99 property. The Exoneration Act conditions refund on defendants\xe2\x80\x99 proof of innocence by clear and convincing evidence, but defendants in petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 position are presumed innocent. Moreover, the Act provides no remedy for assessments tied to invalid misdemeanor convictions. And when, as here, the recoupment amount sought is not large, the cost of mounting a claim under the Act and retaining counsel to pursue it would be prohibitive.

\n

Colorado argues that an Act that provides sufficient process to compensate a defendant for the loss of her liberty must suffice to compensate a defendant for the lesser deprivation of money. But Nelson and Madden seek the return of their property, not compensation for its temporary deprivation. Just as restoration of liberty on reversal of a conviction is not compensation, neither is the return of money taken by the State on account of the conviction. Other procedures cited by Colorado\xe2\x80\x94the need for probable cause to support criminal charges, the jury-trial right, and the State\xe2\x80\x99s burden to prove guilt beyond a reasonable doubt\xe2\x80\x94do not address the risk faced by a defendant whose conviction has been overturned that she will not recover funds taken from her based solely on a conviction no longer valid. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

(3)\xc2\xa0Colorado has no interest in withholding from Nelson and Madden money to which the State currently has zero claim of right. The State has identified no equitable considerations favoring its position, nor indicated any way in which the Exoneration Act embodies such considerations. P.\xc2\xa010.

\n

362 P.\xc2\xa03d 1070 (first judgment) and 364 P.\xc2\xa03d 866 (second judgment), reversed and remanded.

\n

Thomas, J., filed a dissenting opinion. Gorsuch, J., took no part in the consideration or decision of the cases.

SHANNON NELSON, PETITIONER v. COLORADO

\n

LOUIS A. MADDEN, PETITIONER v. COLORADO

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of colorado

\n

[April 19, 2017]

\n

Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

When a criminal conviction is invalidated by a reviewing court and no retrial will occur, is the State obliged to refund fees, court costs, and restitution exacted from the defendant upon, and as a consequence of, the conviction? Our answer is yes. Absent conviction of a crime, one is presumed innocent. Under the Colorado law before us in these cases, however, the State retains conviction-related assessments unless and until the prevailing defendant institutes a discrete civil proceeding and proves her innocence by clear and convincing evidence. This scheme, we hold, offends the\n \nFourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s guarantee of due process.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

Two cases are before us for review. Petitioner Shannon Nelson, in 2006, was convicted by a Colorado jury of five counts\xe2\x80\x94two felonies and three misdemeanors\xe2\x80\x94arising from the alleged sexual and physical abuse of her four children. 362 P.\xc2\xa03d 1070, 1071 (Colo. 2015); App. 25\xe2\x80\x9326. The trial court imposed a prison sentence of 20 years to life and ordered Nelson to pay court costs, fees, and restitution totaling $8,192.50. 362 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 1071. On appeal, Nelson\xe2\x80\x99s conviction was reversed for trial error. Ibid. On retrial, a new jury acquitted Nelson of all charges. Ibid.

\n

Petitioner Louis Alonzo Madden, in 2005, was convicted by a Colorado jury of attempting to patronize a prostituted child and attempted third-degree sexual assault by force. See 364 P.\xc2\xa03d 866, 867 (Colo. 2015). The trial court imposed an indeterminate prison sentence and ordered Madden to pay costs, fees, and restitution totaling $4,413.00. Ibid. The Colorado Supreme Court reversed one of Madden\xe2\x80\x99s convictions on direct review, and a postconviction court vacated the other. Ibid. The State elected not to appeal or retry the case. Ibid.

\n

Between Nelson\xe2\x80\x99s conviction and acquittal, the Colorado Department of Corrections withheld $702.10 from her inmate account, $287.50 of which went to costs and fees[1] and $414.60 to restitution. See 362 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 1071, and n.\xc2\xa01. Following Madden\xe2\x80\x99s conviction, Madden paid Colorado $1,977.75, $1,220 of which went to costs and fees[2] and $757.75 to restitution. See 364 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 867. The sole legal basis for these assessments was the fact of Nelson\xe2\x80\x99s and Madden\xe2\x80\x99s convictions.[3] Absent those convictions, Colorado would have no legal right to exact and retain petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 funds.

\n

Their convictions invalidated, both petitioners moved for return of the amounts Colorado had taken from them. In Nelson\xe2\x80\x99s case, the trial court denied the motion outright. 362 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 1071. In Madden\xe2\x80\x99s case, the postconviction court allowed the refund of costs and fees, but not restitution. 364 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 867\xe2\x80\x93868.

\n

The same Colorado Court of Appeals panel heard both cases and concluded that Nelson and Madden were entitled to seek refunds of all they had paid, including amounts allocated to restitution. See People v. Nelson, 369 P.\xc2\xa03d 625, 628\xe2\x80\x93629 (2013); People v. Madden, 2013 WL 1760869, *1 (Apr. 25, 2013). Costs, fees, and restitution, the court held, must be \xe2\x80\x9ctied to a valid conviction,\xe2\x80\x9d 369 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 627\xe2\x80\x93628, absent which a court must \xe2\x80\x9cretur[n] the defendant to the status quo ante,\xe2\x80\x9d 2013 WL 1760869, at *2.

\n

The Colorado Supreme Court reversed in both cases. A court must have statutory authority to issue a refund, that court stated. 362 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 1077; 364 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 868. Colorado\xe2\x80\x99s Compensation for Certain Exonerated Persons statute (Exoneration Act or Act), Colo. Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa713\xe2\x80\x9365\xe2\x80\x93101, 13\xe2\x80\x9365\xe2\x80\x93102, 13\xe2\x80\x9365\xe2\x80\x93103 (2016), passed in 2013, \xe2\x80\x9cprovides the proper procedure for seeking a refund,\xe2\x80\x9d the court ruled. 362 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 1075, 1077. As no other statute addresses refunds, the court concluded that the Exoneration Act is the \xe2\x80\x9cexclusive process for exonerated defendants seeking a refund of costs, fees, and restitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1078.[4] Because neither Nelson nor Madden had filed a claim under the Act, the court further determined, their trial courts lacked authority to order a refund. Id., at 1075, 1078; 364 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 867.[5] There was no due process problem, the court continued, because the Act \xe2\x80\x9cprovides sufficient process for defendants to seek refunds of costs, fees, and restitution that they paid in connection with their conviction.\xe2\x80\x9d 362 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 1078.

\n

Justice Hood dissented in both cases. Because neither petitioner has been validly convicted, he explained, each must be presumed innocent. Id., at 1079 (Nelson); 364 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 870 (adopting his reasoning from Nelson in Madden). Due process therefore requires some mechanism \xe2\x80\x9cfor the return of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s money,\xe2\x80\x9d Justice Hood maintained, 362 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 1080; as the Exoneration Act required petitioners to prove their innocence, the Act, he concluded, did not supply the remedy due process demands, id., at 1081. We granted certiorari. 579 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2016).

\n

B

\n

The Exoneration Act provides a civil claim for relief \xe2\x80\x9cto compensate an innocent person who was wrongly con-victed.\xe2\x80\x9d 362 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 1075. Recovery under the Act is avail-able only to a defendant who has served all or part of a term of incarceration pursuant to a felony conviction, and whose conviction has been overturned for reasons other than insufficiency of evidence or legal error unrelated to actual innocence. See \xc2\xa713\xe2\x80\x9365\xe2\x80\x93102. To succeed on an Exoneration Act claim, a petitioner must show, by clear and convincing evidence, her actual innocence of the offense of conviction. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa713\xe2\x80\x9365\xe2\x80\x93101(1), 13\xe2\x80\x9365\xe2\x80\x93102(1). A successful petitioner may recoup, in addition to compensation for time served,[6] \xe2\x80\x9cany fine, penalty, court costs, or restitution .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. paid .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. as a result of his or her wrongful conviction.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1075 (quoting \xc2\xa713\xe2\x80\x9365\xe2\x80\x93103(2)(e)(V)).

\n

Under Colorado\xe2\x80\x99s legislation, as just recounted, a defendant must prove her innocence by clear and convincing evidence to obtain the refund of costs, fees, and restitution paid pursuant to an invalid conviction. That scheme, we hold, does not comport with due process. Accordingly, we reverse the judgment of the Supreme Court of Colorado.

\n

II

\n

The familiar procedural due process inspection instructed by Mathews v. Eldridge,\n\n424 U.\xc2\xa0S. 319 (1976)\n, governs these cases. Colorado argues that we should instead apply the standard from Medina v. California,\n\n505 U.\xc2\xa0S. 437,\n445 (1992)\n, and inquire whether Nelson and Madden were exposed to a procedure offensive to a fundamental principle of justice. Medina \xe2\x80\x9cprovide[s] the appropriate framework for assessing the validity of state procedural rules\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9care part of the criminal process.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 443. Such rules concern, for example, the allocation of burdens of proof and the type of evidence qualifying as admissible.[7] These cases, in contrast, concern the continuing deprivation of property after a conviction has been reversed or vacated, with no prospect of reprosecution. See Kaley v. United States, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, n.\xc2\xa04 (2014) (Roberts, C.\xc2\xa0J., dissenting) (slip op., at 10\xe2\x80\x9311, n.\xc2\xa04) (explaining the different offices of Mathews and Medina). Because no further criminal process is implicated, Mathews \xe2\x80\x9cprovides the relevant inquiry.\xe2\x80\x9d 571 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 11, n.\xc2\xa04).

\n

III

\n

Under the Mathews balancing test, a court evaluates (A)\xc2\xa0the private interest affected; (B)\xc2\xa0the risk of erroneous deprivation of that interest through the procedures used; and (C)\xc2\xa0the governmental interest at stake. 424 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 335. All three considerations weigh decisively against Colorado\xe2\x80\x99s scheme.

\n

A

\n

Nelson and Madden have an obvious interest in regaining the money they paid to Colorado. Colorado urges, however, that the funds belong to the State because Nelson\xe2\x80\x99s and Madden\xe2\x80\x99s convictions were in place when the funds were taken. Tr. of Oral Arg. 29\xe2\x80\x9331. But once those convictions were erased, the presumption of their innocence was restored. See, e.g., Johnson v. Mississippi,\n\n486 U.\xc2\xa0S. 578,\n585 (1988)\n (After a \xe2\x80\x9cconviction has been reversed, unless and until [the defendant] should be retried, he must be presumed innocent of that charge.\xe2\x80\x9d).[8] \xe2\x80\x9c[A]xiomatic and elementary,\xe2\x80\x9d the presumption of innocence \xe2\x80\x9clies at the foundation of our criminal law.\xe2\x80\x9d Coffin v. United States,\n\n156 U.\xc2\xa0S. 432,\n453 (1895)\n.[9] Colorado may not retain funds taken from Nelson and Madden solely because of their now-invalidated convictions, see supra, at 2\xe2\x80\x933, and n.\xc2\xa03, for Colorado may not presume a person, adjudged guilty of no crime, nonetheless guilty enough for monetary exactions.[10]

\n

That petitioners prevailed on subsequent review rather than in the first instance, moreover, should be inconsequential. Suppose a trial judge grants a motion to set aside a guilty verdict for want of sufficient evidence. In that event, the defendant pays no costs, fees, or restitution. Now suppose the trial court enters judgment on a guilty verdict, ordering cost, fee, and restitution payments by reason of the conviction, but the appeals court upsets the conviction for evidentiary insufficiency. By what right does the State retain the amount paid out by the defendant? \xe2\x80\x9c[I]t should make no difference that the reviewing court, rather than the trial court, determined the evidence to be insufficient.\xe2\x80\x9d Burks v. United States,\n\n437 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1,\n11 (1978)\n. The vulnerability of the State\xe2\x80\x99s argument that it can keep the amounts exacted so long as it prevailed in the court of first instance is more apparent still if we assume a case in which the sole penalty is a fine. On Colorado\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning, an appeal would leave the defendant emptyhanded; regardless of the outcome of an appeal, the State would have no refund obligation. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 41, 44.[11]

\n

B

\n

Is there a risk of erroneous deprivation of defendants\xe2\x80\x99 interest in return of their funds if, as Colorado urges, the Exoneration Act is the exclusive remedy? Indeed yes, for the Act conditions refund on defendants\xe2\x80\x99 proof of innocence by clear and convincing evidence. \xc2\xa713\xe2\x80\x9365\xe2\x80\x93101(1)(a). But to get their money back, defendants should not be saddled with any proof burden. Instead, as explained supra, at 6\xe2\x80\x937, they are entitled to be presumed innocent.

\n

Furthermore, as Justice Hood noted in dissent, the Act provides no remedy at all for any assessments tied to invalid misdemeanor convictions (Nelson had three). 362 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 1081, n.\xc2\xa01; see \xc2\xa713\xe2\x80\x9365\xe2\x80\x93102(1)(a). And when amounts a defendant seeks to recoup are not large, as is true in Nelson\xe2\x80\x99s and Madden\xe2\x80\x99s cases, see supra, at 2, the cost of mounting a claim under the Exoneration Act and retaining a lawyer to pursue it would be prohibitive.[12]

\n

Colorado argued on brief that if the Exoneration Act provides sufficient process to compensate a defendant for the loss of her liberty, the Act should also suffice \xe2\x80\x9cwhen a defendant seeks compensation for the less significant deprivation of monetary assessments paid pursuant to a conviction that is later overturned.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 40. The comparison is inapt. Nelson and Madden seek restoration of funds they paid to the State, not compensation for temporary deprivation of those funds. Petitioners seek only their money back, not interest on those funds for the period the funds were in the State\xe2\x80\x99s custody. Just as the restoration of liberty on reversal of a conviction is not compensation, neither is the return of money taken by the State on account of the conviction.

\n

Colorado also suggests that \xe2\x80\x9cnumerous pre- and post-deprivation procedures\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94including the need for probable cause to support criminal charges, the jury-trial right, and the State\xe2\x80\x99s burden to prove guilt beyond a reasonable doubt\xe2\x80\x94adequately minimize the risk of erroneous deprivation of property. Id., at 31; see id., at 31\xe2\x80\x9335. But Colorado misperceives the risk at issue. The risk here involved is not the risk of wrongful or invalid conviction any criminal defendant may face. It is, instead, the risk faced by a defendant whose conviction has already been overturned that she will not recover funds taken from her solely on the basis of a conviction no longer valid. None of the above-stated procedures addresses that risk, and, as just explained, the Exoneration Act is not an adequate rem-edy for the property deprivation Nelson and Madden experienced.[13]

\n

C

\n

Colorado has no interest in withholding from Nelson and Madden money to which the State currently has zero claim of right. \xe2\x80\x9cEquitable [c]onsiderations,\xe2\x80\x9d Colorado suggests, may bear on whether a State may withhold funds from criminal defendants after their convictionsare overturned. Brief for Respondent 20\xe2\x80\x9322. Colorado, however, has identified no such consideration relevantto petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 cases, nor has the State indicated anyway in which the Exoneration Act embodies \xe2\x80\x9cequitable considerations.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

IV

\n

Colorado\xe2\x80\x99s scheme fails due process measurement because defendants\xe2\x80\x99 interest in regaining their funds is high, the risk of erroneous deprivation of those funds under the Exoneration Act is unacceptable, and the State has shown no countervailing interests in retaining the amounts in question. To comport with due process, a State may not impose anything more than minimal procedures on the refund of exactions dependent upon a conviction subsequently invalidated.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

The judgments of the Colorado Supreme Court are reversed, and the cases are remanded for further proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.

\n

\n

It is so ordered.

Wolfish,\n\n441 U.\xc2\xa0S. 520 (1979)\n, Colorado asserts that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he presumption of innocence applies only at criminal trials\xe2\x80\x9d and thus has no application here. Brief for Respondent 40, n.\xc2\xa019. Colorado misapprehends Wolfish. Our opinion in that case recognized that \xe2\x80\x9cunder the Due Process Clause,\xe2\x80\x9d a detainee who \xe2\x80\x9chas not been adjudged guilty of any crime\xe2\x80\x9d may not be punished. 441 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 535\xe2\x80\x93536; see id., at 535\xe2\x80\x93540. Wolfish held only that the presumption does not prevent the government from \xe2\x80\x9cdetain[ing a defendant] to ensure his presence at trial .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. so long as [the] conditions and restrictions [of his detention] do not amount to punishment, or otherwise violate the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 536\xe2\x80\x93537.\n\t\n
\n\n9\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0Were Medina applicable, Colorado\xe2\x80\x99s Exoneration Act scheme would similarly fail due process measurement. Under Medina, a criminal procedure violates due process if \xe2\x80\x9cit offends some principle of justice so rooted in the traditions and conscience of our people as to be ranked as fundamental.\xe2\x80\x9d 505 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 445 (quoting Patterson, 432 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 202). The presumption of innocence unquestionably fits that bill.\n\t\n
\n\n10\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0Colorado invites a distinction between convictions merely \xe2\x80\x9cvoid-able,\xe2\x80\x9d rather than \xe2\x80\x9cvoid,\xe2\x80\x9d and urges that the invalidated convictions here fall in the voidable category. See Brief for Respondent 32\xe2\x80\x9333, and n.\xc2\xa011. As Justice Hood noted in dissent, however, \xe2\x80\x9creversal is reversal,\xe2\x80\x9d regardless of the reason, \xe2\x80\x9c[a]nd an invalid conviction is no conviction at all.\xe2\x80\x9d 362 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 1080.\n\t\n
\n\n11\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0The dissent echoes Colorado\xe2\x80\x99s argument. If Nelson and Madden prevailed at trial, the dissent agrees, no costs, fees, or restitution could be exacted. See post, at 6. But if they prevailed on appellate inspection, the State gets to keep their money. See ibid. Under Colorado law, as the dissent reads the Colorado Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, \xe2\x80\x9cmoneys lawfully exacted pursuant to a valid conviction become public funds (or[, in the case of restitution,] the victims\xe2\x80\x99 money).\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 3\xe2\x80\x934. Shut from the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s sights, however, the convictions pursuant to which the State took petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 money were invalid, hence the State had no legal right to retain their money. Given the invalidity of the convictions, does the Exoneration Act afford sufficient process to enable the State to retain the money? Surely, it does not.\n\t\n
\n\n12\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0A successful petitioner under the Exoneration Act can recover reasonable attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees, \xc2\xa713\xe2\x80\x9365\xe2\x80\x93103(2)(e)(IV), but neither a defendant nor counsel is likely to assume the risk of loss when amounts to be gained are not worth the candle.\n\t\n
\n\n13\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0Colorado additionally argues that defendants can request a stay of sentence pending appeal, thereby reducing the risk of erroneous deprivation. See Brief for Respondent 32; \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa716\xe2\x80\x9312\xe2\x80\x93103, 18\xe2\x80\x931.3\xe2\x80\x93702(1)(a) (2016). But the State acknowledged at oral argument that few defendants can meet the requirements a stay pending appeal entails. Tr. of Oral Arg. 33\xe2\x80\x9334. And even when a stay is available, a trial court \xe2\x80\x9cmay require the defendant to deposit the whole or any part of the .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. costs.\xe2\x80\x9d Colo. App. Rule 8.1(a)(3) (2016).\n\t\n

SHANNON NELSON, PETITIONER v. COLORADO

\n

LOUIS A. MADDEN, PETITIONER v. COLORADO

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of colorado

\n

[April 19, 2017]

\n

\n

Justice Alito, concurring in the judgment.

\n

I agree that the judgments of the Colorado Supreme Court must be reversed, but I reach that conclusion by a different route.

\n

I

\n

The proper framework for analyzing these cases is provided by Medina v. California,\n\n505 U.\xc2\xa0S. 437 (1992)\n. Medina applies when we are called upon to \xe2\x80\x9casses[s] the validity of state procedural rules which .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. are part of the criminal process,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 443, and that is precisely the situation here. These cases concern Colorado\xe2\x80\x99s rules for determining whether a defendant can obtain a refund of money that he or she was required to pay pursuant to a judgment of conviction that is later reversed. In holding that these payments must be refunded, the Court relies on a feature of the criminal law, the presumption of innocence. And since the Court demands that refunds occur either automatically or at least without imposing anything more than \xe2\x80\x9cminimal\xe2\x80\x9d procedures, see ante, at 10, it appears that they must generally occur as part of the criminal case. For these reasons, the refund obligation is surely \xe2\x80\x9cpart of the criminal process\xe2\x80\x9d and thus falls squarely within the scope of Medina. The only authority cited by the Court in support of its contrary conclusion is a footnote in a dissent. See ante, at 6 (citing Kaley v. United States, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, n.\xc2\xa04 (2014) (opinion of Roberts, C. J.) (slip op., at 10\xe2\x80\x9311, n.\xc2\xa04)). Under Medina, a state rule of criminal procedure not governed by a specific rule set out in the Bill of Rights violates the Due Process Clause of the\n \nFourteenth Amendment only if it offends a fundamental and deeply rooted principle of justice. 505 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 445. And \xe2\x80\x9c[h]istorical practice is probative of whether a procedural rule can be characterized as fundamental.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 446. Indeed, petitioners invite us to measure the Colorado scheme against traditional practice, reminding us that our \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98first due process cases\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d recognized that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98traditional practice provides a touchstone for constitutional analysis,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 26 (quoting Honda Motor Co. v. Oberg,\n\n512 U.\xc2\xa0S. 415,\n430 (1994)\n). Petitioners then go on to argue at some length that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he traditional rule has always been that when a judgment is reversed, a person who paid money pursuant to that judgment is entitled to receive the money back.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 26; see id., at 26\xe2\x80\x9330. See also Brief for National Association of Criminal Defense Lawyers as Amicus Curiae 4\xe2\x80\x9314 (discussing traditional practice).

\n

The Court, by contrast, turns its back on historical practice, preferring to balance the competing interests according to its own lights. The Court applies the balancing test set out in Mathews v. Eldridge,\n\n424 U.\xc2\xa0S. 319 (1976)\n, a modern invention \xe2\x80\x9cfirst conceived\xe2\x80\x9d to decide what procedures the government must observe before depriving persons of novel forms of property such as welfare or Social Security disability benefits. Dusenbery v. United States,\n\n534 U.\xc2\xa0S. 161,\n167 (2002)\n. Because these interests had not previously been regarded as \xe2\x80\x9cproperty,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court could not draw on historical practice for guidance. Mathews has subsequently been used more widely in civil cases, but we should pause before applying its balancing test in matters of state criminal procedure. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he States have considerable expertise in matters of criminal procedure and the criminal process is grounded in centuries of common-law tradition.\xe2\x80\x9d Medina, supra, at 445\xe2\x80\x93446. Applying the Mathews balancing test to established rules of criminal practice and procedure may result in \xe2\x80\x9cundue interference with both considered legislative judgments and the careful balance that the Constitution strikes between liberty and order.\xe2\x80\x9d Medina, supra, at 443. Where long practice has struck a particular balance between the competing interests of the State and those charged with crimes, we should not lightly disturb that determination. For these reasons, Medina\xe2\x80\x99s historical inquiry, not Mathews, provides the proper framework for use in these cases.[1]

\n

II

\n

Under Medina, the Colorado scheme at issue violates due process. American law has long recognized that when an individual is obligated by a civil judgment to pay money to the opposing party and that judgment is later reversed, the money should generally be repaid. See, e.g., Northwestern Fuel Co. v. Brock,\n\n139 U.\xc2\xa0S. 216,\n219 (1891)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cThe right of restitution of what one has lost by the enforcement of a judgment subsequently reversed has been recognized in the law of England from a very early period .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. \xe2\x80\x9d); Bank of United States v. Bank of Washington, 6 Pet. 8, 17 (1832) (\xe2\x80\x9cOn the reversal of an erroneous judgment, the law raises an obligation in the party to the record, who has received the benefit of the erroneous judgment, to make restitution to the other party for what he has lost\xe2\x80\x9d). This was \xe2\x80\x9ca remedy well known at common law,\xe2\x80\x9d memorialized as \xe2\x80\x9ca part of the judgment of reversal which directed \xe2\x80\x98that the defendant be restored to all things which he has lost on occasion of the judgment aforesaid.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 2 Ruling Case Law \xc2\xa7248, p. 297 (W. McKinney and B. Rich eds. 1914); Duncan v. Kirkpatrick, 13 Serg. & Rawle 292, 294 (Pa. 1825).

\n

As both parties acknowledge, this practice carried over to criminal cases. When a conviction was reversed, defendants could recover fines and monetary penalties assessed as part of the conviction. Brief for Respondent 20\xe2\x80\x9321, and n.\xc2\xa07; Reply Brief 7\xe2\x80\x938, 11; see, e.g., Annot., Right To Recover Back Fine or Penalty Paid in Criminal Proceeding, 26 A. L. R. 1523, 1532, \xc2\xa7VI(a) (1923) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhen a judgment imposing a fine, which is paid, is vacated or reversed on appeal, the court may order restitution of the amount paid .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. \xe2\x80\x9d); 25 C.\xc2\xa0J. \xc2\xa739, p. 1165 (W. Mack, W. Hale, & D. Kiser eds. 1921) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhere a fine illegally imposed has been paid, on reversal of the judgment a writ of restitution may issue against the parties who received the fine\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

The rule regarding recovery, however, \xe2\x80\x9ceven though general in its application, [was] not without exceptions.\xe2\x80\x9d Atlantic Coast Line R. Co. v. Florida,\n\n295 U.\xc2\xa0S. 301,\n309 (1935)\n (Cardozo, J.). The remedy was \xe2\x80\x9cequitable in origin and function,\xe2\x80\x9d and return of the money was \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98not of mere right,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d but \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98rest[ed] in the exercise of a sound discretion.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 309, 310 (quoting Gould v. McFall, 118 Pa. 455, 456 (1888)). This was true in both civil and criminal cases. See, e.g., 25 C.\xc2\xa0J., at 1165 (noting that \xe2\x80\x9crestitution [of fines paid on a conviction later reversed] is not necessarily a matter of right\xe2\x80\x9d); Annot., 26 A. L. R., at 1532, \xc2\xa7VI(a) (Restitution for fines upon reversal of a conviction \xe2\x80\x9cis not a matter of strict legal right, but rather one for the exercise of the court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion\xe2\x80\x9d). The central question courts have asked is whether \xe2\x80\x9cthe possessor will give offense to equity and good conscience if permitted to retain [the successful appellant\xe2\x80\x99s money].\xe2\x80\x9d Atlantic Coast Line, supra, at 309.

\n

This history supports the Court\xe2\x80\x99s rejection of the Colorado Exoneration Act\xe2\x80\x99s procedures. The Act places a heavy burden of proof on defendants, provides no opportunity for a refund for defendants (like Nelson) whose misdemeanor convictions are reversed, and excludes defendants whose convictions are reversed for reasons unrelated to innocence. Brief for Respondent 8, 35, n.\xc2\xa018. These stringent requirements all but guarantee that most defendants whose convictions are reversed have no realistic opportunity to prove they are deserving of refunds. Colorado has abandoned historical procedures that were more generous to successful appellants and incorporated a court\xe2\x80\x99s case-specific equitable judgment. Instead, Colorado has adopted a system that is harsh, inflexible, and prevents most defendants whose convictions are reversed from demonstrating entitlement to a refund. Indeed, the Colorado General Assembly made financial projections based on the assumption that only one person every five years would qualify for a financial award under the Exoneration Act. Colorado Legislative Council Staff Fiscal Note, State and Local Revised Fiscal Impact, HB 13\xe2\x80\x931230, p.\xc2\xa02 (Apr. 22, 2013), online at http://leg.colorado.gov (as last visited Apr. 17, 2017). Accordingly, the Exoneration Act does not satisfy due process requirements. See Cooper v. Oklahoma,\n\n517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 348,\n356 (1996)\n (A state rule of criminal procedure may violate due process where \xe2\x80\x9ca rule significantly more favorable to the defendant has had a long and consistent application\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

III

\n

Although long-established practice supports the Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment, the Court rests its decision on different grounds. In its Mathews analysis, the Court reasons that the reversal of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 convictions restored the presumption of their innocence and that \xe2\x80\x9cColorado may not presume a person, adjudged guilty of no crime, nonetheless guilty enough for monetary exactions.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 7. The implication of this brief statement is that under Mathews, reversal restores the defendant to the status quo ante, see ante, at 3. But the Court does not confront the obvious implications of this reasoning.

\n

For example, if the status quo ante must be restored, why shouldn\xe2\x80\x99t the defendant be compensated for all the adverse economic consequences of the wrongful conviction?[2] After all, in most cases, the fines and payments that a convicted defendant must pay to the court are minor in comparison to the losses that result from conviction and imprisonment, such as attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees, lost income, and damage to reputation. The Court cannot convincingly explain why Mathews\xe2\x80\x99 amorphous balancing test stops short of requiring a full return to the status quo ante when a conviction is reversed. But Medina does.

\n

The American legal system has long treated compensation for the economic consequences of a reversed conviction very differently from the refund of fines and other payments made by a defendant pursuant to a criminal judgment. Statutes providing compensation for time wrongfully spent in prison are a 20th-century innovation: By 1970, only the Federal Government and four States had passed such laws. King, Compensation of Persons Erroneously Confined by the State, 118 U.\xc2\xa0Pa. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1091, 1109 (1970); United States v. Keegan, 71 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 623, 626 (SDNY 1947) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]here seems to have been no legislation by our Government on this subject\xe2\x80\x9d until 1938). Many other jurisdictions have done so since, but under most such laws, compensation is not automatic. Instead, the defendant bears the burden of proving actual innocence (and, sometimes, more). King, supra, at 1110 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe burden of proving innocence in the compensation proceeding has from the start been placed upon the claimant\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Kahn, Presumed Guilty Until Proven Innocent: The Burden of Proof in Wrongful Conviction Claims Under State Compensation Statutes, 44 U. Mich. J.\xc2\xa0L. Reform 123, 145 (2010) (Most U.\xc2\xa0S. compensation statutes \xe2\x80\x9crequire that claimants prove their innocence either by a preponderance of the evidence or by clear and convincing evidence\xe2\x80\x9d (footnote omitted)). In construing the federal statute, courts have held that a compensation proceeding \xe2\x80\x9cis not .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a criminal trial\xe2\x80\x9d and that the burden of proof can be placed on the petitioner. United States v. Brunner, 200 F.\xc2\xa02d 276, 279 (CA6 1952). As noted, Colorado and many other States have similar statutes designed narrowly to compensate those few persons who can demonstrate that they are truly innocent. The Court apparently acknowledges that these statutes pose no constitutional difficulty. That is the correct conclusion, but it is best justified by reference to history and tradition.

\n

IV

\n

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s disregard of historical practice is particu-larly damaging when it comes to the question of restitution. The Court flatly declares that the State is \xe2\x80\x9cobliged to refund .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. restitution\xe2\x80\x9d in just the same way as fees and court costs. Ante, at 1. This conclusion is not supported by historical practice, and it overlooks important differences between restitution, which is paid to the victims of an offense, and fines and other payments that are kept by the State.

\n

Although restitution may be included in a criminal judgment, it has many attributes of a civil judgment in favor of the victim. This is clear under Colorado law. Although the obligation to pay restitution is included in the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s sentence, restitution results in a final civil judgment against the defendant in favor of the State and the victim. Colo. Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa718\xe2\x80\x931.3\xe2\x80\x93603(4)(a)(I) (2016). Entitlement to restitution need not be established beyond a reasonable doubt or in accordance with standard rules of evidence or criminal procedure. People v. Pagan, 165 P.\xc2\xa03d 724, 729 (Colo. App. 2006); Colo. Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa718\xe2\x80\x931.3\xe2\x80\x93603(2)\xe2\x80\x93(3). And the judgment may be enforced either by the State or the victim. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa716\xe2\x80\x9318.5\xe2\x80\x93106(2), \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa716\xe2\x80\x9318.5\xe2\x80\x93107(1)\xe2\x80\x93(4).

\n

The Court ignores the distinctive attributes of restitution, but they merit attention. Because a restitution order is much like a civil judgment, the reversal of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s criminal conviction does not necessarily undermine the basis for restitution. Suppose that a victim successfully sues a criminal defendant civilly and introduces the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s criminal conviction on the underlying conduct as (potentially preclusive) evidence establishing an essential element of a civil claim. See, e.g., 2 K. Broun, McCormick on Evidence \xc2\xa7298, 473\xe2\x80\x93477 (7th ed. 2013) (discussing the admissibility, and potential preclusive effect, of a criminal conviction in subsequent civil litigation). And suppose that the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s criminal conviction is later reversed for a trial error that did not (and could not) infect the later civil proceeding: for example, the admission of evidence barred by the exclusionary rule or a Confrontation Clause violation. It would be unprecedented to suggest that due process requires unwinding the civil judgment simply because it rests in part on a criminal conviction that has since been reversed. And a very similar scenario could unfold with respect to a Colorado restitution judgment. The only salient difference would be that, in the Colorado case, the civil judgment would have been obtained as part of the criminal proceeding itself. It is not clear (and the Court certainly does not explain) why that formal distinction should make a substantive difference.[3]

\n

It is especially startling to insist that a State must provide a refund after enforcing a restitution judgment on the victims\xe2\x80\x99 behalf in reliance on a final judgment that is then vacated on collateral review. Faced with this fact pattern, the Ninth Circuit declined to require reimbursement, reasoning that the Government was a mere \xe2\x80\x9cescrow agent\xe2\x80\x9d executing a then-valid final judgment in favor of a third party. United States v. Hayes, 385 F.\xc2\xa03d 1226, 1230 (2004).

\n

The Court regrettably mentions none of this. Its treatment of restitution is not grounded in any historical analysis, and\xe2\x80\x94save for a brief footnote, ante, at 2\xe2\x80\x933, n.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x94the Court does not account for the distinctive civil status of restitution under Colorado law (or the laws of the many other affected jurisdictions that provide this remedy to crime victims).

\n

Nor does the Court consider how restitution\xe2\x80\x99s unique characteristics might affect the balance that it strikes under Mathews. Ante, at 10. The Court summarily rejects the proposition that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98equitable considerations\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d might militate against a blanket rule requiring the refund of money paid as restitution, see ibid., but why is this so? What if the evidence amply establishes that the defendant injured the victims to whom restitution was paid but the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conviction is reversed on a ground that would be inapplicable in a civil suit? In that situation, is it true, as the Court proclaims, that the State would have \xe2\x80\x9cno interest\xe2\x80\x9d in withholding a refund? Would the Court reach that conclusion if state law mandated a refund from the recipients of the restitution? And if the States and the Federal Government are always required to foot the bill themselves, would that risk discourage them from seeking restitution\xe2\x80\x94or at least from providing funds to victims until the conclusion of appellate review?

\n

It was unnecessary for the Court to issue a sweeping pronouncement on restitution. But if the Court had to address this subject to dispose of these cases, it should have acknowledged that\xe2\x80\x94at least in some circumstances\xe2\x80\x94refunds of restitution payments made under later reversed judgments are not constitutionally required.

\n

\n

For these reasons, I concur only in the judgment.

\n

\n

Justice Thomas, dissenting.

\n

The majority and concurring opinions debate whether the procedural due process framework of Mathews v. Eldridge,\n\n424 U.\xc2\xa0S. 319 (1976)\n, or that of Medina v. California,\n\n505 U.\xc2\xa0S. 437 (1992)\n, governs the question before us. But both opinions bypass the most important question in these cases: whether petitioners can show a substantive entitlement to a return of the money they paid pursuant to criminal convictions that were later reversed or vacated.

\n

The Court assumes, without reference to either state or federal law, that defendants whose convictions have been reversed have a substantive right to any money exacted on the basis of those convictions. By doing so, the Court assumes away the real issue in these cases. As the parties have agreed, the existence of Colorado\xe2\x80\x99s obligation to provide particular procedures depends on whether petitioners have a substantive entitlement to the money. Colorado concedes that \xe2\x80\x9cif [petitioners] have a present entitlement\xe2\x80\x9d to the money\xe2\x80\x94that is, if \xe2\x80\x9cit is their prop-erty\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cthen due process requires [the State to accord] themsome procedure to get it back.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 52. And Colorado acknowledges that the procedural hurdles it could impose before returning the money \xe2\x80\x9cwould be fairly minimal,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 51, because petitioners would need to prove only that their convictions had been reversed and that they had paid a certain sum of money, see ibid. Similarly, petitioners concede that if defendants in their position do not have a substantive right to recover the money\xe2\x80\x94that is, if the money belongs to the State\xe2\x80\x94then Colorado need not \xe2\x80\x9cprovide any procedure to give it back.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 53. If defendants in their position have no entitlement to the money they paid pursuant to their reversed convictions, there would be nothing to adjudicate. In light of these concessions, I can see no justification for the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to address the procedures for adjudicating a substantive entitlement while failing to determine whether a substantive entitlement exists in the first place.

\n

In my view, petitioners have not demonstrated that defendants whose convictions have been reversed possess a substantive entitlement, under either state law or the Constitution, to recover money they paid to the State pursuant to their convictions. Accordingly, I cannot agree with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to reverse the judgments of the Colorado Supreme Court.

\n

I

\n

The\n \nFourteenth Amendment provides that no State shall \xe2\x80\x9cdeprive any person of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law.\xe2\x80\x9d U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Amdt. 14, \xc2\xa71 (emphasis added).[1] To show that Colorado has violated the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s procedural guarantees, as relevant here, petitioners must first establish that they have been deprived of a protected property interest. See Castle Rock v. Gonzales,\n\n545 U.\xc2\xa0S. 748,\n756 (2005)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cThe procedural component of the Due Process Clause does not protect everything that might be described as a benefit: To have a property interest in a benefit, a person clearly must have .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a legitimate claim of entitlement to it\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). \xe2\x80\x9cBecause the Constitution protects rather than creates property interests, the existence of a property interest is determined by reference to \xe2\x80\x98existing rules or understandings that stem from an independent source such as state law.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Phillips v. Washington Legal Foundation,\n\n524 U.\xc2\xa0S. 156,\n164 (1998)\n (quoting Board of Regents of State Colleges v. Roth,\n\n408 U.\xc2\xa0S. 564,\n577 (1972)\n). Petitioners undoubtedly have an \xe2\x80\x9cinterest in regaining the money they paid to Colorado.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 6. But to succeed on their procedural due process claim, petitioners must first point to a recognized property interest in that money, under state or federal law, within the meaning of the\n \nFourteenth Amendment.

\n

A

\n

The parties dispute whether, under Colorado law, the petitioners or the State have a property interest in the money paid by petitioners pursuant to their convictions. Petitioners contend that the money remains their property under state law. Reply Brief 1\xe2\x80\x933; see also Tr. of Oral Arg. 52\xe2\x80\x9354. Colorado counters that when petitioners paid the money pursuant to their convictions, the costs and fees became property of the State and the restitution became property of the victims. See id., at 28\xe2\x80\x9330; Brief forRespondent 41.

\n

The key premise of the Colorado Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s holdings in these cases is that moneys lawfully exacted pursuant to a valid conviction become public funds (or the victims\xe2\x80\x99 money) under Colorado law. The Colorado Supreme Court explained in petitioner Shannon Nelson\xe2\x80\x99s case that \xe2\x80\x9cthe trial court properly ordered [her] to pay costs, fees, and restitution pursuant to valid statutes\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9cthe court correctly distributed th[ose] funds to victims and public funds, as ordered by the statutes.\xe2\x80\x9d 362 P.\xc2\xa03d 1070, 1076 (2015) (emphasis added); accord, 364 P.\xc2\xa03d 866, 868\xe2\x80\x93870 (2016) (applying the same analysis to petitioner Louis Madden\xe2\x80\x99s case). The Colorado Supreme Court further noted that, \xe2\x80\x9c[o]nce the state disburses restitution to the victims, the state no longer controls that money.\xe2\x80\x9d 362 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 1077, n.\xc2\xa04.

\n

The Colorado Supreme Court explained that \xe2\x80\x9cColorado\xe2\x80\x99s constitution protects\xe2\x80\x9d the Colorado Legislature\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccontrol over public money,\xe2\x80\x9d and thus a \xe2\x80\x9ccourt may authorize refunds from public funds only pursuant to statutory authority.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1076\xe2\x80\x931077. The Exoneration Act, the Colorado Supreme Court held, provides the only statutory authority for refunding costs, fees, and restitution when a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conviction is overturned. Id., at 1077\xe2\x80\x931078. Because petitioners had not sought a refund under the Exoneration Act, \xe2\x80\x9cthe trial court lacked the authority to order a refund of Nelson\xe2\x80\x99s costs, fees, and restitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1078; 364 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 867.

\n

At no point in this litigation have petitioners attempted to demonstrate that they satisfy the requirements of the Exoneration Act. Under the Act, Colorado recognizes a substantive entitlement to the kind of property at issue in these cases only if, among other things, the defendant can prove that he is \xe2\x80\x9cactually innocent.\xe2\x80\x9d[2] Colo. Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa713\xe2\x80\x9365\xe2\x80\x93101, 13\xe2\x80\x9365\xe2\x80\x93102 (2016). It is the Exoneration Act alone which defines the scope of the substantive entitlement. This Court has interpreted the Due Process Clause to require that the States provide certain procedures, such as notice and a hearing, by which an individual can prove a substantive entitlement to (or defend against a deprivation of\xc2\xa0) property. But the Clause, properly understood, has nothing to say about the existence or scope of the substantive entitlement itself. See Part I\xe2\x80\x93B, infra. If petitioners want this Court to rewrite the contours ofthe substantive entitlement contained in the Exoneration Act, they err in invoking procedural due process. See Reply Brief 1\xe2\x80\x932 (\xe2\x80\x9cOur argument sounds in procedural due process\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

The majority responds by asserting, without citing any state law, that Colorado \xe2\x80\x9chad no legal right to retain [petitioners\xe2\x80\x99] money\xe2\x80\x9d once their convictions were invalidated. Ante, at 8, n.\xc2\xa011. If this were true as a matter of state law, then certain provisions of the Exoneration Act\xe2\x80\x94which require the State to return costs, fees, and restitution only in limited circumstances following a conviction\xe2\x80\x99s reversal\xe2\x80\x94would be superfluous. Thus, to the extent the majority implicitly suggests that petitioners have a state-law right to an automatic refund (a point about which the majority is entirely unclear), it is plainly incorrect.

\n

B

\n

Because defendants in petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 position do not have a substantive right to recover the money they paid to Colorado under state law, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 asserted right to an automatic refund must arise, if at all, from the Due Process Clause itself. But the Due Process Clause confers no substantive rights. McDonald v. Chicago,\n\n561 U.\xc2\xa0S. 742\n, 811 (2010) (Thomas, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe notion that a constitutional provision that guarantees only \xe2\x80\x98process\xe2\x80\x99 before a person is deprived of life, liberty, or property could define the substance of those rights strains credulity for even the most casual user of words\xe2\x80\x9d). And, in any event, petitioners appear to disavow any substantive due process right to a return of the funds they paid. See Reply Brief 1\xe2\x80\x932; Tr. of Oral Arg. 18\xe2\x80\x9319. In the absence of any property right under state law (apart from the right provided by the Exoneration Act, which petitioners decline to invoke), Colorado\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to return the money is not a \xe2\x80\x9cdepriv[ation]\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9cproperty\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning of the\n \nFourteenth Amendment. Colorado is therefore not required to provide any process at all for the return of that money.

\n

II

\n

No one disputes that if petitioners had never been convicted, Colorado could not have required them to pay the money at issue. And no one disputes that Colorado cannot require petitioners to pay any additional costs, fees, or restitution now that their convictions have been invali-dated. It does not follow, however, that petitioners havea property right in the money they paid pursuant to theirthen-valid convictions, which now belongs to the State and the victims under Colorado law. The Court today announces that petitioners have a right to an automatic refund because the State has \xe2\x80\x9cno legal right\xe2\x80\x9d to that money. Ante, at 8, n.\xc2\xa011. But, intuitive and rhetorical appeal aside, it does not seriously attempt to ground that conclusion in state or federal law. If petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 supposed right to an automatic refund arises under Colorado law, then the Colorado Supreme Court remains free on remand to clarify whether that right in fact exists. If it arises under substantive due process, then the Court\xe2\x80\x99s procedural due process analysis misses the point.

\n

I respectfully dissent.
