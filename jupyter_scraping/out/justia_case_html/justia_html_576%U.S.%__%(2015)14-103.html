BAKER BOTTS L.L.P. et\xc2\xa0al. v. ASARCO LLC

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit

\n

No. 14\xe2\x80\x93103.\xe2\x80\x83Argued February 25, 2015\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 15, 2015

\n

Respondent ASARCO LLC hired petitioner law firms pursuant to \xc2\xa7327(a) of the Bankruptcy Code to assist it in carrying out its duties as a Chapter 11 debtor in possession. See\n\n11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7327(a). When ASARCO emerged from bankruptcy, the law firms filed fee applications requesting fees under \xc2\xa7330(a)(1), which permits bankruptcy courts to \xe2\x80\x9caward .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. reasonable compensation for actual, necessary services rendered by\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7327(a) professionals. ASARCO challenged the applications, but the Bankruptcy Court rejected ASARCO\xe2\x80\x99s objections and awarded the law firms fees for time spent defending the applications. ASARCO appealed to the District Court, which held that the law firms could be awarded fees for defending their fee applications. The Fifth Circuit reversed, holding that \xc2\xa7330(a)(1) did not authorize fee awards for defending fee applications.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Section \xc2\xa7330(a)(1) does not permit bankruptcy courts to award fees to \xc2\xa7327(a) professionals for defending fee applications. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0The American Rule provides the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98basic point of reference\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d for awards of attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees: \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Each litigant pays his own attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees, win or lose, unless a statute or contract provides otherwise.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Hardt v. Reliance Standard Life Ins. Co.,\n\n560 U.\xc2\xa0S. 242\n\xe2\x80\x93253. Because the rule is deeply rooted in the common law, see, e.g., Arcambel v. Wiseman, 3 Dall. 306, this Court will not deviate from it \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98absent explicit statutory authority,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Buckhannon Board & Care Home, Inc. v. West Virginia Dept. of Health and Human Resources,\n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 598\n. Departures from the American Rule have been recognized only in \xe2\x80\x9cspecific and explicit provisions,\xe2\x80\x9d Alyeska Pipeline Service Co. v. Wilderness Society,\n\n421 U.\xc2\xa0S. 240\n, usually containing language that authorizes the award of \xe2\x80\x9ca reasonable attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fee,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cfees,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9clitigation costs,\xe2\x80\x9d and referring to a \xe2\x80\x9cprevailing party\xe2\x80\x9d in the context of an adversarial \xe2\x80\x9caction,\xe2\x80\x9d see generally Hardt, supra, at 253, and nn. 3\xe2\x80\x937. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x934.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0Congress did not depart from the American Rule in \xc2\xa7330(a)(1) for fee-defense litigation. Section 327(a) professionals are hired to serve an estate\xe2\x80\x99s administrator for the benefit of the estate, and \xc2\xa7330(a)(1) authorizes \xe2\x80\x9creasonable compensation for actual, necessary services rendered.\xe2\x80\x9d The word \xe2\x80\x9cservices\xe2\x80\x9d ordinarily refers to \xe2\x80\x9clabor performed for another,\xe2\x80\x9d Webster\xe2\x80\x99s New International Dictionary 2288. Thus, the phrase \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98reasonable compensation for services rendered\xe2\x80\x99 necessarily implies loyal and disinterested service in the interest of\xe2\x80\x9d a client, Woods v. City Nat. Bank & Trust Co. of Chicago,\n\n312 U.\xc2\xa0S. 262\n. Time spent litigating a fee application against the bankruptcy estate\xe2\x80\x99s administrator cannot be fairly described as \xe2\x80\x9clabor performed for\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94let alone \xe2\x80\x9cdisinterested service to\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94that administrator. Had Congress wished to shift the burdens of fee-defense litigation under \xc2\xa7330(a)(1), it could have done so, as it has done in other Bankruptcy Code provisions, e.g., \xc2\xa7110(i)(1)(C). Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0Neither the law firms nor the United States, as amicus curiae, offers a persuasive theory for why \xc2\xa7330(a)(1) should override the American Rule in this context. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

(1)\xc2\xa0The law firms\xe2\x80\x99 view\xe2\x80\x94that fee-defense litigation is part of the \xe2\x80\x9cservices rendered\xe2\x80\x9d to the estate administrator\xe2\x80\x94not only suffers from an unnatural interpretation of the term \xe2\x80\x9cservices rendered,\xe2\x80\x9d but would require a particularly unusual deviation from the American Rule, as it would permit attorneys to be awarded fees for unsuccessfully defending fee applications when most fee-shifting provisions permit awards only to \xe2\x80\x9ca \xe2\x80\x98prevailing party,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Hardt, supra, at 253. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

(2)\xc2\xa0The Government\xe2\x80\x99s argument is also unpersuasive. Its theory\xe2\x80\x94that fees for fee-defense litigation must be understood as a component of the \xe2\x80\x9creasonable compensation for [the underlying] services rendered\xe2\x80\x9d so that compensation for the \xe2\x80\x9cactual .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. services rendered\xe2\x80\x9d will not be diluted by unpaid time spent litigating fees\xe2\x80\x94cannot be reconciled with the relevant text. Section 330(a)(1) does not authorize courts to award \xe2\x80\x9creasonable compensation,\xe2\x80\x9d but \xe2\x80\x9creasonable compensation for actual, necessary services rendered,\xe2\x80\x9d and the Government properly concedes that litigation in defense of a fee application is not a \xe2\x80\x9cservice.\xe2\x80\x9d And \xc2\xa7330(a)(6), which presupposes compensation \xe2\x80\x9cfor the preparation of a fee application,\xe2\x80\x9d does not suggest that time spent defending a fee application must also be compensable. Commissioner v. Jean,\n\n496 U.\xc2\xa0S. 154\n, distinguished.

\n

The Government\xe2\x80\x99s theory ultimately rests on the flawed policy argument that a \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial exception\xe2\x80\x9d is needed to compensate fee-defense litigation and safeguard Congress\xe2\x80\x99 aim of ensuring that talented attorneys take on bankruptcy work. But since no attorneys are entitled to such fees absent express statutory authorization, requiring bankruptcy attorneys to bear the costs of their fee-defense litigation under \xc2\xa7330(a)(1) creates no disincentive to bankruptcy practice. And even if this Court believed that uncompensated fee-defense litigation would fall particularly hard on the bankruptcy bar, it has no \xe2\x80\x9croving authority .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to allow counsel fees .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. whenever [it] might deem them warranted,\xe2\x80\x9d Alyeska Pipeline, supra, at 260. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

751 F.\xc2\xa03d 291, affirmed.

BAKER BOTTS L.L.P. et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v.ASARCO LLC

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit

\n

[June 15, 2015]

\n

Justice Thomas delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

Section 327(a) of the Bankruptcy Code allows bank-ruptcy trustees to hire attorneys, accountants, and other professionals to assist them in carrying out their statutory duties.\n\n11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7327(a). Another provision, \xc2\xa7330(a)(1), states that a bankruptcy court \xe2\x80\x9cmay award .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. reasonable compensation for actual, necessary services rendered by\xe2\x80\x9d those professionals. The question before us is whether \xc2\xa7330(a)(1) permits a bankruptcy court to award attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees for work performed in defending a fee application in court. We hold that it does not and therefore affirm the judgment of the Court of Appeals.

\n

I

\n

In 2005, respondent ASARCO LLC, a copper mining, smelting, and refining company, found itself in financial trouble. Faced with falling copper prices, debt, cash flow deficiencies, environmental liabilities, and a striking work force, ASARCO filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy. As in many Chapter 11 bankruptcies, no trustee was appointed and ASARCO\xe2\x80\x94the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98debtor in possession\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94administered the bankruptcy estate as a fiduciary for the estate\xe2\x80\x99s creditors. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71101(1), 1107(a).

\n

Relying on \xc2\xa7327(a) of the Bankruptcy Code, which permits trustees to employ attorneys and other professionals to assist them in their duties, ASARCO obtained the Bankruptcy Court\xe2\x80\x99s permission to hire two law firms, petitioners Baker Botts L.L.P. and Jordan, Hyden, Womble, Culbreth & Holzer, P.C., to provide legal representation during the bankruptcy.[1] Among other services, the firms prosecuted fraudulent-transfer claims against ASARCO\xe2\x80\x99s parent company and ultimately obtained a judgment against it worth between $7 and $10 billion. This judgment contributed to a successful reorganization in which all of ASARCO\xe2\x80\x99s creditors were paid in full. After over four years in bankruptcy, ASARCO emerged in 2009 with $1.4 billion in cash, little debt, and resolution of its environmental liabilities.

\n

The law firms sought compensation under \xc2\xa7330(a)(1), which provides that a bankruptcy court \xe2\x80\x9cmay award .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. reasonable compensation for actual, necessary services rendered by\xe2\x80\x9d professionals hired under \xc2\xa7327(a). As required by the bankruptcy rules, the two firms filed fee applications. Fed. Rule Bkrtcy. Proc. 2016(a). ASARCO, controlled once again by its parent company, challenged the compensation requested in the applications. After extensive discovery and a 6-day trial on fees, the Bankruptcy Court rejected ASARCO\xe2\x80\x99s objections and awarded the firms approximately $120 million for their work in the bankruptcy proceeding plus a $4.1 million enhancement for exceptional performance. The court also awarded the firms over $5 million for time spent litigating in defense of their fee applications.

\n

ASARCO appealed various aspects of the award to the District Court. As relevant here, the court held that the firms could recover fees for defending their fee application.

\n

The Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit reversed. It reasoned that the American Rule\xe2\x80\x94the rule that each side must pay its own attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9capplies absent explicit statutory .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. authority\xe2\x80\x9d to the contrary and that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Code contains no statutory provision for the recovery of attor-ney fees for defending a fee application.\xe2\x80\x9d In\xc2\xa0re ASARCO, L.L.C., 751 F.\xc2\xa03d 291, 301 (2014) (internal quotation marks omitted). It observed that \xc2\xa7330(a)(1) provides \xe2\x80\x9cthat professional services are compensable only if they are likely to benefit a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s estate or are necessary to case administration.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 299. Because \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he primary beneficiary of a professional fee application, of course, is the professional,\xe2\x80\x9d compensation for litigation defending that application does not fall within \xc2\xa7330(a)(1). Ibid.

\n

We granted certiorari, 573 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014), and now affirm.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cOur basic point of reference when considering the award of attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees is the bedrock principle known as the American Rule: Each litigant pays his own attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees, win or lose, unless a statute or contract provides otherwise.\xe2\x80\x9d Hardt v. Reliance Standard Life Ins. Co.,\n\n560 U.\xc2\xa0S. 242\n\xe2\x80\x93253 (2010) (internal quotation marks omitted). The American Rule has roots in our common law reaching back to at least the 18th century, see Arcambel v. Wiseman, 3 Dall. 306 (1796), and \xe2\x80\x9c[s]tatutes which invade the common law are to be read with a presumption favoring the retention of long-established and familiar [legal] principles,\xe2\x80\x9d Fogerty v. Fantasy, Inc.,\n\n510 U.\xc2\xa0S. 517,\n534 (1994)\n (internal quotation marks and ellipsis omitted). We consequently will not deviate from the American Rule \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98absent explicit statutory authority.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Buckhannon Board & Care Home, Inc. v. West Virginia Dept. of Health and Human Resources,\n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 598,\n602 (2001)\n (quoting Key Tronic Corp. v. United States,\n\n511 U.\xc2\xa0S. 809,\n814 (1994)\n).

\n

We have recognized departures from the American Rule only in \xe2\x80\x9cspecific and explicit provisions for the allowance of attorneys\xe2\x80\x99 fees under selected statutes.\xe2\x80\x9d Alyeska Pipeline Service Co. v. Wilderness Society,\n\n421 U.\xc2\xa0S. 240,\n260 (1975)\n. Although these \xe2\x80\x9c[s]tatutory changes to [the American Rule] take various forms,\xe2\x80\x9d Hardt, supra, at 253, they tend to authorize the award of \xe2\x80\x9ca reasonable attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fee,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cfees,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9clitigation costs,\xe2\x80\x9d and usually refer to a \xe2\x80\x9cprevailing party\xe2\x80\x9d in the context of an adversarial \xe2\x80\x9caction,\xe2\x80\x9d see, e.g.,\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72412(d)(1)(A); 42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71988(b), 2000e\xe2\x80\x935(k); see generally Hardt, supra, at 253, and nn. 3\xe2\x80\x937 (collecting examples).

\n

The attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees provision of the Equal Access to Justice Act offers a good example of the clarity we have required to deviate from the American Rule. See\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72412(d)(1)(A). That section provides that \xe2\x80\x9ca court shall award to a prevailing party other than the United States fees and other expenses .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. incurred by that party in any civil action (other than cases sounding in tort) .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. brought by or against the United States\xe2\x80\x9d under certain conditions. Ibid. As our decision in Commissioner v. Jean,\n\n496 U.\xc2\xa0S. 154 (1990)\n, reveals, there could be little dispute that this provision\xe2\x80\x94which mentions \xe2\x80\x9cfees,\xe2\x80\x9d a \xe2\x80\x9cprevailing party,\xe2\x80\x9d and a \xe2\x80\x9ccivil action\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94is a \xe2\x80\x9cfee-shifting statut[e]\xe2\x80\x9d that trumps the American Rule, id., at 161.

\n

B

\n

Congress did not expressly depart from the American Rule to permit compensation for fee-defense litigation by professionals hired to assist trustees in bankruptcy proceedings. Section 327(a) authorizes the employment of such professionals, providing that a \xe2\x80\x9ctrustee, with the court\xe2\x80\x99s approval, may employ one or more attorneys, accountants, appraisers, auctioneers, or other professional persons, that do not hold or represent an interest adverse to the estate, and that are disinterested persons, to represent or assist [him] in carrying out [his] duties.\xe2\x80\x9d In other words, \xc2\xa7327(a) professionals are hired to serve the administrator of the estate for the benefit of the estate.

\n

Section 330(a)(1) in turn authorizes compensation for these professionals as follows:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cAfter notice to the parties in interest and the United States Trustee and a hearing, and subject to sec-tions 326, 328, and 329, the court may award to a trustee, a consumer privacy ombudsman appointed under section 332, an examiner, an ombudsman appointed under section 333, or a professional person employed under section 327 or 1103\xe2\x80\x94

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(A) reasonable compensation for actual, necessary services rendered by the trustee, examiner, ombudsman, professional person, or attorney and by any paraprofessional person employed by any such person; and

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(B) reimbursement for actual, necessary expenses.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.)

\n

This text cannot displace the American Rule with respect to fee-defense litigation. To be sure, the phrase \xe2\x80\x9creason-able compensation for actual, necessary services rendered\xe2\x80\x9d permits courts to award fees to attorneys for work done to assist the administrator of the estate, as the Bankruptcy Court did here when it ordered ASARCO to pay roughly $120 million for the firms\xe2\x80\x99 work in the bankruptcy proceeding. No one disputes that \xc2\xa7330(a)(1) authorizes an award of attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees for that kind of work. See Alyeska Pipeline, supra, at 260, and n. 33 (listing \xc2\xa7330(a)(1)\xe2\x80\x99s predecessor as an example of a provision authorizing attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees). But the phrase \xe2\x80\x9creasonable compensation for actual, necessary services rendered\xe2\x80\x9d neither specifi-cally nor explicitly authorizes courts to shift the costs ofadversarial litigation from one side to the other\xe2\x80\x94in this case, from the attorneys seeking fees to the administrator of the estate\xe2\x80\x94as most statutes that displace the American Rule do.

\n

Instead, \xc2\xa7330(a)(1) provides compensation for all \xc2\xa7327(a) professionals\xe2\x80\x94whether accountant, attorney, or auctioneer\xe2\x80\x94for all manner of work done in service of the estate administrator. More specifically, \xc2\xa7330(a)(1) allows \xe2\x80\x9creasonable compensation\xe2\x80\x9d only for \xe2\x80\x9cactual, necessary services rendered.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.) That qualification is significant. The word \xe2\x80\x9cservices\xe2\x80\x9d ordinarily refers to \xe2\x80\x9clabor performed for another.\xe2\x80\x9d Webster\xe2\x80\x99s New International Dictionary 2288 (def. 4) (2d ed. 1934); see also Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 1607 (3d ed. 1933) (\xe2\x80\x9cduty or labor to be rendered by one person to another\xe2\x80\x9d); Oxford English Dictionary 517 (def. 19) (1933) (\xe2\x80\x9caction of serving, helping or benefiting; conduct tending to the welfare or advantage of another\xe2\x80\x9d).[2] Thus, in a case addressing \xc2\xa7330(a)\xe2\x80\x99s predecessor, this Court concluded that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98reasonable compensation for services rendered\xe2\x80\x99 necessarily implies loyal and disinterested service in the interest of\xe2\x80\x9d a client. Woods v. City Nat. Bank & Trust Co. of Chicago,\n\n312 U.\xc2\xa0S. 262,\n268 (1941)\n; accord, American United Mut. Life Ins. Co. v. Avon Park,\n\n311 U.\xc2\xa0S. 138,\n147 (1940)\n. Time spent litigating a fee application against the administrator of a bankruptcy estate cannot be fairly described as \xe2\x80\x9clabor performed for\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94let alone \xe2\x80\x9cdisinterested service to\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94that administrator.

\n

This legislative decision to limit \xe2\x80\x9ccompensation\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9cservices rendered\xe2\x80\x9d is particularly telling given that other provisions of the Bankruptcy Code expressly transfer the costs of litigation from one adversarial party to the other. Section 110(i), for instance, provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f a bank-ruptcy petition preparer .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. commits any act that the court finds to be fraudulent, unfair, or deceptive, on the motion of the debtor, trustee, United States trustee (or the bankruptcy administrator, if any),\xe2\x80\x9d the bankruptcy court must \xe2\x80\x9corder the bankruptcy petition preparer to pay the debtor .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. reasonable attorneys\xe2\x80\x99 fees and costs in moving for damages under this subsection.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7110(i)(1)(C). Had Congress wished to shift the burdens of fee-defense litigation under \xc2\xa7330(a)(1) in a similar manner, it easily could have done so. We accordingly refuse \xe2\x80\x9cto invade the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s province by redistributing litigation costs\xe2\x80\x9d here. Alyeska Pipeline, 421 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 271.

\n

III

\n

The law firms, the United States as amicus curiae, and the dissent resist this straightforward interpretation of the statute. The law firms and the Government each offer a theory for why \xc2\xa7330(a)(1) expressly overrides the American Rule in the context of litigation in defense of a fee application, and the dissent embraces the latter. Neither theory is persuasive.

\n

A

\n

We begin with the law firms\xe2\x80\x99 approach. According to the firms, fee-defense litigation is part of the \xe2\x80\x9cservices rendered\xe2\x80\x9d to the estate administrator under \xc2\xa7330(a)(1). See Brief for Petitioners 23\xe2\x80\x9330. As explained above, that reading is untenable. The term \xe2\x80\x9cservices\xe2\x80\x9d in this provision cannot be read to encompass adversarial fee-defense litigation. See Part II\xe2\x80\x93B, supra. Even the dissent agrees on this point. See post, at 1 (opinion of Breyer, J.).

\n

Indeed, reading \xe2\x80\x9cservices\xe2\x80\x9d in this manner could end up compensating attorneys for the unsuccessful defense of a fee application. The firms insist that \xe2\x80\x9cestates do benefit from fee defenses\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94and thus receive a \xe2\x80\x9cservice\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa7330(a)(1)\xe2\x80\x94because \xe2\x80\x9cthe estate has an interest in obtaining a just determination of the amount it should pay its professionals.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 25\xe2\x80\x9326 (internal quotation marks omitted). But that alleged interest\xe2\x80\x94and hence the supposed provision of a \xe2\x80\x9cservice\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94exists whether or not a \xc2\xa7327(a) professional prevails in his fee dispute. We decline to adopt a reading of \xc2\xa7330(a)(1) that would allow courts to pay professionals for arguing for fees they were found never to have been entitled to in the first place. Such a result would not only require an unnatural interpretation of the term \xe2\x80\x9cservices rendered,\xe2\x80\x9d but a particu-larly unusual deviation from the American Rule as well, as\xe2\x80\x9c[m]ost fee-shifting provisions permit a court to award attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees only to a \xe2\x80\x98prevailing party,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98substantially prevailing\xe2\x80\x99 party,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ca \xe2\x80\x98successful\xe2\x80\x99 litigant,\xe2\x80\x9d Hardt, 560 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 253 (footnote omitted). There is no indication that Congress departed from the American Rule in \xc2\xa7330(a)(1) with respect to fee-defense litigation, let alone that it did so in such an unusual manner.

\n

B

\n

The Government\xe2\x80\x99s theory, embraced by the dissent, fares no better. Although the United States agrees that \xe2\x80\x9cthe defense of a fee application does not itself qualify as an independently compensable service,\xe2\x80\x9d it nonetheless contends that \xe2\x80\x9ccompensation for such work is properly viewed as part of the compensation for the underlying services in the bankruptcy proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 25. According to the Government, if an attorney is not repaid for his time spent successfully litigating fees, his compensation for his actual \xe2\x80\x9cservices rendered\xe2\x80\x9d to the estate administrator in the underlying proceeding will be diluted. Id., at 18. The United States thus urges us to treat fees for fee-defense work \xe2\x80\x9cas a component of \xe2\x80\x98reasonable compensation.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 33; accord, post, at 1 (Breyer, J., dissenting). We refuse to do so for several reasons.

\n

1

\n

First and foremost, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s theory cannot be reconciled with the relevant text. Section 330(a)(1) does not authorize courts to award \xe2\x80\x9creasonable compensation\xe2\x80\x9d simpliciter, but \xe2\x80\x9creasonable compensation for actual, necessary services rendered by\xe2\x80\x9d the \xc2\xa7327(a) professional. \xc2\xa7330(a)(1)(A) (emphasis added). Here, the contested award was tied to the firms\xe2\x80\x99 work on the fee-defense litigation and is correctly understood only as compensation for that work. The Government and the dissent properly concede that litigation in defense of a fee application is not a \xe2\x80\x9cservice\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning of \xc2\xa7330(a)(1); it follows that the contested award was not \xe2\x80\x9ccompensation\xe2\x80\x9d for a \xe2\x80\x9cservice.\xe2\x80\x9d Thus, the only way to reach their reading of the statute would be to excise the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cfor actual, necessary services rendered\xe2\x80\x9d from the statute.[3]

\n

Contrary to the Government\xe2\x80\x99s assertion, \xc2\xa7330(a)(6) does not presuppose that courts are free to award compensation based on work that does not qualify as a service to the estate administrator. That provision specifies that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny compensation awarded for the preparation of a fee application shall be based on the level and skill reasonably required to prepare the application.\xe2\x80\x9d The Government argues that because time spent preparing a fee application is compensable, time spent defending it must be too. But the provision cuts the other way. A \xc2\xa7327(a) professional\xe2\x80\x99s preparation of a fee application is best understood as a \xe2\x80\x9cservic[e] rendered\xe2\x80\x9d to the estate administrator under \xc2\xa7330(a)(1), whereas a professional\xe2\x80\x99s defense of that application is not. By way of analogy, it would be natural to describe a car mechanic\xe2\x80\x99s preparation of an itemized bill as part of his \xe2\x80\x9cservices\xe2\x80\x9d to the customer because it allows a customer to understand\xe2\x80\x94and, if necessary, dispute\xe2\x80\x94his expenses. But it would be less natural to describe a subsequent court battle over the bill as part of the \xe2\x80\x9cservices rendered\xe2\x80\x9d to the customer.

\n

The Government used to understand that time spent preparing a fee application was different from time spent defending one for the purposes of \xc2\xa7330(a)(1). Just a few years ago, the U.\xc2\xa0S. Trustee explained that \xe2\x80\x9c[r]easonable charges for preparing .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. fee applications .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. are compensable .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. because the preparation of a fee application is not required for lawyers practicing in areas other than bankruptcy as a condition to getting paid.\xe2\x80\x9d 78 Fed. Reg. 36250 (2013) (emphasis deleted). By contrast, \xe2\x80\x9ctime spent .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. defending .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. fee applications\xe2\x80\x9d is ordinarily \xe2\x80\x9cnot compensable,\xe2\x80\x9d the Trustee observed, as such time can be \xe2\x80\x9cproperly characterized as work that is for the benefit of the professional and not the estate.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

To support its broader interpretation of \xc2\xa7330(a)(6), the Government, echoed by the dissent, relies on our remark in Jean that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]e find no textual or logical argument for treating so differently a party\xe2\x80\x99s preparation of a fee application and its ensuing efforts to support that same application.\xe2\x80\x9d 496 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 162; see post, at 7. But that use of Jean begs the question. Jean addressed a statutory provision that everyone agreed authorized court-awarded fees for fee-defense litigation. 496 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 162. The \xe2\x80\x9conly dispute\xe2\x80\x9d in that context was over what \xe2\x80\x9cfinding [was] necessary to support such an award.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. In resolving that issue, the Court declined to treat fee-application and fee-litigation work differently given that the relevant statutory text\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9ca court shall award to a prevailing party .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. fees and other expenses .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. incurred by that party in any civil action\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94could not support such a distinction. Id., at 158. Here, by contrast, the operative language\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9creasonable compensation for actual, necessary services rendered\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94reaches only the fee-application work. The fact that the provision at issue in Jean \xe2\x80\x9cdid not mention fee-defense work,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 5, is thus irrelevant.

\n

In any event, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s textual foothold for its argument is too insubstantial to support a deviation from the American Rule. The open-ended phrase \xe2\x80\x9creasonable compensation,\xe2\x80\x9d standing alone, is not the sort of \xe2\x80\x9cspecific and explicit provisio[n]\xe2\x80\x9d that Congress must provide in order to alter this default rule. Alyeska Pipeline, 421 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 260.

\n

2

\n

Ultimately, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s theory rests on a flawed and irrelevant policy argument. The United States contends that awarding fees for fee-defense litigation is a \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial exception\xe2\x80\x9d necessary to the proper functioning of the Bankruptcy Code. Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 15, n.\xc2\xa07 (internal quotation marks omitted). Absent this exception, it warns, fee-defense litigation will dilute attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees and result in bankruptcy lawyers receiving less compensation than nonbankruptcy lawyers, thereby undermining the congressional aim of ensuring that talented attorneys will take on bankruptcy work. Accord, post, at 3.

\n

As an initial matter, we find this policy argument unconvincing. In our legal system, no attorneys, regardless of whether they practice in bankruptcy, are entitled to receive fees for fee-defense litigation absent express statutory authorization. Requiring bankruptcy attorneys to pay for the defense of their fees thus will not result inany disparity between bankruptcy and nonbankruptcy lawyers.[4]

\n

The United States nonetheless contends that uncompensated fee litigation in bankruptcy will be particularly costly because multiple parties in interest may object to fee applications, whereas nonbankruptcy fee litigation typically involves just a lawyer and his client. But this argument rests on unsupported predictions of how the statutory scheme will operate in practice, and the Government\xe2\x80\x99s conduct in this case reveals the perils associ-ated with relying on such prognostications to interpretstatutes: The United States took the opposite view below, asserting that \xe2\x80\x9crequiring a professional to bear the normal litigation costs of litigating a contested request for payment .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. dilutes a bankruptcy fee award no more than any litigation over professional fees.\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief for Appellant United States Trustee in No. 11\xe2\x80\x93290 (SD Tex.), p. 15. The speed with which the Government has changed its tune offers a good argument against substituting policy-oriented predictions for statutory text.

\n

More importantly, we would lack the authority to rewrite the statute even if we believed that uncompensated fee litigation would fall particularly hard on the bank-ruptcy bar. \xe2\x80\x9cOur unwillingness to soften the import of Con-gress\xe2\x80\x99 chosen words even if we believe the words lead to a harsh outcome is longstanding,\xe2\x80\x9d and that is no less true in bankruptcy than it is elsewhere. Lamie v. United States Trustee,\n\n540 U.\xc2\xa0S. 526,\n538 (2004)\n. Whether or not the Government\xe2\x80\x99s theory is desirable as a matter of policy, Congress has not granted us \xe2\x80\x9croving authority .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to allow counsel fees .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. whenever [we] might deem them warranted.\xe2\x80\x9d Alyeska Pipeline, supra, at 260. Our job is to followthe text even if doing so will supposedly \xe2\x80\x9cundercut a basic objective of the statute,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 3. Section 330(a)(1) itself does not authorize the award of fees for defending a fee application, and that is the end of the matter.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

As we long ago observed, \xe2\x80\x9cThe general practice of the United States is in opposition\xe2\x80\x9d to forcing one side to pay the other\xe2\x80\x99s attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees, and \xe2\x80\x9ceven if that practice [is] not strictly correct in principle, it is entitled to the respect of the court, till it is changed, or modified, by statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Arcambel, 3 Dall., at 306 (emphasis deleted). We follow that approach today. Because \xc2\xa7330(a)(1) does not explic-itly override the American Rule with respect to fee-defense litigation, it does not permit bankruptcy courts to award compensation for such litigation. We therefore affirm the judgment of the Court of Appeals.

\n

It is so ordered.

BAKER BOTTS L.L.P. et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v.ASARCO LLC

\n

\n

[June 15, 2015]

\n

\n

Justice Breyer, with whom Justice Ginsburg and Justice Kagan join, dissenting.

\n

The Bankruptcy Code authorizes a court to award \xe2\x80\x9creasonable compensation for actual, necessary services rendered by\xe2\x80\x9d various \xe2\x80\x9cprofessional person[s],\xe2\x80\x9d including \xe2\x80\x9cattorneys,\xe2\x80\x9d whom a bankruptcy \xe2\x80\x9ctrustee [has] employ[ed] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to represent or assist the trustee in carrying out the trustee\xe2\x80\x99s duties.\xe2\x80\x9d 11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7327(a), 330(a) (emphasis added). I agree with the Court that a professional\xe2\x80\x99s defense of afee application is not a \xe2\x80\x9cservice\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning ofthe Code. See ante, at 6. But I agree with the Government that compensation for fee-defense work \xe2\x80\x9cis properly viewed as part of the compensation for the underlying services in [a] bankruptcy proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 25. In my view, when a bankruptcy court determines \xe2\x80\x9creasonable compensation,\xe2\x80\x9d it may take into account the expenses that a professional has incurred in defending his or her application for fees.

\n

I

\n

The Bankruptcy Code affords courts broad discretion to decide what constitutes \xe2\x80\x9creasonable compensation.\xe2\x80\x9d The Code provides that a \xe2\x80\x9ccourt shall consider the nature, the extent, and the value of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. services [rendered], taking into account all relevant factors.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7330(a)(3) (emphasis added). Cf. Hensley v. Eckerhart,\n\n461 U.\xc2\xa0S. 424,\n437 (1983)\n (\xe2\x80\x9creemphasiz[ing a trial court\xe2\x80\x99s] discretion in determining the amount of a fee award,\xe2\x80\x9d which \xe2\x80\x9cis appropriate in view of the [trial] court\xe2\x80\x99s superior understanding of the litigation\xe2\x80\x9d). I would hold that it is within a bankruptcy court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion to consider as \xe2\x80\x9crelevant factors\xe2\x80\x9d the cost and effort that a professional has reasonably expendedin order to recover his or her fees.

\n

Where a statute provides for reasonable fees, a court may take into account factors other than hours and hourly rates. Perdue v. Kenny A.,\n\n559 U.\xc2\xa0S. 542\n\xe2\x80\x93557 (2010). For instance, \xe2\x80\x9can enhancement\xe2\x80\x9d to attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees \xe2\x80\x9cmay be appropriate if the attorney\xe2\x80\x99s performance includes an extraordinary outlay of expenses and the litigation is exceptionally protracted.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 555. And \xe2\x80\x9cthere may be extraordinary circumstances in which an attorney\xe2\x80\x99s performance involves exceptional delay in the payment of fees\xe2\x80\x9d that justify additional compensation. Id., at 556. These examples demonstrate that increased compensation is sometimes warranted to reflect exceptional effort or resources expended in order to attain one\xe2\x80\x99s fees.

\n

In that vein, work performed in defending a fee application may, in some cases, be a relevant factor in calculating \xe2\x80\x9creasonable compensation.\xe2\x80\x9d Consider a bankruptcy attorney who earns $50,000\xe2\x80\x94a fee that reflects her hours, rates, and expertise\xe2\x80\x94but is forced to spend $20,000 defending her fee application against meritless objections. It is within a bankruptcy court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion to decide that, taking into account the extensive fee litigation, $50,000 is an insufficient award. The attorney has effectively been paid $30,000, and the bankruptcy court might understandably conclude that such a fee is not \xe2\x80\x9creasonable.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Indeed, this Court has previously acknowledged that work performed in defending a fee application is relevant to a determination of attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees. In Commissioner v. Jean,\n\n496 U.\xc2\xa0S. 154\n\xe2\x80\x93166 (1990), the Court held that fee-defense work is compensable under the Equal Access to Justice Act,\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72412(d)(1)(A). The Courtquoted with approval the Second Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s statement that \xe2\x80\x9c[d]enying attorneys\xe2\x80\x99 fees for time spent in obtaining them would dilute the value of a fees award by forcing attorneys into extensive, uncompensated litigation in order to gain any fees.\xe2\x80\x9d 496 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 162 (quoting Gagne v. Maher, 594 F.\xc2\xa02d 336, 344 (1979); internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

A contrary interpretation of \xe2\x80\x9creasonable compensation\xe2\x80\x9d would undercut a basic objective of the statute. Congress intended to ensure that high-quality attorneys and other professionals would be available to assist trustees in representing and administering bankruptcy estates. To that end, Congress directed bankruptcy courts to consider \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the compensation is reasonable based on the customary compensation charged by comparably skilled practitioners in cases other than cases under\xe2\x80\x9d the Bankruptcy Code. \xc2\xa7330(a)(3)(F). Congress recognized that comparable compensation was necessary to ensure that professionals would \xe2\x80\x9cremain in the bankruptcy field.\xe2\x80\x9d H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 95\xe2\x80\x93595, p. 330 (1977). Cf. Perdue, supra, at 552 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] \xe2\x80\x98reasonable\xe2\x80\x99 fee is a fee that is sufficient to induce a capable attorney to undertake the representation of a meritorious civil rights case\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

In some cases, the extensive process through which a bankruptcy professional defends his or her fees may be so burdensome that additional fees are necessary in order to maintain comparability of compensation. In order to be paid, a professional assisting a trustee must file with the court a detailed application seeking compensation. Fed. Rule Bkrtcy. Proc. 2016(a). The application will not be granted until after the court has conducted a hearing on the matter. \xc2\xa7330(a)(1). And \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he court may, on its own motion or on the motion of the United States Trustee,the United States Trustee for the District or Region, the trustee for the estate, or any other party in interest, award compensation that is less than the amount of compensation that is requested.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7330(a)(2).

\n

By contrast, an attorney representing a private party, or a professional working outside of the bankruptcy context, generally faces fee objections made only by his or her client\xe2\x80\x94and those objections typically are made outside of court, at least initially. This process is comparatively simple, involves fewer parties in interest, and does not necessarily impose litigation costs. Consequently, in order to maintain comparable compensation, a court may find it necessary to account for the relatively burdensome fee-defense process required by the Bankruptcy Code. Accounting for this process ensures that a professional is paid \xe2\x80\x9creasonable compensation.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

II

\n

The majority rests its conclusion upon an interpretation of the statutory language that I find neither legally necessary nor convincing. The majority says that Congress, in writing the reasonable-compensation statute, did not \xe2\x80\x9cdisplace the American Rule with respect to fee-defense litigation.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 5. The American Rule normally requires \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ach litigant\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9cpa[y] his own attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees, win or lose.\xe2\x80\x9d Hardt v. Reliance Standard Life Ins. Co.,\n\n560 U.\xc2\xa0S. 242,\n253 (2010)\n.

\n

But the American Rule is a default rule that applies only where \xe2\x80\x9ca statute or contract\xe2\x80\x9d does not \xe2\x80\x9cprovid[e] otherwise.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. And here, the statute \xe2\x80\x9cprovides otherwise.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Section 330(a)(1)(A) permits a \xe2\x80\x9ccourt [to] award .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. reasonable compensation for actual, necessary services rendered by the trustee, examiner, ombudsman, professional person, or attorney and by any paraprofessional person employed by any such person.\xe2\x80\x9d This Court has recognized that through \xc2\xa7330(a), Congress \xe2\x80\x9cma[d]e specific and explicit [its] provisio[n] for the allowance of attorneys\xe2\x80\x99 fees,\xe2\x80\x9d and thus displaced the American Rule. Alyeska Pipeline Service Co. v. Wilderness Society,\n\n421 U.\xc2\xa0S. 240\n, and n. 33 (1975) (listing \xc2\xa7330(a)\xe2\x80\x99s predecessor among examples of provisions authorizing attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees).

\n

The majority suggests that the American Rule is not displaced with respect to fee-defense work in bankruptcy because \xc2\xa7330(a) does not specifically authorize fees for that particular type of work. See ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x935 (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress did not expressly depart from the American Rule to permit compensation for fee-defense litigation by professionals hired to assist trustees in bankruptcy proceedings\xe2\x80\x9d). To the extent that the majority intends to impose a requirement that a statute must explicitly mention fee defense in order to provide compensation for that work, this requirement is difficult to reconcile with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Jean. There, the Court held that the Equal Access to Justice Act authorizes compensation for fee-defense work. See 496 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 160\xe2\x80\x93166. The fee provision of the Equal Access to Justice Act, as enacted at the time, permitted an \xe2\x80\x9caward to a prevailing party .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of fees and other expenses .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. incurred by that party in any civil action .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. brought by or against the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 158 (quoting\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72412(d)(1)(A) (1988 ed.)). The provision did not mention fee-defense work\xe2\x80\x94but the Court nonetheless held that such work was compensable. See Jean, supra, at 160\xe2\x80\x93166. I would do the same here.

\n

The majority focuses on particular words that appear in the Equal Access to Justice Act: \xe2\x80\x9cfees,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cprevailing party,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ccivil action.\xe2\x80\x9d See ante, at 4. But neither the term \xe2\x80\x9cfees\xe2\x80\x9d nor the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cprevailing party\xe2\x80\x9d relates specifically to fee-defense work. And even assuming that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9ccivil action\xe2\x80\x9d is more easily read to cover fee litigation than the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cactual, necessary services,\xe2\x80\x9d that difference here is beside the point. I find the necessary authority in the words \xe2\x80\x9creasonable compensation,\xe2\x80\x9d not the words \xe2\x80\x9cactual, necessary services.\xe2\x80\x9d In order to ensure that each professional is paid reasonably for compensable services, a court must have the discretion to authorize pay reflecting fee-defense work.

\n

The majority asserts that by interpreting the phrase \xe2\x80\x9creasonable compensation,\xe2\x80\x9d I have effectively \xe2\x80\x9cexcise[d] the phrase \xe2\x80\x98for actual, necessary services rendered\xe2\x80\x99 from the statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9. But the majority misunder-stands my views. The statute permits compensation for fee-defense work as a part of compensation for the underlying services. Thus, where fee-defense work is not necessary to ensure reasonable compensation for some under-lying service, then under my reading of the statute, a court should not consider that work when calculating compensation.

\n

Indeed, to the extent that the majority bases its decision on the specific words of \xc2\xa7330(a), its argument seems weak. The majority disregards direct statutory evidence that Congress intended to give courts the authority to account for reasonable fee-litigation costs. Section 330(a)(6) states that \xe2\x80\x9cany compensation awarded for the preparation of a fee application shall be based on the level and skill reasonably required to prepare the application.\xe2\x80\x9d This provision does not authorize compensation, but rather assumes (through the words \xe2\x80\x9cany compensation awarded\xe2\x80\x9d) pre-existing authorization under \xc2\xa7330(a). And the majority cannot convincingly explain why, under its reading of the statute, fee-application is a compensable \xe2\x80\x9cactual, necessary servic[e] rendered\xe2\x80\x9d to the estate.

\n

The majority asserts that a fee application, unlike fee defense, can be construed as a \xe2\x80\x9cservice\xe2\x80\x9d to the bankruptcy estate. See ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310. The majority draws an analogy between a fee application and an itemized bill prepared by a car mechanic. See ibid. It argues that, like an itemized bill, a fee application is a \xe2\x80\x9cservice\xe2\x80\x9d to the customer. But customers do not generally pay their mechanics for time spent preparing the bill. A mechanic\xe2\x80\x99s bill is not a separate \xe2\x80\x9cservice,\xe2\x80\x9d but rather is a medium through which the mechanic conveys what he or she wants to be paid. Similarly, a legal bill is not a \xe2\x80\x9cservice\xe2\x80\x9d rendered to a client. In fact, ASARCO concedes that attorneys do not charge their clients for time spent preparing legal bills. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 33. A bill prepared by an attorney, or another bankruptcy professional, is not a \xe2\x80\x9cservice\xe2\x80\x9d to the bankruptcy estate.

\n

The majority suggests that a fee application must be a service \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98because the preparation of a fee application is not required for lawyers practicing in areas other than bankruptcy as a condition to getting paid.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10 (quoting 78 Fed. Reg. 36250 (2013)). But if the existence of a legal requirement specific to bankruptcy were sufficient to make an activity a compensable service, then the time that a professional spends at a hearing defending his or her fees would also be compensable. After all, the statute permits a court to award compensation only after \xe2\x80\x9ca hearing\xe2\x80\x9d with respect to the issue. \xc2\xa7330(a)(1). And there is no such requirement for most attorneys, who simply bill their clients and are paid their fees. But the majority does not believe that preparing for or appearing at such a hearing\xe2\x80\x94an integral part of fee-defense work\xe2\x80\x94is compensable. The majority simply cannot reconcile its narrow interpretation of \xe2\x80\x9creasonable compensation\xe2\x80\x9d with \xc2\xa7330(a)(6)\xe2\x80\x99s provision for fee-application preparation fees.

\n

In my view, the majority is wrong to distinguish between the costs of fee preparation and the costs of fee litigation. Cf. Jean, 496 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 162 (\xe2\x80\x9cWe find no textual or logical argument for treating .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. differently a party\xe2\x80\x99s preparation of a fee application and its ensuing efforts to support that same application\xe2\x80\x9d). And the majority should not distinguish between the compensability of fee litigation under the Equal Access to Justice Act and fee litigation under the Bankruptcy Code. Its decision to do so creates anomalies and undermines the basic purpose of the Bankruptcy Code\xe2\x80\x99s fee award provision.

\n

For these reasons, I respectfully dissent.
