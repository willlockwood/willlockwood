\n\t\t\t\tOXFORD HEALTH PLANS LLC v. SUTTER\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the third circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 12\xe2\x80\x93135.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 25, 2013\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 10, 2013\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tRespondent Sutter, a pediatrician, provided medical services to petitioner Oxford Health Plans\xe2\x80\x99 insureds under a fee-for-services contract that required binding arbitration of contractual disputes. He nonetheless filed a proposed class action in New Jersey Superior Court, alleging that Oxford failed to fully and promptly pay him and other physicians with similar Oxford contracts. On Oxford\xe2\x80\x99s motion, the court compelled arbitration. The parties agreed that the arbitrator should decide whether their contract authorized class arbitration, and he concluded that it did. Oxford filed a motion in federal court to vacate the arbitrator\xe2\x80\x99s decision, claiming that he had \xe2\x80\x9cexceeded [his] powers\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa710(a)(4) of the Federal Arbitration Act (FAA), \n\n9 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71 et. seq. The District Court denied the motion, and the Third Circuit affirmed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAfter this Court decided Stolt-Nielsen S.\xc2\xa0A. v. AnimalFeeds Int\xe2\x80\x99l Corp., \n\n559 U.\xc2\xa0S. 662\n\xe2\x80\x94holding that an arbitrator may employ class procedures only if the parties have authorized them\xe2\x80\x94the arbitrator reaffirmed his conclusion that the contract approves class arbitration. Oxford renewed its motion to vacate that decision under \xc2\xa710(a)(4). The District Court denied the motion, and the Third Circuit affirmed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0The arbitrator\xe2\x80\x99s decision survives the limited judicial review allowed by \xc2\xa710(a)(4). Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x88\x929.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0A party seeking relief under \xc2\xa710(a)(4) bears a heavy burden. \xe2\x80\x9cIt is not enough .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to show that the [arbitrator] committed an error\xe2\x80\x94or even a serious error.\xe2\x80\x9d Stolt-Nielsen, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 671. Because the parties \xe2\x80\x9cbargained for the arbitrator\xe2\x80\x99s construction of their agreement,\xe2\x80\x9d an arbitral decision \xe2\x80\x9ceven arguably construing or applying the contract\xe2\x80\x9d must stand, regardless of a court\xe2\x80\x99s view of its (de)merits. Eastern Associated Coal Corp. v. Mine Workers, \n\n531 U.\xc2\xa0S. 57\n. Thus, the sole question on judicial review is whether the arbitrator interpreted the parties\xe2\x80\x99 contract, not whether he construed it correctly. Here, the arbitrator twice did what the parties asked: He considered their contract and decided whether it reflected an agreement to permit class proceedings. That suffices to show that he did not exceed his powers under \xc2\xa710(a)(4). Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x88\x926.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0Stolt-Neilsen does not support Oxford\xe2\x80\x99s contrary view. There, the parties stipulated that they had not reached an agreement on class arbitration, so the arbitrators did not construe the contract, and did not identify any agreement authorizing class proceedings. This Court thus found not that they had misinterpreted the contract but that they had abandoned their interpretive role. Here, in stark contrast, the arbitrator did construe the contract, and did find an agreement to permit class arbitration. So to overturn his decision, this Court would have to find that he misapprehended the parties\xe2\x80\x99 intent. But \xc2\xa710(a)(4) bars that course: It permits courts to vacate an arbitral decision only when the arbitrator strayed from his delegated task of interpreting a contract, not when he performed that task poorly. Oxford\xe2\x80\x99s remaining arguments go to the merits of the arbitrator\xe2\x80\x99s contract interpretation and are thus irrelevant under \xc2\xa710(a)(4). Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x88\x929.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t675 F.\xc2\xa03d 215, affirmed.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tOXFORD HEALTH PLANS LLC, PETITIONER v. JOHN IVAN SUTTER\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\ton writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the third circuit\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Kagan delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tClass arbitration is a matter of consent: An arbitrator may employ class procedures only if the parties have au-thorized them. See Stolt-Nielsen S.\xc2\xa0A. v. AnimalFeeds Int\xe2\x80\x99l Corp., \n\n559 U.\xc2\xa0S. 662, \n684 (2010)\n. In this case, an arbitrator found that the parties\xe2\x80\x99 contract provided for class arbitration. The question presented is whether in doing so he \xe2\x80\x9cexceeded [his] powers\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa710(a)(4) of the Federal Arbitration Act (FAA or Act), \n\n9 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71 et\xc2\xa0seq. We conclude that the arbitrator\xe2\x80\x99s decision survives the limited judicial review \xc2\xa710(a)(4) allows.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondent John Sutter, a pediatrician, entered into a contract with petitioner Oxford Health Plans, a health in-surance company. Sutter agreed to provide medical care to members of Oxford\xe2\x80\x99s network, and Oxford agreed to pay for those services at prescribed rates. Several years later, Sutter filed suit against Oxford in New Jersey Superior Court on behalf of himself and a proposed class of other New Jersey physicians under contract with Oxford. The complaint alleged that Oxford had failed to make full and prompt payment to the doctors, in violation of their agree-ments and various state laws.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOxford moved to compel arbitration of Sutter\xe2\x80\x99s claims, relying on the following clause in their contract:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cNo civil action concerning any dispute arising under this Agreement shall be instituted before any court, and all such disputes shall be submitted to final and binding arbitration in New Jersey, pursuant to the rules of the American Arbitration Association with one arbitrator.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 15\xe2\x80\x9316.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe state court granted Oxford\xe2\x80\x99s motion, thus referring the suit to arbitration.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe parties agreed that the arbitrator should decide whether their contract authorized class arbitration, and he determined that it did. Noting that the question turned on \xe2\x80\x9cconstruction of the parties\xe2\x80\x99 agreement,\xe2\x80\x9d the arbitrator focused on the text of the arbitration clause quoted above. Id., at 30. He reasoned that the clause sent to arbitration \xe2\x80\x9cthe same universal class of disputes\xe2\x80\x9d that it barred the parties from bringing \xe2\x80\x9cas civil actions\xe2\x80\x9d in court: The \xe2\x80\x9cintent of the clause\xe2\x80\x9d was \xe2\x80\x9cto vest in the arbitration process everything that is prohibited from the court process.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 31. And a class action, the arbitrator continued, \xe2\x80\x9cis plainly one of the possible forms of civil action that could be brought in a court\xe2\x80\x9d absent the agreement. Ibid. Accordingly, he concluded that \xe2\x80\x9con its face, the arbitration clause .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. expresses the parties\xe2\x80\x99 intent that class arbitration can be maintained.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 32.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOxford filed a motion in federal court to vacate the arbitrator\xe2\x80\x99s decision on the ground that he had \xe2\x80\x9cexceeded [his] powers\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa710(a)(4) of the FAA. The District Court denied the motion, and the Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit affirmed. See 05\xe2\x80\x93CV\xe2\x80\x932198, 2005 WL 6795061 (D NJ, Oct. 31, 2005), aff\xe2\x80\x99d, 227 Fed. Appx. 135 (2007).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhile the arbitration proceeded, this Court held in Stolt-Nielsen that \xe2\x80\x9ca party may not be compelled under the FAA to submit to class arbitration unless there is a contractual basis for concluding that the party agreed to do so.\xe2\x80\x9d 559 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 684. The parties in Stolt-Nielsen had stipulated that they had never reached an agreement on class arbitration. Relying on \xc2\xa710(a)(4), we vacated the arbitrators\xe2\x80\x99 decision approving class proceedings because, in the absence of such an agreement, the arbitrators had \xe2\x80\x9csimply .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. imposed [their] own view of sound policy.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 672.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOxford immediately asked the arbitrator to reconsider his decision on class arbitration in light of Stolt-Nielsen. The arbitrator issued a new opinion holding that Stolt-Nielsen had no effect on the case because this agreement authorized class arbitration. Unlike in Stolt-Nielsen, the arbitrator explained, the parties here disputed the meaning of their contract; he had therefore been required \xe2\x80\x9cto construe the arbitration clause in the ordinary way to glean the parties\xe2\x80\x99 intent.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 72. And in performing that task, the arbitrator continued, he had \xe2\x80\x9cfound that the arbitration clause unambiguously evinced an intention to allow class arbitration.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 70. The arbitrator con-cluded by reconfirming his reasons for so construing the clause.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOxford then returned to federal court, renewing its effort to vacate the arbitrator\xe2\x80\x99s decision under \xc2\xa710(a)(4). Once again, the District Court denied the motion, and the Third Circuit affirmed. The Court of Appeals first underscored the limited scope of judicial review that \xc2\xa710(a)(4) allows: So long as an arbitrator \xe2\x80\x9cmakes a good faith attempt\xe2\x80\x9d to interpret a contract, \xe2\x80\x9ceven serious errors of law or fact will not subject his award to vacatur.\xe2\x80\x9d 675 F. 3d 215, 220 (2012). Oxford could not prevail under that standard, the court held, because the arbitrator had \xe2\x80\x9cendeavored to give effect to the parties\xe2\x80\x99 intent\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9carticulate[d] a contractual basis for his decision.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 223\xe2\x80\x93224. Oxford\xe2\x80\x99s objections to the ruling were \xe2\x80\x9csimply dressed-up arguments that the arbitrator interpreted its agreement erroneously.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 224.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe granted certiorari, 568 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012), to address a circuit split on whether \xc2\xa710(a)(4) allows a court to vacate an arbitral award in similar circumstances.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Holding that it does not, we affirm the Court of Appeals.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUnder the FAA, courts may vacate an arbitrator\xe2\x80\x99s decision \xe2\x80\x9conly in very unusual circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d First Options of Chicago, Inc. v. Kaplan, \n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 938, \n942 (1995)\n. That limited judicial review, we have explained, \xe2\x80\x9cmaintain[s] arbitration\xe2\x80\x99s essential virtue of resolving disputes straightaway.\xe2\x80\x9d Hall Street Associates, L. L. C. v. Mattel, Inc., \n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 576, \n588 (2008)\n. If parties could take \xe2\x80\x9cfull-bore legal and evidentiary appeals,\xe2\x80\x9d arbitration would become \xe2\x80\x9cmerely a prelude to a more cumbersome and time-consuming judicial review process.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tHere, Oxford invokes \xc2\xa710(a)(4) of the Act, which authorizes a federal court to set aside an arbitral award \xe2\x80\x9cwhere the arbitrator[] exceeded [his] powers.\xe2\x80\x9d A party seeking relief under that provision bears a heavy burden. \xe2\x80\x9cIt is not enough .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to show that the [arbitrator] committed an error\xe2\x80\x94or even a serious error.\xe2\x80\x9d Stolt-Nielsen, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 671. Because the parties \xe2\x80\x9cbargained for the arbitra-tor\xe2\x80\x99s construction of their agreement,\xe2\x80\x9d an arbitral decision \xe2\x80\x9ceven arguably construing or applying the contract\xe2\x80\x9d must stand, regardless of a court\xe2\x80\x99s view of its (de)merits. Eastern Associated Coal Corp. v. Mine Workers, \n\n531 U.\xc2\xa0S. 57, \n62 (2000)\n (quoting Steelworkers v. Enterprise Wheel & Car Corp., \n\n363 U.\xc2\xa0S. 593, \n599 (1960)\n; Paperworkers v. Misco, Inc., \n\n484 U.\xc2\xa0S. 29, \n38 (1987)\n; internal quotation marks omitted). Only if \xe2\x80\x9cthe arbitrator act[s] outside the scope of his contractually delegated authority\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94issuing an award that \xe2\x80\x9csimply reflect[s] [his] own notions of [economic] justice\xe2\x80\x9d rather than \xe2\x80\x9cdraw[ing] its essence from the con-tract\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94may a court overturn his determination. Eastern Associated Coal, 531 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 62 (quoting Misco, 484 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 38). So the sole question for us is whether the arbitrator (even arguably) interpreted the parties\xe2\x80\x99 contract, not whether he got its meaning right or wrong.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAnd we have already all but answered that question just by summarizing the arbitrator\xe2\x80\x99s decisions, see supra, at 2\xe2\x80\x933; they are, through and through, interpretations of the parties\xe2\x80\x99 agreement. The arbitrator\xe2\x80\x99s first ruling recited the \xe2\x80\x9cquestion of construction\xe2\x80\x9d the parties had submitted to him: \xe2\x80\x9cwhether [their] Agreement allows for class action arbitration.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 29\xe2\x80\x9330. To resolve that matter, the arbitrator focused on the arbitration clause\xe2\x80\x99s text, analyzing (whether correctly or not makes no difference) the scope of both what it barred from court and what it sent to arbitration. The arbitrator concluded, based on that textual exegesis, that the clause \xe2\x80\x9con its face .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. expresses the parties\xe2\x80\x99 intent that class action arbitration can be maintained.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 32. When Oxford requested reconsideration in light of Stolt-Nielsen, the arbitrator explained that his prior decision was \xe2\x80\x9cconcerned solely with the par-ties\xe2\x80\x99 intent as evidenced by the words of the arbitration clause itself.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 69. He then ran through his textual analysis again, and reiterated his conclusion: \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he text of the clause itself authorizes\xe2\x80\x9d class arbitration. Id., at 73. Twice, then, the arbitrator did what the parties had asked: He considered their contract and decided whether it reflected an agreement to permit class proceedings. That suffices to show that the arbitrator did not \xe2\x80\x9cexceed[\xc2\xa0] [his] powers.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa710(a)(4).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOxford\xe2\x80\x99s contrary view relies principally on Stolt-Nielsen. As noted earlier, we found there that an arbitration panel exceeded its powers under \xc2\xa710(a)(4) when it ordered a party to submit to class arbitration. See supra, at 3. Oxford takes that decision to mean that \xe2\x80\x9ceven the \xe2\x80\x98high hurdle\xe2\x80\x99 of Section 10(a)(4) review is overcome when an arbitrator imposes class arbitration without a sufficient contractual basis.\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief 5 (quoting Stolt-Nielsen, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 671). Under Stolt-Nielson, Oxford asserts, a court may thus vacate \xe2\x80\x9cas ultra vires\xe2\x80\x9d an arbitral decision like this one for misconstruing a contract to approve class proceedings. Reply Brief 7.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut Oxford misreads Stolt-Nielsen: We overturned the arbitral decision there because it lacked any contractual basis for ordering class procedures, not because it lacked, in Oxford\xe2\x80\x99s terminology, a \xe2\x80\x9csufficient\xe2\x80\x9d one. The parties in Stolt-Nielsen had entered into an unusual stipulation that they had never reached an agreement on class arbitration. See 559 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 668\xe2\x80\x93669, 673. In that circumstance, we noted, the panel\xe2\x80\x99s decision was not\xe2\x80\x94indeed, could not have been\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cbased on a determination regarding the parties\xe2\x80\x99 intent.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 673, n. 4; see id., at 676 (\xe2\x80\x9cTh[e] stipulation left no room for an inquiry regarding the parties\xe2\x80\x99 intent\xe2\x80\x9d). Nor, we continued, did the panel attempt to ascertain whether federal or state law established a \xe2\x80\x9cdefault rule\xe2\x80\x9d to take effect absent an agreement. Id., at 673. Instead, \xe2\x80\x9cthe panel simply imposed its own conception of sound policy\xe2\x80\x9d when it ordered class proceedings. Id., at 675. But \xe2\x80\x9cthe task of an arbitrator,\xe2\x80\x9d we stated, \xe2\x80\x9cis to interpret and enforce a contract, not to make public policy.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 672. In \xe2\x80\x9cimpos[ing] its own policy choice,\xe2\x80\x9d the panel \xe2\x80\x9cthus exceeded its powers.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 677.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe contrast with this case is stark. In Stolt-Nielsen, the arbitrators did not construe the parties\xe2\x80\x99 contract, and did not identify any agreement authorizing class proceedings. So in setting aside the arbitrators\xe2\x80\x99 decision, we found not that they had misinterpreted the contract, but that they had abandoned their interpretive role. Here, the arbitrator did construe the contract (focusing, per usual, on its language), and did find an agreement to permit class arbitration. So to overturn his decision, we would have to rely on a finding that he misapprehended the par-ties\xe2\x80\x99 intent. But \xc2\xa710(a)(4) bars that course: It permits courts to vacate an arbitral decision only when the arbitrator strayed from his delegated task of interpreting a contract, not when he performed that task poorly. Stolt-Nielsen and this case thus fall on opposite sides of the line that \xc2\xa710(a)(4) draws to delimit judicial review of arbitral decisions.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe remainder of Oxford\xe2\x80\x99s argument addresses merely the merits: The arbitrator, Oxford contends at length, badly misunderstood the contract\xe2\x80\x99s arbitration clause. See Brief for Petitioner 21\xe2\x80\x9328. The key text, again, goes as follows: \xe2\x80\x9cNo civil action concerning any dispute arising under this Agreement shall be instituted before any court, and all such disputes shall be submitted to final and binding arbitration.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 15\xe2\x80\x9316. The arbitrator thought that clause sent to arbitration all \xe2\x80\x9ccivil action[s]\xe2\x80\x9d barred from court, and viewed class actions as falling within that category. See supra, at 2. But Oxford points out that the provision submits to arbitration not any \xe2\x80\x9ccivil action[s],\xe2\x80\x9d but instead any \xe2\x80\x9cdispute arising under\xe2\x80\x9d the agreement. And in any event, Oxford claims, a class action is not a form of \xe2\x80\x9ccivil action,\xe2\x80\x9d as the arbitrator thought, but merely a procedural device that may be available in a court. At bottom, Oxford maintains, this is a garden-variety arbi-tration clause, lacking any of the terms or features that would indicate an agreement to use class procedures.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe reject this argument because, and only because, it is not properly addressed to a court. Nothing we say in this opinion should be taken to reflect any agreement with the arbitrator\xe2\x80\x99s contract interpretation, or any quarrel with Oxford\xe2\x80\x99s contrary reading. All we say is that convincing a court of an arbitrator\xe2\x80\x99s error\xe2\x80\x94even his grave error\xe2\x80\x94is not enough. So long as the arbitrator was \xe2\x80\x9carguably construing\xe2\x80\x9d the contract\xe2\x80\x94which this one was\xe2\x80\x94a court may not correct his mistakes under \xc2\xa710(a)(4). Eastern Associated Coal, 531 U. S., at 62 (internal quotation marks omitted). The potential for those mistakes is the price of agreeing to arbitration. As we have held before, we hold again: \xe2\x80\x9cIt is the arbitrator\xe2\x80\x99s construction [of the contract] which was bargained for; and so far as the arbitrator\xe2\x80\x99s decision concerns construction of the contract, the courts have no business overruling him because their interpretation of the contract is different from his.\xe2\x80\x9d Enterprise Wheel, 363 U.\xc2\xa0S. at 599. The arbitrator\xe2\x80\x99s construction holds, however good, bad, or ugly.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn sum, Oxford chose arbitration, and it must now live with that choice. Oxford agreed with Sutter that an arbitrator should determine what their contract meant, including whether its terms approved class arbitration. The arbitrator did what the parties requested: He provided an interpretation of the contract resolving that disputed issue. His interpretation went against Oxford, maybe mistakenly so. But still, Oxford does not get to rerun the matter in a court. Under \xc2\xa710(a)(4), the question for a judge is not whether the arbitrator construed the parties\xe2\x80\x99 contract correctly, but whether he construed it at all. Because he did, and therefore did not \xe2\x80\x9cexceed his powers,\xe2\x80\x9d we cannot give Oxford the relief it wants. We accordingly affirm the judgment of the Court of Appeals.\n\t\t\t
