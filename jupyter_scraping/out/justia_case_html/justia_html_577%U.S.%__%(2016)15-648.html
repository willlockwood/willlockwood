SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

V. L. v. E. L., et\xc2\xa0al.

on petition for writ of certiorari to the supreme court of alabama

No. 15\xe2\x80\x93648\xe2\x80\x83Decided March 7, 2016

Per Curiam.

A Georgia court entered a final judgment of adoption making petitioner V.\xc2\xa0L. a legal parent of the children that she and respondent E.\xc2\xa0L. had raised together from birth. V.\xc2\xa0L. and E.\xc2\xa0L. later separated while living in Alabama. V.\xc2\xa0L. asked the Alabama courts to enforce the Georgia judgment and grant her custody or visitation rights. The Alabama Supreme Court ruled against her, holding that the Full Faith and Credit Clause of the United States Constitution does not require the Alabama courts to respect the Georgia judgment. That judgment of the Alabama Supreme Court is now reversed by this summary disposition.

I

V.\xc2\xa0L. and E.\xc2\xa0L. are two women who were in a relationship from approximately 1995 until 2011. Through assisted reproductive technology, E.\xc2\xa0L. gave birth to a child named S.\xc2\xa0L. in 2002 and to twins named N.\xc2\xa0L. and H.\xc2\xa0L. in 2004. After the children were born, V.\xc2\xa0L. and E.\xc2\xa0L. raised them together as joint parents.

V.\xc2\xa0L. and E.\xc2\xa0L. eventually decided to give legal status to the relationship between V.\xc2\xa0L. and the children by having V.\xc2\xa0L. formally adopt them. To facilitate the adoption, the couple rented a house in Alpharetta, Georgia. V.\xc2\xa0L. then filed an adoption petition in the Superior Court of Fulton County, Georgia. E.\xc2\xa0L. also appeared in that proceeding. While not relinquishing her own parental rights, she gave her express consent to V.\xc2\xa0L.\xe2\x80\x99s adoption of the children as a second parent. The Georgia court determined that V. L. had complied with the applicable requirements of Georgia law, and entered a final decree of adoption allowing V.\xc2\xa0L. to adopt the children and recognizing both V.\xc2\xa0L. and E.\xc2\xa0L. as their legal parents.

V.\xc2\xa0L. and E.\xc2\xa0L. ended their relationship in 2011, while living in Alabama, and V.\xc2\xa0L. moved out of the house that the couple had shared. V.\xc2\xa0L. later filed a petition in the Circuit Court of Jefferson County, Alabama, alleging that E.\xc2\xa0L. had denied her access to the children and interfered with her ability to exercise her parental rights. She asked the Alabama court to register the Georgia adoption judgment and award her some measure of custody or visitation rights. The matter was transferred to the Family Court of Jefferson County. That court entered an order awarding V.\xc2\xa0L. scheduled visitation with the children.

E.\xc2\xa0L. appealed the visitation order to the Alabama Court of Civil Appeals. She argued, among other points, that the Alabama courts should not recognize the Georgia judgment because the Georgia court lacked subject-matter jurisdiction to enter it. The Court of Civil Appeals rejected that argument. It held, however, that the Alabama family court had erred by failing to conduct an evidentiary hearing before awarding V.\xc2\xa0L. visitation rights, and so it remanded for the family court to conduct that hearing.

The Alabama Supreme Court reversed. It held that the Georgia court had no subject-matter jurisdiction under Georgia law to enter a judgment allowing V.\xc2\xa0L. to adopt the children while still recognizing E.\xc2\xa0L.\xe2\x80\x99s parental rights. As a consequence, the Alabama Supreme Court held Alabama courts were not required to accord full faith and credit to the Georgia judgment.

II

The Constitution provides that \xe2\x80\x9cFull Faith and Credit shall be given in each State to the public Acts, Records, and judicial Proceedings of every other State.\xe2\x80\x9d U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Art. IV, \xc2\xa71. That Clause requires each State to recognize and give effect to valid judgments rendered by the courts of its sister States. It serves \xe2\x80\x9cto alter the status of the several states as independent foreign sovereignties, each free to ignore obligations created under the laws or by the judicial proceedings of the others, and to make them integral parts of a single nation.\xe2\x80\x9d Milwaukee County v. M. E. White Co., 296 U.\xc2\xa0S. 268,277 (1935).

With respect to judgments, \xe2\x80\x9cthe full faith and credit obligation is exacting.\xe2\x80\x9d Baker v. General Motors Corp., 522 U.\xc2\xa0S. 222, 233 (1998). \xe2\x80\x9cA final judgment in one State, if rendered by a court with adjudicatory authority over the subject matter and persons governed by the judgment, qualifies for recognition throughout the land.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. A State may not disregard the judgment of a sister State because it disagrees with the reasoning underlying the judgment or deems it to be wrong on the merits. On the contrary, \xe2\x80\x9cthe full faith and credit clause of the Constitution precludes any inquiry into the merits of the cause of action, the logic or consistency of the decision, or the validity of the legal principles on which the judgment is based.\xe2\x80\x9d Milliken v. Meyer, 311 U.\xc2\xa0S. 457,462 (1940).

A State is not required, however, to afford full faith and credit to a judgment rendered by a court that \xe2\x80\x9cdid not have jurisdiction over the subject matter or the relevant parties.\xe2\x80\x9d Underwriters Nat. Assurance Co. v. North Carolina Life & Accident & Health Ins. Guaranty Assn.,455 U.\xc2\xa0S. 691, 705 (1982). \xe2\x80\x9cConsequently, before a court is bound by [a] judgment rendered in another State, it may inquire into the jurisdictional basis of the foreign court\xe2\x80\x99s decree.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. That jurisdictional inquiry, however, is a limited one. \xe2\x80\x9c[I]f the judgment on its face appears to be a \xe2\x80\x98record of a court of general jurisdiction, such jurisdiction over the cause and the parties is to be presumed unless disproved by extrinsic evidence, or by the record itself.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Milliken, supra, at 462 (quoting Adam v. Saenger, 303 U.\xc2\xa0S. 59,62 (1938)).

Those principles resolve this case. Under Georgia law, as relevant here, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he superior courts of the several counties shall have exclusive jurisdiction in all matters of adoption.\xe2\x80\x9d Ga. Code Ann. \xc2\xa719\xe2\x80\x938\xe2\x80\x932(a) (2015). That provision on its face gave the Georgia Superior Court subject-matter jurisdiction to hear and decide the adoption petition at issue here. The Superior Court resolved that matter by entering a final judgment that made V.\xc2\xa0L. the legal adoptive parent of the children. Whatever the merits of that judgment, it was within the statutory grant of jurisdiction over \xe2\x80\x9call matters of adoption.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The Georgia court thus had the \xe2\x80\x9cadjudicatory authority over the subject matter\xe2\x80\x9d required to entitle its judgment to full faith and credit. Baker, supra, at 233.

The Alabama Supreme Court reached a different result by relying on Ga. Code Ann. \xc2\xa719\xe2\x80\x938\xe2\x80\x935(a). That statute states (as relevant here) that \xe2\x80\x9ca child who has any living parent or guardian may be adopted by a third party . . . only if each such living parent and each such guardian has voluntarily and in writing surrendered all of his or her rights to such child.\xe2\x80\x9d The Alabama Supreme Court concluded that this provision prohibited the Georgia Superior Court from allowing V.\xc2\xa0L. to adopt the children while also allowing E.\xc2\xa0L. to keep her existing parental rights. It further concluded that this provision went not to the merits but to the Georgia court\xe2\x80\x99s subject-matter jurisdiction. In reaching that crucial second conclusion, the Alabama Supreme Court seems to have relied solely on the fact that the right to adoption under Georgia law is purely statutory, and \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[t]he requirements of Georgia\xe2\x80\x99s adoptions statutes are mandatory and must be strictly construed in favor of the natural parents.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 23a\xe2\x80\x9324a (quoting In\xc2\xa0re Marks, 300 Ga. App. 239, 243, 684 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 364, 367 (2009)).

That analysis is not consistent with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s controlling precedent. Where a judgment indicates on its face that it was rendered by a court of competent jurisdiction, such jurisdiction \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98is to be presumed unless disproved.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Milliken, supra, at 462 (quoting Adam, supra, at 62). There is nothing here to rebut that presumption. The Georgia statute on which the Alabama Supreme Court relied, Ga. Code Ann. \xc2\xa719\xe2\x80\x938\xe2\x80\x935(a), does not speak in jurisdictional terms; for instance, it does not say that a Georgia court \xe2\x80\x9cshall have jurisdiction to enter an adoption decree\xe2\x80\x9d only if each existing parent or guardian has surrendered his or her parental rights. Neither the Georgia Supreme Court nor any Georgia appellate court, moreover, has construed \xc2\xa719\xe2\x80\x938\xe2\x80\x935(a) as jurisdictional. That construction would also be difficult to reconcile with Georgia law. Georgia recognizes that in general, subject-matter jurisdiction addresses \xe2\x80\x9cwhether a court has jurisdiction to decide a particular class of cases,\xe2\x80\x9d Goodrum v. Goodrum, 283 Ga. 163, 657 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 192 (2008), not whether a court should grant relief in any given case. Unlike \xc2\xa719\xe2\x80\x938\xe2\x80\x932(a), which expressly gives Georgia superior courts \xe2\x80\x9cexclusive jurisdiction in all matters of adoption,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa719\xe2\x80\x938\xe2\x80\x935(a) does not speak to whether a court has the power to decide a general class of cases. It only provides a rule of decision to apply in determining if a particular adoption should be allowed.

Section 19\xe2\x80\x938\xe2\x80\x935(a) does not become jurisdictional just because it is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98mandatory\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98must be strictly construed.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 23a\xe2\x80\x9324a (quoting Marks, supra, at 243, 684 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 367). This Court \xe2\x80\x9chas long rejected the notion that all mandatory prescriptions, however emphatic, are properly typed jurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x9d Gonzalez v. Thaler, 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. 134, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 10\xe2\x80\x9311) (internal quotation marks and ellipsis omitted). Indeed, the Alabama Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning would give jurisdictional status to every requirement of the Georgia adoption statutes, since Georgia law indicates those requirements are all mandatory and must be strictly construed. Marks, supra, at 243, 684 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 367. That result would comport neither with Georgia law nor with common sense.

As Justice Holmes observed more than a century ago, \xe2\x80\x9cit sometimes may be difficult to decide whether certain words in a statute are directed to jurisdiction or to merits.\xe2\x80\x9d Fauntleroy v. Lum, 210 U.\xc2\xa0S. 230\xe2\x80\x93235 (1908). In such cases, especially where the Full Faith and Credit Clause is concerned, a court must be \xe2\x80\x9cslow to read ambiguous words, as meaning to leave the judgment open to dispute, or as intended to do more than fix the rule by which the court should decide.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 235. That time-honored rule controls here. The Georgia judgment appears on its face to have been issued by a court with jurisdiction, and there is no established Georgia law to the contrary. It follows that the Alabama Supreme Court erred in refusing to grant that judgment full faith and credit.

The petition for writ of certiorari is granted. The judgment of the Alabama Supreme Court is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.

It is so ordered.
