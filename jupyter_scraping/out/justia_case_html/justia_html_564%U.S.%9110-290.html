MICROSOFT CORP. v. i4i LIMITED PARTNERSHIP et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the federal circuit

\n

No. 10\xe2\x80\x93290.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 18, 2011\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 9, 2011

\n

In asserting patent invalidity as a defense to an infringement action, an alleged infringer must contend with \xc2\xa7282 of the Patent Act of 1952 (Act), under which \xe2\x80\x9c[a] patent shall be presumed valid\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he burden of establishing invalidity \xe2\x80\xa6 shall rest on the party asserting\xe2\x80\x9d it. Since 1984, the Federal Circuit has read \xc2\xa7282 to require a defendant seeking to overcome the presumption to persuade the factfinder of its invalidity defense by clear and convincing evidence.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents (collectively, i4i) hold the patent at issue, which claims an improved method for editing computer documents. After i4i sued petitioner Microsoft Corp. for willful infringement of that patent, Microsoft counterclaimed and sought a declaration that the patent was invalid under \xc2\xa7102(b)\xe2\x80\x99s on-sale bar, which precludes patent protection for any \xe2\x80\x9cinvention\xe2\x80\x9d that was \xe2\x80\x9con sale in this country\xe2\x80\x9d more than one year prior to the filing of a patent application. The parties agreed that, more than a year before filing its patent application, i4i had sold a software program known as S4 in the United States, but they disagreed over whether that software embodied the invention claimed in i4i\xe2\x80\x99s patent. Relying on the undisputed fact that the S4 software was never presented to the Patent and Trademark Office (PTO) during its examination of the patent application, Microsoft objected to i4i\xe2\x80\x99s proposed jury instruction that the invalidity defense must be proved by clear and convincing evidence. The District Court nevertheless gave that instruction, rejecting Microsoft\xe2\x80\x99s alternative instruction proposing a preponderance of the evidence standard. The jury found that Microsoft willfully infringed the i4i patent and had failed to prove the patent\xe2\x80\x99s invalidity. The Federal Circuit affirmed, relying on its settled interpretation of \xc2\xa7282.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Section 282 requires an invalidity defense to be proved by clear and convincing evidence. Pp. 5\xe2\x80\x9320.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The Court rejects Microsoft\xe2\x80\x99s contention that a defendant need only persuade the jury of a patent invalidity defense by a preponderance of the evidence. Where Congress has prescribed the governing standard of proof, its choice generally controls. Steadman v. SEC, 450 U. S. 91, 95. Congress has made such a choice here. While \xc2\xa7282 includes no express articulation of the standard of proof, where Congress uses a common-law term in a statute, the Court assumes the \xe2\x80\x9cterm \xe2\x80\xa6 comes with a common law meaning.\xe2\x80\x9d Safeco Ins. Co. of America v. Burr, 551 U. S. 47, 58. Here, by stating that a patent is \xe2\x80\x9cpresumed valid,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7282, Congress used a term with a settled common-law meaning. Radio Corp. of America v. Radio Engineering Laboratories, Inc., 293 U. S. 1 (RCA), is authoritative. There, tracing nearly a century of case law, the Court stated, inter alia, that \xe2\x80\x9cthere is a presumption of [patent] validity [that is] not to be overthrown except by clear and cogent evidence,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 2. Microsoft\xe2\x80\x99s contention that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s pre-Act precedents applied a clear-and-convincing standard only in two limited circumstances is unavailing, given the absence of those qualifications from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases. Also unpersuasive is Microsoft\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the Federal Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation must fail because it renders superfluous \xc2\xa7282\xe2\x80\x99s additional statement that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he burden of establishing invalidity \xe2\x80\xa6 shall rest on the party asserting\xe2\x80\x9d it. The canon against superfluity assists only where a competing interpretation gives effect \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98to every clause and word of a statute.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Duncan v. Walker, 533 U. S. 167, 174. Here, no interpretation of \xc2\xa7282 avoids excess language because, under either of Microsoft\xe2\x80\x99s alternative theories\xe2\x80\x94that the presumption only allocates the burden of production or that it shifts both the burdens of production and persuasion\xe2\x80\x94the presumption itself would be unnecessary in light of \xc2\xa7282\xe2\x80\x99s additional statement as to the challenger\xe2\x80\x99s burden. Pp. 5\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Also rejected is Microsoft\xe2\x80\x99s argument that a preponderance standard must at least apply where the evidence before the factfinder was not before the PTO during the examination process. It is true enough that, in these circumstances, \xe2\x80\x9cthe rationale underlying the presumption\xe2\x80\x94that the PTO, in its expertise, has approved the claim\xe2\x80\x94seems much diminished,\xe2\x80\x9d KSR Int\xe2\x80\x99l Co. v. Teleflex Inc., 550 U. S. 398, 426, though other rationales may still animate the presumption. But the question remains whether Congress has specified the applicable standard of proof. As established here today, Congress did just that by codifying the common-law presumption of patent validity and, implicitly, the heightened standard of proof attached to it. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s pre-Act cases never adopted or endorsed Microsoft\xe2\x80\x99s fluctuating standard of proof. And they do not indicate, even in dicta, that anything less than a clear-and-convincing standard would ever apply to an invalidity defense. In fact, the Court indicated to the contrary. See RCA, 293 U. S., at 8. Finally, the Court often applied the heightened standard of proof without mentioning whether the relevant prior-art evidence had been before the PTO examiner, in circumstances strongly suggesting it had not. See, e.g., Smith v. Hall, 301 U. S. 216, 227, 233. Nothing in \xc2\xa7282\xe2\x80\x99s text suggests that Congress meant to depart from that understanding to enact a standard of proof that would rise and fall with the facts of each case. Indeed, had Congress intended to drop the heightened standard of proof where the evidence before the jury varied from that before the PTO, it presumably would have said so expressly. Those pre-Act cases where various Courts of Appeals observed that the presumption is weakened or dissipated where the evidence was never considered by the PTO should be read to reflect the commonsense principle that if the PTO did not have all material facts before it, its considered judgment may lose significant force. Cf. KSR, 550 U. S., at 427. Consistent with that principle, a jury may be instructed to evaluate whether the evidence before it is materially new, and if so, to consider that fact when determining whether an invalidity defense has been proved by clear and convincing evidence. Pp. 14\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0This Court is in no position to judge the comparative force of the parties\xe2\x80\x99 policy arguments as to the wisdom of the clear-and-convincing-evidence standard that Congress adopted. Congress specified the applicable standard of proof in 1952 when it codified the common-law presumption of patent validity. During the nearly 30 years that the Federal Circuit has interpreted \xc2\xa7282 as the Court does today, Congress has often amended \xc2\xa7282 and other patent laws, but apparently has never considered any proposal to lower the standard of proof. Indeed, Congress has left the Federal Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation in place despite ongoing criticism, both from within the Federal Government and without. Accordingly, any recalibration of the standard of proof remains in Congress\xe2\x80\x99 hands. Pp. 18\xe2\x80\x9320.

\n

598 F.\xc2\xa03d 831, affirmed.

MICROSOFT CORPORATION, PETITIONER v. i4i LIMITED PARTNERSHIP et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the federal circuit

\n

[June 9, 2011]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Sotomayor delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under \xc2\xa7282 of the Patent Act of 1952, \xe2\x80\x9c[a] patent shall be presumed valid\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he burden of establishing in-validity of a patent or any claim thereof shall rest on the party asserting such invalidity.\xe2\x80\x9d 35 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7282. We consider whether \xc2\xa7282 requires an invalidity defense to be proved by clear and convincing evidence. We hold that it does.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Pursuant to its authority under the Patent Clause, U. S. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa78, cl.\xc2\xa08, Congress has charged the United States Patent and Trademark Office (PTO) with the task of examining patent applications, 35 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72(a)(1), and issuing patents if \xe2\x80\x9cit appears that the applicant is entitled to a patent under the law,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7131. Congress has set forth the prerequisites for issuance of a patent, which the PTO must evaluate in the examination process. To receive patent protection a claimed invention must, among other things, fall within one of the express categories of patentable subject matter, \xc2\xa7101, and be novel, \xc2\xa7102, and nonobvious, \xc2\xa7103. Most relevant here, the on-sale bar of \xc2\xa7102(b) precludes patent protection for any \xe2\x80\x9cinvention\xe2\x80\x9d that was \xe2\x80\x9con sale in this country\xe2\x80\x9d more than one year prior to the filing of a patent application. See generally Pfaff v. Wells Electronics, Inc., 525 U. S. 55, 67\xe2\x80\x9368 (1998). In evaluating whether these and other statutory conditions have been met, PTO examiners must make various factual determinations\xe2\x80\x94for instance, the state of the prior art in the field and the nature of the advancement embodied in the invention. See Dickinson v. Zurko, 527 U. S. 150, 153 (1999).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Once issued, a patent grants certain exclusive rights to its holder, including the exclusive right to use the invention during the patent\xe2\x80\x99s duration. To enforce that right, a patentee can bring a civil action for infringement if another person \xe2\x80\x9cwithout authority makes, uses, offers to sell, or sells any patented invention, within the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7271(a); see also \xc2\xa7281.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Among other defenses under \xc2\xa7282 of the Patent Act of 1952 (1952 Act), an alleged infringer may assert the invalidity of the patent\xe2\x80\x94that is, he may attempt to prove that the patent never should have issued in the first place. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7282(2), (3). A defendant may argue, for instance, that the claimed invention was obvious at the time and thus that one of the conditions of patentability was lacking. See \xc2\xa7282(2); see also \xc2\xa7103. \xe2\x80\x9cWhile the ultimate question of patent validity is one of law,\xe2\x80\x9d Graham v. John Deere Co.\nof Kansas City, 383 U. S. 1, 17 (1966) (citing Great Atlantic & Pacific Tea Co. v. Supermarket Equipment Corp., 340 U. S. 147, 155 (1950) (Douglas, J., concurring)); see post,\nat 1 (Breyer, J., concurring), the same factual questions underlying the PTO\xe2\x80\x99s original examination of a patent ap-plication will also bear on an invalidity defense in an in-fringement action. See, e.g., 383 U. S., at 17 (describing the \xe2\x80\x9cbasic factual inquiries\xe2\x80\x9d that form the \xe2\x80\x9cbackground\xe2\x80\x9d for evaluating obviousness); Pfaff, 525 U. S., at 67\xe2\x80\x9369 (same, as to the on-sale bar).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In asserting an invalidity defense, an alleged infringer must contend with the first paragraph of \xc2\xa7282, which provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] patent shall be presumed valid\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he burden of establishing invalidity \xe2\x80\xa6 rest[s] on the party asserting such invalidity.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 1] Under the Federal Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s reading of \xc2\xa7282, a defendant seeking to overcome this presumption must persuade the factfinder of its in-validity defense by clear and convincing evidence. Judge Rich, a principal drafter of the 1952 Act, articulated this view for the court in American Hoist & Derrick Co. v. Sowa & Sons, Inc., 725 F.\xc2\xa02d 1350 (CA Fed. 1984). There, the Federal Circuit held that \xc2\xa7282 codified \xe2\x80\x9cthe existing presumption of validity of patents,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 1359 (internal quotation marks omitted)\xe2\x80\x94what, until that point, had been a common-law presumption based on \xe2\x80\x9cthe basic proposition that a government agency such as the [PTO] was presumed to do its job,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. Relying on this Court\xe2\x80\x99s pre-1952 precedent as to the \xe2\x80\x9cforce of the presumption,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. (citing Radio Corp. of America v. Radio Engineering Laboratories, Inc., 293 U. S. 1 (1934) (RCA)), Judge Rich concluded:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[Section] 282 creates a presumption that a patent is valid and imposes the burden of proving invalidity on the attacker. That burden is constant and never changes and is to convince the court of invalidity by clear evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d 725 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 1360.

\n

\nIn the nearly 30 years since American Hoist, the Federal Circuit has never wavered in this interpretation of \xc2\xa7282. See, e.g., Greenwood v. Hattori Seiko Co., 900 F.\xc2\xa02d 238, 240\xe2\x80\x93241 (CA Fed. 1990); Ultra-Tex Surfaces, Inc. v. Hill Bros. Chemical Co., 204 F.\xc2\xa03d 1360, 1367 (CA Fed. 2000); ALZA Corp. v. Andrx Pharmaceuticals, LLC, 603 F.\xc2\xa03d 935, 940 (CA Fed. 2010).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents i4i Limited Partnership and Infrastructures for Information Inc. (collectively, i4i) hold the patent at issue in this suit. The i4i patent claims an improved method for editing computer documents, which stores a document\xe2\x80\x99s content separately from the metacodes asso-ciated with the document\xe2\x80\x99s structure. In 2007, i4i sued petitioner Microsoft Corporation for willful infringement, claiming that Microsoft\xe2\x80\x99s manufacture and sale of certain Microsoft Word products infringed i4i\xe2\x80\x99s patent. In addition to denying infringement, Microsoft counterclaimed and sought a declaration that i4i\xe2\x80\x99s patent was invalid and unenforceable.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Specifically and as relevant here, Microsoft claimed that the on-sale bar of \xc2\xa7102(b) rendered the patent invalid, pointing to i4i\xe2\x80\x99s prior sale of a software program known as S4. The parties agreed that, more than one year prior to the filing of the i4i patent application, i4i had sold S4\nin the United States. They presented opposing arguments to the jury, however, as to whether that software embodied the invention claimed in i4i\xe2\x80\x99s patent. Because the software\xe2\x80\x99s source code had been destroyed years before the commencement of this litigation, the factual dispute turned largely on trial testimony by S4\xe2\x80\x99s two inventors\xe2\x80\x94also the named inventors on the i4i patent\xe2\x80\x94both of whom testified that S4 did not practice the key invention disclosed in the patent.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Relying on the undisputed fact that the S4 software was never presented to the PTO examiner, Microsoft objected to i4i\xe2\x80\x99s proposed instruction that it was required to prove its invalidity defense by clear and convincing evidence. Instead, \xe2\x80\x9cif an instruction on the \xe2\x80\x98clear and convincing\xe2\x80\x99 burden were [to be] given,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 124a, n.\xc2\xa08, Microsoft re-quested the following:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Microsoft\xe2\x80\x99s burden of proving invalidity and unenforceability is by clear and convincing evidence. However, Microsoft\xe2\x80\x99s burden of proof with regard to its\ndefense of invalidity based on prior art that the ex-aminer did not review during the prosecution of the patent-in-suit is by preponderance of the evidence.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\nRejecting the hybrid standard of proof that Microsoft advocated, the District Court instructed the jury that \xe2\x80\x9cMicrosoft has the burden of proving invalidity by clear and convincing evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 195a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The jury found that Microsoft willfully infringed the i4i patent and that Microsoft failed to prove invalidity due to the on-sale bar or otherwise. Denying Microsoft\xe2\x80\x99s post-trial motions, the District Court rejected Microsoft\xe2\x80\x99s contention that the court improperly instructed the jury on the standard of proof. The Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit affirmed.[Footnote 2] 598 F.\xc2\xa03d 831, 848 (2010). Relying on its settled interpretation of \xc2\xa7282, the court explained that it could \xe2\x80\x9cdiscern [no] error\xe2\x80\x9d in the jury instruction requiring Microsoft to prove its invalidity defense by clear and convincing evidence. Ibid. We granted certiorari. 562 U. S. ___ (2010).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0According to Microsoft, a defendant in an infringement action need only persuade the jury of an invalidity defense by a preponderance of the evidence. In the alternative, Microsoft insists that a preponderance standard must apply at least when an invalidity defense rests on evidence that was never considered by the PTO in the examination process. We reject both contentions.[Footnote 3]

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Where Congress has prescribed the governing standard of proof, its choice controls absent \xe2\x80\x9ccountervailing constitutional constraints.\xe2\x80\x9d Steadman v. SEC, 450 U. S. 91, 95 (1981). The question, then, is whether Congress has made such a choice here.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As stated, the first paragraph of \xc2\xa7282 provides that\n\xe2\x80\x9c[a] patent shall be presumed valid\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he burden of establishing invalidity of a patent or any claim thereof shall rest on the party asserting such invalidity.\xe2\x80\x9d Thus, by its express terms, \xc2\xa7282 establishes a presumption of patent validity, and it provides that a challenger must overcome that presumption to prevail on an invalidity defense. But, while the statute explicitly specifies the burden of proof, it includes no express articulation of the standard of proof.[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our statutory inquiry, however, cannot simply end there. We begin, of course, with \xe2\x80\x9cthe assumption that the ordinary meaning of the language\xe2\x80\x9d chosen by Congress \xe2\x80\x9caccurately expresses the legislative purpose.\xe2\x80\x9d Engine Mfrs. Assn. v. South Coast Air Quality Management Dist., 541 U. S. 246, 252 (2004) (internal quotation marks omitted). But where Congress uses a common-law term in a statute, we assume the \xe2\x80\x9cterm \xe2\x80\xa6 comes with a common law meaning, absent anything pointing another way.\xe2\x80\x9d Safeco Ins. Co. of America v. Burr, 551 U. S. 47, 58 (2007) (citing Beck v. Prupis, 529 U. S. 494, 500\xe2\x80\x93501 (2000)). Here, by stating that a patent is \xe2\x80\x9cpresumed valid,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7282, Congress used a term with a settled meaning in the common law.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our decision in RCA, 293 U. S. 1, is authoritative. There, tracing nearly a century of case law from this Court and others, Justice Cardozo wrote for a unanimous Court that \xe2\x80\x9cthere is a presumption of validity, a presumption not to be overthrown except by clear and cogent evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 2. Although the \xe2\x80\x9cforce\xe2\x80\x9d of the presumption found \xe2\x80\x9cvarying expression\xe2\x80\x9d in this Court and elsewhere, id., at 7, Justice Cardozo explained, one \xe2\x80\x9ccommon core of thought and truth\xe2\x80\x9d unified the decisions:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[O]ne otherwise an infringer who assails the validity of a patent fair upon its face bears a heavy burden of persuasion, and fails unless his evidence has more than a dubious preponderance. If that is true where the assailant connects himself in some way with the title of the true inventor, it is so a fortiori where he is a stranger to the invention, without claim of title of his own. If it is true where the assailant launches his attack with evidence different, at least in form, from any theretofore produced in opposition to the patent, it is so a bit more clearly where the evidence is\neven verbally the same.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 8 (internal citation omitted).[Footnote 5]

\n

\nThe common-law presumption, in other words, reflected the universal understanding that a preponderance standard of proof was too \xe2\x80\x9cdubious\xe2\x80\x9d a basis to deem a patent invalid. Ibid.; see also id., at 7 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] patent \xe2\x80\xa6 is presumed to be valid until the presumption has been overcome by convincing evidence of error\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thus, by the time Congress enacted \xc2\xa7282 and declared that a patent is \xe2\x80\x9cpresumed valid,\xe2\x80\x9d the presumption of patent validity had long been a fixture of the common law. According to its settled meaning, a defendant raising an invalidity defense bore \xe2\x80\x9ca heavy burden of persuasion,\xe2\x80\x9d requiring proof of the defense by clear and convincing evidence. Id., at 8. That is, the presumption encompassed not only an allocation of the burden of proof but also an imposition of a heightened standard of proof. Under the general rule that a common-law term comes with its\ncommon-law meaning, we cannot conclude that Congress intended to \xe2\x80\x9cdrop\xe2\x80\x9d the heightened standard proof from\nthe presumption simply because \xc2\xa7282 fails to reiterate it expressly. Neder v. United States, 527 U. S. 1, 23 (1999); see also id., at 21 (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Where Congress uses terms that have accumulated settled meaning under . . . the common law, [we] must infer, unless the statute otherwise dictates, that Congress means to incorporate the established meaning of those terms.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Nationwide Mut. Ins. Co. v. Darden, 503 U. S. 318, 322 (1992))); Standard Oil Co. of N. J. v. United Sates, 221 U. S. 1, 59 (1911) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]here words are employed in a statute which had at the time a well-known meaning at common law or in the law of this country they are presumed to have been used in that sense \xe2\x80\xa6\xe2\x80\x9d). \xe2\x80\x9cOn the contrary, we must presume that Congress intended to incorporate\xe2\x80\x9d the heightened standard of proof, \xe2\x80\x9cunless the statute otherwise dictates.\xe2\x80\x9d Neder, 527 U. S., at 23 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We recognize that it may be unusual to treat a presumption as alone establishing the governing standard of proof. See, e.g., J. Thayer, Preliminary Treatise on Evidence at the Common Law 336\xe2\x80\x93337 (1898) (hereinafter Thayer) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhen \xe2\x80\xa6 we read that the contrary of any particular presumption must be proved beyond a reasonable doubt, \xe2\x80\xa6 it is to be recognized that we have something superadded to the rule of presumption, namely, another rule as to\nthe amount of evidence which is needed to overcome the presumption\xe2\x80\x9d). But given how judges, including Justice Cardozo, repeatedly understood and explained the presumption of patent validity, we cannot accept Microsoft\xe2\x80\x99s argument that Congress used the words \xe2\x80\x9cpresumed valid\xe2\x80\x9d to adopt only a procedural device for \xe2\x80\x9cshifting the burden of production,\xe2\x80\x9d or for \xe2\x80\x9cshifting both the burden of production and the burden of persuasion.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 21\xe2\x80\x9322 (emphasis deleted). Whatever the significance of a presumption in the abstract, basic principles of statutory construction require us to assume that Congress meant to incorporate \xe2\x80\x9cthe cluster of ideas\xe2\x80\x9d attached to the common-law term it adopted. Beck, 529 U. S., at 501 (internal quotation marks omitted). And RCA leaves no doubt that attached to the common-law presumption of patent validity was an expression as to its \xe2\x80\x9cforce,\xe2\x80\x9d 293 U. S., at 7\xe2\x80\x94that is, the standard of proof required to overcome it.[Footnote 6]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Resisting the conclusion that Congress adopted the heightened standard of proof reflected in our pre-1952 cases, Microsoft contends that those cases applied a clear-and-convincing standard of proof only in two limited\ncircumstances, not in every case involving an invalidity defense. First, according to Microsoft, the heightened standard of proof applied in cases \xe2\x80\x9cinvolving oral testimony of prior invention,\xe2\x80\x9d simply to account for the unreliability of such testimony. Brief for Petitioner 25. Second, Microsoft tells us, the heightened standard of proof applied to \xe2\x80\x9cinvalidity challenges based on priority of invention,\xe2\x80\x9d where that issue had previously been litigated\nbetween the parties in PTO proceedings. Id., at 28.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Squint as we may, we fail to see the qualifications that Microsoft purports to identify in our cases. They certainly make no appearance in RCA\xe2\x80\x99s explanation of the presumption of patent validity. RCA simply said, without qualification, \xe2\x80\x9cthat one otherwise an infringer who assails the validity of a patent fair upon its face bears a heavy burden of persuasion, and fails unless his evidence has more than a dubious preponderance.\xe2\x80\x9d 293 U. S., at 8; see also id., at 7 (\xe2\x80\x9cA patent regularly issued, and even more obviously\na patent issued after a hearing of all the rival claimants,\nis presumed to be valid until the presumption has been overcome by convincing evidence of error\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). Nor do they appear in any of our cases as express limitations on the application of the heightened standard of proof. Cf., e.g., Smith v. Hall, 301 U. S. 216, 233 (1937) (citing RCA for the proposition that a \xe2\x80\x9cheavy burden of persuasion \xe2\x80\xa6 rests upon one who seeks to negative novelty in a patent by showing prior use\xe2\x80\x9d); Mumm v. Jacob E. Decker & Sons, 301 U. S. 168, 171 (1937) (\xe2\x80\x9cNot only is the burden to make good this defense upon the party setting it up, but his burden is a heavy one, as it has been held that every reasonable doubt should be resolved against him\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). In fact, Microsoft itself admits that our cases \xe2\x80\x9ccould be read as announcing a heightened standard applicable to all invalidity assertions.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 30 (emphasis deleted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0 Furthermore, we cannot agree that Microsoft\xe2\x80\x99s proposed limitations are inherent\xe2\x80\x94even if unexpressed\xe2\x80\x94in our\npre-1952 cases. As early as 1874 we explained that the burden of proving prior inventorship \xe2\x80\x9crests upon [the de-fendant], and every reasonable doubt should be resolved against him,\xe2\x80\x9d without tying that rule to the vagaries and manipulability of oral testimony. Coffin v. Ogden, 18 Wall. 120, 124 (1874). And, more than 60 years later, we applied that rule where the evidence in support of a prior-use defense included documentary proof\xe2\x80\x94not just oral testimony\xe2\x80\x94in a case presenting no priority issues at all. See Smith, 301 U. S., at 221, 233. Thus, even if Congress searched for some unstated limitations on the heightened standard of proof in our cases, it would have found none.[Footnote 7]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Microsoft also argues that the Federal Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of \xc2\xa7282\xe2\x80\x99s statement that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] patent shall be pre-sumed valid\xe2\x80\x9d must fail because it renders superfluous\nthe statute\xe2\x80\x99s additional statement that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he burden of establishing invalidity of a patent \xe2\x80\xa6 shall rest on the party asserting such invalidity.\xe2\x80\x9d We agree that if the presumption imposes a heightened standard of proof on the patent challenger, then it alone suffices to establish that the defendant bears the burden of persuasion. Cf. Director, Office of Workers\xe2\x80\x99 Compensation Programs v. Greenwich Collieries, 512 U. S. 267, 278 (1994) (\xe2\x80\x9cA standard of proof \xe2\x80\xa6 can apply only to a burden of persuasion\xe2\x80\x9d). Indeed, the Federal Circuit essentially recognized as much in American Hoist. See 725 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1359.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But the canon against superfluity assists only where a competing interpretation gives effect \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98to every clause and word of a statute.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Duncan v. Walker, 533 U. S. 167, 174 (2001) (quoting United States v. Menasche, 348 U. S. 528, 538\xe2\x80\x93539 (1955)); see Bruesewitz v. Wyeth LLC, 562 U. S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 12). Here, no interpretation of \xc2\xa7282\xe2\x80\x94including the two alternatives advanced by Microsoft\xe2\x80\x94avoids excess language. That is, if the presumption only \xe2\x80\x9callocates the burden of production,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 21, or if it instead \xe2\x80\x9cshift[s] both the burden of production and the burden of persuasion,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 22 (emphasis deleted), then it would be unnecessary in light of \xc2\xa7282\xe2\x80\x99s statement that the challenger bears the \xe2\x80\x9cburden of establishing invalidity.\xe2\x80\x9d See 21B Fed. Practice \xc2\xa75122, at 401 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he same party who has the burden of persuasion also starts out with the burden of producing evidence\xe2\x80\x9d). \xe2\x80\x9cThere are times when Congress enacts provisions that are superfluous,\xe2\x80\x9d Corley v. United States, 556 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (Alito, J., dissenting) (slip op., at 3), and the kind of excess language that Microsoft identifies in \xc2\xa7282 is hardly unusual in comparison to other statutes that set forth a presumption, a burden of persuasion, and a standard of proof. Cf., e.g., 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(e)(1).[Footnote 8]

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Reprising the more limited argument that it pressed below, Microsoft argues in the alternative that a preponderance standard must at least apply where the evidence before the factfinder was not before the PTO during the examination process. In particular, it relies on KSR\nInt\xe2\x80\x99l Co. v. Teleflex Inc., 550 U. S. 398 (2007), where we ob-served that, in these circumstances, \xe2\x80\x9cthe rationale underlying the presumption\xe2\x80\x94that the PTO, in its expertise, has approved the claim\xe2\x80\x94seems much diminished.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 426.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That statement is true enough, although other rationales may animate the presumption in such circumstances. See The Barbed Wire Patent, 143 U. S. 275, 292 (1892) (explaining that because the patentee \xe2\x80\x9cfirst published this device; put it upon record; made use of it for a practical purpose; and gave it to the public \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. doubts \xe2\x80\xa6 concerning the actual inventor \xe2\x80\xa6 should be resolved in favor\nof the patentee\xe2\x80\x9d); cf. Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 33 (arguing that even when the administrative cor-rectness rationale has no relevance, the heightened standard of proof \xe2\x80\x9cserves to protect the patent holder\xe2\x80\x99s reliance interests\xe2\x80\x9d in disclosing an invention to the public in\nexchange for patent protection). The question remains, however, whether Congress has specified the applicable standard of proof. As established, Congress did just that by codifying the common-law presumption of patent validity and, implicitly, the heightened standard of proof attached to it.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our pre-1952 cases never adopted or endorsed the kind of fluctuating standard of proof that Microsoft envisions. And they do not indicate, even in dicta, that anything less than a clear-and-convincing standard would ever apply to an invalidity defense raised in an infringement action. To the contrary, the Court spoke on this issue directly in RCA, stating that because the heightened standard of proof applied where the evidence before the court was \xe2\x80\x9cdifferent\xe2\x80\x9d from that considered by the PTO, it applied even more clearly where the evidence was identical. 293 U. S., at 8. Likewise, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s statement that a \xe2\x80\x9cdubious preponderance\xe2\x80\x9d will never suffice to sustain an invalidity defense, ibid., admitted of no apparent exceptions. Finally, this Court often applied the heightened standard of proof without any mention of whether the relevant prior-art evidence had been before the PTO examiner, in circumstances strongly suggesting it had not. See, e.g., Smith, 301 U. S., at 227, 233.[Footnote 9]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nothing in \xc2\xa7282\xe2\x80\x99s text suggests that Congress meant to depart from that understanding to enact a standard of proof that would rise and fall with the facts of each\ncase. Indeed, had Congress intended to drop the heightened standard of proof where the evidence before the jury varied from that before the PTO\xe2\x80\x94and thus to take the unusual and impractical step of enacting a variable standard of proof that must itself be adjudicated in each case, cf. Santosky v. Kramer, 455 U. S. 745, 757 (1982)[Footnote 10]\xe2\x80\x94we assume it would have said so expressly.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To be sure, numerous courts of appeals in the years preceding the 1952 Act observed that the presumption of validity is \xe2\x80\x9cweakened\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cdissipated\xe2\x80\x9d in the circumstance that the evidence in an infringement action was never considered by the PTO. See Jacuzzi Bros., Inc. v. Berkeley Pump Co., 191 F.\xc2\xa02d 632, 634 (CA9 1951) (\xe2\x80\x9clargely dissipated\xe2\x80\x9d); H. Schindler & Co. v. C. Saladino & Sons, 81 F.\xc2\xa02d 649, 651 (CA1 1936) (\xe2\x80\x9cweakened\xe2\x80\x9d); Gillette Safety Razor Co. v. Cliff Weil Cigar Co., 107 F.\xc2\xa02d 105, 107 (CA4 1939) (\xe2\x80\x9cgreatly weakened\xe2\x80\x9d); Butler Mfg. Co. v. Enterprise Cleaning Co., 81 F.\xc2\xa02d 711, 716 (CA8 1936) (\xe2\x80\x9cweakened\xe2\x80\x9d). But we cannot read these cases to hold or even to suggest that a preponderance standard would apply in such circumstances, and we decline to impute such a reading to Congress. Instead, we understand these cases to reflect the same commonsense principle that the Federal Circuit has recognized throughout its existence\xe2\x80\x94namely, that new evidence supporting an invalidity defense may \xe2\x80\x9ccarry more weight\xe2\x80\x9d in an infringement action than evidence previously considered by the PTO, American Hoist, 725 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 1360. As Judge Rich explained:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cWhen new evidence touching validity of the patent not considered by the PTO is relied on, the tribunal considering it is not faced with having to disagree with the PTO or with deferring to its judgment or with taking its expertise into account. The evidence may, therefore, carry more weight and go further toward sustaining the attacker\xe2\x80\x99s unchanging burden.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis deleted)

\n

\nSee also SIBIA Neurosciences, Inc. v. Cadus Pharmaceutical Corp., 225 F.\xc2\xa03d 1349, 1355\xe2\x80\x931356 (CA Fed. 2000) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he alleged infringer\xe2\x80\x99s burden may be more easily car-ried because of th[e] additional [evidence]\xe2\x80\x9d); Group One, Ltd. v. Hallmark Cards, Inc., 407 F.\xc2\xa03d 1297, 1306 (CA Fed. 2005) (similar).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Simply put, if the PTO did not have all material facts before it, its considered judgment may lose significant force. Cf. KSR, 550 U. S., at 427. And, concomitantly, the challenger\xe2\x80\x99s burden to persuade the jury of its invalidity defense by clear and convincing evidence may be easier to sustain. In this respect, although we have no occasion to endorse any particular formulation, we note that a jury instruction on the effect of new evidence can, and when requested, most often should be given. When warranted, the jury may be instructed to consider that it has heard evidence that the PTO had no opportunity to evaluate before granting the patent. When it is disputed whether the evidence presented to the jury differs from that evaluated by the PTO, the jury may be instructed to consider that question. In either case, the jury may be instructed to evaluate whether the evidence before it is materially new, and if so, to consider that fact when determining whether an invalidity defense has been proved by clear and convincing evidence. Cf., e.g., Mendenhall v. Cedarapids, Inc., 5 F.\xc2\xa03d 1557, 1563\xe2\x80\x931564 (CA Fed. 1993); see\nalso Brief for International Business Machines Corp. as Amicus Curiae 31\xe2\x80\x9337. Although Microsoft emphasized in its argument to the jury that S4 was never considered by the PTO, it failed to request an instruction along these lines from the District Court. Now, in its reply brief in this Court, Microsoft insists that an instruction of this kind was warranted. Reply Brief for Petitioner 22\xe2\x80\x9323. That argument, however, comes far too late, and we therefore refuse to consider it. See Rent-A-Center, West, Inc. v. Jackson, 561 U. S. ___ , ___ (2010) (slip op., at 12); cf. Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 51(d)(1)(B).

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The parties and their amici have presented opposing views as to the wisdom of the clear-and-convincing-evidence standard that Congress adopted. Microsoft and its amici contend that the heightened standard of proof dampens innovation by unduly insulating \xe2\x80\x9cbad\xe2\x80\x9d patents from invalidity challenges. They point to the high invalidation rate as evidence that the PTO grants patent protection to too many undeserving \xe2\x80\x9cinventions.\xe2\x80\x9d They claim that inter partes reexamination proceedings before the PTO cannot fix the problem, as some grounds for invalidation (like the on-sale bar at issue here) cannot be raised in such proceedings. They question the deference that the PTO\xe2\x80\x99s expert determinations warrant, in light of the agency\xe2\x80\x99s resources and procedures, which they deem inadequate. And, they insist that the heightened standard of proof essentially causes juries to abdicate their role in reviewing invalidity claims raised in infringement actions.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For their part, i4i and its amici, including the United States, contend that the heightened standard of proof properly limits the circumstances in which a lay jury overturns the considered judgment of an expert agency. They claim that the heightened standard of proof is an essential component of the patent \xe2\x80\x9cbargain,\xe2\x80\x9d see Bonito Boats, Inc. v. Thunder Craft Boats, Inc., 489 U. S. 141, 150\xe2\x80\x93151 (1989), and the incentives for inventors to disclose their innovations to the public in exchange for patent protection. They disagree with the notion that the patent issuance rate is above the optimal level. They explain that limits on the reexamination process reflect a judgment by Congress as to the appropriate degree of interference with patentees\xe2\x80\x99 reliance interests. Finally, they maintain that juries that are properly instructed as to the application of the clear-and-convincing-evidence standard can, and often do, find an invalidity defense established.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We find ourselves in no position to judge the comparative force of these policy arguments. For nearly 30 years, the Federal Circuit has interpreted \xc2\xa7282 as we do today. During this period, Congress has often amended \xc2\xa7282, see, e.g., Pub. L. 104\xe2\x80\x93141, \xc2\xa72, 109 Stat. 352; Pub. L. 98\xe2\x80\x93417, \xc2\xa7203, 98 Stat. 1603; not once, so far as we (and Microsoft) are aware, has it even considered a proposal to lower the standard of proof, see Tr. Oral Arg. 10. Moreover, Congress has amended the patent laws to account for concerns about \xe2\x80\x9cbad\xe2\x80\x9d patents, including by expanding the reexamination process to provide for inter partes proceedings. See Optional Inter Partes Reexamination Procedure Act of 1999, 113 Stat. 1501A\xe2\x80\x93567, codified at 35 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7311 et seq. Through it all, the evidentiary standard adopted in \xc2\xa7282 has gone untouched. Indeed, Congress has left the Federal Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of \xc2\xa7282 in place despite ongoing criticism, both from within the Federal Government and without.[Footnote 11]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress specified the applicable standard of proof\nin 1952 when it codified the common-law presumption of patent validity. Since then, it has allowed the Federal Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s correct interpretation of \xc2\xa7282 to stand. Any re-calibration of the standard of proof remains in its hands.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in full. I write separately be-cause, given the technical but important nature of the invalidity question, I believe it worth emphasizing that in this area of law as in others the evidentiary standard of proof applies to questions of fact and not to questions of law. See, e.g., Addington v. Texas, 441 U. S. 418, 423 (1979). Thus a factfinder must use the \xe2\x80\x9cclear and convincing\xe2\x80\x9d standard where there are disputes about, say, when a product was first sold or whether a prior art reference had been published.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Many claims of invalidity rest, however, not upon factual disputes, but upon how the law applies to facts as given. Do the given facts show that the product was previously \xe2\x80\x9cin public use\xe2\x80\x9d? 35 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7102(b). Do they show that the invention was \xe2\x80\x9cnove[l]\xe2\x80\x9d and that it was \xe2\x80\x9cnon-obvious\xe2\x80\x9d? \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7102, 103. Do they show that the patent ap-plicant described his claims properly? \xc2\xa7112. Where the ultimate question of patent validity turns on the correct answer to legal questions\xe2\x80\x94what these subsidiary legal standards mean or how they apply to the facts as given\xe2\x80\x94today\xe2\x80\x99s strict standard of proof has no application. See, e.g., Graham v. John Deere Co. of Kansas City, 383 U. S. 1, 17 (1966); Minnesota Mining & Mfg. Co. v. Chemque, Inc., 303 F.\xc2\xa03d 1294, 1301 (CA Fed. 2002); Transocean Offshore Deepwater Drilling, Inc. v. Maersk Contractors USA, Inc., 617 F.\xc2\xa03d 1296, 1305 (CA Fed. 2010); cf. Markman v. Westview Instruments, Inc., 517 U. S. 370 (1996).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Courts can help to keep the application of today\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cclear and convincing\xe2\x80\x9d standard within its proper legal bounds by separating factual and legal aspects of an invalidity claim, say, by using instructions based on case-specific circumstances that help the jury make the distinction or by using interrogatories and special verdicts to make clear which specific factual findings underlie the jury\xe2\x80\x99s conclusions. See Fed. Rules Civ. Proc. 49 and 51. By isolating the facts (determined with help of the \xe2\x80\x9cclear and convincing\xe2\x80\x9d standard), courts can thereby assure the proper interpretation or application of the correct legal standard (without use of the \xe2\x80\x9cclear and convincing\xe2\x80\x9d standard). By preventing the \xe2\x80\x9cclear and convincing\xe2\x80\x9d standard from roaming outside its fact-related reservation, courts can increase the likelihood that discoveries or inventions will not receive legal protection where none is due.
