SCA HYGIENE PRODUCTS AKTIEBOLAG et\xc2\xa0al. v. FIRST QUALITY BABY PRODUCTS, LLC, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the federal circuit

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x93927.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 1, 2016\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 21, 2017

\n

In 2003, petitioners (collectively, SCA) notified respondents (collectively, First Quality) that their adult incontinence products infringed an SCA patent. First Quality responded that its own patent antedated SCA\xe2\x80\x99s patent and made it invalid. In 2004, SCA sought reexamination of its patent in light of First Quality\xe2\x80\x99s patent, and in 2007, the Patent and Trademark Office confirmed the SCA patent\xe2\x80\x99s validity. SCA sued First Quality for patent infringement in 2010. The District Court granted summary judgment to First Quality on the grounds of equitable estoppel and laches. While SCA\xe2\x80\x99s appeal was pending, this Court held that laches could not preclude a claim for damages incurred within the Copyright Act\xe2\x80\x99s 3-year limitations period. Petrella v. Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer, Inc., 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. A Federal Circuit panel nevertheless affirmed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s laches holding based on Circuit precedent, which permitted laches to be asserted against a claim for damages incurred within the Patent Act\xe2\x80\x99s 6-year limitations period,\n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7286. The en banc court reheard the case in light of Petrella and reaffirmed the original panel\xe2\x80\x99s laches holding.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Laches cannot be invoked as a defense against a claim for damages brought within \xc2\xa7286\xe2\x80\x99s 6-year limitations period. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0Petrella\xe2\x80\x99s holding rested on both separation-of-powers principles and the traditional role of laches in equity. A statute of limitations reflects a congressional decision that timeliness is better judged by a hard and fast rule instead of a case-specific judicial determination. Applying laches within a limitations period specified by Congress would give judges a \xe2\x80\x9clegislation-overriding\xe2\x80\x9d role that exceeds the Judiciary\xe2\x80\x99s power. 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___. Moreover, applying laches within a limitations period would clash with the gap-filling purpose for which the defense developed in the equity courts. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0Petrella\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning easily fits \xc2\xa7286. There, the Court found in the Copyright Act\xe2\x80\x99s language a congressional judgment that a claim filed within three years of accrual cannot be dismissed on timeliness grounds. 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___. By that same logic, \xc2\xa7286 of the Patent Act represents Congress\xe2\x80\x99s judgment that a patentee may recover damages for any infringement committed within six years of the filing of the claim.

\n

First Quality contends that this case differs from Petrella because a true statute of limitations runs forward from the date a cause of action accrues, whereas \xc2\xa7286\xe2\x80\x99s limitations period runs backward from the filing of the complaint. However, Petrella repeatedly characterized the Copyright Act\xe2\x80\x99s limitations period as running backward from the date the suit was filed. First Quality also contends that a true statute of limitations begins to run when the plaintiff discovers a cause of action, which is not the case with \xc2\xa7286\xe2\x80\x99s limitations period, but ordinarily, a statute of limitations begins to run on the date that the claim accrues, not when the cause of action is discovered. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0The Federal Circuit based its decision on the idea that \xc2\xa7282 of the Patent Act, which provides for \xe2\x80\x9cdefenses in any action involving the validity or infringement of a patent,\xe2\x80\x9d creates an exception to \xc2\xa7286 by codifying laches as such a defense, and First Quality argues that laches is a defense within \xc2\xa7282(b)(1) based on \xe2\x80\x9cunenforceability.\xe2\x80\x9d Even assuming that \xc2\xa7282(b)(1) incorporates a laches defense of some dimension, it does not necessarily follow that the defense may be invoked to bar a claim for damages incurred within the period set out in \xc2\xa7286. Indeed, it would be exceedingly unusual, if not unprecedented, if Congress chose to include in the Patent Act both a statute of limitations for damages and a laches provision applicable to a damages claim. Neither the Federal Circuit, nor any party, has identified a single federal statute that provides such dual protection against untimely claims. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

(d)\xc2\xa0The Federal Circuit and First Quality rely on lower court patent cases decided before the 1952 Patent Act to argue that \xc2\xa7282 codified a pre-1952 practice of permitting laches to be asserted against damages claims. But the most prominent feature of the relevant legal landscape at that time was the well-established rule that laches cannot be invoked to bar a claim for damages incurred within a limitations period specified by Congress. In light of this rule, which Petrella confirmed and restated, 572 U. S., at ___, nothing less than a broad and unambiguous consensus of lower court decisions could support the inference that \xc2\xa7282(b)(1) codifies a very different patent-law-specific rule. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

(e)\xc2\xa0The Federal Circuit and First Quality rely on three types of cases: (1) pre-1938 equity cases; (2) pre-1938 claims at law; and (3) cases decided after the merger of law and equity in 1938. None of these establishes a broad, unambiguous consensus in favor of applying laches to damages claims in the patent context.

\n

Many of the pre-1938 equity cases do not even reveal whether the plaintiff asked for damages, and of the cases in which damages were sought, many merely suggest in dicta that laches might limit damages. The handful of cases that apply laches against a damages claim are too few to establish a settled, national consensus. In any event, the most that can possibly be gathered from a pre-1938 equity case is that laches could defeat a damages claim in an equity court, not that the defense could entirely prevent a patentee from recovering damages.

\n

Similarly, even if all three pre-1938 cases at law cited by First Quality squarely held that laches could be applied to a damages claim within the limitations period, that number would be insufficient to overcome the presumption that Congress legislates against the background of general common-law principles. First Quality argues that the small number of cases at law should not count against its position because there were few patent cases brought at law after 1870, but it is First Quality\xe2\x80\x99s burden to show that Congress departed from the traditional common-law rule.

\n

As for the post-1938 patent case law, there is scant evidence supporting First Quality\xe2\x80\x99s claim that courts continued to apply laches to damages claims after the merger of law and equity. Only two Courts of Appeals held that laches could bar a damages claim, and that does not constitute a settled, uniform practice of applying laches to damages claims. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

(f)\xc2\xa0First Quality\xe2\x80\x99s additional arguments are unconvincing and do not require extended discussion. It points to post-1952 Court of Appeals decisions holding that laches can be invoked as a defense against a damages claim, but nothing that Congress has done since 1952 has altered \xc2\xa7282\xe2\x80\x99s meaning. As for the various policy arguments presented here, this Court cannot overrule Congress\xe2\x80\x99s judgment based on its own policy views. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

SCA HYGIENE PRODUCTS AKTIEBOLAG, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. FIRST QUALITY BABYPRODUCTS, LLC, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the federal circuit

\n

[March 21, 2017]

\n

Justice Alito delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

We return to a subject that we addressed in Petrella v. Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer, Inc., 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014): the relationship between the equitable defense of laches and claims for damages that are brought within the time allowed by a statute of limitations. In Petrella, we held that laches cannot preclude a claim for damages incurred within the Copyright Act\xe2\x80\x99s 3-year limitations period. Id., at ___ (slip op., at 1). \xe2\x80\x9c[L]aches,\xe2\x80\x9d we explained, \xe2\x80\x9ccannot be invoked to bar legal relief\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n the face of a statute of limitations enacted by Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 13). The question in this case is whether Petrella\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning applies to a similar provision of the Patent Act,\n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7286. We hold that it does.

\n

I

\n

Petitioners SCA Hygiene Products Aktiebolag and SCA Personal Care, Inc. (collectively, SCA), manufacture and sell adult incontinence products. In October 2003, SCA sent a letter to respondents (collectively, First Quality), alleging that First Quality was making and selling products that infringed SCA\xe2\x80\x99s rights under U.\xc2\xa0S. Patent No. 6,375,646 B1 (\xe2\x80\x99646 patent). App. 54a. First Quality responded that one of its patents\xe2\x80\x94U.\xc2\xa0S. Patent No. 5,415,649 (Watanabe patent)\xe2\x80\x94antedated the \xe2\x80\x99646 patent and revealed \xe2\x80\x9cthe same diaper construction.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 53a. As a result, First Quality maintained, the \xe2\x80\x99646 patent was invalid and could not support an infringement claim. Ibid. SCA sent First Quality no further correspondence regarding the \xe2\x80\x99646 patent, and First Quality proceeded to develop and market its products.

\n

In July 2004, without notifying First Quality, SCA asked the Patent and Trademark Office (PTO) to initiate a reexamination proceeding to determine whether the \xe2\x80\x99646 patent was valid in light of the Watanabe patent. Id., at 49a\xe2\x80\x9351a. Three years later, in March 2007, the PTO issued a certificate confirming the validity of the \xe2\x80\x99646 patent.

\n

In August 2010, SCA filed this patent infringement action against First Quality. First Quality moved for summary judgment based on laches and equitable estoppel, and the District Court granted that motion on both grounds. 2013 WL 3776173, *12 (WD Ky., July 16, 2013).

\n

SCA appealed to the Federal Circuit, but before the Federal Circuit panel issued its decision, this Court de-cided Petrella. The panel nevertheless held, based on a Fed-eral Circuit precedent, A.\xc2\xa0C. Aukerman Co. v. R.\xc2\xa0L. Chaides Constr. Co., 960 F.\xc2\xa02d 1020 (1992) (en banc), that SCA\xe2\x80\x99s claims were barred by laches.[1]

\n

The Federal Circuit then reheard the case en banc in order to reconsider Aukerman in light of Petrella. But in a 6-to-5 decision, the en banc court reaffirmed Aukerman\xe2\x80\x99s holding that laches can be asserted to defeat a claim for damages incurred within the 6-year period set out in the Patent Act. As it had in Aukerman, the en banc court concluded that Congress, in enacting the Patent Act, had \xe2\x80\x9ccodified a laches defense\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cbarred recovery of legal remedies.\xe2\x80\x9d 807 F.\xc2\xa03d 1311, 1323\xe2\x80\x931329 (2015). Judge Hughes, joined by four other judges, dissented.[2] Id., at 1337\xe2\x80\x931342 (opinion concurring in part and dissenting in part). We granted certiorari. 578 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2016).

\n

II

\n

Laches is \xe2\x80\x9ca defense developed by courts of equity\xe2\x80\x9d to protect defendants against \xe2\x80\x9cunreasonable, prejudicial delay in commencing suit.\xe2\x80\x9d Petrella, supra, at ___, ___ (slip op., at 1, 12). See also 1 D. Dobbs, Law of Remedies \xc2\xa72.3(5), p. 89 (2d ed. 1993) (Dobbs) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe equitable doctrine of laches bars the plaintiff whose unreasonable delay in prosecuting a claim or protecting a right has worked a prejudice to the defendant\xe2\x80\x9d). Before the separate systems of law and equity were merged in 1938, the ordinary rule was that laches was available only in equity courts.[3] See County of Oneida v. Oneida Indian Nation of N.\xc2\xa0Y.,\n\n470 U.\xc2\xa0S. 226\n, n.\xc2\xa016 (1985). This case turns on the application of the defense to a claim for damages, a quintessential legal remedy. We discussed this subject at length in Petrella.

\n

Petrella arose out of a copyright dispute relating to the film Raging Bull. 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 8). The Copyright Act\xe2\x80\x99s statute of limitations requires a copyright holder claiming infringement to file suit \xe2\x80\x9cwithin three years after the claim accrued.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n17 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7507(b). In Petrella, the plaintiff sought relief for alleged acts of infringement that accrued within that 3-year period, but the lower courts nevertheless held that laches barred her claims. See 695 F.\xc2\xa03d 946 (CA9 2012). We reversed, holding that laches cannot defeat a damages claim brought within the period prescribed by the Copyright Act\xe2\x80\x99s statute of limitations. Petrella, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 11\xe2\x80\x9314). And in so holding, we spoke in broad terms. See id., at ___ (slip op., at 13) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]n the face of a statute of limitations enacted by Congress, laches cannot be invoked to bar legal relief\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Petrella\xe2\x80\x99s holding rested on both separation-of-powers principles and the traditional role of laches in equity. Laches provides a shield against untimely claims, id., at ___ (slip op., at 19), and statutes of limitations serve a similar function. When Congress enacts a statute of limitations, it speaks directly to the issue of timeliness and provides a rule for determining whether a claim is timely enough to permit relief. Id., at ___ (slip op., at 11). The enactment of a statute of limitations necessarily reflects a congressional decision that the timeliness of covered claims is better judged on the basis of a generally hard and fast rule rather than the sort of case-specific judicial determination that occurs when a laches defense is asserted. Therefore, applying laches within a limitations period specified by Congress would give judges a \xe2\x80\x9clegislation-overriding\xe2\x80\x9d role that is beyond the Judiciary\xe2\x80\x99s power. Id., at ___ (slip op., at 14). As we stressed in Petrella, \xe2\x80\x9ccourts are not at liberty to jettison Congress\xe2\x80\x99 judgment on the timeliness of suit.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 1).

\n

Applying laches within the limitations period would also clash with the purpose for which the defense developed in the equity courts. As Petrella recounted, the \xe2\x80\x9cprincipal application\xe2\x80\x9d of laches \xe2\x80\x9cwas, and remains, to claims of an equitable cast for which the Legislature has provided no fixed time limitation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 12); see also R. Weaver, E. Shoben, & M. Kelly, Principles of Remedies Law 21 (2d ed. 2011); 1 Dobbs \xc2\xa72.4(4), at 104; 1 J. Story, Commentaries on Equity Jurisprudence \xc2\xa755(a), p. 73 (2d ed. 1839). Laches is a gap-filling doctrine, and where there is a statute of limitations, there is no gap to fill.[4] Petrella, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 14); see also 1 Dobbs \xc2\xa72.4(4), at 108 (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]f the plaintiff has done only what she is permitted to do by statute, and has not misled the defendant [so as to trigger equitable estoppel], the basis for barring the plaintiff seems to have disappeared\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

With Petrella\xe2\x80\x99s principles in mind, we turn to the present dispute.

\n

III

\n

A

\n

Although the relevant statutory provisions in Petrella and this case are worded differently, Petrella\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning easily fits the provision at issue here. As noted, the statute in Petrella precludes a civil action for copyright infringement \xe2\x80\x9cunless it is commenced within three years after the claim accrued.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n17 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7507(b). We saw in this language a congressional judgment that a claim filed within three years of accrual cannot be dismissed on timeliness grounds. 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 11); see also id., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 14\xe2\x80\x9315).

\n

The same reasoning applies in this case. Section 286 of the Patent Act provides: \xe2\x80\x9cExcept as otherwise provided by law, no recovery shall be had for any infringement committed more than six years prior to the filing of the complaint or counterclaim for infringement in the action.\xe2\x80\x9d By the logic of Petrella, we infer that this provision represents a judgment by Congress that a patentee may recover damages for any infringement committed within six years of the filing of the claim.

\n

B

\n

First Quality contends that this case differs from Petrella because \xc2\xa7286 of the Patent Act is not a true statute of limitations. A true statute of limitations, we are told, \xe2\x80\x9cruns forward from the date a cause of action accrues,\xe2\x80\x9d but \xc2\xa7286 \xe2\x80\x9cruns backward from the time of suit.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 41.

\n

Petrella cannot reasonably be distinguished on this ground. First Quality thinks it critical that \xc2\xa7286 \xe2\x80\x9cruns backward from the time of suit,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 41, but Petrella described the Copyright Act\xe2\x80\x99s statute of limitations in almost identical terms. We said that this provision \xe2\x80\x9callows plaintiffs .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to gain retrospective relief running only three years back from the date the complaint was filed.\xe2\x80\x9d 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 6\xe2\x80\x937) (emphasis added). See also id., at ___ (slip op., at 11) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] successful plaintiff can gain retrospective relief only three years back from the time of suit\xe2\x80\x9d). And we described the Copyright Act\xe2\x80\x99s statute of limitations as \xe2\x80\x9ca three-year look-back limitations period.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 4).

\n

First Quality contends that the application of a true statute of limitations, like the defense of laches (but unlike \xc2\xa7286), takes into account the fairness of permitting the adjudication of a particular plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s claim. First Quality argues as follows: \xe2\x80\x9cWhen Congress enacts [a true statute of limitations], it can be viewed as having made a considered judgment about how much delay may occur after a plaintiff knows of a cause of action (i.e., after accrual) before the plaintiff must bring suit\xe2\x80\x94thus potentially leaving no room for judges to evaluate the reasonableness of a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s delay on a case-by-case basis under laches.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 42. According to First Quality, \xc2\xa7286 of the Patent Act is different because it \xe2\x80\x9cturns only on when the infringer is sued, regardless of when the pat-entee learned of the infringement.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

This argument misunderstands the way in which statutes of limitations generally work. First Quality says that the accrual of a claim, the event that triggers the running of a statute of limitations, occurs when \xe2\x80\x9ca plaintiff knows of a cause of action,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., but that is not ordinarily true. As we wrote in Petrella, \xe2\x80\x9c[a] claim ordinarily accrues \xe2\x80\x98when [a] plaintiff has a complete and present cause of action.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 4); see Graham County Soil & Water Conservation Dist. v. United States ex\xc2\xa0rel. Wilson,\n\n545 U.\xc2\xa0S. 409\n\xe2\x80\x93419 (2005). While some claims are subject to a \xe2\x80\x9cdiscovery rule\xe2\x80\x9d under which the limitations period begins when the plaintiff discovers or should have discovered the injury giving rise to the claim, that is not a universal feature of statutes of limitations. See, e.g., ibid. (limitations period in\n\n31 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73731(b)(1) begins to run when the cause of action accrues); TRW Inc. v. Andrews,\n\n534 U.\xc2\xa0S. 19,\n28 (2001)\n (same with regard to\n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71681p). And in Petrella, we specifically noted that \xe2\x80\x9cwe have not passed on the question\xe2\x80\x9d whether the Copyright Act\xe2\x80\x99s statute of limitations is governed by such a rule. 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___, n.\xc2\xa04 (slip op., at 4, n.\xc2\xa04).

\n

For these reasons, Petrella cannot be dismissed as applicable only to what First Quality regards as true statutes of limitations. At least for present purposes, nothing depends on this debatable taxonomy. Compare Automobile Workers v. Hoosier Cardinal Corp.,\n\n383 U.\xc2\xa0S. 696,\n704 (1966)\n (describing \xc2\xa7286 as \xe2\x80\x9cenacting a uniform period of limitations\xe2\x80\x9d); 1 Dobbs \xc2\xa72.4(4), at 107, and n.\xc2\xa033 (same), with A. Stucki Co. v. Buckeye Steel Castings Co., 963 F.\xc2\xa02d 360, 363, n.\xc2\xa03 (CA Fed. 1992) (Section 286 \xe2\x80\x9cis not, strictly speaking, a statute of limitations\xe2\x80\x9d); Standard Oil Co. v. Nippon Shokubai Kagaku Co., Ltd., 754 F.\xc2\xa02d 345, 348 (CA Fed. 1985) (\xe2\x80\x9c[Section] 286 cannot properly be called a \xe2\x80\x98statute of limitations\xe2\x80\x99 in the sense that it defeats the right to bring suit\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

C

\n

The Federal Circuit based its decision on a different footing. Section 286 of the Patent Act begins with the phrase \xe2\x80\x9c[e]xcept as otherwise provided by law,\xe2\x80\x9d and according to the Federal Circuit, \xc2\xa7282 of the Act is a provision that provides otherwise. In its view, \xc2\xa7282 creates an exception to \xc2\xa7286 by codifying laches as a defense to all patent infringement claims, including claims for damages suffered within \xc2\xa7286\xe2\x80\x99s 6-year period. 807 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1329\xe2\x80\x931330. Section 282(b), which does not specifically mention laches, provides in relevant part as follows:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe following shall be defenses in any action involving the validity or infringement of a patent and shall be pleaded:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(1) Noninfringement, absence of liability for infringement or unenforceability.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

The en banc majority below never identified which word or phrase in \xc2\xa7282 codifies laches as a defense, but First Quality argues that laches falls within \xc2\xa7282(b)(1) because laches is a defense based on \xe2\x80\x9cunenforceability.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 28\xe2\x80\x9333.

\n

SCA disputes this interpretation of \xc2\xa7282(b)(1), arguing that laches does not make a patent categorically unenforceable. Reply Brief 6\xe2\x80\x938; see Aukerman, 960 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 1030 (\xe2\x80\x9cRecognition of laches as a defense .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. does not affect the general enforceability of the patent against others\xe2\x80\x9d). We need not decide this question. Even if we assume for the sake of argument that \xc2\xa7282(b)(1) incorporates a laches defense of some dimension, it does not necessarily follow that this defense may be invoked to bar a claim for damages incurred within the period set out in \xc2\xa7286. Indeed, it would be exceedingly unusual, if not unprecedented, if Congress chose to include in the Patent Act both a statute of limitations for damages and a laches provision applicable to a damages claim. Neither the Federal Circuit, nor First Quality, nor any of First Quality\xe2\x80\x99s amici has identified a single federal statute that provides such dual protection against untimely claims.

\n

D

\n

In holding that Congress codified a damages-limiting laches defense, the Federal Circuit relied on patent cases decided by the lower courts prior to the enactment of the Patent Act. After surveying these cases, the Federal Circuit concluded that by 1952 there was a well-established practice of applying laches to such damages claims and that Congress, in adopting \xc2\xa7282, must have chosen to codify such a defense in \xc2\xa7282(b)(1). 807 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1321\xe2\x80\x931329. First Quality now presses a similar argument. We have closely examined the cases on which the Federal Circuit and First Quality rely, and we find that they are insufficient to support the suggested interpretation of the Patent Act. The most prominent feature of the relevant legal landscape at the time of enactment of the Patent Act was the well-established general rule, often repeated by this Court, that laches cannot be invoked to bar a claim for damages incurred within a limitations period specified by Congress. See Holmberg v. Armbrecht,\n\n327 U.\xc2\xa0S. 392,\n395 (1946)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cIf Congress explicitly puts a limit upon the time for enforcing a right which it created, there is an end of the matter\xe2\x80\x9d); United States v. Mack,\n\n295 U.\xc2\xa0S. 480,\n489 (1935)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cLaches within the term of the statute of limitations is no defense at law\xe2\x80\x9d); Wehrman v. Conklin,\n\n155 U.\xc2\xa0S. 314,\n326 (1894)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cThough a good defense in equity, laches is no defense at law. If the plaintiff at law has brought his action within the period fixed by the statute of limitations, no court can deprive him of his right to proceed\xe2\x80\x9d); Cross v. Allen,\n\n141 U.\xc2\xa0S. 528,\n537 (1891)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cSo long as the demands secured were not barred by the statute of limitations, there could be no laches in prosecuting a suit\xe2\x80\x9d). Petrella confirmed and restated this long-standing rule. 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 12) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]his Court has cautioned against invoking laches to bar legal relief\xe2\x80\x9d). If Congress examined the relevant legal landscape when it adopted\n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7282, it could not have missed our cases endorsing this general rule.

\n

The Federal Circuit and First Quality dismiss the significance of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s many reiterations of the general rule because they were not made in patent cases. But as the dissenters below noted, \xe2\x80\x9c[p]atent law is governed by the same common-law principles, methods of statutory interpretation, and procedural rules as other areas of civil litigation.\xe2\x80\x9d 807 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1333 (opinion of Hughes, J.).

\n

In light of the general rule regarding the relationship between laches and statutes of limitations, nothing less than a broad and unambiguous consensus of lower court decisions could support the inference that \xc2\xa7282(b)(1) codifies a very different patent-law-specific rule. No such consensus is to be found.[5]

\n

IV

\n

The pre-1952 cases on which First Quality relies fall into three groups: (1) cases decided by equity courts before 1938; (2) cases decided by law courts before 1938; and (3) cases decided after the merger of equity and law in 1938. We will discuss each group separately.

\n

A

\n

Pre-1938 equity cases

\n

The pre-1938 equity cases are unpersuasive for several, often overlapping reasons. Many do not even reveal whether the plaintiff asked for damages. Indeed, some say nothing at all about the form of relief that was sought, see, e.g., Cummings v. Wilson & Willard Mfg. Co., 4 F.\xc2\xa02d 453 (CA9 1925), and others state only that the plaintiff wanted an accounting of profits, e.g., Westco-Chippewa Pump Co. v. Delaware Elec. & Supply Co., 64 F.\xc2\xa02d 185, 186 (CA3 1933); Wolf Mineral Process Corp. v. Minerals Separation North Am. Corp., 18 F.\xc2\xa02d 483, 484 (CA4 1927). The equitable remedy of an accounting, however, was not the same as damages. The remedy of damages seeks to compensate the victim for its loss, whereas the remedy of an accounting, which Congress abolished in the patent context in 1946,[6] sought disgorgement of ill-gotten profits. See Birdsall v. Coolidge,\n\n93 U.\xc2\xa0S. 64\n\xe2\x80\x9369 (1876); 1 Dobbs \xc2\xa74.3(5), at 611 (\xe2\x80\x9cAccounting holds the defendant liable for his profits, not for damages\xe2\x80\x9d); A. Walker, Patent Laws \xc2\xa7573, p. 401 (1886) (distinguishing between the two remedies); G. Curtis, Law of Patents \xc2\xa7341(a), p. 461 (4th ed. 1873); 2 J. Pomeroy, Treatise on Equitable Remedies \xc2\xa7568, p. 977 (1905).

\n

First Quality argues that courts sometimes used the term \xe2\x80\x9caccounting\xe2\x80\x9d imprecisely to refer to both an accounting of profits and a calculation of damages, Brief for Respondents 19\xe2\x80\x9320, but even if that is true, this loose usage shows only that a reference to \xe2\x80\x9caccounting\xe2\x80\x9d might refer to damages. For that reason, the Federal Circuit did not rely on cases seeking only an accounting, 807 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1326, n.\xc2\xa07, and we likewise exclude such cases from our analysis.

\n

Turning to the cases that actually refer to damages, we note that many of the cases merely suggest in dicta that laches might limit recovery of damages. See, e.g., Hartford-Empire Co. v. Swindell Bros., 96 F.\xc2\xa02d 227, 233, modified on reh\xe2\x80\x99g, 99 F.\xc2\xa02d 61 (CA4 1938). Such dicta \xe2\x80\x9csettles nothing.\xe2\x80\x9d Jama v. Immigration and Customs Enforcement,\n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 335\n, n.\xc2\xa012 (2005). See also Hartford Underwriters Ins. Co. v. Union Planters Bank, N.\xc2\xa0A.,\n\n530 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n\xe2\x80\x9310 (2000); Metropolitan Stevedore Co. v. Rambo,\n\n515 U.\xc2\xa0S. 291,\n300 (1995)\n.

\n

As for the cases in which laches was actually held to bar a claim for damages, e.g., Wolf, Sayer & Heller v. United States Slicing Mach. Co., 261 F. 195, 197\xe2\x80\x93198 (CA7 1919); A.\xc2\xa0R. Mosler & Co. v. Lurie, 209 F. 364, 369\xe2\x80\x93370 (CA2 1913), these cases are too few to establish a settled, national consensus. See Hartford Underwriters, supra, at 10.

\n

Moreover, the most that can possibly be gathered from a pre-1938 equity case is that laches could defeat a damages claim in an equity court, not that the defense could en-tirely prevent a patentee from recovering damages. Before 1870, a patentee wishing to obtain both an injunction against future infringement and damages for past infringement was required to bring two suits, one in an equity court (where injunctive relief but not damages was available), and one in a court of law (where damages but not injunctive relief could be sought). See Beauchamp, The First Patent Litigation Explosion, 125 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J. 848, 913\xe2\x80\x93914 (2016). To rectify this situation, Congress en-acted a law in 1870 authorizing equity courts to award dam-ages in patent-infringement actions. Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa74921. And although statutes of limitations did not generally apply in equity, Congress in 1897 enacted a statute that, like the current \xc2\xa7286, imposed a 6-year limitations period for damages claims and made that statute applicable in both law and equity. \xc2\xa76,\n\n29Stat.\n694. Pointing to cases decided between 1897 and 1938 in which an equity court permitted a defendant in an infringement case to invoke the defense of laches, First Quality contends that Congress, aware of these cases, assumed that the 1952 Act would likewise allow a defendant in an infringement case to claim laches with respect to a claim for damages occurring within a limitations period.

\n

This argument overlooks the fact that a patentee, during the period in question, could always sue for damages in law, where the equitable doctrine of laches did not apply, and could thus avoid any possible laches defense. Thus, accepting First Quality\xe2\x80\x99s argument would not return patentees to the position they held from 1897 to 1938. Instead, it would go much further and permit laches entirely to defeat claims like SCA\xe2\x80\x99s.[7]

\n

B

\n

Pre-1938 claims at law

\n

First Quality cites three Court of Appeals cases in which laches was raised in a proceeding at law and in which, according to First Quality, the defense was held to bar a damages claim. See Universal Coin Lock Co. v. American Sanitary Lock Co., 104 F. 2d 781 (CA7 1939); Banker v. Ford Motor Co., 69 F. 2d 665 (CA3 1934); Ford v. Huff, 296 F. 652 (CA5 1924). But even if all of these cases squarely held that laches could be applied to a damages claim at law within the limitations period, they would still constitute only a handful of decisions out of the corpus of pre-1952 patent cases, and that would not be enough to overcome the presumption that Congress legislates against the background of general common-law principles. See H. McClintock, Handbook of the Principles of Equity \xc2\xa728, p. 75 (2d ed. 1948) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe majority of the courts which have considered the question have refused to enjoin an action at law on the ground of the laches of the plaintiff at law\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

In any event, these cases, like the equity cases, offer minimal support for First Quality\xe2\x80\x99s position. Not one of these cases even mentions the statute of limitations. One of the three, Ford, is not even a patent infringement case; it is a breach-of-contract case arising out of a patent dispute, 296 F., at 654, and it is unclear whether the ground for decision was laches or equitable estoppel. See 807 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1340 (opinion of Hughes, J.). Another, Universal Coin, applied laches to a legal damages claim without any analysis of the propriety of doing so. 104 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 783.

\n

First Quality protests that the paucity of supporting cases at law should not count against its argument since very few patent-infringement cases were brought at law after 1870. Brief for Respondents 25\xe2\x80\x9326. But the fact remains that it is First Quality\xe2\x80\x99s burden to show that Congress departed from the traditional common-law rule highlighted in our cases.[8]

\n

C

\n

Post-merger cases

\n

First Quality claims that courts continued to apply laches to damages claims after the merger of law and equity in 1938, but First Quality\xe2\x80\x99s evidence is scant. During this period, two Courts of Appeals stated in dicta that laches could bar legal damages claims. See Chicago Pneumatic Tool Co. v. Hughes Tool Co., 192 F.\xc2\xa02d 620, 625 (CA10 1951); Shaffer v. Rector Well Equip. Co., 155 F.\xc2\xa02d 344, 347 (CA5 1946). And two others actually held that laches could bar a damages claim. See, e.g., Brennan v. Hawley Prods. Co., 182 F.\xc2\xa02d 945, 948 (CA7 1950); Lukens Steel Co. v. American Locomotive Co., 197 F.\xc2\xa02d 939, 941 (CA2 1952) (alternative holding). This does not constitute a settled, uniform practice of applying laches to damages claims.

\n

After surveying the pre-1952 case law, we are not convinced that Congress, in enacting \xc2\xa7282 of the Patent Act, departed from the general rule regarding the application of laches to damages suffered within the time for filing suit set out in a statute of limitations.

\n

V

\n

First Quality\xe2\x80\x99s additional arguments do not require extended discussion. First Quality points to post-1952 Court of Appeals decisions holding that laches can be invoked as a defense against a damages claim. Noting that Congress has amended \xc2\xa7282 without altering the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98unenforceability\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d language that is said to incorporate a laches defense, First Quality contends that Congress has implicitly ratified these decisions. Brief for Respondents 35\xe2\x80\x9336.

\n

We reject this argument. Nothing that Congress has done since 1952 has altered the meaning of \xc2\xa7282. See Central Bank of Denver, N.\xc2\xa0A. v. First Interstate Bank of Denver, N. A.,\n\n511 U.\xc2\xa0S. 164,\n186 (1994)\n; West Virginia Univ. Hospitals, Inc. v. Casey,\n\n499 U.\xc2\xa0S. 83\n, and n. 7 (1991).

\n

First Quality and its supporting amici also make various policy arguments, but we cannot overrule Congress\xe2\x80\x99s judgment based on our own policy views. We note, however, as we did in Petrella, that the doctrine of equitableestoppel provides protection against some of the problems that First Quality highlights, namely, unscrupulous patentees inducing potential targets of infringement suits to invest in the production of arguably infringing products. 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 19). Indeed, the Federal Circuit held that there are genuine disputes of material fact as to whether equitable estoppel bars First Quality\xe2\x80\x99s claims in this very case. See 807 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1333.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

Laches cannot be interposed as a defense against damages where the infringement occurred within the period prescribed by \xc2\xa7286. The judgment of the Court of Appeals is vacated in part, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

\n

It is so ordered.

Justice Breyer, dissenting.

\n

Laches is a doctrine that bars a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s claim when there has been unreasonable, prejudicial delay in commencing suit. See 1 D. Dobbs, Law of Remedies \xc2\xa72.3(5), p. 89 (2d ed. 1993). The question before us is whether a court can apply this doctrine in a patent infringement action for damages brought within the statute of limitations. The Court holds that a court cannot. Laches, it says, is a \xe2\x80\x9cgap-filling doctrine,\xe2\x80\x9d generally applicable where there is no statute of limitations. But the 1952 Patent Act contains a statute of limitations. Hence there is \xe2\x80\x9cno gap to fill.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 5.

\n

In my view, however, the majority has ignored the fact that, despite the 1952 Act\xe2\x80\x99s statute of limitations, there remains a \xe2\x80\x9cgap\xe2\x80\x9d to fill. See infra, at 2\xe2\x80\x933. Laches fills this gap. And for more than a century courts with virtual unanimity have applied laches in patent damages cases. Congress, when it wrote the 1952 statute, was aware of and intended to codify that judicial practice. I fear that the majority, in ignoring this legal history, opens a new \xe2\x80\x9cgap\xe2\x80\x9d in the patent law, threatening harmful and unfair legal consequences.

\n

I

\n

Consider the relevant statutory language. Section 286 of the Patent Act says: \xe2\x80\x9cExcept as otherwise provided by law, no recovery shall be had for any infringement committed more than six years prior to the filing of the complaint or counterclaim for infringement in the action.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7286 (emphasis added). Section 282 says what the word \xe2\x80\x9cotherwise\xe2\x80\x9d means. It tells us that \xe2\x80\x9cunenforceability\xe2\x80\x9d shall be a defense \xe2\x80\x9cin any action involving the validity or infringement of a patent.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7282(b) (emphasis added).

\n

Two features of this statutory language are important. First, the limitations provision, unlike those in many other statutes, does not set forth a period of time in which to sue, beginning when a claim accrues and then expiring some time later. (The False Claims Act, for example, gives a plaintiff six years from the date of the violation or three years from the date of discovery to file his suit,\n\n31 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73731(b).) Rather, it permits a patentee to sue at any time after an infringement takes place. It simply limits damages to those caused within the preceding six years. That means that a patentee, after learning of a possible infringement in year 1, might wait until year 10 or year 15 or year 20 to bring a lawsuit. And if he wins, he can collect damages for the preceding six years of infringement.

\n

This fact creates a gap. Why? Because a patentee might wait for a decade or more while the infringer (who perhaps does not know or believe he is an infringer) invests heavily in the development of the infringing product (of which the patentee\xe2\x80\x99s invention could be only a small component), while evidence that the infringer might use to, say, show the patent is invalid disappears with time. Then, if the product is a success, the patentee can bring his lawsuit, hoping to collect a significant recovery. And if business-related circumstances make it difficult or impossible for the infringer to abandon its use of the patented invention (i.e., if the infringer is \xe2\x80\x9clocked in\xe2\x80\x9d), then the patentee can keep bringing lawsuits, say, in year 10 (collecting damages from years 4 through 10), in year 16 (collecting damages from years 10 through 16), and in year 20 (collecting any remaining damages). The possibility of this type of outcome reveals a \xe2\x80\x9cgap.\xe2\x80\x9d Laches works to fill the gap by barring recovery when the patentee unreasonably and prejudicially delays suit.

\n

Second, the Patent Act\xe2\x80\x99s language strongly suggests that Congress, when writing the statutory provisions before us, intended to permit courts to continue to use laches to fill this gap. The statute says that there are \xe2\x80\x9cexcept[ions]\xe2\x80\x9d to its 6-year damages limitation rule. It lists \xe2\x80\x9cunenforceability\xe2\x80\x9d as one of those exceptions. At common law, the word \xe2\x80\x9cunenforceability\xe2\x80\x9d had a meaning that encompassed laches. See, e.g., United States v. New Orleans Pacific R. Co.,\n\n248 U.\xc2\xa0S. 507,\n511 (1919)\n (considering whether an agreement \xe2\x80\x9chad become unenforceable by reason of inexcusable laches\xe2\x80\x9d). We often read statutes as incorporating common-law meanings. See Neder v. United States,\n\n527 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1,\n21 (1999)\n. And here there are good reasons for doing so. For one thing, the principal technical drafter of the Patent Act (in a commentary upon which this Court has previously relied, e.g., Warner-Jenkinson Co. v. Hilton Davis Chemical Co.,\n\n520 U.\xc2\xa0S. 17,\n28 (1997)\n) stated that \xc2\xa7282 was meant to codify \xe2\x80\x9cequitable defenses such as laches.\xe2\x80\x9d P. Federico, Commentary on the New Patent Act, 35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0A. 1, 55 (West 1954). For another thing, there is a long history of prior case law that shows with crystal clarity that Congress intended the statute to keep laches as a defense.

\n

II

\n

The pre-1952 case law that I shall discuss is directly relevant because, as this Court has recognized, the 1952 Patent Act was primarily intended to codify existing law. See Halo Electronics v. Pulse Electronics, Inc., 579 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2016) (slip op., at 4); accord, H. R. Rep. No. 1923, 82d Cong., 2d Sess., 3 (1952) (stating that the \xe2\x80\x9cmain purpose\xe2\x80\x9d of the Patent Act was \xe2\x80\x9ccodification and enactment\xe2\x80\x9d of existing law); 98 Cong. Rec. 9323 (1952) (drafter of the Act stating that it was generally intended to \xe2\x80\x9ccodif[y] the present patent laws\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Now consider the existing law that the Patent Act\xe2\x80\x99s drafters intended the Act to reflect. The decisions that find or say or hold that laches can bar monetary relief in patent infringement actions stretch in a virtually unbroken chain from the late 19th century through the Patent Act\xe2\x80\x99s enactment in 1952. They number in the dozens and include every federal appeals court to have considered the matter. (We have found only two contrary decisions, both from the same District Court: Thorpe v. Wm. Courts of equity ordinarily applied laches \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98to claims of an equitable cast for which the Legislature ha[d] provided no fixed time limitation,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 5 (quoting Petrella v. Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer, Inc., 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 12)), not to requests for damages, \xe2\x80\x9ca quintessential legal remedy,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 3. Since \xe2\x80\x9claches is a gap-filling doctrine,\xe2\x80\x9d the fact that it was applied to equit-able claims without statutes of limitations says little about whether it should apply to legal damages claims when \xe2\x80\x9cthere is a statute of limitations,\xe2\x80\x9d and therefore \xe2\x80\x9cno gap to fill.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 5.

\n

Good reply. But no cigar. Why not? (1) Because in 1897 Congress enacted a statute of limitations\xe2\x80\x94very much like the one before us now\xe2\x80\x94for patent claims brought in courts of equity. Ch. 391, \xc2\xa76,\n\n29Stat.\n694 (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]n any suit or action .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. there shall be no recovery of profits or damages for any infringement committed more than six years before\xe2\x80\x9d filing). Thus, after 1897, there was no statute of limitations gap for equity courts to fill, and yet they continued to hold that laches applied. See, e.g., France Mfg., supra, at 609 (\xe2\x80\x9c[N]otwithstanding the statute of limitations, relief may be denied on the ground of laches .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. \xe2\x80\x9d); Dwight & Lloyd, supra, at 827 (Hand, J.) (explaining how laches operates in conjunction with the statute of limit-ations to allow an infringer to \xe2\x80\x9cgarne[r] the harvest of even the earliest of the 6 years to which recovery is in any event limited, with just confidence that he will not be disturbed\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

(2) Because in 1870 Congress enacted a statute that gave courts of equity the power to award legal relief, namely, damages, in patent cases. Act of July 8, 1870, \xc2\xa755,\n\n16Stat.\n206. Congress did not give law courts an equivalent power to grant injunctive relief in patent suits. As a result, from the late 19th century until the merger of law and equity in 1938, nearly all patent litigation\xe2\x80\x94including suits for damages\xe2\x80\x94took place in courts of equity that were applying laches in conjunction with a statute of limitations. See Lemley, Why Do Juries Decide If Patents Are Valid? 99 Va. L. Rev. 1673, 1704 (2013) (discussing the predominance of equity litigation).

\n

(3) Because Congress recognized that damages suits for patent infringement took place almost exclusively in equity courts, not law courts. Whenever Congress wished to modify patent damages law, it rewrote the statutory provisions governing damages in equity, not law. See, e.g., \xc2\xa78,\n\n42Stat.\n392 (modifying the equity damages statute to allow equity courts to award a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable sum\xe2\x80\x9d even if a patentee had difficulty proving actual damages, but making no change to the legal damages provision). The 1952 Congress, seeking to understand whether, or how, laches applied in patent damages cases, would almost certainly have looked to equity practice.

\n

(4) Because, in any event, in those few pre-law/equity-merger cases in which courts of law considered whether laches could bar a patent damages action, they, like their equity counterparts, held that it could. See Universal Coin, 104 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 781\xe2\x80\x93783; Banker, 69 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 666; Ford, supra, at 658. As the majority points out, these cases brought in law courts constitute \xe2\x80\x9conly a handful of decisions.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13. But that is simply because, as I just noted, almost all patent damages litigation took place in courts of equity. Regardless, before the merger of law and equity both law courts and equity courts recognized laches as a defense. And, after the merger of law and equity in 1938, federal courts still applied laches to patent damages claims. E.g., Brennan, 182 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 948 (holding that \xe2\x80\x9claches on the part of the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x9d can \xe2\x80\x9cbar his right to recover damages\xe2\x80\x9d). This, of course, would make no sense if laches for patent damages was really an equity-only rule.

\n

Does the majority have any other good reason to ignore the mountain of authority recognizing laches as a defense? It refers to many general statements in opinions and treatises that say that laches is \xe2\x80\x9cno defense at law.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Mack,\n\n295 U.\xc2\xa0S. 480,\n489 (1935)\n. But these statements are not about patent damages cases. They do not claim to encompass the problem at issue here. And they do not prevent Congress from enacting a statute that, recognizing patent litigation\xe2\x80\x99s history, combines a statute of limitations with a laches defense. And that is what Congress has done in the Patent Act.

\n

The majority also tries to discredit the persuasiveness of the pre-Patent Act case law authority. It goes through the lengthy list of decisions, finding some judicial statements too vague, others just dicta, and still others having confused an equitable claim for \xe2\x80\x9caccounting\xe2\x80\x9d with a legal claim for \xe2\x80\x9cdamages.\xe2\x80\x9d I agree that it has found weaknesses in the reasoning of some individual cases. But those weaknesses were not sufficient to prevent a 1951 treatise writer from concluding, on the basis of the great weight of authority, that in patent cases, \xe2\x80\x9c[l]aches .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. may be interposed in an action at law.\xe2\x80\x9d 3 A. Deller, Walker on Patents 106 (Cum. Supp. 1951).

\n

In any event, with all its efforts, the majority is unable to identify a single case\xe2\x80\x94not one\xe2\x80\x94from any court of appeals sitting in law or in equity before the merger, or sitting after the merger but before 1952, holding that laches could not bar a patent claim for damages. Furthermore, the majority concedes that it is unable to distinguish, by my count, at least six Court of Appeals cases directly holding that laches could bar a patent claim for damages. See Wolf, Sayer & Heller, 261 F. 195; Lurie, 209 F. 364; Universal Coin, supra; Banker, 69 F.\xc2\xa02d 665; Brennan, supra; Lukens, 197 F.\xc2\xa02d 939. And that is the case law situation that Congress faced when it wrote a statute that, as we have said, sought primarily to codify existing patent law. See supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x934.

\n

The majority tries to minimize the overall thrust of this case law by dividing the cases into subgroups and then concluding that the number of undistinguishable precedents in each subgroup is \xe2\x80\x9ctoo few to establish a settled, national consensus.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12. The problem with this approach is that, once we look at the body of case law as a whole, rather than in subgroups, we find what I have said and repeated, namely, that all the cases say the same thing: Laches applies. The majority\xe2\x80\x99s insistence on subdivision makes it sound a little like a Phillies fan who announces that a 9\xe2\x80\x930 loss to the Red Sox was a \xe2\x80\x9cclose one.\xe2\x80\x9d Why close? Because, says the fan, the Phillies lost each inning by only one run.

\n

For the sake of completeness I add that, since 1952, every Federal Court of Appeals to consider the question has held that laches remains available for damages claims brought under the Patent Act. See A. C. Aukerman Co. v. R. L. Chaides Constr. Co., 960 F. 2d 1020, 1030 (CA Fed. 1992) (en banc). Yet, Congress has repeatedly reenacted \n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7282\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cunenforceability\xe2\x80\x9d language without material change. See, e.g., \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa715(a), 20(g)(2)(B),\n\n125Stat.\n328,\n334. See also Texas Dept. of Housing and Community Affairs v. Inclusive Communities Project, Inc., 576 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2015) (slip op., at 14) (holding that congressional reenactment provides \xe2\x80\x9cconvincing support for the conclusion that Congress accepted and ratified the unanimous holdings of the Courts of Appeals\xe2\x80\x9d); Microsoft Corp. v. i4i Ltd. Partnership,\n\n564 U.\xc2\xa0S. 91\n\xe2\x80\x93114 (2011) (when Congress has \xe2\x80\x9coften amended \xc2\xa7282\xe2\x80\x9d while \xe2\x80\x9cle[aving] the Federal Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of \xc2\xa7282 in place,\xe2\x80\x9d any further \xe2\x80\x9crecalibration\xe2\x80\x9d should be left to the Legislature).

\n

III

\n

The majority\xe2\x80\x99s strongest argument is Petrella. There, the Court held that laches could not bar a damages claim brought within the Copyright Act\xe2\x80\x99s limitations period. The present case holds roughly the same in respect to the Patent Act, providing a degree of consistency.

\n

There are relevant differences, however, between patent law and copyright law. For one thing, copyright law, unlike patent law, does not contain a century and a half of history during which courts held that laches and a statute of limitations could coexist. When Congress enacted the Patent Act in 1952, patent statutes had already contained a 6-year statute of limitations for 55 years (since 1897), during which time courts had continued to apply laches to patent damages cases. Copyright law, on the other hand, contained no federal statute of limitations until 1957. See Petrella, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 3).

\n

For another thing, the Copyright Act, unlike the Patent Act, has express provisions that mitigate the unfairness of a copyright holder waiting for decades to bring his lawsuit. A copyright holder who tries to lie in wait to see if a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s investment will prove successful will discover that the Copyright Act allows that defendant to \xe2\x80\x9cprove and offset against .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. profits \xe2\x80\x98deductible expenses\xe2\x80\x99 incurred in generating those profits.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 12) (quoting\n\n17 U. S. C. \xc2\xa7504(b)). Thus, if the defendant invests say $50 million in a film, a copyright holder who waits until year 15 (when the film begins to earn a profit) to bring a lawsuit may be limited to recovering the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s profits less an apportioned amount of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s initial $50 million investment. But the Patent Act has no such deduction provision.

\n

Further, the Court, in Petrella, pointed out that the evidentiary loss that occurs while a copyright holder waits to bring suit is \xe2\x80\x9cat least as likely to affect plaintiffs as it is to disadvantage defendants.\xe2\x80\x9d 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 18). But that symmetry does not exist to the same degree in patent law. To win a copyright suit the copyright holder must show that the defendant copied his work. The death of witnesses and loss of documents from the time of the alleged infringement can therefore significantly impair the copyright holder\xe2\x80\x99s ability to prove his case. There is no such requirement in a patent suit. Patent infringement is a strict-liability offense: There need not be any copying, only an end product (or process) that invades the area the patentee has carved out in his patent.

\n

At the same time, the passage of time may well harm patent defendants who wish to show a patent invalid by raising defenses of anticipation, obviousness, or insufficiency. These kinds of defenses can depend upon contemporaneous evidence that may be lost over time, and they arise far more frequently in patent cases than any of their counterparts do in copyright cases. See Brief for Electronic Frontier Foundation et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 23 (reporting that of all copyright cases pending as of January 2009, only 2.7% of judgment events resulted in a finding of a lack of ownership or validity of the copyright at issue); Allison, Lemley, & Schwartz, Understanding the Realities of Modern Patent Litigation, 92 Texas L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1769, 1778, 1784\xe2\x80\x931785 (2014) (finding that 70% of summary judgment motions in patent cases filed in 2008\xe2\x80\x932009 related to anticipation or obviousness). The upshot is an absence here of the symmetrical effect of delay upon which the Court relied in Petrella.

\n

Finally, there is a \xe2\x80\x9clock-in\xe2\x80\x9d problem that is likely to be more serious where patents are at issue. Once a business chooses to rely on a particular technology, it can become expensive to switch, even if it would have been cheap to do so earlier. See Lee & Melamed, Breaking the Vicious Cycle of Patent Damages, 101 Cornell L. Rev. 385, 409\xe2\x80\x93410 (2016). As a result, a patentee has considerable incentive to delay suit until the costs of switching\xe2\x80\x94and accordingly the settlement value of a claim\xe2\x80\x94are high. The practical consequences of such delay can be significant, as the facts of this case illustrate: First Quality invested hundreds of millions of dollars in its allegedly infringing technologies during the years that SCA waited to bring its suit. App. to Pet. for Cert. 107a\xe2\x80\x93108a. And amici have provided numerous other examples that suggest this fact pattern is far from uncommon. See Brief for Dell et\xc2\xa0al. 11\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

I recognize the Majority\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion that the doctrine of \xe2\x80\x9cequitable estoppel\xe2\x80\x9d might help alleviate some of these problems. See ante, at 15. I certainly hope so. But I would be more \xe2\x80\x9ccautious before adopting changes that disrupt the settled expectations of the inventing commu-nity.\xe2\x80\x9d Festo Corp. v. Shoketsu Kinzoku Kogyo Kabushiki Co.,\n\n535 U.\xc2\xa0S. 722,\n739 (2002)\n.

\n

I add or confess that I believe that Petrella too was wrongly decided. Today\xe2\x80\x99s case helps illustrate why I think that Petrella started this Court down the wrong track. I would stop, finding adequate grounds to distinguish Petrella. But the majority remains \xe2\x80\x9cdetermined to stay the course and continue on, travelling even further away,\xe2\x80\x9d Mathis v. United States, 579 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2016) (Alito, J., dissenting) (slip op., at 9), from Congress\xe2\x80\x99 efforts, in the Patent Act, to promote the \xe2\x80\x9cProgress of Science and useful Arts,\xe2\x80\x9d U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Art. I, \xc2\xa78, cl.\xc2\xa08. Trite but true: Two wrongs don\xe2\x80\x99t make a right.

\n

With respect, I dissent.
