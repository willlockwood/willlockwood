\n\t\t\t\tFEDERAL COMMUNICATIONS COMMISSION et\xc2\xa0al. v. FOX TELEVISION STATIONS, INC., et\xc2\xa0al.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the second circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 10\xe2\x80\x931293.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 10, 2012\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 21, 2012\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tTitle \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71464 bans the broadcast of \xe2\x80\x9cany obscene, indecent, or profane language.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federal Communications Commission (Commission) began enforcing \xc2\xa71464 in the 1970\xe2\x80\x99s. In FCC v. Pacif-ica Foundation, \n\n438 U.\xc2\xa0S. 726\n, this Court found that the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s order banning George Carlin\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cFilthy Words\xe2\x80\x9d monologue passed \n \nFirst Amendment scrutiny, but did not decide whether \xe2\x80\x9can occasional expletive .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. would justify any sanction,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 750. In the ensuing years, the Commission went from strictly observing the narrow circumstances of Pacifica to indicating that it would assess the full context of allegedly indecent broadcasts rather than limit its regulation to an index of indecent words or pictures. However, it continued to note the important difference between isolated and repeated broadcasts of indecent material. And in a 2001 policy statement, it even included, as one of the factors significant to the determination of what was patently offensive, \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the material dwells on or repeats at length\xe2\x80\x9d the offending description or depiction.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt was against this regulatory background that the three incidents at issue took place. Two concern isolated utterances of obscene words during two live broadcasts aired by respondent Fox Television Stations, Inc. The third occurred during an episode of a television show broadcast by respondent ABC Television Network, when the nude buttocks of an adult female character were shown for approximately seven seconds and the side of her breast for a moment. After these incidents, but before the Commission issued Notices of Apparent Liability to Fox and ABC, the Commission issued its Golden Globes Order, declaring for the first time that fleeting expletives could be actionable. It then concluded that the Fox and ABC broadcasts violated this new standard. It found the Fox broadcasts indecent, but declined to propose forfeitures. The Second Circuit reversed, finding the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s decision to modify its indecency enforcement regime to regulate fleeting expletives arbitrary and capricious. This Court reversed and remanded for the Second Circuit to address respondents\xe2\x80\x99 \n \nFirst Amendment challenges. FCC v. Fox Television Stations, Inc., \n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 502\n. On remand, the Second Circuit found the policy unconstitutionally vague and invalidated it in its entirety. In the ABC case, the Commission found the display actionably indecent, and imposed a $27,500 forfeiture on each of the 45 ABC-affiliated stations that aired the episode. The Second Circuit vacated the order in light of its Fox decision.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0Because the Commission failed to give Fox or ABC fair notice prior to the broadcasts in question that fleeting expletives and momentary nudity could be found actionably indecent, the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s standards as applied to these broadcasts were vague. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9318.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0The fundamental principle that laws regulating persons or entities must give fair notice of what conduct is required or proscribed, see, e.g., Connally v. General Constr. Co., \n\n269 U.\xc2\xa0S. 385\n, is essential to the protections provided by the \n \nFifth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Due Process Clause, see United States v. Williams, \n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 285\n, which requires the invalidation of impermissibly vague laws. A conviction or punishment fails to comply with due process if the statute or regulation under which it is obtained \xe2\x80\x9cfails to provide a person of ordinary intelligence fair notice of what is prohibited, or is so standardless that it authorizes or encourages seriously discriminatory enforcement.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The void for vagueness doctrine addresses at least two connected but discrete due process concerns: Regulated parties should know what is required of them so they may act accordingly; and precision and guidance are necessary so that those enforcing the law do not act in an arbitrary or discriminatory way. When speech is involved, rigorous adherence to those requirements is necessary to ensure that ambiguity does not chill protected speech. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9312.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0These concerns are implicated here, where the broadcasters claim that the lengthy procedural history of their cases shows that they did not have fair notice of what was forbidden. Under the 2001 Guidelines in force when the broadcasts occurred, a key consideration was \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the material dwell[ed] on or repeat[ed] at length\xe2\x80\x9d the offending description or depiction, but in the 2004 Golden Globes Order, issued after the broadcasts, the Commission changed course and held that fleeting expletives could be a statutory violation. It then applied this new principle to these cases. Its lack of notice to Fox and ABC of its changed interpretation failed to give them \xe2\x80\x9cfair notice of what is prohibited.\xe2\x80\x9d Williams, supra, at 304. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9313.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0Neither of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s contrary arguments is persuasive. It claims that Fox cannot establish unconstitutional vagueness because the Commission declined to impose a forfeiture on Fox and said that it would not consider the indecent broadcast in renewing station licenses or in other contexts. But the Commission has the statutory power to take into account \xe2\x80\x9cany history of prior offenses\xe2\x80\x9d when setting a forfeiture penalty, \n\n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7503(b)(2)(E), and the due process protection against vague regulations \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not leave [regulated parties] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. at the mercy of noblesse oblige.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Stevens, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. The challenged orders could also have an adverse impact on Fox\xe2\x80\x99s reputation with audiences and advertisers alike. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government argues that ABC had notice that its broadcast would be considered indecent. But an isolated statement in a 1960 Commission decision declaring that televising nudes might be contrary to \xc2\xa71464 does not suffice for the fair notice required when the Government intends to impose over a $1 million fine for allegedly impermissible speech. Moreover, previous Commission decisions had declined to find isolated and brief moments of nudity actionably indecent. In light of these agency decisions, and the absence of any notice in the 2001 Guidance that seven seconds of nude buttocks would be found indecent, ABC lacked constitutionally sufficient notice prior to being sanctioned. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9317.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(d)\xc2\xa0It is necessary to make three observations about this decision\xe2\x80\x99s scope. First, because the Court resolves these cases on fair notice grounds under the Due Process Clause, it need not address the \n \nFirst Amendment implications of the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s indecency policy or reconsider Pacifica at this time. Second, because the Court rules that Fox and ABC lacked notice at the time of their broadcasts that their material could be found actionably indecent under then-existing policies, the Court need not address the constitutionality of the current indecency policy as expressed in the Golden Globes Order and subsequent adjudications. Third, this opinion leaves the Commission free to modify its current indecency policy in light of its determination of the public interest and applicable legal requirements and leaves courts free to review the current, or any modified, policy in light of its content and application. Pp.\xc2\xa017\xe2\x80\x9318.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Kennedy delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn FCC v. Fox Television Stations, Inc., \n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 502, \n529 (2009)\n (Fox I), the Court held that the Federal Communication Commission\xe2\x80\x99s decision to modify its indecency enforcement regime to regulate so-called fleeting expletives was neither arbitrary nor capricious. The Court then declined to address the constitutionality of the policy, however, because the United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit had yet to do so. On remand, the Court of Appeals found the policy was vague and, as a result, unconstitutional. 613 F.\xc2\xa03d 317 (2010). The case now returns to this Court for decision upon the constitutional question.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn Fox I, the Court described both the regulatory framework through which the Commission regulates broadcast indecency and the long procedural history of this case. The Court need not repeat all that history, but some preliminary discussion is necessary to understand the constitutional issue the case now presents.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTitle \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71464 provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hoever utters any obscene, indecent, or profane language by means of radio communication shall be fined .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. or imprisoned not more than two years, or both.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federal Communi-cations Commission (Commission) has been instructed by Congress to enforce \xc2\xa71464 between the hours of 6 a.m. and 10 p.m., see Public Telecommunications Act of 1992, \xc2\xa715(a), \n\n106Stat.\n954, note following \n\n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7303, p.\xc2\xa0113 (Broadcasting of Indecent Programming). And the Commission has applied its regulations to radio and television broadcasters alike, see Fox I, supra, at 505\xe2\x80\x93506; see also 47 CFR \xc2\xa773.3999 (2010) (Commission regulation prohibiting the broadcast of any obscene material or any indecent material between 6 a.m. and 10 p.m.). Although the Commission has had the authority to regulate indecent broadcasts under \xc2\xa71464 since 1948 (and its prede-cessor commission, the Federal Radio Commission, since 1927), it did not begin to enforce \xc2\xa71464 until the 1970\xe2\x80\x99s. See Campbell, Pacifica Reconsidered: Implications for the Current Controversy over Broadcast Indecency, 63 Fed. Com. L.\xc2\xa0J. 195, 198 (2010).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis Court first reviewed the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s indecency policy in FCC v. Pacifica Foundation, \n\n438 U.\xc2\xa0S. 726 (1978)\n. In Pacifica, the Commission determined that George Carlin\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cFilthy Words\xe2\x80\x9d monologue was indecent. It contained \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98language that describes, in terms patently offensive as measured by contemporary community standards for the broadcast medium, sexual or excretory activities and organs, at times of the day when there is a reasonable risk that children may be in the audience.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 732 (quoting 56 F.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0C. 2d 94, 98 (1975)). This Court upheld the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s ruling. The broadcaster\xe2\x80\x99s statutory challenge was rejected. The Court held the Commission was not engaged in impermissible censorship within the meaning of \n\n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7326 (1976 ed.), see 438 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 735\xe2\x80\x93739, and that \xc2\xa71464\xe2\x80\x99s definition of indecency was not confined to speech with an appeal to the prurient interest, see id., at 738\xe2\x80\x93741. Finding no \n \nFirst Amendment violation, the decision explained the constitutional standard under which regulations of broadcasters are assessed. It observed that \xe2\x80\x9cbroadcast media have established a uniquely pervasive presence in the lives of all Americans,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 748, and that \xe2\x80\x9cbroadcasting is uniquely accessible to children, even those too young to read,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 749. In light of these considerations, \xe2\x80\x9cbroadcasting .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. has received the most limited \n \nFirst Amendment protection.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 748. Under this standard the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s order passed constitutional scrutiny. The Court did note the narrowness of its holding, explaining that it was not deciding whether \xe2\x80\x9can occasional expletive .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. would justify any sanction.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 750; see also id., at 760\xe2\x80\x93761 (Powell, J., concur- ring in part and concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9c[C]ertainly the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. does not speak to cases involving the isolated use of a potentially offensive word in the course of a radio broadcast, as distinguished from the verbal shock treatment administered by respondent here\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFrom 1978 to 1987, the Commission did not go beyond the narrow circumstances of Pacifica and brought no indecency enforcement actions. See In\xc2\xa0re Infinity Broadcasting Corp., 3 FCC Rcd. 930 (1987); see also In\xc2\xa0re Application of WGBH Educ. Foundation, 69 F.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0C. 2d 1250, 1254 (1978) (Commission declaring it \xe2\x80\x9cintend[s] strictly to observe the narrowness of the Pacifica holding\xe2\x80\x9d). Recognizing that Pacifica provided \xe2\x80\x9cno general prerogative to intervene in any case where words similar or identical to those in Pacifica are broadcast over a licensed radio or television station,\xe2\x80\x9d the Commission distinguished between the \xe2\x80\x9crepetitive occurrence of the \xe2\x80\x98indecent\xe2\x80\x99 words\xe2\x80\x9d (such as in the Carlin monologue) and an \xe2\x80\x9cisolated\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9coccasional\xe2\x80\x9d expletive, that would not necessarily be actionable. 69 F.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0C. 2d, at 1254.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 1987, the Commission determined it was applying the Pacifica standard in too narrow a way. It stated that in later cases its definition of indecent language would \xe2\x80\x9cappropriately includ[e] a broader range of material than the seven specific words at issue in [the Carlin monologue].\xe2\x80\x9d In re Pacifica Foundation Inc., 2 FCC Rcd. 2698, 2699. Thus, the Commission indicated it would use the \xe2\x80\x9cgeneric definition of indecency\xe2\x80\x9d articulated in its 1975 Pacifica order, Infinity Order, 3 FCC Rcd., at 930, and assess the full context of allegedly indecent broadcasts rather than limiting its regulation to a \xe2\x80\x9ccomprehensive index .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of indecent words or pictorial depictions,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 932.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEven under this context based approach, the Commission continued to note the important difference between isolated and repeated broadcasts of indecent material. See ibid. (considering variables in determining whether material is patently offensive including \xe2\x80\x9cwhether allegedly offensive material is isolated or fleeting\xe2\x80\x9d). In the context of expletives, the Commission determined \xe2\x80\x9cdeliberate and repetitive use in a patently offensive manner is a requisite to a finding of indecency.\xe2\x80\x9d Pacifica Order, 2 FCC Rcd., at 2699. For speech \xe2\x80\x9cinvolving the description or depiction of sexual or excretory functions .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [t]he mere fact that specific words or phrases are not repeated does not mandate a finding that material that is otherwise patently offensive .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is not indecent.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 2001, the Commission issued a policy statement intended \xe2\x80\x9cto provide guidance to the broadcast industry regarding [its] caselaw interpreting \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71464 and [its] enforcement policies with respect to broadcast indecency.\xe2\x80\x9d In\xc2\xa0re Industry Guidance on Commission\xe2\x80\x99s Case Law Interpreting \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71464 and Enforcement Policies Regarding Broadcast Indecency, 16 FCC Rcd. 7999. In that document the Commission restated that for material to be indecent it must depict sexual or excretory organs or activities and be patently offensive as measured by contemporary community standards for the broadcast medium. Id., at 8002. Describing the framework of what it considered patently offensive, the Commission explained that three factors had proved significant:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c(1) [T]he explicitness or graphic nature of the description or depiction of sexual or excretory organs or activities; (2) whether the material dwells on or repeats at length descriptions of sexual or excretory organs or activities; (3) whether the material appears to pander or is used to titillate, or whether the material appears to have been presented for its shock value.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 8003 (emphasis deleted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tAs regards the second of these factors, the Commission explained that \xe2\x80\x9c[r]epetition of and persistent focus on sexual or excretory material have been cited consistently as factors that exacerbate the potential offensiveness of broadcasts. In contrast, where sexual or excretory references have been made once or have been passing or fleeting in nature, this characteristic has tended to weigh against a finding of indecency.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 8008. The Commission then gave examples of material that was not found indecent because it was fleeting and isolated, id., at 8008\xe2\x80\x938009 (citing, e.g., L.\xc2\xa0M. Communications of South Carolina, Inc. (WYBB(FM)), 7 FCC Rcd. 1595 (MMB 1992) (finding \xe2\x80\x9ca fleeting and isolated utterance\xe2\x80\x9d in the context of live and spontaneous programming not actionable)), and contrasted it with fleeting references that were found patently offensive in light of other factors, 16 FCC Rcd., at 8009 (citing, e.g., Tempe Radio, Inc. (KUPD\xe2\x80\x93FM), 12 FCC Rcd. 21828 (MMB 1997) (finding fleeting language that clearly refers to sexual activity with a child to be patently offensive)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt was against this regulatory background that the three incidents of alleged indecency at issue here took place. First, in the 2002 Billboard Music Awards, broadcast by respondent Fox Television Stations, Inc., the singer Cher exclaimed during an unscripted acceptance speech: \xe2\x80\x9cI\xe2\x80\x99ve also had my critics for the last 40 years saying that I was on my way out every year. Right. So f\xc2\xa0*** \xe2\x80\x98em.\xe2\x80\x9d 613 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 323. Second, Fox broadcast the Billboard Music Awards again in 2003. There, a person named Nicole Richie made the following unscripted remark while presenting an award: \xe2\x80\x9cHave you ever tried to get cow s*** out of a Prada purse? It\xe2\x80\x99s not so f\xc2\xa0***ing simple.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The third in-cident involved an episode of NYPD Blue, a regular tele-vision show broadcast by respondent ABC Television Network. The episode broadcast on February 25, 2003, showed the nude buttocks of an adult female character for approximately seven seconds and for a moment the side of her breast. During the scene, in which the character was preparing to take a shower, a child portraying her boy-friend\xe2\x80\x99s son entered the bathroom. A moment of awkwardness followed. 404 Fed. Appx. 530, 533\xe2\x80\x93534 (CA2 2011). The Commission received indecency complaints about all three broadcasts. See Fox I, 556 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 510; 404 Fed. Appx., at 534.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAfter these incidents, but before the Commission issued Notices of Apparent Liability to Fox and ABC, the Commission issued a decision sanctioning NBC for a comment made by the singer Bono during the 2003 Golden Globe Awards. Upon winning the award for Best Original Song, Bono exclaimed: \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98This is really, really, f\xc2\xa0***ing brilliant. Really, really great.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d In\xc2\xa0re Complaints Against Various Broadcast Licensees Regarding Their Airing of the \xe2\x80\x9cGolden Globe Awards\xe2\x80\x9d Program, 19 FCC Rcd. 4975, 4976, n.\xc2\xa04 (2004) (Golden Globes Order). Reversing a decision by its enforcement bureau, the Commission found the use of the F-word actionably indecent. Id., at 4975\xe2\x80\x934976. The Commission held that the word was \xe2\x80\x9cone of the most vul-gar, graphic and explicit descriptions of sexual activity in the English language,\xe2\x80\x9d and thus found \xe2\x80\x9cany use of that word or a variation, in any context, inherently has a sex-ual connotation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 4978\xe2\x80\x934979. Turning to the isolated nature of the expletive, the Commission reversed prior rulings that had found fleeting expletives not indecent. The Commission held \xe2\x80\x9cthe mere fact that specific words or phrases are not sustained or repeated does not mandate a finding that material that is otherwise patently offensive to the broadcast medium is not indecent.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 4980; see also id., at 4982 (\xe2\x80\x9cJust as the Court [in Pacifica] held that .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the George Carlin routine \xe2\x80\x98could have enlarged a child\xe2\x80\x99s vocabulary in an instant,\xe2\x80\x99 we believe that even isolated broadcasts of the \xe2\x80\x98F-Word\xe2\x80\x99 in situations such as that here could do so as well\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tC\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEven though the incidents at issue in these cases took place before the Golden Globes Order, the Commission applied its new policy regarding fleeting expletives and fleeting nudity. It found the broadcasts by respondents Fox and ABC to be in violation of this standard.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs to Fox, the Commission found the two Billboard Awards broadcasts indecent in In\xc2\xa0re Complaints Regarding Various Television Broadcasts Between February 2, 2002, and March 8, 2005, 21 FCC Rcd. 2664 (2006). Numerous parties petitioned for a review of the order in the United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit. The Court of Appeals granted the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s request for a voluntary remand so that it could respond to the parties\xe2\x80\x99 objections. Fox Television Stations, Inc. v. FCC, 489 F.\xc2\xa03d 444, 453 (2007). In its remand order, the Commission applied its tripartite definition of patently offensive material from its 2001 Order and found that both broadcasts fell well within its scope. See In\xc2\xa0re Complaints Regarding Various Television Broadcasts Between February 2, 2002, and March 8, 2005, 21 FCC Rcd. 13299 (2006) (Remand Order); see also Fox I, supra, at 511\xe2\x80\x93513 (discussing in detail the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s findings). As pertains to the constitutional issue in these cases, the Commission noted that under the policy clarified in the Golden Globes Order, \xe2\x80\x9ccategorically requiring repeated use of expletives in order to find material indecent is inconsistent with our general approach to indecency enforcement.\xe2\x80\x9d Remand Order, 21 FCC Rcd., at 13308; see also id., at 13325 (\xe2\x80\x9c[U]nder our Golden Globe precedent, the fact that Cher used the \xe2\x80\x98F-word\xe2\x80\x99 once does not remove her comment from the realm of actionable indecency\xe2\x80\x9d). Though the Commission deemed Fox should have known Nicole Richie\xe2\x80\x99s comments were actionably indecent even prior to the Golden Globes Order, 21 FCC Rcd., at 13307, it declined to propose a forfeiture in light of the limited nature of the Sec-ond Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s remand. Id., at 13321. The Commission acknowledged that \xe2\x80\x9cit was not apparent that Fox could be penalized for Cher\xe2\x80\x99s comment at the time it was broadcast.\xe2\x80\x9d And so, as in the Golden Globes case it imposed no penalty for that broadcast. Id., at 13324, 13326.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFox and various intervenors returned to the United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit, raising ad-ministrative, statutory, and constitutional challenges to the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s indecency regulations. See Fox Television Stations, Inc. v. FCC, 489 F.\xc2\xa03d 444. In a 2-to-1 decision, with Judge Leval dissenting, the Court of Appeals found the Remand Order arbitrary and capricious because \xe2\x80\x9cthe FCC has made a 180-degree turn regarding its treatment of \xe2\x80\x98fleeting expletives\xe2\x80\x99 without providing a reasoned explanation justifying the about-face.\xe2\x80\x9d 489 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 455. While noting its skepticism as to whether the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s fleeting expletive regime \xe2\x80\x9cwould pass constitutional muster,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court of Appeals found it unnecessary to ad-dress the issue. Id., at 462.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe case came here on certiorari. Citing the Administrative Procedure Act, \n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7551 et\xc2\xa0seq., this Court noted that the Judiciary may set aside agency action that is arbitrary or capricious. In the context of a change in policy (such as the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s determination that fleeting expletives could be indecent), the decision held an agency, in the ordinary course, should acknowledge that it is in fact changing its position and \xe2\x80\x9cshow that there are good reasons for the new policy.\xe2\x80\x9d Fox I, 553 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 515. There is no need, however, for an agency to provide detailed justifications for every change or to show that the reasons for the new policy are better than the reasons for the old one. Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJudged under this standard, the Court in Fox I found the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s new indecency enforcement policy neither arbitrary nor capricious. Id., at 517. The Court noted the Commission had acknowledged breaking new ground in ruling that fleeting and nonliteral expletives could be indecent under the controlling standards; the Court concluded the agency\xe2\x80\x99s reasons for expanding the scope of its enforcement activity were rational. Ibid. Not only was it \xe2\x80\x9ccertainly reasonable to determine that it made no sense to distinguish between literal and nonliteral uses of offensive words,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., but the Court agreed that the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s decision to \xe2\x80\x9clook at the patent offensiveness of even isolated uses of sexual and excretory words fits with the context-based approach [approved] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in Pacifica.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Given that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ven isolated utterances can .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. constitute harmful \xe2\x80\x98first blow[s]\xe2\x80\x99 to children,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court held that the Commission could \xe2\x80\x9cdecide it needed to step away from its old regime where nonrepetitive use of an expletive was per\xc2\xa0se nonactionable.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 518. Having found the agency\xe2\x80\x99s action to be neither arbitrary nor capricious, the Court remanded for the Court of Appeals to address respondents\xe2\x80\x99 \n \nFirst Amendment challenges. Id., at 529\xe2\x80\x93530.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOn remand from Fox I, the Court of Appeals held the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s indecency policy unconstitutionally vague and invalidated it in its entirety. 613 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 327. The Court of Appeals found the policy, as expressed in the 2001 Guidance and subsequent Commission decisions, failed to give broadcasters sufficient notice of what would be considered indecent. Surveying a number of Commission adjudications, the court found the Commission was inconsistent as to which words it deemed patently offensive. See id., at 330. It also determined that the Com-mission\xe2\x80\x99s presumptive prohibition on the F-word and the S-word was plagued by vagueness because the Commission had on occasion found the fleeting use of those words not indecent provided they occurred during a bona fide news interview or were \xe2\x80\x9cdemonstrably essential to the nature of an artistic or educational work.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 331 (internal quotation marks omitted). The Commission\xe2\x80\x99s application of these exceptions, according to the Court of Appeals, left broadcasters guessing whether an expletive would be deemed artistically integral to a program or whether a particular broadcast would be considered a bona fide news interview. The Court of Appeals found the vagueness in-herent in the policy had forced broadcasters to \xe2\x80\x9cchoose between not airing .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. controversial programs [or] risking massive fines or possibly even loss of their licenses.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 334. And the court found that there was \xe2\x80\x9cample evidence in the record\xe2\x80\x9d that this harsh choice had led to a chill of protected speech. Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe procedural history regarding ABC is more brief. On February 19, 2008, the Commission issued a forfeiture order finding the display of the woman\xe2\x80\x99s nude buttocks in NYPD Blue was actionably indecent. See In\xc2\xa0re Complaints Against Various Television Licensees Concerning Their February 24, 2003 Broadcast of the Program \xe2\x80\x9cNYPD Blue\xe2\x80\x9d, 23 FCC Rcd. 3147 (2008). The Commission determined that, regardless of medical definitions, displays of buttocks fell within the category of displays of sexual or excretory organs because the depiction was \xe2\x80\x9cwidely associated with sexual arousal and closely associated by most people with excretory activities.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 3150. The scene was deemed patently offensive as measured by contemporary community standards, ibid.; and the Commission determined that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he female actor\xe2\x80\x99s nudity is presented in a manner that clearly panders to and titillates the audience,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 3153. Unlike in the Fox case, the Commission imposed a forfeiture of $27,500 on each of the 45 ABC-affiliated stations that aired the indecent episode. In a summary order the United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit vacated the forfeiture order, determining that it was bound by its Fox decision striking down the entirety of the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s indecency policy. See 404 Fed. Appx., at 533.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government sought review of both judgments, see Brief for Petitioners 1, and this Court granted certiorari, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ____ (2011). These are the cases before us.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA fundamental principle in our legal system is that laws which regulate persons or entities must give fair notice of conduct that is forbidden or required. See Connally v. General Constr. Co., \n\n269 U.\xc2\xa0S. 385, \n391 (1926)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] statute which either forbids or requires the doing of an act in terms so vague that men of common intelligence must necessarily guess at its meaning and differ as to its application, violates the first essential of due process of law\xe2\x80\x9d); Papachristou v. Jacksonville, \n\n405 U.\xc2\xa0S. 156, \n162 (1972)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cLiving under a rule of law entails various suppositions, one of which is that \xe2\x80\x98[all persons] are entitled to be informed as to what the State commands or forbids\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Lanzetta v. New Jersey, \n\n306 U.\xc2\xa0S. 451, \n453 (1939)\n (alteration in original))). This requirement of clarity in regulation is essential to the protections provided by the Due Process Clause of the \n \nFifth Amendment. See United States v. Williams, \n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 285, \n304 (2008)\n. It requires the invalidation of laws that are impermissibly vague. A conviction or punishment fails to comply with due process if the statute or regulation under which it is obtained \xe2\x80\x9cfails to provide a person of ordinary intelligence fair notice of what is prohibited, or is so standardless that it authorizes or encourages seriously discriminatory enforcement.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. As this Court has explained, a regulation is not vague because it may at times be difficult to prove an incriminating fact but rather because it is unclear as to what fact must be proved. See id., at 306.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEven when speech is not at issue, the void for vagueness doctrine addresses at least two connected but discrete due process concerns: first, that regulated parties should know what is required of them so they may act accordingly; second, precision and guidance are necessary so that those enforcing the law do not act in an arbitrary or discriminatory way. See Grayned v. City of Rockford, \n\n408 U.\xc2\xa0S. 104\n\xe2\x80\x93109 (1972). When speech is involved, rigorous adherence to those requirements is necessary to ensure that ambiguity does not chill protected speech.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThese concerns are implicated here because, at the out-set, the broadcasters claim they did not have, and do not have, sufficient notice of what is proscribed. And leaving aside any concerns about facial invalidity, they contend that the lengthy procedural history set forth above shows that the broadcasters did not have fair notice of what was forbidden. Under the 2001 Guidelines in force when the broadcasts occurred, a key consideration was \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98whether the material dwell[ed] on or repeat[ed] at length\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the offending description or depiction. 613 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 322. In the 2004 Golden Globes Order, issued after the broadcasts, the Commission changed course and held that fleeting expletives could be a statutory violation. Fox I, 556 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 512. In the challenged orders now under review the Commission applied the new principle promulgated in the Golden Globes Order and determined fleeting expletives and a brief moment of indecency were action-ably indecent. This regulatory history, however, makes it apparent that the Commission policy in place at the time of the broadcasts gave no notice to Fox or ABC that a fleeting expletive or a brief shot of nudity could be actionably indecent; yet Fox and ABC were found to be in violation. The Commission\xe2\x80\x99s lack of notice to Fox and ABC that its interpretation had changed so the fleeting moments of indecency contained in their broadcasts were a violation of \xc2\xa71464 as interpreted and enforced by the agency \xe2\x80\x9cfail[ed] to provide a person of ordinary intelligence fair notice of what is prohibited.\xe2\x80\x9d Williams, supra, at 304. This would be true with respect to a regulatory change this abrupt on any subject, but it is surely the case when applied to the regulations in question, regulations that touch upon \xe2\x80\x9csensitive areas of basic \n \nFirst Amendment freedoms,\xe2\x80\x9d Baggett v. Bullitt, \n\n377 U.\xc2\xa0S. 360, \n372 (1964)\n; see also Reno v. American Civil Liberties Union, \n\n521 U.\xc2\xa0S. 844\n\xe2\x80\x93871 (1997) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe vagueness of [a content-based regulation of speech] raises special \n \nFirst Amendment concerns because of its ob-vious chilling effect\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government raises two arguments in response, but neither is persuasive. As for the two fleeting expletives, the Government concedes that \xe2\x80\x9cFox did not have reason-able notice at the time of the broadcasts that the Com-mission would consider non-repeated expletives indecent.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 28, n.\xc2\xa03. The Government argues, nonetheless, that Fox \xe2\x80\x9ccannot establish unconstitutional vagueness on that basis .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. because the Commission did not impose a sanction where Fox lacked such notice.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. As the Court observed when the case was here three Terms ago, it is true that the Commission declined to impose any forfeiture on Fox, see 556 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 513, and in its order the Commission claimed that it would not con-sider the indecent broadcasts either when considering whether to renew stations\xe2\x80\x99 licenses or \xe2\x80\x9cin any other context,\xe2\x80\x9d 21 FCC Rcd., at 13321, 13326. This \xe2\x80\x9cpolicy of forbearance,\xe2\x80\x9d as the Government calls it, does not suffice to make the issue moot. Brief for Petitioners 31. Though the Commission claims it will not consider the prior indecent broadcasts \xe2\x80\x9cin any context,\xe2\x80\x9d it has the statutory power to take into account \xe2\x80\x9cany history of prior offenses\xe2\x80\x9d when setting the level of a forfeiture penalty. See \n\n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7503(b)(2)(E). Just as in the \n \nFirst Amendment context, the due process protection against vague regulations \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not leave [regulated parties] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. at the mercy of noblesse oblige.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Stevens, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 18). Given that the Commission found it was \xe2\x80\x9cnot inequitable to hold Fox responsible for [the 2003 broadcast],\xe2\x80\x9d 21 FCC Rcd., at 13314, and that it has the statutory authority to use its finding to increase any future penalties, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s assurance it will elect not to do so is insufficient to remedy the constitutional violation.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn addition, when combined with the legal consequence described above, reputational injury provides further rea-son for granting relief to Fox. Cf. Paul v. Davis, \n\n424 U.\xc2\xa0S. 693\n\xe2\x80\x93709 (1976) (explaining that an \xe2\x80\x9calteration of legal status .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. combined with the injury resulting from the defamation\xe2\x80\x9d justifies the invocation of procedural safeguards). As respondent CBS points out, findings of wrongdoing can result in harm to a broadcaster\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9creputation with viewers and advertisers.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent CBS Television Network Affiliates Assn. et\xc2\xa0al. 17. This observation is hardly surprising given that the challenged orders, which are contained in the permanent Commission record, describe in strongly disapproving terms the indecent material broadcast by Fox, see, e.g., 21 FCC Rcd., at 13310\xe2\x80\x9313311, \xc2\xb630 (noting the \xe2\x80\x9cexplicit, graphic, vulgar, and shocking nature of Ms. Richie\xe2\x80\x99s comments\xe2\x80\x9d), and Fox\xe2\x80\x99s efforts to protect children from being exposed to it, see id., at 13311, \xc2\xb633 (finding Fox had failed to exercise \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98rea-sonable judgment, responsibility, and sensitivity to the public\xe2\x80\x99s needs and tastes to avoid [a] patently offensive broadcas[t]\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). Commission sanctions on broadcasters for indecent material are widely publicized. See, e.g., F.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0C. Fines Fox, N.\xc2\xa0Y. Times, Feb.\xc2\xa026, 2008, p.\xc2\xa0E2; F.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0C. Plans Record Fine for CBS, Washington Post, Sept. 24, 2004, p. E1. The challenged orders could have an adverse impact on Fox\xe2\x80\x99s reputation that audiences and advertisers alike are entitled to take into account.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWith respect to ABC, the Government with good reason does not argue no sanction was imposed. The fine against ABC and its network affiliates for the seven seconds of nudity was nearly $1.24 million. See Brief for Respondent ABC, Inc., et\xc2\xa0al. 7 (hereinafter ABC Brief). The Government argues instead that ABC had notice that the scene in NYPD Blue would be considered indecent in light of a 1960 decision where the Commission declared that the \xe2\x80\x9ctelevising of nudes might well raise a serious question of programming contrary to \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71464.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 32 (quoting Enbanc Programming Inquiry, 44 FCC 2303, 2307 (internal quotation marks omitted)). This argument does not prevail. An isolated and ambiguous statement from a 1960 Commission decision does not suffice for the fair notice required when the Government intends to impose over a $1 million fine for allegedly impermissible speech. The Commission, furthermore, had released decisions before sanctioning ABC that declined to find isolated and brief moments of nudity actionably indecent. See, e.g., In\xc2\xa0re Application of WGBH, 69 F.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0C. 2d, at 1251, 1255 (declining to find broadcasts contain- ing nudity to be indecent and emphasizing the difference between repeated and isolated expletives); In re WPBN/ WTOM License Subsidiary, Inc., 15 FCC Rcd. 1838, 1840 (2000) (finding full frontal nudity in Schind- ler\xe2\x80\x99s List not indecent). This is not to say, of course, that a graphic scene from Schindler\xe2\x80\x99s List involving nude concentration camp prisoners is the same as the shower scene from NYPD Blue. It does show, however, that the Government can point to nothing that would have given ABC affirmative notice that its broadcast would be considered actionably indecent. It is likewise not sufficient for the Commission to assert, as it did in its order, that though \xe2\x80\x9cthe depiction [of nudity] here is not as lengthy or repeated\xe2\x80\x9d as in some cases, the shower scene nonetheless \xe2\x80\x9cdoes contain more shots or lengthier depictions of nudity\xe2\x80\x9d than in other broadcasts found not indecent. 23 FCC Rcd., at 3153. This broad language fails to demonstrate that ABC had fair notice that its broadcast could be found indecent. In fact, a Commission ruling prior to the airing of the NYPD Blue episode had deemed 30 seconds of nude buttocks \xe2\x80\x9cvery brief\xe2\x80\x9d and not actionably indecent in the context of the broadcast. See Letter from Norman Goldstein to David Molina, FCC File No. 97110028 (May 26, 1999), in App. to Brief for Respondent ABC Television Affiliates Assn. et\xc2\xa0al. 1a; see also Letter from Edythe Wise to Susan Cavin, FCC File No. 91100738 (Aug. 13, 1992), id., at 18a, 19a. In light of this record of agency decisions, and the absence of any notice in the 2001 Guidance that seven seconds of nude buttocks would be found indecent, ABC lacked constitutionally sufficient notice prior to being sanctioned.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Commission failed to give Fox or ABC fair notice prior to the broadcasts in question that fleeting expletives and momentary nudity could be found actionably indecent. Therefore, the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s standards as applied to these broadcasts were vague, and the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s orders must be set aside.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is necessary to make three observations about the scope of this decision. First, because the Court resolves these cases on fair notice grounds under the Due Process Clause, it need not address the \n \nFirst Amendment implications of the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s indecency policy. It is argued that this Court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling in Pacifica (and the less rigorous standard of scrutiny it provided for the regulation of broadcasters, see \n\n438 U.\xc2\xa0S. 726\n) should be overruled because the rationale of that case has been overtaken by technological change and the wide availability of multiple other choices for listeners and viewers. See, e.g., ABC Brief 48\xe2\x80\x9357; Brief for Respondent Fox Television Stations, Inc., et\xc2\xa0al. 15\xe2\x80\x9326. The Government for its part maintains that when it licenses a conventional broadcast spectrum, the public may assume that the Government has its own interest in setting certain standards. See Brief for Petitioners 40\xe2\x80\x9353. These arguments need not be addressed here. In light of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding that the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s policy failed to provide fair notice it is unnecessary to reconsider Pacifica at this time. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis leads to a second observation. Here, the Court rules that Fox and ABC lacked notice at the time of their broadcasts that the material they were broadcasting could be found actionably indecent under then-existing policies. Given this disposition, it is unnecessary for the Court to address the constitutionality of the current indecency policy as expressed in the Golden Globes Order and sub-sequent adjudications. The Court adheres to its normal practice of declining to decide cases not before it. See, e.g., Sweatt v. Painter, \n\n339 U.\xc2\xa0S. 629, \n631 (1950)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cBroader issues have been urged for our consideration, but we adhere to the principle of deciding constitutional ques- tions only in the context of the particular case before the Court\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThird, this opinion leaves the Commission free to modify its current indecency policy in light of its determination of the public interest and applicable legal requirements. And it leaves the courts free to review the current policy or any modified policy in light of its content and application.\n\t\t\t
