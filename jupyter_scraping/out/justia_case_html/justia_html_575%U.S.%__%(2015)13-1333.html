COLEMAN, aka COLEMAN-BEY v. TOLLEFSON et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

No. 13\xe2\x80\x931333.\xe2\x80\x83Argued February 23, 2015\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 18, 2015[1]

\n

Ordinarily, a federal litigant who is too poor to pay court fees may proceed in forma pauperis. This means that the litigant may commence a civil action without prepaying fees or paying certain expenses. See\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71915(a). But a special \xe2\x80\x9cthree strikes\xe2\x80\x9d provision prevents a court from affording in forma pauperis status to a prisoner who \xe2\x80\x9chas, on 3 or more prior occasions, while incarcerated .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. , brought an action or appeal in a court of the United States that was dismissed on the grounds that it is frivolous, malicious, or fails to state a claim upon which relief may be granted.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71915(g).

\n

Petitioner Coleman, a state prisoner, filed three federal lawsuits that were dismissed on grounds enumerated in \xc2\xa71915(g). While the third dismissal was pending on appeal, he filed four additional federal lawsuits, moving to proceed in forma pauperis in each. The District Court refused to permit him to proceed in forma pauperis in any of those lawsuits, holding that a prior dismissal is a strike under \xc2\xa71915(g) even if it is pending on appeal. The Sixth Circuit agreed with the District Court.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0A prior dismissal on one of \xc2\xa71915(g)\xe2\x80\x99s statutorily enumerated grounds counts as a strike, even if the dismissal is the subject of an ongoing appeal. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0Coleman suggests that that a dismissal should count as a strike only once appellate review is complete. But the word \xe2\x80\x9cdismissed\xe2\x80\x9d does not normally include subsequent appellate activity. See, e.g., Heintz v. Jenkins,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 291\n. And \xc2\xa71915 itself describes dismissal as an action taken by a single court, not as a sequence of events involving multiple courts. See \xc2\xa71915(e). Coleman further contends that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cprior occasions\xe2\x80\x9d creates ambiguity. But nothing about that phrase transforms a dismissal into a dismissal-plus-appellate-review. In the context of \xc2\xa71915(g), a \xe2\x80\x9cprior occasion\xe2\x80\x9d merely means a previous instance in which a \xe2\x80\x9cprisoner has .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. brought an action or appeal .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that was dismissed on\xe2\x80\x9d statutorily enumerated grounds.

\n

A literal reading of the \xe2\x80\x9cthree strikes\xe2\x80\x9d provision is consistent with the statute\xe2\x80\x99s treatment of the trial and appellate states of litigation as distinct. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71915(a)(2), (a)(3), (b)(1), (e)(2), (g). It is also supported by the way in which the law ordinarily treats trial court judgments, i.e., a judgment normally takes effect despite a pending appeal, see Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 62; Fed. Rule App. Proc. 8(a), and its preclusive effect is generally immediate, notwithstanding any appeal, see Clay v. United States,\n\n537 U.\xc2\xa0S. 522\n.

\n

Finally, the statute\xe2\x80\x99s purpose favors this Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation. The \xe2\x80\x9cthree strikes\xe2\x80\x9d provision was \xe2\x80\x9cdesigned to filter out the bad claims and facilitate consideration of the good,\xe2\x80\x9d Jones v. Bock,\n\n549 U.\xc2\xa0S. 199\n. To refuse to count a prior dismissal because of a pending appeal would produce a leaky filter, because a prisoner could file many new lawsuits before reaching the end of the often lengthy appellate process. By contrast, the Court perceives no great risk that an erroneous trial court dismissal might wrongly deprive a prisoner of in forma pauperis status in a subsequent lawsuit. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0Coleman also argues that if the dismissal of a third complaint counts as a third strike, a litigant will lose the ability to appeal in forma pauperis from that strike itself. He believes this is a result that Congress could not possibly have intended. Because Coleman is not appealing from a third-strike trial-court dismissal here, the Court declines to address that question. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

733 F.\xc2\xa03d 175, affirmed.

Breyer, J., delivered the opinion for a unanimous Court.

ANDRE LEE COLEMAN, aka ANDRE LEE COLEMAN-BEY, PETITIONER v. TODD TOLLEFSON, et\xc2\xa0al.

ANDRE LEE COLEMAN, aka ANDRE LEE COLEMAN-BEY, PETITIONER v. BERTINA BOWERMAN, et\xc2\xa0al.

ANDRE LEE COLEMAN, aka ANDRE LEE COLEMAN-BEY, PETITIONER v. AARON J. VROMAN, et\xc2\xa0al.

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

[May 18, 2015]

\n

Justice Breyer delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

Ordinarily, a federal litigant who is too poor to pay court fees may proceed in forma pauperis. This means that the litigant may commence a civil action without prepaying fees or paying certain expenses. See\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71915. But a special \xe2\x80\x9cthree strikes\xe2\x80\x9d provision prevents a court from affording in forma pauperis status where the litigant is a prisoner and he or she \xe2\x80\x9chas, on 3 or more prior occasions, while incarcerated\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0, brought an action or appeal in a court of the United States that was dismissed on the grounds that it is frivolous, malicious, or fails to state a claim upon which relief may be granted.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71915(g).

\n

Prior to this litigation, a Federal District Court had dismissed on those grounds three actions brought by a state prisoner. While the third dismissal was pending on appeal, the prisoner sought to bring several additional actions in the federal courts. The question before us is whether the prisoner may litigate his new actions in forma pauperis. Where an appeals court has not yet decided whether a prior dismissal is legally proper, should courts count, or should they ignore, that dismissal when calcu-lating how many qualifying dismissals the litigant has suffered?

\n

We conclude that the courts must count the dismissal even though it remains pending on appeal. The litigant here has accumulated three prior dismissals on statutorily enumerated grounds. Consequently, a court may not afford him in forma pauperis status with respect to his additional civil actions.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

Congress first enacted an in forma pauperis statute in 1892. See Act of July 20, ch. 209,\n\n27Stat.\n252. Congress recognized that \xe2\x80\x9cno citizen sh[ould] be denied an opportunity to commence, prosecute, or defend an action, civil or criminal, in any court of the United States, solely because his poverty makes it impossible for him to pay or secure the costs.\xe2\x80\x9d Adkins v. E.\xc2\xa0I. DuPont de Nemours & Co.,\n\n335 U.\xc2\xa0S. 331,\n342 (1948)\n (internal quotation marks omitted). It therefore permitted a citizen to \xe2\x80\x9ccommence and prosecute to conclusion any such .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. action without being required to prepay fees or costs, or give security therefor before or after bringing suit.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71,\n\n27Stat.\n252. The current statute permits an individual to litigate a federal action in forma pauperis if the individual files an affidavit stating, among other things, that he or she is unable to prepay fees \xe2\x80\x9cor give security therefor.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71915(a)(1).

\n

Even in 1892, \xe2\x80\x9cCongress recognized .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that a litigant whose filing fees and court costs are assumed by the public, unlike a paying litigant, lacks an economic incentive to refrain from filing frivolous, malicious, or repetitive lawsuits.\xe2\x80\x9d Neitzke v. Williams,\n\n490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 319,\n324 (1989)\n. And as the years passed, Congress came to see that prisoner suits in particular represented a disproportionate share of federal filings. Jones v. Bock,\n\n549 U.\xc2\xa0S. 199\n\xe2\x80\x93203 (2007). It responded by \xe2\x80\x9cenact[ing] a variety of reforms designed to filter out the bad claims [filed by prisoners] and facilitate consideration of the good.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 204. Among those reforms was the \xe2\x80\x9cthree strikes\xe2\x80\x9d rule here at issue. The rule, which applies to in forma pauperis status, reads in its entirety as follows:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIn no event shall a prisoner bring a civil action or appeal a judgment in a civil action or proceeding [in forma pauperis] if the prisoner has, on 3 or more prior occasions, while incarcerated or detained in any facil-ity, brought an action or appeal in a court of the United States that was dismissed on the grounds that it is frivolous, malicious, or fails to state a claim upon which relief may be granted, unless the prisoner is under imminent danger of serious physical injury.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71915(g).

\n

B

\n

The petitioner, Andr\xc3\xa9 Lee Coleman, is incarcerated at the Baraga Correctional Facility in Michigan. By 2010, three federal lawsuits filed by Coleman during his incarceration had been dismissed as frivolous (or on other grounds enumerated in \xc2\xa71915(g)). Nonetheless, when Coleman filed four new federal lawsuits between April 2010 and January 2011, he moved to proceed in forma pauperis in each. He denied that his third dismissed lawsuit counted as a strike under \xc2\xa71915(g). That is because he had appealed the dismissal, and the appeals court had not yet ruled. Thus, in Coleman\xe2\x80\x99s view, he had fewer than three qualifying dismissals, and was eligible for in forma pauperis status under the statute.

\n

The District Court rejected Coleman\xe2\x80\x99s argument. It held that \xe2\x80\x9ca dismissal counts as a strike even if it is pending on appeal at the time that the plaintiff files his new action.\xe2\x80\x9d No. 10\xe2\x80\x93cv\xe2\x80\x93337 (WD Mich., Apr. 12, 2011), App. to Pet. for Cert. 21a, 24a. It thus refused to permit Coleman to proceed in forma pauperis in any of his four suits.

\n

On appeal, a divided panel of the Sixth Circuit agreed with the District Court. 733 F.\xc2\xa03d 175 (2013). It resolved the four cases using slightly different procedures. In one of the four cases, the Sixth Circuit affirmed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment. In the remaining three cases, it denied Coleman\xe2\x80\x99s request to proceed in forma pauperis on appeal. It subsequently dismissed the three cases for want of prosecution after Coleman failed to pay the appellate filing fees.

\n

In contrast to the Sixth Circuit, the vast majority of the other Courts of Appeals have held that a prior dismissal on a statutorily enumerated ground does not count as a strike while an appeal of that dismissal remains pending. See Henslee v. Keller, 681 F.\xc2\xa03d 538, 541 (CA4 2012) (listing, and joining, courts that have adopted the majority view). In light of the division of opinion among the Circuits, we granted Coleman\xe2\x80\x99s petition for certiorari.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

In our view, the Sixth Circuit majority correctly applied \xc2\xa71915(g). A prior dismissal on a statutorily enumerated ground counts as a strike even if the dismissal is the subject of an appeal. That, after all, is what the statute literally says. The \xe2\x80\x9cthree strikes\xe2\x80\x9d provision applies where a prisoner \xe2\x80\x9chas, on 3 or more prior occasions . . . brought an action or appeal . . . that was dismissed on\xe2\x80\x9d certain grounds. \xc2\xa71915(g) (emphasis added). Coleman believes that we should read the statute as if it referred to an \xe2\x80\x9caffirmed dismissal,\xe2\x80\x9d as if it considered a trial court dismissal to be provisional, or as if it meant that a dismissal falls within the statute\xe2\x80\x99s scope only when the litigant has no further chance to secure a reversal. But the statute itself says none of these things.

\n

Instead, the statute refers to whether an action or appeal \xe2\x80\x9cwas dismissed.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71915(g). The linguistic term \xe2\x80\x9cdismiss,\xe2\x80\x9d taken alone, does not normally include subsequent appellate activity. See, e.g., Heintz v. Jenkins,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 291,\n294 (1995)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he District Court dismissed [the] lawsuit for failure to state a claim.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. However, the Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit reversed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment\xe2\x80\x9d); Gray v. Netherland,\n\n518 U.\xc2\xa0S. 152,\n158 (1996)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Suffolk Circuit Court dismissed petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s state petition for a writ of habeas corpus. The Virginia Supreme Court affirmed the dismissal\xe2\x80\x9d). Indeed, \xc2\xa71915 itself describes dismissal as an action taken by a single court, not as a sequence of events involving multiple courts. See \xc2\xa71915(e)(2) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he court shall dismiss the case at any time if the court determines that\xe2\x80\x94(A) the allegation of poverty is untrue; or (B) the action or appeal\xe2\x80\x94(i) is frivolous or malicious; [or] (ii) fails to state a claim on which relief may be granted\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)).

\n

Coleman insists that \xc2\xa71915(g) is not so clear. Even if the term \xe2\x80\x9cdismissed\xe2\x80\x9d is unambiguous, contends Coleman, the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cprior occasions\xe2\x80\x9d creates ambiguity. Coleman observes that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98may refer to a single moment or to a continuing event: to an appeal, independent of the underlying action, or to the continuing claim, inclusive of both the action and its appeal.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 17 (quoting Henslee, supra, at 542). Coleman believes that a \xe2\x80\x9cprior occasion\xe2\x80\x9d in the context of \xc2\xa71915(g) may therefore include both a dismissal on an enumerated ground and any subsequent appeal.

\n

We find it difficult to agree. Linguistically speaking, we see nothing about the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cprior occasions\xe2\x80\x9d that would transform a dismissal into a dismissal-plus-appellate-review. An \xe2\x80\x9coccasion\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9ca particular occurrence,\xe2\x80\x9d a \xe2\x80\x9chappening,\xe2\x80\x9d or an \xe2\x80\x9cincident.\xe2\x80\x9d Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third New International Dictionary 1560 (3d ed. 1993). And the statute provides the content of that occurrence, happening, or incident: It is an instance in which a \xe2\x80\x9cprisoner has\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. brought an action or appeal in a court of the United States that was dismissed on\xe2\x80\x9d statutorily enumerated grounds. \xc2\xa71915(g). Under the plain language of the statute, when Coleman filed the suits at issue here, he had already experienced three such \xe2\x80\x9cprior occasions.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Our literal reading of the phrases \xe2\x80\x9cprior occasions\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cwas dismissed\xe2\x80\x9d is consistent with the statute\xe2\x80\x99s discussion of actions and appeals. The in forma pauperis statute repeatedly treats the trial and appellate stages of litigation as distinct. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71915(a)(2), (a)(3), (b)(1), (e)(2), (g). Related provisions reflect a congressional focus upon trial court dismissal as an important separate element of the statutory scheme. See \xc2\xa71915A (requiring a district court to screen certain prisoner complaints \xe2\x80\x9cas soon as practicable\xe2\x80\x9d and to dismiss any portion of the complaint that \xe2\x80\x9cis frivolous, malicious, or fails to state a claim upon which relief may be granted\xe2\x80\x9d);\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71997e(c)(1) (similar). We have found nothing in these provisions indicating that Congress considered a trial court dismissal and an appellate court decision as if they were a single entity\xe2\x80\x94or that Congress intended the former to take effect only when affirmed by the latter.

\n

Our literal reading of the \xe2\x80\x9cthree strikes\xe2\x80\x9d provision also is supported by the way in which the law ordinarily treats trial court judgments. Unless a court issues a stay, a trial court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment (say, dismissing a case) normally takes effect despite a pending appeal. See Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 62; Fed. Rule App. Proc. 8(a). And a judgment\xe2\x80\x99s preclusive effect is generally immediate, notwithstanding any appeal. See Clay v. United States,\n\n537 U.\xc2\xa0S. 522,\n527 (2003)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cTypically, a federal judgment becomes final for . . . claim preclusion purposes when the district court disassociates itself from the case, leaving nothing to be done at the court of first instance save execution of the judgment\xe2\x80\x9d). The ordinary rules of civil procedure thus provide additional support for our interpretation of the statute. See Jones, 549 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 211\xe2\x80\x93216 (applying the ordinary rules of civil procedure where the procedural requirements for prison litigation do not call for an alternative).

\n

Finally, the statute\xe2\x80\x99s purpose favors our interpretation. The \xe2\x80\x9cthree strikes\xe2\x80\x9d provision was \xe2\x80\x9cdesigned to filter out the bad claims and facilitate consideration of the good.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 204. To refuse to count a prior dismissal because of a pending appeal would produce a leaky filter. Appeals take time. During that time, a prisoner could file many lawsuits, including additional lawsuits that are frivolous, malicious, or fail to state a claim upon which relief may be granted. Indeed, Coleman filed these four cases after he suffered his third qualifying dismissal, in October 2009, and before the affirmance of that order, in March 2011.

\n

We recognize that our interpretation of the statute may create a different risk: An erroneous trial court dismissal might wrongly deprive a prisoner of in forma pauperis status with respect to lawsuits filed after a dismissal but before its reversal on appeal. But that risk does not seem great. For one thing, the Solicitor General informs us that he has been able to identify only two instances in which a Court of Appeals has reversed a District Court\xe2\x80\x99s issuance of a third strike. Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 22, n.\xc2\xa05. For another, where a court of appeals reverses a third strike, in some instances the prisoner will be able to refile his or her lawsuit after the reversal, seeking in forma pauperis status at that time. Further, if the statute of limitations governing that lawsuit has run out before the court of appeals reverses the third strike, the Solicitor General assures us that prisoners will find relief in Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 60(b). According to the Solicitor General, a prisoner may move to reopen his or her interim lawsuits (reinstating the cases as of the dates originally filed) and may then seek in forma pauperis status anew. In any event, we believe our interpretation of the statute hews more closely to its meaning and objective than does Coleman\xe2\x80\x99s alternative.

\n

B

\n

Coleman makes an additional argument. He poses a hypothetical: What if this case had involved an attempt to appeal from the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s dismissal of his third complaint instead of an attempt to file several additional complaints? If the dismissal were counted as his third strike, Coleman asserts, he would lose the ability to appeal in forma pauperis from that strike itself. He believes that this result, which potentially could deprive him of appellate review, would be unfair. He further believes that it would be such a departure from the federal courts\xe2\x80\x99 normal appellate practice that Congress could not possibly have intended it.

\n

The Solicitor General, while subscribing to our interpretation of the statute, supports Coleman on this point. The Solicitor General says that we can and should read the statute to afford a prisoner in forma pauperis status with respect to an appeal from a third qualifying dismissal\xe2\x80\x94even if it does not allow a prisoner to file a fourth case during that time. He believes that the statute, in referring to dismissals \xe2\x80\x9con 3 or more prior occasions,\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71915(g) (emphasis added), means that a trial court dismissal qualifies as a strike only if it occurred in a prior, different, lawsuit.

\n

We need not, and do not, now decide whether the Solicitor General\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation (or some other interpretation with the same result) is correct. That is because Coleman is not here appealing from a third-strike trial-court dismissal. He is appealing from the denial of in forma pauperis status with respect to several separate suits filed after the trial court dismissed his earlier third-strike suit. With respect to those suits, the earlier dismissals certainly took place on \xe2\x80\x9cprior occasions.\xe2\x80\x9d If and when the situation that Coleman hypothesizes does arise, the courts can consider the problem in context.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

For the reasons stated, we hold that a prisoner who has accumulated three prior qualifying dismissals under \xc2\xa71915(g) may not file an additional suit in forma pauperis while his appeal of one such dismissal is pending. The judgments of the Court of Appeals are

\n

Affirmed.
