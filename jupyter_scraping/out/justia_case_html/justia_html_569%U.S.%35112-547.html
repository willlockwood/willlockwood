\n\t\t\t\tMETRISH, WARDEN v. LANCASTER\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 12\xe2\x80\x93547.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 24, 2013\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 20, 2013\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tOn April 23, 1993, respondent Burt Lancaster, a former police officer with a long history of severe mental-health problems, shot and killed his girlfriend. At his 1994 jury trial in Michigan state court, Lancaster asserted a defense of diminished capacity. Under then-prevailing Michigan Court of Appeals precedent, the diminished-capacity defense permitted a legally sane defendant to present evidence of mental illness to negate the specific intent required to commit a particular crime. Apparently unpersuaded by Lancaster\xe2\x80\x99s defense, the jury convicted him of first-degree murder and a related firearm offense. Lancaster, however, later obtained federal habeas relief from these convictions.\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBy the time of Lancaster\xe2\x80\x99s retrial, the Michigan Supreme Court had rejected the diminished-capacity defense in its 2001 decision in Carpenter. Although the murder with which Lancaster was charged occurred several years before Carpenter was decided, the judge at his second trial applied Carpenter and therefore disallowed renewal of his diminished-capacity defense. Lancaster was again convicted. Affirming, the Michigan Court of Appeals rejected Lancaster\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s retroactive application of Carpenter vio- lated due process.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tLancaster reasserted his due process claim in a federal habeas petition. The District Court denied the petition, but the Sixth Circuit reversed. Concluding that the Michigan Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s 2001 rejection of the diminished-capacity defense was unforeseeable in April 1993, when Lancaster killed his girlfriend, the Sixth Circuit held that, by rejecting Lancaster\xe2\x80\x99s due process claim, the Michigan Court of Appeals had unreasonably applied clearly established federal law.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0Lancaster is not entitled to federal habeas relief. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9315.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0Under the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA), Lancaster may obtain federal habeas relief only if the Michigan Court of Appeals, in rejecting his due process claim, unreasonably applied \xe2\x80\x9cclearly established Federal law, as determined by [this] Court.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1). This standard is \xe2\x80\x9cdifficult to meet\xe2\x80\x9d: Lancaster must show that the Michigan Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 decision rested on \xe2\x80\x9can error well understood and comprehended in existing law beyond any possibility for fairminded disagreement.\xe2\x80\x9d Harrington v. Richter, 562 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. To determine whether Lancaster has satisfied that demanding standard, the Court first considers two key decisions: Bouie v. City of Columbia, \n\n378 U.\xc2\xa0S. 347\n, and Rogers v. Tennessee, \n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 451\n. It then considers whether the Michigan Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 decision qualifies as an unreasonable application of those decisions to Lancaster\xe2\x80\x99s case. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x935.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0Bouie concerned African-American petitioners who had refused to leave a South Carolina drug store\xe2\x80\x99s whites-only restaurant area after entering without notice that the store\xe2\x80\x99s policy barred their entry. They were convicted under a South Carolina trespass statute pro- hibiting \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98entry upon the lands of another .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. after notice from the owner or tenant prohibiting such entry.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 378 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 349\xe2\x80\x93350. The South Carolina Supreme Court based its affirmance of the petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 convictions on its prior decision in Mitchell, where the court held that the trespass statute reached both unauthorized entries and \xe2\x80\x9cthe act of remaining on the premises of another after receiving notice to leave.\xe2\x80\x9d 378 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 350. Mitchell, however, was rendered 21 months after the petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 arrest. This Court held that the Due Process Clause prohibited Mitchell\xe2\x80\x99s retroactive application to the Bouie petitioners, stressing that Mitchell\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the state trespass statute was \xe2\x80\x9cclearly at variance with the statutory language\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cha[d] not the slightest support in prior South Carolina decisions.\xe2\x80\x9d 378 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 356. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn Rogers, the petitioner contested the Tennessee Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s retroactive abolition of the common-law \xe2\x80\x9cyear and a day rule,\xe2\x80\x9d which barred a murder conviction \xe2\x80\x9cunless [the] victim had died by the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s act within a year and a day of the act.\xe2\x80\x9d 532 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 453. This Court found no due process violation. \xe2\x80\x9c[J]udicial alteration of a common law doctrine of criminal law,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court held, \xe2\x80\x9cviolates the principle of fair warning, and hence must not be given retroactive effect, only where [the alteration] is \xe2\x80\x98unexpected and indefensible by reference to the law which had been expressed prior to the conduct in issue.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 462. Judged by this standard, the retroactive abolition of the year and a day rule encountered no constitutional impediment. The rule was \xe2\x80\x9cwidely viewed as an outdated relic of the common law,\xe2\x80\x9d had been routinely rejected by modern courts and legislators, and had been mentioned in reported Tennessee decisions \xe2\x80\x9conly three times, and each time in dicta.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 462\xe2\x80\x93464. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x938.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0The Michigan Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 rejection of Lancaster\xe2\x80\x99s due process claim does not represent an unreasonable application of the law this Court declared in Bouie and Rogers. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9315.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(1)\xc2\xa0The Michigan Court of Appeals first recognized the diminished-capacity defense in 1973. Two years later, the Michigan Legislature prescribed comprehensive requirements for defenses based on mental illness or retardation. In 1978, the Michigan Court of Appeals ruled that the diminished-capacity defense fit within the codified definition of insanity. The Michigan Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s 2001 decision in Carpenter, however, rejected that position, holding that the diminished-capacity defense was not encompassed within the Michigan Legislature\xe2\x80\x99s comprehensive scheme for mental-illness defenses and thus could not be invoked by criminal defendants. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9312. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(2)\xc2\xa0In light of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent and the history of Michigan\xe2\x80\x99s diminished-capacity defense, the Michigan Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 decision applying Carpenter retroactively is not \xe2\x80\x9can unreasonable application of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. clearly established [f]ederal law.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1). This case is a far cry from Bouie, where the South Carolina Supreme Court unexpectedly expanded \xe2\x80\x9cnarrow and precise statutory language\xe2\x80\x9d that, as written, did not reach the petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 conduct. 378 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 352. In Carpenter, by contrast, the Michigan Supreme Court rejected a diminished-capacity defense that the court reasonably found to have no home in a comprehensive, on-point statute enacted by the Michigan Legislature. Although Lancaster\xe2\x80\x99s due process claim is arguably less weak than the due process claim rejected in Rogers, the Court did not hold in Rogers that a newly announced judicial rule may be applied retroactively only if the rule it replaces was an \xe2\x80\x9coutdated relic\xe2\x80\x9d rarely appearing in a jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x99s case law. 532 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 462\xe2\x80\x93467. Distinguishing Rogers thus does little to bolster Lancaster\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the Michigan Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 decision unreasonably applied clearly established federal law. This Court has never found a due process violation in circumstances remotely resembling Lancaster\xe2\x80\x99s case\xe2\x80\x94i.e., where a state supreme court, squarely addressing a particular issue for the first time, rejected a consistent line of lower court decisions based on the supreme court\xe2\x80\x99s reasonable interpretation of the language of a controlling statute. Fairminded jurists could conclude that a state supreme court decision of that order is not \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98unexpected and indefensible by reference to [existing] law.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 462. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9315. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t683 F.\xc2\xa03d 740, reversed.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tLINDA METRISH, WARDEN, PETITIONER v. BURT LANCASTER\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\ton writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBurt Lancaster was convicted in Michigan state court of first-degree murder and a related firearm offense. At the time the crime was committed, Michigan\xe2\x80\x99s intermediate appellate court had repeatedly recognized \xe2\x80\x9cdiminished capacity\xe2\x80\x9d as a defense negating the mens rea element of first-degree murder. By the time of Lancaster\xe2\x80\x99s trial and conviction, however, the Michigan Supreme Court in People v. Carpenter, 464 Mich. 223, 627 N. W. 2d 276 (2001), had rejected the defense. Lancaster asserts that retroactive application of the Michigan Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Carpenter denied him due process of law. On habeas review, a federal court must assess a claim for relief under the demanding standard set by the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA). Under that standard, Lancaster may gain relief only if the state-court decision he assails \xe2\x80\x9cwas contrary to, or in- volved an unreasonable application of, clearly established Federal law, as determined by [this] Court.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1). We hold that Lancaster\xe2\x80\x99s petition does not meet AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s requirement and that the United States Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit erred in granting him federal habeas relief.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOn April 23, 1993, Lancaster, a former police officer with a long history of severe mental-health problems, shot and killed his girlfriend in a shopping-plaza parking lot. At his 1994 jury trial in Michigan state court, Lancaster admitted that he had killed his girlfriend but asserted insanity and diminished-capacity defenses. Under then-prevailing Michigan Court of Appeals precedent, a defendant who pleaded diminished capacity, although he was legally sane, could \xe2\x80\x9coffer evidence of some mental abnormality to negate the specific intent required to commit a particular crime.\xe2\x80\x9d Carpenter, 464 Mich., at 232, 627 N. W. 2d, at 280. If a defendant succeeded in showing that mental illness prevented him from \xe2\x80\x9cform[ing] the specific state of mind required as an essential element of a crime,\xe2\x80\x9d he could \xe2\x80\x9cbe convicted only of a lower grade of the offense not requiring that particular mental element.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tApparently unpersuaded by Lancaster\xe2\x80\x99s defenses, the jury convicted him of first-degree murder, in violation of Mich. Comp. Laws Ann. \xc2\xa7750.316 (West 1991),\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t and possessing a firearm in the commission of a felony, in vio- lation of \xc2\xa7750.227b (West Cum. Supp. 2004). Lancaster later obtained federal habeas relief from these convictions, however, because, in conflict with Batson v. Kentucky, \n\n476 U.\xc2\xa0S. 79 (1986)\n, the prosecutor had exercised a race-based peremptory challenge to remove a potential juror. See Lancaster v. Adams, 324 F.\xc2\xa03d 423 (CA6 2003).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tLancaster was retried in 2005. By that time, the Michigan Supreme Court had disapproved the \xe2\x80\x9cseries of [Michigan Court of Appeals] decisions\xe2\x80\x9d recognizing the diminished-capacity defense. Carpenter, 464 Mich., at 235, 627 N. W. 2d, at 282. In rejecting the defense, Michigan\xe2\x80\x99s high court observed that, in 1975, the Michigan Legislature had enacted \xe2\x80\x9ca comprehensive statutory scheme concerning de- fenses based on either mental illness or mental retardation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 236, 627 N. W. 2d, at 282. That scheme, the Michigan Supreme Court concluded, \xe2\x80\x9cdemonstrate[d] the Legislature\xe2\x80\x99s intent to preclude the use of any evidence of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s lack of mental capacity short of legal insan- ity to avoid or reduce criminal responsibility.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAlthough the murder with which Lancaster was charged occurred several years before the Michigan Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Carpenter, the judge presiding at Lancaster\xe2\x80\x99s second trial applied Carpenter\xe2\x80\x99s holding and therefore disallowed renewal of Lancaster\xe2\x80\x99s diminished-capacity defense. Following a bench trial, Lancaster was again convicted. The trial court imposed a sentence of life imprisonment for the first-degree murder conviction and a consecutive two-year sentence for the related firearm offense.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tLancaster appealed, unsuccessfully, to the Michigan Court of Appeals. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 76a\xe2\x80\x9378a. The appeals court rejected Lancaster\xe2\x80\x99s argument that retro- active application of Carpenter to his case violated his right to due process. \xe2\x80\x9c[D]ue process concerns prevent retroactive application [of judicial decisions] in some cases,\xe2\x80\x9d the court acknowledged, \xe2\x80\x9cespecially .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. where the decision is unforeseeable and has the effect of changing existing law.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 77a. But Carpenter \xe2\x80\x9cdid not involve a change in the law,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court of Appeals reasoned, \xe2\x80\x9cbecause it concerned an unambiguous statute that was interpreted by the [Michigan] Supreme Court for the first time.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 77a.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAfter the Michigan Supreme Court declined review, Lancaster reasserted his due process claim in a federal habeas petition filed under \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254. The District Court denied the petition, 735 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 750 (ED Mich. 2010), but it granted a certificate of appealability, see \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72253(c).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA divided panel of the Sixth Circuit reversed. 683 F.\xc2\xa03d 740 (2012). The Michigan Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Carpenter was unforeseeable, the Court of Appeals major- ity concluded, given (1) the Michigan Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 consistent recognition of the diminished-capacity defense; (2) the Michigan Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s repeated references to the defense without casting a shadow of doubt on it; and (3) the inclusion of the diminished-capacity defense in the Michigan State Bar\xe2\x80\x99s pattern jury instructions. 683 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 745\xe2\x80\x93749. These considerations persuaded the Sixth Circuit majority that, in rejecting Lancaster\xe2\x80\x99s due process claim, the Michigan Court of Appeals had unreasonably applied clearly established federal law. Id., at 752\xe2\x80\x93753. Accordingly, the Sixth Circuit ruled that Lancaster was en- titled to a new trial at which he could present his diminished-capacity defense. Id., at 754. Dissenting, Chief Judge Batchelder concluded that the \xe2\x80\x9cMichigan Court of Appeals[\xe2\x80\x99] denial of Lancaster\xe2\x80\x99s due process claim was reasonable .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. because the diminished-capacity defense was not well-established in Michigan and its elimination was, therefore, foreseeable.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 755.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis Court granted certiorari. 568 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2013).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo obtain federal habeas relief under AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s strictures, Lancaster must establish that, in rejecting his due process claim, the Michigan Court of Appeals unreason- ably applied federal law clearly established in our decisions. See \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t This standard, we have explained, is \xe2\x80\x9cdifficult to meet\xe2\x80\x9d: To obtain habeas corpus relief from a federal court, a state prisoner must show that the challenged state-court ruling rested on \xe2\x80\x9can error well understood and comprehended in existing law beyond any possibility for fairminded disagreement.\xe2\x80\x9d Harrington v. Richter, 562 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 12\xe2\x80\x9313). To determine whether Lancaster has satisfied that demanding standard, we consider first two of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s key decisions: Bouie v. City of Columbia, \n\n378 U.\xc2\xa0S. 347 (1964)\n, and Rogers v. Tennessee, \n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 451 (2001)\n. We then consider whether the Michigan Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 decision qualifies as an unreasonable application of those decisions to the particular circumstances of Lancaster\xe2\x80\x99s case.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn Bouie, the African-American petitioners were convicted of trespass under South Carolina law after they refused to comply with orders to leave a drug store\xe2\x80\x99s restaurant department, a facility reserved for white customers. 378 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 348\xe2\x80\x93349. This Court held that the convictions violated the due process requirement that \xe2\x80\x9ca criminal statute give fair warning of the conduct which it prohibits.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 350. The state statute under which the petitioners were convicted, the Court emphasized, prohibited \xe2\x80\x9centry upon the lands of another .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. after notice from the owner or tenant prohibiting such entry.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 349\xe2\x80\x93350 (emphasis added and internal quotation marks omitted). It was undisputed that the petitioners were invited to enter the store and had received no notice that they were barred from the restaurant area before they occupied booth seats. Id., at 350. Nevertheless, the South Carolina Supreme Court affirmed the petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 convictions based on its prior decision in Charleston v. Mitchell, 239 S.\xc2\xa0C. 376, 123 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 512 (1961). Bouie, 378 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 350, n.\xc2\xa02. The Mitchell decision, which the South Carolina Supreme Court found dispositive, was rendered 21 months after the petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 arrest. 378 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 348, 350, n.\xc2\xa02. Mitchell held that the trespass statute under which the petitioners were convicted reached not only unauthorized entries; it proscribed as well \xe2\x80\x9cthe act of remaining on the premises of another after receiving notice to leave.\xe2\x80\x9d 378 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 350.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe held that the Due Process Clause prohibited Mitchell\xe2\x80\x99s retroactive application to the Bouie petitioners. In so ruling, we stressed that Mitchell\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the South Carolina trespass statute was \xe2\x80\x9cclearly at variance with the statutory language\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cha[d] not the slightest support in prior South Carolina decisions.\xe2\x80\x9d 378 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 356. Due process, we said, does not countenance an \xe2\x80\x9cunforeseeable and retroactive judicial expansion of narrow and precise statutory language.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 352.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn Rogers, the petitioner contested the Tennessee Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s retroactive abolition of the common-law \xe2\x80\x9cyear and a day rule.\xe2\x80\x9d 532 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 453. That rule barred a murder conviction \xe2\x80\x9cunless [the] victim had died by the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s act within a year and a day of the act.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The victim in Rogers had died some 15 months after the petitioner stabbed him. Id., at 454. We held that the Tennessee Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to adhere to the year and a day rule in the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s case did not violate due process. Id., at 466\xe2\x80\x93467. The \xe2\x80\x9cdue process limitations on the retroactive application of judicial decisions,\xe2\x80\x9d we explained, are not coextensive with the limitations placed on legislatures by the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s Ex Post Facto Clauses. Id., at 459. See also U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa79, cl.\xc2\xa03; id., \xc2\xa710, cl.\xc2\xa01; Calder v. Bull, 3 Dall. 386, 390 (1798) (seriatim opinion of Chase, J.) (describing four categories of laws prohibited by the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s Ex Post Facto Clauses). Strictly applying ex post facto principles to judicial decisionmaking, we recognized, \xe2\x80\x9cwould place an unworkable and unacceptable restraint on normal judicial processes and would be incompatible with the resolution of uncertainty that marks any evolving legal system.\xe2\x80\x9d Rogers, 532 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 461. \xe2\x80\x9c[J]udicial alteration of a common law doctrine of criminal law,\xe2\x80\x9d we therefore held, \xe2\x80\x9cviolates the principle of fair warning, and hence must not be given retroactive effect, only where [the alteration] is \xe2\x80\x98unexpected and indefensible by reference to the law which had been expressed prior to the conduct in issue.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 462 (quoting Bouie, 378 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 354).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJudged by this standard, we explained, the retroactive abolition of the year and a day rule encountered no constitutional impediment. First, the rule was \xe2\x80\x9cwidely viewed as an outdated relic of the common law\xe2\x80\x9d and had been \xe2\x80\x9clegislatively or judicially abolished in the vast majority of jurisdictions recently to have addressed the issue.\xe2\x80\x9d Rogers, 532 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 462\xe2\x80\x93463. Second, the rule \xe2\x80\x9chad only the most tenuous foothold\xe2\x80\x9d in Tennessee, having been mentioned in reported Tennessee decisions \xe2\x80\x9conly three times, and each time in dicta.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 464. Abolishing the obsolete rule in Rogers\xe2\x80\x99 case, we were satisfied, was not \xe2\x80\x9cthe sort of unfair and arbitrary judicial action against which the Due Process Clause aims to protect.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 466\xe2\x80\x93467.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tDoes the Michigan Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 rejection of Lan- caster\xe2\x80\x99s due process claim represent an unreasonable ap- plication of the law we declared in Bouie and Rogers? Addressing that question, we first summarize the history of the diminished-capacity defense in Michigan.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Michigan Court of Appeals first recognized the defense in People v. Lynch, 47 Mich. App. 8, 208 N. W. 2d 656 (1973). See Carpenter, 464 Mich., at 233, 627 N.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa02d, at 281. The defendant in Lynch was convicted of first-degree murder for starving her newborn daughter. 47 Mich. App., at 9, 208 N. W. 2d, at 656. On appeal, the defendant challenged the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s exclusion of psychiatric testimony \xe2\x80\x9cbearing on [her] state of mind.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 14, 208 N. W. 2d, at 659. She sought to introduce this evidence not to show she was legally insane at the time of her child\xe2\x80\x99s death.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Instead, her plea was that she lacked the mens rea necessary to commit first-degree murder. Ibid. Reversing the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conviction and remanding for a new trial, the Michigan Court of Appeals rejected the view \xe2\x80\x9cthat mental capacity is an all or nothing matter and that only insanity .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. negates criminal intent.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 20, 208 N.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa02d, at 662. Aligning itself with the \xe2\x80\x9cmajority .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. view,\xe2\x80\x9d the court permitted defendants to present relevant psychiatric \xe2\x80\x9ctestimony bearing on intent.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 20\xe2\x80\x9321, 208 N. W. 2d, at 662\xe2\x80\x93663. See also id., at 20, 208 N. W. 2d, at 662 (noting that \xe2\x80\x9csuch medical proof\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9csometimes called proof of diminished or partial responsibility\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 1975, two years after the Michigan Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 decision in Lynch, the Michigan Legislature enacted \xe2\x80\x9ca com- prehensive statutory scheme setting forth the requirements for and the effects of asserting a defense based on either mental illness or mental retardation.\xe2\x80\x9d Carpenter, 464 Mich., at 226, 627 N. W. 2d, at 277. See also 1975 Mich. Pub. Acts pp. 384\xe2\x80\x93388. That legislation, which remained in effect at the time of the April 1993 shooting at issue here, provided that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] person is legally insane if, as a result of mental illness .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. or .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. mental retardation .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that person lacks substantial capacity either to appreciate the wrongfulness of his conduct or to conform his con- duct to the requirements of law.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 386 (codified as amended, Mich. Comp. Laws Ann. \xc2\xa7768.21a(1) (West 2000)). The legislature required defendants in felony cases to notify the prosecution and the court at least 30 days before trial of their intent to assert an insanity defense. 1975 Mich. Pub. Acts p. 385 (codified as amended, \xc2\xa7768.20a(1)). Defendants raising an insanity defense, the legislature further provided, must submit to a court-ordered psychiatric examination. Id., at 385 (codified as amended, \xc2\xa7768.20a(2)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe 1975 Act also introduced the verdict of \xe2\x80\x9cguilty but mentally ill\xe2\x80\x9d for defendants who suffer from mental illness but do not satisfy the legal definition of insanity. Id., at 387 (codified as amended, \xc2\xa7768.36(1) (West Cum. Supp. 2013)). The legislature provided for the psychiatric evaluation and treatment of defendants found \xe2\x80\x9cguilty but mentally ill\xe2\x80\x9d but did not exempt them from the sentencing provisions applicable to defendants without mental illness. Id., at 387\xe2\x80\x93388 (codified as amended, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7768.36(3)\xe2\x80\x93(4)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAlthough the 1975 Act did not specifically address the defense of diminished capacity, the Michigan Court of Appeals ruled in 1978 that the defense \xe2\x80\x9ccomes within th[e] codified definition of legal insanity.\xe2\x80\x9d People v. Mangia- pane, 85 Mich. App. 379, 395, 271 N. W. 2d 240, 249. Therefore, the court held, a defendant claiming that he lacked the \xe2\x80\x9cmental capacity to entertain the specific intent required as an element of the crime with which he [was] charged\xe2\x80\x9d had to comply with the statutory procedural requirements applicable to insanity defenses, including the requirements of pretrial notice and submission to court-ordered examination. Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBecause the 1975 Act did not indicate which party bears the burden of proof on the issue of insanity, Michigan courts continued to apply the common-law burden-shifting framework in effect at the time of the insanity defense\xe2\x80\x99s codification. See People v. McRunels, 237 Mich. App. 168, 172, 603 N. W. 2d 95, 98 (1999). Under that framework, a criminal defendant bore the initial burden of present- ing some evidence of insanity, at which point the burden shifted to the prosecution to prove the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s sanity beyond a reasonable doubt. See In\xc2\xa0re Certified Question, 425 Mich. 457, 465\xe2\x80\x93466, 390 N. W. 2d 620, 623\xe2\x80\x93624 (1986); People v. Savoie, 419 Mich. 118, 126, 349 N. W. 2d 139, 143 (1984). The Michigan Court of Appeals applied the same burden-shifting framework to the diminished-capacity defense. See People v. Denton, 138 Mich. App. 568, 571\xe2\x80\x93572, 360 N. W. 2d 245, 247\xe2\x80\x93248 (1984).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 1994, however, the Michigan Legislature amended Mich. Comp. Laws Ann. \xc2\xa7768.21a, the statute codifying the insanity defense, to provide that the defendant bears \xe2\x80\x9cthe burden of proving the defense of insanity by a preponderance of the evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d 1994 Mich. Pub. Acts p. 252 (codified at \xc2\xa7768.21a(3)). In Carpenter, the defendant argued that the trial court had erred by applying the 1994 Act to require him to establish his diminished-capacity defense by a preponderance of the evidence. 464 Mich., at 225\xe2\x80\x93226, 235, 627 N. W. 2d, at 277, 282. Rejecting this contention, the Michigan Court of Appeals affirmed the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s convictions. See People v. Carpenter, No. 204051, 1999 WL 33438799 (July 16, 1999) (per curiam). Consistent with its decision in Mangiapane, the court held that the 1994 statutory amendments applied to defendants raising the diminished-capacity defense, and it further held that requiring defendants to establish their diminished capacity by a preponderance of the evidence did not unconstitutionally relieve the prosecution of its burden to prove the mens rea elements of a crime beyond a reasonable doubt. Id., at *1\xe2\x80\x93*2.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn turn, the Michigan Supreme Court also affirmed, but it did so on an entirely different ground. As earlier stated, see supra, at 2\xe2\x80\x933, the court concluded that in no case could criminal defendants invoke the diminished-capacity defense, for that defense was not encompassed within the \xe2\x80\x9ccomprehensive statutory scheme\xe2\x80\x9d the Michigan Legislature had enacted to govern defenses based on mental illness or retardation. Carpenter, 464 Mich., at 236, 627 N. W. 2d, at 282. Noting that previously it had \xe2\x80\x9cacknowledged in passing the concept of the diminished capacity defense,\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Michigan\xe2\x80\x99s high court emphasized that it had \xe2\x80\x9cnever specifically authorized .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. use [of the defense] in Michigan courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 232\xe2\x80\x93233, 627 N. W. 2d, at 281. Squarely addressing the issue for the first time, the court concluded that the diminished-capacity defense was incompatible with the Michigan Legislature\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cconclusiv[e] determin[ation]\xe2\x80\x9d of the circumstances under which \xe2\x80\x9cmental incapacity can serve as a basis for relieving [a defendant] from criminal responsibility.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 237, 627 N.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa02d, at 283. The statutory scheme enacted by the Michigan Legislature, the court held, \xe2\x80\x9ccreated an all or nothing insanity defense.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. But cf. supra, at 9. A defendant who is \xe2\x80\x9cmentally ill or retarded yet not legally insane,\xe2\x80\x9d the court explained, \xe2\x80\x9cmay be found \xe2\x80\x98guilty but mentally ill,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d but the legislature had foreclosed the use of \xe2\x80\x9cevidence of mental incapacity short of insanity .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to avoid or reduce criminal responsibility by negating specific intent.\xe2\x80\x9d 464 Mich., at 237, 627 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d, at 283.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Michigan Court of Appeals concluded that applying Carpenter retroactively to Lancaster\xe2\x80\x99s case did not violate due process, for Carpenter \xe2\x80\x9cconcerned an unambiguous statute that was interpreted by the [Michigan] Supreme Court for the first time.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 77a. As earlier Michigan Court of Appeals decisions indicate, see supra, at 8\xe2\x80\x9310, the bearing of the 1975 legislation on the diminished-capacity defense may not have been apparent pre-Carpenter. But in light of our precedent and the his- tory recounted above, see Part II\xe2\x80\x93B\xe2\x80\x931, supra, the Michigan Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 decision applying Carpenter retroac- tively does not warrant disapprobation as \xe2\x80\x9can unreasonable application of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. clearly established [f]ederal law.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis case is a far cry from Bouie, where, unlike Rogers, the Court held that the retroactive application of a judicial decision violated due process. In Bouie, the South Caro- lina Supreme Court had unexpectedly expanded \xe2\x80\x9cnarrow and precise statutory language\xe2\x80\x9d that, as written, did not reach the petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 conduct. 378 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 352. In Carpenter, by contrast, the Michigan Supreme Court rejected a diminished-capacity defense that the court reasonably found to have no home in a comprehensive, on-point statute enacted by the Michigan Legislature. Carpenter thus presents the inverse of the situation this Court confronted in Bouie. Rather than broadening a statute that was narrow on its face, Carpenter disapproved lower court precedent recognizing a defense Michigan\xe2\x80\x99s high court found, on close inspection, to lack statutory grounding. The situation we confronted in Bouie bears scant resemblance to this case, and our resolution of that controversy hardly makes disallowance of Lancaster\xe2\x80\x99s diminished-capacity defense an unreasonable reading of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s law.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOn the other hand, as the Sixth Circuit recognized, see 683 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 749\xe2\x80\x93751, Lancaster\xe2\x80\x99s argument against applying Carpenter retroactively is arguably less weak than the argument opposing retroactivity we rejected in Rogers. Unlike the year and a day rule at issue in Rogers, the diminished-capacity defense is not an \xe2\x80\x9coutdated relic of the common law\xe2\x80\x9d widely rejected by modern courts and legislators. 532 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 462. To the contrary, the Model Penal Code sets out a version of the defense. See ALI, Model Penal Code \xc2\xa74.02(1), pp. 216\xe2\x80\x93217 (1985) (\xe2\x80\x9cEvidence that the defendant suffered from a mental disease or defect is admissible whenever it is relevant to prove that the defendant did or did not have a state of mind that is an element of the offense.\xe2\x80\x9d). See also id., Comment 2, at 219 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Institute perceived no justification for a limitation on evidence that may bear significantly on a determination of the mental state of the defendant at the time of the commission of the crime.\xe2\x80\x9d). And not long before the 1993 shooting at issue here, the American Bar Association had approved criminal-justice guidelines that (1) favored the admissibility of mental-health evidence offered to ne- gate mens rea, and (2) reported that a majority of States allowed presentation of such evidence in at least some circumstances. See ABA Criminal Justice Mental Health Standards \xc2\xa77\xe2\x80\x936.2, and Commentary, pp. 347\xe2\x80\x93349, and n.\xc2\xa02 (1989). See also Clark v. Arizona, \n\n548 U.\xc2\xa0S. 735, \n800 (2006)\n (Kennedy, J., dissenting) (reporting that in 2006, \xe2\x80\x9ca substantial majority of the States\xe2\x80\x9d permitted the introduction of \xe2\x80\x9cmental-illness evidence to negate mens rea\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFurthermore, the year and a day rule was mentioned only three times in dicta in Tennessee reported decisions. Rogers, 532 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 464. The diminished-capacity defense, by contrast, had been adhered to repeatedly by the Michigan Court of Appeals. See supra, at 8\xe2\x80\x9310. It had also been \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98acknowledged in passing\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in Michigan Supreme Court decisions and was reflected in the Michigan State Bar\xe2\x80\x99s pattern jury instructions. 683 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 746\xe2\x80\x93749 (quoting Carpenter, 464 Mich., at 232, 627 N. W. 2d, at 281).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThese considerations, however, are hardly sufficient to warrant federal habeas relief under \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1)\xe2\x80\x99s demanding standard. See Williams v. Taylor, \n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 362, \n410 (2000)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]n unreasonable application of federal law is different from an incorrect appli- cation of federal law.\xe2\x80\x9d). Rogers did not hold that a newly announced judicial rule may be applied retroactively only if the rule it replaces was an \xe2\x80\x9coutdated relic\xe2\x80\x9d rarely appearing in a jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x99s case law. 532 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 462\xe2\x80\x93467. Distinguishing Rogers, a case in which we rejected a due process claim, thus does little to bolster Lancaster\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the Michigan Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 decision unreasonably applied clearly established federal law. See Williams, 529 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 412 (the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cclearly established [f]ederal law\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) \xe2\x80\x9crefers to the holdings .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions as of the time of the relevant state-court decision\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis Court has never found a due process violation in circumstances remotely resembling Lancaster\xe2\x80\x99s case\xe2\x80\x94i.e., where a state supreme court, squarely addressing a particular issue for the first time, rejected a consistent line of lower court decisions based on the supreme court\xe2\x80\x99s reasonable interpretation of the language of a controlling statute. Fairminded jurists could conclude that a state supreme court decision of that order is not \xe2\x80\x9cunexpected and indefensible by reference to [existing] law.\xe2\x80\x9d Rogers, 532 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 462 (internal quotation marks omitted). Lancaster therefore is not entitled to federal habeas relief on his due process claim.\n\t\t\t
