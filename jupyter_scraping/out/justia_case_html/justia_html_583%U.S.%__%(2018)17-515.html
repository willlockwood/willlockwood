SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

\n

CNH INDUSTRIAL N.V., et\xc2\xa0al. v. JACK REESE, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on petition for writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

No. 17\xe2\x80\x93515.\xe2\x80\x83Decided February 20, 2018

\n

\n

Per Curiam.

\n

Three Terms ago, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in M&G Polymers USA, LLC v. Tackett, 574 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015), held that the Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit was required to interpret collective-bargaining agreements according to \xe2\x80\x9cordinary principles of contract law.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 1). Before Tackett, the Sixth Circuit applied a series of \xe2\x80\x9cYard-Man inferences,\xe2\x80\x9d stemming from its decision in International Union, United Auto, Aerospace, & Agricultural Implement Workers of Am. v. Yard-Man, Inc., 716 F. 2d 1476 (1983). In accord with the Yard-Man inferences, courts presumed, in a variety of circumstances, that collective-bargaining agreements vested retiree benefits for life. See Tackett, 574 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 7\xe2\x80\x9310). But Tackett \xe2\x80\x9creject[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d these inferences \xe2\x80\x9cas inconsistent with ordinary principles of contract law.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 14).

\n

In this case, the Sixth Circuit held that the same Yard-Man inferences it once used to presume lifetime vesting can now be used to render a collective-bargaining agreement ambiguous as a matter of law, thus allowing courts to consult extrinsic evidence about lifetime vesting. 854 F.\xc2\xa03d 877, 882\xe2\x80\x93883 (2017). This analysis cannot be squared with Tackett. A contract is not ambiguous unless it is subject to more than one reasonable interpretation, and the Yard-Man inferences cannot generate a reason- able interpretation because they are not \xe2\x80\x9cordinary principles of contract law,\xe2\x80\x9d Tackett, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 14). Because the Sixth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s analysis is \xe2\x80\x9cYard-Man re-born, re-built, and re-purposed for new adventures,\xe2\x80\x9d 854 F. 3d, at 891 (Sutton, J., dissenting), we reverse.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

This Court has long held that collective-bargaining agreements must be interpreted \xe2\x80\x9caccording to ordinary principles of contract law.\xe2\x80\x9d Tackett, 574 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 7) (citing Textile Workers v. Lincoln Mills of Ala.,\n\n353 U.\xc2\xa0S. 448\n\xe2\x80\x93457 (1957)). Prior to Tackett, the Sixth Circuit purported to follow this rule, but it used a unique series of \xe2\x80\x9cYard-Man inferences\xe2\x80\x9d that no other circuit applied. 574 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 7). For example, the Sixth Circuit presumed that \xe2\x80\x9ca general durational clause\xe2\x80\x9d in a collective-bargaining agreement \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98says nothing about the vesting of retiree benefits\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in that agreement. Id., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 9\xe2\x80\x9310) (quoting Noe v. PolyOne Corp., 520 F.\xc2\xa03d 548, 555 (CA6 2008)). If the collective-bargaining agreement lacked \xe2\x80\x9ca termination provision specifically addressing retiree benefits\xe2\x80\x9d but contained specific termination provisions for other benefits, the Sixth Circuit presumed that the retiree benefits vested for life. Tackett, supra, at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 7\xe2\x80\x938) (citing Yard-Man, supra, at 1480). The Sixth Circuit also presumed vesting if \xe2\x80\x9ca provision .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. \xe2\x80\x98tie[d] eligibility for retirement-health benefits to eligibility for a pension.\xe2\x80\x9d 574 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 10) (quoting Noe, supra, at 558).

\n

This Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Tackett \xe2\x80\x9creject[ed] the Yard-Man inferences as inconsistent with ordinary principles of contract law.\xe2\x80\x9d 574 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 14). Most obviously, the Yard-Man inferences erroneously \xe2\x80\x9crefused to apply general durational clauses to provisions governing retiree benefits.\xe2\x80\x9d 574 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 12). This refusal \xe2\x80\x9cdistort[ed] the text of the agreement and conflict[ed] with the principle of contract law that the written agreement is presumed to encompass the whole agreement of the parties.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

The Yard-Man inferences also incorrectly inferred lifetime vesting whenever \xe2\x80\x9ca contract is silent as to the duration of retiree benefits.\xe2\x80\x9d 574 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 14). The \xe2\x80\x9ctraditional principle,\xe2\x80\x9d Tackett explained, is that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98contractual obligations will cease, in the ordinary course, upon termination of the bargaining agreement.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 13) (quoting Litton Financial Printing Div., Litton Business Systems, Inc. v. NLRB,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 190,\n207 (1991)\n). \xe2\x80\x9c[C]ontracts that are silent as to their duration will ordinarily be treated not as \xe2\x80\x98operative in perpetuity\xe2\x80\x99 but as \xe2\x80\x98operative for a reasonable time.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 574 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 13) (quoting 3 A. Corbin, Corbin on Contracts \xc2\xa7553, p. 216 (1960)). In fact, the Sixth Circuit had followed this principle in cases involving noncollectively bargained agreements, see Sprague v. General Motors Corp., 133 F.\xc2\xa03d 388, 400 (1998) (en banc), which \xe2\x80\x9conly underscore[d] Yard-Man\xe2\x80\x99s deviation from ordinary principles of contract law.\xe2\x80\x9d Tackett, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 13).

\n

As for the tying of retiree benefits to pensioner status, Tackett rejected this Yard-Man inference as \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary to Congress\xe2\x80\x99 determination\xe2\x80\x9d in the Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974 (ERISA),\n\n88Stat.\n891. 574 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 11). The Sixth Circuit adopted this inference on the assumption that retiree health benefits are \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a form of delayed compensation or reward for past services,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d like a pension. Id., at ___ (slip op., at 4) (quoting Yard-Man, supra, at 1482). But ERISA distinguishes between plans that \xe2\x80\x9cresul[t] in a deferral of income,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71002(2)(A)(ii), and plans that offer medical benefits, \xc2\xa71002(1)(A). See Tackett, 574 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 11). Tackett thus concluded that this and the other \xe2\x80\x9cinferences applied in Yard-Man and its progeny\xe2\x80\x9d do not \xe2\x80\x9crepresent ordinary principles of contract law.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 10).

\n

B

\n

Like Tackett, this case involves a dispute between retirees and their former employer about whether an expired collective-bargaining agreement created a vested right to lifetime health care benefits. In 1998, CNH Industrial N.\xc2\xa0V. and CNH Industrial America LLC (collectively, CNH) agreed to a collective-bargaining agreement. The 1998 agreement provided health care benefits under a group benefit plan to certain \xe2\x80\x9c[e]mployees who retire under the .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. Pension Plan.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. A\xe2\x80\x93116. \xe2\x80\x9cAll other coverages,\xe2\x80\x9d such as life insurance, ceased upon retirement. Ibid. The group benefit plan was \xe2\x80\x9cmade part of\xe2\x80\x9d the collective-bargaining agreement and \xe2\x80\x9cr[an] concurrently\xe2\x80\x9d with it. Id., at A\xe2\x80\x93114. The 1998 agreement contained a general durational clause stating that it would terminate in May 2004. Id., at A\xe2\x80\x93115. The agreement also stated that it \xe2\x80\x9cdispose[d] of any and all bargaining issues, whether or not presented during negotiations.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

When the 1998 agreement expired in 2004, a class of CNH retirees and surviving spouses (collectively, the retirees) filed this lawsuit, seeking a declaration that their health care benefits vested for life and an injunction preventing CNH from changing them. While their lawsuit was pending, this Court decided Tackett. Based on Tackett, the District Court initially awarded summary judgment to CNH. But after reconsideration, it awarded summary judgment to the retirees. 143 F.\xc2\xa0Supp.\xc2\xa03d 609 (ED Mich. 2015).

\n

The Sixth Circuit affirmed in relevant part. 854 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 879. The court began by noting that the 1998 agreement was \xe2\x80\x9csilent\xe2\x80\x9d on whether health care benefits vested for life. Id., at 882. Although the agreement contained a general durational clause, the Sixth Circuit found that clause inconclusive for two reasons. First, the 1998 agreement \xe2\x80\x9ccarved out certain benefits\xe2\x80\x9d like life insurance \xe2\x80\x9cand stated that those coverages ceased at a time different than other provisions.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; see App. to Pet. for Cert. A\xe2\x80\x93116. Second, the 1998 agreement \xe2\x80\x9ctied\xe2\x80\x9d health care benefits to pension eligibility. 854 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 882; see App. to Pet. for Cert. A\xe2\x80\x93116. These conditions rendered the 1998 agreement ambiguous, according to the Sixth Circuit, which allowed it to consult extrinsic evidence. 854 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 883. And that evidence supported lifetime vesting. Ibid. The Sixth Circuit acknowledged that these features of the agreement are the same ones it used to \xe2\x80\x9cinfer vesting\xe2\x80\x9d under Yard-Man, but it concluded that nothing in Tackett precludes this kind of analysis: \xe2\x80\x9cThere is surely a difference between finding ambiguity from silence and finding vesting from silence.\xe2\x80\x9d 854 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 882.[1]

\n

Judge Sutton dissented. See id., at 887\xe2\x80\x93893. He concluded that the 1998 agreement was unambiguous because \xe2\x80\x9cthe company never promised to provide healthcare benefits for life, and the agreement contained a durational clause that limited all of the benefits.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 888. Judge Sutton noted that, in finding ambiguity, the panel major- ity relied on the same inferences that this Court proscribed in Tackett. See 854 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 890\xe2\x80\x93891. But ambiguity, he explained, requires \xe2\x80\x9ctwo competing interpretations, both of which are fairly plausible,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 890, and \xe2\x80\x9c[a] forbidden inference cannot generate a plausible reading,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 891. The panel majority\xe2\x80\x99s contrary decision, Judge Sutton concluded, \xe2\x80\x9cabrad[ed] an inter-circuit split (and an intra-circuit split) that the Supreme Court just sutured shut.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 890.[2]

\n

II

\n

The decision below does not comply with Tackett\xe2\x80\x99s direction to apply ordinary contract principles. True, one such principle is that, when a contract is ambiguous, courts can consult extrinsic evidence to determine the parties\xe2\x80\x99 intentions. See 574 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (Ginsburg, J., concurring) (slip op., at 1) (citing 11 R. Lord, Williston on Contracts \xc2\xa730:7, pp. 116\xe2\x80\x93124 (4th ed. 2012) (Williston)). But a contract is not ambiguous unless, \xe2\x80\x9cafter applying established rules of interpretation, [it] remains reasonably susceptible to at least two reasonable but conflicting meanings.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., \xc2\xa730:4, at 53\xe2\x80\x9354 (footnote omitted). Here, that means the 1998 agreement was not ambiguous unless it could reasonably be read as vesting health care benefits for life.

\n

The Sixth Circuit read it that way only by employing the inferences that this Court rejected in Tackett. The Sixth Circuit did not point to any explicit terms, implied terms, or industry practice suggesting that the 1998 agreement vested health care benefits for life. Cf. 574 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (Ginsburg, J., concurring) (slip op., at 2). Instead, it found ambiguity in the 1998 agreement by applying several of the Yard-Man inferences: It declined to apply the general durational clause to the health care benefits, and then it inferred vesting from the presence of specific termination provisions for other benefits and the tying of health care benefits to pensioner status.

\n

Tackett rejected those inferences precisely because they are not \xe2\x80\x9cestablished rules of interpretation,\xe2\x80\x9d 11\xc2\xa0Williston \xc2\xa730:4, at 53\xe2\x80\x9354. The Yard-Man inferences \xe2\x80\x9cdistort the text of the agreement,\xe2\x80\x9d fail \xe2\x80\x9cto apply general durational clauses,\xe2\x80\x9d erroneously presume lifetime vesting from silence, and contradict how \xe2\x80\x9cCongress specifically defined\xe2\x80\x9d key terms in ERISA. Tackett, 574 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 11\xe2\x80\x9314). Tackett thus rejected these inferences not because of the consequences that the Sixth Circuit attached to them\xe2\x80\x94presuming vesting versus finding ambiguity\xe2\x80\x94but because they are not a valid way to read a contract. They cannot be used to create a reasonable interpretation any more than they can be used to create a presumptive one.

\n

Tellingly, no other Court of Appeals would find ambigu- ity in these circumstances. When a collective-bargaining agreement is merely silent on the question of vesting, other courts would conclude that it does not vest benefits for life.[3] Similarly, when an agreement does not specify a duration for health care benefits in particular, other courts would simply apply the general durational clause.[4] And other courts would not find ambiguity from the tying of retiree benefits to pensioner status.[5] The approach taken in these other decisions \xe2\x80\x9conly underscores\xe2\x80\x9d how the decision below \xe2\x80\x9cdeviat[ed] from ordinary principles of contract law.\xe2\x80\x9d Tackett, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 13).

\n

Shorn of Yard-Man inferences, this case is straightforward. The 1998 agreement contained a general durational clause that applied to all benefits, unless the agreement specified otherwise. No provision specified that the health care benefits were subject to a different durational clause. The agreement stated that the health benefits plan \xe2\x80\x9cr[an] concurrently\xe2\x80\x9d with the collective-bargaining agreement, tying the health care benefits to the duration of the rest of the agreement. App. to Pet. for Cert. A\xe2\x80\x93114. If the parties meant to vest health care benefits for life, they easily could have said so in the text. But they did not. And they specified that their agreement \xe2\x80\x9cdispose[d] of any and all bargaining issues\xe2\x80\x9d between them. Id., at A\xe2\x80\x93115. Thus, the only reasonable interpretation of the 1998 agree- ment is that the health care benefits expired when the collective-bargaining agreement expired in May 2004. \xe2\x80\x9cWhen the intent of the parties is unambiguously expressed in the contract, that expression controls, and the court\xe2\x80\x99s inquiry should proceed no further.\xe2\x80\x9d Tackett, supra, at ___ (Ginsburg, J., concurring) (slip op., at 1) (citing 11 Williston \xc2\xa730:6, at 98\xe2\x80\x93104).

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

Because the decision below is not consistent with Tackett, the petition for a writ of certiorari and the motions for leave to file briefs amici curiae are granted. We reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeals and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

\n

It is so ordered.
