\n

certiorari to the supreme court of new mexico

\n

No. 09\xe2\x80\x9310876.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 2, 2011\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 23, 2011

\n

The Sixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Confrontation Clause gives the accused \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n all criminal prosecutions, \xe2\x80\xa6 the right \xe2\x80\xa6 to be confronted with the witnesses against him.\xe2\x80\x9d In Crawford v. Washington, 541 U. S. 36, 59, this Court held that the Clause permits admission of \xe2\x80\x9c[t]estimonial statements of witnesses absent from trial \xe2\x80\xa6 only where the declarant is unavailable, and only where the defendant has had a prior opportunity to cross-examine.\xe2\x80\x9d Later, in Melendez-Diaz v. Massachusetts, 557 U. S. ___, the Court declined to create a \xe2\x80\x9cforensic evidence\xe2\x80\x9d exception to Crawford, holding that a forensic laboratory report, created specifically to serve as evidence in a criminal proceeding, ranked as \xe2\x80\x9ctestimonial\xe2\x80\x9d for Confrontation Clause purposes. Absent stipulation, the Court ruled, the prosecution may not introduce such a report without offering a live witness competent to testify to the truth of the report\xe2\x80\x99s statements. 557 U. S., at ___.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s jury trial on charges of driving while intoxicated (DWI) occurred after Crawford, but before Melendez-Diaz. Principal evidence against him was a forensic laboratory report certifying that his blood-alcohol concentration was well above the threshold for aggravated DWI. Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s blood sample had been tested at the New Mexico Department of Health, Scientific Laboratory Division (SLD), by a forensic analyst named Caylor, who completed, signed, and certified the report. However, the prosecution neither called Caylor to testify nor asserted he was unavailable; the record showed only that Caylor was placed on unpaid leave for an undisclosed reason. In lieu of Caylor, the State called another analyst, Razatos, to validate the report. Razatos was familiar with the testing device used to analyze Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s blood and with the laboratory\xe2\x80\x99s testing procedures, but had neither participated in nor observed the test on Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s blood sample. Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s counsel objected, asserting that introduction of Caylor\xe2\x80\x99s report without his testimony would violate the Confrontation Clause, but the trial court overruled the objection, admitted the SLD report as a business record, and permitted Razatos to testify. Bullcoming was convicted, and, while his appeal was pending before the New Mexico Supreme Court, this Court decided Melendez-Diaz. The state high court acknowledged that the SLD report qualified as testimonial evidence under Melendez-Diaz, but held that the report\xe2\x80\x99s admission did not violate the Confrontation Clause because: (1) certifying analyst Caylor was a mere scrivener who simply transcribed machine-generated test results, and (2) SLD analyst Razatos, although he did not participate in testing Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s blood, qualified as an expert witness with respect to the testing machine and SLD procedures. The court affirmed Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s conviction.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The judgment is reversed, and the case is remanded.

\n

147 N.\xc2\xa0M. 487, 226 P.\xc2\xa03d 1, reversed and remanded.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court with respect to all but Part IV and footnote 6. The Confrontation Clause, the opinion concludes, does not permit the prosecution to introduce a forensic laboratory report containing a testimonial certification, made in order to prove a fact at a criminal trial, through the in-court testimony of an analyst who did not sign the certification or personally perform or observe the performance of the test reported in the certification. The accused\xe2\x80\x99s right is to be confronted with the analyst who made the certification, unless that analyst is unavailable at trial, and the accused had an opportunity, pretrial, to cross-examine that particular scientist. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0If an out-of-court statement is testimonial, it may not be introduced against the accused at trial unless the witness who made the statement is unavailable and the accused has had a prior opportunity to confront that witness. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(i)\xc2\xa0Caylor\xe2\x80\x99s certification reported more than a machine-generated number: It represented that he received Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s blood sample intact with the seal unbroken; that he checked to make sure that the forensic report number and the sample number corresponded; that he performed a particular test on Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s sample, adhering to a precise protocol; and that he left the report\xe2\x80\x99s remarks section blank, indicating that no circumstance or condition affected the sample\xe2\x80\x99s integrity or the analysis\xe2\x80\x99 validity. These representations, relating to past events and human actions not revealed in raw, machine-produced data, are meet for cross-examination. The potential ramifications of the state court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning, therefore, raise red flags. Most witnesses testify to their observations of factual conditions or events. Where, for example, a police officer\xe2\x80\x99s report recorded an objective fact such as the read-out of a radar gun, the state court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning would permit another officer to introduce the information, so long as he or she was equipped to testify about the technology the observing officer deployed and the police department\xe2\x80\x99s standard operating procedures. As, e.g., Davis v. Washington, 547 U. S. 813, 826, makes plain, however, such testimony would violate the Confrontation Clause. The comparative reliability of an analyst\xe2\x80\x99s testimonial report does not dispense with the Clause. Crawford, 541 U. S., at 62. The analysts who write reports introduced as evidence must be made available for confrontation even if they have \xe2\x80\x9cthe scientific acumen of Mme. Curie and the veracity of Mother Teresa.\xe2\x80\x9d Melendez-Diaz, 557 U. S., at ___, n.\xc2\xa06. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(ii)\xc2\xa0Nor was Razatos an adequate substitute witness simply because he qualified as an expert with respect to the testing machine and the SLD\xe2\x80\x99s laboratory procedures. Surrogate testimony of the kind Razatos was equipped to give could not convey what Caylor knew or observed about the events he certified, nor expose any lapses or lies on Caylor\xe2\x80\x99s part. Significantly, Razatos did not know why Caylor had been placed on unpaid leave. With Caylor on the stand, Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s counsel could have asked Caylor questions designed to reveal whether Caylor\xe2\x80\x99s incompetence, evasiveness, or dishonesty accounted for his removal from work. And the State did not assert that Razatos had any independent opinion concerning Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s blood alcohol content. More fundamentally, the Confrontation Clause does not tolerate dispensing with confrontation simply because the court believes that questioning one witness about another\xe2\x80\x99s testimonial statements provides a fair enough opportunity for cross-examination. Although the purpose of Sixth Amendment rights is to ensure a fair trial, it does not follow that such rights can be disregarded because, on the whole, the trial is fair. United States v. Gonzalez-Lopez, 548 U. S. 140, 145. If a \xe2\x80\x9cparticular guarantee\xe2\x80\x9d is violated, no substitute procedure can cure the violation. Id., at 146. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Melendez-Diaz precluded the State\xe2\x80\x99s argument that introduction of the SLD report did not implicate the Confrontation Clause because the report is nontestimonial. Like the certificates in Melendez-Diaz, the SLD report is undoubtedly an \xe2\x80\x9caffirmation made for the purpose of establishing or proving some fact\xe2\x80\x9d in a criminal proceeding. 557 U. S., at ___. Created solely for an \xe2\x80\x9cevidentiary purpose,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at ___, the report ranks as testimonial. In all material respects, the SLD report resembles the certificates in Melendez-Diaz. Here, as there, an officer provided seized evidence to a state laboratory required by law to assist in police investigations. Like the Melendez-Diaz analysts, Caylor tested the evidence and prepared a certificate concerning the result of his analysis. And like the Melendez-Diaz certificates, Caylor\xe2\x80\x99s report here is \xe2\x80\x9cformalized\xe2\x80\x9d in a signed document, Davis, 547 U. S., at 837, n.\xc2\xa02. Also noteworthy, the SLD report form contains a legend referring to municipal and magistrate courts\xe2\x80\x99 rules that provide for the admission of certified blood-alcohol analyses. Thus, although the SLD report was not notarized, the formalities attending the report were more than adequate to qualify Caylor\xe2\x80\x99s assertions as testimonial. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9316.

DONALD BULLCOMING, PETITIONER v. NEW\nMEXICO

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of\nnew mexico

\n

[June 23, 2011]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court, except as to Part IV and footnote 6.*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Melendez-Diaz v. Massachusetts, 557 U. S. ___ (2009), this Court held that a forensic laboratory report stating that a suspect substance was cocaine ranked as testimonial for purposes of the Sixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Confrontation Clause. The report had been created specifically to serve as evidence in a criminal proceeding. Absent stipulation, the Court ruled, the prosecution may not introduce such a report without offering a live witness competent to testify to the truth of the statements made in the report.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the case before us, petitioner Donald Bullcoming \nwas arrested on charges of driving while intoxicated (DWI). Principal evidence against Bullcoming was a foren-sic laboratory report certifying that Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s blood-alcohol concentration was well above the threshold for aggravated DWI. At trial, the prosecution did not call as a witness the analyst who signed the certification. Instead, the State called another analyst who was familiar with the laboratory\xe2\x80\x99s testing procedures, but had neither participated in nor observed the test on Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s blood sample. The New Mexico Supreme Court determined that, although the blood-alcohol analysis was \xe2\x80\x9ctestimonial,\xe2\x80\x9d the Confrontation Clause did not require the certifying analyst\xe2\x80\x99s in-court testimony. Instead, New Mexico\xe2\x80\x99s high court held, live testimony of another analyst satisfied the constitutional requirements.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The question presented is whether the Confrontation Clause permits the prosecution to introduce a forensic laboratory report containing a testimonial certification\xe2\x80\x94made for the purpose of proving a particular fact\xe2\x80\x94through the in-court testimony of a scientist who did not sign the certification or perform or observe the test reported in the certification. We hold that surrogate testimony of that order does not meet the constitutional requirement. The accused\xe2\x80\x99s right is to be confronted with the analyst who made the certification, unless that analyst is unavailable at trial, and the accused had an opportunity, pretrial, to cross-examine that particular scientist.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In August 2005, a vehicle driven by petitioner Donald Bullcoming rear-ended a pick-up truck at an intersection in Farmington, New Mexico. When the truckdriver exited his vehicle and approached Bullcoming to exchange insurance information, he noticed that Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s eyes were bloodshot. Smelling alcohol on Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s breath, the truckdriver told his wife to call the police. Bullcoming left the scene before the police arrived, but was soon apprehended by an officer who observed his performance of field sobriety tests. Upon failing the tests, Bullcoming was arrested for driving a vehicle while \xe2\x80\x9cunder the influence of intoxicating liquor\xe2\x80\x9d (DWI), in violation of N.\xc2\xa0M. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa766\xe2\x80\x938\xe2\x80\x93102 (2004).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because Bullcoming refused to take a breath test, the police obtained a warrant authorizing a blood-alcohol analy-sis. Pursuant to the warrant, a sample of Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s blood was drawn at a local hospital. To determine Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s blood-alcohol concentration (BAC), the police \nsent the sample to the New Mexico Department of Health, Scientific Laboratory Division (SLD). In a standard SLD form titled \xe2\x80\x9cReport of Blood Alcohol Analysis,\xe2\x80\x9d participants in the testing were identified, and the forensic analyst certified his finding. App. 62.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0SLD\xe2\x80\x99s report contained in the top block \xe2\x80\x9cinformation \xe2\x80\xa6 filled in by [the] arresting officer.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (capitalization omitted). This information included the \xe2\x80\x9creason [the] suspect [was] stopped\xe2\x80\x9d (the officer checked \xe2\x80\x9cAccident\xe2\x80\x9d), and the date (\xe2\x80\x9c8.14.05\xe2\x80\x9d) and time (\xe2\x80\x9c18:25 PM\xe2\x80\x9d) the blood sample was drawn. Ibid. (capitalization omitted). The arresting officer also affirmed that he had arrested Bullcoming and witnessed the blood draw. Ibid. The next two blocks contained certifications by the nurse who drew Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s blood and the SLD intake employee who received the blood sample sent to the laboratory. Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Following these segments, the report presented the \xe2\x80\x9ccertificate of analyst,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. (capitalization omitted), com-pleted and signed by Curtis Caylor, the SLD forensic analyst assigned to test Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s blood sample. Id., at 62, 64\xe2\x80\x9365. Caylor recorded that the BAC in Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s sample was 0.21 grams per hundred milliliters, an inordinately high level. Id., at 62. Caylor also affirmed that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he seal of th[e] sample was received intact and broken in the laboratory,\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cthe statements in [the analyst\xe2\x80\x99s block of the report] are correct,\xe2\x80\x9d and that he had \xe2\x80\x9cfollowed the procedures set out on the reverse of th[e] report.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Those \xe2\x80\x9cprocedures\xe2\x80\x9d instructed analysts, inter alia, to \xe2\x80\x9cre-tai[n] the sample container and the raw data from the analysis,\xe2\x80\x9d and to \xe2\x80\x9cnot[e] any circumstance or condition which might affect the integrity of the sample or otherwise affect the validity of the analysis.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 65. Finally, in a block headed \xe2\x80\x9ccertificate of reviewer,\xe2\x80\x9d the SLD examiner who reviewed Caylor\xe2\x80\x99s analysis certified that Caylor was qualified to conduct the BAC test, and that the \xe2\x80\x9cestablished procedure\xe2\x80\x9d for handling and analyzing Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s sample \xe2\x80\x9cha[d] been followed.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 62 (capitalization omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0SLD analysts use gas chromatograph machines to determine BAC levels. Operation of the machines requires specialized knowledge and training. Several steps are involved in the gas chromatograph process, and human error can occur at each step.[Footnote 1]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Caylor\xe2\x80\x99s report that Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s BAC was 0.21 supported a prosecution for aggravated DWI, the threshold for which is a BAC of 0.16 grams per hundred milliliters, \xc2\xa766\xe2\x80\x938\xe2\x80\x93102(D)(1). The State accordingly charged Bullcoming with this more serious crime.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The case was tried to a jury in November 2005, after our decision in Crawford v. Washington, 541 U. S. 36 (2004), but before Melendez-Diaz. On the day of trial, the State announced that it would not be calling SLD analyst Curtis Caylor as a witness because he had \xe2\x80\x9cvery recently [been] put on unpaid leave\xe2\x80\x9d for a reason not revealed. 2010\xe2\x80\x93NMSC\xe2\x80\x93007, \xc2\xb68, 226 P.\xc2\xa03d 1, 6 (internal quotation marks omitted); App. 58. A startled defense counsel objected. The prosecution, she complained, had never disclosed, until trial commenced, that the witness \xe2\x80\x9cout there \xe2\x80\xa6 [was] not the analyst [of Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s sample].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 46. Counsel stated that, \xe2\x80\x9chad [she] known that the analyst [who tested Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s blood] was not available,\xe2\x80\x9d her opening, indeed, her entire defense \xe2\x80\x9cmay very well have been dramatically different.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 47. The State, however, proposed to introduce Caylor\xe2\x80\x99s finding as a \xe2\x80\x9cbusiness record\xe2\x80\x9d during the testimony of Gerasimos Razatos, an SLD scientist who had neither observed nor reviewed Caylor\xe2\x80\x99s analysis. Id., at 44.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s counsel opposed the State\xe2\x80\x99s proposal. Id., at 44\xe2\x80\x9345. Without Caylor\xe2\x80\x99s testimony, defense counsel maintained, introduction of the analyst\xe2\x80\x99s finding would violate Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s Sixth Amendment right \xe2\x80\x9cto be confronted with the witnesses against him.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.[Footnote 2] The trial court overruled the objection, id., at 46\xe2\x80\x9347, and admitted the SLD report as a business record, id., at 44\xe2\x80\x9346, 57.[Footnote 3] The jury convicted Bullcoming of aggravated DWI, and the New Mexico Court of Appeals upheld the conviction, concluding that \xe2\x80\x9cthe blood alcohol report in the present case was non-testimonial and prepared routinely with guarantees of trustworthiness.\xe2\x80\x9d 2008\xe2\x80\x93NMCA\xe2\x80\x93097, \xc2\xa717, 189 P.\xc2\xa03d 679, 685.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s appeal was pending before the New Mexico Supreme Court, this Court decided Melendez-Diaz. In that case, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Massachusetts courts [had] admitted into evidence affidavits reporting the results of forensic analysis which showed that material seized by the police and connected to the defendant was cocaine.\xe2\x80\x9d 557 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 1). Those affidavits, the Court held, were \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98testimonial,\xe2\x80\x99 rendering the affiants \xe2\x80\x98witnesses\xe2\x80\x99 subject to the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s right of confrontation under the Sixth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In light of Melendez-Diaz, the New Mexico Supreme Court acknowledged that the blood-alcohol report introduced at Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s trial qualified as testimonial evidence. Like the affidavits in Melendez-Diaz, the court observed, the report was \xe2\x80\x9cfunctionally identical to live, in-court testimony, doing precisely what a witness does on direct examination.\xe2\x80\x9d 226 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 8 (quoting Melendez-Diaz, 557 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 4)).[Footnote 4] Nevertheless, for two reasons, the court held that admission of the report did not violate the Confrontation Clause.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, the court said certifying analyst Caylor \xe2\x80\x9cwas a mere scrivener,\xe2\x80\x9d who \xe2\x80\x9csimply transcribed the results gen-erated by the gas chromatograph machine.\xe2\x80\x9d 226 P.\xc2\xa03d, \nat 8\xe2\x80\x939. Second, SLD analyst Razatos, although he did not participate in testing Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s blood, \xe2\x80\x9cqualified as \nan expert witness with respect to the gas chromatograph machine.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 9. \xe2\x80\x9cRazatos provided live, in-court testimony,\xe2\x80\x9d the court stated, \xe2\x80\x9cand, thus, was available for cross-examination regarding the operation of the \xe2\x80\xa6 machine, the results of [Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s] BAC test, and the SLD\xe2\x80\x99s established laboratory procedures.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Razatos\xe2\x80\x99 testimony was crucial, the court explained, because Bullcoming could not cross-examine the machine or the written report. Id., at 10. But \xe2\x80\x9c[Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s] right of confrontation was preserved,\xe2\x80\x9d the court concluded, because Razatos was a qualified analyst, able to serve as a surrogate for Caylor. Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari to address this question: Does the Confrontation Clause permit the prosecution to introduce a forensic laboratory report containing a testimonial certification, made in order to prove a fact at a criminal trial, through the in-court testimony of an analyst who did not sign the certification or personally perform or observe the performance of the test reported in the certification. 561 U. S. ___ (2010). Our answer is in line with controlling precedent: As a rule, if an out-of-court statement is testimonial in nature, it may not be introduced against the accused at trial unless the witness who made the statement is unavailable and the accused has had a prior opportunity to confront that witness. Because the New Mexico Supreme Court permitted the testimonial statement of one witness, i.e., Caylor, to enter into evidence through the in-court testimony of a second person, i.e., Razatos, we reverse that court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Sixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Confrontation Clause confers upon the accused \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n all criminal prosecutions, \xe2\x80\xa6 the right \xe2\x80\xa6 to be confronted with the witnesses against him.\xe2\x80\x9d In a pathmarking 2004 decision, Crawford v. Washington, we overruled Ohio v. Roberts, 448 U. S. 56 (1980), which had interpreted the Confrontation Clause to allow admission of absent witnesses\xe2\x80\x99 testimonial statements based on a judicial determination of reliability. See Roberts, 448 U. S., at 66. Rejecting Roberts\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9camorphous notions of \xe2\x80\x98re-liability,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Crawford, 541 U. S., at 61, Crawford held \nthat fidelity to the Confrontation Clause permitted admission of \xe2\x80\x9c[t]estimonial statements of witnesses absent from trial \xe2\x80\xa6 only where the declarant is unavailable, and only where the defendant has had a prior opportunity to cross-examine,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 59. See Michigan v. Bryant, 562 U. S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 7) (\xe2\x80\x9c[F]or testimonial evidence to be admissible, the Sixth Amendment \xe2\x80\x98demands what the common law required: unavailability [of the witness] and a prior opportunity for cross-examination.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Crawford, 541 U. S., at 68)). Melendez-Diaz, relying on Crawford\xe2\x80\x99s rationale, refused to create a \xe2\x80\x9cforensic evidence\xe2\x80\x9d exception to this rule. 557 U. S., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 11\xe2\x80\x9315).[Footnote 5] An analyst\xe2\x80\x99s certification prepared in connection with a criminal investigation or prosecution, the Court held, is \xe2\x80\x9ctestimonial,\xe2\x80\x9d and therefore within the compass of the Confrontation Clause. Id., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 15\xe2\x80\x9318).[Footnote 6]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The State in the instant case never asserted that the analyst who signed the certification, Curtis Caylor, was unavailable. The record showed only that Caylor was placed on unpaid leave for an undisclosed reason. See supra, at 5. Nor did Bullcoming have an opportunity \nto cross-examine Caylor. Crawford and Melendez-Diaz, therefore, weigh heavily in Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s favor. The New Mexico Supreme Court, however, although recognizing that the SLD report was testimonial for purposes of the Confrontation Clause, considered SLD analyst Razatos an adequate substitute for Caylor. We explain first why Razatos\xe2\x80\x99 appearance did not meet the Confrontation Clause requirement. We next address the State\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the SLD report ranks as \xe2\x80\x9cnontestimonial,\xe2\x80\x9d and therefore \xe2\x80\x9c[was] not subject to the Confrontation Clause\xe2\x80\x9d in the first place. Brief for Respondent 7 (capitalization omitted).

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The New Mexico Supreme Court held surrogate testimony adequate to satisfy the Confrontation Clause in \nthis case because analyst Caylor \xe2\x80\x9csimply transcribed the resul[t] generated by the gas chromatograph machine,\xe2\x80\x9d presenting no interpretation and exercising no independent judgment. 226 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 8. Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ctrue \xe2\x80\x98accuser,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the court said, was the machine, while testing analyst Caylor\xe2\x80\x99s role was that of \xe2\x80\x9cmere scrivener.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 9. Caylor\xe2\x80\x99s certification, however, reported more than a machine-generated number. See supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x934.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Caylor certified that he received Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s blood sample intact with the seal unbroken, that he checked to make sure that the forensic report number and the sample number \xe2\x80\x9ccorrespond[ed],\xe2\x80\x9d and that he performed on Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s sample a particular test, adhering to a precise protocol. App. 62\xe2\x80\x9365. He further represented, by leaving the \xe2\x80\x9c[r]emarks\xe2\x80\x9d section of the report blank, that no \xe2\x80\x9ccircumstance or condition \xe2\x80\xa6 affect[ed] the integrity of the sample or \xe2\x80\xa6 the validity of the analysis.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 62, 65. These representations, relating to past events and human actions not revealed in raw, machine-produced data, are meet for cross-examination.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The potential ramifications of the New Mexico Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning, furthermore, raise red flags. Most wit-nesses, after all, testify to their observations of factual conditions or events, e.g., \xe2\x80\x9cthe light was green,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cthe hour was noon.\xe2\x80\x9d Such witnesses may record, on the spot, what they observed. Suppose a police report recorded an objective fact\xe2\x80\x94Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s counsel posited the address above the front door of a house or the read-out of a radar gun. See Brief for Petitioner 35. Could an officer other than the one who saw the number on the house or gun present the information in court\xe2\x80\x94so long as that officer was equipped to testify about any technology the observing officer deployed and the police department\xe2\x80\x99s standard operating procedures? As our precedent makes plain, the answer is emphatically \xe2\x80\x9cNo.\xe2\x80\x9d See Davis v. Washington, 547 U. S. 813, 826 (2006) (Confrontation Clause may not be \xe2\x80\x9cevaded by having a note-taking police[ officer] recite the \xe2\x80\xa6 testimony of the declarant\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis deleted)); Melendez-Diaz, 557 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 6) (Kennedy, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Court made clear in Davis that it will not permit the testimonial statement of one witness to enter into evidence through the in-court testimony of a second.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The New Mexico Supreme Court stated that the number registered by the gas chromatograph machine called for \nno interpretation or exercise of independent judgment on Caylor\xe2\x80\x99s part. 226 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 8\xe2\x80\x939. We have already explained that Caylor certified to more than a machine-generated number. See supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x934. In any event, the comparative reliability of an analyst\xe2\x80\x99s testimonial report drawn from machine-produced data does not overcome the Sixth Amendment bar. This Court settled in Crawford that the \xe2\x80\x9cobviou[s] reliab[ility]\xe2\x80\x9d of a testimonial statement does not dispense with the Confrontation Clause. 541 U. S., at 62; see id., at 61 (Clause \xe2\x80\x9ccommands, not that evidence be reliable, but that reliability be assessed in a particular manner: by testing [the evidence] in the crucible of cross-examination\xe2\x80\x9d). Accordingly, the analysts who write reports that the prosecution introduces must be made available for confrontation even if they possess \xe2\x80\x9cthe scientific acumen of Mme. Curie and the veracity of Mother Teresa.\xe2\x80\x9d Melendez-Diaz, 557 U. S., at ___, n.\xc2\xa06 (slip op., at 14, n.\xc2\xa06).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Recognizing that admission of the blood-alcohol analysis depended on \xe2\x80\x9clive, in-court testimony [by] a qualified ana-lyst,\xe2\x80\x9d 226 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 10, the New Mexico Supreme Court believed that Razatos could substitute for Caylor because Razatos \xe2\x80\x9cqualified as an expert witness with respect to the gas chromatograph machine and the SLD\xe2\x80\x99s labora-\ntory procedures,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 9. But surrogate testimony of the kind Razatos was equipped to give could not convey what Caylor knew or observed about the events his certification concerned, i.e., the particular test and testing process he employed.[Footnote 7] Nor could such surrogate testimony expose any lapses or lies on the certifying analyst\xe2\x80\x99s part.[Footnote 8] Significant here, Razatos had no knowledge of the reason why Caylor had been placed on unpaid leave. With Caylor on the stand, Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s counsel could have asked questions designed to reveal whether incompetence, evasiveness, or dishonesty accounted for Caylor\xe2\x80\x99s removal from his work station. Notable in this regard, the State never asserted that Caylor was \xe2\x80\x9cunavailable\xe2\x80\x9d; the prosecution conveyed only that Caylor was on uncompensated leave. Nor did the State assert that Razatos had any \xe2\x80\x9cindependent opinion\xe2\x80\x9d concerning Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s BAC. See Brief for Respondent 58, n.\xc2\xa015. In this light, Caylor\xe2\x80\x99s live testimony could hardly be typed \xe2\x80\x9ca hollow formality,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 4.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0More fundamentally, as this Court stressed in Crawford, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he text of the Sixth Amendment does not sug-\ngest any open-ended exceptions from the confrontation re-quirement to be developed by the courts.\xe2\x80\x9d 541 U. S., at 54. Nor is it \xe2\x80\x9cthe role of courts to extrapolate from the words of the [Confrontation Clause] to the values behind it, and then to enforce its guarantees only to the extent they serve (in the courts\xe2\x80\x99 views) those underlying values.\xe2\x80\x9d Giles v. California, 554 U. S. 353, 375 (2008). Accordingly, the Clause does not tolerate dispensing with confrontation simply because the court believes that questioning one witness about another\xe2\x80\x99s testimonial statements provides a fair enough opportunity for cross-examination.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A recent decision involving another Sixth Amendment right\xe2\x80\x94the right to counsel\xe2\x80\x94is instructive. In United States v. Gonzalez-Lopez, 548 U. S. 140 (2006), the Government argued that illegitimately denying a defendant his counsel of choice did not violate the Sixth Amend-\nment where \xe2\x80\x9csubstitute counsel\xe2\x80\x99s performance\xe2\x80\x9d did not demonstrably prejudice the defendant. Id., at 144\xe2\x80\x93145. This Court rejected the Government\xe2\x80\x99s argument. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]rue enough,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court explained, \xe2\x80\x9cthe purpose of the rights set forth in [the Sixth] Amendment is to ensure a fair trial; but it does not follow that the rights can be disregarded so long as the trial is, on the whole, fair.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 145. If a \xe2\x80\x9cparticular guarantee\xe2\x80\x9d of the Sixth Amendment is violated, no substitute procedure can cure the violation, and \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o additional showing of prejudice is required to make the violation \xe2\x80\x98complete.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 146. If repre-sentation by substitute counsel does not satisfy the Sixth Amendment, neither does the opportunity to confront a substitute witness.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In short, when the State elected to introduce Caylor\xe2\x80\x99s certification, Caylor became a witness Bullcoming had the right to confront. Our precedent cannot sensibly be read any other way. See Melendez-Diaz, 557 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 6) (Kennedy, J., dissenting) (Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding means \xe2\x80\x9cthe \xe2\x80\xa6 analyst who must testify is the person who signed the certificate\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We turn, finally, to the State\xe2\x80\x99s contention that the SLD\xe2\x80\x99s blood-alcohol analysis reports are nontestimonial in character, therefore no Confrontation Clause question even arises in this case. Melendez-Diaz left no room for that argument, the New Mexico Supreme Court concluded, \nsee 226 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 7\xe2\x80\x938; supra, at 7, a conclusion we find inescapable.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Melendez-Diaz, a state forensic laboratory, on police request, analyzed seized evidence (plastic bags) and reported the laboratory\xe2\x80\x99s analysis to the police (the substance found in the bags contained cocaine). 557 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 2). The \xe2\x80\x9ccertificates of analysis\xe2\x80\x9d prepared by the analysts who tested the evidence in Melendez-Diaz, this Court held, were \xe2\x80\x9cincontrovertibly \xe2\x80\xa6 affirmation[s] made for the purpose of establishing or proving some fact\xe2\x80\x9d in a criminal proceeding. Id., at ___ (slip op., at 4) (internal quotation marks omitted). The same purpose was served by the certificate in question here.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The State maintains that the affirmations made by analyst Caylor were not \xe2\x80\x9cadversarial\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cinquisitorial,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 27\xe2\x80\x9333; instead, they were simply observations of an \xe2\x80\x9cindependent scientis[t]\xe2\x80\x9d made \xe2\x80\x9caccording to a non-adversarial public duty,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 32\xe2\x80\x9333. That argument fares no better here than it did in Melendez-Diaz. A document created solely for an \xe2\x80\x9cevidentiary purpose,\xe2\x80\x9d Melendez-Diaz clarified, made in aid of a police investigation, ranks as testimonial. 557 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 5) (forensic reports available for use at trial are \xe2\x80\x9ctestimonial statements\xe2\x80\x9d and certifying analyst is a \n\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98witness\xe2\x80\x99 for purposes of the Sixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Distinguishing Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s case from Melendez-Diaz, where the analysts\xe2\x80\x99 findings were contained in certificates \xe2\x80\x9csworn to before a notary public,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at ___ (slip op., at 2), the State emphasizes that the SLD report of Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s BAC was \xe2\x80\x9cunsworn.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 13; post, at 2 (\xe2\x80\x9conly sworn statement\xe2\x80\x9d here was that of Razatos, \xe2\x80\x9cwho was present and [did] testif[y]\xe2\x80\x9d). As the New Mexico Supreme Court recognized, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the absence of [an] oath [i]s not dispositive\xe2\x80\x99 in determining if a statement is testimonial.\xe2\x80\x9d 226 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 8 (quoting Crawford, 541 U. S., at 52). Indeed, in Crawford, this Court rejected as untenable any construction of the Confrontation Clause that would render inadmissible only sworn ex parte affidavits, while leaving admission of formal, but unsworn statements \xe2\x80\x9cperfectly OK.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 52\xe2\x80\x9353, n.\xc2\xa03. Reading the Clause in this \xe2\x80\x9cimplausible\xe2\x80\x9d manner, ibid., the Court noted, would make the right to confrontation easily erasable. See Davis, 547 U. S., at 830\xe2\x80\x93831, n.\xc2\xa05; id., at 838 (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment in part and dissenting in part).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In all material respects, the laboratory report in this case resembles those in Melendez-Diaz. Here, as in Melendez-Diaz, a law-enforcement officer provided seized evidence to a state laboratory required by law to assist in police investigations, N.\xc2\xa0M. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa729\xe2\x80\x933\xe2\x80\x934 (2004). Like the analysts in Melendez-Diaz, analyst Caylor tested the evidence and prepared a certificate concerning the result of his analysis. App. 62. Like the Melendez-Diaz certificates, Caylor\xe2\x80\x99s certificate is \xe2\x80\x9cformalized\xe2\x80\x9d in a signed document, Davis, 547 U. S., at 837, n.\xc2\xa02 (opinion of Thomas, J.), headed a \xe2\x80\x9creport,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 62. Noteworthy as well, the SLD report form contains a legend referring to municipal and magistrate courts\xe2\x80\x99 rules that provide for the admission of certified blood-alcohol analyses.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, the formalities attending the \xe2\x80\x9creport of blood alcohol analysis\xe2\x80\x9d are more than adequate to qualify Caylor\xe2\x80\x99s assertions as testimonial. The absence of notarization does not remove his certification from Confrontation Clause governance. The New Mexico Supreme Court, guided by Melendez-Diaz, correctly recognized that Caylor\xe2\x80\x99s report \xe2\x80\x9cfell within the core class of testimonial statements\xe2\x80\x9d 226 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 7, described in this Court\xe2\x80\x99s leading Confrontation Clause decisions: Melendez-Diaz, 557 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 4); Davis, 547 U. S., at 830; Crawford, 541 U. S., at 51\xe2\x80\x9352.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The State and its amici urge that unbending applica-tion of the Confrontation Clause to forensic evidence would impose an undue burden on the prosecution. This argument, also advanced in the dissent, post, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311, largely repeats a refrain rehearsed and rejected in Melendez-Diaz. See 557 U. S., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 19\xe2\x80\x9323). The con-stitutional requirement, we reiterate, \xe2\x80\x9cmay not [be] disregard[ed] \xe2\x80\xa6 at our convenience,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at ___ (slip op., at 19), and the predictions of dire consequences, we again observe, are dubious, see id., at ___ (slip op., at 19\xe2\x80\x9320).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0New Mexico law, it bears emphasis, requires the lab-oratory to preserve samples, which can be retested by other analysts, see N.\xc2\xa0M. Admin. Code \xc2\xa77.33.2.15(A)(4)\xe2\x80\x93(6) (2010), available at http://www.nmcpr.state.nm.us/nmac/\n_title07/T07C033.htm, and neither party questions SLD\xe2\x80\x99s compliance with that requirement. Retesting \xe2\x80\x9cis almost always an option \xe2\x80\xa6 in [DWI] cases,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Public Defender Service for District of Columbia et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 25 (hereinafter PDS Brief), and the State had that option here: New Mexico could have avoided any Confrontation Clause problem by asking Razatos to retest the sample, and then testify to the results of his retest rather than to the results of a test he did not conduct or observe.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Notably, New Mexico advocates retesting as an effective means to preserve a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s confrontation right \xe2\x80\x9cwhen the [out-of-court] statement is raw data or a mere transcription of raw data onto a public record.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 53\xe2\x80\x9354. But the State would require the defendant to initiate retesting. Id., at 55; post, at 4 (defense \xe2\x80\x9cremains free to \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. call and examine the technician who performed a test\xe2\x80\x9d), post, at 8 (\xe2\x80\x9cfree retesting\xe2\x80\x9d is available to defendants). The prosecution, however, bears the burden of proof. Melendez-Diaz, 557 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 19) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he Confrontation Clause imposes a burden on the prosecution to present its witnesses, not on the defendant to bring those adverse witnesses into court.\xe2\x80\x9d). Hence the obligation to propel retesting when the original analyst is unavailable is the State\xe2\x80\x99s, not the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s. See Taylor v. Illinois, 484 U. S. 400, 410, n. 14 (1988) (Confrontation Clause\xe2\x80\x99s requirements apply \xe2\x80\x9cin every case, whether or not the defendant seeks to rebut the case against him or to present a case of his own\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Furthermore, notice-and-demand procedures, long in ef-fect in many jurisdictions, can reduce burdens on foren-\nsic laboratories. Statutes governing these procedures typically \xe2\x80\x9crender \xe2\x80\xa6 otherwise hearsay forensic reports admissible[,] while specifically preserving a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s right to demand that the prosecution call the author/\nanalyst of [the] report.\xe2\x80\x9d PDS Brief 9; see Melendez-\nDiaz, 557 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 20) (observing that notice-and-demand statutes \xe2\x80\x9cpermit the defendant to assert (or forfeit by silence) his Confrontation Clause right after receiving notice of the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s intent to use a forensic analyst\xe2\x80\x99s report\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even before this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Crawford, moreover, it was common prosecutorial practice to call the forensic analyst to testify. Prosecutors did so \xe2\x80\x9cto bolster the persuasive power of [the State\xe2\x80\x99s] case[,] \xe2\x80\xa6 [even] when the defense would have preferred that the analyst did not testify.\xe2\x80\x9d PDS Brief 8.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We note also the \xe2\x80\x9csmall fraction of \xe2\x80\xa6 cases\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cactually proceed to trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Melendez-Diaz, 557 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 20) (citing estimate that \xe2\x80\x9cnearly 95% of convictions in state and federal courts are obtained via guilty plea\xe2\x80\x9d). And, \xe2\x80\x9cwhen cases in which forensic analysis has been conducted [do] go to trial,\xe2\x80\x9d defendants \xe2\x80\x9cregularly \xe2\x80\xa6 [stipulate] to the admission of [the] analysis.\xe2\x80\x9d PDS Brief 20. \xe2\x80\x9c[A]s a result, analysts testify in only a very small percentage of cases,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 21, for \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t is unlikely that defense counsel will insist on live testimony whose effect will be merely to highlight rather than cast doubt upon the forensic analysis.\xe2\x80\x9d Melendez-Diaz, 557 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 22).[Footnote 9]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Tellingly, in jurisdictions in which \xe2\x80\x9cit is the [acknowledged] job of \xe2\x80\xa6 analysts to testify in court \xe2\x80\xa6 about their test results,\xe2\x80\x9d the sky has not fallen. PDS Brief 23. State and municipal laboratories \xe2\x80\x9cmake operational and staffing decisions\xe2\x80\x9d to facilitate analysts\xe2\x80\x99 appearance at trial. Ibid. Prosecutors schedule trial dates to accommodate analysts\xe2\x80\x99 availability, and trial courts liberally grant continuances when unexpected conflicts arise. Id., at 24\xe2\x80\x9325. In rare cases in which the analyst is no longer employed by the laboratory at the time of trial, \xe2\x80\x9cthe prosecution makes the effort to bring that analyst \xe2\x80\xa6 to court.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 25. And, as is the practice in New Mexico, see supra, at 16, laboratories ordinarily retain additional samples, enabling them to run tests again when necessary.[Footnote 10]

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Sotomayor, concurring in part.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I agree with the Court that the trial court erred by admitting the blood alcohol concentration (BAC) report. I write separately first to highlight why I view the report at issue to be testimonial\xe2\x80\x94specifically because its \xe2\x80\x9cprimary purpose\xe2\x80\x9d is evidentiary\xe2\x80\x94and second to emphasize the limited reach of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under our precedents, the New Mexico Supreme Court was correct to hold that the certified BAC report in this case is testimonial. 2010\xe2\x80\x93NMSC\xe2\x80\x93007, \xc2\xb618, 226 P.\xc2\xa03d 1, 8.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To determine if a statement is testimonial, we must decide whether it has \xe2\x80\x9ca primary purpose of creating an out-of-court substitute for trial testimony.\xe2\x80\x9d Michigan v. Bryant, 562 U. S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 11). When the \xe2\x80\x9cprimary purpose\xe2\x80\x9d of a statement is \xe2\x80\x9cnot to create a record for trial,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., \xe2\x80\x9cthe admissibility of [the] statement is the concern of state and federal rules of evidence, not the Confrontation Clause,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at ___ (slip op., at 12).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This is not the first time the Court has faced the question of whether a scientific report is testimonial. As the Court explains, ante, at 14\xe2\x80\x9315, in Melendez-Diaz v. Massachusetts, 557 U. S. ___ (2009), we held that \xe2\x80\x9ccertificates of analysis,\xe2\x80\x9d completed by employees of the State Laboratory Institute of the Massachusetts Department of Public Health, id., at ___ (slip op., at 2), were testimonial because they were \xe2\x80\x9cincontrovertibly \xe2\x80\xa6 \xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9csolemn declaration[s] or affirmation[s] made for the purpose of establishing or proving some fact,\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d id., at ___ (slip op., at 4) (quoting Crawford v. Washington, 541 U. S. 36, 51 (2004), in turn quoting 2 N. Webster, An American Dictionary of the English Language (1828)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As we explained earlier this Term in Michigan v. Bryant, 562 U. S. ___ (2010), \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n making the primary purpose determination, standard rules of hearsay \xe2\x80\xa6 will be relevant.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 11\xe2\x80\x9312).[Footnote 1] As applied to a scientific report, Melendez-Diaz explained that pursuant to Federal Rule of Evidence 803, \xe2\x80\x9c[d]ocuments kept in the regular course of business may ordinarily be admitted at trial despite their hearsay status,\xe2\x80\x9d except \xe2\x80\x9cif the regularly conducted business activity is the production of evidence for use at trial.\xe2\x80\x9d 557 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 15\xe2\x80\x9316)\n(citing Fed. Rule Evid. 803(6)). In that circumstance, the hearsay rules bar admission of even business records. Re-latedly, in the Confrontation Clause context, business\nand public records \xe2\x80\x9care generally admissible absent confrontation \xe2\x80\xa6 because\xe2\x80\x94having been created for the administration of an entity\xe2\x80\x99s affairs and not for the purpose of establishing or proving some fact at trial\xe2\x80\x94they are not testimonial.\xe2\x80\x9d Melendez-Diaz, 557 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 18). We concluded, therefore, that because the purpose of the certificates of analysis was use at trial, they were not properly admissible as business or public records under the hearsay rules, id., at ___ (slip op., at 15\xe2\x80\x9316), nor were they admissible under the Confrontation Clause, id., at ___ (slip op., at 18). The hearsay rule\xe2\x80\x99s recognition of the certificates\xe2\x80\x99 evidentiary purpose thus confirmed our decision that the certificates were testimonial under the primary purpose analysis required by the Confrontation Clause. See id., at ___ (slip op., at 5) (explaining that under Massachusetts law not just the purpose but the \xe2\x80\x9csole purpose of the affidavits was to provide\xe2\x80\x9d evidence).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Similarly, in this case, for the reasons the Court sets forth the BAC report and Caylor\xe2\x80\x99s certification on it clearly have a \xe2\x80\x9cprimary purpose of creating an out-of-court substitute for trial testimony.\xe2\x80\x9d Bryant, 562 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 11). The Court also explains why the BAC report is not materially distinguishable from the certificates we held testimonial in Melendez-Diaz. See 557 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 2, 4\xe2\x80\x935).\xc2\xa0[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The formality inherent in the certification further suggests its evidentiary purpose. Although \xe2\x80\x9c[f]ormality is not the sole touchstone of our primary purpose inquiry,\xe2\x80\x9d a statement\xe2\x80\x99s formality or informality can shed light on whether a particular statement has a primary purpose\nof use at trial. Bryant, 562 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 19).\xc2\xa0[Footnote 3] \nI agree with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s assessment that the certificate at issue here is a formal statement, despite the absence of notarization. Ante, at 14\xe2\x80\x9315; Crawford, 541 U. S., at 52 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he absence of [an] oath [is] not dispositive\xe2\x80\x9d). The formality derives from the fact that the analyst is asked to sign his name and \xe2\x80\x9ccertify\xe2\x80\x9d to both the result and the statements on the form. A \xe2\x80\x9ccertification\xe2\x80\x9d requires one\n\xe2\x80\x9c[t]o attest\xe2\x80\x9d that the accompanying statements are true. Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 258 (9th ed. 2009) (definition of \xe2\x80\x9ccertify\xe2\x80\x9d); see also id., at 147 (defining \xe2\x80\x9cattest\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o bear witness; testify,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o affirm to be true or genuine; to authenticate by signing as a witness\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, I am compelled to conclude that the report has a \xe2\x80\x9cprimary purpose of creating an out-of-court substitute for trial testimony,\xe2\x80\x9d Bryant, 562 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 11), which renders it testimonial.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After holding that the report was testimonial, the New Mexico Supreme Court nevertheless held that its admission was permissible under the Confrontation Clause for two reasons: because Caylor was a \xe2\x80\x9cmere scrivener,\xe2\x80\x9d and because Razatos could be cross-examined on the workings of the gas chromatograph and laboratory procedures. 226 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 8\xe2\x80\x9310. The Court convincingly explains why those rationales are incorrect. Ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9313. Therefore, the New Mexico court contravened our precedents in holding that the report was admissible via Razatos\xe2\x80\x99 testimony.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although this case is materially indistinguishable from the facts we considered in Melendez-Diaz, I highlight \nsome of the factual circumstances that this case does not present.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, this is not a case in which the State suggested an alternate purpose, much less an alternate primary purpose, for the BAC report. For example, the State has not claimed that the report was necessary to provide Bullcoming with medical treatment. See Bryant, 562 U. S., at ___ , n.\xc2\xa09 (slip op., at 15, n.\xc2\xa09) (listing \xe2\x80\x9cStatements for Purposes of Medical Diagnosis or Treatment\xe2\x80\x9d under Federal Rule of Evidence 803(4) as an example of statements that are\n\xe2\x80\x9cby their nature, made for a purpose other than use in a prosecution\xe2\x80\x9d); Melendez-Diaz, 557 U. S., at ___, n. 2 (slip op., at 6, n.\xc2\xa02) (\xe2\x80\x9c[M]edical reports created for treatment purposes \xe2\x80\xa6 would not be testimonial under our decision today\xe2\x80\x9d); Giles v. California, 554 U. S. 353, 376 (2008) (\xe2\x80\x9c[S]tatements to physicians in the course of receiving treatment would be excluded, if at all, only by hearsay rules\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, this is not a case in which the person testifying is a supervisor, reviewer, or someone else with a personal, albeit limited, connection to the scientific test at issue. Razatos conceded on cross-examination that he played no role in producing the BAC report and did not observe any portion of Curtis Caylor\xe2\x80\x99s conduct of the testing. App. 58. The court below also recognized Razatos\xe2\x80\x99 total lack of connection to the test at issue. 226 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 6. It would be a different case if, for example, a supervisor who observed an analyst conducting a test testified about the results or a report about such results. We need not address what degree of involvement is sufficient because here Razatos had no involvement whatsoever in the relevant test and report.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Third, this is not a case in which an expert witness was asked for his independent opinion about underlying testimonial reports that were not themselves admitted into evidence. See Fed. Rule Evid. 703 (explaining that facts or data of a type upon which experts in the field would reasonably rely in forming an opinion need not be admissible in order for the expert\xe2\x80\x99s opinion based on the facts and data to be admitted). As the Court notes, ante, at 12, the State does not assert that Razatos offered an independent, expert opinion about Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s blood alcohol concentration. Rather, the State explains, \xe2\x80\x9c[a]side from reading a report that was introduced as an exhibit, Mr. Razatos offered no opinion about Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s blood alcohol content \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 58, n.\xc2\xa015 (citation omitted). Here the State offered the BAC report, including Caylor\xe2\x80\x99s testimonial statements, into evidence. We would face a different question if asked to determine the constitutionality of allowing an expert witness to discuss others\xe2\x80\x99 testimonial statements if the testimonial statements were not themselves admitted as evidence.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, this is not a case in which the State introduced only machine-generated results, such as a printout from a gas chromatograph. The State here introduced Caylor\xe2\x80\x99s statements, which included his transcription of a blood alcohol concentration, apparently copied from a gas chromatograph printout, along with other statements about the procedures used in handling the blood sample. See ante, at 10; App. 62 (\xe2\x80\x9cI certify that I followed the procedures set out on the reverse of this report, and the statements in this block are correct\xe2\x80\x9d). Thus, we do not decide whether, as the New Mexico Supreme Court suggests, 226 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 10, a State could introduce (assuming an adequate chain of custody foundation) raw data generated by a machine in conjunction with the testimony of an expert witness. See Reply Brief for Petitioner 16, n.\xc2\xa05.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case does not present, and thus the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion does not address, any of these factual scenarios.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As in Melendez-Diaz, the primary purpose of the BAC report is clearly to serve as evidence. It is therefore tes-timonial, and the trial court erred in allowing the State\nto introduce it into evidence via Razatos\xe2\x80\x99 testimony. I respectfully concur.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Kennedy, with whom the Chief Justice, Justice Breyer, and Justice Alito join, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Sixth Amendment Confrontation Clause binds the States and the National Government. Pointer v. Texas, 380 U. S. 400, 403 (1965). Two Terms ago, in a case arising from a state criminal prosecution, the Court interpreted the Clause to mandate exclusion of a laboratory report sought to be introduced based on the authority of that report\xe2\x80\x99s own sworn statement that a test had been performed yielding the results as shown. Melendez-Diaz v. Massachusetts, 557 U. S. ___ (2009). The Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in that case held the report inadmissible because no one was present at trial to testify to its contents.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Whether or not one agrees with the reasoning and the result in Melendez-Diaz, the Court today takes the new and serious misstep of extending that holding to instances like this one. Here a knowledgeable representative of the laboratory was present to testify and to explain the lab\xe2\x80\x99s processes and the details of the report; but because he was not the analyst who filled out part of the form and transcribed onto it the test result from a machine printout, the Court finds a confrontation violation. Some of the principal objections to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s underlying theory have been set out earlier and need not be repeated here. See id., at ___ (Kennedy, J., dissenting). Additional reasons, applicable to the extension of that doctrine and to the new ruling in this case, are now explained in support of this respectful dissent.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Before today, the Court had not held that the Confrontation Clause bars admission of scientific findings when an employee of the testing laboratory authenticates the findings, testifies to the laboratory\xe2\x80\x99s methods and practices, and is cross-examined at trial. Far from replacing live testimony with \xe2\x80\x9csystematic\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cextrajudicial\xe2\x80\x9d examinations, Davis v. Washington, 547 U. S. 813, 835, 836 (2006) (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment in part and dissenting in part) (emphasis deleted and internal quotation marks omitted), these procedures are fully consistent with the Confrontation Clause and with well-established principles for ensuring that criminal trials are conducted in full accord with requirements of fairness and reliability and with the confrontation guarantee. They do not \xe2\x80\x9cresemble Marian proceedings.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 837.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The procedures followed here, but now invalidated by the Court, make live testimony rather than the \xe2\x80\x9csolemnity\xe2\x80\x9d of a document the primary reason to credit the labo-ratory\xe2\x80\x99s scientific results. Id., at 838. Unlike Melendez-Diaz, where the jury was asked to credit a labora-\ntory\xe2\x80\x99s findings based solely on documents that were\n\xe2\x80\x9cquite plainly affidavits,\xe2\x80\x9d 557 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 1) (Thomas, J., concurring) (internal quotation marks omitted), here the signature, heading, or legend on the document were routine authentication elements for a report that would be assessed and explained by in-court testimony subject to full cross-examination. The only sworn statement at issue was that of the witness who was present and who testified.

\n

\n\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The record reveals that the certifying analyst\xe2\x80\x99s role here was no greater than that of anyone else in the chain of custody. App. 56 (laboratory employee\xe2\x80\x99s testimony agreeing that \xe2\x80\x9conce the material is prepared and placed in the machine, you don\xe2\x80\x99t need any particular expertise to record the results\xe2\x80\x9d). The information contained in the report was the result of a scientific process comprising multiple participants\xe2\x80\x99 acts, each with its own evidentiary significance. These acts included receipt of the sample at the laboratory; recording its receipt; storing it; placing the sample into the testing device; transposing the printout of the results of the test onto the report; and review of the results. See Id., at 48\xe2\x80\x9356; see also Brief for State of New Mexico Dept. of Health Scientific Laboratory Division as Amicus Curiae 4 (hereinafter New Mexico Scientific Laboratory Brief) (\xe2\x80\x9cEach blood sample has original testing work by . . . as many as seve[n] analysts . . . .\xe2\x80\x9d); App. 62 (indicating that this case involved three laboratory analysts who, respectively, received, analyzed, and reviewed analysis of the sample); cf. Brief for State of Indiana et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae in Briscoe v. Virginia, O. T. 2009, No. 07\xe2\x80\x9311191, p. 10 (hereinafter Indiana Brief) (explaining that DNA analysis can involve the combined efforts of up to 40 analysts).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the New Mexico scientific laboratory where the blood sample was processed, analyses are run in batches involving 40\xe2\x80\x9360 samples. Each sample is identified by a computer-generated number that is not linked back to the file containing the name of the person from whom the sample came until after all testing is completed. See New Mexico Scientific Laboratory Brief 26. The analysis is mechanically performed by the gas chromatograph, which may operate\xe2\x80\x94as in this case\xe2\x80\x94after all the laboratory employees leave for the day. See id., at 17. And whatever the result, it is reported to both law enforcement and the defense. See id., at 36.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The representative of the testing laboratory whom the prosecution called was a scientific analyst named Mr. Razatos. He testified that he \xe2\x80\x9chelp[ed] in overseeing the administration of these programs throughout the State,\xe2\x80\x9d and he was qualified to answer questions concerning each of these steps. App. 49. The Court has held that the government need not produce at trial \xe2\x80\x9ceveryone who laid hands on the evidence,\xe2\x80\x9d Melendez-Diaz, supra, at ___, n.\xc2\xa01 (slip op., at 5, n.\xc2\xa01). Here, the defense used the opportunity in cross-examination to highlight the absence at trial of certain laboratory employees. Under questioning by Bullcoming\xe2\x80\x99s attorney, Razatos acknowledged that his name did not appear on the report; that he did not receive the sample, perform the analysis, or complete the review; and that he did not know the reason for some personnel decisions. App. 58. After weighing arguments from defense counsel concerning these admissions, and after con-sidering the testimony of Mr. Razatos, who knew the laboratory\xe2\x80\x99s protocols and processes, the jury found no reasonable doubt as to the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s guilt.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In these circumstances, requiring the State to call the technician who filled out a form and recorded the results of a test is a hollow formality. The defense remains free to challenge any and all forensic evidence. It may call and examine the technician who performed a test. And it may call other expert witnesses to explain that tests are not always reliable or that the technician might have made a mistake. The jury can then decide whether to credit the test, as it did here. The States, furthermore, can assess the progress of scientific testing and enact or adopt statutes and rules to ensure that only reliable evidence is admitted. Rejecting these commonsense arguments and the concept that reliability is a legitimate concern, the Court today takes a different course. It once more assumes for itself a central role in mandating detailed evidentiary rules, thereby extending and confirming Melendez-Diaz\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cvast potential to disrupt criminal procedures.\xe2\x80\x9d 557 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 3) (Kennedy, J., dissenting).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The protections in the Confrontation Clause, and indeed the Sixth Amendment in general, are designed to ensure\na fair trial with reliable evidence. But the Crawford v. Washington, 541 U. S. 36 (2004), line of cases has treated the reliability of evidence as a reason to exclude it. Id., at 61\xe2\x80\x9362. Today, for example, the Court bars admission of a lab report because it \xe2\x80\x9cis formalized in a signed document.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 15 (internal quotation marks omitted). The Court\xe2\x80\x99s unconventional and unstated premise is that\nthe State\xe2\x80\x94by acting to ensure a statement\xe2\x80\x99s reliability\xe2\x80\x94makes the statement more formal and therefore less likely to be admitted. Park, Is Confrontation the Bottom Line? 19 Regent U. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 459, 461 (2007). That is so, the Court insists, because reliability does not animate the Confrontation Clause. Ante, at 11; Melendez-Diaz, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 11\xe2\x80\x9312); Crawford, supra, at 61\xe2\x80\x9362. Yet just this Term the Court ruled that, in another confrontation context, reliability was an essential part of the constitutional inquiry. See Michigan v. Bryant, 562 U. S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2010) (slip op., at 11\xe2\x80\x9312, 14\xe2\x80\x9315).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Like reliability, other principles have weaved in and out of the Crawford jurisprudence. Solemnity has sometimes been dispositive, see Melendez-Diaz, 557 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 6); id., at ___ (slip op., at 1) (Thomas, J., concurring), and sometimes not, see Davis, 547 U. S., at 834\xe2\x80\x93837, 841 (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment in part and dissenting in part). So, too, with the elusive distinction between utterances aimed at proving past events, and those calculated to help police keep the peace. Compare Davis, supra, and Bryant, 562 U. S., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 24\xe2\x80\x9330), with id., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 5\xe2\x80\x939) (Scalia, J., dissenting).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is not even clear which witnesses\xe2\x80\x99 testimony could render a scientific report admissible under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s approach. Melendez-Diaz stated an inflexible rule: Where \xe2\x80\x9canalysts\xe2\x80\x99 affidavits\xe2\x80\x9d included \xe2\x80\x9ctestimonial statements,\xe2\x80\x9d defendants were \xe2\x80\x9centitled to be confronted with the analysts\xe2\x80\x9d themselves. 557 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 5) (internal quotation marks omitted). Now, the Court reveals, this rule is either less clear than it first appeared or too strict to be followed. A report is admissible, today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion states, if a \xe2\x80\x9clive witness competent to testify to the truth of the statements made in the report\xe2\x80\x9d appears. Ante, at 1. Such witnesses include not just the certifying analyst, but also any \xe2\x80\x9cscientist who . . . perform[ed] or observe[d] the test reported in the certification.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 2.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Today\xe2\x80\x99s majority is not committed in equal shares to a common set of principles in applying the holding of Crawford. Compare Davis, supra (opinion for the Court by Scalia, J.), with id., at 834 (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment in part and dissenting in part); and Bryant, supra, (opinion for the Court by Sotomayor, J.), with id., at ___ (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment), and id., at ___ (Scalia, J., dissenting), and id., at ___ (Ginsburg, J., dissenting); and ante, at ___ (slip op., at 1) (opinion of the Court), with ante, at ___ (slip op., at 1) (Sotomayor, J., concurring). That the Court in the wake of Crawford has had such trouble fashioning a clear vision of that case\xe2\x80\x99s meaning is unsettling; for Crawford binds every judge in every criminal trial in every local, state, and federal court in the Nation. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s prior decisions leave trial judges to \xe2\x80\x9cguess what future rules this Court will distill from the sparse constitutional text,\xe2\x80\x9d Melendez-Diaz, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 2) (Kennedy, J., dissenting), or to struggle to apply an \xe2\x80\x9camorphous, if not entirely subjective,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9chighly context-dependent inquiry\xe2\x80\x9d involving \xe2\x80\x9copen-ended balancing.\xe2\x80\x9d Bryant, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 15\xe2\x80\x9316) (Scalia, J., dissenting) (internal quotation marks omitted) (listing 11 factors relevant under the majority\xe2\x80\x99s approach).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The persistent ambiguities in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s approach are symptomatic of a rule not amenable to sensible applications. Procedures involving multiple participants illustrate the problem. In Melendez-Diaz the Court insisted that its opinion did not require everyone in the chain of custody to testify but then qualified that \xe2\x80\x9cwhat testimony is introduced must . . . be introduced live.\xe2\x80\x9d 557 U. S.,\nat ___, n. 1 (slip op., at 5, n.\xc2\xa01); ante, at 6, n. 2. This\ncould mean that a statement that evidence remained in law-enforcement custody is admissible if the statement\xe2\x80\x99s maker appears in court. If so, an intern at police headquarters could review the evidence log, declare that chain of custody was retained, and so testify. The rule could also be that that the intern\xe2\x80\x99s statement\xe2\x80\x94which draws on statements in the evidence log\xe2\x80\x94is inadmissible unless every officer who signed the log appears at trial. That rule, if applied to this case, would have conditioned admissibility of the report on the testimony of three or more identified witnesses. See App. 62. In other instances, 7 or even 40 witnesses could be required. See supra, at 3. The court has thus\xe2\x80\x94in its fidelity to Melendez-Diaz\xe2\x80\x94boxed itself into a choice of evils: render the Confrontation Clause pro forma or construe it so that its dictates are unworkable.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Crawford itself does not compel today\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion. It\nis true, as Crawford confirmed, that the Confrontation Clause seeks in part to bar the government from replicating trial procedures outside of public view. See 541 U. S., at 50; Bryant, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 11\xe2\x80\x9312). Crawford explained that the basic purpose of the Clause was to address the sort of abuses exemplified at the notorious treason trial of Sir Walter Raleigh. 541 U. S., at 51. On this view the Clause operates to bar admission of out-of-court statements obtained through formal interrogation in preparation for trial. The danger is that innocent defendants may be convicted on the basis of unreliable, untested statements by those who observed\xe2\x80\x94or claimed to have observed\xe2\x80\x94preparation for or commission of the crime. And, of course, those statements might not have been uttered at all or\xe2\x80\x94even if spoken\xe2\x80\x94might not have been true.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A rule that bars testimony of that sort, however, provides neither cause nor necessity to impose a constitutional bar on the admission of impartial lab reports like the instant one, reports prepared by experienced technicians in laboratories that follow professional norms and scientific protocols. In addition to the constitutional right to call witnesses in his own defense, the defendant in this case was already protected by checks on potential prosecutorial abuse such as free retesting for defendants; result-blind issuance of reports; testing by an independent agency; routine processes performed en masse, which reduce opportunities for targeted bias; and labs operating pursuant to scientific and professional norms and oversight. See Brief for Respondent 5, 14\xe2\x80\x9315, 41, 54; New Mexico Scientific Laboratory Brief 2, 26.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In addition to preventing the State from conducting ex parte trials, Crawford\xe2\x80\x99s rejection of the regime of Ohio v. Roberts, 448 U. S. 56 (1980), seemed to have two under-lying jurisprudential objectives. One was to delink the intricacies of hearsay law from a constitutional mandate; and the other was to allow the States, in their own courts and legislatures and without this Court\xe2\x80\x99s supervision, to explore and develop sensible, specific evidentiary rules pertaining to the admissibility of certain statements. These results were to be welcomed, for this Court lacks the experience and day-to-day familiarity with the trial process to suit it well to assume the role of national tribunal for rules of evidence. Yet far from pursuing these objectives, the Court rejects them in favor of their opposites.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Instead of freeing the Clause from reliance on hearsay doctrines, the Court has now linked the Clause with hearsay rules in their earliest, most rigid, and least refined formulations. See, e.g., Mosteller, Remaking Confrontation Clause and Hearsay Doctrine Under the Challenge\nof Child Sexual Abuse Prosecutions, 1993 U. Ill. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 691, 739\xe2\x80\x93740, 742, 744\xe2\x80\x93746; Gallanis, The Rise of Modern Evidence Law, 84 Iowa L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 499, 502\xe2\x80\x93503, 514\xe2\x80\x93515, 533\xe2\x80\x93537 (1999). In cases like Melendez-Diaz and this one, the Court has tied the Confrontation Clause to 18th century hearsay rules unleavened by principles tending to make those rules more sensible. Sklansky, Hearsay\xe2\x80\x99s Last Hurrah, 2009 S.\xc2\xa0Ct. Rev. 1, 5\xe2\x80\x936, 36. As a result, the Court has taken the Clause far beyond its most important ap-plication, which is to forbid sworn, ex parte, out-of-court statements by unconfronted and available witnesses who observed the crime and do not appear at trial.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, the States are not just at risk of having some of their hearsay rules reviewed by this Court. They often are foreclosed now from contributing to the formulation and enactment of rules that make trials fairer and more reliable. For instance, recent state laws allowing admission\nof well-documented and supported reports of abuse by women whose abusers later murdered them must give way, unless that abuser murdered with the specific purpose of foreclosing the testimony. Giles v. California, 554 U. S. 353 (2008); Sklansky, supra, at 14\xe2\x80\x9315. Whether those statutes could provide sufficient indicia of reliability and other safeguards to comply with the Confrontation Clause as it should be understood is, to be sure, an open question. The point is that the States cannot now participate in the development of this difficult part of the law.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In short, there is an ongoing, continued, and systemic displacement of the States and dislocation of the federal structure. Cf. Melendez-Diaz, supra, at ___, ___, ___ (slip op., at 2\xe2\x80\x933, 22\xe2\x80\x9323). If this Court persists in applying wooden formalism in order to bar reliable testimony offered by the prosecution\xe2\x80\x94testimony thought proper for many decades in state and federal courts committed to devising fair trial processes\xe2\x80\x94then the States might find it necessary and appropriate to enact statutes to accommodate this new, intrusive federal regime. If they do, those rules could remain on State statute books for decades, even if subsequent decisions of this Court were to better implement the objectives of Crawford. This underscores the disruptive, long-term structural consequences of decisions like the one the Court announces today.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0States also may decide it is proper and appropriate to enact statutes that require defense counsel to give advance notice if they are going to object to introduction of a report without the presence in court of the technician who prepared it. Indeed, today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion relies upon laws of that sort as a palliative to the disruption it is causing. Ante, at 17 (plurality opinion). It is quite unrealistic, however, to think that this will take away from the defense the incentives to insist on having the certifying analyst present. There is in the ordinary case that proceeds to trial no good reason for defense counsel to waive the right of confrontation as the Court now interprets it.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion repeats an assertion from Melendez-Diaz that its decision will not \xe2\x80\x9cimpose an undue burden on the prosecution.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16 (plurality opinion). But evidence to the contrary already has begun to mount. See, e.g., Brief for State of California et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 7 (explaining that the 10 toxicologists for the Los Angeles Police Department spent 782 hours at 261 court appearances during a 1-year period); Brief for National District Attorneys Assocation et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 23 (observing that each blood-alcohol analyst in California processes 3,220 cases per year on average). New and more rigorous empirical studies further detailing the unfortunate effects of Melendez-Diaz are sure to be forthcoming.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the meantime, New Mexico\xe2\x80\x99s experience exemplifies the problems ahead. From 2008 to 2010, subpoenas requiring New Mexico analysts to testify in impaired-driving cases rose 71%, to 1,600\xe2\x80\x94or 8 or 9 every workday. New Mexico Scientific Laboratory Brief 2. In a State that is the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s fifth largest by area and that employs just 10 total analysts, id., at 3, each analyst in blood alcohol cases recently received 200 subpoenas per year, id., at 33. The analysts now must travel great distances on most working days. The result has been, in the laboratory\xe2\x80\x99s words, \xe2\x80\x9cchaotic.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 5. And if the defense raises an objection and the analyst is tied up in another court proceeding; or on leave; or absent; or delayed in transit; or no longer employed; or ill; or no longer living, the defense gets a windfall. As a result, good defense attorneys will object in ever-greater numbers to a prosecution failure or inability to produce laboratory analysts at trial. The concomitant increases in subpoenas will further impede the state laboratory\xe2\x80\x99s ability to keep pace with its obligations. Scarce state resources could be committed to other urgent needs in the criminal justice system.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Seven years after its initiation, it bears remembering that the Crawford approach was not preordained. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s missteps have produced an interpretation of the word \xe2\x80\x9cwitness\xe2\x80\x9d at odds with its meaning elsewhere in the Constitution, including elsewhere in the Sixth Amendment, see Amar, Sixth Amendment First Principles, 84 Geo. L.\xc2\xa0J. 641, 647, 691\xe2\x80\x93696 (1996), and at odds with\nthe sound administration of justice. It is time to return\nto solid ground. A proper place to begin that return is to decline to extend Melendez-Diaz to bar the reliable, commonsense evidentiary framework the State sought to follow in this case.
