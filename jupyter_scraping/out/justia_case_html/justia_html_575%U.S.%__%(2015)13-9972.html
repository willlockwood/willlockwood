RODRIGUEZ v. UNITED STATES

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eighth circuit

\n

No. 13\xe2\x80\x939972.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 21, 2015\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 21, 2015

\n

Officer Struble, a K\xe2\x80\x939 officer, stopped petitioner Rodriguez for driving on a highway shoulder, a violation of Nebraska law. After Struble attended to everything relating to the stop, including, inter alia, checking the driver\xe2\x80\x99s licenses of Rodriguez and his passenger and issuing a warning for the traffic offense, he asked Rodriguez for permission to walk his dog around the vehicle. When Rodriguez refused, Struble detained him until a second officer arrived. Struble then retrieved his dog, who alerted to the presence of drugs in the vehicle. The ensuing search revealed methamphetamine. Seven or eight minutes elapsed from the time Struble issued the written warning until the dog alerted.

\n

\n \n \n \n Rodriguez was indicted on federal drug charges. He moved to suppress the evidence seized from the vehicle on the ground, among others, that Struble had prolonged the traffic stop without reasonable suspicion in order to conduct the dog sniff. The Magistrate Judge recommended denial of the motion. He found no reasonable suspicion supporting detention once Struble issued the written warning. Under Eighth Circuit precedent, however, he concluded that prolonging the stop by \xe2\x80\x9cseven to eight minutes\xe2\x80\x9d for the dog sniff was only a de minimis intrusion on Rodriguez\xe2\x80\x99s\n \nFourth Amendment rights and was for that reason permissible. The District Court then denied the motion to suppress. Rodriguez entered a conditional guilty plea and was sentenced to five years in prison. The Eighth Circuit affirmed. Noting that the seven or eight minute delay was an acceptable \xe2\x80\x9cde minimis intrusion on Rodriguez\xe2\x80\x99s personal liberty,\xe2\x80\x9d the court declined to reach the question whether Struble had reasonable suspicion to continue Rodriguez\xe2\x80\x99s detention after issuing the written warning.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0

\n

\n \n \n \n 1.\xc2\xa0Absent reasonable suspicion, police extension of a traffic stop in order to conduct a dog sniff violates the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s shield against unreasonable seizures.

\n

\n \n A routine traffic stop is more like a brief stop under Terry v. Ohio,\n\n392 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n, than an arrest, see, e.g., Arizona v. Johnson,\n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 323\n. Its tolerable duration is determined by the seizure\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cmission,\xe2\x80\x9d which is to address the traffic violation that warranted the stop, Illinois v. Caballes,\n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 405\n and attend to related safety concerns. Authority for the seizure ends when tasks tied to the traffic infraction are\xe2\x80\x94or reasonably should have been\xe2\x80\x94completed. The\n \nFourth Amendment may tolerate certain unrelated investigations that do not lengthen the roadside detention, Johnson, 555 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 327\xe2\x80\x93328 (questioning); Caballes, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 406, 408 (dog sniff), but a traffic stop \xe2\x80\x9cbecome[s] unlawful if it is prolonged beyond the time reasonably required to complete th[e] mission\xe2\x80\x9d of issuing a warning ticket, id., at 407.

\n

\n \n Beyond determining whether to issue a traffic ticket, an officer\xe2\x80\x99s mission during a traffic stop typically includes checking the driver\xe2\x80\x99s license, determining whether there are outstanding warrants against the driver, and inspecting the automobile\xe2\x80\x99s registration and proof of insurance. These checks serve the same objective as enforcement of the traffic code: ensuring that vehicles on the road are operated safely and responsibly. See Delaware v. Prouse,\n\n440 U.\xc2\xa0S. 648\n\xe2\x80\x93659. Lacking the same close connection to roadway safety as the ordinary inquiries, a dog sniff is not fairly characterized as part of the officer\xe2\x80\x99s traffic mission.

\n

\n \n In concluding that the de minimis intrusion here could be offset by the Government\xe2\x80\x99s interest in stopping the flow of illegal drugs, the Eighth Circuit relied on Pennsylvania v. Mimms,\n\n434 U.\xc2\xa0S. 106\n. The Court reasoned in Mimms that the government\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9clegitimate and weighty\xe2\x80\x9d interest in officer safety outweighed the \xe2\x80\x9cde minimis\xe2\x80\x9d additional intrusion of requiring a driver, lawfully stopped, to exit a vehicle, id., at 110\xe2\x80\x93111. The officer-safety interest recognized in Mimms, however, stemmed from the danger to the officer associated with the traffic stop itself. On-scene investigation into other crimes, in contrast, detours from the officer\xe2\x80\x99s traffic-control mission and therefore gains no support from Mimms.

\n

\n \n The Government\xe2\x80\x99s argument that an officer who completes all traffic-related tasks expeditiously should earn extra time to pursue an unrelated criminal investigation is unpersuasive, for a traffic stop \xe2\x80\x9cprolonged beyond\xe2\x80\x9d the time in fact needed for the officer to complete his traffic-based inquiries is \xe2\x80\x9cunlawful,\xe2\x80\x9d Caballes, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 407. The critical question is not whether the dog sniff occurs before or after the officer issues a ticket, but whether conducting the sniff adds time to the stop. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\n \n \n \n 2.\xc2\xa0The determination adopted by the District Court that detention for the dog sniff was not independently supported by individualized suspicion was not reviewed by the Eighth Circuit. That question therefore remains open for consideration on remand. P.\xc2\xa09.

\n

741 F.\xc2\xa03d 905, vacated and remanded.

SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

No. 13\xe2\x80\x939972

_________________

DENNYS RODRIGUEZ, PETITIONER v. UNITED STATES

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eighth circuit

[April 21, 2015]

Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

In Illinois v. Caballes,543 U. S. 405 (2005), this Court held that a dog sniff conducted during a lawful traffic stop does not violate the Fourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s proscription of unreasonable seizures. This case presents the question whether the Fourth Amendment tolerates a dog sniff conducted after completion of a traffic stop. We hold that a police stop exceeding the time needed to handle the matter for which the stop was made violates the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s shield against unreasonable seizures. A seizure justified only by a police-observed traffic violation, therefore, \xe2\x80\x9cbecome[s] unlawful if it is prolonged beyond the time reasonably required to complete th[e] mission\xe2\x80\x9d of issuing a ticket for the violation. Id., at 407. The Court so recognized in Caballes, and we adhere to the line drawn in that decision.

I

Just after midnight on March 27, 2012, police officer Morgan Struble observed a Mercury Mountaineer veer slowly onto the shoulder of Nebraska State Highway 275 for one or two seconds and then jerk back onto the road. Nebraska law prohibits driving on highway shoulders, see Neb. Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa760\xe2\x80\x936,142 (2010), and on that basis, Struble pulled the Mountaineer over at 12:06 a.m. Struble is a K\xe2\x80\x939 officer with the Valley Police Department in Ne-braska, and his dog Floyd was in his patrol car that night. Two men were in the Mountaineer: the driver, Dennys Rodriguez, and a front-seat passenger, Scott Pollman.

Struble approached the Mountaineer on the passenger\xe2\x80\x99s side. After Rodriguez identified himself, Struble asked him why he had driven onto the shoulder. Rodriguez replied that he had swerved to avoid a pothole. Struble then gathered Rodriguez\xe2\x80\x99s license, registration, and proof of insurance, and asked Rodriguez to accompany him to the patrol car. Rodriguez asked if he was required to do so, and Struble answered that he was not. Rodriguez decided to wait in his own vehicle.

After running a records check on Rodriguez, Struble returned to the Mountaineer. Struble asked passenger Pollman for his driver\xe2\x80\x99s license and began to question him about where the two men were coming from and where they were going. Pollman replied that they had traveled to Omaha, Nebraska, to look at a Ford Mustang that was for sale and that they were returning to Norfolk, Ne-braska. Struble returned again to his patrol car, where he completed a records check on Pollman, and called for a second officer. Struble then began writing a warning ticket for Rodriguez for driving on the shoulder of the road.

Struble returned to Rodriguez\xe2\x80\x99s vehicle a third time to issue the written warning. By 12:27 or 12:28 a.m., Struble had finished explaining the warning to Rodriguez, and had given back to Rodriguez and Pollman the documents obtained from them. As Struble later testified, at that point, Rodriguez and Pollman \xe2\x80\x9chad all their documents back and a copy of the written warning. I got all the reason[s] for the stop out of the way[,] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. took care of all the business.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 70.

Nevertheless, Struble did not consider Rodriguez \xe2\x80\x9cfree to leave.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 69\xe2\x80\x9370. Although justification for the traffic stop was \xe2\x80\x9cout of the way,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 70, Struble asked for permission to walk his dog around Rodriguez\xe2\x80\x99s vehicle. Rodriguez said no. Struble then instructed Rodriguez to turn off the ignition, exit the vehicle, and stand in front of the patrol car to wait for the second officer. Rodriguez complied. At 12:33 a.m., a deputy sheriff arrived. Struble retrieved his dog and led him twice around the Mountaineer. The dog alerted to the presence of drugs halfway through Struble\xe2\x80\x99s second pass. All told, seven or eight minutes had elapsed from the time Struble issued the written warning until the dog indicated the presence of drugs. A search of the vehicle revealed a large bag of methamphetamine.

Rodriguez was indicted in the United States District Court for the District of Nebraska on one count of possession with intent to distribute 50 grams or more of methamphetamine, in violation of 21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7841(a)(1) and (b)(1). He moved to suppress the evidence seized from his car on the ground, among others, that Struble had prolonged the traffic stop without reasonable suspicion in order to conduct the dog sniff.

After receiving evidence, a Magistrate Judge recommended that the motion be denied. The Magistrate Judge found no probable cause to search the vehicle independent of the dog alert. App. 100 (apart from \xe2\x80\x9cinformation given by the dog,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cOfficer Struble had [no]thing other than a rather large hunch\xe2\x80\x9d). He further found that no reasonable suspicion supported the detention once Struble issued the written warning. He concluded, however, that under Eighth Circuit precedent, extension of the stop by \xe2\x80\x9cseven to eight minutes\xe2\x80\x9d for the dog sniff was only a de minimis intrusion on Rodriguez\xe2\x80\x99s Fourth Amendment rights and was therefore permissible.

The District Court adopted the Magistrate Judge\xe2\x80\x99s factual findings and legal conclusions and denied Rodriguez\xe2\x80\x99s motion to suppress. The court noted that, in the Eighth Circuit, \xe2\x80\x9cdog sniffs that occur within a short time following the completion of a traffic stop are not constitutionally prohibited if they constitute only de minimis intrusions.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 114 (quoting United States v. Alexander, 448 F.\xc2\xa03d 1014, 1016 (CA8 2006)). The court thus agreed with the Magistrate Judge that the \xe2\x80\x9c7 to 10 minutes\xe2\x80\x9d added to the stop by the dog sniff \xe2\x80\x9cwas not of constitu-tional significance.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 114. Impelled by that decision, Rodriguez entered a conditional guilty plea and was sentenced to five years in prison.

The Eighth Circuit affirmed. The \xe2\x80\x9cseven- or eight-minute delay\xe2\x80\x9d in this case, the opinion noted, resembled delays that the court had previously ranked as permissible. 741 F.\xc2\xa03d 905, 907 (2014). The Court of Appeals thus ruled that the delay here constituted an acceptable \xe2\x80\x9cde minimis intrusion on Rodriguez\xe2\x80\x99s personal liberty.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 908. Given that ruling, the court declined to reach the question whether Struble had reasonable suspicion to continue Rodriguez\xe2\x80\x99s detention after issuing the written warning.

We granted certiorari to resolve a division among lower courts on the question whether police routinely may extend an otherwise-completed traffic stop, absent reason-able suspicion, in order to conduct a dog sniff. 573 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014). Compare, e.g., United States v. Morgan, 270 F.\xc2\xa03d 625, 632 (CA8 2001) (postcompletion delay of \xe2\x80\x9cwell under ten minutes\xe2\x80\x9d permissible), with, e.g., State v. Baker, 2010 UT 18, \xc2\xb613, 229 P.\xc2\xa03d 650, 658 (2010) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]ithout additional reasonable suspicion, the officer must allow the seized person to depart once the purpose of the stop has concluded.\xe2\x80\x9d).

II

A seizure for a traffic violation justifies a police investigation of that violation. \xe2\x80\x9c[A] relatively brief encounter,\xe2\x80\x9d a routine traffic stop is \xe2\x80\x9cmore analogous to a so-called \xe2\x80\x98Terry stop\xe2\x80\x99 .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. than to a formal arrest.\xe2\x80\x9d Knowles v. Iowa,525 U.\xc2\xa0S. 113,117 (1998) (quoting Berkemer v. McCarty,468 U.\xc2\xa0S. 420,439 (1984), in turn citing Terry v. Ohio,392 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1 (1968)). See also Arizona v. Johnson,555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 323,330 (2009). Like a Terry stop, the tolerable duration of police inquiries in the traffic-stop context is determined by the seizure\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cmission\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94to address the traffic violation that warranted the stop, Caballes, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 407, and attend to related safety concerns, infra, at 6\xe2\x80\x937. See also United States v. Sharpe,470 U.\xc2\xa0S. 675,685 (1985); Florida v. Royer,460 U.\xc2\xa0S. 491,500 (1983) (plurality opinion) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe scope of the detention must be carefully tailored to its underlying justification.\xe2\x80\x9d). Because addressing the infraction is the purpose of the stop, it may \xe2\x80\x9clast no longer than is necessary to effectuate th[at] purpose.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. See also Caballes, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 407. Authority for the seizure thus ends when tasks tied to the traffic infraction are\xe2\x80\x94or reasonably should have been\xe2\x80\x94completed. See Sharpe, 470 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 686 (in determining the reasonable duration of a stop, \xe2\x80\x9cit [is] appropriate to examine whether the police diligently pursued [the] investigation\xe2\x80\x9d).

Our decisions in Caballes and Johnson heed these constraints. In both cases, we concluded that the Fourth Amendment tolerated certain unrelated investigations that did not lengthen the roadside detention. Johnson, 555 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 327\xe2\x80\x93328 (questioning); Caballes, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 406, 408 (dog sniff). In Caballes, however, we cautioned that a traffic stop \xe2\x80\x9ccan become unlawful if it is prolonged beyond the time reasonably required to complete th[e] mission\xe2\x80\x9d of issuing a warning ticket. 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 407. And we repeated that admonition in Johnson: The seizure remains lawful only \xe2\x80\x9cso long as [unrelated] inquiries do not measurably extend the duration of the stop.\xe2\x80\x9d 555 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 333. See also Muehler v. Mena,544 U.\xc2\xa0S. 93,101 (2005) (because unrelated inquiries did not \xe2\x80\x9cexten[d] the time [petitioner] was detained[,] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. no additional Fourth Amendment justification .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. was required\xe2\x80\x9d). An officer, in other words, may conduct certain unrelated checks during an otherwise lawful traffic stop. But contrary to Justice Alito\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion, post, at 4, n.\xc2\xa02, he may not do so in a way that prolongs the stop, absent the reasonable suspicion ordinarily demanded to justify detaining an individ-ual. But see post, at 1\xe2\x80\x932 (Alito, J., dissenting) (premising opinion on the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s own finding of \xe2\x80\x9creasonable suspicion,\xe2\x80\x9d although the District Court reached the opposite conclusion, and the Court of Appeals declined to consider the issue).

Beyond determining whether to issue a traffic ticket, an officer\xe2\x80\x99s mission includes \xe2\x80\x9cordinary inquiries incident to [the traffic] stop.\xe2\x80\x9d Caballes, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 408. Typically such inquiries involve checking the driver\xe2\x80\x99s license, determining whether there are outstanding warrants against the driver, and inspecting the automobile\xe2\x80\x99s registration and proof of insurance. See Delaware v. Prouse,440 U.\xc2\xa0S. 648\xe2\x80\x93660 (1979). See also 4 W. LaFave, Search and Seizure \xc2\xa79.3(c), pp. 507\xe2\x80\x93517 (5th ed. 2012). These checks serve the same objective as enforcement of the traffic code: ensuring that vehicles on the road are operated safely and responsibly. See Prouse, 440 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 658\xe2\x80\x93659; LaFave, Search and Seizure \xc2\xa79.3(c), at 516 (A \xe2\x80\x9cwarrant check makes it possible to determine whether the apparent traffic violator is wanted for one or more previous traffic offenses.\xe2\x80\x9d).

A dog sniff, by contrast, is a measure aimed at \xe2\x80\x9cdetect[ing] evidence of ordinary criminal wrongdoing.\xe2\x80\x9d Indianapolis v. Edmond,531 U.\xc2\xa0S. 32\xe2\x80\x9341 (2000). See also Florida v. Jardines,569 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2013) (slip op., at 7\xe2\x80\x938). Candidly, the Government acknowledged at oral argument that a dog sniff, unlike the routine measures just mentioned, is not an ordinary incident of a traffic stop. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 33. Lacking the same close connection to roadway safety as the ordinary inquiries, a dog sniff is not fairly characterized as part of the officer\xe2\x80\x99s traffic mission.

In advancing its de minimis rule, the Eighth Circuit relied heavily on our decision in Pennsylvania v. Mimms,434 U.\xc2\xa0S. 106 (1977) (per curiam). See United States v. $404,905.00 in U.\xc2\xa0S. Currency, 182 F.\xc2\xa03d 643, 649 (CA8 1999). In Mimms, we reasoned that the government\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9clegitimate and weighty\xe2\x80\x9d interest in officer safety outweighs the \xe2\x80\x9cde minimis\xe2\x80\x9d additional intrusion of requiring a driver, already lawfully stopped, to exit the vehicle. 434 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 110\xe2\x80\x93111. See also Maryland v. Wilson,519 U.\xc2\xa0S. 408\xe2\x80\x93415 (1997) (passengers may be required to exit vehicle stopped for traffic violation). The Eighth Circuit, echoed in Justice Thomas\xe2\x80\x99s dissent, believed that the imposition here similarly could be offset by the Government\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cstrong interest in interdicting the flow of illegal drugs along the nation\xe2\x80\x99s highways.\xe2\x80\x9d $404,905.00 in U.\xc2\xa0S. Currency, 182 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 649; see post, at 9.

Unlike a general interest in criminal enforcement, however, the government\xe2\x80\x99s officer safety interest stems from the mission of the stop itself. Traffic stops are \xe2\x80\x9cespecially fraught with danger to police officers,\xe2\x80\x9d Johnson, 555 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 330 (internal quotation marks omitted), so an officer may need to take certain negligibly burdensome precautions in order to complete his mission safely. Cf. United States v. Holt, 264 F.\xc2\xa03d 1215, 1221\xe2\x80\x931222 (CA10 2001) (en banc) (recognizing officer safety justification for criminal record and outstanding warrant checks), abrogated on other grounds as recognized in United States v. Stewart, 473 F.\xc2\xa03d 1265, 1269 (CA10 2007). On-scene investigation into other crimes, however, detours from that mission. See supra, at 6\xe2\x80\x937. So too do safety precautions taken in order to facilitate such detours. But cf. post, at 2\xe2\x80\x933 (Alito, J., dissenting). Thus, even assuming that the imposition here was no more intrusive than the exit order in Mimms, the dog sniff could not be justified on the same basis. Highway and officer safety are interests different in kind from the Government\xe2\x80\x99s endeavor to detect crime in general or drug trafficking in particular.

The Government argues that an officer may \xe2\x80\x9cincremental[ly]\xe2\x80\x9d prolong a stop to conduct a dog sniff so long as the officer is reasonably diligent in pursuing the traffic-related purpose of the stop, and the overall duration of the stop remains reasonable in relation to the duration of other traffic stops involving similar circumstances. Brief for United States 36\xe2\x80\x9339. The Government\xe2\x80\x99s argument, in effect, is that by completing all traffic-related tasks expeditiously, an officer can earn bonus time to pursue an unrelated criminal investigation. See also post, at 2\xe2\x80\x935 (Thomas, J., dissenting) (embracing the Government\xe2\x80\x99s argument). The reasonableness of a seizure, however, depends on what the police in fact do. See Knowles, 525 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 115\xe2\x80\x93117. In this regard, the Government acknowledges that \xe2\x80\x9can officer always has to be reasonably diligent.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 49. How could diligence be gauged other than by noting what the officer actually did and how he did it? If an officer can complete traffic-based inquiries expeditiously, then that is the amount of \xe2\x80\x9ctime reasonably required to complete [the stop\xe2\x80\x99s] mission.\xe2\x80\x9d Caballes, 543 U. S., at 407. As we said in Caballes and reiterate today, a traffic stop \xe2\x80\x9cprolonged beyond\xe2\x80\x9d that point is \xe2\x80\x9cunlawful.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The critical question, then, is not whether the dog sniff occurs before or after the officer issues a ticket, as Justice Alito supposes, post, at 2\xe2\x80\x934, but whether conducting the sniff \xe2\x80\x9cprolongs\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94i.e., adds time to\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cthe stop,\xe2\x80\x9d supra, at 6.

III

The Magistrate Judge found that detention for the dog sniff in this case was not independently supported by individualized suspicion, see App. 100, and the District Court adopted the Magistrate Judge\xe2\x80\x99s findings, see id., at 112\xe2\x80\x93113. The Court of Appeals, however, did not review that determination. But see post, at 1, 10\xe2\x80\x9312 (Thomas, J., dissenting) (resolving the issue, nevermind that the Court of Appeals left it unaddressed); post, at 1\xe2\x80\x932 (Alito, J., dissenting) (upbraiding the Court for addressing the sole issue decided by the Court of Appeals and characterizing the Court\xe2\x80\x99s answer as \xe2\x80\x9cunnecessary\xe2\x80\x9d because the Court, instead, should have decided an issue the Court of Appeals did not decide). The question whether reasonable suspicion of criminal activity justified detaining Rodriguez beyond completion of the traffic infraction investigation, therefore, remains open for Eighth Circuit consideration on remand.

For the reasons stated, the judgment of the United States Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit is vacated, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

It is so ordered.

DENNYS RODRIGUEZ, PETITIONER v. UNITED STATES

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eighth circuit

[April 21, 2015]

Justice Thomas, with whom Justice Alito joins, and with whom Justice Kennedy joins as to all but Part III, dissenting.

Ten years ago, we explained that \xe2\x80\x9cconducting a dog sniff [does] not change the character of a traffic stop that is lawful at its inception and otherwise executed in a reasonable manner.\xe2\x80\x9d Illinois v. Caballes,543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 405,408 (2005). The only question here is whether an officer executed a stop in a reasonable manner when he waited to conduct a dog sniff until after he had given the driver a written warning and a backup unit had arrived, bringing the overall duration of the stop to 29 minutes. Because the stop was reasonably executed, no Fourth Amendment violation occurred. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding to the contrary cannot be reconciled with our decision in Caballes or a number of common police practices. It was also unnecessary, as the officer possessed reasonable suspicion to continue to hold the driver to conduct the dog sniff. I respectfully dissent.

I

The Fourth Amendment protects \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he right of the people to be secure in their persons, houses, papers, and effects, against unreasonable searches and seizures.\xe2\x80\x9d U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Amdt. 4. As the text indicates, and as we have repeatedly confirmed, \xe2\x80\x9cthe ultimate touchstone of the Fourth Amendment is \xe2\x80\x98reasonableness.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brigham City v. Stuart,547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 398,403 (2006). We have defined reasonableness \xe2\x80\x9cin objective terms by examining the totality of the circumstances,\xe2\x80\x9d Ohio v. Robinette,519 U.\xc2\xa0S. 33,39 (1996), and by considering \xe2\x80\x9cthe traditional protections against unreasonable searches and seizures afforded by the common law at the time of the framing,\xe2\x80\x9d Atwater v. Lago Vista,532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 318,326 (2001) (internal quotation marks omitted). When traditional protections have not provided a definitive answer, our precedents have \xe2\x80\x9canalyzed a search or seizure in light of traditional standards of reasonableness by assessing, on the one hand, the degree to which it intrudes upon an individual\xe2\x80\x99s privacy and, on the other, the degree to which it is needed for the promotion of legitimate governmental interests.\xe2\x80\x9d Virginia v. Moore,553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 164,171 (2008) (internal quotation marks omitted).

Although a traffic stop \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutes a \xe2\x80\x98seizure\xe2\x80\x99 of \xe2\x80\x98persons\xe2\x80\x99 within the meaning of [the Fourth Amendment],\xe2\x80\x9d such a seizure is constitutionally \xe2\x80\x9creasonable where the police have probable cause to believe that a traffic violation has occurred.\xe2\x80\x9d Whren v. United States,517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 806\xe2\x80\x93810 (1996). But \xe2\x80\x9ca seizure that is lawful at its inception can violate the Fourth Amendment if its manner of execution unreasonably infringes interests protected by the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Caballes, supra, at 407.

Because Rodriguez does not dispute that Officer Struble had probable cause to stop him, the only question is whether the stop was otherwise executed in a reasonable manner. See Brief for Appellant in No. 13\xe2\x80\x931176 (CA8), p.\xc2\xa04, n.\xc2\xa02. I easily conclude that it was. Approximately 29 minutes passed from the time Officer Struble stopped Rodriguez until his narcotics-detection dog alerted to the presence of drugs. That amount of time is hardly out of the ordinary for a traffic stop by a single officer of a vehicle containing multiple occupants even when no dog sniff is involved. See, e.g., United States v. Ellis, 497 F.\xc2\xa03d 606 (CA6 2007) (22 minutes); United States v. Barragan, 379 F.\xc2\xa03d 524 (CA8 2004) (approximately 30 minutes). During that time, Officer Struble conducted the ordinary activities of a traffic stop\xe2\x80\x94he approached the vehicle, questioned Rodriguez about the observed violation, asked Pollman about their travel plans, ran serial warrant checks on Rodriguez and Pollman, and issued a written warning to Rodriguez. And when he decided to conduct a dog sniff, he took the precaution of calling for backup out of concern for his safety. See 741 F.\xc2\xa03d 905, 907 (CA8 2014); see also Pennsylvania v. Mimms,434 U.\xc2\xa0S. 106,110 (1977) (per curiam) (officer safety is a \xe2\x80\x9clegitimate and weighty\xe2\x80\x9d concern relevant to reasonableness).

As Caballes makes clear, the fact that Officer Struble waited until after he gave Rodriguez the warning to conduct the dog sniff does not alter this analysis. Because \xe2\x80\x9cthe use of a well-trained narcotics-detection dog . . . generally does not implicate legitimate privacy interests,\xe2\x80\x9d 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 409, \xe2\x80\x9cconducting a dog sniff would not change the character of a traffic stop that is lawful at its inception and otherwise executed in a reasonable manner,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 408. The stop here was \xe2\x80\x9clawful at its inception and otherwise executed in a reasonable manner.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. As in Caballes, \xe2\x80\x9cconducting a dog sniff [did] not change the character of [the] traffic stop,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., and thus no Fourth Amendment violation occurred.

II

Rather than adhere to the reasonableness requirement that we have repeatedly characterized as the \xe2\x80\x9ctouchstone of the Fourth Amendment,\xe2\x80\x9d Brigham City, supra, at 403, the majority constructed a test of its own that is inconsistent with our precedents.

A

The majority\xe2\x80\x99s rule requires a traffic stop to \xe2\x80\x9cen[d] when tasks tied to the traffic infraction are\xe2\x80\x94or reasonably should have been\xe2\x80\x94completed.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 5. \xe2\x80\x9cIf an officer can complete traffic-based inquiries expeditiously, then that is the amount of time reasonably required to complete the stop\xe2\x80\x99s mission\xe2\x80\x9d and he may hold the individual no longer. Ante, at 8 (internal quotation marks and alterations omitted). The majority\xe2\x80\x99s rule thus imposes a one-way ratchet for constitutional protection linked to the characteristics of the individual officer conducting the stop: If a driver is stopped by a particularly efficient officer, then he will be entitled to be released from the traffic stop after a shorter period of time than a driver stopped by a less efficient officer. Similarly, if a driver is stopped by an officer with access to technology that can shorten a records check, then he will be entitled to be released from the stop after a shorter period of time than an individual stopped by an officer without access to such technology.

I \xe2\x80\x9ccannot accept that the search and seizure protections of the Fourth Amendment are so variable and can be made to turn upon such trivialities.\xe2\x80\x9d Whren, 517 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 815 (citations omitted). We have repeatedly explained that the reasonableness inquiry must not hinge on the characteristics of the individual officer conducting the seizure. We have held, for example, that an officer\xe2\x80\x99s state of mind \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not invalidate [an] action taken as long as the circumstances, viewed objectively, justify that action.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 813 (internal quotation marks omitted). We have spurned theories that would make the Fourth Amendment \xe2\x80\x9cchange with local law enforcement practices.\xe2\x80\x9d Moore, supra, at 172. And we have rejected a rule that would require the offense establishing probable cause to be \xe2\x80\x9cclosely related to\xe2\x80\x9d the offense identified by the arresting officer, as such a rule would make \xe2\x80\x9cthe constitutionality of an arrest .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. vary from place to place and from time to time, depending on whether the arresting officer states the reason for the detention and, if so, whether he correctly identifies a general class of offense for which probable cause exists.\xe2\x80\x9d Devenpeck v. Alford,543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 146,154 (2004) (internal quotation marks and citation omitted). In Devenpeck, a unanimous Court explained: \xe2\x80\x9cAn arrest made by a knowledgeable, veteran officer would be valid, whereas an arrest made by a rookie in precisely the same circumstances would not. We see no reason to ascribe to the Fourth Amendment such arbitrarily variable protection.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

The majority\xe2\x80\x99s logic would produce similarly arbitrary results. Under its reasoning, a traffic stop made by a rookie could be executed in a reasonable manner, whereas the same traffic stop made by a knowledgeable, veteran officer in precisely the same circumstances might not, if in fact his knowledge and experience made him capable of completing the stop faster. We have long rejected interpretations of the Fourth Amendment that would produce such haphazard results, and I see no reason to depart from our consistent practice today.

B

As if that were not enough, the majority also limits the duration of the stop to the time it takes the officer to complete a narrow category of \xe2\x80\x9ctraffic-based inquiries.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8. According to the majority, these inquiries include those that \xe2\x80\x9cserve the same objective as enforcement of the traffic code: ensuring that vehicles on the road are operated safely and responsibly.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 6. Inquiries directed to \xe2\x80\x9cdetecting evidence of ordinary criminal wrongdoing\xe2\x80\x9d are not traffic-related inquiries and thus cannot count toward the overall duration of the stop. Ibid. (internal quotation marks and alteration omitted).

The combination of that definition of traffic-related inquiries with the majority\xe2\x80\x99s officer-specific durational limit produces a result demonstrably at odds with our decision in Caballes. Caballes expressly anticipated that a traffic stop could be reasonably prolonged for officers to engage in a dog sniff. We explained that no Fourth Amendment violation had occurred in Caballes, where the \xe2\x80\x9cduration of the stop .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. was entirely justified by the traffic offense and the ordinary inquiries incident to such a stop,\xe2\x80\x9d but suggested a different result might attend a case \xe2\x80\x9cinvolving a dog sniff that occurred during an unreasonably prolonged traffic stop.\xe2\x80\x9d 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 407\xe2\x80\x93408 (emphasis added). The dividing line was whether the overall duration of the stop exceeded \xe2\x80\x9cthe time reasonably required to complete th[e] mission,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 407, not, as the majority suggests, whether the duration of the stop \xe2\x80\x9cin fact\xe2\x80\x9d exceeded the time necessary to complete the traffic-related inquiries, ante, at 8.

The majority\xe2\x80\x99s approach draws an artificial line between dog sniffs and other common police practices. The lower courts have routinely confirmed that warrant checks are a constitutionally permissible part of a traffic stop, see, e.g., United States v. Simmons, 172 F.\xc2\xa03d 775, 778 (CA11 1999); United States v. Mendez, 118 F.\xc2\xa03d 1426, 1429 (CA10 1997); United States v. Shabazz, 993 F.\xc2\xa02d 431, 437 (CA5 1993), and the majority confirms that it finds no fault in these measures, ante, at 6. Yet its reasoning suggests the opposite. Such warrant checks look more like they are directed to \xe2\x80\x9cdetecting evidence of ordinary criminal wrongdoing\xe2\x80\x9d than to \xe2\x80\x9censuring that vehicles on the road are operated safely and responsibly.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 6 (internal quotation marks and alteration omitted). Perhaps one could argue that the existence of an outstanding warrant might make a driver less likely to operate his vehicle safely and responsibly on the road, but the same could be said about a driver in possession of contraband. A driver confronted by the police in either case might try to flee or become violent toward the officer. But under the majority\xe2\x80\x99s analysis, a dog sniff, which is directed at uncovering that problem, is not treated as a traffic-based inquiry. Warrant checks, arguably, should fare no better. The majority suggests that a warrant check is an ordinary inquiry incident to a traffic stop because it can be used \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98to determine whether the apparent traffic violator is wanted for one or more previous traffic offenses.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 6 (quoting 4 W. LaFave, Search and Seizure \xc2\xa79.3(c), p. 516 (5th ed. 2012)). But as the very treatise on which the majority relies notes, such checks are a \xe2\x80\x9cmanifest[ation of\xc2\xa0] the \xe2\x80\x98war on drugs\xe2\x80\x99 motivation so often underlying [routine traffic] stops,\xe2\x80\x9d and thus are very much like the dog sniff in this case. Id., \xc2\xa79.3(c), at 507\xe2\x80\x93508.

Investigative questioning rests on the same basis as the dog sniff. \xe2\x80\x9cAsking questions is an essential part of police investigations.\xe2\x80\x9d Hiibel v. Sixth Judicial Dist. Court of Nev., Humboldt Cty.,542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 177,185 (2004). And the lower courts have routinely upheld such questioning during routine traffic stops. See, e.g., United States v. Rivera, 570 F.\xc2\xa03d 1009, 1013 (CA8 2009); United States v. Childs, 277 F.\xc2\xa03d 947, 953\xe2\x80\x93954 (CA7 2002). The majority\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning appears to allow officers to engage in some questioning aimed at detecting evidence of ordinary criminal wrongdoing. Ante, at 5. But it is hard to see how such inquiries fall within the \xe2\x80\x9cseizure\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x98mission\xe2\x80\x99 [of\xc2\xa0] address[ing] the traffic violation that warranted the stop,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cattend[ing] to related safety concerns.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Its reasoning appears to come down to the principle that dogs are different.

C

On a more fundamental level, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s inquiry elides the distinction between traffic stops based on probable cause and those based on reasonable suspicion. Probable cause is the \xe2\x80\x9ctraditional justification\xe2\x80\x9d for the seizure of a person. Whren, 517 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 817 (emphasis deleted); see also Dunaway v. New York,442 U.\xc2\xa0S. 200, 207\xe2\x80\x93208 (1979). This Court created an exception to that rule in Terry v. Ohio,392 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1 (1968), permitting \xe2\x80\x9cpolice officers who suspect criminal activity to make limited intrusions on an individual\xe2\x80\x99s personal security based on less than probable cause,\xe2\x80\x9d Michigan v. Summers,452 U.\xc2\xa0S. 692,698 (1981). Reasonable suspicion is the justification for such seizures. Prado Navarette v. California, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 3).

Traffic stops can be initiated based on probable cause or reasonable suspicion. Although the Court has commented that a routine traffic stop is \xe2\x80\x9cmore analogous to a so-called \xe2\x80\x98Terry stop\xe2\x80\x99 than to a formal arrest,\xe2\x80\x9d it has rejected the notion \xe2\x80\x9cthat a traffic stop supported by probable cause may not exceed the bounds set by the Fourth Amendment on the scope of a Terry stop.\xe2\x80\x9d Berkemer v. McCarty,468 U.\xc2\xa0S. 420, and n. 29 (1984) (citation omitted).

Although all traffic stops must be executed reasonably, our precedents make clear that traffic stops justified by reasonable suspicion are subject to additional limitations that those justified by probable cause are not. A traffic stop based on reasonable suspicion, like all Terry stops, must be \xe2\x80\x9cjustified at its inception\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9creasonably related in scope to the circumstances which justified the interference in the first place.\xe2\x80\x9d Hiibel, 542 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 185 (internal quotation marks omitted). It also \xe2\x80\x9ccannot continue for an excessive period of time or resemble a traditional arrest.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 185\xe2\x80\x93186 (citation omitted). By contrast, a stop based on probable cause affords an officer considerably more leeway. In such seizures, an officer may engage in a warrantless arrest of the driver, Atwater, 532 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 354, a warrantless search incident to arrest of the driver, Riley v. California, 573 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 5), and a warrantless search incident to arrest of the vehicle if it is reasonable to believe evidence relevant to the crime of arrest might be found there, Arizona v. Gant,556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 332,335 (2009).

The majority casually tosses this distinction aside. It asserts that the traffic stop in this case, which was undisputedly initiated on the basis of probable cause, can last no longer than is in fact necessary to effectuate the mission of the stop. Ante, at 8. And, it assumes that the mission of the stop was merely to write a traffic ticket, rather than to consider making a custodial arrest. Ante, at 5. In support of that durational requirement, it relies primarily on cases involving Terry stops. See ante, at 5\xe2\x80\x937 (citing Arizona v. Johnson,555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 323 (2009) (analyzing \xe2\x80\x9cstop and frisk\xe2\x80\x9d of passenger in a vehicle temporarily seized for a traffic violation); United States v. Sharpe,470 U.\xc2\xa0S. 675 (1985) (analyzing seizure of individuals based on suspicion of marijuana trafficking); Florida v. Royer,460 U.\xc2\xa0S. 491 (1983) (plurality opinion) (analyzing seizure of man walking through airport on suspicion of narcotics activity)).

The only case involving a traffic stop based on probable cause that the majority cites for its rule is Caballes. But, that decision provides no support for today\xe2\x80\x99s restructuring of our Fourth Amendment jurisprudence. In Caballes, the Court made clear that, in the context of a traffic stop supported by probable cause, \xe2\x80\x9ca dog sniff would not change the character of a traffic stop that is lawful at its inception and otherwise executed in a reasonable manner.\xe2\x80\x9d 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 408. To be sure, the dissent in Caballes would have \xe2\x80\x9cappl[ied] Terry\xe2\x80\x99s reasonable-relation test .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to determine whether the canine sniff impermissibly expanded the scope of the initially valid seizure of Caballes.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 420 (Ginsburg, J., dissenting). But even it conceded that the Caballes majority had \xe2\x80\x9cimplicitly [rejected] the application of Terry to a traffic stop converted, by calling in a dog, to a drug search.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 421.

By strictly limiting the tasks that define the durational scope of the traffic stop, the majority accomplishes today what the Caballes dissent could not: strictly limiting the scope of an officer\xe2\x80\x99s activities during a traffic stop justified by probable cause. In doing so, it renders the difference between probable cause and reasonable suspicion virtually meaningless in this context. That shift is supported neither by the Fourth Amendment nor by our precedents interpreting it. And, it results in a constitutional framework that lacks predictability. Had Officer Struble ar-rested, handcuffed, and taken Rodriguez to the police station for his traffic violation, he would have complied with the Fourth Amendment. See Atwater, supra, at 354\xe2\x80\x93355. But because he made Rodriguez wait for seven or eight extra minutes until a dog arrived, he evidently committed a constitutional violation. Such a view of the Fourth Amendment makes little sense.

III

Today\xe2\x80\x99s revision of our Fourth Amendment jurisprudence was also entirely unnecessary. Rodriguez suffered no Fourth Amendment violation here for an entirely independent reason: Officer Struble had reasonable suspicion to continue to hold him for investigative purposes. Our precedents make clear that the Fourth Amendment permits an officer to conduct an investigative traffic stop when that officer has \xe2\x80\x9ca particularized and objective basis for suspecting the particular person stopped of criminal activity.\xe2\x80\x9d Prado Navarette, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 3) (internal quotation marks omitted). Reasonable suspicion is determined by looking at \xe2\x80\x9cthe whole picture,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., taking into account \xe2\x80\x9cthe factual and practical considerations of everyday life on which reasonable and prudent men, not legal technicians, act,\xe2\x80\x9d Ornelas v. United States,517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 690,695 (1996) (internal quotation marksomitted).

Officer Struble testified that he first became suspicious that Rodriguez was engaged in criminal activity for a number of reasons. When he approached the vehicle, he smelled an \xe2\x80\x9coverwhelming odor of air freshener coming from the vehicle,\xe2\x80\x9d which is, in his experience, \xe2\x80\x9ca common attempt to conceal an odor that [people] don\xe2\x80\x99t want .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to be smelled by the police.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 20\xe2\x80\x9321. He also observed, upon approaching the front window on the passenger side of the vehicle, that Rodriguez\xe2\x80\x99s passenger, Scott Pollman, appeared nervous. Pollman pulled his hat down low, puffed nervously on a cigarette, and refused to make eye contact with him. The officer thought he was \xe2\x80\x9cmore nervous than your typical passenger\xe2\x80\x9d who \xe2\x80\x9cdo[esn\xe2\x80\x99t] have anything to worry about because [t]hey didn\xe2\x80\x99t commit a [traffic] violation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 34.

Officer Struble\xe2\x80\x99s interactions with the vehicle\xe2\x80\x99s occupants only increased his suspicions. When he asked Rod-riguez why he had driven onto the shoulder, Rodriguez claimed that he swerved to avoid a pothole. But that story could not be squared with Officer Struble\xe2\x80\x99s observation of the vehicle slowly driving off the road before being jerked back onto it. And when Officer Struble asked Pollman where they were coming from and where they were going, Pollman told him they were traveling from Omaha, Nebraska, back to Norfolk, Nebraska, after looking at a vehicle they were considering purchasing. Pollman told the officer that he had neither seen pictures of the vehicle nor confirmed title before the trip. As Officer Struble explained, it \xe2\x80\x9cseemed suspicious\xe2\x80\x9d to him \xe2\x80\x9cto drive .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. approximately two hours .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. late at night to see a vehicle sight unseen to possibly buy it,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 26, and to go from Norfolk to Omaha to look at it because \xe2\x80\x9c[u]sually people leave Omaha to go get vehicles, not the other way around\xe2\x80\x9d due to higher Omaha taxes, id., at 65.

These facts, taken together, easily meet our standard for reasonable suspicion. \xe2\x80\x9c[N]ervous, evasive behavior is a pertinent factor in determining reasonable suspicion,\xe2\x80\x9d Illinois v. Wardlow,528 U.\xc2\xa0S. 119,124 (2000), and both vehicle occupants were engaged in such conduct. The officer also recognized heavy use of air freshener, which, in his experience, indicated the presence of contraband in the vehicle. \xe2\x80\x9c[C]ommonsense judgments and inferences about human behavior\xe2\x80\x9d further support the officer\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that Pollman\xe2\x80\x99s story about their trip was likely a cover story for illegal activity. Id., at 125. Taking into account all the relevant facts, Officer Struble possessed reasonable suspicion of criminal activity to conduct the dog sniff.

Rodriguez contends that reasonable suspicion cannot exist because each of the actions giving rise to the officer\xe2\x80\x99s suspicions could be entirely innocent, but our cases easily dispose of that argument. Acts that, by themselves, might be innocent can, when taken together, give rise to reasonable suspicion. United States v. Arvizu,534 U.\xc2\xa0S. 266\xe2\x80\x93275 (2002). Terry is a classic example, as it involved two individuals repeatedly walking back and forth, looking into a store window, and conferring with one another as well as with a third man. 392 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 6. The Court reasoned that this \xe2\x80\x9cseries of acts, each of them perhaps innocent in itself, . . . together warranted further investigation,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 22, and it has reiterated that analysis in a number of cases, see, e.g., Arvizu, supra, at 277; United States v. Sokolow,490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\xe2\x80\x9310 (1989). This one is no different.

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

I would conclude that the police did not violate the Fourth Amendment here. Officer Struble possessed probable cause to stop Rodriguez for driving on the shoulder, and he executed the subsequent stop in a reasonable manner. Our decision in Caballes requires no more. The majority\xe2\x80\x99s holding to the contrary is irreconcilable with Caballes and a number of other routine police practices, distorts the distinction between traffic stops justified by probable cause and those justified by reasonable suspicion, and abandons reasonableness as the touchstone of the Fourth Amendment. I respectfully dissent.

DENNYS RODRIGUEZ, PETITIONER v. UNITED STATES

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eighth circuit

[April 21, 2015]

Justice Alito, dissenting.

This is an unnecessary,[1] impractical, and arbitrary decision. It addresses a purely hypothetical question: whether the traffic stop in this case would be unreason-able if the police officer, prior to leading a drug-sniffing dog around the exterior of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s car, did not already have reasonable suspicion that the car contained drugs. In fact, however, the police officer did have reasonable suspicion, and, as a result, the officer was justified in detaining the occupants for the short period of time (seven or eight minutes) that is at issue.

The relevant facts are not in dispute. Officer Struble, who made the stop, was the only witness at the suppression hearing, and his testimony about what happened was not challenged. Defense counsel argued that the facts recounted by Officer Struble were insufficient to establish reasonable suspicion, but defense counsel did not dispute those facts or attack the officer\xe2\x80\x99s credibility. Similarly, the Magistrate Judge who conducted the hearing did not question the officer\xe2\x80\x99s credibility. And as Justice Thomas\xe2\x80\x99s opinion shows, the facts recounted by Officer Struble \xe2\x80\x9ceasily meet our standard for reasonable suspicion.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11 (dissenting opinion); see also, e.g., United States v. Carpenter, 462 F. 3d 981, 986\xe2\x80\x93987 (CA8 2006) (finding reasonable suspicion for a dog sniff based on implausible travel plans and nervous conduct); United States v. Ludwig, 641 F. 3d 1243, 1248\xe2\x80\x931250 (CA10 2011) (finding reasonable suspicion for a dog sniff where, among other things, the officer smelled \xe2\x80\x9cstrong masking odors,\xe2\x80\x9d the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9caccount of his travel was suspect,\xe2\x80\x9d and the defendant \xe2\x80\x9cwas exceptionally nervous throughout his encounter\xe2\x80\x9d).

Not only does the Court reach out to decide a question not really presented by the facts in this case, but the Court\xe2\x80\x99s answer to that question is arbitrary. The Court refuses to address the real Fourth Amendment question: whether the stop was unreasonably prolonged. Instead, the Court latches onto the fact that Officer Struble delivered the warning prior to the dog sniff and proclaims that the authority to detain based on a traffic stop ends when a citation or warning is handed over to the driver. The Court thus holds that the Fourth Amendment was vio-lated, not because of the length of the stop, but simply be-cause of the sequence in which Officer Struble chose to perform his tasks.

This holding is not only arbitrary; it is perverse since Officer Struble chose that sequence for the purpose of protecting his own safety and possibly the safety of others. See App. 71\xe2\x80\x9372. Without prolonging the stop, Officer Struble could have conducted the dog sniff while one of the tasks that the Court regards as properly part of the traffic stop was still in progress, but that sequence would have entailed unnecessary risk. At approximately 12:19 a.m., after collecting Pollman\xe2\x80\x99s driver\xe2\x80\x99s license, Officer Struble did two things. He called in the information needed to do a records check on Pollman (a step that the Court recognizes was properly part of the traffic stop), and he requested that another officer report to the scene. Officer Struble had decided to perform a dog sniff but did not want to do that without another officer present. When occupants of a vehicle who know that their vehicle contains a large amount of illegal drugs see that a drug-sniffing dog has alerted for the presence of drugs, they will almost certainly realize that the police will then proceed to search the vehicle, discover the drugs, and make arrests. Thus, it is reasonable for an officer to believe that an alert will increase the risk that the occupants of the vehicle will attempt to flee or perhaps even attack the officer. See, e.g., United States v. Dawdy, 46 F.\xc2\xa03d 1427, 1429 (CA8 1995) (recounting scuffle between officer and defendant after drugs were discovered).

In this case, Officer Struble was concerned that he was outnumbered at the scene, and he therefore called for backup and waited for the arrival of another officer before conducting the sniff. As a result, the sniff was not completed until seven or eight minutes after he delivered the warning. But Officer Struble could have proceeded with the dog sniff while he was waiting for the results of the records check on Pollman and before the arrival of the second officer. The drug-sniffing dog was present in Officer Struble\xe2\x80\x99s car. If he had chosen that riskier sequence of events, the dog sniff would have been completed before the point in time when, according to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis, the authority to detain for the traffic stop ended. Thus, an action that would have been lawful had the officer made the unreasonable decision to risk his life became un-lawful when the officer made the reasonable decision to wait a few minutes for backup. Officer Struble\xe2\x80\x99s error\xe2\x80\x94apparently\xe2\x80\x94was following prudent procedures motivated by legitimate safety concerns. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding therefore makes no practical sense. And nothing in the Fourth Amendment, which speaks of reasonableness, compels this arbitrary line.

The rule that the Court adopts will do little good going forward.[2] It is unlikely to have any appreciable effect on the length of future traffic stops. Most officers will learn the prescribed sequence of events even if they cannot fathom the reason for that requirement. (I would love to be the proverbial fly on the wall when police instructors teach this rule to officers who make traffic stops.)

For these reasons and those set out in Justice Thomas\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, I respectfully dissent.
