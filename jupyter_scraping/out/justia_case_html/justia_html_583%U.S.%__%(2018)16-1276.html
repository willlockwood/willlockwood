DIGITAL REALTY TRUST, INC. v. SOMERS

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 16\xe2\x80\x931276.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 28, 2017\xe2\x80\x94Decided February 21, 2018

\n

Endeavoring to root out corporate fraud, Congress passed the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 (Sarbanes-Oxley) and the 2010 Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act (Dodd-Frank). Both Acts shield whistleblowers from retaliation, but they differ in important respects. Sarbanes-Oxley applies to all \xe2\x80\x9cemployees\xe2\x80\x9d who report misconduct to the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC or Commission), any other federal agency, Congress, or an internal supervisor.\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71514A(a)(1). Dodd-Frank defines a \xe2\x80\x9cwhistleblower\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cany individual who provides .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. information relating to a violation of the securities laws to the Commission, in a manner established, by rule or regulation, by the Commission.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(a)(6). A whistleblower so defined is eligible for an award if original information provided to the SEC leads to a successful enforcement action. \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(b)\xe2\x80\x93(g). And he or she is protected from retaliation in three situations, see \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h)(1)(A)(i)\xe2\x80\x93(iii), including for \xe2\x80\x9cmaking disclosures that are required or protected under\xe2\x80\x9d Sarbanes-Oxley or other specified laws, \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h)(1)(A)(iii). Sarbanes-Oxley\xe2\x80\x99s anti-retaliation provision contains an administrative-exhaustion requirement and a 180-day administrative complaint-filing deadline, see\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71514A(b)(1)(A), (2)(D), whereas Dodd-Frank permits a whistleblower to sue an employer directly in federal district court, with a default six-year limitation period, see \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h)(1)(B)(i), (iii)(I)(aa).

\n

The SEC\xe2\x80\x99s regulations implementing the Dodd-Frank provision contain two discrete whistleblower definitions. For purposes of the award program, Rule 21F\xe2\x80\x932 requires a whistleblower to \xe2\x80\x9cprovide the Commission with information\xe2\x80\x9d relating to possible securities-law violations. 17 CFR \xc2\xa7240.21F\xe2\x80\x932(a)(1). For purposes of the anti-retaliation protections, however, the Rule does not require SEC reporting. See \xc2\xa7240.21F\xe2\x80\x932(b)(1)(i)\xe2\x80\x93(ii).

\n

Respondent Paul Somers alleges that petitioner Digital Realty Trust, Inc. (Digital Realty) terminated his employment shortly after he reported to senior management suspected securities-law violations by the company. Somers filed suit, alleging, inter alia, a claim of whistleblower retaliation under Dodd-Frank. Digital Realty moved to dismiss that claim on the ground that Somers was not a whistleblower under \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h) because he did not alert the SEC prior to his termination. The District Court denied the motion, and the Ninth Circuit affirmed. The Court of Appeals concluded that \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h) does not necessitate recourse to the SEC prior to gaining \xe2\x80\x9cwhistleblower\xe2\x80\x9d status, and it accorded deference to the SEC\xe2\x80\x99s regulation under Chevron U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc.,\n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 837\n.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Dodd-Frank\xe2\x80\x99s anti-retaliation provision does not extend to an individual, like Somers, who has not reported a violation of the securities laws to the SEC. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0A statute\xe2\x80\x99s explicit definition must be followed, even if it varies from a term\xe2\x80\x99s ordinary meaning. Burgess v. United States,\n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 124\n. Section 78u\xe2\x80\x936(a) instructs that the statute\xe2\x80\x99s definition of \xe2\x80\x9cwhistleblower\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cshall apply\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n this section,\xe2\x80\x9d that is, throughout \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936. The Court must therefore interpret the term \xe2\x80\x9cwhistleblower\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h), the anti-retaliation provision, in accordance with that definition.

\n

The whistleblower definition operates in conjunction with the three clauses of \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h)(1)(A) to spell out the provision\xe2\x80\x99s scope. The definition first describes who is eligible for protection\xe2\x80\x94namely, a \xe2\x80\x9cwhistleblower\xe2\x80\x9d who provides pertinent information \xe2\x80\x9cto the Commission.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(a)(6). The three clauses then describe what conduct, when engaged in by a \xe2\x80\x9cwhistleblower,\xe2\x80\x9d is shielded from employment discrimination. An individual who meets both measures may invoke Dodd-Frank\xe2\x80\x99s protections. But an individual who falls outside the protected category of \xe2\x80\x9cwhistleblowers\xe2\x80\x9d is ineligible to seek redress under the statute, regardless of the conduct in which that individual engages. This reading is reinforced by another whistleblower-protection provision in Dodd-Frank, see\n\n12 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa75567(b), which imposes no requirement that information be conveyed to a government agency. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s understanding is corroborated by Dodd-Frank\xe2\x80\x99s purpose and design. The core objective of Dodd-Frank\xe2\x80\x99s whistleblower program is to aid the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s enforcement efforts by \xe2\x80\x9cmotivat[ing] people who know of securities law violations to tell the SEC.\xe2\x80\x9d S. Rep. No. 111\xe2\x80\x93176, p.\xc2\xa038 (emphasis added). To that end, Congress provided monetary awards to whistleblowers who furnish actionable information to the Commission. Congress also complemented the financial incentives for SEC reporting by heightening protection against retaliation. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0Somers and the Solicitor General contend that Dodd-Frank\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cwhistleblower\xe2\x80\x9d definition applies only to the statute\xe2\x80\x99s award program and not, as the definition plainly states, to its anti-retaliation provision. Their concerns do not support a departure from the statutory text. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

(1)\xc2\xa0They claim that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reading would vitiate the protections of clause (iii) for whistleblowers who make disclosures to persons and entities other than the SEC. See \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h)(1)(A)(iii). But the plain-text reading of the statute leaves the third clause with substantial meaning by protecting a whistleblower who reports misconduct both to the SEC and to another entity, but suffers retaliation because of the latter, non-SEC, disclosure. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

(2)\xc2\xa0Nor would the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reading jettison protections for auditors, attorneys, and other employees who are required to report information within the company before making external disclosures. Such employees would be shielded as soon as they also provide relevant information to the Commission. And Congress may well have considered adequate the safeguards already afforded to such employees by Sarbanes-Oxley. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

(3)\xc2\xa0Applying the \xe2\x80\x9cwhistleblower\xe2\x80\x9d definition as written, Somers and the Solicitor General further protest, will allow \xe2\x80\x9cidentical misconduct\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9cgo punished or not based on the happenstance of a separate report\xe2\x80\x9d to the SEC. Brief for Respondent 37\xe2\x80\x9338. But it is understandable that the statute\xe2\x80\x99s retaliation protections, like its financial rewards, would be reserved for employees who have done what Dodd-Frank seeks to achieve by reporting information about unlawful activity to the SEC. P.\xc2\xa016.

\n

(4)\xc2\xa0The Solicitor General observes that the statute contains no apparent requirement of a \xe2\x80\x9ctemporal or topical connection between the violation reported to the Commission and the internal disclosure for which the employee suffers retaliation.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 25. The Court need not dwell on related hypotheticals, which veer far from the case at hand. Pp.\xc2\xa016\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

(5)\xc2\xa0Finally, the interpretation adopted here would not undermine clause (ii) of \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h)(1)(A), which prohibits retaliation against a whistleblower for \xe2\x80\x9cinitiating, testifying in, or assisting in any investigation or .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. action of the Commission based upon\xe2\x80\x9d information conveyed to the SEC by a whistleblower in accordance with the statute. The statute delegates authority to the Commission to establish the \xe2\x80\x9cmanner\xe2\x80\x9d in which a whistleblower may provide information to the SEC. \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(a)(6). Nothing prevents the Commission from enumerating additional means of SEC reporting, including through testimony protected by clause (ii). P.\xc2\xa018.

\n

(d)\xc2\xa0Because \xe2\x80\x9cCongress has directly spoken to the precise question at issue,\xe2\x80\x9d Chevron, 467 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 842, deference is not accorded to the contrary view advanced by the SEC in Rule 21F\xe2\x80\x932. Pp.\xc2\xa018\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

850 F.\xc2\xa03d 1045, reversed and remanded.

DIGITAL REALTY TRUST, INC., PETITIONER v. PAUL SOMERS

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[February 21, 2018]

\n

\n

Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

Endeavoring to root out corporate fraud, Congress passed the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002,\n\n116Stat.\n745 (Sarbanes-Oxley), and the 2010 Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act,\n\n124Stat.\n1376 (Dodd-Frank). Both Acts shield whistleblowers from retaliation, but they differ in important respects. Most notably, Sarbanes-Oxley applies to all \xe2\x80\x9cemployees\xe2\x80\x9d who report misconduct to the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC or Commission), any other federal agency, Congress, or an internal supervisor.\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71514A(a)(1). Dodd-Frank delineates a more circumscribed class; it defines \xe2\x80\x9cwhistleblower\xe2\x80\x9d to mean a person who provides \xe2\x80\x9cinformation relating to a violation of the securities laws to the Commission.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(a)(6). A whistleblower so defined is eligible for an award if original information he or she provides to the SEC leads to a successful enforcement action. \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(b)\xe2\x80\x93(g). And, most relevant here, a whistleblower is protected from retaliation for, inter alia, \xe2\x80\x9cmaking disclosures that are required or protected under\xe2\x80\x9d Sarbanes-Oxley, the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, the criminal anti-retaliation proscription at\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71513(e), or any other law subject to the SEC\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction. 15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h)(1)(A)(iii).

\n

The question presented: Does the anti-retaliation provision of Dodd-Frank extend to an individual who has not reported a violation of the securities laws to the SEC and therefore falls outside the Act\xe2\x80\x99s definition of \xe2\x80\x9cwhistleblower\xe2\x80\x9d? Pet. for Cert. (I). We answer that question \xe2\x80\x9cNo\xe2\x80\x9d: To sue under Dodd-Frank\xe2\x80\x99s anti-retaliation provision, a person must first \xe2\x80\x9cprovid[e] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. information relating to a violation of the securities laws to the Commission.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(a)(6).

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cTo safeguard investors in public companies and restore trust in the financial markets following the collapse of Enron Corporation,\xe2\x80\x9d Congress enacted Sarbanes-Oxley in 2002. Lawson v. FMR LLC,\n\n571 U.\xc2\xa0S. 429\n, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 1). Most pertinent here, Sarbanes-Oxley created new protections for employees at risk of retaliation for reporting corporate misconduct. See\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71514A. Section 1514A prohibits certain companies from discharging or otherwise \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminat[ing] against an employee in the terms and conditions of employment because\xe2\x80\x9d the employee \xe2\x80\x9cprovid[es] information .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. or otherwise assist[s] in an investigation regarding any conduct which the employee reasonably believes constitutes a violation\xe2\x80\x9d of certain criminal fraud statutes, any SEC rule or regulation, or \xe2\x80\x9cany provision of Federal law relating to fraud against shareholders.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71514A(a)(1). An employee qualifies for protection when he or she provides information or assistance either to a federal regulatory or law enforcement agency, Congress, or any \xe2\x80\x9cperson with supervisory authority over the employee.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71514A(a)(1)(A)\xe2\x80\x93(C).[1]

\n

To recover under \xc2\xa71514A, an aggrieved employee must exhaust administrative remedies by \xe2\x80\x9cfiling a complaint with the Secretary of Labor.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71514A(b)(1)(A); see Lawson, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 5\xe2\x80\x936). Congress prescribed a 180-day limitation period for filing such a complaint. \xc2\xa71514A(b)(2)(D). If the agency \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not issue a final decision within 180 days of the filing of [a] complaint, and the [agency\xe2\x80\x99s] delay is not due to bad faith on the claimant\xe2\x80\x99s part, the claimant may proceed to federal district court for de\xc2\xa0novo review.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 6) (citing \xc2\xa71514A(b)). An employee who prevails in a proceeding under \xc2\xa71514A is \xe2\x80\x9centitled to all relief necessary to make the employee whole,\xe2\x80\x9d including reinstatement, backpay with interest, and any \xe2\x80\x9cspecial damages sustained as a result of the discrimination,\xe2\x80\x9d among such damages, litigation costs. \xc2\xa71514A(c).

\n

B

\n

1

\n

At issue in this case is the Dodd-Frank anti-retaliation provision enacted in 2010, eight years after the enactment of Sarbanes-Oxley. Passed in the wake of the 2008 financial crisis, Dodd-Frank aimed to \xe2\x80\x9cpromote the financial stability of the United States by improving accountability and transparency in the financial system.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n124Stat.\n1376.

\n

Dodd-Frank responded to numerous perceived shortcomings in financial regulation. Among them was the SEC\xe2\x80\x99s need for additional \xe2\x80\x9cpower, assistance and money at its disposal\xe2\x80\x9d to regulate securities markets. S. Rep. No. 111\xe2\x80\x93176, pp. 36, 37 (2010). To assist the Commission \xe2\x80\x9cin identifying securities law violations,\xe2\x80\x9d the Act established \xe2\x80\x9ca new, robust whistleblower program designed to motivate people who know of securities law violations to tell the SEC.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 38. And recognizing that \xe2\x80\x9cwhistleblowers often face the difficult choice between telling the truth and .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. committing \xe2\x80\x98career suicide,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Congress sought to protect whistleblowers from employment discrimination. Id., at 111, 112.

\n

Dodd-Frank implemented these goals by adding a new provision to the Securities Exchange Act of 1934:\n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936. Section 78u\xe2\x80\x936 begins by defining a \xe2\x80\x9cwhistleblower\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cany individual who provides .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. information relating to a violation of the securities laws to the Commission, in a manner established, by rule or regulation, by the Commission.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(a)(6) (emphasis added). That definition, the statute directs, \xe2\x80\x9cshall apply\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n this section\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94i.e., throughout \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936. \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(a).

\n

Section 78u\xe2\x80\x936 affords covered whistleblowers both incentives and protection. First, the section creates an award program for \xe2\x80\x9cwhistleblowers who voluntarily provid[e] original information to the Commission that le[ads] to the successful enforcement of [a] covered judicial or administrative action.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(b)(1). A qualifying whistleblower is entitled to a cash award of 10 to 30 percent of the monetary sanctions collected in the enforcement action. See \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(b)(1)(A)\xe2\x80\x93(B).

\n

Second, \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h) prohibits an employer from discharging, harassing, or otherwise discriminating against a \xe2\x80\x9cwhistleblower\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cbecause of any lawful act done by the whistleblower\xe2\x80\x9d in three situations: first, \xe2\x80\x9cin providing information to the Commission in accordance with [\xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936],\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h)(1)(A)(i); second, \xe2\x80\x9cin initiating, testifying in, or assisting in any investigation or .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. action of the Commission based upon\xe2\x80\x9d information provided to the SEC in accordance with \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936, \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h)(1)(A)(ii); and third, \xe2\x80\x9cin making disclosures that are required or protected under\xe2\x80\x9d either Sarbanes-Oxley, the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, the criminal anti-retaliation prohibition at\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71513(e),[2] or \xe2\x80\x9cany other law, rule, or regulation subject to the jurisdiction of the Commission,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h)(1)(A)(iii). Clause (iii), by cross-referencing Sarbanes-Oxley and other laws, protects disclosures made to a variety of individuals and entities in addition to the SEC. For example, the clause shields an employee\xe2\x80\x99s reports of wrongdoing to an internal supervisor if the reports are independently safeguarded from retaliation under Sarbanes-Oxley. See supra, at 2\xe2\x80\x933.[3]

\n

The recovery procedures under the anti-retaliation provisions of Dodd-Frank and Sarbanes-Oxley differ in critical respects. First, unlike Sarbanes-Oxley, which contains an administrative-exhaustion requirement and a 180-day administrative complaint-filing deadline, see\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71514A(b)(1)(A), (2)(D), Dodd-Frank permits a whistleblower to sue a current or former employer directly in federal district court, with a default limitation period of six years, see \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h)(1)(B)(i), (iii)(I)(aa). Second, Dodd-Frank instructs a court to award to a prevailing plaintiff double backpay with interest, see \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h)(1)(C)(ii), while Sarbanes-Oxley limits recovery to actual backpay with interest, see\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71514A(c)(2)(B). Like Sarbanes-Oxley, however, Dodd-Frank authorizes reinstatement and compensation for litigation costs, expert witness fees, and reasonable attorneys\xe2\x80\x99 fees. Compare \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h)(1)(C)(i), (iii), with\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71514A(c)(2)(A), (C).[4]

\n

2

\n

Congress authorized the SEC \xe2\x80\x9cto issue such rules and regulations as may be necessary or appropriate to implement the provisions of [\xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936] consistent with the purposes of this section.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(j). Pursuant to this author- ity, the SEC published a notice of proposed rulemaking to \xe2\x80\x9cImplemen[t] the Whistleblower Provisions\xe2\x80\x9d of Dodd-Frank. 75 Fed. Reg. 70488 (2010). Proposed Rule 21F\xe2\x80\x932(a) defined a \xe2\x80\x9cwhistleblower,\xe2\x80\x9d for purposes of both the award and anti-retaliation provisions of \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936, as one or more individuals who \xe2\x80\x9cprovide the Commission with information relating to a potential violation of the securities laws.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 70519 (proposed 17 CFR \xc2\xa7240.21F\xe2\x80\x932(a)). The proposed rule, the agency noted, \xe2\x80\x9ctracks the statutory definition of a \xe2\x80\x98whistleblower\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d by requiring information reporting to the SEC itself. 75 Fed. Reg. 70489.

\n

In promulgating the final Rule, however, the agency changed course. Rule 21F\xe2\x80\x932, in finished form, contains two discrete \xe2\x80\x9cwhistleblower\xe2\x80\x9d definitions. See 17 CFR \xc2\xa7240.21F\xe2\x80\x932(a)\xe2\x80\x93(b) (2017). For purposes of the award program, the Rule states that \xe2\x80\x9c[y]ou are a whistleblower if .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. you provide the Commission with information .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. relat[ing] to a possible violation of the Federal securities laws.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7240.21F\xe2\x80\x932(a)(1) (emphasis added). The information must be provided to the SEC through its website or by mailing or faxing a specified form to the SEC Office of the Whistleblower. See ibid.; \xc2\xa7240.21F\xe2\x80\x939(a)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(2).

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cFor purposes of the anti-retaliation protections,\xe2\x80\x9d however, the Rule states that \xe2\x80\x9c[y]ou are a whistleblower if .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [y]ou possess a reasonable belief that the information you are providing relates to a possible securities law violation\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c[y]ou provide that information in a manner described in\xe2\x80\x9d clauses (i) through (iii) of \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h)(1)(A). 17 CFR \xc2\xa7240.21F\xe2\x80\x932(b)(1)(i)\xe2\x80\x93(ii). \xe2\x80\x9cThe anti-retaliation protections apply,\xe2\x80\x9d the Rule emphasizes, \xe2\x80\x9cwhether or not you satisfy the requirements, procedures and conditions to qualify for an award.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7240.21F\xe2\x80\x932(b)(1)(iii). An individual may therefore gain anti-retaliation protection as a \xe2\x80\x9cwhistleblower\xe2\x80\x9d under Rule 21F\xe2\x80\x932 without providing information to the SEC, so long as he or she provides information in a manner shielded by one of the anti-retaliation provision\xe2\x80\x99s three clauses. For example, a report to a company supervisor would qualify if the report garners protection under the Sarbanes-Oxley anti-retaliation provision.[5]

\n

C

\n

Petitioner Digital Realty Trust, Inc. (Digital Realty) is a real estate investment trust that owns, acquires, and develops data centers. See Brief for Petitioner 3. Digital Realty employed respondent Paul Somers as a Vice President from 2010 to 2014. See 119 F.\xc2\xa0Supp.\xc2\xa03d 1088, 1092 (ND Cal. 2015). Somers alleges that Digital Realty terminated him shortly after he reported to senior management suspected securities-law violations by the company. See ibid. Although nothing impeded him from alerting the SEC prior to his termination, he did not do so. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 45. Nor did he file an administrative complaint within 180 days of his termination, rendering him ineligible for relief under Sarbanes-Oxley. See ibid.;\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71514A(b)(2)(D).

\n

Somers brought suit in the United\xc2\xa0States District Court for the Northern District of California alleging, inter alia, a claim of whistleblower retaliation under Dodd-Frank. Digital Realty moved to dismiss that claim, arguing that \xe2\x80\x9cSomers does not qualify as a \xe2\x80\x98whistleblower\xe2\x80\x99 under [\xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h)] because he did not report any alleged law violations to the SEC.\xe2\x80\x9d 119 F.\xc2\xa0Supp.\xc2\xa03d, at 1094. The District Court denied the motion. Rule 21F\xe2\x80\x932, the court observed, does not necessitate recourse to the SEC prior to gaining \xe2\x80\x9cwhistleblower\xe2\x80\x9d status under Dodd-Frank. See id., at 1095\xe2\x80\x931096. Finding the statutory scheme ambiguous, the court accorded deference to the SEC\xe2\x80\x99s Rule under Chevron U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc.,\n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 837 (1984)\n. See 119 F.\xc2\xa0Supp.\xc2\xa03d, at 1096\xe2\x80\x931106.

\n

On interlocutory appeal, a divided panel of the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit affirmed. 850 F.\xc2\xa03d 1045 (2017). The majority acknowledged that Dodd-Frank\xe2\x80\x99s definitional provision describes a \xe2\x80\x9cwhistleblower\xe2\x80\x9d as an individual who provides information to the SEC itself. Id., at 1049. But applying that definition to the anti-retaliation provision, the majority reasoned, would narrow the third clause of \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h)(1)(A) \xe2\x80\x9cto the point of absurd- ity\xe2\x80\x9d: The statute would protect employees only if they \xe2\x80\x9creported possible securities violations both internally and to the SEC.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Such dual reporting, the majority believed, was unlikely to occur. Ibid. Therefore, the majority concluded, the statute should be read to protect employees who make disclosures privileged by clause (iii) of \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h)(1)(A), whether or not those employees also provide information to the SEC. Id., at 1050. In any event, the majority held, the SEC\xe2\x80\x99s resolution of any statutory ambiguity warranted deference. Ibid. Judge Owens dissented. In his view, the statutory definition of whistleblower was clear, left no room for interpretation, and plainly governed. Id., at 1051.

\n

Two other Courts of Appeals have weighed in on the question before us. The Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit has held that employees must provide information to the SEC to avail themselves of Dodd-Frank\xe2\x80\x99s anti-retaliation safeguard. See Asadi v. G.\xc2\xa0E. Energy (USA), L.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0C., 720 F.\xc2\xa03d 620, 630 (2013). A divided panel of the Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit reached the opposite conclusion, over a dissent by Judge Jacobs. See Berman v. NEO@OGILVY LLC, 801 F.\xc2\xa03d 145, 155 (2013). We granted certiorari to resolve this conflict, 582 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2017), and now reverse the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment.

\n

II

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cWhen a statute includes an explicit definition, we must follow that definition,\xe2\x80\x9d even if it varies from a term\xe2\x80\x99s ordinary meaning. Burgess v. United States,\n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 124,\n130 (2008)\n (internal quotation marks omitted). This principle resolves the question before us.

\n

A

\n

Our charge in this review proceeding is to determine the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cwhistleblower\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h), Dodd-Frank\xe2\x80\x99s anti-retaliation provision. The definition section of the statute supplies an unequivocal answer: A \xe2\x80\x9cwhistleblower\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cany individual who provides .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. information relating to a violation of the securities laws to the Commission.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(a)(6) (emphasis added). Leaving no doubt as to the definition\xe2\x80\x99s reach, the statute instructs that the \xe2\x80\x9cdefinitio[n] shall apply\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n this section,\xe2\x80\x9d that is, throughout \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936. \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(a)(6).

\n

The whistleblower definition operates in conjunction with the three clauses of \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h)(1)(A) to spell out the provision\xe2\x80\x99s scope. The definition first describes who is eligible for protection\xe2\x80\x94namely, a whistleblower who provides pertinent information \xe2\x80\x9cto the Commission.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(a)(6). The three clauses of \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h)(1)(A) then describe what conduct, when engaged in by a whistle- blower, is shielded from employment discrimination. See \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h)(1)(A)(i)\xe2\x80\x93(iii). An individual who meets both measures may invoke Dodd-Frank\xe2\x80\x99s protections. But an individual who falls outside the protected category of \xe2\x80\x9cwhistleblowers\xe2\x80\x9d is ineligible to seek redress under the statute, regardless of the conduct in which that individual engages.

\n

Reinforcing our reading, another whistleblower-protection provision in Dodd-Frank imposes no requirement that information be conveyed to a government agency. Title 10 of the statute, which created the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB), prohibits discrimination against a \xe2\x80\x9ccovered employee\xe2\x80\x9d who, among other things, \xe2\x80\x9cprovide[s] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. information to [his or her] employer, the Bureau, or any other State, local, or Federal, government authority or law enforcement agency relating to\xe2\x80\x9d a violation of a law subject to the CFPB\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction.\n\n12 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa75567(a)(1). To qualify as a \xe2\x80\x9ccovered employee,\xe2\x80\x9d an individual need not provide information to the CFPB, or any other entity. See \xc2\xa75567(b) (\xe2\x80\x9ccovered employee\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9cany individual performing tasks related to the offering or provision of a consumer financial product or service\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hen Congress includes particular language in one section of a statute but omits it in another[,] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. this Court presumes that Congress intended a difference in meaning.\xe2\x80\x9d Loughrin v. United States, 573 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 6) (internal quotation marks and alteration omitted). Congress placed a government-reporting requirement in \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h), but not elsewhere in the same statute. Courts are not at liberty to dispense with the condition\xe2\x80\x94tell the SEC\xe2\x80\x94Congress imposed.

\n

B

\n

Dodd-Frank\xe2\x80\x99s purpose and design corroborate our comprehension of \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h)\xe2\x80\x99s reporting requirement. The \xe2\x80\x9ccore objective\xe2\x80\x9d of Dodd-Frank\xe2\x80\x99s robust whistleblower program, as Somers acknowledges, Tr. of Oral Arg. 45, is \xe2\x80\x9cto motivate people who know of securities law violations to tell the SEC,\xe2\x80\x9d S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 111\xe2\x80\x93176, at 38 (emphasis added). By enlisting whistleblowers to \xe2\x80\x9cassist the Government [in] identify[ing] and prosecut[ing] persons who have violated securities laws,\xe2\x80\x9d Congress undertook to improve SEC enforcement and facilitate the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9crecover[y] [of] money for victims of financial fraud.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 110. To that end, \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936 provides substantial monetary rewards to whistleblowers who furnish actionable information to the SEC. See \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(b).

\n

Financial inducements alone, Congress recognized, may be insufficient to encourage certain employees, fearful of employer retaliation, to come forward with evidence of wrongdoing. Congress therefore complemented the Dodd-Frank monetary incentives for SEC reporting by heightening protection against retaliation. While Sarbanes-Oxley contains an administrative-exhaustion requirement, a 180-day administrative complaint-filing deadline, and a remedial scheme limited to actual damages, Dodd-Frank provides for immediate access to federal court, a generous statute of limitations (at least six years), and the opportunity to recover double backpay. See supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x936. Dodd-Frank\xe2\x80\x99s award program and anti-retaliation provision thus work synchronously to motivate individuals with knowledge of illegal activity to \xe2\x80\x9ctell the SEC.\xe2\x80\x9d S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 111\xe2\x80\x93176, at 38.

\n

When enacting Sarbanes-Oxley\xe2\x80\x99s whistleblower regime, in comparison, Congress had a more far-reaching objective: It sought to disturb the \xe2\x80\x9ccorporate code of silence\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cdiscourage[d] employees from reporting fraudulent behavior not only to the proper authorities, such as the FBI and the SEC, but even internally.\xe2\x80\x9d Lawson, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 4) (internal quotation marks omitted). Accordingly, the Sarbanes-Oxley anti-retaliation provision covers employees who report fraud not only to the SEC, but also to any other federal agency, Congress, or an internal supervisor. See\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71514A(a)(1).

\n

C

\n

In sum, Dodd-Frank\xe2\x80\x99s text and purpose leave no doubt that the term \xe2\x80\x9cwhistleblower\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h) carries the meaning set forth in the section\xe2\x80\x99s definitional provision. The disposition of this case is therefore evident: Somers did not provide information \xe2\x80\x9cto the Commission\xe2\x80\x9d before his termination, \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(a)(6), so he did not qualify as a \xe2\x80\x9cwhistleblower\xe2\x80\x9d at the time of the alleged retaliation. He is therefore ineligible to seek relief under \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h).

\n

III

\n

Somers and the Solicitor General tender a different view of Dodd-Frank\xe2\x80\x99s compass. The whistleblower definition, as they see it, applies only to the statute\xe2\x80\x99s award program, not to its anti-retaliation provision, and thus not, as the definition plainly states, throughout \xe2\x80\x9cthis section,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(a). See Brief for Respondent 30; Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 10\xe2\x80\x9311. For purposes of the anti-retaliation provision alone, they urge us to construe the term \xe2\x80\x9cwhistleblower\xe2\x80\x9d in its \xe2\x80\x9cordinary sense,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., without any SEC-reporting requirement. Brief for Respondent 18.

\n

Doing so, Somers and the Solicitor General contend, would align with our precedent, specifically Lawson v. Suwannee Fruit & S. S. Co.,\n\n336 U.\xc2\xa0S. 198 (1949)\n, and Utility Air Regulatory Group v. EPA, 573 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014). In those decisions, we declined to apply a statutory definition that ostensibly governed where doing so would have been \xe2\x80\x9cincompatible with .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. Congress\xe2\x80\x99 regulatory scheme,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at ___ (slip op., at 18) (internal quotation marks omitted), or would have \xe2\x80\x9cdestroy[ed] one of the [statute\xe2\x80\x99s] major purposes,\xe2\x80\x9d Suwannee Fruit, 336 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 201.

\n

This case is of a piece, Somers and the Solicitor General maintain. Applying the statutory definition here, they variously charge, would \xe2\x80\x9ccreate obvious incongruities,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 19 (internal quotation marks omitted), \xe2\x80\x9cproduce anomalous results,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 22, \xe2\x80\x9cvitiate much of the [statute\xe2\x80\x99s] protection,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 20 (internal quotation marks omitted), and, as the Court of Appeals put it, narrow clause (iii) of \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h)(1)(A) \xe2\x80\x9cto the point of absurdity,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 35 (quoting 850 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1049). We next address these concerns and explain why they do not lead us to depart from the statutory text.

\n

A

\n

It would gut \xe2\x80\x9cmuch of the protection afforded by\xe2\x80\x9d the third clause of \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h)(1)(a), Somers and the Solicitor General urge most strenuously, to apply the whistleblower definition to the anti-retaliation provision. Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 20 (internal quotation marks omitted); Brief for Respondent 28\xe2\x80\x9329. As earlier noted, see supra, at 4\xe2\x80\x935, clause (iii) prohibits retaliation against a \xe2\x80\x9cwhistleblower\xe2\x80\x9d for \xe2\x80\x9cmaking disclosures\xe2\x80\x9d to various persons and entities, including but not limited to the SEC, to the extent those disclosures are \xe2\x80\x9crequired or protected under\xe2\x80\x9d various laws other than Dodd-Frank. \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h)(1)(A)(iii). Applying the statutory definition of whistleblower, however, would limit clause (iii)\xe2\x80\x99s protection to \xe2\x80\x9conly those individuals who report to the Commission.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 22.

\n

The plain-text reading of the statute undoubtedly shields fewer individuals from retaliation than the alternative proffered by Somers and the Solicitor General. But we do not agree that this consequence \xe2\x80\x9cvitiate[s]\xe2\x80\x9d clause (iii)\xe2\x80\x99s protection, id., at 20 (internal quotation marks omitted), or ranks as \xe2\x80\x9cabsur[d],\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 35 (internal quotation marks omitted).[6] In fact, our reading leaves the third clause with \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial meaning.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 32.

\n

With the statutory definition incorporated, clause (iii) protects a whistleblower who reports misconduct both to the SEC and to another entity, but suffers retaliation because of the latter, non-SEC, disclosure. That would be so, for example, where the retaliating employer is un- aware that the employee has alerted the SEC. In such a case, without clause (iii), retaliation for internal reporting would not be reached by Dodd-Frank, for clause (i) applies only where the employer retaliates against the employee \xe2\x80\x9cbecause of\xe2\x80\x9d the SEC reporting. \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h)(1)(A). More- over, even where the employer knows of the SEC reporting, the third clause may operate to dispel a proof problem: The employee can recover under the statute without having to demonstrate whether the retaliation was motivated by the internal report (thus yielding protection under clause (iii)) or by the SEC disclosure (thus gaining protection under clause (i)).

\n

While the Solicitor General asserts that limiting the protections of clause (iii) to dual reporters would \xe2\x80\x9cshrink to insignificance the [clause\xe2\x80\x99s] ban on retaliation,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 22 (internal quotation marks omitted), he offers scant evidence to support that assertion. Tugging in the opposite direction, he reports that approximately 80 percent of the whistleblowers who received awards in 2016 \xe2\x80\x9creported internally before reporting to the Commission.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 23. And Digital Realty cites real-world examples of dual reporters seeking Dodd-Frank or Sarbanes-Oxley recovery for alleged retaliation. See Brief for Petitioner 33, and n.\xc2\xa04 (collecting cases). Overlooked by Somers and the Solicitor General, in dual-reporting cases, retaliation not prompted by SEC disclosures (and thus unaddressed by clause (i)) is likely commonplace: The SEC is required to protect the identity of whistleblowers, see \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h)(2)(A), so employers will often be unaware that an employee has reported to the Commission. In any event, even if the number of individuals qualifying for protection under clause (iii) is relatively limited, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t is our function to give the statute the effect its language suggests, however modest that may be.\xe2\x80\x9d Morrison v. National Australia Bank Ltd.,\n\n561 U.\xc2\xa0S. 247,\n270 (2010)\n.

\n

B

\n

Somers and the Solicitor General express concern that our reading would jettison protection for auditors, attorneys, and other employees subject to internal-reporting requirements. See Brief for Respondent 35; Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 21. Sarbanes-Oxley, for example, requires auditors and attorneys to report certain information within the company before making disclosures externally. See 15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa778j\xe2\x80\x931(b), 7245; 17 CFR \xc2\xa7205.3. If the whistleblower definition applies, Somers and the Solicitor General fear, these professionals will be \xe2\x80\x9cle[ft] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. vulnerable to discharge or other retaliatory action for complying with\xe2\x80\x9d their internal-reporting obligations. Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 22 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

Our reading shields employees in these circumstances, however, as soon as they also provide relevant information to the Commission. True, such employees will remain ineligible for Dodd-Frank\xe2\x80\x99s protection until they tell the SEC, but this result is consistent with Congress\xe2\x80\x99 aim to encourage SEC disclosures. See S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 111\xe2\x80\x93176, at 38; supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x934, 11. Somers worries that lawyers and auditors will face retaliation quickly, before they have a chance to report to the SEC. Brief for Respondent 35\xe2\x80\x9336. But he offers nothing to show that Congress had this concern in mind when it enacted \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h). Indeed, Congress may well have considered adequate the safeguards already afforded by Sarbanes-Oxley, protections specifi- cally designed to shield lawyers, accountants, and similar professionals. See Lawson, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 17).

\n

C

\n

Applying the whistleblower definition as written, Somers and the Solicitor General further protest, will create \xe2\x80\x9can incredibly unusual statutory scheme\xe2\x80\x9d: \xe2\x80\x9c[I]dentical misconduct\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94i.e., retaliating against an employee for internal reporting\xe2\x80\x94will \xe2\x80\x9cgo punished or not based on the happenstance of a separate report\xe2\x80\x9d to the SEC, of which the wrongdoer may \xe2\x80\x9cnot even be aware.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 37\xe2\x80\x9338. See also Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 24. The upshot, the Solicitor General warns, \xe2\x80\x9cwould [be] substantially diminish[ed] Dodd-Fran[k] deterrent effect.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

Overlooked in this protest is Dodd-Frank\xe2\x80\x99s core objective: to prompt reporting to the SEC. Supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x934, 11. In view of that precise aim, it is understandable that the statute\xe2\x80\x99s retaliation protections, like its financial rewards, would be reserved for employees who have done what Dodd-Frank seeks to achieve, i.e., they have placed information about unlawful activity before the Commission to aid its enforcement efforts.

\n

D

\n

Pointing to another purported anomaly attending the reading we adopt today, the Solicitor General observes that neither the whistleblower definition nor \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h) contains any requirement of a \xe2\x80\x9ctemporal or topical connection between the violation reported to the Commission and the internal disclosure for which the employee suffers retaliation.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 25. It is therefore possible, the Solicitor General posits, that \xe2\x80\x9can employee who was fired for reporting accounting fraud to his supervisor in 2017 would have a cause of action under [\xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h)] if he had reported an insider-trading violation by his previous employer to the Commission in 2012.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. For its part, Digital Realty agrees that this scenario could arise, but does not see it as a cause for concern: \xe2\x80\x9cCongress,\xe2\x80\x9d it states, \xe2\x80\x9ccould reasonably have made the policy judgment that individuals who report securities-law violations to the SEC should receive broad protection over time against retaliation for a variety of disclosures.\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief 11.

\n

We need not dwell on the situation hypothesized by the Solicitor General, for it veers far from the case before us. We note, however, that the interpretation offered by Somers and the Solicitor General\xe2\x80\x94i.e., ignoring the statutory definition when construing the anti-retaliation provision\xe2\x80\x94raises an even thornier question about the law\xe2\x80\x99s scope. Their view, which would not require an employee to provide information relating to a securities-law violation to the SEC, could afford Dodd-Frank protection to an employee who reports information bearing no relationship whatever to the securities laws. That prospect could be imagined based on the broad array of federal statutes and regulations cross-referenced by clause (iii) of the anti-retaliation provision. E.g., \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71513(e) (criminalizing retaliation for \xe2\x80\x9cproviding to a law enforcement officer any truthful information relating to the commission .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of any Federal offense\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); see supra, at 5, and n. 2. For example, an employee fired for reporting a coworker\xe2\x80\x99s drug dealing to the Federal Bureau of Investigation might be protected. Brief for Petitioner 38. It would make scant sense, however, to rank an FBI drug-trafficking informant a whistleblower under Dodd-Frank, a law concerned only with encouraging the reporting of \xe2\x80\x9csecurities law violations.\xe2\x80\x9d S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 111\xe2\x80\x93176, at 38 (emphasis added).

\n

E

\n

Finally, the interpretation we adopt, the Solicitor General adds, would undermine not just clause (iii) of \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h)(1)(A), but clause (ii) as well. Clause (ii) prohibits retaliation against a whistleblower for \xe2\x80\x9cinitiating, testifying in, or assisting in any investigation or .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. action of the Commission based upon\xe2\x80\x9d information conveyed to the SEC by a whistleblower in accordance with the statute. \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h)(1)(A)(ii). If the whistleblower definition is applied to \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(h), the Solicitor General states, \xe2\x80\x9can employer could fire an employee for giving .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. testimony [to the SEC] if the employee had not previously reported to the Commission online or through the specified written form\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94i.e., the methods currently prescribed by Rule 21F\xe2\x80\x939 for a whistleblower to provide information to the Commission. Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 20\xe2\x80\x9321 (citing 17 CFR \xc2\xa7240.21F\xe2\x80\x939(a)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(2)).

\n

But the statute expressly delegates authority to the SEC to establish the \xe2\x80\x9cmanner\xe2\x80\x9d in which information may be provided to the Commission by a whistleblower. See \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936(a)(6). Nothing in today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion prevents the agency from enumerating additional means of SEC reporting\xe2\x80\x94including through testimony protected by clause (ii).

\n

IV

\n

For the foregoing reasons, we find the statute\xe2\x80\x99s definition of \xe2\x80\x9cwhistleblower\xe2\x80\x9d clear and conclusive. Because \xe2\x80\x9cCongress has directly spoken to the precise question at issue,\xe2\x80\x9d Chevron, 467 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 842, we do not accord deference to the contrary view advanced by the SEC in Rule 21F\xe2\x80\x932. See 17 CFR \xc2\xa7240.21F\xe2\x80\x932(b)(1); supra, at 6\xe2\x80\x937. The statute\xe2\x80\x99s unambiguous whistleblower definition, in short, precludes the Commission from more expansively interpreting that term. See Burgess, 553 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 130.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

The judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

I join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in full. I write separately only to note my disagreement with the suggestion in my colleague\xe2\x80\x99s concurrence that a Senate Report is not an appropriate source for this Court to consider when interpreting a statute.

\n

Legislative history is of course not the law, but that does not mean it cannot aid us in our understanding of a law. Just as courts are capable of assessing the reliability and utility of evidence generally, they are capable of assessing the reliability and utility of legislative-history materials.

\n

Committee reports, like the Senate Report the Court discusses here, see ante, at 3\xe2\x80\x934, 11\xe2\x80\x9312, 16\xe2\x80\x9318, are a particularly reliable source to which we can look to ensure our fidelity to Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intended meaning. See Garcia v. United States,\n\n469 U.\xc2\xa0S. 70,\n76 (1984)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cIn surveying legislative history we have repeatedly stated that the authoritative source for finding the Legislature\xe2\x80\x99s intent lies in the Committee Reports on the bill, which \xe2\x80\x98represen[t] the considered and collective understanding of those Congressmen involved in drafting and studying proposed legislation\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Zuber v. Allen,\n\n396 U.\xc2\xa0S. 168,\n186 (1969)\n)). Bills presented to Congress for consideration are generally accompanied by a committee report. Such reports are typically circulated at least two days before a bill is to be considered on the floor and provide Members of Congress and their staffs with information about \xe2\x80\x9ca bill\xe2\x80\x99s context, purposes, policy implications, and details,\xe2\x80\x9d along with information on its supporters and opponents. R. Katzmann, Judging Statutes 20, and n. 62 (2014) (citing A. LaRue, Senate Manual Containing the Standing Rules, Orders, Laws, and Resolutions Affecting the Business of the United States Senate, S. Doc. No. 107\xe2\x80\x931, p. 17 (2001)). These materials \xe2\x80\x9chave long been important means of informing the whole chamber about proposed legislation,\xe2\x80\x9d Katzmann, Judging Statutes, at 19, a point Members themselves have emphasized over the years.[1]* It is thus no surprise that legislative staffers view committee and conference reports as the most reliable type of legislative history. See Gluck & Bressman, Statutory Interpretation From the Inside\xe2\x80\x94An Empirical Study of Congressional Drafting, Delegation and the Canons: Part I, 65 Stan. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 901, 977 (2013).

\n

Legislative history can be particularly helpful when a statute is ambiguous or deals with especially complex matters. But even when, as here, a statute\xe2\x80\x99s meaning can clearly be discerned from its text, consulting reliable legislative history can still be useful, as it enables us to corroborate and fortify our understanding of the text. See, e.g., Tapia v. United States,\n\n564 U.\xc2\xa0S. 319\n\xe2\x80\x93332 (2011); Carr v. United States,\n\n560 U.\xc2\xa0S. 438\n\xe2\x80\x93458 (2010). Moreover, confirming our construction of a statute by considering reliable legislative history shows respect for and promotes comity with a coequal branch of Government. See Katzmann, Judging Statutes, at 35\xe2\x80\x9336.

\n

For these reasons, I do not think it wise for judges to close their eyes to reliable legislative history\xe2\x80\x94and the realities of how Members of Congress create and enact laws\xe2\x80\x94when it is available.
