PAROLINE v. UNITED STATES et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit

\n

No. 12\xe2\x80\x938561.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 22, 2014\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 23, 2014

\n

The respondent victim in this case was sexually abused as a young girl in order to produce child pornography. When she was 17, she learned that images of her abuse were being trafficked on the Internet, in effect repeating the original wrongs, for she knew that her humiliation and hurt would be renewed well into the future as thousands of additional wrongdoers witnessed those crimes. Petitioner Paroline pleaded guilty in federal court to possessing images of child pornography, which included two of the victim, in violation of\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72252. The victim then sought restitution under \xc2\xa72259, requesting nearly $3 million in lost income and about $500,000 in future treatment and counseling costs. The District Court declined to award restitution, concluding that the Government had not met its burden of proving what losses, if any, were proximately caused by Paroline\xe2\x80\x99s offense. The victim sought a writ of mandamus, asking the Fifth Circuit to direct the District Court to order Paroline to pay restitution. Granting the writ on rehearing en banc, the Fifth Circuit held, inter alia, that \xc2\xa72259 did not limit restitution to losses proximately caused by the defendant, and that each defendant who possessed the victim\xe2\x80\x99s images should be made liable for the victim\xe2\x80\x99s entire losses from the trade in her images.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n 1.\xc2\xa0Restitution is proper under \xc2\xa72259 only to the extent the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s offense proximately caused a victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses. This provision has a broad restitutionary purpose, stating that a district court \xe2\x80\x9cshall order restitution for any offense\xe2\x80\x9d under Chapter 110 of Title 18, such as Paroline\xe2\x80\x99s possession offense; requiring district courts to order defendants \xe2\x80\x9cto pay the victim .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the full amount of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses as determined by the court,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72259(b)(1); and expressly making \xe2\x80\x9cissuance of a restitution order .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. mandatory,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72259(b)(4)(A). The Government has the \xe2\x80\x9cburden of demonstrating the amount of the [victim\xe2\x80\x99s] loss.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73664(e).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n To say one event proximately caused another means, first, that the former event caused the latter, i.e., actual cause or cause in fact; and second, that it is a proximate cause, i.e., it has a sufficient connection to the result. The concept of proximate causation is applicable in both criminal and tort law, and the analysis is parallel in many instances. Section 2259(c) defines a victim as \xe2\x80\x9cthe individual harmed as a result of a commission of a crime under this chapter.\xe2\x80\x9d The words \xe2\x80\x9cas a result of\xe2\x80\x9d plainly suggest causation, and the referent of \xe2\x80\x9ca crime\xe2\x80\x9d is the offense of conviction. The \xe2\x80\x9cfull amount of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72259(b)(1), includes \xe2\x80\x9cany costs incurred by the victim\xe2\x80\x9d for six enumerated categories of expense, \xc2\xa72259(b)(3). The reference to \xe2\x80\x9ccosts incurred by the victim\xe2\x80\x9d is most naturally understood as costs arising \xe2\x80\x9cas a result of\xe2\x80\x9d the offense of conviction, i.e., the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conduct. And the last of the six enumerated categories\xe2\x80\x94for \xe2\x80\x9cother losses suffered .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. as a proximate result of the offense,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72259(b)(3)(F)\xe2\x80\x94clearly states that the causal requirement is one of proximate cause. This reading is supported by the canon of construction that, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen several words are followed by a clause which is applicable as much to the first and other words as to the last, the natural construction of the language demands that the clause be read as applicable to all.\xe2\x80\x9d Porto Rico Railway, Light & Power Co. v. Mor,\n\n253 U.\xc2\xa0S. 345\n. The reading also presents a commonsense way to impose sensible limitations on claims for attenuated costs. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n 2.\xc2\xa0Applying the statute\xe2\x80\x99s causation requirements in this case, victims should be compensated and defendants should be held to account for the impact of their conduct on those victims, but defendants should only be made liable for the consequences and gravity of their own conduct, not the conduct of others. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9325.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (a)\xc2\xa0A somewhat atypical causal process underlies the losses here. It may be simple to prove aggregate losses, i.e., \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral losses,\xe2\x80\x9d stemming from the ongoing traffic in the victim\xe2\x80\x99s images, but the question for \xc2\xa72259 purposes is how much of these general losses were the \xe2\x80\x9cproximate result\xe2\x80\x9d of an individual defendant\xe2\x80\x99s offense. Here, the victim\xe2\x80\x99s costs of treatment and lost income resulting from the trauma of knowing that images of her abuse are being viewed over and over are direct and foreseeable results of child-pornography crimes, provided the prerequisite of factual causation is satisfied. The primary problem, then, is the proper standard of causation in fact. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (b)\xc2\xa0A showing of but-for causation is not the proper standard here, for it is not possible to prove that the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses would be less but for one possessor\xe2\x80\x99s individual role in the large, loosely connected network through which her images circulate. The victim and the Government urge the Court to read \xc2\xa72259 to require a less restrictive causation standard in child-pornography cases like this. They endorse the theory of \xe2\x80\x9caggregate causation,\xe2\x80\x9d one formulation of which finds factual causation satisfied where a wrongdoer\xe2\x80\x99s conduct, though alone \xe2\x80\x9cinsufficient .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to cause the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s harm,\xe2\x80\x9d is, \xe2\x80\x9cwhen combined with conduct by other persons,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cmore than sufficient to cause the harm.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 Restatement (Third) of Torts: Liability for Physicaland Emotional Harm \xc2\xa727, Comment f. Tort law teaches that such alternative causal tests, though a kind of legal fiction, may be necessary to vindicate the law\xe2\x80\x99s purposes, for it would be anomalous to turn away a person harmed by the combined acts of many wrongdoers simply because none of those wrongdoers alone caused the harm, and nonsensical to adopt a rule whereby individuals hurt by the combined wrongful acts of many would have no redress, while those hurt by the acts of one person alone would. These are sound principles. Taken too far, however, such alternative causal standards would treat each possessor as the cause in fact of all the trauma and attendant losses incurred as a result of all the ongoing traffic in the victim\xe2\x80\x99s images. Aggregate causation logic should not be adopted in an incautious manner in the context of criminal restitution, which differs from tort law in numerous respects. Paroline\xe2\x80\x99s contribution to the causal process underlying the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses was very minor, both compared to the combined acts of all other relevant offenders and compared to the contributions of other individual offenders, particularly distributors and the initial producer of the child pornography. Congress gave no indication that it intended the statute to be applied in an expansive manner so starkly contrary to the principle that restitution should reflect the consequences of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s own conduct. The victim claims that holding each possessor liable for her entire losses would be fair and practical in part because offenders can seek contribution from one another, but there is no general federal right to contribution and no specific statutory authorization for contribution here. Her severe approach could also raise questions under the Excessive Fines Clause of the\n \nEighth Amendment. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (c)\xc2\xa0While the victim\xe2\x80\x99s expansive reading must be rejected, that does not mean the broader principles underlying aggregate causation theories are irrelevant to determining the proper outcome in cases like this. The cause of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s general losses is the trade in her images, and Paroline is a part of that cause. Just as it undermines the purposes of tort law to turn away plaintiffs harmed by several wrongdoers, it would undermine \xc2\xa72259\xe2\x80\x99s purposes to turn away victims in cases like this. With respect to the statute\xe2\x80\x99s remedial purpose, there is no question that it would produce anomalous results to say that no restitution is appropriate in these circumstances, for harms of the kind the victim endured here are a major reason why child pornography is outlawed. The unlawful conduct of everyone who reproduces, distributes, or possesses images of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s abuse\xe2\x80\x94including Paroline\xe2\x80\x94plays a part in sustaining and aggravating this tragedy. And there is no doubt Congress wanted restitution for such victims. Denying restitution would also be at odds with \xc2\xa72259\xe2\x80\x99s penological purposes, which include the need to impress upon offenders that their conduct produces concrete and devastating harms for real, identifiable victims. Thus, where it can be shown both that a defendant possessed a victim\xe2\x80\x99s images and that a victim has outstanding losses caused by the continuing traffic in her images but where it is impossible to trace a particular amount of those losses to the individual defendant utilizing a more traditional causal inquiry, a court should order restitution in an amount that comports with the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s relative role in the causal process underlying the victim\xe2\x80\x99s general losses.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n District courts should use discretion and sound judgment in determining the proper amount of restitution. A variety of factors may serve as guideposts. Courts might, as a start, determine the amount of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses caused by the continuing traffic in the victim\xe2\x80\x99s images, and then base an award on factors bearing on the relative causal significance of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conduct in producing those losses. The victim finds this approach untenable because her losses are \xe2\x80\x9cindivisible,\xe2\x80\x9d but the Court is required to define a causal standard that effects the statute\xe2\x80\x99s purposes, not to apply tort-law concepts in a mechanical way in the criminal restitution context. She also argues she will be consigned to \xe2\x80\x9cpiecemeal\xe2\x80\x9d restitution that may never lead to full recovery, but Congress has not promised victims full and swift restitution at the cost of holding a defendant liable for an amount drastically out of proportion to his individual causal relation to those losses. Furthermore, this approach better effects the need to impress upon defendants that their acts are not irrelevant or victimless. Pp.\xc2\xa019\xe2\x80\x9325.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (d)\xc2\xa0Though this approach is not without difficulties, courts can only do their best to apply the statute as written in a workable manner, faithful to the competing principles at stake: that victims should be compensated and that defendants should be held to account for the impact of their own conduct, not the conduct of others. District courts, which routinely exercise wide discretion both in sentencing generally and in fashioning restitution orders, should be able to apply the causal standard defined here without further detailed guidance. P.\xc2\xa025.

DOYLE RANDALL PAROLINE, PETITIONER v. UNITED STATES, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit

\n

[April 23, 2014]

\n

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Justice Kennedy delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This case presents the question of how to determine the amount of restitution a possessor of child pornography must pay to the victim whose childhood abuse appears in the pornographic materials possessed. The relevant statutory provisions are set forth at\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72259. Enacted as a component of the Violence Against Women Act of 1994, \xc2\xa72259 requires district courts to award restitution for certain federal criminal offenses, including child-pornography possession.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Petitioner Doyle Randall Paroline pleaded guilty to such an offense. He admitted to possessing between 150 and 300 images of child pornography, which included two that depicted the sexual exploitation of a young girl, now a young woman, who goes by the pseudonym \xe2\x80\x9cAmy\xe2\x80\x9d for this litigation. The question is what causal relationship must be established between the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conduct and a victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses for purposes of determining the right to, and the amount of, restitution under \xc2\xa72259.

\n

I

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Three decades ago, this Court observed that \xe2\x80\x9cthe exploitive use of children in the production of pornography has become a serious national problem.\xe2\x80\x9d New York v. Ferber,\n\n458 U.\xc2\xa0S. 747,\n749 (1982)\n. The demand for child pornography harms children in part because it drives production, which involves child abuse. The harms caused by child pornography, however, are still more extensive because child pornography is \xe2\x80\x9ca permanent record\xe2\x80\x9d of the depicted child\xe2\x80\x99s abuse, and \xe2\x80\x9cthe harm to the child is exacerbated by [its] circulation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 759. Because child pornography is now traded with ease on the Internet, \xe2\x80\x9cthe number of still images and videos memorializing the sexual assault and other sexual exploitation of children, many very young in age, has grown exponentially.\xe2\x80\x9d United States Sentencing Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, P. Saris et\xc2\xa0al., FederalChild Pornography Offenses 3 (2012) (hereinafter Sentencing Comm\xe2\x80\x99n Report).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n One person whose story illustrates the devastating harm caused by child pornography is the respondent victim in this case. When she was eight and nine years old, she was sexually abused by her uncle in order to produce child pornography. Her uncle was prosecuted, required to pay about $6,000 in restitution, and sentenced to a lengthy prison term. The victim underwent an initial course of therapy beginning in 1998 and continuing into 1999. By the end of this period, her therapist\xe2\x80\x99s notes reported that she was \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98back to normal\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d; her involvement in dance and other age-appropriate activities, and the support of her family, justified an optimistic assessment. App. 70\xe2\x80\x9371. Her functioning appeared to decline in her teenage years, however; and a major blow to her recovery came when, at the age of 17, she learned that images of her abuse were being trafficked on the Internet. Id., at 71. The digital images were available nationwide and no doubt worldwide. Though the exact scale of the trade in her images is unknown, the possessors to date easily number in the thousands. The knowledge that her images were circulated far and wide renewed the victim\xe2\x80\x99s trauma and made it difficult for her to recover from her abuse. As she explained in a victim impact statement submitted to the District Court in this case:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cEvery day of my life I live in constant fear that someone will see my pictures and recognize me and that I will be humiliated all over again. It hurts me to know someone is looking at them\xe2\x80\x94at me\xe2\x80\x94when I was just a little girl being abused for the camera. I did not choose to be there, but now I am there forever in pictures that people are using to do sick things. I want it all erased. I want it all stopped. But I am powerless to stop it just like I was powerless to stop my uncle.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. My life and my feelings are worse now because the crime has never really stopped and will never really stop.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. It\xe2\x80\x99s like I am being abused over and over and over again.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 60\xe2\x80\x9361.

\n

The victim says in her statement that her fear and trauma make it difficult for her to trust others or to feel that she has control over what happens to her. Id., at 63.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The full extent of this victim\xe2\x80\x99s suffering is hard to grasp. Her abuser took away her childhood, her self-conception of her innocence, and her freedom from the kind of nightmares and memories that most others will never know. These crimes were compounded by the distribution of images of her abuser\xe2\x80\x99s horrific acts, which meant the wrongs inflicted upon her were in effect repeated; for she knew her humiliation and hurt were and would be renewed into the future as an ever-increasing number of wrongdoers witnessed the crimes committed against her.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Petitioner Paroline is one of the individuals who possessed this victim\xe2\x80\x99s images. In 2009, he pleaded guilty in federal court to one count of possession of material involving the sexual exploitation of children in violation of\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72252. 672 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 781, 783 (ED Tex. 2009). Paroline admitted to knowing possession of between 150 and 300 images of child pornography, two of which depicted the respondent victim. Ibid. The victim sought restitution under \xc2\xa72259, asking for close to $3.4 million, consistingof nearly $3 million in lost income and about $500,000in future treatment and counseling costs. App. 52, 104. She also sought attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees and costs. 672 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 783. The parties submitted competing expert reports. They stipulated that the victim did not know who Paroline was and that none of her claimed losses flowed from any specific knowledge about him or his offense conduct. Id., at 792, and n.\xc2\xa011; App. 230.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n After briefing and hearings, the District Court declined to award restitution. 672 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 793. The District Court observed that \xe2\x80\x9ceveryone involved with child pornography\xe2\x80\x94from the abusers and producers to the end-users and possessors\xe2\x80\x94contribute[s] to [the victim\xe2\x80\x99s] ongoing harm.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 792. But it concluded that the Government had the burden of proving the amount of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses \xe2\x80\x9cdirectly produced by Paroline that would not have occurred without his possession of her images.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 791. The District Court found that, under this standard, the Government had failed to meet its burden of proving what losses, if any, were proximately caused by Paroline\xe2\x80\x99s offense. It thus held that \xe2\x80\x9can award of restitution is not appropriate in this case.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 793.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The victim sought a writ of mandamus, asking the United States Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit to direct the District Court to order Paroline to pay restitution in the amount requested. In\xc2\xa0re Amy, 591 F.\xc2\xa03d 792, 793 (2009). The Court of Appeals denied relief. Id., at 795. The victim sought rehearing. Her rehearing request was granted, as was her petition for a writ of mandamus. In\xc2\xa0re Amy Unknown, 636 F.\xc2\xa03d 190, 201 (2011).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Fifth Circuit reheard the case en banc along with another case, in which the defendant, Michael Wright, had raised similar issues in appealing an order of restitution under \xc2\xa72259, see United States v. Wright, 639 F.\xc2\xa03d 679, 681 (2011) (per curiam). As relevant, the Court of Appeals set out to determine the level of proof required to award restitution to victims in cases like this. It held that \xc2\xa72259 did not limit restitution to losses proximately caused by the defendant, and each defendant who possessed the victim\xe2\x80\x99s images should be made liable for the victim\xe2\x80\x99s entire losses from the trade in her images, even though other offenders played a role in causing those losses. In\xc2\xa0re Amy Unknown, 701 F.\xc2\xa03d 749, 772\xe2\x80\x93774 (2012) (en banc).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Paroline sought review here. Certiorari was granted to resolve a conflict in the Courts of Appeals over the proper causation inquiry for purposes of determining the entitlement to and amount of restitution under \xc2\xa72259. 570 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2013). For the reasons set forth, the decision of the Court of Appeals is vacated.

\n

II

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Title\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72259(a) provides that a district court \xe2\x80\x9cshall order restitution for any offense\xe2\x80\x9d under Chapter 110 of Title 18, which covers a number of offenses involving the sexual exploitation of children and child pornography in particular. Paroline was convicted of knowingly possessing child pornography under \xc2\xa72252, a Chapter 110 offense.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Section 2259 states a broad restitutionary purpose: It requires district courts to order defendants \xe2\x80\x9cto pay the victim .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the full amount of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses as determined by the court,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72259(b)(1), and expressly states that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he issuance of a restitution order under this section is mandatory,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72259(b)(4)(A). Section 2259(b)(2) provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n order of restitution under this section shall be issued and enforced in accordance with section 3664,\xe2\x80\x9d which in turn provides in relevant part that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he burden of demonstrating the amount of the loss sustained by a victim as a result of the offense shall be on the attorney for the Government,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73664(e).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The threshold question the Court faces is whether \xc2\xa72259 limits restitution to those losses proximately caused by the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s offense conduct. The Fifth Circuit held that it does not, contrary to the holdings of other Courts of Appeals to have addressed the question. Compare, e.g., 701 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 752 (no general proximate-cause requirement applies under \xc2\xa72259), with United States v. Rogers, 714 F.\xc2\xa03d 82, 89 (CA1 2013) (general proximate-cause requirement applies under \xc2\xa72259); United States v. Benoit, 713 F.\xc2\xa03d 1, 20 (CA10 2013) (same); United States v. Fast, 709 F.\xc2\xa03d 712, 721\xe2\x80\x93722 (CA8 2013) (same); United States v. Laraneta, 700 F.\xc2\xa03d 983, 989\xe2\x80\x93990 (CA7 2012) (same); United States v. Burgess, 684 F.\xc2\xa03d 445, 456\xe2\x80\x93457 (CA4 2012) (same); United States v. Evers, 669 F.\xc2\xa03d 645, 659 (CA6 2012) (same); United States v. Aumais, 656 F.\xc2\xa03d 147, 153 (CA2 2011) (same); United States v. Kennedy, 643 F.\xc2\xa03d 1251, 1261 (CA9 2011) (same); United States v. Monzel, 641 F.\xc2\xa03d 528, 535 (CADC 2011) (same); United States v. McDaniel, 631 F.\xc2\xa03d 1204, 1208\xe2\x80\x931209 (CA11 2011) (same).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n As a general matter, to say one event proximately caused another is a way of making two separate but related assertions. First, it means the former event caused the latter. This is known as actual cause or cause in fact. The concept of actual cause \xe2\x80\x9cis not a metaphysical one but an ordinary, matter-of-fact inquiry into the existence .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of a causal relation as laypeople would view it.\xe2\x80\x9d 4 F. Harper, F. James, & O. Gray, Torts \xc2\xa720.2, p. 100 (3d ed. 2007).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Every event has many causes, however, see ibid., and only some of them are proximate, as the law uses that term. So to say that one event was a proximate cause of another means that it was not just any cause, but one with a sufficient connection to the result. The idea of proximate cause, as distinct from actual cause or cause in fact, defies easy summary. It is \xe2\x80\x9ca flexible concept,\xe2\x80\x9d Bridge v. Phoenix Bond & Indemnity Co.,\n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 639,\n654 (2008)\n, that generally \xe2\x80\x9crefers to the basic requirement that .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. there must be \xe2\x80\x98some direct relation between the injury asserted and the injurious conduct alleged,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d CSX Transp., Inc. v. McBride, 564 U. S. ___, ___ (2011) (Roberts, C.\xc2\xa0J., dissenting) (slip op., at 3) (quoting Holmes v. Securities Investor Protection Corporation,\n\n503 U.\xc2\xa0S. 258,\n268 (1992)\n). The concept of proximate causation is applicable in both criminal and tort law, and the analysis is parallel in many instances. 1 W. LaFave, Substantive Criminal Law \xc2\xa76.4(c), p. 471 (2d ed. 2003) (hereinafter LaFave). Proximate cause is often explicated in terms of foreseeability or the scope of the risk created by the predicate conduct. See, e.g., ibid.; 1 Restatement (Third) of Torts: Liability for Physical and Emotional Harm \xc2\xa729, p. 493 (2005) (hereinafter Restatement). A requirement of proximate cause thus serves, inter alia, to preclude liability in situations where the causal link between conduct and result is so attenuated that the consequence is more aptly described as mere fortuity. Exxon Co., U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. v. Sofec, Inc.,\n\n517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 830\n\xe2\x80\x93839 (1996).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n All parties agree \xc2\xa72259 imposes some causation requirement. The statute defines a victim as \xe2\x80\x9cthe individual harmed as a result of a commission of a crime under this chapter.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72259(c). The words \xe2\x80\x9cas a result of\xe2\x80\x9d plainly suggest causation. See Pacific Operators Offshore, LLP v. Valladolid, 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 13); see also Burrage v. United States, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 5). And a straightforward reading of \xc2\xa72259(c) indicates that the term \xe2\x80\x9ca crime\xe2\x80\x9d refers to the offense of conviction. Cf. Hughey v. United States,\n\n495 U.\xc2\xa0S. 411,\n416 (1990)\n. So if the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s offense conduct did not cause harm to an individual, that individual is by definition not a \xe2\x80\x9cvictim\xe2\x80\x9d entitled to restitution under \xc2\xa72259.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n As noted above, \xc2\xa72259 requires a court to order restitution for \xe2\x80\x9cthe full amount of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72259(b)(1), which the statute defines to include \xe2\x80\x9cany costs incurred by the victim\xe2\x80\x9d for six enumerated categories of expense, \xc2\xa72259(b)(3). The reference to \xe2\x80\x9ccosts incurred by the victim\xe2\x80\x9d is most naturally understood as costs stemming from the source that qualifies an individual as a \xe2\x80\x9cvictim\xe2\x80\x9d in the first place\xe2\x80\x94namely, ones arising \xe2\x80\x9cas a result of\xe2\x80\x9d the offense. Thus, as is typically the case with criminal restitution, \xc2\xa72259 is intended to compensate victims for losses caused by the offense of conviction. See id., at 416. This is an important point, for it means the central concern of the causal inquiry must be the conduct of the particular defendant from whom restitution is sought.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n But there is a further question whether restitution under \xc2\xa72259 is limited to losses proximately caused by the offense. As noted, a requirement of proximate cause is more restrictive than a requirement of factual cause alone. Even if \xc2\xa72259 made no express reference to proximate causation, the Court might well hold that a showing of proximate cause was required. Proximate cause is a standard aspect of causation in criminal law and the law of torts. See 1 LaFave \xc2\xa76.4(a), at 464\xe2\x80\x93466; W. Keeton, D. Dobbs, R. Keeton, & D. Owen, Prosser and Keeton on Law of Torts \xc2\xa741, p. 263 (5th ed. 1984) (hereinafter Prosser and Keeton). Given proximate cause\xe2\x80\x99s traditional role in causation analysis, this Court has more than once found a proximate-cause requirement built into a statute that did not expressly impose one. See Holmes, supra, at 265\xe2\x80\x93268; Associated Gen. Contractors of Cal., Inc. v. Carpenters,\n\n459 U.\xc2\xa0S. 519\n\xe2\x80\x93536 (1983); see also CSX Transp., Inc., supra, at ___ (Roberts, C.\xc2\xa0J., dissenting) (slip op., at 4) (\xe2\x80\x9cWe have applied the standard requirement of proximate cause to actions under federal statutes where the text did not expressly provide for it\xe2\x80\x9d); Lexmark Int\xe2\x80\x99l, Inc. v. Static Control Components, Inc., ante, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Here, however, the interpretive task is easier, for the requirement of proximate cause is in the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text. The statute enumerates six categories of covered losses. \xc2\xa72259(b)(3). These include certain medical services, \xc2\xa72259(b)(3)(A); physical and occupational therapy, \xc2\xa72259(b)(3)(B); transportation, temporary housing, and child care, \xc2\xa72259(b)(3)(C); lost income, \xc2\xa72259(b)(3)(D); attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees and costs, \xc2\xa72259(b)(3)(E); and a final catchall category for \xe2\x80\x9cany other losses suffered by the victim as a proximate result of the offense,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72259(b)(3)(F).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The victim argues that because the \xe2\x80\x9cproximate result\xe2\x80\x9d language appears only in the final, catchall category of losses set forth at \xc2\xa72259(b)(3)(F), the statute has noproximate-cause requirement for losses falling within the prior enumerated categories. She justifies this reading of \xc2\xa72259(b) in part on the grammatical rule of the last antecedent, \xe2\x80\x9caccording to which a limiting clause or phrase .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. should ordinarily be read as modifying only the noun or phrase that it immediately follows.\xe2\x80\x9d Barnhart v. Thomas,\n\n540 U.\xc2\xa0S. 20,\n26 (2003)\n. But that rule is \xe2\x80\x9cnot an absolute and can assuredly be overcome by other indicia of meaning.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The Court has not applied it in a mechanical way where it would require accepting \xe2\x80\x9cunlikely premises.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Hayes,\n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 415,\n425 (2009)\n.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Other canons of statutory construction, moreover, work against the reading the victim suggests. \xe2\x80\x9cWhen several words are followed by a clause which is applicable as much to the first and other words as to the last, the natural construction of the language demands that the clause be read as applicable to all.\xe2\x80\x9d Porto Rico Railway, Light & Power Co. v. Mor,\n\n253 U.\xc2\xa0S. 345,\n348 (1920)\n. Furthermore, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t is .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a familiar canon of statutory construction that [catchall] clauses are to be read as bringing within a statute categories similar in type to those specifically enumerated.\xe2\x80\x9d Federal Maritime Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Seatrain Lines, Inc.,\n\n411 U.\xc2\xa0S. 726,\n734 (1973)\n. Here, \xc2\xa72259(b)(3)(F) defines a broad, final category of \xe2\x80\x9cother losses suffered .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. as a proximate result of the offense.\xe2\x80\x9d That category is most naturally understood as a summary of the type of losses covered\xe2\x80\x94i.e., losses suffered as a proximate result of the offense.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The victim says that if Congress had wanted to limit the losses recoverable under \xc2\xa72259 to those proximately caused by the offense, it could have written the statute the same way it wrote \xc2\xa72327, which provides for restitution to victims of telemarketing fraud. Section 2327, which is written and structured much like \xc2\xa72259, simply defines the term \xe2\x80\x9cfull amount of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9call losses suffered by the victim as a proximate result of the offense.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72327(b)(3). In essence the victim argues that the first five categories of losses enumerated in \xc2\xa72259(b)(3) would be superfluous if all were governed by a proximate-cause requirement. That, however, is unpersuasive. The first five categories provide guidance to district courts as to the specific types of losses Congress thought would often be the proximate result of a Chapter 110 offense and could as a general matter be included in an award of restitution.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Reading the statute to impose a general proximate-cause limitation accords with common sense. As noted above, proximate cause forecloses liability in situations where the causal link between conduct and result is so attenuated that the so-called consequence is more akin to mere fortuity. For example, suppose the traumatized victim of a Chapter 110 offender needed therapy and had a car accident on the way to her therapist\xe2\x80\x99s office. The resulting medical costs, in a literal sense, would be a factual result of the offense. But it would be strange indeed to make a defendant pay restitution for these costs. The victim herself concedes Congress did not intend costs like these to be recoverable under \xc2\xa72259. Brief for Respondent Amy 45. But she claims that it is unnecessary to \xe2\x80\x9cread .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. into\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72259 a proximate-cause limitation in order to exclude costs of that sort. Ibid. She says the statute \xe2\x80\x9ccontextually and inferentially require[s] a nexus for why\xe2\x80\x9d the losses were sustained\xe2\x80\x94i.e., a sufficient connection to child pornography. Id., at 46.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The victim may be right that the concept of proximate cause is not necessary to impose sensible limitations on restitution for remote consequences. But one very effective way, and perhaps the most obvious way, of excluding costs like those arising from the hypothetical car accident described above would be to incorporate a proximate-cause limitation into the statute. Congress did so, and for reasons given above the proximate-cause requirement applies to all the losses described in \xc2\xa72259. Restitution is therefore proper under \xc2\xa72259 only to the extent the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s offense proximately caused a victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses.

\n

III

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n There remains the difficult question of how to apply the statute\xe2\x80\x99s causation requirements in this case. The problem stems from the somewhat atypical causal process underlying the losses the victim claims here. It is perhaps simple enough for the victim to prove the aggregate losses, including the costs of psychiatric treatment and lost income, that stem from the ongoing traffic in her images as a whole. (Complications may arise in disaggregating losses sustained as a result of the initial physical abuse, but those questions may be set aside for present purposes.) These losses may be called, for convenience\xe2\x80\x99s sake, a victim\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral losses.\xe2\x80\x9d The difficulty is in determining the \xe2\x80\x9cfull amount\xe2\x80\x9d of those general losses, if any, that are the proximate result of the offense conduct of a particular defendant who is one of thousands who have possessed and will in the future possess the victim\xe2\x80\x99s images but who has no other connection to the victim.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In determining the amount of general losses a defendant must pay under \xc2\xa72259 the ultimate question is how much of these losses were the \xe2\x80\x9cproximate result,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72259(b)(3)(F), of that individual\xe2\x80\x99s offense. But the most difficult aspect of this inquiry concerns the threshold requirement of causation in fact. To be sure, the requirement of proximate causation, as distinct from mere causation in fact, would prevent holding any possessor liable for losses caused in only a remote sense. But the victim\xe2\x80\x99s costs of treatment and lost income resulting from the trauma of knowing that images of her abuse are being viewed over and over are direct and foreseeable results of child-pornography crimes, including possession, assuming the prerequisite of factual causation is satisfied. The primary problem, then, is the proper standard of causation in fact.

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The traditional way to prove that one event was a fac-tual cause of another is to show that the latter would not have occurred \xe2\x80\x9cbut for\xe2\x80\x9d the former. This approach is a familiar part of our legal tradition, see 1 LaFave \xc2\xa76.4(b), at 467\xe2\x80\x93468; Prosser and Keeton \xc2\xa741, at 266, and no party disputes that a showing of but-for causation would satisfy \xc2\xa72259\xe2\x80\x99s factual-causation requirement. Sometimes that showing could be made with little difficulty. For example, but-for causation could be shown with ease in many cases involving producers of child pornography, see \xc2\xa72251(a); parents who permit their children to be used for child-pornography production, see \xc2\xa72251(b); individuals who sell children for such purposes, see \xc2\xa72251A; or the initial distributor of the pornographic images of a child, see \xc2\xa72252.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In this case, however, a showing of but-for causation cannot be made. The District Court found that the Government failed to prove specific losses caused by Paroline in a but-for sense and recognized that it would be \xe2\x80\x9cincredibly difficult\xe2\x80\x9d to do so in a case like this. 672 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 791\xe2\x80\x93793. That finding has a solid foundation in the record, and it is all but unchallenged in this Court. See Brief for Respondent Amy 63; Brief for United States 19, 25. But see Supp. Brief for United States 8\xe2\x80\x9310. From the victim\xe2\x80\x99s perspective, Paroline was just one of thousands of anonymous possessors. To be sure, the victim\xe2\x80\x99s precise degree of trauma likely bears a relation to the total number of offenders; it would probably be less if only 10 rather than thousands had seen her images. But it is not possible to prove that her losses would be less (and by how much) but for one possessor\xe2\x80\x99s individual role in the large, loosely connected network through which her images circulate. See Sentencing Comm\xe2\x80\x99n Report, at ii, xx. Even without Paroline\xe2\x80\x99s offense, thousands would have viewed and would in the future view the victim\xe2\x80\x99s images, so it cannot be shown that her trauma and attendant losses would have been any different but for Paroline\xe2\x80\x99s offense. That is especially so given the parties\xe2\x80\x99 stipulation that the victim had no knowledge of Paroline. See supra, at 4.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Recognizing that losses cannot be substantiated under a but-for approach where the defendant is an anonymous possessor of images in wide circulation on the Internet, the victim and the Government urge the Court to read \xc2\xa72259 to require a less restrictive causation standard, at least in this and similar child-pornography cases. They are correct to note that courts have departed from the but-for standard where circumstances warrant, especially where the combined conduct of multiple wrongdoers produces a bad outcome. See Burrage, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 10) (acknowledging \xe2\x80\x9cthe undoubted reality that courts have not always required strict but-for causality, even where criminal liability is at issue\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The victim and the Government look to the literature on criminal and tort law for alternatives to the but-for test. The Court has noted that the \xe2\x80\x9cmost common\xe2\x80\x9d exception to the but-for causation requirement is applied where \xe2\x80\x9cmultiple sufficient causes independently .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. produce a result,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid.; see also 1 LaFave \xc2\xa76.4(b), at 467\xe2\x80\x93469; 1 Restatement \xc2\xa727, at 376. This exception is an ill fit here, as all parties seem to recognize. Paroline\xe2\x80\x99s possession of two images of the victim was surely not sufficient to cause her entire losses from the ongoing trade in her images. Nor is there a practical way to isolate some subset of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s general losses that Paroline\xe2\x80\x99s conduct alone would have been sufficient to cause. See Brief for United States 26, n.\xc2\xa011.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Understandably, the victim and the Government thus concentrate on a handful of less demanding causation tests endorsed by authorities on tort law. One prominent treatise suggests that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen the conduct of two or more actors is so related to an event that their combined conduct, viewed as a whole, is a but-for cause of the event, and application of the but-for rule to them individually would absolve all of them, the conduct of each is a cause in fact of the event.\xe2\x80\x9d Prosser and Keeton \xc2\xa741, at 268. The Restatement adopts a similar exception for \xe2\x80\x9c[m]ultiple sufficient causal sets.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 Restatement \xc2\xa727, Comment f, at 380\xe2\x80\x93381. This is where a wrongdoer\xe2\x80\x99s conduct, though alone \xe2\x80\x9cinsufficient .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to cause the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s harm,\xe2\x80\x9d is, \xe2\x80\x9cwhen combined with conduct by other persons,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cmore than sufficient to cause the harm.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The Restatement offers as an example a case in which three people independently but simultaneously lean on a car, creating enough combined force to roll it off a cliff. Ibid. Even if each exerted too little force to move the car, and the force exerted by any two was sufficient to the move the car, each individual is a factual cause of the car\xe2\x80\x99s destruction. Ibid. The Government argues that these authorities \xe2\x80\x9cprovide ample support for an \xe2\x80\x98aggregate\xe2\x80\x99 causation theory,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 18, and that such a theory would best effectuate congressional intent in cases like this, id., at 18\xe2\x80\x9319. The victim says much the same. Brief for Respondent Amy 42\xe2\x80\x9343.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n These alternative causal tests are a kind of legal fiction or construct. If the conduct of a wrongdoer is neither necessary nor sufficient to produce an outcome, that conduct cannot in a strict sense be said to have caused the outcome. Nonetheless, tort law teaches that alternative and less demanding causal standards are necessary in certain circumstances to vindicate the law\xe2\x80\x99s purposes. It would be anomalous to turn away a person harmed by the combined acts of many wrongdoers simply because none of those wrongdoers alone caused the harm. And it would be nonsensical to adopt a rule whereby individuals hurt by the combined wrongful acts of many (and thus in many instances hurt more badly than otherwise) would haveno redress, whereas individuals hurt by the acts of one person alone would have a remedy. Those are the prin-ciples that underlie the various aggregate causation tests the victim and the Government cite, and they are sound principles.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n These alternative causal standards, though salutary when applied in a judicious manner, also can be taken too far. That is illustrated by the victim\xe2\x80\x99s suggested approach to applying \xc2\xa72259 in cases like this. The victim says that under the strict logic of these alternative causal tests, each possessor of her images is a part of a causal set sufficient to produce her ongoing trauma, so each possessor should be treated as a cause in fact of all the trauma and all the attendant losses incurred as a result of the entire ongoing traffic in her images. Id., at 43. And she argues that if this premise is accepted the further requirement of proximate causation poses no barrier, for she seeks restitution only for those losses that are the direct and foreseeable result of child-pornography offenses. Because the statute requires restitution for the \xe2\x80\x9cfull amount of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses,\xe2\x80\x9d including \xe2\x80\x9cany .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. losses suffered by the victim as a proximate result of the offense,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72259(b), she argues that restitution is required for the entire aggregately caused amount.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The striking outcome of this reasoning\xe2\x80\x94that each possessor of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s images would bear the consequences of the acts of the many thousands who possessed those images\xe2\x80\x94illustrates why the Court has been reluctant to adopt aggregate causation logic in an incautious manner, especially in interpreting criminal statutes where there is no language expressly suggesting Congress intended that approach. See Burrage, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 11\xe2\x80\x9312). Even if one were to refer just to the law of torts, it would be a major step to say there is a sufficient causal link between the injury and the wrong so that all the victim\xe2\x80\x99s general losses were \xe2\x80\x9csuffered .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. as a proximate result of [Paroline\xe2\x80\x99s] offense,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72259(b)(3)(F).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n And there is special reason not to do so in the context of criminal restitution. Aside from the manifest procedural differences between criminal sentencing and civil tort lawsuits, restitution serves purposes that differ from (though they overlap with) the purposes of tort law. See, e.g., Kelly v. Robinson,\n\n479 U.\xc2\xa0S. 36\n, n.\xc2\xa010 (1986) (noting that restitution is, inter alia, \xe2\x80\x9can effective rehabilitative penalty\xe2\x80\x9d). Legal fictions developed in the law of torts cannot be imported into criminal restitution and applied to their utmost limits without due consideration of these differences.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Contrary to the victim\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion, this is not akin to a case in which a \xe2\x80\x9cgang of ruffians\xe2\x80\x9d collectively beats a person, or in which a woman is \xe2\x80\x9cgang raped by five men on one night or by five men on five sequential nights.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent Amy 55. First, this case does not involve a set of wrongdoers acting in concert, see Prosser and Keeton \xc2\xa752, at 346 (discussing full liability for a joint enter-prise); for Paroline had no contact with the overwhelming majority of the offenders for whose actions the victim would hold him accountable. Second, adopting the victim\xe2\x80\x99s approach would make an individual possessor liable for the combined consequences of the acts of not just 2, 5, or even 100 independently acting offenders; but instead, a number that may reach into the tens of thousands. See Brief for Respondent Amy 65.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n It is unclear whether it could ever be sensible to embrace the fiction that this victim\xe2\x80\x99s entire losses were the \xe2\x80\x9cproximate result,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72259(b)(3)(F), of a single possessor\xe2\x80\x99s offense. Paroline\xe2\x80\x99s contribution to the causal process underlying the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses was very minor, both compared to the combined acts of all other relevant offenders, and in comparison to the contributions of other individual offenders, particularly distributors (who may have caused hundreds or thousands of further viewings) and the initial producer of the child pornography. See 1 Restatement \xc2\xa736, and Comment a, at 597\xe2\x80\x93598 (recognizing a rule excluding from liability individuals whose contribution to a causal set that factually caused the outcome \xe2\x80\x9cpales by comparison to the other contributions to that causal set\xe2\x80\x9d). But see id., \xc2\xa727, Reporters\xe2\x80\x99 Note, Comment i, at 395 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe conclusion that none of\xe2\x80\x9d two dozen small contributions to a sufficient causal set was a cause of the outcome \xe2\x80\x9cis obviously untenable\xe2\x80\x9d). Congress gave no indication that it intended its statute to be applied in the expansive manner the victim suggests, a manner contrary to the bedrock principle that restitution should reflect the consequences of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s own conduct, see Hughey, 495 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 416, not the conduct of thousands of geographically and temporally distant offenders acting independently, and with whom the defendant had no contact.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The victim argues that holding each possessor liable for her entire losses would be fair and practical, in part because offenders may seek contribution from one another. Brief for Respondent Amy 58. If that were so, it might mitigate to some degree the concerns her approach presents. But there is scant authority for her contention that offenders convicted in different proceedings in different jurisdictions and ordered to pay restitution to the same victim may seek contribution from one another. There is no general federal right to contribution. Northwest Airlines, Inc. v. Transport Workers,\n\n451 U.\xc2\xa0S. 77\n\xe2\x80\x9397 (1981). Nor does the victim point to any clear statutory basis for a right to contribution in these circumstances. She thus suggests that this Court should imply a cause of action. Brief for Respondent Amy 58. But that is a rare step in any circumstance. See, e.g., Stoneridge Investment Partners, LLC v. Scientific-Atlanta, Inc.,\n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 148\n\xe2\x80\x93165 (2008); Musick, Peeler & Garrett v. Employers Ins. of Wausau,\n\n508 U.\xc2\xa0S. 286,\n291 (1993)\n (noting that this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents \xe2\x80\x9cteach that the creation of new rights ought to be left to legislatures, not courts\xe2\x80\x9d). And it would do little to address the practical problems offenders would face in seeking contribution in any event, see Brief for United States 45\xe2\x80\x9346, problems with which the victim fails to grapple.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The reality is that the victim\xe2\x80\x99s suggested approach would amount to holding each possessor of her images liable for the conduct of thousands of other independently acting possessors and distributors, with no legal or practical avenue for seeking contribution. That approach is so severe it might raise questions under the Excessive Fines Clause of the\n \nEighth Amendment. To be sure, this Court has said that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Excessive Fines Clause was intended to limit only those fines directly imposed by, and payable to, the government.\xe2\x80\x9d Browning-Ferris Industries of Vt., Inc. v. Kelco Disposal, Inc.,\n\n492 U.\xc2\xa0S. 257,\n268 (1989)\n. But while restitution under \xc2\xa72259 is paid to a victim, it is imposed by the Government \xe2\x80\x9cat the culmination of a criminal proceeding and requires conviction of an underlying\xe2\x80\x9d crime, United States v. Bajakajian,\n\n524 U.\xc2\xa0S. 321,\n328 (1998)\n. Thus, despite the differences between restitution and a traditional fine, restitution still implicates \xe2\x80\x9cthe prosecutorial powers of government,\xe2\x80\x9d Browning-Ferris, supra, at 275. The primary goal of restitution is remedial or compensatory, cf. Bajakajian, supra, at 329, but it also serves punitive purposes, see Pasquantino v. United States,\n\n544 U.\xc2\xa0S. 349,\n365 (2005)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cThe purpose of awarding restitution\xe2\x80\x9d under\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73663A \xe2\x80\x9cis .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to mete out appropriate criminal punishment\xe2\x80\x9d); Kelly, 479 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 49, n.\xc2\xa010. That may be \xe2\x80\x9csufficient to bring [it] within the purview of the Excessive Fines Clause,\xe2\x80\x9d Bajakajian, supra, at 329, n.\xc2\xa04. And there is a real question whether holding a single possessor liable for millions of dollars in losses collectively caused by thousands of independent actors might be excessive and disproportionate in these circumstances. These concerns offer further reason not to interpret the statute the way the victim suggests.

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The contention that the victim\xe2\x80\x99s entire losses from the ongoing trade in her images were \xe2\x80\x9csuffered .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. as a proximate result\xe2\x80\x9d of Paroline\xe2\x80\x99s offense for purposes of \xc2\xa72259 must be rejected. But that does not mean the broader principles underlying the aggregate causation theories the Government and the victim cite are irrelevant to determining the proper outcome in cases like this. The cause of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s general losses is the trade in her images. And Paroline is a part of that cause, for he is one of those who viewed her images. While it is not possible to identify a discrete, readily definable incremental loss he caused, it is indisputable that he was a part of the overall phenomenon that caused her general losses. Just as it undermines the purposes of tort law to turn away plaintiffs harmed by several wrongdoers, it would undermine the remedial and penological purposes of \xc2\xa72259 to turn away victims in cases like this.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n With respect to the statute\xe2\x80\x99s remedial purpose, there can be no question that it would produce anomalous results to say that no restitution is appropriate in these circumstances. It is common ground that the victim suffers continuing and grievous harm as a result of her knowledge that a large, indeterminate number of individuals have viewed and will in the future view images of the sexual abuse she endured. Brief for Petitioner 50; Brief for Respondent Wright 4; Brief for United States 23; Brief for Respondent Amy 60. Harms of this sort are a major reason why child pornography is outlawed. See Ferber, 458 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 759. The unlawful conduct of everyone who reproduces, distributes, or possesses the images of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s abuse\xe2\x80\x94including Paroline\xe2\x80\x94plays a part in sustaining and aggravating this tragedy. And there can be no doubt Congress wanted victims to receive restitution for harms like this. The law makes restitution \xe2\x80\x9cmandatory,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72259(b)(4), for child-pornography offenses under Chapter 110, language that indicates Congress\xe2\x80\x99 clear intent that victims of child pornography be compensated by the perpetrators who contributed to their anguish. It would undermine this intent to apply the statute in a way that would render it a dead letter in child-pornography prosecutions of this type.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Denying restitution in cases like this would also be at odds with the penological purposes of \xc2\xa72259\xe2\x80\x99s mandatory restitution scheme. In a sense, every viewing of child pornography is a repetition of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s abuse. One reason to make restitution mandatory for crimes like this is to impress upon offenders that their conduct produces concrete and devastating harms for real, identifiable victims. See Kelly, supra, at 49, n.\xc2\xa010 (\xe2\x80\x9cRestitution is an effective rehabilitative penalty because it forces the defendant to confront, in concrete terms, the harm his actions have caused\xe2\x80\x9d). It would be inconsistent with this purpose to apply the statute in a way that leaves offenders with the mistaken impression that child-pornography possession (at least where the images are in wide circulation) is a victimless crime.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n If the statute by its terms required a showing of strict but-for causation, these purposes would be beside the point. But the text of the statute is not so limited. Although Congress limited restitution to losses that are the \xe2\x80\x9cproximate result\xe2\x80\x9d of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s offense, such unelaborated causal language by no means requires but-for causation by its terms. See Burrage, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 8) (courts need not read phrases like \xe2\x80\x9cresults from\xe2\x80\x9d to require but-for causality where there is \xe2\x80\x9ctextual or contextual\xe2\x80\x9d reason to conclude otherwise). As the authorities the Government and the victim cite show, the availability of alternative causal standards where circumstances warrant is, no less than the but-for test itself as a default, part of the background legal tradition against which Congress has legislated, cf. id., at ___ (slip op., at 10). It would be unacceptable to adopt a causal standard so strict that it would undermine congressional intent where neither the plain text of the statute nor legal tradition demands such an approach.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In this special context, where it can be shown both that a defendant possessed a victim\xe2\x80\x99s images and that a victim has outstanding losses caused by the continuing traffic in those images but where it is impossible to trace a particular amount of those losses to the individual defendant by recourse to a more traditional causal inquiry, a court applying \xc2\xa72259 should order restitution in an amount that comports with the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s relative role in the causal process that underlies the victim\xe2\x80\x99s general losses. The amount would not be severe in a case like this, given the nature of the causal connection between the conduct of a possessor like Paroline and the entirety of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s general losses from the trade in her images, which are the product of the acts of thousands of offenders. It would not, however, be a token or nominal amount. The required restitution would be a reasonable and circumscribed award imposed in recognition of the indisputable role of the offender in the causal process underlying the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses and suited to the relative size of that causal role. This would serve the twin goals of helping the victim achieve eventual restitution for all her child-pornography losses and impressing upon offenders the fact that child-pornography crimes, even simple possession, affect real victims.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n There remains the question of how district courts should go about determining the proper amount of restitution. At a general level of abstraction, a court must assess as best it can from available evidence the significance of the individual defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conduct in light of the broader causal process that produced the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses. This cannot be a precise mathematical inquiry and involves the use of discretion and sound judgment. But that is neither unusual nor novel, either in the wider context of criminal sentencing or in the more specific domain of restitution. It is well recognized that district courts by necessity \xe2\x80\x9cexercise .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. discretion in fashioning a restitution order.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73664(a). Indeed, a district court is expressly authorized to conduct a similar inquiry where multiple defendants who have \xe2\x80\x9ccontributed to the loss of a victim\xe2\x80\x9d appear before it. \xc2\xa73664(h). In that case it may \xe2\x80\x9capportion liability among the defendants to reflect the level of contribution to the victim\xe2\x80\x99s loss .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of each defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Assessing an individual defendant\xe2\x80\x99s role in the causal process behind a child-pornography victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses does not involve a substantially different or greater exercise of discretion.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n There are a variety of factors district courts might consider in determining a proper amount of restitution, and it is neither necessary nor appropriate to prescribe a precise algorithm for determining the proper restitution amount at this point in the law\xe2\x80\x99s development. Doing so would unduly constrain the decisionmakers closest to the facts of any given case. But district courts might, as a starting point, determine the amount of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses caused by the continuing traffic in the victim\xe2\x80\x99s images (excluding, of course, any remote losses like the hypothetical car accident described above, see supra, at 10), then set an award of restitution in consideration of factors that bear on the relative causal significance of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conduct in producing those losses. These could include the number of past criminal defendants found to have contributed to the victim\xe2\x80\x99s general losses; reasonable predictions of the number of future offenders likely to be caught and convicted for crimes contributing to the victim\xe2\x80\x99s general losses; any available and reasonably reliable estimate of the broader number of offenders involved (most of whom will, of course, never be caught or convicted); whether the defendant reproduced or distributed images of the victim; whether the defendant had any connection to the initial production of the images; how many images of the victim the defendant possessed; and other facts relevant to the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s relative causal role. See Brief for United States 49.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n These factors need not be converted into a rigid formula, especially if doing so would result in trivial restitution orders. They should rather serve as rough guideposts for determining an amount that fits the offense. The resulting amount fixed by the court would be deemed the amount of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s general losses that were the \xe2\x80\x9cproximate result of the offense\xe2\x80\x9d for purposes of \xc2\xa72259, and thus the \xe2\x80\x9cfull amount\xe2\x80\x9d of such losses that should be awarded. The court could then set an appropriate payment schedule in consideration of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s financial means. See \xc2\xa73664(f)(2).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The victim says this approach is untenable because her losses are \xe2\x80\x9cindivisible\xe2\x80\x9d in the sense that term is used by tort law, i.e., that there is no \xe2\x80\x9creasonable basis for the factfinder to determine .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the amount of damages separately caused by\xe2\x80\x9d any one offender\xe2\x80\x99s conduct. Restatement (Third) of Torts: Apportionment of Liability \xc2\xa726, p. 320 (1999). The premise of her argument is that because it is in a sense a fiction to say Paroline caused $1,000 in losses, $10,000 in losses, or any other lesser amount, it is necessary to embrace the much greater fiction that Paroline caused all the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses from the ongoing trade in her images. But that is a non sequitur. The Court is required to define a causal standard that effects the statute\xe2\x80\x99s purposes, not to apply tort-law concepts in a mechanical way in the criminal restitution context. Even if the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses are fully \xe2\x80\x9cindivisible\xe2\x80\x9d in this sense (which is debatable), treating Paroline as a proximate cause of all the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses\xe2\x80\x94especially in the absence of a workable system of contribution\xe2\x80\x94stretches the fiction of aggregate causation to its breaking point. Treating him as a cause of a smaller amount of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s general losses, taking account of his role in the overall causal process behind those losses, effects the statute\xe2\x80\x99s purposes; avoids the nonsensical result of turning away victims emptyhanded; and does so without sacrificing the need for proportional-ity in sentencing.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The victim also argues that this approach would consign her to \xe2\x80\x9cpiecemeal\xe2\x80\x9d restitution and leave her to face \xe2\x80\x9cdecades of litigation that might never lead to full recovery,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent Amy 57, which \xe2\x80\x9cwould convert Congress\xe2\x80\x99s promise to child pornography victims into an empty gesture,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 66. But Congress has not promisedvictims full and swift restitution at all costs. To be sure, the statute states a strong restitutionary purpose; but that purpose cannot be twisted into a license to hold a defendant liable for an amount drastically out of proportion to his own individual causal relation to the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Furthermore, an approach of this sort better effects the need to impress upon defendants that their acts are not irrelevant or victimless. As the Government observes, Reply Brief for United States 18, it would undermine this important purpose of criminal restitution if the victim simply collected her full losses from a handful of wealthy possessors and left the remainder to pay nothing because she had already fully collected. Of course the victim should someday collect restitution for all her child-pornography losses, but it makes sense to spread payment among a larger number of offenders in amounts more closely in proportion to their respective causal roles and their own circumstances so that more are made aware, through the concrete mechanism of restitution, of the impact of child-pornography possession on victims.

\n

C

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This approach is not without its difficulties. Restitution orders should represent \xe2\x80\x9can application of law,\xe2\x80\x9d not \xe2\x80\x9ca decisionmaker\xe2\x80\x99s caprice,\xe2\x80\x9d Philip Morris USA v. Williams,\n\n549 U.\xc2\xa0S. 346,\n352 (2007)\n (internal quotation marks omitted), and the approach articulated above involves discretion and estimation. But courts can only do their best to apply the statute as written in a workable manner, faithful to the competing principles at stake: that victims should be compensated and that defendants should be held to account for the impact of their conduct on those victims, but also that defendants should be made liable for the consequences and gravity of their own conduct, not the conduct of others. District courts routinely exercise wide discretion both in sentencing as a general matter and more specifically in fashioning restitution orders. There is no reason to believe they cannot apply the causal standard defined above in a reasonable manner without further detailed guidance at this stage in the law\xe2\x80\x99s elaboration. Based on its experience in prior cases of this kind, the Government\xe2\x80\x94which, as noted above, see supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x936, bears the burden of proving the amount of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses, \xc2\xa73664(e)\xe2\x80\x94could also inform district courts of restitution sought and ordered in other cases.

\n

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Fifth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the requirements of \xc2\xa72259 was incorrect. The District Court likewise erred in requiring a strict showing of but-for causation. The judgment of the Court of Appeals is vacated, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Justice Sotomayor, dissenting.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This Court has long recognized the grave \xe2\x80\x9cphysiological, emotional, and mental\xe2\x80\x9d injuries suffered by victims of child pornography. New York v. Ferber,\n\n458 U.\xc2\xa0S. 747,\n758 (1982)\n. The traffic in images depicting a child\xe2\x80\x99s sexual abuse, we have observed, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98poses an even greater threat to the child victim than does sexual abuse or prostitution\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d because the victim must \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98go through life knowing that the recording is circulating within the mass distribution system for child pornography.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 759, n. 10. As we emphasized in a later case, the images cause \xe2\x80\x9ccontinuing harm by haunting the chil[d] in years to come.\xe2\x80\x9d Osborne v. Ohio,\n\n495 U. S. 103,\n111 (1990)\n.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Congress enacted\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72259 against this backdrop. The statute imposes a \xe2\x80\x9cmandatory\xe2\x80\x9d duty on courts to order restitution to victims of federal offenses involving the sexual abuse of children, including the possession of child pornography. \xc2\xa72259(b)(4). And it commands that for any such offense, a court \xe2\x80\x9cshall direct the defendant to pay the victim .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the full amount of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72259(b)(1).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court interprets this statute to require restitution in a \xe2\x80\x9ccircumscribed\xe2\x80\x9d amount less than the \xe2\x80\x9centirety of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. losses,\xe2\x80\x9d a total it instructs courts to estimate based on the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9crelative role\xe2\x80\x9d in the victim\xe2\x80\x99s harm. Ante, at 21. That amount, the Court holds, should be neither \xe2\x80\x9cnominal\xe2\x80\x9d nor \xe2\x80\x9csevere.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n I appreciate the Court\xe2\x80\x99s effort to achieve what it perceives to be a just result. It declines to require restitution for a victim\xe2\x80\x99s full losses, a result that might seem incongruent to an individual possessor\xe2\x80\x99s partial role in a harm in which countless others have participated. And it rejects the position advanced by Paroline and the dissenting opinion of The Chief Justice, which would result in no restitution in cases like this for the perverse reason that a child has been victimized by too many.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court\xe2\x80\x99s approach, however, cannot be reconciled with the law that Congress enacted. Congress mandated restitution for the \xe2\x80\x9cfull amount of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72259(b)(1), and did so within the framework of settled tort law principles that treat defendants like Paroline jointly and severally liable for the indivisible consequences of their intentional, concerted conduct. And to the extent an award for the full amount of a victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses may lead to fears of unfair treatment for particular defendants, Congress provided a mechanism to accommodate those concerns: Courts are to order \xe2\x80\x9cpartial payments\xe2\x80\x9d on a periodic schedule if the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s financial circumstances or other \xe2\x80\x9cinterest[s] of justice\xe2\x80\x9d so require. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73664(f\xc2\xa0)(3), 3572(d)(1). I would accordingly affirm the Fifth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s holding that the District Court \xe2\x80\x9cmust enter a restitution order reflecting the \xe2\x80\x98full amount of [Amy\xe2\x80\x99s] losses,\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9d In\xc2\xa0re Amy Unknown, 701 F.\xc2\xa03d 749, 774 (2012), and instruct the court to consider a periodic payment schedule on remand.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n There are two distinct but related questions in this case: First, whether Paroline\xe2\x80\x99s conduct bears a sufficient causal nexus to Amy\xe2\x80\x99s harm, and second, if such a nexus exists, how much restitution Paroline should be required to pay. Beginning with causation, I agree with the majority that proximate causation is beyond dispute because the medical and economic losses suffered by Amy are \xe2\x80\x9cdirect and foreseeable results of child-pornography crimes.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12; accord, ante, at 3 (Roberts, C.\xc2\xa0J., dissenting). The real issue, then, is \xe2\x80\x9cthe proper standard of causation in fact.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12 (majority opinion).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The majority and I share common ground on much of this issue. We agree that the ordinary way to prove cause-in-fact is to show that a result would not have occurred \xe2\x80\x9cbut for\xe2\x80\x9d the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conduct. Burrage v. United States, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 6). We also agree that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98strict but-for causality\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98not always required,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and that alternative standards of factual causation are appropriate \xe2\x80\x9cwhere there is \xe2\x80\x98textual or contextual\xe2\x80\x99 reason to conclude\xe2\x80\x9d as much. Ante, at 13, 21 (quoting Burrage, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 8, 10). And most importantly, we agree that there are ample reasons to reject a strict but-for causality requirement in \xc2\xa72259. See ante, at 21.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Starting with the text, \xc2\xa72259 declares that a court \xe2\x80\x9cshall order restitution for any offense under this chapter.\xe2\x80\x9d The possession of child pornography, \xc2\xa72252, is an offense under the relevant chapter, and the term \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d creates \xe2\x80\x9can obligation impervious to judicial discretion,\xe2\x80\x9d Lexecon Inc. v. Milberg Weiss Bershad Hynes & Lerach,\n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 26,\n35 (1998)\n. So the text could not be clearer: A court must order restitution against a person convicted of possessing child pornography. Section 2259(b)(4) underscores this directive by declaring that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he issuance of a restitution order under this section is mandatory.\xe2\x80\x9d And the statute\xe2\x80\x99s title\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cmandatory restitution\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94reinforces it further still.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Interpreting \xc2\xa72259 to require but-for causality would flout these simple textual commands. That is because \xe2\x80\x9ca showing of but-for causation cannot be made\xe2\x80\x9d in this case and many like it. Ante, at 12. Even without Paroline\xe2\x80\x99s offense, it is a regrettable fact that \xe2\x80\x9cthousands would have viewed and would in the future view [Amy\xe2\x80\x99s] images,\xe2\x80\x9d such that \xe2\x80\x9cit cannot be shown that her trauma and attendant losses would have been any different but for Parolin[e].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 13. A but-for requirement would thus make restitution under \xc2\xa72259 the opposite of \xe2\x80\x9cmandatory\xe2\x80\x9d; it would preclude restitution to the victim of the typical child pornography offense for the nonsensical reason that the child has been victimized by too many.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Such an approach would transform \xc2\xa72259 into something unrecognizable to the Congress that wrote it. When Congress passed \xc2\xa72259 in 1994, it was common knowledge that child pornography victims suffer harm at the hands of numerous offenders who possess their images in common, whether in print, film, or electronic form. See, e.g., Shouvlin, Preventing the Sexual Exploitation of Children: A Model Act, 17 Wake Forest L. Rev. 535, 544 (1981) (describing the \xe2\x80\x9cenormous number of magazines\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9chundreds of films\xe2\x80\x9d produced each year depicting the sexual abuse of children, which were circulated to untold numbers of offenders through a \xe2\x80\x9cwell-organized distribution system [that] ensures that even the small towns have access to [the] material\xe2\x80\x9d); Doyle, FBI Probing Child Porn on Computers, San Francisco Chronicle, Dec. 5, 1991,p. A23 (describing complaint that \xe2\x80\x9cchild pornographic pho-tographs\xe2\x80\x9d were circulating via the \xe2\x80\x9cAmerica On-Line com-puter service\xe2\x80\x9d). Congress was also acutely aware of the severe injuries that victims of child pornography suffer at the hands of criminals who possess and view the recorded images of their sexual abuse. Congress found, for example, that the \xe2\x80\x9ccontinued existence\xe2\x80\x9d and circulation of child pornography images \xe2\x80\x9ccauses the child victims of sexual abuse continuing harm by haunting those children in future years.\xe2\x80\x9d Child Pornography Prevention Act of 1996, \xc2\xa7121,\n\n110Stat.\n3009\xe2\x80\x9326, Congressional Findings (2), notes following\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72251 (hereinafter \xc2\xa72251 Findings). It is inconceivable that Congress would have imposed a mandatory restitution obligation on the possessors who contribute to these \xe2\x80\x9ccontinuing harm[s],\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., onlyto direct courts to apply a but-for cause requirementthat would prevent victims from actually obtaining any recovery.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n There is, of course, an alternative standard for determining cause-in-fact that would be consistent with the text of \xc2\xa72259 and the context in which it was enacted: aggregate causation. As the majority points out, aggregate causation was, \xe2\x80\x9cno less than the but-for test itself,\xe2\x80\x9d a \xe2\x80\x9cpart of the background legal tradition against which Congress\xe2\x80\x9d legislated. Ante, at 21. And under this standard, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[w]hen the conduct of two or more actors is so re-lated to an event that their combined conduct, viewed as a whole, is a but-for cause of the event, and application of the but-for rule to them individually would absolve all of them, the conduct of each is a cause in fact of the event.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14 (quoting W. Keeton, D. Dobbs, R. Keeton, & D. Owen, Prosser and Keeton on Law of Torts \xc2\xa741, p. 268 (5th ed. 1984) (hereinafter Prosser and Keeton)).[1] Paroline and his fellow offenders plainly qualify as factual causes under this approach because Amy\xe2\x80\x99s losses would not have occurred but for their combined conduct, and because applying the but-for rule would excuse them all.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n There is every reason to think Congress intended \xc2\xa72259 to incorporate aggregate causation. Whereas a but-for requirement would set \xc2\xa72259\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cmandatory\xe2\x80\x9d restitution command on a collision course with itself, the aggregate causation standard follows directly from the statute. Section 2259 is unequivocal; it offers no safety-in-numbers exception for defendants who possess images of a child\xe2\x80\x99s abuse in common with other offenders. And the aggregate causation standard exists to avoid exactly that kind of exception. See Prosser and Keeton \xc2\xa741, at 268\xe2\x80\x93269 (aggregate causation applies where multiple defendants \xe2\x80\x9cbea[r] a like relationship\xe2\x80\x9d to a victim\xe2\x80\x99s injury, and where \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ach seeks to escape liability for a reason that, if recognized, would likewise protect each other defendant in the group, thus leaving the [victim] without a remedy in the face of the fact that had none of them acted improperly the [victim] would not have suffered the harm\xe2\x80\x9d); Restatement (Third) of Torts: Liability for Physical and Emotional Harm \xc2\xa727, Comment f, p. 380 (2005) (similar).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n At bottom, Congress did not intend \xc2\xa72259 to create a safe harbor for those who inflict upon their victims the proverbial death by a thousand cuts. Given the very nature of the child pornography market\xe2\x80\x94in which a large class of offenders contribute jointly to their victims\xe2\x80\x99 harm by trading in their images\xe2\x80\x94a but-for causation requirement would swallow \xc2\xa72259\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cmandatory\xe2\x80\x9d restitution command, leaving victims with little hope of recovery. That is all the \xe2\x80\x9ctextual [and] contextual\xe2\x80\x9d reason necessary to conclude that Congress incorporated aggregate causation into \xc2\xa72259. Burrage, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 8).

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The dissent of The Chief Justice suggests that a contrary conclusion is compelled by our decision in Hughey v. United States,\n\n495 U.\xc2\xa0S. 411 (1990)\n. Hughey involved a defendant who had been convicted of a single count of unauthorized credit card use, which resulted in $10,412 in losses. Id., at 414. The Government nonetheless re-quested restitution for additional losses based on different counts in the indictment that the Government had agreed to dismiss. Id., at 413. We declined the Government\xe2\x80\x99s request, reasoning that restitution was to be tied to the offense of conviction. Id., at 418.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n That commonsense holding, of course, casts no doubt on the ordinary practice of requiring restitution for losses caused by an offense for which a defendant is convicted, where the loss is the product of the combined conduct of multiple offenders. What troubles my colleagues in this case, then, is not the concept of restitution in cases involving losses caused by more than one offender. Their objection is instead to restitution in cases where the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses are caused by too many offenders. As The Chief Justice puts it, Congress wrote a law that would enable Amy to recover if only her images had been circulated by \xe2\x80\x9ca single distributor\xe2\x80\x9d to just a \xe2\x80\x9chandful of possessors.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 6\xe2\x80\x937. But because she has been victimized by numerous distributors and thousands of possessors, she gets nothing. It goes without saying that Congress did not intend that result.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n My colleagues in dissent next assert that no restitution may be awarded because of \xc2\xa73664(e), which describes the Government\xe2\x80\x99s burden of showing the \xe2\x80\x9closs sustained by a victim as a result of the offense.\xe2\x80\x9d But that provision is nothing close to a \xe2\x80\x9cdirect answer\xe2\x80\x9d to this case. Ante, at 4. It simply restates the question: What should a court do when the losses sustained by a victim are the \xe2\x80\x9cresult of the [defendant\xe2\x80\x99s] offense,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73664(e), but that result is produced in combination with the offenses of others? One answer is that the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s offense is a cause-in-fact only of losses for which it was a but-for cause. A second is that the offense is a cause-in-fact of losses for which it was part of the aggregate cause. The former would preclude restitution in cases like this; the latter would allow it. Given Congress\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cmandatory\xe2\x80\x9d command that courts \xe2\x80\x9cshall order restitution for any offense,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72259(a), (b)(4), it is beyond clear which answer Congress chose.[2]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Chief Justice\xe2\x80\x99s dissent also fails to contend with the ramifications of the suggestion that \xc2\xa73664(e) forecloses entry of restitution in cases where a victim suffers indivisible losses as a result of the aggregate conduct of numerous offenders. It claims that this reading of \xc2\xa73664(e) \xe2\x80\x9cwill work just fine\xe2\x80\x9d for \xe2\x80\x9ccommon crimes\xe2\x80\x9d such as assault. Ante, at 5\xe2\x80\x936. But what about a victim of a vicious gang assault, where a single offender\xe2\x80\x99s conduct cannot be labeled a but-for cause of any discrete injury? Such offenses are, unfortunately, all too common. See, e.g., Wheelock v. United States, 2013 WL 2318145, *2 (ED Wisc., May 28, 2013) (defendant convicted for his participation in a gang rape of a 13-year-old victim in which he \xe2\x80\x9cand several other individuals had provided alcohol to the girl and, after she became intoxicated and unconscious, sexually assaulted her\xe2\x80\x9d); United States v. Homer B., 1990 WL 79705 (CA9, June 14, 1990) (similar). I would have thought it beyond refute that the victim of such a tragic offense would be entitled to restitution even though none of her losses may be attributed solely to any individual defendant. If the opinion of The Chief Justice is in agreement, it does not explain why the result should be any different for victims like Amy, who have suffered heart wrenching losses at the hands of thousands of offenders rather than a few.[3]

\n

II

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The majority accepts aggregate causation at least to an extent, ruling that \xc2\xa72259 requires possessors to pay some amount of restitution even though \xe2\x80\x9cit is impossible\xe2\x80\x9d to say that they caused \xe2\x80\x9ca particular amount of [a victim\xe2\x80\x99s] losses .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. by recourse to a more traditional [but-for] causal inquiry.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 21. But the majority resists the \xe2\x80\x9cstrict logic\xe2\x80\x9d of aggregate causation for fear that doing so would produce the \xe2\x80\x9cstriking outcome\xe2\x80\x9d of an award against an individual possessor \xe2\x80\x9cfor the entire aggregately caused amount.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 15\xe2\x80\x9316. The majority accordingly holds that \xe2\x80\x9ca court applying \xc2\xa72259 should order restitution in an amount that comports with the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s relative\xe2\x80\x9d contribution to \xe2\x80\x9cthe victim\xe2\x80\x99s general losses.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 21.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The majority\xe2\x80\x99s apportionment approach appears to be a sensible one. It would, for instance, further the goal of \xe2\x80\x9cproportionality in sentencing,\xe2\x80\x9d avoid \xe2\x80\x9cturning away victims emptyhanded,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cspread payment among\xe2\x80\x9d offenders. Ante, at 24\xe2\x80\x9325. But it suffers from a far more fundamental problem: It contravenes the language Congress actually used. Section 2259 directs courts to enter restitution not for a \xe2\x80\x9cproportional\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9crelative\xe2\x80\x9d amount, but for the \xe2\x80\x9cfull amount of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72259(b)(1). That command is unequivocal, and it is buttressed by the tort law tradition of joint and several liability within which Congress legislated.

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Once a defendant is found to bear a sufficient causal nexus to a victim\xe2\x80\x99s harm, \xc2\xa72259 provides a straightforward instruction on how much restitution a court is to order: \xe2\x80\x9cThe order of restitution under this section shall direct the defendant to pay the victim .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the full amount of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72259(b)(1). Because the word \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d imposes a \xe2\x80\x9cdiscretionless obligatio[n],\xe2\x80\x9d Lopez v. Davis,\n\n531 U.\xc2\xa0S. 230,\n241 (2001)\n, a court considering a \xc2\xa72259 restitution request has no license to deviate from the statute\xe2\x80\x99s command. It must enter an order for the \xe2\x80\x9cfull amount of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses,\xe2\x80\x9d regardless of whether other defendants may have contributed to the same victim\xe2\x80\x99s harm.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n If there were any doubt on the matter, Congress eliminated it in \xc2\xa72259(b)(4)(B)(ii), which bars a court from \xe2\x80\x9cdeclin[ing] to issue [a restitution] order under this section\xe2\x80\x9d on the ground that a victim \xe2\x80\x9cis entitled to receive compensation for his or her injuries from the proceeds of insurance or any other source.\xe2\x80\x9d One \xe2\x80\x9cother source\xe2\x80\x9d from which a victim would be \xe2\x80\x9centitled to receive compensation\xe2\x80\x9d is, of course, other offenders who possess images of her sexual abuse. It is unthinkable that Congress would have expressly forbidden courts to award victims no restitution because their harms have been aggregately caused by many offenders, only to permit restitution orders for a single penny for the same reason.

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n As the majority recognizes, Congress did not draft \xc2\xa72259 in a vacuum; it did so in the context of settled tort law traditions. See ante, at 14\xe2\x80\x9315; see also Meyer v. Holley,\n\n537 U.\xc2\xa0S. 280,\n285 (2003)\n (Congress \xe2\x80\x9clegislates against a legal background of ordinary tort-related\xe2\x80\x9d principles). Section 2259 functions as a tort statute, one designed to ensure that victims will recover compensatory damages in an efficient manner concurrent with criminal proceedings. See Restatement of Torts \xc2\xa7901, p. 537 (1939) (the purposes of tort law include \xe2\x80\x9cto give compensation, indemnity, or restitution for harms\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cto punish wrongdoers\xe2\x80\x9d); Dolan v. United States,\n\n560 U. S. 605,\n612 (2010)\n (the \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive purpose\xe2\x80\x9d of the related Mandatory Victims Restitution Act of 1996, \xc2\xa73664, is \xe2\x80\x9cto ensure that victims of a crime receive full restitution\xe2\x80\x9d). And the nature of the child pornography industry and the indivisible quality of the injuries suffered by its victims make this a paradigmatic situation in which traditional tort law principles would require joint and several liability. By requiring restitution for the \xe2\x80\x9cfull amount of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72259(b)(1), Congress did not depart from these principles; it embraced them.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n First, the injuries caused by child pornography possessors are impossible to apportion in any practical sense. It cannot be said, for example, that Paroline\xe2\x80\x99s offense alone required Amy to attend five additional minutes of therapy, or that it caused some discrete portion of her lost income. The majority overlooks this fact, ordering courts to surmise some \xe2\x80\x9ccircumscribed\xe2\x80\x9d amount of loss based on a list of factors. Ante, at 21, 22\xe2\x80\x9323; see also ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x9310 (Roberts, C.\xc2\xa0J., dissenting). Section 2259\xe2\x80\x99s full restitution requirement dispenses with this guesswork, however, and in doing so it harmonizes with the settled tort law tradition concerning indivisible injuries. As this Court explained this rule in Edmonds v. Compagnie Generale Transatlantique,\n\n443 U.\xc2\xa0S. 256 (1979)\n, unless a plaintiff\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cinjury is divisible and the causation of each part can be separately assigned to each tortfeasor,\xe2\x80\x9d the rule is that a \xe2\x80\x9ctortfeasor is not relieved of liability for the entire harm he caused just because another\xe2\x80\x99s negligence was also a factor in effecting the injury.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 260, n.\xc2\xa08; see also Prosser and Keeton \xc2\xa752, at 347 (joint and several liability applies to injuries that \xe2\x80\x9care obviously incapable of any reasonable or practical division\xe2\x80\x9d); Feneff v. Boston & Maine\xc2\xa0R. Co., 196 Mass. 575, 580, 82 N.\xc2\xa0E. 705, 707 (1907) (similar).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Second, Congress adopted \xc2\xa72259 against the backdrop of the rule governing concerted action by joint tortfeasors, which specifies that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]here two or more [tortfeasors] act in concert, it is well settled .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that each will be liable for the entire result.\xe2\x80\x9d Prosser and Keeton \xc2\xa752, at 346. The degree of concerted action required by the rule is not inordinate; \xe2\x80\x9cif one person acts to produce injury with full knowledge that others are acting in a similar manner and that his conduct will contribute to produce a single harm, a joint tort has been consummated even when there is no prearranged plan.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 F. Harper, F. James, & O. Gray, The Law of Torts \xc2\xa710.1, p. 699 (1st ed. 1956) (hereinafter 1 Harper and James); see also, e.g., Troop v. Dew, 150 Ark. 560, 565, 234 S. W. 992, 994 (1921) (defendants jointly liable for uncoordinated acts where they were \xe2\x80\x9cworking to a common purpose\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Child pornography possessors are jointly liable under this standard, for they act in concert as part of a global network of possessors, distributors, and producers who pursue the common purpose of trafficking in images of child sexual abuse. As Congress itself recognized, \xe2\x80\x9cpossessors of such material\xe2\x80\x9d are an integral part of the \xe2\x80\x9cmarket for the sexual exploitative use of children.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72251 Finding (12). Moreover, although possessors like Paroline may not be familiar with every last participant in the market for child sexual abuse images, there is little doubt that they act with knowledge of the inevitable harms caused by their combined conduct. Paroline himself admitted to possessing between 150 and 300 images of minors engaged in sexually explicit conduct, which he downloaded from other offenders on the Internet. See 672 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 781, 783; App. 146. By communally browsing and downloading Internet child pornography, offenders like Paroline \xe2\x80\x9cfuel the process\xe2\x80\x9d that allows the industry to flourish. O\xe2\x80\x99Connell, Paedophiles Networking on the Internet, in Child Abuse on the Internet: Ending the Silence 77 (C. Arnaldo ed. 2001). Indeed, one expert describes Internet child pornography networks as \xe2\x80\x9can example of a complex criminal conspiracy,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid.\xe2\x80\x94the quintessential concerted action to which joint and several liability attaches.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Lastly, \xc2\xa72259\xe2\x80\x99s full restitution requirement conforms to what Congress would have understood to be the uniform rule governing joint and several liability for intentional torts. Under that rule, \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ach person who commits a tort that requires intent is jointly and severally liable for any indivisible injury legally caused by the tortious conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d Restatement (Third) of Torts: Apportionment of Liability \xc2\xa712, p. 110 (2007). There is little doubt that the possession of images of a child being sexually abused would amount to an intentional invasion of privacy tort\xe2\x80\x94and an extreme one at that. See Restatement (Second) of Torts \xc2\xa7652B, p. 378 (1976) (\xe2\x80\x9cOne who intentionally intrudes, physically or otherwise, upon [another\xe2\x80\x99s] private affairs or concerns, is subject to liability .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. if the intrusion would be highly offensive to a reasonable person\xe2\x80\x9d).[4]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Section 2259\xe2\x80\x99s imposition of joint and several liability makes particular sense when viewed in light of this intentional tort rule. For at the end of the day, the question of how to allocate losses among defendants is really a choice between placing the risk of loss on the defendants (since one who is caught first may be required to pay more than his fair share) or the victim (since an apportionment regime would risk preventing her from obtaining full recovery). Whatever the merits of placing the risk of loss on a victim in the context of a negligence-based offense, Congress evidently struck the balance quite differently in this context, placing the risk on the morally culpable possessors of child pornography and not their innocent child victims.

\n

C

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Notwithstanding \xc2\xa72259\xe2\x80\x99s text and the longstanding tort law traditions that support it, the majority adopts an apportionment approach based on its concern that joint and several liability might lead to unfairness as applied to individual defendants. See ante, at 15\xe2\x80\x9322. The majority finds this approach necessary because \xc2\xa72259 does not provide individual defendants with the ability to seek contribution from other offenders. Ante, at 17\xe2\x80\x9318. I agree that the statute does not create a cause of action for contribution, but unlike the majority I do not think the absence of contribution suggests that Congress intended the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cfull amount of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses\xe2\x80\x9d to mean something less than that. For instead of expending judicial resources on disputes between intentional tortfeasors, Congress crafted a different mechanism for preventing inequitable treatment of individual defendants\xe2\x80\x94the use of periodic payment schedules.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Section 2259(b)(2) directs that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n order of restitution under this section shall be issued and enforced in accordance with section 3664.\xe2\x80\x9d Section 3664(f\xc2\xa0)(1)(A) in turn reiterates \xc2\xa72259\xe2\x80\x99s command that courts \xe2\x80\x9cshall order restitution to each victim in the full amount of each victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses.\xe2\x80\x9d But \xc2\xa73664 goes on to distinguish between the amount of restitution ordered and the schedule on which payments are to be made. Thus, \xc2\xa73664(f\xc2\xa0)(2) states that a court \xe2\x80\x9cshall .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. specify in the restitution order .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the schedule according to whic[h] the restitution is to be paid,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xc2\xa73664(f\xc2\xa0)(3)(A) provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] restitution order may direct the defendant to make a single, lump sum payment\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cpartial payments at specified intervals.\xe2\x80\x9d Critically, in choosing between lump-sum and partial payments, courts \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d consider \xe2\x80\x9cthe financial resources and other assets of the defendant,\xe2\x80\x9d along with \xe2\x80\x9cany financial obligationsof the defendant, including obligations to dependents.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73664(f\xc2\xa0)(2)(A), (C).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Applying these factors to set an appropriate payment schedule in light of any individual child pornography possessor\xe2\x80\x99s financial circumstances would not be difficult; indeed, there is already a robust body of case law clarifying how payment schedules are to be set under \xc2\xa73664(f\xc2\xa0). For example, Courts of Appeals have uniformly found it an abuse of discretion to require defendants to make immediate lump-sum payments for the full amount of a restitution award when they do not have the ability to do so. In such cases, Congress has instead required courts to impose periodic payment schedules. See, e.g., United States v. McGlothlin, 249 F.\xc2\xa03d 783, 784 (CA8 2001) (reversing lump-sum payment order where defendant \xe2\x80\x9chad no ability to pay the restitution immediately,\xe2\x80\x9d and requiring District Court to set a periodic payment schedule); United States v. Myers, 198 F.\xc2\xa03d 160, 168\xe2\x80\x93169 (CA5 1999) (same). The existing body of law also provides guidance as to proper payment schedules. Compare, e.g., United States v. Calbat, 266 F.\xc2\xa03d 358, 366 (CA5 2001) (annual payment of $41,000 an abuse of discretion where defendant had a net worth of $6,400 and yearly income of $39,000), with United States v. Harris, 60 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 169, 180 (SDNY 1999) (setting payment schedule for the greater of $35 per month or 10% of defendant\xe2\x80\x99s gross income).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Section 3664\xe2\x80\x99s provision for partial periodic payments thus alleviates any concerns of unfairness for the vast number of child pornography defendants who have modest financial resources. A more difficult challenge is presented, however, by the case of a wealthy defendant who wouldbe able to satisfy a large restitution judgment in an immediate lump-sum payment. But the statute is fully capable of ensuring just results for these defendants, too. For in addition to an offender\xe2\x80\x99s financial circumstances, \xc2\xa73664 permits courts to consider other factors \xe2\x80\x9cin the interest of justice\xe2\x80\x9d when deciding whether to impose a payment schedule. See \xc2\xa73664(f\xc2\xa0)(2) (district court shall specify payment schedule \xe2\x80\x9cpursuant to section 3572\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa73572(d)(1) (restitution order shall be payable in periodic installments if \xe2\x80\x9cin the interest of justice\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Accordingly, in the context of a restitution order against a wealthy child pornography possessor, it would likely be in the interest of justice for a district court to set a payment schedule requiring the defendant to pay restitution in amounts equal to the periodic losses that the district court finds will actually be \xe2\x80\x9cincurred by the victim,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72259(b)(3), in the given timeframe. In this case, for example, Amy\xe2\x80\x99s expert estimates that she will suffer approximately $3.4 million in losses from medical costs and lost income over the next 60 years of her life, or approximately $56,000 per year. If that estimate is deemed accurate, a court would enter a restitution order against a wealthy defendant for the full $3.4 million amount of Amy\xe2\x80\x99s losses, and could make it payable on an annual schedule of $56,000 per year. Doing so would serve the interest of justice because the periodic payment schedule would allow the individual wealthy defendant\xe2\x80\x99s ultimate burden to be substantially offset by payments made by other offenders,[5] while the entry of the full restitution award would provide certainty to Amy that she will be made whole for her losses.

\n

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Although I ultimately reach a different conclusion as to the proper interpretation of the statutory scheme, I do appreciate the caution with which the Court has announced its approach. For example, the Court expressly rejects the possibility of district courts entering restitution orders for \xe2\x80\x9ctoken or nominal amount[s].\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 21. That point is important because, if taken out of context, aspects of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion might be construed otherwise. For instance, the Court states that in estimating a restitution amount, a district court may consider \xe2\x80\x9cthe broader number of offenders involved (most of whom will, of course, never be caught or convicted).\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 23. If that factor is given too much weight, it could lead to ex-actly the type of trivial restitution awards the Court disclaims. Amy\xe2\x80\x99s counsel has noted, for instance, that in light of the large number of persons who possess her images, a truly proportional approach to restitution would lead to an award of just $47 against any individual defendant. Brief for Respondent Amy 65. Congress obviously did not intend that outcome, and the Court wisely refuses to permit it.[6]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In the end, of course, it is Congress that will have the final say. If Congress wishes to recodify its full restitution command, it can do so in language perhaps even more clear than \xc2\xa72259\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cmandatory\xe2\x80\x9d directive to order restitution for the \xe2\x80\x9cfull amount of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses.\xe2\x80\x9d Congress might amend the statute, for example, to include the term \xe2\x80\x9caggregate causation.\xe2\x80\x9d Alternatively, to avoid the uncertainty in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s apportionment approach, Congress might wish to enact fixed minimum restitution amounts. See, e.g., \xc2\xa72255 (statutorily imposed $150,000 minimum civil remedy). In the meanwhile, it is my hope that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s approach will not unduly undermine the ability of victims like Amy to recover for\xe2\x80\x94and from\xe2\x80\x94the unfathomable harms they have sustained.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n I certainly agree with the Court that Amy deserves restitution, and that Congress\xe2\x80\x94by making restitution mandatory for victims of child pornography\xe2\x80\x94meant that she have it. Unfortunately, the restitution statute that Congress wrote for child pornography offenses makes it impossible to award that relief to Amy in this case. Instead of tailoring the statute to the unique harms caused by child pornography, Congress borrowed a generic restitution standard that makes restitution contingent on the Government\xe2\x80\x99s ability to prove, \xe2\x80\x9cby the preponderance of the evidence,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cthe amount of the loss sustained by a victim as a result of\xe2\x80\x9d the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s crime.\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73664(e). When it comes to Paroline\xe2\x80\x99s crime\xe2\x80\x94possession of two of Amy\xe2\x80\x99s images\xe2\x80\x94it is not possible to do anything more than pick an arbitrary number for that \xe2\x80\x9camount.\xe2\x80\x9d And arbitrary is not good enough for the criminal law.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court attempts to design a more coherent restitution system, focusing on \xe2\x80\x9cthe defendant\xe2\x80\x99s relative role in the causal process that underlies the victim\xe2\x80\x99s general losses.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 21. But this inquiry, sensible as it may be, is not the one Congress adopted. After undertaking the inquiry that Congress did require, the District Court in this case concluded that the Government could not meet its statutory burden of proof. Before this Court, the Government all but concedes the point. See Brief for United States 25 (\xe2\x80\x9cit is practically impossible to know whether [Amy\xe2\x80\x99s] losses would have been slightly lower if one were to subtract one defendant, or ten, or fifty\xe2\x80\x9d). I must regretfully dissent.

\n

I

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Section 2259(a) of Title 18 directs that a district court \xe2\x80\x9cshall order restitution for any offense under this chapter,\xe2\x80\x9d which includes Paroline\xe2\x80\x99s offense of knowingly possessing child pornography in violation of section 2252. In case Congress\xe2\x80\x99s purpose were not clear from its use of \xe2\x80\x9cshall,\xe2\x80\x9d section 2259(b)(4) then emphasizes that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he issuance of a restitution order under this section is mandatory.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Section 2259(b)(1) spells out who may receive restitution, and for what. It provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he order of restitution under this section shall direct the defendant to pay the victim (through the appropriate court mechanism) the full amount of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses as determined by the court pursuant to [section 2259(b)(2)].\xe2\x80\x9d The term \xe2\x80\x9c \xe2\x80\x98victim\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d is defined as \xe2\x80\x9cthe individual harmed as a result of a commission of a crime under this chapter.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72259(c). And the term \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98full amount of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses\xe2\x80\x99 includes any costs incurred by the victim for .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. medical services relating to physical, psychiatric, or psychological care\xe2\x80\x9d; \xe2\x80\x9clost income\xe2\x80\x9d; and \xe2\x80\x9cany other losses suffered by the victim as a proximate result of the offense.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72259(b)(3)(A), (D), (F).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Section 2259(b)(2) then describes how the district court must calculate restitution. It provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n order of restitution under this section shall be issued and enforced in accordance with section 3664 in the same manner as an order under section 3663A.\xe2\x80\x9d Unlike section 2259, sections 3663A and 3664 were not designed specifically for child pornography offenses; they are part of the Mandatory Victims Restitution Act of 1996 and supply general resti-tution guidelines for many federal offenses. Most relevant here, section 3664(e) provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny dispute as to the proper amount or type of restitution shall be resolved by the court by the preponderance of the evidence. The burden of demonstrating the amount of the loss sustained by a victim as a result of the offense shall be on the attorney for the Government.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n As the Court explains, the statute allows restitution only for those losses that were the \xe2\x80\x9cproximate result\xe2\x80\x9d of Paroline\xe2\x80\x99s offense. See ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9311 (citing \xc2\xa72259(b)(3)). Contrary to Paroline\xe2\x80\x99s argument, the proximate cause re-quirement is easily satisfied in this case. It was readily foreseeable that Paroline\xe2\x80\x99s crime could cause Amy to suffer precisely the types of losses that she claims: future lost wages, costs for treatment and counseling, and attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees and costs, all of which are eligible losses enumerated in section 2259(b)(3). There is a \xe2\x80\x9cdirect relation\xe2\x80\x9d between those types of injuries and Paroline\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cinjurious conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d Holmes v. Securities Investor Protection Corporation,\n\n503 U.\xc2\xa0S. 258,\n268 (1992)\n. I therefore agree with the Court that if Paroline actually caused those losses, he also proximately caused them. See ante, at 12.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The more pressing problem is the statutory requirement of actual causation. See Burrage v. United States, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 6) (the ordinary meaning of the term \xe2\x80\x9cresults from\xe2\x80\x9d requires proof that the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conduct was the \xe2\x80\x9cactual cause\xe2\x80\x9d of the injury). Here too the Court correctly holds that the statute precludes the restitution award sought by Amy and preferred by Justice Sotomayor\xe2\x80\x99s dissent, which would hold Paroline responsible for Amy\xe2\x80\x99s entire loss. See ante, at 16\xe2\x80\x9319; contra, post, at 7\xe2\x80\x9316. Congress has authorized restitution only for \xe2\x80\x9cthe amount of the loss sustained by a victim as a result of the offense.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73664(e). We have interpreted virtually identical language, in the predecessor statute to section 3664, to require \xe2\x80\x9crestitution to be tied to the loss caused by the offense of conviction.\xe2\x80\x9d Hughey v. United States,\n\n495 U.\xc2\xa0S. 411,\n418 (1990)\n (citing\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73580(a) (1982 ed.); emphasis added). That is, restitution may not be imposed for losses caused by any other crime or any other defendant.[1]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Justice Sotomayor\xe2\x80\x99s dissent dismisses section 3664(e), which is Congress\xe2\x80\x99s direct answer to the very question presented by this case, namely, how to resolve a \xe2\x80\x9cdispute as to the proper amount .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of restitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Justice Sotomayor thinks the answer to that question begins and ends with the statement in section 2259(b)(1) that the defendant must pay \xe2\x80\x9cthe full amount of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses.\xe2\x80\x9d See post, at 1, 2, 10, 15. But losses from what? Theanswer is found in the rest of that sentence: \xe2\x80\x9cthe full amount of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses as determined by the court pursuant to paragraph 2.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72259(b)(1) (emphasis added). \xe2\x80\x9c[P]aragraph 2,\xe2\x80\x9d of course, instructs that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n order of restitution under this section shall be issued and enforced in accordance with section 3664 in the same manner as an order under section 3663A.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72259(b)(2). And it is section 3664 that provides the statute\xe2\x80\x99s burden of proof and specifies that the defendant pay for those losses sustained \xe2\x80\x9cas a result of the offense\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94that is, his offense. \xc2\xa73664(e).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The offense of conviction here was Paroline\xe2\x80\x99s possession of two of Amy\xe2\x80\x99s images. No one suggests Paroline\xe2\x80\x99s crime actually caused Amy to suffer millions of dollars in losses, so the statute does not allow a court to award millions of dollars in restitution. Determining what amount the statute does allow\xe2\x80\x94the amount of Amy\xe2\x80\x99s losses that Paro-line\xe2\x80\x99s offense caused\xe2\x80\x94is the real difficulty of this case. See ante, at 12.

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Regrettably, Congress provided no mechanism for answering that question. If actual causation is to be determined using the traditional, but-for standard, then the Court acknowledges that \xe2\x80\x9ca showing of but-for causation cannot be made\xe2\x80\x9d in this case. Ante, at 12. Amy would have incurred all of her lost wages and counseling costs even if Paroline had not viewed her images. The Government and Amy respond by offering an \xe2\x80\x9caggregate\xe2\x80\x9d causation theory borrowed from tort law. But even if we apply this \xe2\x80\x9clegal fiction,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 15, and assume, for purposes of argument, that Paroline\xe2\x80\x99s crime contributed something to Amy\xe2\x80\x99s total losses, that suffices only to establish causation in fact. It is not sufficient to award restitution under the statute, which requires a further determination of the amount that Paroline must pay. He must pay \xe2\x80\x9cthe full amount of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses,\xe2\x80\x9d yes, but \xe2\x80\x9cas determined by\xe2\x80\x9d section 3664\xe2\x80\x94that is, the full amount of the losses he caused. The Government has the burden to establish that amount, and no one has suggested a plausible means for the Government to carry that burden.[2]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The problem stems from the nature of Amy\xe2\x80\x99s injury. As explained, section 3664 is a general statute designed to provide restitution for more common crimes, such as fraud and assault. The section 3664(e) standard will work just fine for most crime victims, because it will usually not be difficult to identify the harm caused by the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s offense. The dispute will usually just be over the amount of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s loss\xe2\x80\x94for example, the value of lost assets or the cost of a night in the hospital.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Amy has a qualitatively different injury. Her loss, while undoubtedly genuine, is a result of the collective actions of a huge number of people\xe2\x80\x94beginning with her uncle who abused her and put her images on the Internet, to the distributors who make those images more widely avail-able, to the possessors such as Paroline who view her im-ages. The harm to Amy was produced over time, gradually, by tens of thousands of persons acting independently from one another.[3] She suffers in particular from her knowledge that her images are being viewed online by an unknown number of people, and from her fear that any person she meets might recognize her from having witnessed her abuse. App. 59\xe2\x80\x9366. But Amy does not know who Paroline is. Id., at 295, n.\xc2\xa011. Nothing in the record comes close to establishing that Amy would have suffered less if Paroline had not possessed her images, let alone how much less. See Brief for United States 25. Amy\xe2\x80\x99s injury is indivisible, which means that Paroline\xe2\x80\x99s particular share of her losses is unknowable. And yet it is proof of Paroline\xe2\x80\x99s particular share that the statute requires.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n By simply importing the generic restitution statute without accounting for the diffuse harm suffered by victims of child pornography, Congress set up a restitution system sure to fail in cases like this one. Perhaps a case with different facts, say, a single distributor and only a handful of possessors, would be susceptible of the proof the statute requires. But when tens of thousands of copies (or more) of Amy\xe2\x80\x99s images have changed hands all across the world for more than a decade, a demand for the Government to prove \xe2\x80\x9cthe amount of the loss sustained by a victim as a result of the offense\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94the offense before the court in any particular case\xe2\x80\x94is a demand for the impos-sible. \xc2\xa73664(e) (emphasis added). When Congress con-ditioned restitution on the Government\xe2\x80\x99s meeting that burden of proof, it effectively precluded restitution inmost cases involving possession or distribution of child pornography.

\n

II

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The District Court in Paroline\xe2\x80\x99s case found that the Government could not meet its statutory burden of proof. The Government does not really contest that holding here; it instead asks to be held to a less demanding standard. Having litigated this issue for years now in virtually every Circuit, the best the Government has come up with is to tell courts awarding restitution to look at what other courts have done. But that is not a workable guide, not least because courts have taken vastly different approaches to materially indistinguishable cases. According to the Government\xe2\x80\x99s lodging in this case, District Courts awarding less than Amy\xe2\x80\x99s full losses have imposed restitution orders varying from $50 to $530,000.[4] Restitution Awards for Amy Through December 11, 2013, Lodging of United States. How is a court supposed to use those figures as any sort of guidance? Pick the median figure? The mean? Something else?

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n More to the point, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s submission lacks any basis in law. That the first district courts confronted with Amy\xe2\x80\x99s case awarded $1,000, or $5,000, or $530,000, for no articulable reason, is not a legal basis for awarding one of those figures in Paroline\xe2\x80\x99s case. The statute requires proof of this defendant\xe2\x80\x99s harm done, not the going rate. And of course, as the Government acknowledges, its approach \xe2\x80\x9cdoesn\xe2\x80\x99t work very well\xe2\x80\x9d in the first case brought by a particular victim. Tr. of Oral Arg. 24.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The majority\xe2\x80\x99s proposal is to have a district court \xe2\x80\x9cassess as best it can from available evidence the significance of the individual defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conduct in light of the broader causal process that produced the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante,at 22. Even if that were a plausible way to design a restitution system for Amy\xe2\x80\x99s complex injury, there is no way around the fact that it is not the system that Congress created. The statute requires restitution to be based exclusively on the losses that resulted from the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s crime\xe2\x80\x94not on the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s relative culpability. The majority\xe2\x80\x99s plan to situate Paroline along a spectrum of offenders who have contributed to Amy\xe2\x80\x99s harm will not assist a district court in calculating the amount of Amy\xe2\x80\x99s losses\xe2\x80\x94the amount of her lost wages and counseling costs\xe2\x80\x94that was caused by Paroline\xe2\x80\x99s crime (or that of any other defendant).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court is correct, of course, that awarding Amy no restitution would be contrary to Congress\xe2\x80\x99s remedial and penological purposes. See ante, at 20. But we have previously refused to allow \xe2\x80\x9cpolicy considerations\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94including an \xe2\x80\x9cexpansive declaration of purpose,\xe2\x80\x9d and the need to \xe2\x80\x9ccompensate victims for the full losses they suffered\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94to deter us from reading virtually identical statutory language to require proof of the harm caused solely by the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s particular offense. Hughey, 495 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 420\xe2\x80\x93421.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Moreover, even the Court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9crelative role in the causal process\xe2\x80\x9d approach to the statute, ante, at 21, is unlikely to make Amy whole. To the extent that district courts do form a sort of consensus on how much to award, experience shows that the amount in any particular case will be quite small\xe2\x80\x94the significant majority of defendants have been ordered to pay Amy $5,000 or less. Lodging of United States. This means that Amy will be stuck litigatingfor years to come. The Court acknowledges that Amy may end up with \xe2\x80\x9cpiecemeal\xe2\x80\x9d restitution, yet responds simply that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress has not promised victims full and swift restitution at all costs.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 24.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Amy will fare no better if district courts consider the other factors suggested by the majority, including the number of defendants convicted of possessing Amy\xe2\x80\x99s im-ages, a rough estimate of those likely to be convicted in the future, and an even rougher estimate of the total number of persons involved in her harm. Ante, at 23. In the first place, only the last figure is relevant, because Paroline\xe2\x80\x99s relative significance can logically be measured only in light of everyone who contributed to Amy\xe2\x80\x99s injury\xe2\x80\x94not just those who have been, or will be, caught and convicted. Even worse, to the extent it is possible to project the total number of persons who have viewed Amy\xe2\x80\x99s images, that number is tragically large, which means that restitution awards tied to it will lead to a pitiful recovery in every case. See Brief for Respondent Amy 65 (estimating Paro-line\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98market share\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of Amy\xe2\x80\x99s harm at 1/71,000, or $47). The majority says that courts should not impose \xe2\x80\x9ctrivial restitution orders,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 23, but it is hard to see how a court fairly assessing this defendant\xe2\x80\x99s relative contribution could do anything else.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Nor can confidence in judicial discretion save the statute from arbitrary application. See ante, at 22, 25\xe2\x80\x9326. It is true that district courts exercise substantial discretion in awarding restitution and imposing sentences in general. But they do not do so by mere instinct. Courts are instead guided by statutory standards: in the restitution context, a fair determination of the losses caused by the individual defendant under section 3664(e); in sentencing more generally, the detailed factors in section 3553(a). A contrary approach\xe2\x80\x94one that asks district judges to impose restitution or other criminal punishment guided solely by their own intuitions regarding comparative fault\xe2\x80\x94would undermine the requirement that every criminal defendant receive due process of law.

\n

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision today means that Amy will not go home with nothing. But it would be a mistake for that salutary outcome to lead readers to conclude that Amy has prevailed or that Congress has done justice for victims of child pornography. The statute as written allows no recovery; we ought to say so, and give Congress a chance to fix it.
