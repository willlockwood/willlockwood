MICHIGAN v. BAY MILLS INDIAN COMMUNITY et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

No. 12\xe2\x80\x93515.\xe2\x80\x83Argued December 2, 2013\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 27, 2014

\n

The State of Michigan, petitioner, entered into a compact with respondent Bay Mills Indian Community pursuant to the Indian Gaming Regulatory Act (IGRA). See\n\n25 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72710(d)(1)(C). The compact authorizes Bay Mills to conduct class III gaming activities (i.e., to operate a casino) on Indian lands located within the State\xe2\x80\x99s borders, but prohibits it from doing so outside that territory. Bay Mills later opened a second casino on land it had purchased through a congressionally established land trust. The Tribe claimed it could operate a casino there because the property qualified as Indian land. Michigan disagreed and sued the Tribe under \xc2\xa72710(d)(7)(A)(ii), which allows a State to enjoin \xe2\x80\x9cclass III gaming activity located on Indian lands and conducted in violation of any Tribal-State compact.\xe2\x80\x9d The District Court granted the injunction, but the Sixth Circuit vacated. It held that tribal sovereign immunity barred the suit unless Congress provided otherwise, and that \xc2\xa72710(d)(7)(A)(ii) only authorized suits to enjoin gaming activity located \xe2\x80\x9con Indian lands,\xe2\x80\x9d whereas Michigan\xe2\x80\x99s complaint alleged the casino was outside such territory.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Michigan\xe2\x80\x99s suit against Bay Mills is barred by tribal sovereign immunity. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9321.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (a)\xc2\xa0As \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98domestic dependent nations,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Indian tribes exercise \xe2\x80\x9cinherent sovereign authority\xe2\x80\x9d that is subject to plenary control by Congress. Oklahoma Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Citizen Band Potawatomi Tribe of Okla.,\n\n498 U.\xc2\xa0S. 505\n. Unless and \xe2\x80\x9cuntil Congress acts, the tribes retain\xe2\x80\x9d their historic sovereign authority. United States v. Wheeler,\n\n435 U.\xc2\xa0S. 313\n. Among the core aspects of sovereignty that tribes possess\xe2\x80\x94subject to congressional action\xe2\x80\x94is the \xe2\x80\x9ccommon-law immunity from suit traditionally enjoyed by sovereign powers.\xe2\x80\x9d Santa Clara Pueblo v. Martinez,\n\n436 U.\xc2\xa0S. 49\n. That immunity applies whether a suit is brought by a State, see, e.g., Puyallup Tribe, Inc. v. Department of Game of Wash.,\n\n433 U.\xc2\xa0S. 165\n, or arises from a tribe\xe2\x80\x99s commercial activities off Indian lands, see Kiowa Tribe of Okla. v. Manufacturing Technologies, Inc.,\n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 751\n. Therefore, unless Congress has \xe2\x80\x9cunequivocally\xe2\x80\x9d authorized Michigan\xe2\x80\x99s suit, C & L Enterprises, Inc. v. Citizen Band Potawatomi Tribe of Okla.,\n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 411\n, it must be dismissed. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (b)\xc2\xa0IGRA\xe2\x80\x99s plain terms do not authorize this suit. Section 2710(d)(7)(A)(ii) partially abrogates tribal immunity with respect to class III gaming located \xe2\x80\x9con Indian lands,\xe2\x80\x9d but the very premise of Michigan\xe2\x80\x99s suit is that Bay Mills\xe2\x80\x99 casino is unlawful because it is outside Indian lands. Michigan argues that the casino is authorized, licensed, and operated from within the reservation, and that such administrative action constitutes \xe2\x80\x9cclass III gaming activity.\xe2\x80\x9d However, numerous other IGRA provisions make clear that \xe2\x80\x9cclass III gaming activity\xe2\x80\x9d refers to the gambling that goes on in a casino, not the off-site licensing of such games. See, e.g., \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72710(d)(3)(C)(i), (d)(9). IGRA\xe2\x80\x99s history and design also explain why Congress would have authorized a State to enjoin illegal tribal gaming on Indian lands but not on lands subject to the State\xe2\x80\x99s own sovereign jurisdiction. Congress adopted IGRA in response to California v. Cabazon Band of Mission Indians,\n\n480 U.\xc2\xa0S. 202\n\xe2\x80\x93222, which held that States lacked regulatory authority over gaming on Indian lands but left intact States\xe2\x80\x99 regulatory power over tribal gaming outside Indian territory. A State therefore has many tools to enforce its law on state land that it does not possess in Indian territory, including, e.g., bringing a civil or criminal action against tribal officials rather than the tribe itself for conducting illegal gaming. A State can also use its leverage in negotiating an IGRA compact to bargain for a waiver of the tribe\xe2\x80\x99s immunity. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (c)\xc2\xa0Michigan urges the Court to overrule Kiowa and hold that tribal immunity does not apply to commercial activity outside Indian territory. However, \xe2\x80\x9cany departure\xe2\x80\x9d from precedent \xe2\x80\x9cdemands special justification,\xe2\x80\x9d Arizona v. Rumsey,\n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 203\n, and Michigan offers nothing more than arguments already rejected in Kiowa. Kiowa rejected these arguments because it is fundamentally Congress\xe2\x80\x99s job to determine whether or how to limit tribal immunity; Congress had restricted tribal immunity \xe2\x80\x9cin limited circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d like \xc2\xa72710(d)(7)(A)(ii), while \xe2\x80\x9cin other statutes\xe2\x80\x9d declaring an \xe2\x80\x9cintention not to alter it.\xe2\x80\x9d 523 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 758. Kiowa therefore chose to \xe2\x80\x9cdefer to the role Congress may wish to exercise in this important judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Congress has since reflected on Kiowa and decided to retain tribal immunity in a case like this. Having held that the issue is up to Congress, the Court cannot reverse itself now simply because some may think Congress\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion wrong. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9321.

\n

MICHIGAN, PETITIONER v. BAY MILLS INDIANCOMMUNITY et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

[May 27, 2014]

\n

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Justice Kagan delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The question in this case is whether tribal sovereign immunity bars Michigan\xe2\x80\x99s suit against the Bay Mills Indian Community for opening a casino outside Indian lands. We hold that immunity protects Bay Mills from this legal action. Congress has not abrogated tribal sovereign immunity from a State\xe2\x80\x99s suit to enjoin gaming off a reservation or other Indian lands. And we decline to revisit our prior decisions holding that, absent such an abrogation (or a waiver), Indian tribes have immunity even when a suit arises from off-reservation commercial activity. Michigan must therefore resort to other mechanisms, including legal actions against the responsible individuals, to resolve this dispute.

\n

I

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Indian Gaming Regulatory Act (IGRA or Act),\n\n102Stat.\n2467,\n\n25 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72701 et\xc2\xa0seq., creates a framework for regulating gaming activity on Indian lands.[1] See \xc2\xa72702(3) (describing the statute\xe2\x80\x99s purpose as establishing \xe2\x80\x9cregulatory authority .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [and] standards for gaming on Indian lands\xe2\x80\x9d). The Act divides gaming into three classes. Class III gaming, the most closely regulated and the kind involved here, includes casino games, slot machines, and horse racing. See \xc2\xa72703(8). A tribe may conduct such gaming on Indian lands only pursuant to, and in compliance with, a compact it has negotiated with the sur-rounding State. See \xc2\xa72710(d)(1)(C). A compact typically prescribes rules for operating gaming, allocates law enforcement authority between the tribe and State, and provides remedies for breach of the agreement\xe2\x80\x99s terms. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72710(d)(3)(C)(ii), (v). Notable here, IGRA itself authorizes a State to bring suit against a tribe for certain conduct violating a compact: Specifically, \xc2\xa72710(d)(7)(A)(ii) allows a State to sue in federal court to \xe2\x80\x9cenjoin a class III gaming activity located on Indian lands and conducted in violation of any Tribal-State compact .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that is in effect.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Pursuant to the Act, Michigan and Bay Mills, a federally recognized Indian Tribe, entered into a compact in 1993. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 73a\xe2\x80\x9396a. The compact empowers Bay Mills to conduct class III gaming on \xe2\x80\x9cIndian lands\xe2\x80\x9d; conversely, it prohibits the Tribe from doing so outside that territory. Id., at 78a, 83a; see n.\xc2\xa01, supra. The compact also contains a dispute resolution mechanism, which sends to arbitration any contractual differences the parties cannot settle on their own. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 89a\xe2\x80\x9390a. A provision within that arbitration section states that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]othing in this Compact shall be deemed a waiver\xe2\x80\x9d of either the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s or the State\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign immunity. Id., at 90a. Since entering into the com-pact, Bay Mills has operated class III gaming, as authorized, on its reservation in Michigan\xe2\x80\x99s Upper Peninsula.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In 2010, Bay Mills opened another class III gaming facility in Vanderbilt, a small village in Michigan\xe2\x80\x99s Lower Peninsula about 125 miles from the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s reservation. Bay Mills had bought the Vanderbilt property with accrued interest from a federal appropriation, which Congress had made to compensate the Tribe for 19th-century takings of its ancestral lands. See Michigan Indian Land Claims Settlement Act,\n\n111Stat.\n2652. Congress had directed that a portion of the appropriated funds go into a \xe2\x80\x9cLand Trust\xe2\x80\x9d whose earnings the Tribe was to use to improve or purchase property. According to the legislation, any land so acquired \xe2\x80\x9cshall be held as Indian lands are held.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7107(a)(3), id., at 2658. Citing that provision, Bay Mills contended that the Vanderbilt property was \xe2\x80\x9cIndian land\xe2\x80\x9d under IGRA and the compact; and the Tribe thus claimed authority to operate a casino there.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Michigan disagreed: The State sued Bay Mills in federal court to enjoin operation of the new casino, alleging that the facility violated IGRA and the compact because it was located outside Indian lands. The same day Michigan filed suit, the federal Department of the Interior issued an opinion concluding (as the State\xe2\x80\x99s complaint said) that the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s use of Land Trust earnings to purchase the Vanderbilt property did not convert it into Indian territory. See App. 69\xe2\x80\x93101. The District Court entered a preliminary injunction against Bay Mills, which promptly shut down the new casino and took an interlocutory appeal. While that appeal was pending, Michigan amended its complaint to join various tribal officials as defendants, as well as to add state law and federal common law claims. The Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit then vacated the injunction, holding (among other things) that tribal sovereign immunity barred Michigan\xe2\x80\x99s suit against Bay Mills unless Congress provided otherwise, and that \xc2\xa72710(d)(7)(A)(ii) did not authorize the action. See 695 F.\xc2\xa03d 406, 413\xe2\x80\x93415 (2012). That provision of IGRA, the Sixth Circuit reasoned, permitted a suit against the Tribe to enjoin only gaming activity located on Indian lands, whereas the State\xe2\x80\x99s complaint alleged that the Vanderbilt casino was outside such territory. See id., at 412.[2] Accordingly, the Court of Appeals concluded that Michigan could proceed, if at all, solely against the individual defendants, and it remanded to the District Court to consider those claims. See id., at 416\xe2\x80\x93417.[3] Although no injunc-tion is currently in effect, Bay Mills has not reopened the Vanderbilt casino.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We granted certiorari to consider whether tribal sovereign immunity bars Michigan\xe2\x80\x99s suit against Bay Mills, 570 U.\xc2\xa0S. __ (2013), and we now affirm the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 judgment.

\n

II

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Indian tribes are \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98domestic dependent nations\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d that exercise \xe2\x80\x9cinherent sovereign authority.\xe2\x80\x9d Oklahoma Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Citizen Band Potawatomi Tribe of Okla.,\n\n498 U.\xc2\xa0S. 505,\n509 (1991)\n (Potawatomi) (quoting Cherokee Nation v. Georgia, 5 Pet. 1, 17 (1831)). As dependents, the tribes are subject to plenary control by Congress. See United States v. Lara,\n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 193,\n200 (2004)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he Constitution grants Congress\xe2\x80\x9d powers \xe2\x80\x9cwe have consistently described as \xe2\x80\x98plenary and exclusive\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9clegislate in respect to Indian tribes\xe2\x80\x9d). And yet they remain \xe2\x80\x9cseparate sovereigns pre-existing the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Santa Clara Pueblo v. Martinez,\n\n436 U.\xc2\xa0S. 49,\n56 (1978)\n. Thus, unless and \xe2\x80\x9cuntil Congress acts, the tribes retain\xe2\x80\x9d their historic sovereign authority. United States v. Wheeler,\n\n435 U.\xc2\xa0S. 313,\n323 (1978)\n.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Among the core aspects of sovereignty that tribes possess\xe2\x80\x94subject, again, to congressional action\xe2\x80\x94is the \xe2\x80\x9ccommon-law immunity from suit traditionally enjoyed by sovereign powers.\xe2\x80\x9d Santa Clara Pueblo, 436 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 58. That immunity, we have explained, is \xe2\x80\x9ca necessary corollary to Indian sovereignty and self-governance.\xe2\x80\x9d Three Affiliated Tribes of Fort Berthold Reservation v. Wold Engineering, P. C.,\n\n476 U.\xc2\xa0S. 877,\n890 (1986)\n; cf. The Federalist No. 81, p. 511 (B. Wright ed. 1961) (A. Hamilton) (It is \xe2\x80\x9cinherent in the nature of sovereignty not to be amenable\xe2\x80\x9d to suit without consent). And the qualified nature of Indian sovereignty modifies that principle only by placing a tribe\xe2\x80\x99s immunity, like its other governmental powers and attributes, in Congress\xe2\x80\x99s hands. See United States v. United States Fidelity & Guaranty Co.,\n\n309 U.\xc2\xa0S. 506,\n512 (1940)\n (USF&G) (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is as though the immunity which was theirs as sovereigns passed to the United States for their benefit\xe2\x80\x9d). Thus, we have time and again treated the \xe2\x80\x9cdoctrine of tribal immunity [as] settled law\xe2\x80\x9d and dismissed any suit against a tribe absent congressional authorization (or a waiver). Kiowa Tribe of Okla. v. Manufacturing Technologies, Inc.,\n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 751,\n756 (1998)\n.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In doing so, we have held that tribal immunity applies no less to suits brought by States (including in their own courts) than to those by individuals. First in Puyallup Tribe, Inc. v. Department of Game of Wash.,\n\n433 U.\xc2\xa0S. 165\n\xe2\x80\x93168, 172\xe2\x80\x93173 (1977), and then again in Potawatomi, 498 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 509\xe2\x80\x93510, we barred a State seeking to enforce its laws from filing suit against a tribe, rejecting arguments grounded in the State\xe2\x80\x99s own sovereignty. In each case, we said a State must resort to other remedies, even if they would be less \xe2\x80\x9cefficient.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 514; see Kiowa, 523 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 755 (\xe2\x80\x9cThere is a difference between the right to demand compliance with state laws and the means available to enforce them\xe2\x80\x9d). That is because, as we have often stated (and contrary to the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s novel pronouncement, see post, at 3 (opinion of Thomas, J.) (hereinafter the dissent)), tribal immunity \xe2\x80\x9cis a matter of federal law and is not subject to diminution by the States.\xe2\x80\x9d 523 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 756 (citing Three Affiliated Tribes, 476 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 891; Washington v. Confederated Tribes of Colville Reservation,\n\n447 U.\xc2\xa0S. 134,\n154 (1980)\n). Or as we elsewhere explained: While each State at the Constitutional Convention surrendered its immunity from suit by sister States, \xe2\x80\x9cit would be absurd to suggest that the tribes\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94at a conference \xe2\x80\x9cto which they were not even parties\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94similarly ceded their immunity against state-initiated suits. Blatchford v. Native Village of Noatak,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 775,\n782 (1991)\n.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Equally important here, we declined in Kiowa to make any exception for suits arising from a tribe\xe2\x80\x99s commercial activities, even when they take place off Indian lands. In that case, a private party sued a tribe in state court for defaulting on a promissory note. The plaintiff asked this Court to confine tribal immunity to suits involving conduct on \xe2\x80\x9creservations or to noncommercial activities.\xe2\x80\x9d 523 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 758. We said no. We listed Puyallup, Potawa-tomi, and USF&G as precedents applying immunity to a suit predicated on a tribe\xe2\x80\x99s commercial conduct\xe2\x80\x94respectively, fishing, selling cigarettes, and leasing coal mines. 523 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 754\xe2\x80\x93755. Too, we noted that Puyallup involved enterprise \xe2\x80\x9cboth on and off [the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s] reservation.\xe2\x80\x9d 523 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 754 (quoting 433 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 167). \xe2\x80\x9c[O]ur precedents,\xe2\x80\x9d we thus concluded, have not previously \xe2\x80\x9cdrawn the[ ] distinctions\xe2\x80\x9d the plaintiff pressed in the case. 523 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 755. They had established a broad principle, from which we thought it improper suddenly to start carving out exceptions. Rather, we opted to \xe2\x80\x9cdefer\xe2\x80\x9d to Congress about whether to abrogate tribal immunity for off-reservation commercial conduct. Id., at 758, 760; see infra, at 17\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Our decisions establish as well that such a congressional decision must be clear. The baseline position, we have often held, is tribal immunity; and \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o abrogate [such] immunity, Congress must \xe2\x80\x98unequivocally\xe2\x80\x99 express that purpose.\xe2\x80\x9d C\xc2\xa0&\xc2\xa0L Enterprises, Inc. v. Citizen Band Potawatomi Tribe of Okla.,\n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 411,\n418 (2001)\n (quoting Santa Clara Pueblo, 436 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 58). That rule of construction reflects an enduring principle of Indian law: Although Congress has plenary authority over tribes, courts will not lightly assume that Congress in fact intends to undermine Indian self-government. See, e.g., id., at 58\xe2\x80\x9360; Iowa Mut. Ins. Co. v. LaPlante,\n\n480 U.\xc2\xa0S. 9,\n18 (1987)\n; United States v. Dion,\n\n476 U.\xc2\xa0S. 734\n\xe2\x80\x93739 (1986).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The upshot is this: Unless Congress has authorized Michigan\xe2\x80\x99s suit, our precedents demand that it be dismissed.[4] And so Michigan, naturally enough, makes two arguments: first, that IGRA indeed abrogates the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s immunity from the State\xe2\x80\x99s suit; and second, that if it does not, we should revisit\xe2\x80\x94and reverse\xe2\x80\x94our decision in Kiowa, so that tribal immunity no longer applies to claims arising from commercial activity outside Indian lands. We consider\xe2\x80\x94and reject\xe2\x80\x94each contention in turn.

\n

III

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n IGRA partially abrogates tribal sovereign immunity in \xc2\xa72710(d)(7)(A)(ii)\xe2\x80\x94but this case, viewed most naturally, falls outside that term\xe2\x80\x99s ambit. The provision, as noted above, authorizes a State to sue a tribe to \xe2\x80\x9cenjoin a class III gaming activity located on Indian lands and conducted in violation of any Tribal-State compact.\xe2\x80\x9d See supra, at 2; Kiowa, 523 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 758 (citing the provision as an example of legislation \xe2\x80\x9crestrict[ing] tribal immunity from suit in limited circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d). A key phrase in that abrogation is \xe2\x80\x9con Indian lands\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94three words reflecting IGRA\xe2\x80\x99s overall scope (and repeated some two dozen times in the statute). A State\xe2\x80\x99s suit to enjoin gaming activity on Indian lands (assuming other requirements are met, see n.\xc2\xa06, infra) falls within \xc2\xa72710(d)(7)(A)(ii); a similar suit to stop gaming activity off Indian lands does not. And that creates a fundamental problem for Michigan. After all, the very premise of this suit\xe2\x80\x94the reason Michigan thinks Bay Mills is acting unlawfully\xe2\x80\x94is that the Vanderbilt casino is outside Indian lands. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 59a\xe2\x80\x9360a. By dint of that theory, a suit to enjoin gaming in Vanderbilt is correspondingly outside \xc2\xa72710(d)(7)(A)(ii)\xe2\x80\x99s abrogation of immunity.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Michigan first attempts to fit this suit within \xc2\xa72710(d)(7)(A)(ii) by relocating the \xe2\x80\x9cclass III gaming activity\xe2\x80\x9d to which it is objecting. True enough, Michigan states, the Vanderbilt casino lies outside Indian lands. But Bay Mills \xe2\x80\x9cauthorized, licensed, and operated\xe2\x80\x9d that casino from within its own reservation. Brief for Michigan 20. According to the State, that necessary administrative action\xe2\x80\x94no less than, say, dealing craps\xe2\x80\x94is \xe2\x80\x9cclass III gaming activ-ity,\xe2\x80\x9d and because it occurred on Indian land, this suit to enjoin it can go forward.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n But that argument comes up snake eyes, because numerous provisions of IGRA show that \xe2\x80\x9cclass III gaming activity\xe2\x80\x9d means just what it sounds like\xe2\x80\x94the stuff involved in playing class III games. For example, \xc2\xa72710(d)(3)(C)(i) refers to \xe2\x80\x9cthe licensing and regulation of [a class III gaming] activity\xe2\x80\x9d and \xc2\xa72710(d)(9) concerns the \xe2\x80\x9coperation of a class III gaming activity.\xe2\x80\x9d Those phrases make perfect sense if \xe2\x80\x9cclass III gaming activity\xe2\x80\x9d is what goes on in a casino\xe2\x80\x94each roll of the dice and spin of the wheel. But they lose all meaning if, as Michigan argues, \xe2\x80\x9cclass III gaming activity\xe2\x80\x9d refers equally to the off-site licensing or operation of the games. (Just plug in those words and see what happens.) See also \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72710(b)(2)(A), (b)(4)(A), (c)(4), (d)(1)(A) (similarly referring to class II or III \xe2\x80\x9cgaming activity\xe2\x80\x9d). The same holds true throughout the statute. Section 2717(a)(1) specifies fees to be paid by \xe2\x80\x9ceach gaming operation that conducts a class II or class III gaming activity\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94signifying that the gaming activity is the gambling in the poker hall, not the proceedings of the off-site administrative authority. And \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72706(a)(5) and 2713(b)(1) together describe a federal agency\xe2\x80\x99s power to \xe2\x80\x9cclos[e] a gaming activity\xe2\x80\x9d for \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial violation[s]\xe2\x80\x9d of law\xe2\x80\x94e.g., to shut down crooked blackjack tables, not the tribal regulatory body meant to oversee them. Indeed, consider IGRA\xe2\x80\x99s very first finding: Many tribes, Congress stated, \xe2\x80\x9chave licensed gaming activities on Indian lands,\xe2\x80\x9d thereby necessitating federal regulation. \xc2\xa72701(1). The \xe2\x80\x9cgaming activit[y]\xe2\x80\x9d is (once again) the gambling. And that means \xc2\xa72710(d)(7)(A)(ii) does not allow Michigan\xe2\x80\x99s suit even if Bay Mills took action on its reservation to license or oversee the Vanderbilt facility.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Stymied under \xc2\xa72710(d)(7)(A)(ii), Michigan next urges us to adopt a \xe2\x80\x9cholistic method\xe2\x80\x9d of interpreting IGRA that would allow a State to sue a tribe for illegal gaming off, no less than on, Indian lands. Brief for Michigan 30. Michi-gan asks here that we consider \xe2\x80\x9cIGRA\xe2\x80\x99s text and structure as a whole.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 28. But (with one briefly raised exception) Michigan fails to identify any specific textual or structural features of the statute to support its proposed result.[5] Rather, Michigan highlights a (purported) anomaly of the statute as written: that it enables a State to sue a tribe for illegal gaming inside, but not outside, Indian country. \xe2\x80\x9c[W]hy,\xe2\x80\x9d Michigan queries, \xe2\x80\x9cwould Congress authorize a state to obtain a federal injunction against illegal tribal gaming on Indian lands, but not on lands subject to the state\xe2\x80\x99s own sovereign jurisdiction?\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief 1. That question has no answer, Michigan argues: Whatever words Congress may have used in IGRA, it could not have intended that senseless outcome. See Brief for Michigan 28.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n But this Court does not revise legislation, as Michigan proposes, just because the text as written creates an apparent anomaly as to some subject it does not address. Truth be told, such anomalies often arise from statutes, if for no other reason than that Congress typically legislates by parts\xe2\x80\x94addressing one thing without examining all others that might merit comparable treatment. Rejecting a similar argument that a statutory anomaly (between property and non-property taxes) made \xe2\x80\x9cnot a whit of sense,\xe2\x80\x9d we explained in one recent case that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress wrote the statute it wrote\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94meaning, a statute going so far and no further. See CSX Transp., Inc. v. Alabama Dept. of Revenue, 562 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 17\xe2\x80\x9318). The same could be said of IGRA\xe2\x80\x99s abrogation of tribal immunity for gaming \xe2\x80\x9con Indian lands.\xe2\x80\x9d This Court has no roving license, in even ordinary cases of statutory interpretation, to disregard clear language simply on the view that (in Michigan\xe2\x80\x99s words) Congress \xe2\x80\x9cmust have intended\xe2\x80\x9d something broader. Brief for Michigan 32. And still less do we have that warrant when the consequence would be to expand an abrogation of immunity, because (as explained earlier) \xe2\x80\x9cCongress must \xe2\x80\x98unequivocally\xe2\x80\x99 express [its] purpose\xe2\x80\x9d to subject a tribe to litigation. C\xc2\xa0&\xc2\xa0L Enterprises, 532 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 418; see supra, at 7.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In any event, IGRA\xe2\x80\x99s history and design provide a more than intelligible answer to the question Michigan poses about why Congress would have confined a State\xe2\x80\x99s authority to sue a tribe as \xc2\xa72710(d)(7)(A)(ii) does. Congress adopted IGRA in response to this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in California v. Cabazon Band of Mission Indians,\n\n480 U.\xc2\xa0S. 202\n\xe2\x80\x93222 (1987), which held that States lacked any regulatory authority over gaming on Indian lands. Cabazon left fully intact a State\xe2\x80\x99s regulatory power over tribal gaming outside Indian territory\xe2\x80\x94which, as we will soon show, is capacious. See infra, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313. So the problem Congress set out to address in IGRA (Cabazon\xe2\x80\x99s ouster of state authority) arose in Indian lands alone. And the solution Congress devised, naturally enough, reflected that fact. See, e.g., Seminole Tribe of Fla. v. Florida,\n\n517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 44,\n58 (1996)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he Act grants the States a power that they would not otherwise have, viz., some measure of author-ity over gaming on Indian lands\xe2\x80\x9d). Everything\xe2\x80\x94literally everything\xe2\x80\x94in IGRA affords tools (for either state or federal officials) to regulate gaming on Indian lands, and nowhere else. Small surprise that IGRA\xe2\x80\x99s abrogation of tribal immunity does that as well.[6]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n And the resulting world, when considered functionally, is not nearly so \xe2\x80\x9cenigma[tic]\xe2\x80\x9d as Michigan suggests. Reply Brief 1. True enough, a State lacks the ability to sue a tribe for illegal gaming when that activity occurs off the reservation. But a State, on its own lands, has many other powers over tribal gaming that it does not possess (absent consent) in Indian territory. Unless federal law provides differently, \xe2\x80\x9cIndians going beyond reservation boundaries\xe2\x80\x9d are subject to any generally applicable state law. See Wagnon v. Prairie Band Potawatomi Nation,\n\n546 U.\xc2\xa0S. 95,\n113 (2005)\n (quoting Mescalero Apache Tribe v. Jones,\n\n411 U.\xc2\xa0S. 145,\n148 (1973)\n). So, for example, Michigan could, in the first instance, deny a license to Bay Mills for an off-reservation casino. See Mich. Comp. Laws Ann. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7432.206\xe2\x80\x93432.206a (West 2001). And if Bay Mills went ahead anyway, Michigan could bring suit against tribal officials or employees (rather than the Tribe itself\xc2\xa0) seeking an injunction for, say, gambling without a license. See \xc2\xa7432.220; see also \xc2\xa7600.3801(1)(a) (West 2013) (designating illegal gambling facilities as public nuisances). As this Court has stated before, analogizing to Ex parte Young,\n\n209 U.\xc2\xa0S. 123 (1908)\n, tribal immunity does not bar such a suit for injunctive relief against individuals, includ-ing tribal officers, responsible for unlawful conduct. See Santa Clara Pueblo, 436 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 59. And to the extent civil remedies proved inadequate, Michigan could resort to its criminal law, prosecuting anyone who maintains\xe2\x80\x94or even frequents\xe2\x80\x94an unlawful gambling establishment. See Mich. Comp. Laws Ann. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7432.218 (West 2001), 750.303, 750.309 (West 2004). In short (and contrary to the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s unsupported assertion, see post, at 11), the panoply of tools Michigan can use to enforce its law on its own lands\xe2\x80\x94no less than the suit it could bring on Indian lands under \xc2\xa72710(d)(7)(A)(ii)\xe2\x80\x94can shutter, quickly and permanently, an illegal casino.[7]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Finally, if a State really wants to sue a tribe for gaming outside Indian lands, the State need only bargain for a waiver of immunity. Under IGRA, a State and tribe negotiating a compact \xe2\x80\x9cmay include .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. remedies for breach of contract,\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n25 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72710(d)(3)(C)(v)\xe2\x80\x94including a provision allowing the State to bring an action against the tribe in the circumstances presented here. States have more than enough leverage to obtain such terms because a tribe cannot conduct class III gaming on its lands without a compact, see \xc2\xa72710(d)(1)(C), and cannot sue to enforce a State\xe2\x80\x99s duty to negotiate a compact in good faith, see Seminole Tribe, 517 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 47 (holding a State immune from such suits). So as Michigan forthrightly acknowl-edges, \xe2\x80\x9ca party dealing with a tribe in contract negotiations has the power to protect itself by refusing to deal absent the tribe\xe2\x80\x99s waiver of sovereign immunity from suit.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Michigan 40. And many States have taken that path. See Brief for Seminole Tribe of Florida et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 12\xe2\x80\x9322 (listing compacts with waivers of tribal immunity). To be sure, Michigan did not: As noted earlier, the compact at issue here, instead of authorizing judicial remedies, sends disputes to arbitration and expressly retains each party\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign immunity. See supra, at 2. But Michigan\xe2\x80\x94like any State\xe2\x80\x94could have insisted on a different deal (and indeed may do so now for the future, because the current compact has expired and remains in effect only until the parties negotiate a new one, see Tr. of Oral Arg. 21). And in that event, the limitation Congress placed on IGRA\xe2\x80\x99s abrogation of tribal immunity\xe2\x80\x94whether or not anomalous as an abstract matter\xe2\x80\x94would have made no earthly difference.

\n

IV

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Because IGRA\xe2\x80\x99s plain terms do not abrogate Bay Mills\xe2\x80\x99 immunity from this suit, Michigan (and the dissent) must make a more dramatic argument: that this Court should \xe2\x80\x9crevisit[ ] Kiowa\xe2\x80\x99s holding\xe2\x80\x9d and rule that tribes \xe2\x80\x9chave no immunity for illegal commercial activity outside their sovereign territory.\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief 8, 10; see post, at 1. Michigan argues that tribes increasingly participate in off-reservation gaming and other commercial activity, and operate in that capacity less as governments than as private businesses. See Brief for Michigan 38 (noting, among other things, that \xe2\x80\x9ctribal gaming revenues have more than tripled\xe2\x80\x9d since Kiowa). Further, Michigan contends, tribes have broader immunity from suits arising from such conduct than other sovereigns\xe2\x80\x94most notably, because Congress enacted legislation limiting foreign nations\xe2\x80\x99 immunity for commercial activity in the United States. See id., at 41;\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71605(a)(2). It is time, Michigan concludes, to \xe2\x80\x9clevel[ ] the playing field.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Michigan 38.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n But this Court does not overturn its precedents lightly. Stare decisis, we have stated, \xe2\x80\x9cis the preferred course because it promotes the evenhanded, predictable, and consistent development of legal principles, fosters reliance on judicial decisions, and contributes to the actual and perceived integrity of the judicial process.\xe2\x80\x9d Payne v. Tennessee,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 808,\n827 (1991)\n. Although \xe2\x80\x9cnot an inexorable command,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 828, stare decisis is a foundation stone of the rule of law, necessary to ensure that legal rules develop \xe2\x80\x9cin a principled and intelligible fashion,\xe2\x80\x9d Vasquez v. Hillery,\n\n474 U.\xc2\xa0S. 254,\n265 (1986)\n. For that reason, this Court has always held that \xe2\x80\x9cany departure\xe2\x80\x9d from the doctrine \xe2\x80\x9cdemands special justification.\xe2\x80\x9d Arizona v. Rumsey,\n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 203,\n212 (1984)\n.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n And that is more than usually so in the circumstances here. First, Kiowa itself was no one-off: Rather, in rejecting the identical argument Michigan makes, our decision reaffirmed a long line of precedents, concluding that \xe2\x80\x9cthe doctrine of tribal immunity\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94without any exceptions for commercial or off-reservation conduct\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cis settled law and controls this case.\xe2\x80\x9d 523 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 756; see id., at 754\xe2\x80\x93755; supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x937. Second, we have relied on Kiowa subsequently: In another case involving a tribe\xe2\x80\x99s off-reservation commercial conduct, we began our analysis with Kiowa\xe2\x80\x99s holding that tribal immunity applies to such activity (and then found that the Tribe had waived its protection). See C\xc2\xa0&\xc2\xa0L Enterprises, 532 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 418. Third, tribes across the country, as well as entities and individuals doing business with them, have for many years relied on Kiowa (along with its forebears and progeny), negotiating their contracts and structuring their transactions against a backdrop of tribal immunity. As in other cases involving contract and property rights, concerns of stare decisis are thus \xe2\x80\x9cat their acme.\xe2\x80\x9d State Oil Co. v. Khan,\n\n522 U.\xc2\xa0S. 3,\n20 (1997)\n. And fourth (a point we will later revisit, see infra, at 17\xe2\x80\x9320), Congress exercises primary authority in this area and \xe2\x80\x9cremains free to alter what we have done\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94another factor that gives \xe2\x80\x9cspecial force\xe2\x80\x9d to stare decisis. Patterson v. McLean Credit Union,\n\n491 U.\xc2\xa0S. 164\n\xe2\x80\x93173 (1989). To overcome all these reasons for this Court to stand pat, Michigan would need an ace up its sleeve.[8]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n But instead, all the State musters are retreads of assertions we have rejected before. Kiowa expressly considered the view, now offered by Michigan, that \xe2\x80\x9cwhen tribes take part in the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s commerce,\xe2\x80\x9d immunity \xe2\x80\x9cextends beyond what is needed to safeguard tribal self-governance.\xe2\x80\x9d 523 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 758. (Indeed, as Kiowa noted, see id., at 757, Potawatomi had less than a decade earlier rejected Oklahoma\xe2\x80\x99s identical contention that \xe2\x80\x9cbecause tribal business activities .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. are now so detached from traditional tribal interests,\xe2\x80\x9d immunity \xe2\x80\x9cno longer makes sense in [the commercial] context,\xe2\x80\x9d 498 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 510.) So too, the Kiowa Court comprehended the trajectory of tribes\xe2\x80\x99 commercial activity (which is the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s exclusive rationale for ignoring stare decisis, see post, at 10\xe2\x80\x9313). In the preceding decade, tribal gaming revenues had increased more than thirty fold[9] (dwarfing the still strong rate of growth since that time, see supra, at 14\xe2\x80\x9315); and Kiowa noted the flourishing of other tribal enterprises, ranging from cigarette sales to ski resorts, see 523 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 758. Moreover, the Kiowa Court understood that other sovereigns did not enjoy similar immunity for commercial activities outside their territory; that seeming \xe2\x80\x9canomal[y]\xe2\x80\x9d was a principal point in the dissenting opinion. See id., at 765 (Stevens, J., dissenting). Kiowa did more, in fact, than acknowledge those arguments; it expressed a fair bit of sympathy toward them. See id., at 758 (noting \xe2\x80\x9creasons to doubt the wisdom of perpetuating the doctrine\xe2\x80\x9d as to off-reservation commercial conduct). Yet the decision could not have been any clearer: \xe2\x80\x9cWe decline to draw [any] distinction\xe2\x80\x9d that would \xe2\x80\x9cconfine [immunity] to reservations or to noncommercial activities.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We ruled that way for a single, simple reason: because it is fundamentally Congress\xe2\x80\x99s job, not ours, to determine whether or how to limit tribal immunity. The special brand of sovereignty the tribes retain\xe2\x80\x94both its nature and its extent\xe2\x80\x94rests in the hands of Congress. See Lara, 541 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 200; Wheeler, 435 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 323. Kiowa chose to respect that congressional responsibility (as Potawatomi had a decade earlier) when it rejected the precursor to Michigan\xe2\x80\x99s argument: Whatever our view of the merits, we explained, \xe2\x80\x9cwe defer to the role Congress may wish to exercise in this important judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d 523 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 758; see Potawatomi, 498 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 510 (stating that because \xe2\x80\x9cCongress has always been at liberty to dispense with\xe2\x80\x9d or limit tribal immunity, \xe2\x80\x9cwe are not disposed to modify\xe2\x80\x9d its scope). Congress, we said\xe2\x80\x94drawing an analogy to its role in shaping foreign sovereign immunity[10]\xe2\x80\x94has the greater capacity \xe2\x80\x9cto weigh and accommodate the competing policy concerns and reliance interests\xe2\x80\x9d involved in the issue. 523 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 759. And Congress repeatedly had done just that: It had restricted tribal immunity \xe2\x80\x9cin limited circum-stances\xe2\x80\x9d (including, we noted, in \xc2\xa72710(d)(7)(A)(ii)), while \xe2\x80\x9cin other statutes\xe2\x80\x9d declaring an \xe2\x80\x9cintention not to alter\xe2\x80\x9d the doctrine. Id., at 758; see Potawatomi, 498 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 510 (citing statutory provisions involving tribal immunity). So too, we thought, Congress should make the call whether to curtail a tribe\xe2\x80\x99s immunity for off-reservation commer-cial conduct\xe2\x80\x94and the Court should accept Congress\xe2\x80\x99s judgment.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n All that we said in Kiowa applies today, with yet one more thing: Congress has now reflected on Kiowa and made an initial (though of course not irrevocable) decision to retain that form of tribal immunity. Following Kiowa, Congress considered several bills to substantially modify tribal immunity in the commercial context. Two in particular\xe2\x80\x94drafted by the chair of the Senate Appropriations Subcommittee on the Interior\xe2\x80\x94expressly referred to Kiowa and broadly abrogated tribal immunity for most torts and breaches of contract. See S. 2299, 105th Cong., 2d Sess. (1998); S. 2302, 105th Cong., 2d Sess. (1998). But instead of adopting those reversals of Kiowa, Congress chose to enact a far more modest alternative requiring tribes either to disclose or to waive their immunity in contracts needing the Secretary of the Interior\xe2\x80\x99s approval. See Indian Tribal Economic Development and Contract Encouragement Act of 2000, \xc2\xa72,\n\n114Stat.\n46 (codified at\n\n25 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa781(d)(2)); see also F. Cohen, Handbook of Federal Indian Law \xc2\xa77.05[1][b], p. 643 (2012). Since then, Congress has continued to exercise its plenary authority over tribal immunity, specifically preserving immunity in some contexts and abrogating it in others, but never adopting the change Michigan wants.[11] So rather than confronting, as we did in Kiowa, a legislative vacuum as to the precise issue presented, we act today against the backdrop of a congressional choice: to retain tribal immunity (at least for now) in a case like this one.[12]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Reversing Kiowa in these circumstances would scale the heights of presumption: Beyond upending \xe2\x80\x9clong-established principle[s] of tribal sovereign immunity,\xe2\x80\x9d that action would replace Congress\xe2\x80\x99s considered judgment with our contrary opinion. Potawatomi, 498 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 510. As Kiowa recognized, a fundamental commitment of Indian law is judicial respect for Congress\xe2\x80\x99s primary role in defining the contours of tribal sovereignty. See 523 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 758\xe2\x80\x93760; see also Santa Clara Pueblo, 436 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 60 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] proper respect .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. for the plenary authority of Congress in this area cautions that [the courts] tread lightly\xe2\x80\x9d); Cohen, supra, \xc2\xa72.01[1], at 110 (\xe2\x80\x9cJudicial deference to the paramount authority of Congress in matters concerning Indian policy remains a central and indispensable principle of the field of Indian law\xe2\x80\x9d). That commitment gains only added force when Congress has already reflected on an issue of tribal sovereignty, including immunity from suit, and declined to change settled law. And that force must grow greater still when Congress considered that issue partly at our urging. See Kiowa, 523 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 758 (hinting, none too subtly, that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress may wish to exercise\xe2\x80\x9d its authority over the question presented). Having held in Kiowa that this issue is up to Congress, we cannot reverse ourselves because some may think its conclusion wrong. Congress of course may always change its mind\xe2\x80\x94and we would readily defer to that new decision. But it is for Congress, now more than ever, to say whether to create an exception to tribal immunity for off-reservation commercial activity. As in Kiowa\xe2\x80\x94except still more so\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cwe decline to revisit our case law[,] and choose\xe2\x80\x9d instead \xe2\x80\x9cto defer to Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 760.

\n

V

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n As \xe2\x80\x9cdomestic dependent nations,\xe2\x80\x9d Indian tribes exercise sovereignty subject to the will of the Federal Government. Cherokee Nation, 5 Pet., at 17. Sovereignty implies immunity from lawsuits. Subjection means (among much else) that Congress can abrogate that immunity as and to the extent it wishes. If Congress had authorized this suit, Bay Mills would have no valid grounds to object. But Congress has not done so: The abrogation of immunity in IGRA applies to gaming on, but not off, Indian lands. We will not rewrite Congress\xe2\x80\x99s handiwork. Nor will we create a freestanding exception to tribal immunity for all off-reservation commercial conduct. This Court has declined that course once before. To choose it now would entail both overthrowing our precedent and usurping Congress\xe2\x80\x99s current policy judgment. Accordingly, Michigan may not sue Bay Mills to enjoin the Vanderbilt casino, but must instead use available alternative means to accomplish that object.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We affirm the Sixth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

MICHIGAN, PETITIONER v. BAY MILLS INDIANCOMMUNITY et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

[May 27, 2014]

\n

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Justice Sotomayor, concurring.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The doctrine of tribal immunity has been a part of American jurisprudence for well over a century. See, e.g., Parks v. Ross, 11 How. 362 (1851); Struve, Tribal Immu-nity and Tribal Courts, 36 Ariz. St. L. J. 137, 148\xe2\x80\x93155 (2004) (tracing the origins of the doctrine to the mid-19th cen-tury); Wood, It Wasn\xe2\x80\x99t An Accident: The Tribal Sovereign Immunity Story, 62 Am. U. L. Rev. 1587, 1640\xe2\x80\x931641 (2013) (same). And in more recent decades, this Court has consistently affirmed the doctrine. See, e.g., United States v. United States Fidelity & Guaranty Co.,\n\n309 U.\xc2\xa0S. 506 (1940)\n; Puyallup Tribe, Inc. v. Department of Game of Wash.,\n\n433 U.\xc2\xa0S. 165 (1977)\n; C & L Enterprises, Inc. v. Citizen Band Potawatomi Indian Tribe of Okla.,\n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 411,\n418 (2001)\n. Despite this history, the principal dissent chides the Court for failing to offer a sufficient basis for the doctrine of tribal immunity, post, at 3 (opinion of Thomas, J.), and reasons that we should at least limit the doctrine of tribal sovereign immunity in ways that resemble restrictions on foreign sovereign immunity.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The majority compellingly explains why stare decisis and deference to Congress\xe2\x80\x99 careful regulatory scheme require affirming the decision below. I write separately to further detail why both history and comity counsel against limiting Tribes\xe2\x80\x99 sovereign immunity in the manner the principal dissent advances.

\n

I

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Long before the formation of the United States, Tribes \xe2\x80\x9cwere self-governing sovereign political communities.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Wheeler,\n\n435 U.\xc2\xa0S. 313\n\xe2\x80\x93323 (1978). And Tribes \xe2\x80\x9chave not given up their full sovereignty.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 323. Absent contrary congressional acts, Tribes \xe2\x80\x9cretain their existing sovereign powers\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cpossess those aspects of sovereignty not withdrawn by treaty or statute, or by implication as a necessary result of their dependent status.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. See also\n\n25 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71301(1) (affirming Tribes\xe2\x80\x99 continued \xe2\x80\x9cpowers of self-government\xe2\x80\x9d). In this case then, the question is what type of immunity federal courts should accord to Tribes, commensurate with their retained sovereignty.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In answering this question, the principal dissent analogizes tribal sovereign immunity to foreign sovereign immunity. Foreign sovereigns (unlike States) are generally not immune from suits arising from their commercial activities. Post, at 4; see also Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act of 1976,\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71605(a)(2) (commercial-activity exception to foreign sovereign immunity). This analogy, however, lacks force. Indian Tribes have never historically been classified as \xe2\x80\x9cforeign\xe2\x80\x9d governments in federal courts even when they asked to be.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The case of Cherokee Nation v. Georgia, 5 Pet. 1 (1831), is instructive. In 1828 and 1829, the Georgia Legislature enacted a series of laws that purported to nullify acts of the Cherokee government and seize Cherokee land, among other things. Id., at 7\xe2\x80\x938. The Cherokee Nation sued Georgia in this Court, alleging that Georgia\xe2\x80\x99s laws violated federal law and treaties. Id., at 7. As the constitutional basis for jurisdiction, the Tribe relied on Article III, \xc2\xa72, cl. 1, which extends the federal judicial power to cases \xe2\x80\x9cbetween a state, or the citizens thereof, and foreign states, citizens, or subjects.\xe2\x80\x9d 5 Pet., at 15 (internal quotation marks omitted). But this Court concluded that it lacked jurisdiction because Tribes were not \xe2\x80\x9cforeign state[s].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 20. The Court reasoned that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he condition of the Indians in relation to the United States is perhaps unlike that of any other two people in existence.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 16. Tribes were more akin to \xe2\x80\x9cdomestic dependent nations,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court explained, than to foreign nations. Id., at 17. We have repeatedly relied on that characterization in subsequent cases. See, e.g., Oklahoma Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Citizen Band Potawatomi Tribe of Okla.,\n\n498 U.\xc2\xa0S. 505,\n509 (1991)\n; Merrion v. Jicarilla Apache Tribe,\n\n455 U.\xc2\xa0S. 130,\n141 (1982)\n. Two centuries of jurisprudence therefore weigh against treating Tribes like foreign visitors in American courts.

\n

II

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The principal dissent contends that whenever one sovereign is sued in the courts of another, the question whether to confer sovereign immunity is not a matter of right but rather one of \xe2\x80\x9ccomity.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 3. But in my view, the premise leads to a different conclusion than the one offered by the dissent. Principles of comity strongly counsel in favor of continued recognition of tribal sovereign immunity, including for off-reservation commercial conduct.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Comity\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cthat is, \xe2\x80\x98a proper respect for [a sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s] functions,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Sprint Communications, Inc. v. Jacobs, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2013) (slip op., at 7)\xe2\x80\x94fosters \xe2\x80\x9crespectful, harmonious relations\xe2\x80\x9d between governments, Wood v. Milyard, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 7). For two reasons, these goals are best served by recognizing sovereign immunity for Indian Tribes, including immunity for off-reservation conduct, except where Congress has expressly abrogated it. First, a legal rule that permitted States to sue Tribes, absent their consent, for commercial conduct would be anomalous in light of the existing prohibitions against Tribes\xe2\x80\x99 suing States in like circumstances. Such disparate treatment of these two classes of domestic sovereigns would hardly signal the Federal Government\xe2\x80\x99s respect for tribal sovereignty. Second, Tribes face a number of barriers to raising revenue in traditional ways. If Tribes are ever to become more self-sufficient, and fund a more substantial portion of their own governmental functions, commercial enterprises will likely be a central means of achieving that goal.

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We have held that Tribes may not sue States in federal court, Blatchford v. Native Village of Noatak,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 775 (1991)\n, including for commercial conduct that chiefly impacts Indian reservations, Seminole Tribe of Fla. v. Florida,\n\n517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 44 (1996)\n. In Seminole Tribe, the Tribe sued the State of Florida in federal court under the Indian Gaming Regulatory Act (IGRA)\xe2\x80\x94the same statute petitioner relies on here. The suit alleged that Florida had breached its statutory \xe2\x80\x9cduty to negotiate in good faith with [the Tribe] toward the formation of a [gaming] compact.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 47. This Court held that state sovereign immunity prohibited such a suit.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Importantly, the Court barred the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s suit against Florida even though the case involved the State\xe2\x80\x99s conduct in the course of commercial negotiations. As this Court later observed, relying in part on Seminole Tribe, the doctrine of state sovereign immunity is not \xe2\x80\x9cany less robust\xe2\x80\x9d when the case involves conduct \xe2\x80\x9cthat is undertaken for profit, that is traditionally performed by private citizens and corporations, and that otherwise resembles the behavior of \xe2\x80\x98market participants.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d College Savings Bank v. Florida Prepaid Postsecondary Ed. Expense Bd.,\n\n527 U.\xc2\xa0S. 666,\n684 (1999)\n. Nor did Seminole Tribe adopt a state corollary to the \xe2\x80\x9coff-reservation\xe2\x80\x9d exception to tribal sovereign immunity that the principal dissent urges today. To the contrary, the negotiations in Seminole Tribe concerned gaming on Indian lands, not state lands.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n As the principal dissent observes, \xe2\x80\x9ccomity is about one sovereign respecting the dignity of another.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 4. This Court would hardly foster respect for the dignity of Tribes by allowing States to sue Tribes for commercial activity on State lands, while prohibiting Tribes from suing States for commercial activity on Indian lands. Both States and Tribes are domestic governments who come to this Court with sovereignty that they have not entirely ceded to the Federal Government.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Similar asymmetry would result if States could sue Tribes in state courts.[1] In Nevada v. Hicks,\n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 353,\n355 (2001)\n, this Court considered whether a tribal court had \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction over civil claims against state officials who entered tribal land to execute a search warrant against a tribe member suspected of having violated state law outside the reservation.\xe2\x80\x9d It held that the tribal court did not. Id., at 374. In reaching that conclusion, the Court observed that \xe2\x80\x9c[s]tate sovereignty does not end at a reservation\xe2\x80\x99s border.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 361. And relying on similar principles, some federal courts have more explicitly held that tribal courts may not entertain suits against States. See, e.g., Montana v. Gilham, 133 F.\xc2\xa03d 1133, 1136\xe2\x80\x931137 (CA9 1998) (holding that while neither \xe2\x80\x9cthe\n \nEleventh Amendment [n]or congressional act\xe2\x80\x9d barred suits against States in tribal courts, \xe2\x80\x9cthe inherent sovereign powers of the States\xe2\x80\x9d barred such suits). To the extent Tribes are barred from suing in tribal courts, it would be anomalous to permit suits against Tribes in state courts.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Two of the dissenting opinions implicitly address this asymmetry. The principal dissent reasons that States and Tribes should be treated differently for purposes of sovereign immunity because\xe2\x80\x94unlike tribal sovereign immu-nity\xe2\x80\x94state sovereign immunity has constitutional origins. Post, at 3, n.\xc2\xa01. Justice Ginsburg offers another view: that Tribes and States should both receive less immunity. She expresses concerns about cases like Seminole Tribe, pointing to dissents that have catalogued the many problems associated with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s sprawling state sovereign immunity jurisprudence. Post, at 1\xe2\x80\x932 (citing, among others, Alden v. Maine,\n\n527 U. S. 706,\n814 (1999)\n (Souter, J., dissenting)).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n As things stand, however, Seminole Tribe and its prog-eny remain the law. And so long as that is so, comity would be ill-served by unequal treatment of States and Tribes. If Tribes cannot sue States for commercial activities on tribal lands, the converse should also be true. Any other result would fail to respect the dignity of Indian Tribes.

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The principal dissent contends that Tribes have emerged as particularly \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial and successful\xe2\x80\x9d commercial actors. Post, at 13. The dissent expresses concern that, although tribal leaders can be sued for prospective relief, ante, at 13 (majority opinion), Tribes\xe2\x80\x99 purportedly growing coffers remain unexposed to broad damages liability. Post, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311. These observations suffer from two flaws.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n First, not all Tribes are engaged in highly lucrative commercial activity. Nearly half of federally recognized Tribes in the United States do not operate gaming facilities at all. A. Meister, Casino City\xe2\x80\x99s Indian Gaming Industry Report 28 (2009\xe2\x80\x932010 ed.) (noting that \xe2\x80\x9conly 237, or 42 percent, of the 564 federally recognized Native American tribes in the U.\xc2\xa0S. operate gaming\xe2\x80\x9d).[2] And even among the Tribes that do, gaming revenue is far from uniform. As of 2009, fewer than 20% of Indian gaming facilities accounted for roughly 70% of the revenues from such facilities. Ibid. One must therefore temper any impression that Tribes across the country have suddenly and uniformly found their treasuries filled with gaming revenue.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Second, even if all Tribes were equally successful in generating commercial revenues, that would not justify the commercial-activity exception urged by the principal dissent. For tribal gaming operations cannot be understood as mere profit-making ventures that are wholly separate from the Tribes\xe2\x80\x99 core governmental functions. A key goal of the Federal Government is to render Tribes more self-sufficient, and better positioned to fund their own sovereign functions, rather than relying on federal funding.\n\n25 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72702(1) (explaining that Congress\xe2\x80\x99 purpose in enacting IGRA was \xe2\x80\x9cto provide a statutory basis for the operation of gaming by Indian tribes as a means of promoting tribal economic development, self-sufficiency, and strong tribal governments\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Cohen\xe2\x80\x99s Handbook of Federal Indian Law 1357\xe2\x80\x931373 (2012) (Cohen\xe2\x80\x99s Handbook) (describing various types of federal financial assistance that Tribes receive). And tribal business operations are critical to the goals of tribal self-sufficiency because such enterprises in some cases \xe2\x80\x9cmay be the only means by which a tribe can raise revenues,\xe2\x80\x9d Struve, 36 Ariz. St. L. J., at 169. This is due in large part to the insuperable (and often state-imposed) barriers Tribes face in raising revenue through more traditional means.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n For example, States have the power to tax certain individuals and companies based on Indian reservations, making it difficult for Tribes to raise revenue from those sources. See Oklahoma Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Citizen Band Potawatomi Tribe of Okla.,\n\n498 U.\xc2\xa0S. 505\n (allowing State to collect taxes on sales to non-Indians on Indian land); Arizona Dept. of Revenue v. Blaze Constr. Co.,\n\n526 U.\xc2\xa0S. 32 (1999)\n (allowing taxation of companies owned by non-Indians on Indian land); Thomas v. Gay,\n\n169 U. S. 264 (1898)\n (allowing taxation of property owned by non-Indians on Indian land). States may also tax reservation land that Congress has authorized individuals to hold in fee, regardless of whether it is held by Indians or non-Indians. See Cass County v. Leech Lake Band of Chip-pewa Indians,\n\n524 U.\xc2\xa0S. 103 (1998)\n (States may tax Indian reservation land if Congress made the land subject to sale under the Indian General Allotment Act of 1887 (also known as the Dawes Act)); County of Yakima v. Confederated Tribes and Bands of Yakima Nation,\n\n502 U.\xc2\xa0S. 251 (1992)\n (same).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n As commentators have observed, if Tribes were to impose their own taxes on these same sources, the resulting double taxation would discourage economic growth. Fletcher, In Pursuit of Tribal Economic Development as a Substitute for Reservation Tax Revenue, 80 N.\xc2\xa0D. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 759, 771 (2004); see also Cowan, Double Taxation in Indian Country: Unpacking the Problem and Analyzing the Role of the Federal Government in Protecting Tribal Governmental Revenues, 2 Pittsburgh Tax Rev. 93, 95 (2005); Enterprise Zones, Hearings before the Subcommittee on Select Revenue Measures of the House Committee On Ways and Means, 102d Cong., 1st Sess., 234 (1991) (statement of Peterson Zah, President of the Navajo Nation) (\xe2\x80\x9c[D]ouble taxation interferes with our ability to encourage economic activity and to develop effective reve-nue generating tax programs. Many businesses may find it easier to avoid doing business on our reservations rather than .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. bear the brunt of an added tax burden\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n If non-Indians controlled only a small amount of prop-erty on Indian reservations, and if only a negligible amount of land was held in fee, the double-taxation concern might be less severe. But for many Tribes, that is not the case. History explains why this is so: Federal policies enacted in the late 19th and early 20th centuries rendered a devastating blow to tribal ownership. In 1887, Congress enacted the Dawes Act.\n\n24Stat.\n388. That Act had two major components relevant here. First, it converted the property that belonged to Indian Tribes into fee property, and allotted the land to individual Indians. Id., at 388\xe2\x80\x93389. Much of this land passed quickly to non-Indian owners. Royster, The Legacy of Allotment, 27 Ariz. St. L. J. 1, 12 (1995). Indeed, by 1934, the amount of land that passed from Indian Tribes to non-Indians totaled 90 million acres. See Cohen\xe2\x80\x99s Handbook 74. Other property passed to non-Indians when destitute Indians found themselves unable to pay state taxes, resulting in sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s sales. Royster, supra, at 12.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n A second component of the Dawes Act opened \xe2\x80\x9csurplus\xe2\x80\x9d land on Indian reservations to settlement by non-Indians.\n\n24Stat.\n389\xe2\x80\x93390. Selling surplus lands to non-Indians was part of a more general policy of forced assimilation. See Cohen\xe2\x80\x99s Handbook 75. Sixty million acres of land passed to non-Indian hands as a result of surplus programs. Royster, supra, at 13.[3]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n These policies have left a devastating legacy, as the cases that have come before this Court demonstrate. We noted in Montana v. United States,\n\n450 U.\xc2\xa0S. 544,\n548 (1981)\n, for example, that due in large part to the Dawes Act, 28% of the Crow Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s reservation in Montana was held in fee by non-Indians. Similarly, Justice White observed in Brendale v. Confederated Tribes and Bands of Yakima Nation,\n\n492 U. S. 408,\n414 (1989)\n (plurality opinion), that 20% of the Yakima Nation\xe2\x80\x99s reservation was owned in fee. For reservations like those, it is particu-larly impactful that States and local governments may tax property held by non-Indians, Thomas, 169 U. S., at 264\xe2\x80\x93265, and land held in fee as a result of the Dawes Act. See County of Yakima, 502 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 259.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Moreover, Tribes are largely unable to obtain substantial revenue by taxing tribal members who reside on non-fee land that was not allotted under the Dawes Act. As one scholar recently observed, even if Tribes imposed high taxes on Indian residents, \xe2\x80\x9cthere is very little income, property, or sales they could tax.\xe2\x80\x9d Fletcher, supra, at 774. The poverty and unemployment rates on Indian reservations are significantly greater than the national average. See n.\xc2\xa04, infra. As a result, \xe2\x80\x9cthere is no stable tax base on most reservations.\xe2\x80\x9d Fletcher, supra, at 774; see Williams, Small Steps on the Long Road to Self-Sufficiency for In-dian Nations: The Indian Tribal Governmental Tax Status Act of 1982, 22 Harv. J. Legis. 335, 385 (1985).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n To be sure, poverty has decreased over the past few decades on reservations that have gaming activity. One recent study found that between 1990 and 2000, the presence of a tribal casino increased average per capita income by 7.4% and reduced the family poverty rate by 4.9 percentage points. Anderson, Tribal Casino Impacts on Amer-ican Indians Well-Being: Evidence From Reservation-Level Census Data, 31 Contemporary Economic Policy 291, 298 (Apr. 2013). But even reservations that have gaming continue to experience significant poverty, especially relative to the national average. See id., at 296. The same is true of Indian reservations more generally.[4]

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Both history and proper respect for tribal sovereignty\xe2\x80\x94or comity\xe2\x80\x94counsel against creating a special \xe2\x80\x9ccommercial activity\xe2\x80\x9d exception to tribal sovereign immunity. For these reasons, and for the important reasons of stare decisis and deference to Congress outlined in the majority opinion, I concur.

MICHIGAN, PETITIONER v. BAY MILLS INDIANCOMMUNITY et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

[May 27, 2014]

\n

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Justice Thomas, with whom Justice Scalia, Justice Ginsburg, and Justice Alito join, dissenting.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In Kiowa Tribe of Okla. v. Manufacturing Technologies, Inc.,\n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 751 (1998)\n, this Court extended the judge-made doctrine of tribal sovereign immunity to bar suits arising out of an Indian tribe\xe2\x80\x99s commercial activities conducted outside its territory. That was error. Such an expansion of tribal immunity is unsupported by any rationale for that doctrine, inconsistent with the limits on tribal sovereignty, and an affront to state sovereignty.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n That decision, wrong to begin with, has only worsened with the passage of time. In the 16 years since Kiowa, tribal commerce has proliferated and the inequities engendered by unwarranted tribal immunity have multiplied. Nevertheless, the Court turns down a chance to rectify its error. Still lacking a substantive justification for Kiowa\xe2\x80\x99s rule, the majority relies on notions of deference to Congress and stare decisis. Because those considerations do not support (and cannot sustain) Kiowa\xe2\x80\x99s unjustifiable rule and its mounting consequences, I respectfully dissent.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n There is no substantive basis for Kiowa\xe2\x80\x99s extension of tribal immunity to off-reservation commercial acts. As this Court explained in Kiowa, the common-law doctrine of tribal sovereign immunity arose \xe2\x80\x9calmost by accident.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 756. The case this Court typically cited as the doctrine\xe2\x80\x99s source \xe2\x80\x9csimply does not stand for that proposition,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. (citing Turner v. United States,\n\n248 U.\xc2\xa0S. 354 (1919)\n), and later cases merely \xe2\x80\x9creiterated the doctrine\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cwith little analysis,\xe2\x80\x9d 523 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 757. In fact, far from defending the doctrine of tribal sovereign immunity, the Kiowa majority \xe2\x80\x9cdoubt[ed] the wisdom of perpetuating the doctrine.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 758. The majority here suggests just one post\xc2\xa0hoc justification: that tribes automatically receive immunity as an incident to their historic sovereignty. But that explanation fails to account for the fact that immunity does not apply of its own force in the courts of another sovereign. And none of the other colorable rationales for the doctrine\xe2\x80\x94i.e., considerations of comity, and protection of tribal self-sufficiency and self-government\xe2\x80\x94supports extending immunity to suits arising out of a tribe\xe2\x80\x99s commercial activities conducted beyond its territory.

\n

1

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Despite the Indian tribes\xe2\x80\x99 subjection to the authority and protection of the United States Government, this Court has deemed them \xe2\x80\x9cdomestic dependent nations\xe2\x80\x9d that retain limited attributes of their historic sovereignty. Cherokee Nation v. Georgia, 5 Pet. 1, 17 (1831); see also United States v. Wheeler,\n\n435 U.\xc2\xa0S. 313,\n323 (1978)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cThe sovereignty that the Indian tribes retain is of a unique and limited character\xe2\x80\x9d). The majority suggests that tribal immunity is one such attribute of sovereignty that tribes have retained. See ante, at 5; Brief for Respondent Bay Mills Indian Community 48; On that view, immunity from suit applies automatically, on the theory that it is simply \xe2\x80\x9cinherent in the nature of sovereignty,\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 81, p. 548 (J. Cooke ed. 1961).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This basis for immunity\xe2\x80\x94the only substantive basis the majority invokes\xe2\x80\x94is unobjectionable when a tribe raises immunity as a defense in its own courts. We have long recognized that in the sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s own courts, \xe2\x80\x9cthe sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s power to determine the jurisdiction of its own courts and to define the substantive legal rights of its citizens adequately explains the lesser authority to define its own immunity.\xe2\x80\x9d Kiowa, supra, at 760 (Stevens, J., dissenting) (citing Kawananakoa v. Polyblank,\n\n205 U.\xc2\xa0S. 349,\n353 (1907)\n). But this notion cannot support a tribe\xe2\x80\x99s claim of immunity in the courts of another sovereign\xe2\x80\x94either a State (as in Kiowa) or the United States (as here). Sovereign immunity is not a freestanding \xe2\x80\x9cright\xe2\x80\x9d that applies of its own force when a sovereign faces suit in the courts of another. Republic of Austria v. Altmann,\n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 677,\n688 (2004)\n. Rather, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s claim to immunity in the courts of a second sovereign .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. normally depends on the second sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s law.\xe2\x80\x9d Kiowa, supra, at 760\xe2\x80\x93761 (Stevens, J., dissenting); see, e.g., Altmann, supra, at 711 (Breyer, J., concurring) (application of foreign sovereign immunity \xe2\x80\x9cis a matter, not of legal right, but of \xe2\x80\x98grace and comity\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d).[1] In short, to the extent an Indian tribe may claim immunity in federal or state court, it is because federal or state law provides it, not merely because the tribe is sovereign. Outside of tribal courts, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s inherent-immunity argument is hardly persuasive.

\n

2

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Immunity for independent foreign nations in federal courts is grounded in international \xe2\x80\x9ccomity,\xe2\x80\x9d Verlinden B.\xc2\xa0V. v. Central Bank of Nigeria,\n\n461 U.\xc2\xa0S. 480,\n486 (1983)\n, i.e., respecting the dignity of other sovereigns so as not to\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cimperil the amicable relations between governments and vex the peace of nations,\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Banco Nacional de Cuba v. Sabbatino,\n\n376 U.\xc2\xa0S. 398,\n418 (1964)\n. But whatever its relevance to tribal immunity, comity is an ill-fitting justification for extending immunity to tribes\xe2\x80\x99 off-reservation commercial activities. Even with respect to fully sovereign foreign nations, comity has long been discarded as a sufficient reason to grant immunity for commercial acts. In 1976, Congress provided that foreign states are not immune from suits based on their \xe2\x80\x9ccommercial activity\xe2\x80\x9d in the United States or abroad. Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act,\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71605(a)(2); see also Alfred Dunhill of London, Inc. v. Republic of Cuba,\n\n425 U.\xc2\xa0S. 682\n\xe2\x80\x93704 (1976) (plurality opinion of White, J., joined by Burger, C.\xc2\xa0J., and Powell and Rehnquist, JJ.) (\xe2\x80\x9cSubjecting for-eign governments to the rule of law in their commercial dealings\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cunlikely to touch very sharply on \xe2\x80\x98na-tional nerves,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n their commercial capacities, for-eign governments do not exercise powers peculiar tosovereigns\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n There is a further reason that comity cannot support tribal immunity for off-reservation commercial activities. At bottom, comity is about one sovereign respecting the dignity of another. See Nevada v. Hall,\n\n440 U.\xc2\xa0S. 410,\n416 (1979)\n. But permitting immunity for a tribe\xe2\x80\x99s off-reservation acts represents a substantial affront to a different set of sovereigns\xe2\x80\x94the States, whose sovereignty is guaranteed by the Constitution, see New York v. United States,\n\n505 U.\xc2\xa0S. 144,\n188 (1992)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Constitution .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. \xe2\x80\x98leaves to the several States a residuary and inviolable sovereignty\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting The Federalist No. 39, at 256)). When an Indian tribe engages in commercial activity outside its own territory, it necessarily acts within the territory of a sovereign State. This is why, \xe2\x80\x9c[a]bsent express federal law to the contrary, Indians going beyond reservation boundaries have generally been held subject to nondiscriminatory state law otherwise applicable to all citizens of the State.\xe2\x80\x9d Mescalero Apache Tribe v. Jones,\n\n411 U.\xc2\xa0S. 145\n\xe2\x80\x93149 (1973). A rule barring all suits against a tribe arising out of a tribe\xe2\x80\x99s conduct within state territory\xe2\x80\x94whether private actions or (as here) actions brought by the State itself\xe2\x80\x94stands in stark contrast to a State\xe2\x80\x99s broad regulatory authority over Indians within its own territory. Indeed, by foreclosing key mechanisms upon which States depend to enforce their laws against tribes engaged in off-reservation commercial activity, such a rule effects a breathtaking pre-emption of state power. Kiowa, 523 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 764 (Stevens, J., dissenting). What is worse, because that rule of immunity also applies in state courts, it strips the States of their prerogative \xe2\x80\x9cto decide for themselves whether to accord such immunity to Indian tribes as a matter of comity.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 760 (same). The States may decide whether to grant immunity in their courts to other sovereign States, see Hall, supra, at 417\xe2\x80\x93418 (a State\xe2\x80\x99s immunity from suit in the courts of a second State depends on whether the second has chosen to extend immunity to the first \xe2\x80\x9cas a matter of comity\xe2\x80\x9d), but when it comes to Indian tribes, this Court has taken that right away. Kiowa, supra, at 765 (Stevens, J., dissenting).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Nor does granting tribes immunity with respect to their commercial conduct in state territory serve the practical aim of comity: allaying friction between sovereigns. See Banco Nacional de Cuba, supra, at 417\xe2\x80\x93418. We need look no further than this case (and many others cited by petitioner and amici States) to see that such broad immunity has only aggravated relationships between States and tribes throughout the country. See infra, at 11\xe2\x80\x9313; see generally Brief for State of Alabama et\xc2\xa0al. 11\xe2\x80\x9316; Brief for State of Oklahoma 8\xe2\x80\x9310, 12\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

3

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This Court has previously suggested that recognizing tribal immunity furthers a perceived congressional goal of promoting tribal self-sufficiency and self-governance. See Kiowa, supra, at 757; Three Affiliated Tribes of Fort Berthold Reservation v. Wold Engineering, P.\xc2\xa0C.,\n\n476 U.\xc2\xa0S. 877,\n890 (1986)\n. Whatever the force of this assertion as a general matter, it is easy to reject as a basis for extending tribal immunity to off-reservation commercial activities. In Kiowa itself, this Court dismissed the self-sufficiency rationale as \xe2\x80\x9cinapposite to modern, wide-ranging tribal enterprises extending well beyond traditional tribal customs and activities.\xe2\x80\x9d 523 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 757\xe2\x80\x93758. The Court expressed concern that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n this economic context, immunity can harm those who are unaware that they are dealing with a tribe, who do not know of tribal immunity, or who have no choice in the matter, as in the case of tort victims.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 758.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Nor is immunity for off-reservation commercial acts necessary to protect tribal self-governance. As the Kiowa majority conceded, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n our interdependent and mobile society, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. tribal immunity extends beyond what is needed to safeguard tribal self-governance.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Such broad immunity far exceeds the modest scope of tribal sover-eignty, which is limited only to \xe2\x80\x9cwhat is necessary to pro-tect tribal self-government or to control internal relations.\xe2\x80\x9d Montana v. United States,\n\n450 U.\xc2\xa0S. 544,\n564 (1981)\n; see also Nevada v. Hicks,\n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 353,\n392 (2001)\n (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]ribes retain sovereign interests in activities that occur on land owned and controlled by the tribe .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d). And no party has suggested that immunity from the isolated suits that may arise out of extraterritorial commercial dealings is somehow fundamental to protecting tribal government or regulating a tribe\xe2\x80\x99s internal affairs.

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Despite acknowledging that there is scant substantive justification for extending tribal immunity to off-reservation commercial acts, this Court did just that in Kiowa. See 523 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 758. The Kiowa majority admitted that the Court\xe2\x80\x94rather than Congress\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9chas taken the lead in drawing the bounds of tribal immunity.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 759. Nevertheless, the Court adopted a rule of expansive immunity purportedly to \xe2\x80\x9cdefer to the role Congress may wish to exercise in this important judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 758.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This asserted \xe2\x80\x9cdeference\xe2\x80\x9d to Congress was a fiction and remains an enigma, however, because the Kiowa Court did not actually leave to Congress the decision whether to extend tribal immunity. Tribal immunity is a common-law doctrine adopted and shaped by this Court. Okla-homa Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Citizen Band Potawatomi Tribe of Okla.,\n\n498 U.\xc2\xa0S. 505,\n510 (1991)\n; Kiowa, 523 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 759. Before Kiowa, we had never held that tribal sovereign immunity applied to off-reservation commercial activities.[2] Thus, faced with an unresolved question about a common-law doctrine of its own design, the Kiowa Court had to make a choice: tailor the immunity to the realities of their commercial enterprises, or \xe2\x80\x9cgrant .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0virtually unlimited tribal immunity.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 764 (Stevens, J., dissenting). The Court took the latter course. In doing so, it did not \xe2\x80\x9cdefe[r] to Congress or exercis[e] \xe2\x80\x98caution,\xe2\x80\x99\xe2\x80\x94rather, it .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. creat[ed] law.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 765 (citation omitted). To be sure, Congress had the power to \xe2\x80\x9calter\xe2\x80\x9d that decision if it wanted. Id., at 759 (majority opinion). But Congress has the authority to do that with respect to any nonconstitutional decision involving federal law, and the mere existence of this authority could not be the basis for choosing one outcome over another in Kiowa.[3]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Accident or no, it was this Court, not Congress, that adopted the doctrine of tribal sovereign immunity in the first instance. And it was this Court that left open a question about its scope. Why should Congress\xe2\x80\x94and only Congress, according to the Kiowa Court\xe2\x80\x94have to take on a problem this Court created? In other areas of federal common law, until Congress intervenes, it is up to us to correct our errors. See, e.g., Exxon Shipping Co. v. Baker,\n\n554 U.\xc2\xa0S. 471,\n507 (2008)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]f, in the absence of legislation, judicially derived standards leave the door open to outlier punitive-damages awards [in maritime law], it is hard to see how the judiciary can wash its hands of a problem it created, simply by calling quantified standards legislative\xe2\x80\x9d); National Metropolitan Bank v. United States,\n\n323 U.\xc2\xa0S. 454,\n456 (1945)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]n the absence of an applicable Act of Congress, federal courts must fashion the governing rules\xe2\x80\x9d in commercial-paper cases affecting the rights and liabilities of the United States). We have the same duty here.

\n

II

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Today, the Court reaffirms Kiowa. Unsurprisingly, it offers no new substantive defense for Kiowa\xe2\x80\x99s indefensible view of tribal immunity. Instead, the majority relies on a combination of the Kiowa Court\xe2\x80\x99s purported deference to Congress and considerations of stare decisis. I have already explained why it was error to ground the Kiowa rule in deference to Congress. I turn now to stare decisis. Contrary to the majority\xe2\x80\x99s claim, that policy does not require us to preserve this Court\xe2\x80\x99s mistake in Kiowa. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s failure to justify Kiowa\xe2\x80\x99s rule and the decision\xe2\x80\x99s untoward consequences outweigh the majority\xe2\x80\x99s arguments for perpetuating the error.

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Stare decisis may sometimes be \xe2\x80\x9cthe preferred course,\xe2\x80\x9d but as this Court acknowledges, it is \xe2\x80\x9cnot an inexorable command.\xe2\x80\x9d Payne v. Tennessee,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 808,\n827,\n828 (1991)\n. \xe2\x80\x9c[W]hen governing decisions are unworkable or are badly reasoned,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 827, or \xe2\x80\x9cexperience has pointed up the precedent\xe2\x80\x99s shortcomings,\xe2\x80\x9d Pearson v. Callahan,\n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 223,\n233 (2009)\n, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98this Court has never felt constrained to follow precedent,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Payne, supra, at 827. See also Gulfstream Aerospace Corp. v. Mayacamas Corp.,\n\n485 U. S. 271\n\xe2\x80\x93283 (1988) (overruling precedent as \xe2\x80\x9cdeficient in utility and sense,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cunsound in theory, unworkable and arbitrary in practice, and unnecessary to achieve any legitimate goals\xe2\x80\x9d). The discussion above explains why Kiowa was unpersuasive on its own terms. Now, the adverse consequences of that decision make it even more untenable.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In the 16 years since Kiowa, the commercial activities of tribes have increased dramatically. This is especially evident within the tribal gambling industry. Combined tribal gaming revenues in 28 States have more than tripled\xe2\x80\x94from $8.5 billion in 1998 to $27.9 billion in 2012. National Indian Gaming Commission, 2012 Indian Gaming Revenues Increase 2.7 Percent (July 23, 2013), online at http://www.nigc.gov\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0LinkClick.aspx?fileticket\xc2\xa0=\xc2\xa0Fhd5shyZ1fM%3D(all Internet materials as visited May 2, 2014, and available in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file). But tribal businesses extend well beyond gambling and far past reservation borders. In addition to ventures that take advantage of on-reservation resources (like tourism, recreation, mining, forestry, and agriculture), tribes engage in \xe2\x80\x9cdomestic and international business ventures\xe2\x80\x9d including manufacturing, retail, banking, construction, energy, telecommunications, and more. Graham, An Interdisciplinary Approach to American Indian Economic Development, 80 N.\xc2\xa0D. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 597, 600\xe2\x80\x93604 (2004). Tribal enterprises run the gamut: they sell cigarettes and prescription drugs online; engage in foreign financing; and operate greeting cards companies, national banks, cement plants, ski resorts, and hotels. Ibid.; see also, e.g., The Harvard Project on American Indian Economic Development, The State of the Native Nations 124 (2008) (Ho-Chunk, Inc., a tribal corporationof the Winnebago Tribe of Nebraska, operates \xe2\x80\x9chotelsin Nebraska and Iowa,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cnumerous retail groceryand convenience stores,\xe2\x80\x9d a \xe2\x80\x9ctobacco and gasoline distribution company,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ca temporary labor service provider\xe2\x80\x9d); Four Fires, San Manuel Band of Mission Indians, http://www.sanmanuel-nsn.gov/fourfires.php.html) (four Tribes from California and Wisconsin jointly own and operate a $43 million hotel in Washington, D.\xc2\xa0C.). These manifold com-mercial enterprises look the same as any other\xe2\x80\x94except immunity renders the tribes largely litigation-proof.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n As the commercial activity of tribes has proliferated, the conflict and inequities brought on by blanket tribal immunity have also increased. Tribal immunity significantly limits, and often extinguishes, the States\xe2\x80\x99 ability to protect their citizens and enforce the law against tribal busi-nesses. This case is but one example: No one can seriously dispute that Bay Mills\xe2\x80\x99 operation of a casino outside its reservation (and thus within Michigan territory) would violate both state law and the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s compact with Michigan. Yet, immunity poses a substantial impediment to Michigan\xe2\x80\x99s efforts to halt the casino\xe2\x80\x99s operation permanently. The problem repeats itself every time a tribe fails to pay state taxes, harms a tort victim, breaches a contract, or otherwise violates state laws, and tribal immu-nity bars the only feasible legal remedy. Given the wide reach of tribal immunity, such scenarios are commonplace.[4] See, e.g., Oneida Indian Nation of New York v. Madison Cty., 605 F.\xc2\xa03d 149, 163 (CA2 2010) (Cabranes, J., joined by Hall, J., concurring) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe holding in this case comes down to this: an Indian tribe can purchase land (including land that was never part of a reservation); refuse to pay lawfully-owed taxes; and suffer no consequences because the taxing authority cannot sue to collect the taxes owed\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Furry v. Miccosukee Tribe of Indians of Fla., 685 F.\xc2\xa03d 1224 (CA11 2012) (Tribe immune from a suit arising out of a fatal off-reservation car crash that alleged negligence and violation of state dram shop laws); Native American Distributing v. Seneca-Cayuga Tobacco Co., 546 F.\xc2\xa03d 1288 (CA10 2008) (tribal officials and a tobacco-products manufacturer were immune from a suit brought by a national distributor alleging breach of contract and interstate market manipulation); Tonasket v. Sargent, 830 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1078 (ED Wash. 2011) (tribal immunity foreclosed an action against the Tribe for illegal price fixing, antitrust violations, and unfair competition), aff\xe2\x80\x99d, 510 Fed. Appx. 648 (CA9 2013); Multimedia Games, Inc. v. WLGC Acquisition Corp., 214 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1131 (ND Okla. 2001) (tribal immunity barred a suit alleging copyright infringement, unfair competition, breach of contract, and other claims against a tribal business development agency).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In the wake of Kiowa, tribal immunity has also been exploited in new areas that are often heavily regulated by States. For instance, payday lenders (companies that lend consumers short-term advances on paychecks at interest rates that can reach upwards of 1,000 percent per annum) often arrange to share fees or profits with tribes so they can use tribal immunity as a shield for conduct of questionable legality. Martin & Schwartz, The Alliance Between Payday Lenders and Tribes: Are Both Tribal Sovereignty and Consumer Protection at Risk? 69 Wash. & Lee L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 751, 758\xe2\x80\x93759, 777 (2012). Indian tribes have also created conflict in certain States by asserting tribal immunity as a defense against violations of state campaign finance laws. See generally Moylan, Sovereign Rules of the Game: Requiring Campaign Finance Disclosure in the Face of Tribal Sovereign Immunity, 20 B.\xc2\xa0U. Pub. Interest L.\xc2\xa0J. 1 (2010).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In sum, any number of Indian tribes across the country have emerged as substantial and successful competitors in interstate and international commerce, both within and beyond Indian lands. As long as tribal immunity remains out of sync with this reality, it will continue to invite problems, including de\xc2\xa0facto deregulation of highly regulated activities; unfairness to tort victims; and increas-ingly fractious relations with States and individuals alike. The growing harms wrought by Kiowa\xe2\x80\x99s unjustifiable rule fully justify overruling it.

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In support of its adherence to stare decisis, the majority asserts that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress has now reflected on Kiowa\xe2\x80\x9d and has decided to \xe2\x80\x9cretain\xe2\x80\x9d the decision. Ante, at 18; see also ante, at 19 (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e act today against the backdrop of an apparent congressional choice: to keep tribal immunity .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in a case like this one\xe2\x80\x9d). On its face, however, this is a curious assertion. To this day, Congress has never granted tribal sovereign immunity in any shape or form\xe2\x80\x94much less immunity that extends as far as Kiowa went. What the majority really means, I gather, is that the Court must stay its hand because Congress has implicitly approved of Kiowa\xe2\x80\x99s rule by not overturning it.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This argument from legislative inaction is unavailing. As a practical matter, it is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98impossible to assert with any degree of assurance that congressional failure to act represents\xe2\x80\x99 affirmative congressional approval of\xe2\x80\x9d one of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions. Patterson v. McLean Credit Union,\n\n491 U.\xc2\xa0S. 164\n, n.\xc2\xa01 (1989) (quoting Johnson v. Transportation Agency, Santa Clara Cty.,\n\n480 U.\xc2\xa0S. 616,\n672 (1987)\n (Scalia, J., dissenting)); see also Girouard v. United States,\n\n328 U.\xc2\xa0S. 61,\n69 (1946)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is at best treacherous to find in congressional silence alone the adoption of a controlling rule of law\xe2\x80\x9d); Helvering v. Hallock,\n\n309 U.\xc2\xa0S. 106\n, 121 (1940) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e walk on quicksand when we try to find in the absence of corrective legislation a controlling legal principle\xe2\x80\x9d). There are many reasons Congress might not act on a decision like Kiowa, and most of them have nothing at all to do with Congress\xe2\x80\x99 desire to preserve the decision. See Johnson, 480 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 672 (Scalia, J., dissenting) (listing various kinds of legislative inertia, including an \xe2\x80\x9cinability to agree upon how to alter the status quo\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cindifference to the status quo\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Even assuming the general validity of arguments from legislative inaction, they are a poor fit in this common-law context. Such arguments are typically based on the premise that the failure of later Congresses to reject a judicial decision interpreting a statute says something about what Congress understands the statute to mean. See, e.g., id., at 629, n.\xc2\xa07 (majority opinion). But it is not clear why Congress\xe2\x80\x99 unenacted \xe2\x80\x9copinion\xe2\x80\x9d has any relevance to determining the correctness of a decision about a doctrine created and shaped by this Court. Giving dispositive weight to congressional silence regarding a common-law decision of this Court effectively codifies that decision based only on Congress\xe2\x80\x99 failure to address it. This approach is at odds with our Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s requirements for enacting law. Cf. Patterson, supra, at 175, n.\xc2\xa01 (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress may legislate .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. only through the passage of a bill which is approved by both Houses and signed by the President. Congressional inaction cannot amend a duly enacted statute\xe2\x80\x9d (citation omitted)). It is also the direct opposite of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s usual approach in common-law cases, where we have made clear that, \xe2\x80\x9cin the absence of an applicable Act of Congress, federal courts must fashion the governing rules.\xe2\x80\x9d National Metropolitan Bank, 323 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 456; see also supra, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312; Moragne v. States Marine Lines, Inc.,\n\n398 U.\xc2\xa0S. 375,\n378 (1970)\n (precedent barring recovery for wrongful death, \xe2\x80\x9csomewhat dubious even when rendered, is such an unjustifiable anomaly in the present maritime [common] law that it should no longer be followed\xe2\x80\x9d).[5] Allowing legislative inaction to guide common-law decisionmaking is not deference, but abdication.[6]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In any event, because legislative inaction is usually indeterminate, we \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98require very persuasive circumstances enveloping Congressional silence to debar this Court from reexamining its own doctrines.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Girouard, supra, at 69. Here, the majority provides nothing that solidifies the inference of approval it draws from congressional silence in the wake of Kiowa.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n First, the majority cites two Senate bills that proposed to abrogate tribal immunity for contract and tort claims against tribes. See S. 2299, 105th Cong., 2d Sess. (1998) (contract claims); S. 2302, 105th Cong., 2d Sess. (1998) (tort claims). Neither bill expresses Congress\xe2\x80\x99 views on Kiowa\xe2\x80\x99s rule, for both died in committee without a vote.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Second, the majority notes various post-Kiowa enactments that either abrogate tribal immunity in various limited contexts or leave it be. See ante, at 18, 19, n.\xc2\xa010. None of these enactments provides a reason to believe that Congress both considered and approved Kiowa\xe2\x80\x99s holding. None of them targets with any precision the immunity of Indian tribes for off-reservation commercial activities. See, e.g., Indian Tribal Economic Development and Contract Encouragement Act of 2000 (codified at\n\n25 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa781(d)(2)) (for contracts that encumber Indian lands for more than seven years, tribes must either provide for breach-of-contract remedies or disclose tribal immunity if applicable). And given the exceedingly narrow contexts in which these provisions apply, see, e.g., Arizona Water Settlements Act, \xc2\xa7213(a)(2),\n\n118Stat.\n3531 (abrogating one tribe\xe2\x80\x99s immunity for the limited purpose of enforcing water settlements), the far stronger inference is that Congress simply did not address Kiowa or its extension of immunity in these Acts; rather, Congress considered only whether an abrogation of judge-made tribal immunity was necessary to the narrow regulatory scheme on the table. See, e.g., Prevent All Cigarette Trafficking Act of 2009, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72(e), 3(a),\n\n124Stat.\n1101,\n1108.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The majority posits that its inference of congressional approval of Kiowa is stronger because Congress failed to act after the Kiowa Court \xe2\x80\x9curg[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d Congress to consider the question presented. Ante, at 17, 19\xe2\x80\x9320 (quoting Kiowa, 523 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 758) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e defer to the role Congress may wish to exercise in this important judgment\xe2\x80\x9d). But this circumstance too raises any number of inferences. Congress is under no obligation to review and respond to every statement this Court makes; perhaps legislative inertia simply won out. The majority seems to suggest that Congress understood Kiowa to assign the burgeoning problems of expansive common-law immunity to the Legislature, and then chose to let those problems fester. But Congress has not explained its inaction, and we should not pretend that it has done so by remaining silent after we supposedly prodded it to say something. Even if we credit the relevance of post-Kiowa congressional silence in this common-law context\xe2\x80\x94and I do not\xe2\x80\x94there is certainly not enough evidence of congressional acquiescence here \xe2\x80\x9cthat we can properly place on the shoulders of Congress the burden of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s own error.\xe2\x80\x9d Girouard, 328 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 69\xe2\x80\x9370.

\n

C

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The majority\xe2\x80\x99s remaining arguments for retaining Kiowa are also unconvincing.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n First, the majority characterizes Kiowa as one case in a \xe2\x80\x9clong line of precedents\xe2\x80\x9d in which the Court has recognized tribal immunity \xe2\x80\x9cwithout any exceptions for commercial or off-reservation conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 15. True, the Court has relied on tribal immunity as a general matter in several cases. But not until Kiowa were we required to decide whether immunity should extend to commercial activities beyond Indian reservations. See supra, at 7. And after Kiowa, we have mentioned it only once, and then only in dicta. C & L Enterprises, Inc. v. Citizen Band Potawatomi Tribe of Okla.,\n\n532 U. S. 411,\n418 (2001)\n (holding that the Tribe had waived its immunity in a construction contract). Thus, overturning Kiowa would overturn Kiowa only.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Second, the majority suggests that tribes and their business partners have now relied on Kiowa in structuring their contracts and transactions. Ante, at 15. But even when Kiowa extended the scope of tribal immunity, it was readily apparent that the Court had strong misgivings about it. Not one Member of the Kiowa Court identified a substantive justification for its extension of immunity: Three would not have expanded the immunity in the first place, Kiowa, 523 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 760 (Stevens, J., dissenting), and the other six essentially expressed hope that Congress would overrule the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision, see id., at 758\xe2\x80\x93759. Against that backdrop, it would hardly be reasonable for a tribe to rely on Kiowa as a permanent grant of immunity for off-reservation commercial activities. In any event, the utter absence of a reasoned justification for Kiowa\xe2\x80\x99s rule and its growing adverse effects easily outweigh this generalized assertion of reliance. See, e.g., Leegin Creative Leather Products, Inc. v. PSKS, Inc.,\n\n551 U.\xc2\xa0S. 877,\n906 (2007)\n (in the antitrust context, overturning the per\xc2\xa0se rule against vertical price restraints in part because the \xe2\x80\x9creliance interests\xe2\x80\x9d in the case could not \xe2\x80\x9cjustify an inefficient rule\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In Kiowa, this Court adopted a rule without a reason: a sweeping immunity from suit untethered from commercial realities and the usual justifications for immunity, premised on the misguided notion that only Congress can place sensible limits on a doctrine we created. The decision was mistaken then, and the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to reaffirm it in the face of the unfairness and conflict it has engendered is doubly so. I respectfully dissent.
