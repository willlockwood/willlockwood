Syllabus

COVENTRY HEALTH CARE OF MISSOURI, INC., fka GROUP HEALTH PLAN, INC. v. NEVILS

\n

certiorari to the supreme court of missouri

\n

No. 16\xe2\x80\x93149.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 1, 2017\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 18, 2017

\n

The Federal Employees Health Benefits Act of 1959 (FEHBA) authorizes the Office of Personnel Management (OPM) to contract with private carriers for federal employees\xe2\x80\x99 health insurance.\n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa78902(a), (d). FEHBA contains an express-preemption provision, \xc2\xa78902(m)(1), which states that the \xe2\x80\x9cterms of any contract under this chapter which relate to the nature, provision, or extent of coverage or benefits (including payments with respect to benefits) shall supersede and preempt any State or local law .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. which relates to health insurance or plans.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

OPM\xe2\x80\x99s contracts have long required private carriers to seek subrogation and reimbursement. Accordingly, OPM\xe2\x80\x99s regulations make a carrier\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cright to pursue and receive subrogation and reimbursement recoveries .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a condition of and a limitation on the nature of benefits or benefit payments and on the provision of benefits under the plan\xe2\x80\x99s coverage.\xe2\x80\x9d 5 CFR \xc2\xa7890.106(b)(1). In 2015, OPM published a new rule confirming that a carrier\xe2\x80\x99s subrogation and reimbursement rights and responsibilities \xe2\x80\x9crelate to the nature, provision, and extent of coverage or benefits (including payments with respect to benefits) within the meaning of\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa78902(m)(1), and \xe2\x80\x9care\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. effective notwithstanding any state or local law, or any regulation issued thereunder, which relates to health insurance or plans.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7890.106(h).

\n

Respondent Jodie Nevils was insured under a FEHBA plan offered by petitioner Coventry Health Care of Missouri. When Nevils was injured in an automobile accident, Coventry paid his medical expenses. Coventry subsequently asserted a lien against part of the settlement Nevils recovered from the driver who caused his injuries. Nevils satisfied the lien, then filed a class action in Missouri state court, alleging that, under Missouri law, which does not permit subrogation or reimbursement in this context, Coventry had unlawfully obtained reimbursement. Coventry countered that \xc2\xa78902(m)(1) preempted the state law. The trial court granted summary judgment in Coventry\xe2\x80\x99s favor, and the Missouri Court of Appeals affirmed. The Missouri Supreme Court reversed. Finding \xc2\xa78902(m)(1) susceptible to diverse plausible readings, the court invoked a \xe2\x80\x9cpresumption against preemption\xe2\x80\x9d to conclude that the federal statute\xe2\x80\x99s preemptive scope excluded subrogation and reimbursement. On remand from this Court for further consideration in light of OPM\xe2\x80\x99s 2015 rule, the Missouri Supreme Court adhered to its earlier decision. A majority of the Missouri Supreme Court also held that \xc2\xa78902(m)(1) violates the Supremacy Clause.

\n

Held:

\n

1.\xc2\xa0Because contractual subrogation and reimbursement prescriptions plainly \xe2\x80\x9crelate to .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. payments with respect to benefits,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa78902(m)(1), they override state laws barring subrogation and reimbursement. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0This reading best comports with \xc2\xa78902(m)(1)\xe2\x80\x99s text, context, and purpose. Contractual provisions for subrogation and reimbursement \xe2\x80\x9crelate to .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. payments with respect to benefits\xe2\x80\x9d because subrogation and reimbursement rights yield just such payments. When a carrier exercises its right to either reimbursement or subrogation, it receives from either the beneficiary or a third party \xe2\x80\x9cpayment\xe2\x80\x9d respecting the benefits the carrier had previously paid. The carrier\xe2\x80\x99s very provision of benefits triggers the right to payment. Congress\xe2\x80\x99 use of the expansive phrase \xe2\x80\x9crelate to,\xe2\x80\x9d which \xe2\x80\x9cexpress[es] a broad pre-emptive purpose,\xe2\x80\x9d Morales v. Trans World Airlines, Inc.,\n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 374\n, weighs against Nevils\xe2\x80\x99 effort to narrow the term \xe2\x80\x9cpayments\xe2\x80\x9d to exclude payments that occur \xe2\x80\x9clong after\xe2\x80\x9d a carrier\xe2\x80\x99s provision of benefits. Nevils\xe2\x80\x99 argument that Congress intended to preempt only state coverage requirements, e.g., inclusion of acupuncture and chiropractic services, also miscarries.

\n

The statutory context and purpose reinforce this conclusion. FEHBA concerns \xe2\x80\x9cbenefits from a federal health insurance plan for federal employees that arise from a federal law.\xe2\x80\x9d Bell v. Blue Cross & Blue Shield of Okla., 823 F.\xc2\xa03d 1198, 1202. Strong and \xe2\x80\x9cdistinctly federal interests are involved,\xe2\x80\x9d Empire HealthChoice Assurance, Inc. v. McVeigh,\n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 677\n, in uniform administration of the program, free from state interference, particularly in regard to coverage, benefits, and payments. The Federal Government also has a significant financial stake in subrogation and reimbursement. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0 McVeigh\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion that \xc2\xa78902(m)(1) has two \xe2\x80\x9cplausible\xe2\x80\x9d interpretations, 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 698, Nevils asserts, supports application of the presumption against preemption here. But the Court never chose between the two readings set out in McVeigh, because doing so was not pertinent to the discrete question whether federal courts have subject-matter jurisdiction over FEHBA reimbursement actions. Having decided in McVeigh that \xc2\xa78902(m)(1) is a \xe2\x80\x9cchoice-of-law prescription,\xe2\x80\x9d not a \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction-conferring provision,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 697, the Court had no cause to consider \xc2\xa78902(m)(1)\xe2\x80\x99s text, context, and purpose, as it does here. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

2.\xc2\xa0The regime Congress enacted is compatible with the Supremacy Clause. The statute itself, not a contract, strips state law of its force. FEHBA contract terms have preemptive force only if they fall within \xc2\xa78902(m)(1)\xe2\x80\x99s preemptive scope. Many other federal statutes found to preempt state law, including the Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974 and the Federal Arbitration Act, leave the context-specific scope of preemption to contractual terms. While \xc2\xa78902(m)(1)\xe2\x80\x99s phrasing may differ from those other statutes\xe2\x80\x99, FEHBA\xe2\x80\x99s express-preemption provision manifests the same intent to preempt state law. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

492 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d 918, reversed and remanded.

\n

Ginsburg, J., delivered the opinion of the Court, in which all other Members joined, except Gorsuch, J., who took no part in the consideration or decision of the case. Thomas, J., filed a concurring opinion.

COVENTRY HEALTH CARE OF MISSOURI, INC., fka GROUP HEALTH PLAN, INC., PETITIONER v. JODIE NEVILS

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of missouri

\n

[April 18, 2017]

\n

\n

Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

In the Federal Employees Health Benefits Act of 1959 (FEHBA),\n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa78901 et\xc2\xa0seq., Congress authorized the Office of Personnel Management (OPM) to contract with private carriers for federal employees\xe2\x80\x99 health insurance. \xc2\xa78902(a), (d). FEHBA contains a provision expressly preempting state law. \xc2\xa78902(m)(1). That provision reads:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe terms of any contract under this chapter which relate to the nature, provision, or extent of coverage or benefits (including payments with respect to benefits) shall supersede and preempt any State or local law, or any regulation issued thereunder, which relates to health insurance or plans.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Contracts OPM negotiates with private carriers provide for reimbursement and subrogation. Reimbursement requires an insured employee who receives payment from another source (e.g., the proceeds yielded by a tort claim) to return healthcare costs earlier paid out by the carrier. Subrogation involves transfer of the right to a third-party payment from the insured employee to the carrier, who can then pursue the claim against the third party. Several States, however, Missouri among them, bar enforcement of contractual subrogation and reimbursement provisions.

\n

The questions here presented: Does FEHBA\xe2\x80\x99s express-preemption prescription, \xc2\xa78902(m)(1), override state law prohibiting subrogation and reimbursement; and if \xc2\xa78902(m)(1) has that effect, is the statutory prescription consistent with the Supremacy Clause, U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Art. VI, cl. 2? We hold, contrary to the decision of the Missouri Supreme Court, that contractual subrogation and reimbursement prescriptions plainly \xe2\x80\x9crelate to .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. payments with respect to benefits,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa78902(m)(1); therefore, by statutory instruction, they override state law barring subrogation and reimbursement. We further hold, again contrary to the Missouri Supreme Court, that the regime Congress enacted is compatible with the Supremacy Clause. Section 8902(m)(1) itself, not the contracts OPM negotiates, triggers the federal preemption. As Congress directed, where FEHBA contract terms \xe2\x80\x9crelate to the nature, provision, or extent of coverage or benefits (including payments with respect to benefits),\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa78902(m)(1) ensures that those terms will be uniformly enforceable nationwide, free from state interference.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

FEHBA \xe2\x80\x9cestablishes a comprehensive program of health insurance for federal employees.\xe2\x80\x9d Empire HealthChoice Assurance, Inc. v. McVeigh,\n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 677,\n682 (2006)\n. As just noted, supra, at 1, FEHBA contains an express-preemption provision, \xc2\xa78902(m)(1). FEHBA assigns to OPM broad administrative and rulemaking authority over the program. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa78901\xe2\x80\x938913. OPM contracts with private insurance carriers to offer a range of healthcare plans. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa78902, 8903.

\n

OPM\xe2\x80\x99s contracts with private carriers have long included provisions requiring those carriers to seek subrogation and reimbursement. Accordingly, OPM has issued detailed regulations governing subrogation and reimbursement clauses in FEHBA contracts. See 5 CFR \xc2\xa7890.106 (2016). Under those regulations, a carrier\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cright to pursue and receive subrogation and reimbursement recoveries constitutes a condition of and a limitation on the nature of benefits or benefit payments and on the provision of benefits under the plan\xe2\x80\x99s coverage.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7890.106(b)(1).

\n

In 2015, after notice and comment, OPM published a rule confirming that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] carrier\xe2\x80\x99s rights and responsibilities pertaining to subrogation and reimbursement under any [FEHBA] contract relate to the nature, provision, and extent of coverage or benefits (including payments with respect to benefits) within the meaning of\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa78902(m)(1). \xc2\xa7890.106(h). Such \xe2\x80\x9crights and responsibilities,\xe2\x80\x9d OPM\xe2\x80\x99s rule provides, \xe2\x80\x9care .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. effective notwithstanding any state or local law, or any regulation issued thereunder, which relates to health insurance or plans.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Its rule, OPM explained, \xe2\x80\x9ccomports with longstanding Federal policy and furthers Congres[s\xe2\x80\x99] goals of reducing health care costs and enabling uniform, nationwide application of [FEHBA] contracts.\xe2\x80\x9d 80 Fed. Reg. 29203 (2015) (final rule).

\n

B

\n

Respondent Jodie Nevils is a former federal employee who enrolled in and was insured under a FEHBA plan offered by petitioner Coventry Health Care of Missouri.[1] Nevils v. Group Health Plan, Inc., 418 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d 451, 453 (Mo. 2014) (Nevils I\xc2\xa0). When Nevils was injured in an automobile accident, Coventry paid his medical expenses. Ibid. Nevils sued the driver who caused his injuries and recovered a settlement award. Ibid. Based on its contract with OPM, see App. to Pet. for Cert. 129a\xe2\x80\x93130a, Coventry asserted a lien for $6,592.24 against part of the settlement proceeds to cover medical bills it had paid. Nevils I, 418 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 453. Nevils repaid that amount, thereby satisfying the lien. Ibid.

\n

Nevils then filed this class action against Coventry in Missouri state court, alleging that Coventry had unlawfully obtained reimbursement. Ibid. Nevils premised his claim on Missouri law, which does not permit subrogation or reimbursement in this context, see, e.g., Benton House, LLC v. Cook & Younts Ins., Inc., 249 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d 878, 881\xe2\x80\x93882 (Mo. App. 2008). Coventry countered that \xc2\xa78902(m)(1) makes subrogation and reimbursement clauses in FEHBA contracts enforceable notwithstanding state law. The trial court granted summary judgment in Coventry\xe2\x80\x99s favor, Nevils v. Group Health Plan, Inc., No. 11SL\xe2\x80\x93CC00535 (Cir. Ct., St.\xc2\xa0Louis Cty., Mo., May 21, 2012), App. to Pet. for Cert. 28a, 32a, and the Missouri Court of Appeals affirmed, Nevils v. Group Health Plan, Inc., 2012 WL 6689542, *5 (Dec. 26, 2012).

\n

The Missouri Supreme Court reversed. Nevils I, 418 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 457. That court began with \xe2\x80\x9cthe assumption that the historic police powers of the States [are] not to be superseded by .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. Federal Act unless that [is] the clear and manifest purpose of Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 454 (quoting Cipollone v. Liggett Group, Inc.,\n\n505 U.\xc2\xa0S. 504,\n516 (1992)\n) (alterations in original). Finding \xc2\xa78902(m)(1) susceptible to diverse \xe2\x80\x9cplausible readings,\xe2\x80\x9d the court invoked a \xe2\x80\x9cpresumption against preemption\xe2\x80\x9d to conclude that the federal statute\xe2\x80\x99s preemptive scope excluded subrogation and reimbursement. 418 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 455.

\n

Judge Wilson, joined by Judge Breckenridge, concurred in the judgment. Id., at 457. Observing that \xe2\x80\x9cit defies logic to insist that benefit repayment terms do not relate to the nature or extent of Nevils\xe2\x80\x99 benefits,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 460 (emphasis deleted), Judge Wilson concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress plainly intended for \xc2\xa78902(m)(1) to apply to the benefit repayment terms in [Coventry\xe2\x80\x99s] contract,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 462. He nevertheless concurred, reasoning that the Supremacy Clause did not authorize preemption based on the terms of FEHBA contracts. Id., at 462\xe2\x80\x93465.

\n

Coventry sought our review, and we invited the Solicitor General to file a brief expressing the views of the United States. Coventry Health Care of Mo., Inc. v. Nevils, 574 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014). While Coventry\xe2\x80\x99s petition was pending, OPM finalized its rule governing subrogation and reimbursement. See supra, at 3. This Court granted certiorari, vacated the Missouri Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment, and remanded for further consideration in light of OPM\xe2\x80\x99s recently adopted rule. Coventry Health Care of Mo., Inc. v. Nevils, 576 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015).

\n

On remand, the Missouri Supreme Court adhered to its earlier decision. Nevils v. Group Health Plan, Inc., 492 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d 918, 920, 925 (2016). OPM\xe2\x80\x99s rule, the court maintained, \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not overcome the presumption against preemption and demonstrate Congress\xe2\x80\x99 clear and manifest intent to preempt state law.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 920.

\n

Judge Wilson again concurred, this time joined by a majority of the judges of the Missouri Supreme Court. Id., at 925.[2] In their view, Congress\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cattempt to give preemptive effect to the provisions of a contract between the federal government and a private party is not a valid application of the Supremacy Clause\xe2\x80\x9d and, \xe2\x80\x9ctherefore, does not displace Missouri law here.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

We granted certiorari to resolve conflicting interpretations of \xc2\xa78902(m)(1). 580 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2016). Compare 492 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa02d, at 925 (majority opinion), with Bell v. Blue Cross & Blue Shield of Okla., 823 F.\xc2\xa03d 1198, 1199 (CA8 2016) (\xc2\xa78902(m)(1) preempts state antisubrogation law); Helfrich v. Blue Cross & Blue Shield Assn., 804 F.\xc2\xa03d 1090, 1092 (CA10 2015) (same).

\n

II

\n

Section 8902(m)(1) places two preconditions on federal preemption. See supra, at 1. The parties agree that Missouri\xe2\x80\x99s law prohibiting subrogation and reimbursement meets one of the two limitations, i.e., the State\xe2\x80\x99s law \xe2\x80\x9crelates to health insurance or plans.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa78902(m)(1). They dispute only whether the subrogation and reimbursement requirements in OPM\xe2\x80\x99s contract with Coventry \xe2\x80\x9crelate to the nature, provision, or extent of coverage or benefits,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cincluding payments with respect to benefits.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

Coventry contends that \xc2\xa78902(m)(1) unambiguously covers the contractual terms at issue here. In any event, Coventry, joined by the United States as amicus curiae, urges that the rule published by OPM in 2015 leaves no room for doubt that insurance-contract terms providing for subrogation and reimbursement fall within \xc2\xa78902(m)(1)\xe2\x80\x99s preemptive scope. See supra, at 3. Deference is due to OPM\xe2\x80\x99s reading, Coventry and the United States assert, under Chevron U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc.,\n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 837 (1984)\n. In Nevils\xe2\x80\x99 view, by contrast, \xc2\xa78902(m)(1) does not preempt state antisubrogation and antireimbursement laws in light of the presumption against preemption. Given that presumption, Nevils maintains, OPM\xe2\x80\x99s rule is not entitled to deference. Though we have called Nevils\xe2\x80\x99 construction \xe2\x80\x9cplausible,\xe2\x80\x9d McVeigh, 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 698, the reading advanced by Coventry and the United States best comports with \xc2\xa78902(m)(1)\xe2\x80\x99s text, context, and purpose.

\n

A

\n

Contractual provisions for subrogation and reimbursement \xe2\x80\x9crelate to .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. payments with respect to benefits\xe2\x80\x9d because subrogation and reimbursement rights yield just such payments. When a carrier exercises its right to either reimbursement or subrogation, it receives from either the beneficiary or a third party \xe2\x80\x9cpayment\xe2\x80\x9d respecting the benefits the carrier had previously paid. The carrier\xe2\x80\x99s very provision of benefits triggers the right to payment. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 31; Helfrich, 804 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1106; Bell, 823 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1204.

\n

Congress\xe2\x80\x99 use of the expansive phrase \xe2\x80\x9crelate to\xe2\x80\x9d shores up that understanding. We have \xe2\x80\x9crepeatedly recognized\xe2\x80\x9d that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9crelate to\xe2\x80\x9d in a preemption clause \xe2\x80\x9cexpress[es] a broad pre-emptive purpose.\xe2\x80\x9d Morales v. Trans World Airlines, Inc.,\n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 374,\n383 (1992)\n; accord Northwest, Inc. v. Ginsberg, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 5, 9). Congress characteristically employs the phrase to reach any subject that has \xe2\x80\x9ca connection with, or reference to,\xe2\x80\x9d the topics the statute enumerates. Morales, 504 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 384. The phrase therefore weighs against Nevils\xe2\x80\x99 effort to narrow the term \xe2\x80\x9cpayments\xe2\x80\x9d to exclude payments that occur \xe2\x80\x9clong after\xe2\x80\x9d a carrier\xe2\x80\x99s provision of benefits (Brief for Respondent 27 (quoting McVeigh, 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 697)). See Nevils I, 418 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 460 (Wilson, J., concurring); cf. Hillman v. Maretta, 569 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2013) (slip op., at 10) (in the Federal Employees\xe2\x80\x99 Group Life Insurance Act context, it \xe2\x80\x9cmakes no difference\xe2\x80\x9d whether state law withholds benefits in the first instance or instead takes them away after they have been paid). Given language notably \xe2\x80\x9cexpansive [in] sweep,\xe2\x80\x9d Morales, 504 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 384 (internal quotation marks omitted), Nevils\xe2\x80\x99 argument that Congress intended to preempt only state coverage requirements (e.g., for acupuncture and chiropractic services, see Brief for Respondent 36) also miscarries.

\n

The statutory context and purpose reinforce our conclusion. FEHBA concerns \xe2\x80\x9cbenefits from a federal health insurance plan for federal employees that arise from a federal law\xe2\x80\x9d in an area with a \xe2\x80\x9clong history of federal involvement.\xe2\x80\x9d Bell, 823 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1202. Strong and \xe2\x80\x9cdistinctly federal interests are involved,\xe2\x80\x9d McVeigh, 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 696, in uniform administration of the program, free from state interference, particularly in regard to coverage, benefits, and payments. The Federal Government, more-over, has a significant financial stake. OPM estimates that, in 2014 alone, FEHBA \xe2\x80\x9ccarriers were reimbursed by approximately $126 million in subrogation recoveries.\xe2\x80\x9d 80 Fed. Reg. 29203. Such \xe2\x80\x9crecoveries translate to premium cost savings for the federal government and [FEHBA] enrollees.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

B

\n

Invoking our suggestion in McVeigh that \xc2\xa78902(m)(1) has two \xe2\x80\x9cplausible\xe2\x80\x9d interpretations, 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 698, Nevils nonetheless urges us to apply a presumption against preemption because \xc2\xa78902(m)(1) does not clearly cover contractual terms pertaining to subrogation and reimbursement. This argument is blind to McVeigh\xe2\x80\x99s context.

\n

In McVeigh, we considered the discrete question whether\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71331 gives federal courts subject-matter jurisdiction over FEHBA reimbursement actions. See 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 683. Our principal holding was that \xc2\xa71331 did not confer federal jurisdiction. Ibid.; see Bell, 823 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1205.

\n

The carrier in McVeigh, as part of its argument in favor of federal jurisdiction, asserted that \xc2\xa78902(m)(1) itself conferred federal jurisdiction. See 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 697. In responding to that assertion, we summarized competing interpretations of \xc2\xa78902(m)(1) advanced in briefing, readings that map closely onto the parties\xe2\x80\x99 positions here. See ibid. (carrier and United States as amicus curiae urged interpretation similar to Coventry\xe2\x80\x99s; an amicus brief in support of beneficiary offered interpretation similar to Nevils\xe2\x80\x99).

\n

We made no choice between the two interpretationsset out in McVeigh, however, because the answer madeno difference to the question there presented. Id., at698. \xe2\x80\x9c[E]ven if FEHBA\xe2\x80\x99s preemption provision reaches contract-based reimbursement claims,\xe2\x80\x9d we explained, \xe2\x80\x9cthat provision is not sufficiently broad to confer federal jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Because \xc2\xa78902(m)(1) is a \xe2\x80\x9cchoice-of-law prescription,\xe2\x80\x9d not a \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction-conferring provision,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 697, we had no cause to consider \xc2\xa78902(m)(1)\xe2\x80\x99s text, context, and purpose, as we do today, see supra, at 6\xe2\x80\x938.[3]

\n

III

\n

Nevils further contends that, if \xc2\xa78902(m)(1) covers subrogation and reimbursement clauses in OPM contracts, then the statute itself would violate the Supremacy Clause by assigning preemptive effect to the terms of a contract, not to the laws of the United States. We conclude, however, that the statute, not a contract, strips state law of its force.

\n

Without \xc2\xa78902(m)(1), there would be no preemption of state insurance law. FEHBA contract terms have preemptive force only as they \xe2\x80\x9crelate to the nature, provision, or extent of coverage or benefits (including payments with respect to benefits),\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa78902(m)(1)\xe2\x80\x94i.e., when the contract terms fall within the statute\xe2\x80\x99s preemptive scope. It is therefore the statute that \xe2\x80\x9censures that [FEHBA contract] terms will be uniformly enforceable nationwide, notwithstanding any state law relating to health insurance or plans.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 28 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

Many other federal statutes preempt state law in this way, leaving the context-specific scope of preemption to contractual terms. The Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974 (ERISA),\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71001 et\xc2\xa0seq., for example, preempts \xe2\x80\x9cany and all State laws insofar as they\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. relate to any employee benefit plan.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71144(a). And the Federal Arbitration Act (FAA),\n\n9 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71 et\xc2\xa0seq., limits the grounds for denying enforcement of \xe2\x80\x9cwritten provision[s] in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. contract[s]\xe2\x80\x9d providing for arbitration, thereby preempting state laws that would otherwise interfere with such contracts. \xc2\xa72. This Court has several times held that those statutes preempt state law, see, e.g., Gobeille v. Liberty Mut. Ins. Co., 577 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2016) (slip op., at 5\xe2\x80\x9312) (ERISA); Marmet Health Care Center, Inc. v. Brown,\n\n565 U.\xc2\xa0S. 530\n\xe2\x80\x93534 (2012) (per curiam) (FAA), and Nevils does not contend that those measures violate the Supremacy Clause, see Brief for Respondent 22.

\n

Nevils instead attempts to distinguish those other statutes by highlighting a particular textual feature of \xc2\xa78902(m)(1): Section 8902(m)(1) states that the \xe2\x80\x9cterms of any contract\xe2\x80\x9d between OPM and a carrier \xe2\x80\x9cshall supersede and preempt\xe2\x80\x9d certain state or local laws. (Emphasis added.) That formulation, Nevils asserts, violates the Supremacy Clause\xe2\x80\x99s mandate that only the \xe2\x80\x9cLaws of the United States\xe2\x80\x9d may reign supreme over state law. U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Art. VI, cl.\xc2\xa02 (emphasis added). Nevils\xe2\x80\x99 argument elevates semantics over substance. While Congress\xe2\x80\x99 formulation might differ from the phrasing of other statutes, \xc2\xa78902(m)(1) manifests the same intent to preempt state law.[4] Because we do not require Congress to employ a particular linguistic formulation when preempting state law, Nevils\xe2\x80\x99 Supremacy Clause challenge fails.[5]

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

For the reasons stated, the judgment of the Supreme Court of Missouri is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.
