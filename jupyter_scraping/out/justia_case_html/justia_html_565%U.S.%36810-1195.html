\n\t\t\t\tMIMS v. ARROW FINANCIAL SERVICES, LLC\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 10\xe2\x80\x931195.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 28, 2011\xe2\x80\x94Decided January 18, 2012\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tConsumer complaints about abuses of telephone technology\xe2\x80\x94for example, computerized calls to private homes\xe2\x80\x94prompted Congress to pass the Telephone Consumer Protection Act of 1991 (TCPA or Act), \n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7227. Congress determined that federal legislation was needed because telemarketers, by operating interstate, were escaping state-law prohibitions on intrusive nuisance calls. The Act bans certain invasive telemarketing practices and directs the Federal Communications Commission (FCC) to prescribe implementing regulations. It authorizes States to bring civil actions to enjoin prohibited practices and recover damages on their residents\xe2\x80\x99 behalf, \n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. A. \xc2\xa7227(g)(1) (Supp. 2011), and provides that jurisdiction over these state-initiated suits lies exclusively in the U.\xc2\xa0S. district courts, \xc2\xa7227(g)(2). It also permits a private person to seek redress for violations of the Act or regulations \xe2\x80\x9cin an appropriate court of [a] State,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cif [such an action is] otherwise permitted by the laws or rules of court of [that] State.\xe2\x80\x9d 47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7227(b)(3), (c)(5).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioner Mims filed a damages action in Federal District Court, alleging that respondent Arrow, seeking to collect a debt, violated the TCPA by repeatedly using an automatic telephone dialing system or prerecorded or artificial voice to call Mims\xe2\x80\x99s cellular phone without his consent. Mims invoked the court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cfederal question\xe2\x80\x9d jurisdiction, i.e., its authority to adjudicate claims \xe2\x80\x9carising under the .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. laws .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d \n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71331. The District Court, affirmed by the Eleventh Circuit, dismissed Mims\xe2\x80\x99s complaint for want of subject-matter jurisdiction, concluding that the TCPA had vested jurisdiction over private actions exclusively in state courts.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0The TCPA\xe2\x80\x99s permissive grant of jurisdiction to state courts does not deprive the U.\xc2\xa0S. district courts of federal-question jurisdiction over private TCPA suits. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9318.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0Because federal law creates the right of action and provides the rules of decision, Mims\xe2\x80\x99s TCPA claim, in \xc2\xa71331\xe2\x80\x99s words, plainly \xe2\x80\x9caris[es] under\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9claws .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Arrow agrees that this action arises under federal law, but urges that Congress vested exclusive adjudicatory authority over private TCPA actions in state courts. In cases \xe2\x80\x9carising under\xe2\x80\x9d federal law, there is a presumption of concurrent state-court jurisdiction, rebuttable if \xe2\x80\x9cCongress affirmatively ousts the state courts of jurisdiction over a particular federal claim.\xe2\x80\x9d Tafflin v. Levitt, \n\n493 U.\xc2\xa0S. 455\n\xe2\x80\x93459. Arrow acknowledges the presumption, but maintains that \xc2\xa71331 creates no converse presumption in favor of federal-court jurisdiction. Instead, Arrow urges, the TCPA, a later, more specific statute, displaces \xc2\xa71331, an earlier, more general prescription. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSection 1331 is not swept away so easily. The principle that district courts possess federal-question jurisdiction under \xc2\xa71331 when federal law creates a private right of action and furnishes the substantive rules of decision endures unless Congress divests federal courts of their \xc2\xa71331 adjudicatory authority. See, e.g., Verizon Md. Inc. v. Public Serv. Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of Md., \n\n535 U.\xc2\xa0S. 635\n. Accordingly, the District Court retains \xc2\xa71331 jurisdiction over Mims\xe2\x80\x99s complaint unless the TCPA, expressly or by fair implication, excludes federal-court adjudication. See id., at 644. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9310.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0Arrow\xe2\x80\x99s arguments do not persuade this Court that Congress eliminated \xc2\xa71331 jurisdiction over private TCPA actions. Title \n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7227(b)(3)\xe2\x80\x99s language may be state-court oriented, but \xe2\x80\x9cthe grant of jurisdiction to one court does not, of itself, imply that the jurisdiction is to be exclusive,\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Bank of New York & Trust Co., \n\n296 U.\xc2\xa0S. 463\n. Nothing in \xc2\xa7227(b)(3)\xe2\x80\x99s permissive language makes state-court jurisdiction exclusive, or otherwise purports to oust federal courts of their \xc2\xa71331 jurisdiction. The provision does not state that a private plaintiff may bring a TCPA action \xe2\x80\x9conly\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cexclusively\xe2\x80\x9d in state court. In contrast, \n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. A. \xc2\xa7227(g)(2) (Supp. 2011) vests \xe2\x80\x9cexclusive jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d over state-initiated TCPA suits in the federal courts. Section 227(g)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s exclusivity prescription \xe2\x80\x9creinforce[s] the conclusion that [\n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7227(b)(3)\xe2\x80\x99s] silence .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. leaves the jurisdictional grant of \xc2\xa71331 untouched. For where otherwise applicable jurisdiction was meant to be excluded, it was excluded expressly.\xe2\x80\x9d Verizon Md., 535 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 644. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tArrow argues that Congress had no reason to provide for a private action \xe2\x80\x9cin an appropriate [state] court,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7227(b)(3), if it did not mean to make the state forum exclusive, for state courts would have concurrent jurisdiction even if Congress had said nothing at all. But, as already noted, Congress had simultaneously made federal-court jurisdiction exclusive in TCPA enforcement actions brought by state authorities, see \n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. A. \xc2\xa7227(g)(2) (Supp. 2011), and may simply have wanted to avoid any argument that federal jurisdiction was also exclusive for private actions. Moreover, by providing that private actions may be brought in state court \xe2\x80\x9cif otherwise permitted by the laws or rules of court of [the] State,\xe2\x80\x9d \n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7227(b)(3), Congress arguably gave States leeway they would otherwise lack to decide whether to entertain TCPA claims.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tArrow further asserts that making state-court jurisdiction over \xc2\xa7227(b)(3) claims exclusive serves Congress\xe2\x80\x99 objective of enabling States to control telemarketers whose interstate operations evaded state law. Even so, jurisdiction conferred by \n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71331 should hold firm against \xe2\x80\x9cmere implication flowing from subsequent legislation.\xe2\x80\x9d Colorado River Water Conservation Dist. v. United States, \n\n424 U.\xc2\xa0S. 800\n, n.\xc2\xa015. Furthermore, had Congress sought only to fill a gap in the States\xe2\x80\x99 enforcement capabilities, it could have provided that out-of-state telemarketing calls directed into a State would be subject to the receiving State\xe2\x80\x99s laws. Instead, Congress enacted detailed, uniform, federal substantive prescriptions and provided for a regulatory regime administered by a federal agency. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tArrow\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on a statement by Senator Hollings, the TCPA\xe2\x80\x99s sponsor, is misplaced. The remarks nowhere mention federal-court jurisdiction or otherwise suggest that \n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7227(b)(3) is intended to divest federal courts of authority over TCPA claims. Even if Hollings and other TCPA supporters expected private actions to proceed solely in state courts, their expectation would not control this Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment on \xc2\xa71331\xe2\x80\x99s compass. Arrow\xe2\x80\x99s arguments that federal courts will be inundated by $500-per-violation TCPA claims or that defendants could use federal-court removal to force small-claims-court plaintiffs to abandon suit seem more imaginary than real. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9318.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t421 Fed. Appx. 920, reversed and remanded.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis case concerns enforcement, through private suits, of the Telephone Consumer Protection Act of 1991 (TCPA or Act), \n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7227. Voluminous consumer complaints about abuses of telephone technology\xe2\x80\x94for example, computerized calls dispatched to private homes\xe2\x80\x94prompted Congress to pass the TCPA. Congress de- termined that federal legislation was needed because telemarketers, by operating interstate, were escaping state-law prohibitions on intrusive nuisance calls. The Act bans certain practices invasive of privacy and directs the Federal Communications Commission (FCC or Commission) to prescribe implementing regulations. It authorizes States to bring civil actions to enjoin prohibited practices and to recover damages on their residents\xe2\x80\x99 behalf. The Commission must be notified of such suits and may intervene in them. Jurisdiction over state-initiated TCPA suits, Congress provided, lies exclusively in the U.\xc2\xa0S. district courts. Congress also provided for civil actions by private parties seeking redress for violations of the TCPA or of the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s implementing regulations.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioner Marcus D. Mims, complaining of multiple violations of the Act by respondent Arrow Financial Services, LLC (Arrow), a debt-collection agency, commenced an action for damages against Arrow in the U. S. District Court for the Southern District of Florida. Mims invoked the court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cfederal question\xe2\x80\x9d jurisdiction, i.e., its authority to adjudicate claims \xe2\x80\x9carising under the .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. laws .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d \n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71331. The District Court, affirmed by the U. S. Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit, dismissed Mims\xe2\x80\x99s complaint for want of subject-matter jurisdiction. Both courts relied on Congress\xe2\x80\x99 specification, in the TCPA, that a private person may seek redress for violations of the Act (or of the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s regulations thereunder) \xe2\x80\x9cin an appropriate court of [a] State,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cif [such an action is] otherwise permitted by the laws or rules of court of [that] State.\xe2\x80\x9d 47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7227(b)(3), (c)(5).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe question presented is whether Congress\xe2\x80\x99 provision for private actions to enforce the TCPA renders state courts the exclusive arbiters of such actions. We have long recognized that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] suit arises under the law that creates the cause of action.\xe2\x80\x9d American Well Works Co. v. Layne & Bowler Co., \n\n241 U.\xc2\xa0S. 257, \n260 (1916)\n. Beyond doubt, the TCPA is a federal law that both creates the claim Mims has brought and supplies the substantive rules that will govern the case. We find no convincing reason to read into the TCPA\xe2\x80\x99s permissive grant of jurisdiction to state courts any barrier to the U. S. district courts\xe2\x80\x99 exercise of the general federal-question jurisdiction they have possessed since 1875. See Act of Mar. 3, 1875, \xc2\xa71, \n\n18Stat.\n470; 13D C. Wright, A. Miller, E. Cooper, & R. Freer, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa73561, p. 163 (3d ed. 2008) (hereinafter Wright & Miller). We hold, therefore, that federal and state courts have concurrent jurisdiction over private suits arising under the TCPA.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn enacting the TCPA, Congress made several findings relevant here. \xe2\x80\x9cUnrestricted telemarketing,\xe2\x80\x9d Congress determined, \xe2\x80\x9ccan be an intrusive invasion of privacy.\xe2\x80\x9d TCPA, 105 Stat. 2394, note following \n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7227 (Congressional Findings) (internal quotation marks omitted). In particular, Congress reported, \xe2\x80\x9c[m]any consumers are outraged over the proliferation of intrusive, nuisance [telemarketing] calls to their homes.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted). \xe2\x80\x9c[A]utomated or prerecorded telephone calls\xe2\x80\x9d made to private residences, Congress found, were rightly regarded by recipients as \xe2\x80\x9can invasion of privacy.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted). Although over half the States had enacted statutes restricting telemarketing, Congress believed that federal law was needed because \xe2\x80\x9ctelemarketers [could] evade [state-law] prohibitions through interstate operations.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted). See also S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 102\xe2\x80\x93178, p.\xc2\xa03 (1991) (\xe2\x80\x9c[B]ecause States do not have jurisdiction over interstate calls[,] [m]any States have expressed a desire for Federal legislation .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. .\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSubject to exceptions not pertinent here, the TCPA prin- cipally outlaws four practices. First, the Act makes it unlawful to use an automatic telephone dialing system or an artificial or prerecorded voice message, without the prior express consent of the called party, to call any emergency telephone line, hospital patient, pager, cellular telephone, or other service for which the receiver is charged for the call. See \n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7227(b)(1)(A). Sec-ond, the TCPA forbids using artificial or prerecorded voice messages to call residential telephone lines with- out prior express consent. \xc2\xa7227(b)(1)(B). Third, the Act proscribes sending unsolicited advertisements to fax machines. \xc2\xa7227(b)(1)(C). Fourth, it bans using automatic telephone dialing systems to engage two or more of a business\xe2\x80\x99 telephone lines simultaneously. \xc2\xa7227(b)(1)(D).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe TCPA delegates authority to the FCC to ban ar- tificial and prerecorded voice calls to businesses, \xc2\xa7227(b)(2)(A), and to exempt particular types of calls from the law\xe2\x80\x99s requirements, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7227(b)(2)(B), (C). The Act also directs the FCC to prescribe regulations to protect the privacy of residential telephone subscribers, possibly through the creation of a national \xe2\x80\x9cdo not call\xe2\x80\x9d system. \xc2\xa7227(c).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress provided complementary means of enforcing the Act. State Attorneys General may \xe2\x80\x9cbring a civil action on behalf of [State] residents,\xe2\x80\x9d if the Attorney General \xe2\x80\x9chas reason to believe that any person has engaged .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in a pattern or practice\xe2\x80\x9d of violating the TCPA or FCC regulations thereunder. \n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. A. \xc2\xa7227(g)(1) (Supp. 2011). \xe2\x80\x9cThe district courts of the United States .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. have exclusive jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d over all TCPA actions brought by State Attorneys General. \xc2\xa7227(g)(2). The Commission may intervene in such suits. \xc2\xa7227(g)(3).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tA similar provision authorizes a private right of action for a violation of the FCC\xe2\x80\x99s implementing regulations.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMims, a Florida resident, alleged that Arrow, seeking to collect a debt, repeatedly used an automatic telephone dialing system or prerecorded or artificial voice to call Mims\xe2\x80\x99s cellular phone without his consent. Commencing suit in the U.\xc2\xa0S. District Court for the Southern District of Florida, Mims charged that Arrow \xe2\x80\x9cwillfully or knowingly violated the TCPA.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 14. He sought declaratory relief, a permanent injunction, and damages. Id., at 18\xe2\x80\x9319.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe District Court held that it lacked subject-matter jurisdiction over Mims\xe2\x80\x99s TCPA claim. Under Eleventh Circuit precedent, the District Court explained, federal-question jurisdiction under \n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71331 was unavailable \xe2\x80\x9cbecause Congress vested jurisdiction over [private actions under] the TCPA exclusively in state courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Civ. No. 09\xe2\x80\x9322347 (SD Fla., Apr. 1, 2010), App. to Pet. for Cert. 4a\xe2\x80\x935a (citing Nicholson v. Hooters of Augusta, Inc., 136 F.\xc2\xa03d 1287 (CA11 1998)). Adhering to Circuit precedent, the U.\xc2\xa0S. Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit affirmed. 421 Fed. Appx. 920, 921 (2011) (quoting Nicholson, 136 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1287\xe2\x80\x931288 (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress granted state courts exclusive jurisdiction over private actions under the [TCPA].\xe2\x80\x9d)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe granted certiorari, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2011), to resolve a split among the Circuits as to whether Congress granted state courts exclusive jurisdiction over private actions brought under the TCPA. Compare Murphey v. Lanier, 204 F. 3d 911, 915 (CA9 2000) (U.\xc2\xa0S. district courts lack federal-question jurisdiction over private TCPA actions), ErieNet, Inc. v. Velocity Net, Inc., 156 F. 3d 513, 519 (CA3 1998) (same), Foxhall Realty Law Offices, Inc. v. Telecommunications Premium Servs., Ltd., 156 F. 3d 432, 434 (CA2 1998) (same), Nicholson, 136 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1287\xe2\x80\x931288, Chair King, Inc. v. Houston Cellular Corp., 131 F. 3d 507, 514 (CA5 1997) (same), and International Science & Technology Inst. v. Inacom Communications, Inc., 106 F. 3d 1146, 1158 (CA4 1997) (same), with Charvat v. EchoStar Satellite, LLC, 630 F.\xc2\xa03d 459, 463\xe2\x80\x93465 (CA6 2010) (U.\xc2\xa0S. district courts have federal-question jurisdiction over private TCPA actions), Brill v. Countrywide Home Loans, Inc., 427 F.\xc2\xa03d 446, 447 (CA7 2005) (same), and ErieNet, 156 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 521 (Alito, J., dissenting) (same). We now hold that Congress did not deprive federal courts of federal-question jurisdiction over private TCPA suits.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFederal courts, though \xe2\x80\x9ccourts of limited jurisdiction,\xe2\x80\x9d Kokkonen v. Guardian Life Ins. Co. of America, \n\n511 U.\xc2\xa0S. 375, \n377 (1994)\n, in the main \xe2\x80\x9chave no more right to decline the exercise of jurisdiction which is given, then to usurp that which is not given.\xe2\x80\x9d Cohens v. Virginia, 6 Wheat. 264 (1821). Congress granted federal courts general federal-question jurisdiction in 1875. See Act of Mar. 3, 1875, \xc2\xa71, \n\n18Stat.\n470.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t As now codified, the law provides: \xe2\x80\x9cThe district courts shall have original jurisdiction of all civil actions arising under the Constitution, laws, or treaties of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d \n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71331. The statute originally included an amount-in-controversy requirement, set at $500. See Act of Mar. 3, 1875, \xc2\xa71, \n\n18Stat.\n470. Recognizing the responsibility of federal courts to decide claims, large or small, arising under federal law, Congress in 1980 eliminated the amount-in-controversy requirement in federal-question (but not diversity) cases. See Federal Question Jurisdictional Amendments Act of 1980, \n\n94Stat.\n2369 (amending \n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71331). See also H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 96\xe2\x80\x931461, p.\xc2\xa01 (1980).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t7\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Apart from deletion of the amount-in-controversy requirement, the general federal-question provision has remained essentially unchanged since 1875. See 13D Wright & Miller 163.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBecause federal law creates the right of action and provides the rules of decision, Mims\xe2\x80\x99s TCPA claim, in \n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71331\xe2\x80\x99s words, plainly \xe2\x80\x9caris[es] under\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9claws .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d As already noted, supra, at 2, \xe2\x80\x9c[a] suit arises under the law that creates the cause of action.\xe2\x80\x9d American Well Works, 241 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 260. Al- though courts have described this formulation as \xe2\x80\x9cmore useful for inclusion than for .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. exclusion,\xe2\x80\x9d Merrell Dow Pharmaceuticals Inc. v. Thompson, \n\n478 U.\xc2\xa0S. 804, \n809, n. \n5 (1986)\n (quoting T. B. Harms Co. v. Eliscu, 339 F. 2d 823, 827 (CA2 1964)), there is no serious debate that a federally created claim for relief is generally a \xe2\x80\x9csufficient con- dition for federal-question jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Grable & Sons Metal Products, Inc. v. Darue Engineering & Mfg., \n\n545 U.\xc2\xa0S. 308, \n317 (2005)\n.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t8\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tArrow agrees that this action arises under federal law, see Tr. of Oral Arg. 27, but urges that Congress vested exclusive adjudicatory authority over private TCPA actions in state courts. In cases \xe2\x80\x9carising under\xe2\x80\x9d federal law, we note, there is a \xe2\x80\x9cdeeply rooted presumption in favor of concurrent state court jurisdiction,\xe2\x80\x9d rebuttable if \xe2\x80\x9cCongress affirmatively ousts the state courts of jurisdiction over a particular federal claim.\xe2\x80\x9d Tafflin v. Levitt, \n\n493 U.\xc2\xa0S. 455\n\xe2\x80\x93459 (1990). E.g., \n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71333 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe district courts shall have original jurisdiction, exclusive of the courts of the States, of: (1) Any civil case of admiralty or maritime jurisdiction .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. .\xe2\x80\x9d). The presumption of concurrent state-court jurisdiction, we have recognized, can be overcome \xe2\x80\x9cby an explicit statutory directive, by unmistakable implication from legislative history, or by a clear incompatibility between state-court jurisdiction and federal interests.\xe2\x80\x9d Gulf Offshore Co. v. Mobil Oil Corp., \n\n453 U.\xc2\xa0S. 473, \n478 (1981)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tArrow readily acknowledges the presumption of con- current state-court jurisdiction, but maintains that \n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71331 creates no converse presumption in favor of federal-court jurisdiction. Instead, Arrow urges, the TCPA, a later, more specific statute, displaces \xc2\xa71331, an earlier, more general prescription. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 28\xe2\x80\x9329; Brief for Respondent 31.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSection 1331, our decisions indicate, is not swept away so easily. As stated earlier, see supra, at 8, when federal law creates a private right of action and furnishes the substantive rules of decision, the claim arises under federal law, and district courts possess federal-question jurisdiction under \xc2\xa71331.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t9\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t That principle endures unless Congress divests federal courts of their \xc2\xa71331 adjudica- tory authority. See, e.g., Verizon Md. Inc. v. Public Serv. Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of Md., \n\n535 U.\xc2\xa0S. 635, \n642 (2002)\n (Nothing in \n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7252(e)(6) \xe2\x80\x9cdivest[s] the district courts of their authority under \n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71331 to review the [state agency\xe2\x80\x99s] order for compliance with federal law.\xe2\x80\x9d); K mart Corp. v. Cartier, Inc., \n\n485 U.\xc2\xa0S. 176\n\xe2\x80\x93183 (1988) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe District Court would be divested of [\xc2\xa71331] jurisdiction .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. if this action fell within one of several specific grants of exclusive jurisdiction to the Court of International Trade [under \n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71581(a) or \xc2\xa71581(i)(3)].\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c[D]ivestment of district court jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d should be found no more readily than \xe2\x80\x9cdivestmen[t] of state court jurisdiction,\xe2\x80\x9d given \xe2\x80\x9cthe longstanding and explicit grant of federal question jurisdiction in \n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71331.\xe2\x80\x9d ErieNet, 156 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 523 (Alito, J., dissenting); see Gonell, Note, Statutory Interpretation of Federal Jurisdictional Statutes: Jurisdiction of the Private Right of Action under the TCPA, 66 Ford. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1895, 1929\xe2\x80\x931930 (1998). Accordingly, the District Court retains \xc2\xa71331 jurisdiction over Mims\xe2\x80\x99s complaint unless the TCPA, expressly or by fair implication, excludes federal-court adjudication. See Verizon Md., 535 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 644; Gonell, supra, at 1929 (Jurisdiction over private TCPA actions \xe2\x80\x9cis proper under \xc2\xa71331 unless Congress enacted a partial repeal of \xc2\xa71331 in the TCPA.\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tArrow\xe2\x80\x99s arguments do not persuade us that Congress has eliminated \xc2\xa71331 jurisdiction over private actions under the TCPA.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe language of the TCPA\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cA person or entity may, if otherwise permitted by the laws or rules of court of a State, bring [an action] in an appropriate court of that State,\xe2\x80\x9d \n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7227(b)(3)\xe2\x80\x94Arrow asserts, is uniquely state-court oriented. See Brief for Respondent 13. That may be, but \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t is a general rule that the grant of jurisdiction to one court does not, of itself, imply that the jurisdiction is to be exclusive.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Bank of New York & Trust Co., \n\n296 U.\xc2\xa0S. 463, \n479 (1936)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNothing in the permissive language of \xc2\xa7227(b)(3) makes state-court jurisdiction exclusive, or otherwise purports to oust federal courts of their \n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71331 jurisdiction over federal claims. See, e.g., Verizon Md., 535 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 643 (\xe2\x80\x9c[N]othing in \n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7252(e)(6) purports to strip [\xc2\xa71331] jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d). Cf. Yellow Freight System, Inc. v. Donnelly, \n\n494 U.\xc2\xa0S. 820, \n823 (1990)\n (Title VII\xe2\x80\x99s language\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9c[e]ach United States district court .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall have jurisdiction of actions brought under this subchapter,\xe2\x80\x9d \n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x935(f)(3)\xe2\x80\x94does not \xe2\x80\x9cous[t] state courts of their presumptive jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). Congress may indeed provide a track for a federal claim exclusive of \xc2\xa71331. See, e.g., \n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7405(h) (\xe2\x80\x9cNo action .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall be brought under [\xc2\xa71331] to recover on any claim arising under [Title II of the Social Security Act].\xe2\x80\x9d); Weinberger v. Salfi, \n\n422 U.\xc2\xa0S. 749\n\xe2\x80\x93757 (1975). Congress has done nothing of that sort here, however.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTitle \n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7227(b)(3) does not state that a private plaintiff may bring an action under the TCPA \xe2\x80\x9conly\xe2\x80\x9d in state court, or \xe2\x80\x9cexclusively\xe2\x80\x9d in state court. The absence of such a statement contrasts with the Act\xe2\x80\x99s instruction on suits instituted by State Attorneys General. As earlier noted, see supra, at 4, 47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0A. \xc2\xa7227(g)(2) (Supp. 2011) vests \xe2\x80\x9cexclusive jurisdiction over [such] actions\xe2\x80\x9d in \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he district courts of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t10\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Section 227(g)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s exclusivity prescription \xe2\x80\x9creinforce[s] the conclusion that [\n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7227(b)(3)\xe2\x80\x99s] silence .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. leaves the jurisdictional grant of \xc2\xa71331 untouched. For where otherwise applicable jurisdiction was meant to be excluded, it was excluded expressly.\xe2\x80\x9d Verizon Md., 535 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 644; see ErieNet, 156 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 522 (Alito, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9c[47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0A. \xc2\xa7227(g)(2) (Supp. 2011)] reveals that, while drafting the TCPA, Congress knew full well how to grant exclusive jurisdiction with mandatory language. The most natural interpretation of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 failure to use similar language in [47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7]227(b)(3) is that Congress did not intend to grant exclusive jurisdiction in that section.\xe2\x80\x9d); Brill, 427 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 451 (\xe2\x80\x9c[47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0A. \xc2\xa7227(g)(2) (Supp. 2011)] is explicit about exclusivity, while [47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C.] \xc2\xa7227(b)(3) is not; the natural inference is that the state forum mentioned in \xc2\xa7227(b)(3) is optional rather than mandatory.\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t11\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tArrow urges that Congress would have had no reason to provide for a private action \xe2\x80\x9cin an appropriate [state] court,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7227(b)(3), if it did not mean to make the state forum exclusive. Had Congress said nothing at all about bringing private TCPA claims in state courts, Arrow observes, those courts would nevertheless have concurrent jurisdiction. See supra, at 9. True enough, but Con- gress had simultaneously provided for TCPA enforcement actions by state authorities, 47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0A. \xc2\xa7227(g) (Supp. 2011), and had made federal district courts exclusively competent in such cases, \xc2\xa7227(g)(2). \n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMaking state-court jurisdiction over \xc2\xa7227(b)(3) claims exclusive, Arrow further asserts, \xe2\x80\x9cfits hand in glove with [Congress\xe2\x80\x99] objective\xe2\x80\x9d: enabling States to control telemarketers whose interstate operations evaded state law. Id., at 15. Even so, we have observed, jurisdiction conferred by \n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71331 should hold firm against \xe2\x80\x9cmere implication flowing from subsequent legislation.\xe2\x80\x9d Colorado River Water Conservation Dist. v. United States, \n\n424 U.\xc2\xa0S. 800, \n808, \n809, n. \n15 (1976)\n (quoting Rosencrans v. United States, \n\n165 U.\xc2\xa0S. 257, \n262 (1897)\n).\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe are not persuaded, moreover, that Congress sought only to fill a gap in the States\xe2\x80\x99 enforcement capabilities. Had Congress so limited its sights, it could have passed a statute providing that out-of-state telemarketing calls directed into a State would be subject to the laws of the receiving State. Congress did not enact such a law. Instead, it enacted detailed, uniform, federal substantive prescriptions and provided for a regulatory regime administered by a federal agency. See \n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7227. TCPA liability thus depends on violation of a federal statutory requirement or an FCC regulation, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7227(b)(3)(A), (c)(5), not on a violation of any state substantive law.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFirst, the views of a single legislator, even a bill\xe2\x80\x99s sponsor, are not controlling. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNothing in the text, structure, purpose, or legislative history of the TCPA calls for displacement of the federal-question jurisdiction U.\xc2\xa0S. district courts ordinarily have under \n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71331. In the absence of direction from Congress stronger than any Arrow has advanced, we apply the familiar default rule: Federal courts have \xc2\xa71331 jurisdiction over claims that arise under federal law. Because federal law gives rise to the claim for relief Mims has stated and specifies the substantive rules of decision, the Eleventh Circuit erred in dismissing Mims\xe2\x80\x99s case for lack of subject-matter jurisdiction.\n\t\t\t
