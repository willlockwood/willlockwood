JAY SHAWN JOHNSON, PETITIONER v. CALIFORNIA

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of california

\n

[May 3, 2004]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Per Curiam.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari in this case to review a decision of the Supreme Court of California interpreting Batson v. Kentucky, 476 U. S. 79 (1986). 540 U. S. ___ (2003). The case was briefed and argued, but we now conclude that we are without jurisdiction in the matter.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The California Supreme Court reversed the California Court of Appeal\xe2\x80\x99s decision reversing petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s conviction. 30 Cal. 4th 1302, 71 P.\xc2\xa03d 270 (2003). The Court of Appeal held that petitioner was entitled to relief under California v. Wheeler, 22 Cal. 3d 258, 583 P.\xc2\xa02d 748 (1978), and Batson v. Kentucky, supra. 88 Cal. App. 4th 318, 105 Cal. Rptr. 2d 727 (2001). It also noted petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s separate evidentiary and prosecutorial misconduct claims, App. 87, but did not determine whether those claims would independently support reversal of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s conviction. The California Supreme Court addressed only the Wheeler/Batson claim, and, after reversing on that ground, remanded \xe2\x80\x9cfor further proceedings consistent with [its] opinion.\xe2\x80\x9d 30 Cal. 4th., at 1328, 71 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 287.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71257, our jurisdiction is limited to review of \xe2\x80\x9c[f]inal judgments or decrees rendered by the highest court of a State in which a decision could be had.\xe2\x80\x9d In Cox Broadcasting Corp. v. Cohn, 420 U. S. 469 (1975), we described four exceptional categories of cases to be regarded as \xe2\x80\x9cfinal\xe2\x80\x9d on the federal issue despite the ordering of further proceedings in the lower state courts. In a post-oral-argument supplemental brief, petitioner argues that the fourth of these categories fits this case. That category involves situations:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cwhere the federal issue has been finally decided in the state courts with further proceedings pending in which the party seeking review here might prevail on the merits on nonfederal grounds, thus rendering unnecessary review of the federal issue by this Court, and where reversal of the state court on the federal issue would be preclusive of any further litigation on the relevant cause of action rather than merely controlling the nature and character of, or determining the admissibility of evidence in, the state proceedings still to come. In these circumstances, if a refusal immediately to review the state-court decision might seriously erode federal policy, the Court has entertained and decided the federal issue, which itself has been finally determined by the state courts for purposes of the state litigation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 482\xe2\x80\x93483.

\n

\nHere, petitioner can make no convincing claim of erosion of federal policy that is not common to all decisions rejecting a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s Batson claim. The fourth category therefore does not apply. See Florida v. Thomas, 532 U. S. 774, 780 (2001). \xe2\x80\x9cA contrary conclusion would permit the fourth exception to swallow the rule.\xe2\x80\x9d See Flynt v. Ohio, 451 U. S. 619, 622 (1981) (per curiam).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The present case comes closest to fitting in the third Cox category, but ultimately falls outside of it. That category involves \xe2\x80\x9cthose situations where the federal claim has been finally decided, with further proceedings on the merits in the state courts to come, but in which later review of the federal issue cannot be had, whatever the ultimate outcome of the case.\xe2\x80\x9d Cox Broadcasting, Corp., supra, at 481. In the event that the California Court of Appeal on remand affirms the judgment of conviction, petitioner could once more seek review of his Batson claim in the Supreme Court of California\xe2\x80\x94albeit unsuccessfully\xe2\x80\x94and then seek certiorari on that claim from this Court.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Compliance with the provisions of \xc2\xa71257 is an essential prerequisite to our deciding the merits of a case brought here under that section. It is our obligation to raise any question of such compliance on our own motion, even though counsel has not called our attention to it. See, e.g., Mansfield, C. & L. M. R. Co. v. Swan, 111 U. S. 379, 384 (1884). But as the present case illustrates, we are not always successful in policing this gatekeeping function without the aid of counsel.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Part of the problem was that the California Court of Appeal\xe2\x80\x99s decision was certified by that court for partial publication. It addressed the Wheeler/Batson claim in the published portion. 88 Cal. App. 4th 318, 105 Cal. Rptr. 2d 727 (2001). In the unpublished portion, the court briefly addressed petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s evidentiary claims to provide guidance for the trial court on retrial, and noted that it would not address whether petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s objections were properly preserved or consider petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s prosecutorial misconduct claim. App. 58. Petitioner appended only the published portion of the California Court of Appeal\xe2\x80\x99s decision to his petition for a writ of certiorari. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 14.1(i) instructs petitioners to include, inter alia, any \xe2\x80\x9crelevant opinions . . . entered in the case\xe2\x80\x9d in the appendix to the petition for certiorari. The full opinion of the California Court of Appeal was not filed in this Court until the joint appendix to the briefs on the merits was filed. App. 58\xe2\x80\x93\n112. Had the full opinion been brought to this Court\xe2\x80\x99s attention, it might have been more evident to us that the Supreme Court of California\xe2\x80\x99s decision was not final for the purposes of \xc2\xa71257.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A petition for certiorari must demonstrate to this Court that it has jurisdiction to review the judgment. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 14.1(g). And a respondent has a duty to \xe2\x80\x9caddress any perceived misstatement of fact or law in the petition that bears on what issues properly would be before the Court if certiorari were granted.\xe2\x80\x9d Rule 15.2. Our Rules also require that each party provide a statement for the basis of our jurisdiction in its brief on the merits. Rule 24.1(e). At all stages in this case, both parties represented that our jurisdiction was proper pursuant to \xc2\xa71257(a). Pet. for Cert. 1; Brief in Opposition 1; Brief for Petitioner 1; Brief for Respondent 1.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It behooves counsel for both petitioner and respondent to assure themselves that the decision for which review is sought is indeed a \xe2\x80\x9c[f]inal judgmen[t]\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa71257. Such attention is mandated by our Rules and will avoid the expenditure of resources of both counsel and of this Court on an abortive proceeding such as the present one.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We dismiss the case for want of jurisdiction.

\n

It is so ordered.
