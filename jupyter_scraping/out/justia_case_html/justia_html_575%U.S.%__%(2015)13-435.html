SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

Syllabus

Omnicare, Inc., et\xc2\xa0al. v.\xc2\xa0Laborers District Council Construction Industry Pension Fund et\xc2\xa0al.

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

No. 13\xe2\x80\x93435.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 3, 2014\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 24, 2015

The Securities Act of 1933 requires that a company wishing to issue securities must first file a registration statement containing specified information about the issuing company and the securities offered. See 15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa777g, 77aa. The registration statement may also include other representations of fact or opinion. To protect investors and promote compliance with these disclosure requirements, \xc2\xa711 of the Act creates two ways to hold issuers liable for a registration statement\xe2\x80\x99s contents: A purchaser of securities may sue an issuer if the registration statement either \xe2\x80\x9ccontain[s] an untrue statement of a material fact\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9comit[s] to state a material fact .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. necessary to make the statements therein not misleading.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa777k(a). In either case, the buyer need not prove that the issuer acted with any intent to deceive or defraud. Herman & MacLean v. Huddleston, 459 U.\xc2\xa0S. 375 \xe2\x80\x93382.

Petitioner Omnicare, a pharmacy services company, filed a registration statement in connection with a public offering of common stock. In addition to the required disclosures, the registration statement contained two statements expressing the company\xe2\x80\x99s opinion that it was in compliance with federal and state laws. After the Federal Government filed suit against Omnicare for allegedly receiving kickbacks from pharmaceutical manufacturers, respondents, pension funds that purchased Omnicare stock (hereinafter Funds), sued Omnicare under \xc2\xa711. They claimed that Omnicare\xe2\x80\x99s legal-compliance statements constituted \xe2\x80\x9cuntrue statement[s] of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. material fact\xe2\x80\x9d and that Omnicare \xe2\x80\x9comitted to state [material] facts necessary\xe2\x80\x9d to make those statements not misleading.

The District Court granted Omnicare\xe2\x80\x99s motion to dismiss. Because the Funds had not alleged that Omnicare\xe2\x80\x99s officers knew they were violating the law, the court found that the Funds had failed to state a \xc2\xa711 claim. The Sixth Circuit reversed. Acknowledging that the statements at issue expressed opinions, the court held that no showing of subjective disbelief was required. In the court\xe2\x80\x99s view, the Funds\xe2\x80\x99 allegations that Omnicare\xe2\x80\x99s legal-compliance opinions were objectively false sufficed to support their claim.

Held:

1. A statement of opinion does not constitute an \xe2\x80\x9cuntrue statement of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. fact\xe2\x80\x9d simply because the stated opinion ultimately proves incorrect. The Sixth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s contrary holding wrongly conflates facts and opinions. A statement of fact expresses certainty about a thing, whereas a statement of opinion conveys only an uncertain view as to that thing. Section 11 incorporates that distinction in its first clause by exposing issuers to liability only for \xe2\x80\x9cuntrue statement[s] of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. fact.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa777k(a) (emphasis added). Because a statement of opinion admits the possibility of error, such a statement remains true\xe2\x80\x94and thus is not an \xe2\x80\x9cuntrue statement of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. fact\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94even if the opinion turns out to have been wrong.

But opinion statements are not wholly immune from liability under \xc2\xa711\xe2\x80\x99s first clause. Every such statement explicitly affirms one fact: that the speaker actually holds the stated belief. A statement of opinion thus qualifies as an \xe2\x80\x9cuntrue statement of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. fact\xe2\x80\x9d if that fact is untrue\xe2\x80\x94i.e., if the opinion expressed was not sincerely held. In addition, opinion statements can give rise to false-statement liability under \xc2\xa711 if they contain embedded statements of untrue facts. Here, however, Omnicare\xe2\x80\x99s sincerity is not contested and the statements at issue are pure opinion statements. The Funds thus cannot establish liability under \xc2\xa711\xe2\x80\x99s first clause. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9310.

2. If a registration statement omits material facts about the issuer\xe2\x80\x99s inquiry into, or knowledge concerning, a statement of opinion, and if those facts conflict with what a reasonable investor, reading the statement fairly and in context, would take from the statement itself, then \xc2\xa711\xe2\x80\x99s omissions clause creates liability. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9320.

(a) For purposes of \xc2\xa711\xe2\x80\x99s omissions clause, whether a statement is \xe2\x80\x9cmisleading\xe2\x80\x9d is an objective inquiry that depends on a reasonable investor\xe2\x80\x99s perspective. Cf. TSC Industries, Inc. v. Northway, Inc., 426 U.\xc2\xa0S. 438 . Omnicare goes too far by claiming that no reasonable person, in any context, can understand a statement of opinion to convey anything more than the speaker\xe2\x80\x99s own mindset. A reasonable investor may, depending on the circumstances, understand an opinion statement to convey facts about the speaker\xe2\x80\x99s basis for holding that view. Specifically, an issuer\xe2\x80\x99s statement of opinion may fairly imply facts about the inquiry the issuer conducted or the knowledge it had. And if the real facts are otherwise, but not provided, the opinion statement will mislead by omission.

An opinion statement, however, is not misleading simply because the issuer knows, but fails to disclose, some fact cutting the other way. A reasonable investor does not expect that every fact known to an issuer supports its opinion statement. Moreover, whether an omission makes an expression of opinion misleading always depends on context. Reasonable investors understand opinion statements in light of the surrounding text, and \xc2\xa711 creates liability only for the omission of material facts that cannot be squared with a fair reading of the registration statement as a whole. Omnicare\xe2\x80\x99s arguments to the contrary are unavailing. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9319.

(b) Because neither court below considered the Funds\xe2\x80\x99 omissions theory under the right standard, this case is remanded for a determination of whether the Funds have stated a viable omissions claim. On remand, the court must review the Funds\xe2\x80\x99 complaint to determine whether it adequately alleges that Omnicare omitted from the registration statement some specific fact that would have been material to a reasonable investor. If so, the court must decide whether the alleged omission rendered Omnicare\xe2\x80\x99s opinion statements misleading in context. Pp.\xc2\xa019\xe2\x80\x9320.

719 F.\xc2\xa03d 498, vacated and remanded.

OMNICARE, INC., et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. LABORERS DISTRICT COUNCIL CONSTRUCTION INDUSTRY PENSION FUND et\xc2\xa0al.

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

[March 24, 2015]

Justice Kagan delivered the opinion of the Court.

Before a company may sell securities in interstate commerce, it must file a registration statement with the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC). If that document either \xe2\x80\x9ccontain[s] an untrue statement of a material fact\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9comit[s] to state a material fact .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. necessary to make the statements therein not misleading,\xe2\x80\x9d a purchaser of the stock may sue for damages. 15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa777k(a). This case requires us to decide how each of those phrases applies to statements of opinion.

I

The Securities Act of 1933, 48Stat. 74, 15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa777a et\xc2\xa0seq., protects investors by ensuring that companies issuing securities (known as \xe2\x80\x9cissuers\xe2\x80\x9d) make a \xe2\x80\x9cfull and fair disclosure of information\xe2\x80\x9d relevant to a public offering. Pinter v. Dahl, 486 U.\xc2\xa0S. 622, 646 (1988) . The linchpin of the Act is its registration requirement. With limited exceptions not relevant here, an issuer may offer securities to the public only after filing a registration statement. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa777d, 77e. That statement must contain specified information about both the company itself and the security for sale. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa777g, 77aa. Beyond those required disclosures, the issuer may include additional representations of either fact or opinion.

Section 11 of the Act promotes compliance with these disclosure provisions by giving purchasers a right of action against an issuer or designated individuals (directors, partners, underwriters, and so forth) for material misstatements or omissions in registration statements. As relevant here, that section provides:

\xe2\x80\x9cIn case any part of the registration statement, when such part became effective, contained an untrue statement of a material fact or omitted to state a material fact required to be stated therein or necessary to make the statements therein not misleading, any person acquiring such security .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [may] sue.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa777k(a).

Section 11 thus creates two ways to hold issuers liable for the contents of a registration statement\xe2\x80\x94one focusing on what the statement says and the other on what it leaves out. Either way, the buyer need not prove (as he must to establish certain other securities offenses) that the defendant acted with any intent to deceive or defraud. Herman & MacLean v. Huddleston, 459 U.\xc2\xa0S. 375 \xe2\x80\x93382 (1983).

This case arises out of a registration statement that petitioner Omnicare filed in connection with a public offering of common stock. Omnicare is the nation\xe2\x80\x99s largest provider of pharmacy services for residents of nursing homes. Its registration statement contained (along with all mandated disclosures) analysis of the effects of various federal and state laws on its business model, including its acceptance of rebates from pharmaceutical manufacturers. See, e.g., App. 88\xe2\x80\x93107, 132\xe2\x80\x93140, 154\xe2\x80\x93166. Of significance here, two sentences in the registration statement expressed Omnicare\xe2\x80\x99s view of its compliance with legal requirements:

\xe2\x80\x9cWe believe our contract arrangements with other healthcare providers, our pharmaceutical suppliers and our pharmacy practices are in compliance with applicable federal and state laws.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 95.

\xe2\x80\x9cWe believe that our contracts with pharmaceutical manufacturers are legally and economically valid arrangements that bring value to the healthcare system and the patients that we serve.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 137.

Accompanying those legal opinions were some caveats. On the same page as the first statement above, Omnicare mentioned several state-initiated \xe2\x80\x9cenforcement actions against pharmaceutical manufacturers\xe2\x80\x9d for offering payments to pharmacies that dispensed their products; it then cautioned that the laws relating to that practice might \xe2\x80\x9cbe interpreted in the future in a manner inconsistent with our interpretation and application.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 96. And adjacent to the second statement, Omnicare noted that the Federal Government had expressed \xe2\x80\x9csignificant concerns\xe2\x80\x9d about some manufacturers\xe2\x80\x99 rebates to pharmacies and warned that business might suffer \xe2\x80\x9cif these price concessions were no longer provided.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 136\xe2\x80\x93137.

Respondents here, pension funds that purchased Omnicare stock in the public offering (hereinafter Funds), brought suit alleging that the company\xe2\x80\x99s two opinion statements about legal compliance give rise to liability under \xc2\xa711. Citing lawsuits that the Federal Government later pressed against Omnicare, the Funds\xe2\x80\x99 complaint maintained that the company\xe2\x80\x99s receipt of payments from drug manufacturers violated anti-kickback laws. See id., at 181\xe2\x80\x93186, 203\xe2\x80\x93226. Accordingly, the complaint asserted, Omnicare made \xe2\x80\x9cmaterially false\xe2\x80\x9d representations about legal compliance. Id., at 274. And so too, the complaint continued, the company \xe2\x80\x9comitted to state [material] facts necessary\xe2\x80\x9d to make its representations not misleading. Id., at 273. The Funds claimed that none of Omnicare\xe2\x80\x99s officers and directors \xe2\x80\x9cpossessed reasonable grounds\xe2\x80\x9d for thinking that the opinions offered were truthful and complete. Id., at 274. Indeed, the complaint noted that one of Omnicare\xe2\x80\x99s attorneys had warned that a particular contract \xe2\x80\x9ccarrie[d] a heightened risk\xe2\x80\x9d of liability under anti-kickback laws. Id., at 225 (emphasis deleted). At the same time, the Funds made clear that in light of \xc2\xa711\xe2\x80\x99s strict liability standard, they chose to \xe2\x80\x9cexclude and disclaim any allegation that could be construed as alleging fraud or intentional or reckless misconduct.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 273.

The District Court granted Omnicare\xe2\x80\x99s motion to dismiss. See Civ. No. 2006\xe2\x80\x9326 (ED Ky., Feb. 13, 2012), App. to Pet. for Cert. 28a, 38a\xe2\x80\x9340a, 2012 WL 462551, *4\xe2\x80\x93*5. In the court\xe2\x80\x99s view, \xe2\x80\x9cstatements regarding a company\xe2\x80\x99s belief as to its legal compliance are considered \xe2\x80\x98soft\xe2\x80\x99 information\xe2\x80\x9d and are actionable only if those who made them \xe2\x80\x9cknew [they] were untrue at the time.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 38a. The court concluded that the Funds\xe2\x80\x99 complaint failed to meet that standard because it nowhere claimed that \xe2\x80\x9cthe company\xe2\x80\x99s officers knew they were violating the law.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 39a. The Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit reversed. See 719 F.\xc2\xa03d 498 (2013). It acknowledged that the two statements highlighted in the Funds\xe2\x80\x99 complaint expressed Omnicare\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9copinion\xe2\x80\x9d of legal compliance, rather than \xe2\x80\x9chard facts.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 504 (quoting In\xc2\xa0re Sofamor Danek Group Inc., 123 F.\xc2\xa03d 394, 401\xe2\x80\x93402 (CA6 1997)). But even so, the court held, the Funds had to allege only that the stated belief was \xe2\x80\x9cobjectively false\xe2\x80\x9d; they did not need to contend that anyone at Omnicare \xe2\x80\x9cdisbelieved [the opinion] at the time it was expressed.\xe2\x80\x9d 719 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 506 (quoting Fait v. Regions Financial Corp., 655 F.\xc2\xa03d 105, 110 (CA2 2011)).

We granted certiorari, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014), to consider how \xc2\xa711 pertains to statements of opinion. We do so in two steps, corresponding to the two parts of \xc2\xa711 and the two theories in the Funds\xe2\x80\x99 complaint. We initially address the Funds\xe2\x80\x99 claim that Omnicare made \xe2\x80\x9cuntrue statement[s] of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. material fact\xe2\x80\x9d in offering its views on legal compliance. \xc2\xa777k(a); see App. 273\xe2\x80\x93274. We then take up the Funds\xe2\x80\x99 argument that Omnicare \xe2\x80\x9comitted to state a material fact .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. necessary to make the statements [in its registration filing] not misleading.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa777k(a); see App. 273\xe2\x80\x93274. Unlike both courts below, we see those allegations as presenting different issues.[1] In resolving the first, we discuss when an opinion itself constitutes a factual misstatement. In analyzing the second, we address when an opinion may be rendered misleading by the omission of discrete factual representations. Because we find that the Court of Appeals applied the wrong standard, we vacate its decision.

II

The Sixth Circuit held, and the Funds now urge, that a statement of opinion that is ultimately found incorrect\xe2\x80\x94even if believed at the time made\xe2\x80\x94may count as an \xe2\x80\x9cuntrue statement of a material fact.\xe2\x80\x9d 15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C \xc2\xa777k(a); see 719 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 505; Brief for Respondents 20\xe2\x80\x9326. As the Funds put the point, a statement of belief may make an implicit assertion about the belief\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csubject matter\xe2\x80\x9d: To say \xe2\x80\x9cwe believe X is true\xe2\x80\x9d is often to indicate that \xe2\x80\x9cX is in fact true.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 23; see Tr. of Oral Arg. 36. In just that way, the Funds conclude, an issuer\xe2\x80\x99s statement that \xe2\x80\x9cwe believe we are following the law\xe2\x80\x9d conveys that \xe2\x80\x9cwe in fact are following the law\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94which is \xe2\x80\x9cmaterially false,\xe2\x80\x9d no matter what the issuer thinks, if instead it is violating an anti-kickback statute. Brief for Respondents 1.

But that argument wrongly conflates facts and opinions. A fact is \xe2\x80\x9ca thing done or existing\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n actual happening.\xe2\x80\x9d Webster\xe2\x80\x99s New International Dictionary 782 (1927). An opinion is \xe2\x80\x9ca belief[,] a view,\xe2\x80\x9d or a \xe2\x80\x9csentiment which the mind forms of persons or things.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1509. Most important, a statement of fact (\xe2\x80\x9cthe coffee is hot\xe2\x80\x9d) expresses certainty about a thing, whereas a statement of opinion (\xe2\x80\x9cI think the coffee is hot\xe2\x80\x9d) does not. See ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9cAn opinion, in ordinary usage .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. does not imply .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. definiteness .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. or certainty\xe2\x80\x9d); 7 Oxford English Dictionary 151 (1933) (an opinion \xe2\x80\x9crests[s] on grounds insufficient for complete demonstration\xe2\x80\x9d). Indeed, that difference between the two is so ingrained in our everyday ways of speaking and thinking as to make resort to old dictionaries seem a mite silly. And Congress effectively incorporated just that distinction in \xc2\xa711\xe2\x80\x99s first part by exposing issuers to liability not for \xe2\x80\x9cuntrue statement[s]\xe2\x80\x9d full stop (which would have included ones of opinion), but only for \xe2\x80\x9cuntrue statement[s] of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. fact.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa777k(a) (emphasis added).

Consider that statutory phrase\xe2\x80\x99s application to two hypothetical statements, couched in ways the Funds claim are equivalent. A company\xe2\x80\x99s CEO states: \xe2\x80\x9cThe TVs we manufacture have the highest resolution available on the market.\xe2\x80\x9d Or, alternatively, the CEO transforms that factual statement into one of opinion: \xe2\x80\x9cI believe\xe2\x80\x9d (or \xe2\x80\x9cI think\xe2\x80\x9d) \xe2\x80\x9cthe TVs we manufacture have the highest resolution available on the market.\xe2\x80\x9d The first version would be an untrue statement of fact if a competitor had introduced a higher resolution TV a month before\xe2\x80\x94even assuming the CEO had not yet learned of the new product. The CEO\xe2\x80\x99s assertion, after all, is not mere puffery, but a determinate, verifiable statement about her company\xe2\x80\x99s TVs; and the CEO, however innocently, got the facts wrong. But in the same set of circumstances, the second version would remain true. Just as she said, the CEO really did believe, when she made the statement, that her company\xe2\x80\x99s TVs had the sharpest picture around. And although a plaintiff could later prove that opinion erroneous, the words \xe2\x80\x9cI believe\xe2\x80\x9d themselves admitted that possibility, thus precluding liability for an untrue statement of fact. That remains the case if the CEO\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, as here, concerned legal compliance. If, for example, she said, \xe2\x80\x9cI believe our marketing practices are lawful,\xe2\x80\x9d and actually did think that, she could not be liable for a false statement of fact\xe2\x80\x94even if she afterward discovered a longtime violation of law. Once again, the statement would have been true, because all she expressed was a view, not a certainty, about legal compliance.

That still leaves some room for \xc2\xa711\xe2\x80\x99s false-statement provision to apply to expressions of opinion. As even Omnicare acknowledges, every such statement explicitly affirms one fact: that the speaker actually holds the stated belief. See Brief for Petitioners 15\xe2\x80\x9316; W. Keeton, D. Dobbs, R. Keeton, & D. Owen, Prosser and Keeton on the Law of Torts \xc2\xa7109, p. 755 (5th ed. 1984) (Prosser and Keeton) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]n expression of opinion is itself always a statement of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the fact of the belief, the existing state of mind, of the one who asserts it\xe2\x80\x9d). For that reason, the CEO\xe2\x80\x99s statement about product quality (\xe2\x80\x9cI believe our TVs have the highest resolution available on the market\xe2\x80\x9d) would be an untrue statement of fact\xe2\x80\x94namely, the fact of her own belief\xe2\x80\x94if she knew that her company\xe2\x80\x99s TVs only placed second. And so too the statement about legal compliance (\xe2\x80\x9cI believe our marketing practices are lawful\xe2\x80\x9d) would falsely describe her own state of mind if she thought her company was breaking the law. In such cases, \xc2\xa711\xe2\x80\x99s first part would subject the issuer to liability (assuming the misrepresentation were material).[2]

In addition, some sentences that begin with opinion words like \xe2\x80\x9cI believe\xe2\x80\x9d contain embedded statements of fact\xe2\x80\x94as, once again, Omnicare recognizes. See Reply Brief 6. Suppose the CEO in our running hypothetical said: \xe2\x80\x9cI believe our TVs have the highest resolution available because we use a patented technology to which our competitors do not have access.\xe2\x80\x9d That statement may be read to affirm not only the speaker\xe2\x80\x99s state of mind, as described above, but also an underlying fact: that the company uses a patented technology. See Virginia Bankshares, Inc. v. Sandberg, 501 U. S. 1083, 1109 (1991) (Scalia, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment) (showing that a statement can sometimes be \xe2\x80\x9cmost fairly read as affirming separately both the fact of the [speaker\xe2\x80\x99s] opinion and the accuracy of the facts\xe2\x80\x9d given to support or explain it (emphasis deleted)). Accordingly, liability under \xc2\xa711\xe2\x80\x99s false-statement provision would follow (once again, assuming materiality) not only if the speaker did not hold the belief she professed but also if the supporting fact she supplied were untrue.

But the Funds cannot avail themselves of either of those ways of demonstrating liability. The two sentences to which the Funds object are pure statements of opinion: To simplify their content only a bit, Omnicare said in each that \xe2\x80\x9cwe believe we are obeying the law.\xe2\x80\x9d And the Funds do not contest that Omnicare\xe2\x80\x99s opinion was honestly held. Recall that their complaint explicitly \xe2\x80\x9cexclude[s] and disclaim[s]\xe2\x80\x9d any allegation sounding in fraud or deception. App. 273. What the Funds instead claim is that Omni-care\xe2\x80\x99s belief turned out to be wrong\xe2\x80\x94that whatever the company thought, it was in fact violating anti-kickback laws. But that allegation alone will not give rise to liability under \xc2\xa711\xe2\x80\x99s first clause because, as we have shown, a sincere statement of pure opinion is not an \xe2\x80\x9cuntrue statement of material fact,\xe2\x80\x9d regardless whether an investor can ultimately prove the belief wrong. That clause, limited as it is to factual statements, does not allow investors to second-guess inherently subjective and uncertain assessments. In other words, the provision is not, as the Court of Appeals and the Funds would have it, an invitation to Monday morning quarterback an issuer\xe2\x80\x99s opinions.

III

A

That conclusion, however, does not end this case because the Funds also rely on \xc2\xa711\xe2\x80\x99s omissions provision, alleging that Omnicare \xe2\x80\x9comitted to state facts necessary\xe2\x80\x9d to make its opinion on legal compliance \xe2\x80\x9cnot misleading.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 273; see \xc2\xa777k(a).[3] As all parties accept, whether a statement is \xe2\x80\x9cmisleading\xe2\x80\x9d depends on the perspective of a reasonable investor: The inquiry (like the one into materiality) is objective. Cf. TSC Industries, Inc. v. Northway, Inc., 426 U.\xc2\xa0S. 438, 445 (1976) (noting that the securities laws care only about the \xe2\x80\x9csignificance of an omitted or misrepresented fact to a reasonable investor\xe2\x80\x9d). We therefore must consider when, if ever, the omission of a fact can make a statement of opinion like Omnicare\xe2\x80\x99s, even if literally accurate, misleading to an ordinary investor.

Omnicare claims that is just not possible. On its view, no reasonable person, in any context, can understand a pure statement of opinion to convey anything more than the speaker\xe2\x80\x99s own mindset. See Reply Brief 5\xe2\x80\x936. As long as an opinion is sincerely held, Omnicare argues, it cannot mislead as to any matter, regardless what related facts the speaker has omitted. Such statements of belief (concludes Omnicare) are thus immune from liability under \xc2\xa711\xe2\x80\x99s second part, just as they are under its first.[4]

That claim has more than a kernel of truth. A reason-able person understands, and takes into account, the differ-ence we have discussed above between a statement of fact and one of opinion. See supra, at 6\xe2\x80\x937. She recognizes the import of words like \xe2\x80\x9cI think\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cI believe,\xe2\x80\x9d and grasps that they convey some lack of certainty as to the statement\xe2\x80\x99s content. See, e.g., Restatement (Second) of Contracts \xc2\xa7168, Comment a, p. 456 (1979) (noting that a statement of opinion \xe2\x80\x9cimplies that [the speaker] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is not certain enough of what he says\xe2\x80\x9d to do without the qualifying language). And that may be especially so when the phrases appear in a registration statement, which the reasonable investor expects has been carefully wordsmithed to comply with the law. When reading such a document, the investor thus distinguishes between the sentences \xe2\x80\x9cwe believe X is true\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cX is true.\xe2\x80\x9d And because she does so, the omission of a fact that merely rebuts the latter statement fails to render the former misleading. In other words, a statement of opinion is not misleading just because external facts show the opinion to be incorrect. Reasonable investors do not understand such statements as guarantees, and \xc2\xa711\xe2\x80\x99s omissions clause therefore does not treat them that way.

But Omnicare takes its point too far, because a reason-able investor may, depending on the circumstances, under-stand an opinion statement to convey facts about how the speaker has formed the opinion\xe2\x80\x94or, otherwise put, about the speaker\xe2\x80\x99s basis for holding that view. And if the real facts are otherwise, but not provided, the opinion statement will mislead its audience. Consider an unadorned statement of opinion about legal compliance: \xe2\x80\x9cWe believe our conduct is lawful.\xe2\x80\x9d If the issuer makes that statement without having consulted a lawyer, it could be misleadingly incomplete. In the context of the securities market, an investor, though recognizing that legal opinions can prove wrong in the end, still likely expects such an assertion to rest on some meaningful legal inquiry\xe2\x80\x94rather than, say, on mere intuition, however sincere.[5] Similarly, if the issuer made the statement in the face of its lawyers\xe2\x80\x99 contrary advice, or with knowledge that the Federal Government was taking the opposite view, the investor again has cause to complain: He expects not just that the issuer believes the opinion (however irrationally), but that it fairly aligns with the information in the issuer\xe2\x80\x99s possession at the time.[6] Thus, if a registration statement omits material facts about the issuer\xe2\x80\x99s inquiry into or knowledge concerning a statement of opinion, and if those factsconflict with what a reasonable investor would take from the statement itself, then \xc2\xa711\xe2\x80\x99s omissions clause creates liability.[7]

An opinion statement, however, is not necessarily misleading when an issuer knows, but fails to disclose, some fact cutting the other way. Reasonable investors understand that opinions sometimes rest on a weighing of competing facts; indeed, the presence of such facts is one reason why an issuer may frame a statement as an opinion, thus conveying uncertainty. See supra, at 6\xe2\x80\x937, 11. Suppose, for example, that in stating an opinion about legal compliance, the issuer did not disclose that a single junior attorney expressed doubts about a practice\xe2\x80\x99s legal-ity, when six of his more senior colleagues gave a stamp of approval. That omission would not make the statement of opinion misleading, even if the minority position ulti-mately proved correct: A reasonable investor does not expect that every fact known to an issuer supports its opinion statement.[8]

Moreover, whether an omission makes an expression of opinion misleading always depends on context. Registration statements as a class are formal documents, filed with the SEC as a legal prerequisite for selling securities to the public. Investors do not, and are right not to, expect opinions contained in those statements to reflect baseless, off-the-cuff judgments, of the kind that an individual might communicate in daily life. At the same time, an investor reads each statement within such a document, whether of fact or of opinion, in light of all its surrounding text, including hedges, disclaimers, and apparently conflicting information. And the investor takes into account the customs and practices of the relevant industry. So an omission that renders misleading a statement of opinion when viewed in a vacuum may not do so once that statement is considered, as is appropriate, in a broader frame. The reasonable investor understands a statement of opinion in its full context, and \xc2\xa711 creates liability only for the omission of material facts that cannot be squared with such a fair reading.

These principles are not unique to \xc2\xa711: They inhere, too, in much common law respecting the tort of misrepresentation.[9] The Restatement of Torts, for example, recognizes that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] statement of opinion as to facts not disclosed and not otherwise known to the recipient may\xe2\x80\x9d in some circumstances reasonably \xe2\x80\x9cbe interpreted by him as an implied statement\xe2\x80\x9d that the speaker \xe2\x80\x9cknows facts sufficient to justify him in forming\xe2\x80\x9d the opinion, or that he at least knows no facts \xe2\x80\x9cincompatible with [the] opinion.\xe2\x80\x9d Restatement (Second) of Torts \xc2\xa7539, p. 85 (1976).[10] When that is so, the Restatement explains, liability may result from omission of facts\xe2\x80\x94for example, the fact that the speaker failed to conduct any investigation\xe2\x80\x94that rebut the recipient\xe2\x80\x99s predictable inference. See id., Comment a, at 86; id., Comment b, at 87. Similarly, the leading treatise in the area explains that \xe2\x80\x9cit has been recognized very often that the expression of an opinion may carry with it an implied assertion, not only that the speaker knows no facts which would preclude such an opinion, but that he does know facts which justify it.\xe2\x80\x9d Prosser and Keeton \xc2\xa7109, at 760. That is especially (and traditionally) the case, the treatise continues, where\xe2\x80\x94as in a registration statement\xe2\x80\x94a speaker \xe2\x80\x9cholds himself out or is understood as having special knowledge of the matter which is not available to the plaintiff.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 760\xe2\x80\x93761 (footnote omitted); see Restatement (Second) of Torts \xc2\xa7539, Comment b, at 86 (noting that omissions relating to an opinion\xe2\x80\x99s basis are \xe2\x80\x9cparticularly\xe2\x80\x9d likely to give rise to liability when the speaker has \xe2\x80\x9cspecial knowledge of facts unknown to the recipient\xe2\x80\x9d); Smith v. Land and House Property Corp., [1884] 28 Ch. D. 7, 15 (App. Cas.) (appeal taken from Eng.) (opinion of Bowen, L.\xc2\xa0J.) (When \xe2\x80\x9cthe facts are not equally known to both sides, then a statement of opinion by the one who knows the facts best .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. impliedly states that [the speaker] knows facts which justify his opinion\xe2\x80\x9d).[11]

And the purpose of \xc2\xa711 supports this understanding of how the omissions clause maps onto opinion statements. Congress adopted \xc2\xa711 to ensure that issuers \xe2\x80\x9ctell[\xc2\xa0] the whole truth\xe2\x80\x9d to investors. H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 85, 73d Cong., 1st Sess., 2 (1933) (quoting President Roosevelt\xe2\x80\x99s message to Congress). For that reason, literal accuracy is not enough: An issuer must as well desist from misleading investors by saying one thing and holding back another. Omnicare would nullify that statutory requirement for all sentences starting with the phrases \xe2\x80\x9cwe believe\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cwe think.\xe2\x80\x9d But those magic words can preface nearly any conclusion, and the resulting statements, as we have shown, remain perfectly capable of misleading investors. See supra, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312. Thus, Omnicare\xe2\x80\x99s view would punch a hole in the statute for half-truths in the form of opinion statements. And the difficulty of showing that such statements are literally false\xe2\x80\x94which requires proving an issuer did not believe them, see supra, at 7\xe2\x80\x938\xe2\x80\x94would make that opening yet more consequential: Were Omni-care right, companies would have virtual carte blanche to assert opinions in registration statements free from worry about \xc2\xa711. That outcome would ill-fit Congress\xe2\x80\x99s decision to establish a strict liability offense promoting \xe2\x80\x9cfull and fair disclosure\xe2\x80\x9d of material information. Pinter, 486 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 646; see supra, at 1\xe2\x80\x932.

Omnicare argues, in response, that applying \xc2\xa711\xe2\x80\x99s omissions clause in the way we have described would have \xe2\x80\x9cadverse policy consequences.\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief 17 (capitalization omitted). According to Omnicare, any inquiry into the issuer\xe2\x80\x99s basis for holding an opinion is \xe2\x80\x9chopelessly amorphous,\xe2\x80\x9d threatening \xe2\x80\x9cunpredictable\xe2\x80\x9d and possibly \xe2\x80\x9cmassive\xe2\x80\x9d liability. Id., at 2; Brief for Petitioners 34, 36. And because that is so, Omnicare claims, many issuers will choose not to disclose opinions at all, thus \xe2\x80\x9cdepriving [investors] of potentially helpful information.\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief 19; see Tr. of Oral Arg. 59\xe2\x80\x9361.

But first, that claim is, just as Omnicare labels it, one of \xe2\x80\x9cpolicy\xe2\x80\x9d; and Congress gets to make policy, not the courts. The decision Congress made, for the reasons we have indicated, was to extend \xc2\xa711 liability to all statements rendered misleading by omission. In doing so, Congress no doubt made \xc2\xa711 less cut-and-dry than a law prohibiting only false factual statements. Section 11\xe2\x80\x99s omissions clause, as applied to statements of both opinion and fact, necessarily brings the reasonable person into the analysis, and asks what she would naturally understand a statement to convey beyond its literal meaning. And for expressions of opinion, that means considering the foundation she would expect an issuer to have before making the statement. See supra, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312. All that, however, is a feature, not a bug, of the omissions provision.

Moreover, Omnicare way overstates both the looseness of the inquiry Congress has mandated and the breadth of liability that approach threatens. As we have explained, an investor cannot state a claim by alleging only that an opinion was wrong; the complaint must as well call into question the issuer\xe2\x80\x99s basis for offering the opinion. See supra, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312. And to do so, the investor cannot just say that the issuer failed to reveal its basis. Section 11\xe2\x80\x99s omissions clause, after all, is not a general disclosure requirement; it affords a cause of action only when an issuer\xe2\x80\x99s failure to include a material fact has rendered a published statement misleading. To press such a claim, an investor must allege that kind of omission\xe2\x80\x94and not merely by means of conclusory assertions. See Ashcroft v. Iqbal, 556 U. S. 662, 678 (2009) (\xe2\x80\x9cThreadbare recitals of the elements of a cause of action, supported by mere conclusory statements, do not suffice\xe2\x80\x9d). To be specific: The investor must identify particular (and material) facts going to the basis for the issuer\xe2\x80\x99s opinion\xe2\x80\x94facts about the inquiry the issuer did or did not conduct or the knowledge it did or did not have\xe2\x80\x94whose omission makes the opinion statement at issue misleading to a reasonable person reading the statement fairly and in context. See supra, at 11\xe2\x80\x9314. That is no small task for an investor.

Nor does the inquiry such a complaint triggers ask anything unusual of courts. Numerous legal rules hinge on what a reasonable person would think or expect. In requiring courts to view statements of opinion from an ordinary investor\xe2\x80\x99s perspective, \xc2\xa711\xe2\x80\x99s omissions clause demands nothing more complicated or unmanageable. Indeed, courts have for decades engaged in just that inquiry, with no apparent trouble, in applying the common law of misrepresentation. See supra, at 14\xe2\x80\x9315.

Finally, we see no reason to think that liability for misleading opinions will chill disclosures useful to investors. Nothing indicates that \xc2\xa711\xe2\x80\x99s application to misleading factual assertions in registration statements has caused such a problem. And likewise, common-law doctrines of opinion liability have not, so far as anyone knows, deterred merchants in ordinary commercial transactions from asserting helpful opinions about their products. That absence of fallout is unsurprising. Sellers (whether of stock or other items) have strong economic incentives to .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. well, sell (i.e., hawk or peddle). Those market-based forces push back against any inclination to underdisclose. And to avoid exposure for omissions under \xc2\xa711, an issuer need only divulge an opinion\xe2\x80\x99s basis, or else make clear the real tentativeness of its belief. Such ways of conveying opinions so that they do not mislead will keep valuable information flowing. And that is the only kind of information investors need. To the extent our decision today chills misleading opinions, that is all to the good: In enacting \xc2\xa711, Congress worked to ensure better, not just more, information.

B

Our analysis on this score counsels in favor of sending the case back to the lower courts for decision. Neither court below considered the Funds\xe2\x80\x99 omissions theory with the right standard in mind\xe2\x80\x94or indeed, even recognized the distinct statutory questions that theory raises. See supra, at 4\xe2\x80\x935. We therefore follow our ordinary practice of remanding for a determination of whether the Funds have stated a viable omissions claim (or, if not, whether they should have a chance to replead).

In doing so, however, we reemphasize a few crucial points pertinent to the inquiry on remand. Initially, as we have said, the Funds cannot proceed without identifying one or more facts left out of Omnicare\xe2\x80\x99s registration statement. See supra, at 17\xe2\x80\x9318. The Funds\xe2\x80\x99 recitation of the statutory language\xe2\x80\x94that Omnicare \xe2\x80\x9comitted to state facts necessary to make the statements made not misleading\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94is not sufficient; neither is the Funds\xe2\x80\x99 conclusory allegation that Omnicare lacked \xe2\x80\x9creasonable grounds for the belief\xe2\x80\x9d it stated respecting legal compliance. App. 273\xe2\x80\x93274. At oral argument, however, the Funds highlighted another, more specific allegation in their complaint: that an attorney had warned Omnicare that a particular contract \xe2\x80\x9ccarrie[d] a heightened risk\xe2\x80\x9d of legal exposure under anti-kickback laws. Id., at 225 (emphasis omitted); see Tr. of Oral Arg. 42, 49; supra, at 4. On remand, the court must review the Funds\xe2\x80\x99 complaint to determine whether it adequately alleged that Omnicare had omitted that (purported) fact, or any other like it, from the registration statement. And if so, the court must determine whether the omitted fact would have been material to a reasonable investor\xe2\x80\x94i.e., whether \xe2\x80\x9cthere is a substantial likelihood that a reasonable [investor] would consider it important.\xe2\x80\x9d TSC Industries, 426 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 449.

Assuming the Funds clear those hurdles, the court must ask whether the alleged omission rendered Omnicare\xe2\x80\x99s legal compliance opinions misleading in the way described earlier\xe2\x80\x94i.e., because the excluded fact shows that Omnicare lacked the basis for making those statements that a reasonable investor would expect. See supra, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312. Insofar as the omitted fact at issue is the attorney\xe2\x80\x99s warning, that inquiry entails consideration of such matters as the attorney\xe2\x80\x99s status and expertise and other legal information available to Omnicare at the time. See supra, at 13. Further, the analysis of whether Omnicare\xe2\x80\x99s opinion is misleading must address the statement\xe2\x80\x99s context. See supra, at 14. That means the court must take account of whatever facts Omnicare did provide about legal compliance, as well as any other hedges, disclaimers, or qualifications it included in its registration statement. The court should consider, for example, the information Omnicare offered that States had initiated enforcement actions against drug manufacturers for giving rebates to pharmacies, that the Federal Government had expressed concerns about the practice, and that the relevant laws \xe2\x80\x9ccould \xe2\x80\x9cbe interpreted in the future in a manner\xe2\x80\x9d that would harm Omnicare\xe2\x80\x99s business. See App. 95\xe2\x80\x9396, 136\xe2\x80\x93137; supra, at 3.

With these instructions and for the reasons stated, we vacate the judgment below and remand the case for further proceedings.

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

[March 24, 2015]

Justice Scalia, concurring in part and concurring in the judgment.

Section 11 of the Securities Act of 1933 imposes liability where a registration statement \xe2\x80\x9ccontain[s] an untrue statement of a material fact\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9comit[s] to state a material fact necessary to make the statements therein not misleading.\xe2\x80\x9d 15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa777k(a). I agree with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s discussion of what it means for an expression of opinion to state an untrue material fact. But an expression of opinion implies facts (beyond the fact that the speaker believes his opinion) only where a reasonable listener would understand it to do so. And it is only when expressions of opinion do imply these other facts that they can be \xe2\x80\x9cmisleading\xe2\x80\x9d without the addition of other \xe2\x80\x9cmaterial facts.\xe2\x80\x9d The Court\xe2\x80\x99s view would count far more expressions of opinion to convey collateral facts than I\xe2\x80\x94or the common law\xe2\x80\x94would, and I therefore concur only in part.

The common law recognized that most listeners hear \xe2\x80\x9cI believe,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cin my estimation,\xe2\x80\x9d and other related phrases as disclaiming the assertion of a fact. Hence the (somewhat overbroad) common-law rule that a plaintiff cannot establish a misrepresentation claim \xe2\x80\x9cfor misstatements of opinion, as distinguished from those of fact.\xe2\x80\x9d W. Keeton, D. Dobbs, R. Keeton, & D. Owen, Prosser and Keeton on Torts \xc2\xa7109, p. 755 (5th ed. 1984) (Prosser & Keeton). A fraudulent misrepresentation claim based on an expression of opinion could lie for the one fact the opinion reli-ably conveyed: that the speaker in fact held the stated opinion. Restatement of Torts \xc2\xa7525, Comment c, p. 60 (1938). And, in some circumstances, the common law acknowledged that an expression of opinion reasonably implied \xe2\x80\x9cthat the maker knows of no fact incompatible with his opinion.\xe2\x80\x9d Id. \xc2\xa7539(1), at 91. The no-facts-incompatible-with-the-opinion standard was a demanding one; it meant that a speaker\xe2\x80\x99s judgment had to \xe2\x80\x9cvar[y] so far from the truth that no reasonable man in his position could have such an opinion.\xe2\x80\x9d Restatement of Contracts \xc2\xa7474(b), p. 902, and Comment b (1932). But without more, a listener could only reasonably interpret expressions of opinion as conveying this limited assurance of a speaker\xe2\x80\x99s understanding of facts.

In a few areas, the common law recognized the possibility that a listener could reasonably infer from an expression of opinion not only (1) that the speaker sincerely held it, and (2) that the speaker knew of no facts incompatible with the opinion, but also (3) that the speaker had a reasonable basis for holding the opinion. This exceptional recognition occurred only where it was \xe2\x80\x9cvery reasonable or probable\xe2\x80\x9d that a listener should place special confidence in a speaker\xe2\x80\x99s opinion. Prosser & Keeton \xc2\xa7109, at 760\xe2\x80\x93761. This included two main categories, both of which were carve-outs from the general rule that \xe2\x80\x9cthe ordinary man has a reasonable competence to form his own opinion,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cis not justified in relying [on] the .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. opinion\xe2\x80\x9d of another. Restatement of Torts \xc2\xa7542, Comment a, at 95. First, expressions of opinion made in the context of a relationship of trust, such as between doctors and patients. Second, expressions of opinion made by an expert in his capacity as an expert (for example, a jeweler\xe2\x80\x99s statement of opinion about the value of a diamond). These exceptions allowed a listener to deal with those special expressions of opinion as though they were facts. As the leading treatise put it, \xe2\x80\x9cthe ordinary man is free to deal in reliance upon the opinion of an expert jeweler as to the value of a diamond [or] of an attorney upon a point of law.\xe2\x80\x9d Prosser & Keeton \xc2\xa7109, at 761. But what reasonable person would assume that a lawyer\xe2\x80\x99s assessment of a diamond or a jeweler\xe2\x80\x99s opinion on a point of law implied an educated investigation?

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s expansive application of \xc2\xa711\xe2\x80\x99s omissions clause to expressions of opinion produces a far broader field of misrepresentation; in fact, it produces almost the opposite of the common-law rule. The Court holds that a reasonable investor is right to expect a reasonable basis for all opinions in registration statements\xe2\x80\x94for example, the conduct of a \xe2\x80\x9cmeaningful .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. inquiry,\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94unless that is sufficiently disclaimed. Ante, at 12, 14\xe2\x80\x9315, 18\xe2\x80\x9320. Take the Court\xe2\x80\x99s hypothetical opinion regarding legal compliance. When a disclosure statement says \xe2\x80\x9cwe believe our conduct is lawful,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 12, the Court thinks this should be understood to suggest that a lawyer was consulted, since a reasonable investigation on this point would require consulting a lawyer. But this approach is incompatible with the common law, which had no \xe2\x80\x9clegal opinions are different\xe2\x80\x9d exception. See Restatement of Torts \xc2\xa7545, at 102.

It is also incompatible with common sense. It seems to me strange to suggest that a statement of opinion as generic as \xe2\x80\x9cwe believe our conduct is lawful\xe2\x80\x9d conveys the implied assertion of fact \xe2\x80\x9cwe have conducted a meaningful legal investigation before espousing this opinion.\xe2\x80\x9d It is strange to ignore the reality that a director might rely on industry practice, prior experience, or advice from regulators\xe2\x80\x94rather than a meaningful legal investigation\xe2\x80\x94in concluding the firm\xe2\x80\x99s conduct is lawful. The effect of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s rule is to adopt a presumption of expertise on all topics volunteered within a registration statement.

It is reasonable enough to adopt such a presumption for those matters that are required to be set forth in a registration statement. Those are matters on which the management of a corporation are experts. If, for example, the registration statement said \xe2\x80\x9cwe believe that the corporation has $5,000,000 cash on hand,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cwe believe the corporation has 7,500 shares of common stock outstanding,\xe2\x80\x9d the public is entitled to assume that the management has done the necessary research, so that the asserted \xe2\x80\x9cbelief\xe2\x80\x9d is undoubtedly correct. But of course a registration statement would never preface such items, within the expertise of the management, with a \xe2\x80\x9cwe believe that.\xe2\x80\x9d Full compliance with the law, however, is another matter. It is not specifically required to be set forth in the statement, and when management prefaces that volunteered information with a \xe2\x80\x9cwe believe that,\xe2\x80\x9d it flags the fact that this is not within our area of expertise, but we think we are in compliance.

Moreover, even if one assumes that a corporation issuing a registration statement is (by operation of law) an \xe2\x80\x9cexpert\xe2\x80\x9d with regard to all matters stated or opined about, I would still not agree with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s disposition. The Court says the following:

\xe2\x80\x9cSection 11\xe2\x80\x99s omissions clause, as applied to statements of both opinion and fact, necessarily brings the reason-able person into the analysis, and asks what she would naturally understand a statement to convey beyond its literal meaning. And for expressions of opinion, that means considering the foundation she would expect an issuer to have before making the statement.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 17 (emphasis added).

The first sentence is true enough\xe2\x80\x94but \xe2\x80\x9cwhat she [the reasonable (female) person, and even he, the reasonable (male) person] would naturally understand a statement [of opinion] to convey\xe2\x80\x9d is not that the statement has the foundation she (the reasonable female person) considers adequate. She is not an expert, and is relying on the advice of an expert\xe2\x80\x94who ought to know how much \xe2\x80\x9cfoundation\xe2\x80\x9d is needed. She would naturally understand that the expert has conducted an investigation that he (or she or it) considered adequate. That is what relying upon the opinion of an expert means.

The common law understood this distinction. An action for fraudulent misrepresentation based on an opinion of an expert[1]* was only allowed when the expression of the opinion conveyed a fact\xe2\x80\x94the \xe2\x80\x9cfact\xe2\x80\x9d that summarized the expert\xe2\x80\x99s knowledge. Prosser and Keeton \xc2\xa7109, at 761. And a fact was actionable only if the speaker knew it was false, if he knew he did not know it, or if he knew the listener would understand the statement to have a basis that the speaker knew was not true. Restatement of Torts \xc2\xa7526, at 63\xe2\x80\x9364. Ah!, the majority might say, so a speaker is liable for knowing he lacks the listener\xe2\x80\x99s reasonable basis! If the speaker knows\xe2\x80\x94is actually aware\xe2\x80\x94that the listener will understand an expression of opinion to have a specific basis that it does not have, then of course he satisfies this element of the tort.

But more often, when any basis is implied at all, both sides will understand that the speaker implied a \xe2\x80\x9creason-able basis,\xe2\x80\x9d but honestly disagree on what that means. And the common law supplied a solution for this: A speaker was liable for ambiguous statements\xe2\x80\x94misunderstandings\xe2\x80\x94as fraudulent misrepresentations only where he both knew of the ambiguity and intended that the listener fall prey to it. Id. \xc2\xa7527, at 66. In other words, even assuming both parties knew (a prerequisite to liability) that the expression of opinion implied a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable investigation,\xe2\x80\x9d if the speaker and listener honestly disagreed on the nature of that investigation, the speaker was not liable for a fraudulent misrepresentation unless he subjectively intended the deception. And so in no circumstance would the listener\xe2\x80\x99s belief of a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable basis\xe2\x80\x9d control: If the speaker subjectively believes he lacks a reasonable basis, then his statement is simply a knowing misrepresentation. Id. \xc2\xa7526(a), at 63. If he does not know of the ambiguity, or knows of it, but does not intend to deceive, he is not liable. Id. \xc2\xa7527, at 66. That his basis for belief was \xe2\x80\x9cobjectively unreasonable\xe2\x80\x9d does not impart liability, so long as the belief was genuine.

This aligns with common sense. When a client receives advice from his lawyer, it is surely implicit in that advice that the lawyer has conducted a reasonable investigation\xe2\x80\x94reasonable, that is, in the lawyer\xe2\x80\x99s estimation. The client is relying on the expert lawyer\xe2\x80\x99s judgment for the amount of investigation necessary, no less than for the legal conclusion. To be sure, if the lawyer conducts an investigation that he does not believe is adequate, he would be liable for misrepresentation. And if he conducts an investigation that he believes is adequate but is objectively unreasonable (and reaches an incorrect result), he may be liable for malpractice. But on the latter premise he is not liable for misrepresentation; all that was implicit in his advice was that he had conducted an investigation he deemed adequate. To rely on an expert\xe2\x80\x99s opinion is to rely on the expert\xe2\x80\x99s evaluation of how much time to spend on the question at hand.

The objective test proposed by the Court\xe2\x80\x94inconsistent with the common law and common intuitions about statements of opinion\xe2\x80\x94invites roundabout attacks upon expressions of opinion. Litigants seeking recompense for a corporation\xe2\x80\x99s expression of belief that turned out, after the fact, to be incorrect can always charge that even though the belief rested upon an investigation the corporation thought to be adequate, the investigation was not \xe2\x80\x9cobjectively adequate.\xe2\x80\x9d

Nor is this objective test justified by \xc2\xa711\xe2\x80\x99s absence of a mens rea requirement, as the Court suggests. Ante, at 15 n.10. Some of my citation of the common law is meant to illustrate when a statement of opinion contains an implied warranty of reasonable basis. But when it does so, the question then becomes whose reasonable basis. My illustration of the common-law requirements for misrepresentation is meant to show that a typical listener assumes that the speaker\xe2\x80\x99s reasonable basis controls. That show-ing is not contradicted by \xc2\xa711\xe2\x80\x99s absence of a mens rea requirement.

Not to worry, says the Court. Sellers of securities need \xe2\x80\x9conly divulge an opinion\xe2\x80\x99s basis, or else make clear the real tentativeness of [their] belief[s].\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 18. One wonders what the function of \xe2\x80\x9cin my estimation\xe2\x80\x9d is, then, except as divulging such hesitation. Or what would be sufficient for the Court. \xe2\x80\x9cIn my highly tentative estimation?\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cIn my estimation that, consistent with Omnicare, should be understood as an opinion only?\xe2\x80\x9d Reasonable speakers do not speak this way, and reasonable listeners do not receive opinions this way. When an expert expresses an opinion instead of stating a fact, it implies (1) thathe genuinely believes the opinion, (2) that he believes his basis for the opinion is sufficient, and (most important) (3) that he is not certain of his result. Nothing more. This approach would have given lower courts and investors far more guidance and would largely have avoided the Funds\xe2\x80\x99 attack upon Omnicare\xe2\x80\x99s opinions as though Omnicare held those opinions out to be facts.

I therefore concur only in part and in the judgment.

OMNICARE, INC., et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. LABORERS DISTRICT COUNCIL CONSTRUCTION INDUSTRY PENSION FUND et\xc2\xa0al.

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

[March 24, 2015]

Justice Thomas, concurring in the judgment.

I agree with the Court that the statements of opinion at issue in this case do not contain an untrue statement of a material fact. 15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa777k(a); ante, at 6\xe2\x80\x939. I write separately because I do not think it advisable to opine, as the majority does, on an additional theory of liability that is not properly before us.

The question whether and under what circumstances an omission may make a statement of opinion misleading is one that we should have left to the lower courts to decide on remand. As the majority acknowledges, that question was never passed on below. See ante, at 19. With good reason: Apart from a few conclusory allegations in their complaint and some pro\xc2\xa0forma references to \xe2\x80\x9cmisleading statements and omissions\xe2\x80\x9d in their briefs, respondents did not elaborate on the omissions theory of liability before either the District Court or the Court of Appeals. They certainly did not articulate the theory the majority now adopts until they filed their merits brief before this Court. And it was not until oral argument that they identified a factual allegation in their complaint that might serve to state a claim under that theory. See ante, at 19\xe2\x80\x9320. This delay is unsurprising given that, although various Courts of Appeals have discussed the theory, they have been reluctant to commit to it. See MHC Mut. Conversion Fund, L. P. v. Sandler O\xe2\x80\x99Neill & Partners, L. P., 761 F.\xc2\xa03d 1109, 1116 (CA10 2014) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is difficult to find many [courts] actually holding a security issuer liable on this basis, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the approach has been questioned by others on various grounds\xe2\x80\x9d); see also ibid., n. 5.

We should exercise the same caution. This Court rarely prides itself on being a pioneer of novel legal claims, as \xe2\x80\x9c[o]urs is a court of final review and not first view.\xe2\x80\x9d Zivotofsky v. Clinton, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 12) (internal quotation marks omitted). Thus, as a general rule, \xe2\x80\x9cwe do not decide in the first instance issues not decided below.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted). This includes fashioning innovative theories of liability as much as it includes applying those theories to the circumstances of the case.

The Court has previously relied on a lower court\xe2\x80\x99s failure to address an issue below as a reason for declining to address it here, even when the question was fairly presented in the petition and fully vetted by other lower courts. See, e.g., CSX Transp., Inc. v. Alabama Dept. of Revenue, 562 U.\xc2\xa0S. 277, 284, n. 5 (2011) ; see also id., at 303, n.\xc2\xa03 (Thomas, J., dissenting). Surely the feature that distinguishes this case\xe2\x80\x94a novel legal theory that is not fairly included in the question presented\xe2\x80\x94counsels more strongly in favor of avoidance.

As Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s concurrence reveals, the scope of this theory of liability is far from certain. And the highly fact-intensive nature of the omissions theory provides an additional reason not to address it at this time. The majority acknowledges that the facts a reasonable investor may infer from a statement of opinion depend on the context. And yet it opines about certain facts an investor may infer from an issuer\xe2\x80\x99s legal compliance opinion: that such an opinion is based on legal advice, for example, or that it is not contradicted by the Federal Government. See ante, at 12. These inferences may seem sensible enough in a vacuum, but lower courts would do well to heed the majority\xe2\x80\x99s admonition that every statement of opinion must be considered \xe2\x80\x9cin a broader frame,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 14, taking into account all the facts of the statement and its context. Would that the majority had waited for the \xe2\x80\x9cbroader frame\xe2\x80\x9d of an actual case before weighing in on the omissions theory.
