\n\t\t\t\tSTANDARD FIRE INSURANCE CO. v. KNOWLES\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eighth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 11\xe2\x80\x931450.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 7, 2013\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 19, 2013\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe Class Action Fairness Act of 2005 (CAFA) gives federal district courts original jurisdiction over class actions in which, among other things, the matter in controversy exceeds $5 million in sum or value, 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71332(d)(2), (5), and provides that to determine whether a matter exceeds that amount the \xe2\x80\x9cclaims of the individual class members must be aggregated,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71332(d)(6). When respondent Knowles filed a proposed class action in Arkansas state court against petitioner Standard Fire Insurance Company, he stipulated that he and the class would seek less than $5 million in damages. Pointing to CAFA, petitioner removed the case to the Federal District Court, but it remanded to the state court, concluding that the amount in controversy fell below the CAFA threshold in light of Knowles\xe2\x80\x99 stipulation, even though it found that the amount would have fallen above the threshold absent the stipulation. The Eighth Circuit declined to hear petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s appeal.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0Knowles\xe2\x80\x99 stipulation does not defeat federal jurisdiction under CAFA. Pp. 3\xe2\x88\x927.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0Here, the precertification stipulation can tie Knowles\xe2\x80\x99 hands because stipulations are binding on the party who makes them, see Christian Legal Soc. Chapter of Univ. of Cal., Hastings College of Law v. Martinez, 561 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___. However, the stipulation does not speak for those Knowles purports to represent, for a plaintiff who files a proposed class action cannot legally bind members of the proposed class before the class is certified. See Smith v. Bayer Corp., 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. Because Knowles lacked authority to concede the amount in controversy for absent class members, the District Court wrongly concluded that his stipulation could overcome its finding that the CAFA jurisdictional threshold had been met. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x88\x924.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0Knowles concedes that federal jurisdiction cannot be based on contingent future events. Yet, because a stipulation must be binding and a named plaintiff cannot bind precertification class members, the amount he stipulated is in effect contingent. CAFA does not forbid a federal court to consider the possibility that a nonbinding, amount-limiting, stipulation may not survive the class certification process. To hold otherwise would, for CAFA jurisdictional purposes, treat a nonbinding stipulation as if it were binding, exalt form over substance, and run counter to CAFA\xe2\x80\x99s objective: ensuring \xe2\x80\x9cFederal court consideration of interstate cases of national importance.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72(b)(2), \n\n119Stat.\n5.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt may be simpler for a federal district court to value the amount in controversy on the basis of a stipulation, but ignoring a nonbinding stipulation merely requires the federal judge to do what she must do in cases with no stipulation: aggregate the individual class members\xe2\x80\x99 claims. While individual plaintiffs may avoid removal to federal court by stipulating to amounts that fall below the federal jurisdictional threshold, the key characteristic of such stipulations\xe2\x80\x94missing here\xe2\x80\x94is that they are legally binding on all plaintiffs. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x88\x927.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tVacated and remanded.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tTHE STANDARD FIRE INSURANCE COMPANY, PETITIONER v. GREG KNOWLES\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\ton writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eighth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t[March 19, 2013]\n\t\t\t

\n

\n

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Breyer delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Class Action Fairness Act of 2005 (CAFA) provides that the federal \xe2\x80\x9cdistrict courts shall have original jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d over a civil \xe2\x80\x9cclass action\xe2\x80\x9d if, among other things, the \xe2\x80\x9cmatter in controversy exceeds the sum or value of $5,000,000.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71332(d)(2), (5). The statute adds that \xe2\x80\x9cto determine whether the matter in controversy exceeds the sum or value of $5,000,000,\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9cclaims of the individual class members shall be aggregated.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71332(d)(6).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe question presented concerns a class-action plaintiff who stipulates, prior to certification of the class, that he, and the class he seeks to represent, will not seek damages that exceed $5 million in total. Does that stipulation remove the case from CAFA\xe2\x80\x99s scope? In our view, it does not.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn April 2011 respondent, Greg Knowles, filed this proposed class action in an Arkansas state court against petitioner, the Standard Fire Insurance Company. Knowles claimed that, when the company had made certain homeowner\xe2\x80\x99s insurance loss payments, it had un-lawfully failed to include a general contractor fee. And Knowles sought to certify a class of \xe2\x80\x9chundreds, and pos-sibly thousands\xe2\x80\x9d of similarly harmed Arkansas policyholders. App. to Pet. for Cert. 66. In describing the relief sought, the complaint says that the \xe2\x80\x9cPlaintiff and Class stipulate they will seek to recover total aggregate damages of less than five million dollars.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 60. An attached affidavit stipulates that Knowles \xe2\x80\x9cwill not at any time during this case .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. seek damages for the class .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in excess of $5,000,000 in the aggregate.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 75.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOn May 18, 2011, the company, pointing to CAFA\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdictional provision, removed the case to Federal District Court. See \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71332(d); \xc2\xa71453. Knowles argued for remand on the ground that the District Court lacked jurisdiction. He claimed that the \xe2\x80\x9csum or value\xe2\x80\x9d of the \xe2\x80\x9camount in controversy\xe2\x80\x9d fell beneath the $5 million threshold. App. to Pet. for Cert. 2. On the basis of evidence presented by the company, the District Court found that that the \xe2\x80\x9csum or value\xe2\x80\x9d of the \xe2\x80\x9camount in contro-versy\xe2\x80\x9d would, in the absence of the stipulation, have fallen just above the $5 million threshold. Id., at 2, 8. Nonetheless, in light of Knowles\xe2\x80\x99 stipulation, the court concluded that the amount fell beneath the threshold. The court con-sequently ordered the case remanded to the state court. Id., at 15.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe company appealed from the remand order, but the Eighth Circuit declined to hear the appeal. Id., at 1. See \n\n28 U. S. C. \xc2\xa71453(c)(1) (2006 ed., Supp. V) (providing discretion to hear an appeal from a remand order). The company petitioned for a writ of certiorari. And, in light of divergent views in the lower courts, we granted the writ. Compare Frederick v. Hartford Underwriters Ins. Co., 683 F. 3d 1242, 1247 (CA10 2012) (a proposed class-action representative\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cattempt to limit damages in the complaint is not dispositive when determining the amount in controversy\xe2\x80\x9d); with Rolwing v. Nestle Holdings, Inc., 666 F. 3d 1069, 1072 (CA8 2012) (a precertification \xe2\x80\x9cbinding stipulation limiting damages sought to an amount not exceeding $5 million can be used to defeat CAFA jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCAFA provides the federal district courts with \xe2\x80\x9coriginal jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d to hear a \xe2\x80\x9cclass action\xe2\x80\x9d if the class has more than 100 members, the parties are minimally diverse, and the \xe2\x80\x9cmatter in controversy exceeds the sum or value of $5,000,000.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71332(d)(2), (5)(B). To \xe2\x80\x9cdetermine whether the matter in controversy\xe2\x80\x9d exceeds that sum, \xe2\x80\x9cthe claims of the individual class members shall be aggregated.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71332(d)(6). And those \xe2\x80\x9cclass members\xe2\x80\x9d include \xe2\x80\x9cpersons (named or unnamed) who fall within the definition of the proposed or certified class.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71332(d) (1)(D) (emphasis added).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs applied here, the statute tells the District Court to determine whether it has jurisdiction by adding up the value of the claim of each person who falls within the definition of Knowles\xe2\x80\x99 proposed class and determine whether the resulting sum exceeds $5 million. If so, there is jurisdiction and the court may proceed with the case. The District Court in this case found that resulting sum would have exceeded $5 million but for the stipulation. And we must decide whether the stipulation makes a critical difference.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn our view, it does not. Our reason is a simple one: Stipulations must be binding. See 9 J. Wigmore, Evidence \xc2\xa72588, p.\xc2\xa0821 (J. Chadbourn rev. 1981) (defining a \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial admission or stipulation\xe2\x80\x9d as an \xe2\x80\x9cexpress waiver made .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. by the party or his attorney conceding for the purposes of the trial the truth of some alleged fact\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis deleted)); Christian Legal Soc. Chapter of Univ. of Cal., Hast- ings College of Law v. Martinez, 561 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 10) (describing a stipulation as \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98binding and conclusive\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98not subject to subsequent variation\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting 83 C.\xc2\xa0J.\xc2\xa0S., Stipulations \xc2\xa793 (2000))); 9 Wigmore, supra, \xc2\xa72590, at 822 (the \xe2\x80\x9cvital feature\xe2\x80\x9d of a judicial admission is \xe2\x80\x9cuniversally conceded to be its conclusiveness upon the party making it\xe2\x80\x9d). The stipulation Knowles prof-fered to the District Court, however, does not speak for those he purports to represent.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThat is because a plaintiff who files a proposed class action cannot legally bind members of the proposed class before the class is certified. See Smith v. Bayer Corp., 564 U. S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 15) (\xe2\x80\x9cNeither a proposed class action nor a rejected class action may bind nonparties\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at ___ (slip op., at 13) (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[A] nonnamed class member is [not] a party to the class-action litigation before the class is certified\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Devlin v. Scardelletti, \n\n536 U. S. 1\n, n.\xc2\xa01 (2002) (Scalia, J., dissenting))); Brief for Respondent 12 (conceding that \xe2\x80\x9ca damages limitation .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. cannot have a binding effect on the merits of absent class members\xe2\x80\x99 claims unless and until the class is certified\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBecause his precertification stipulation does not bind anyone but himself, Knowles has not reduced the value of the putative class members\xe2\x80\x99 claims. For jurisdictional purposes, our inquiry is limited to examining the case \xe2\x80\x9cas of the time it was filed in state court,\xe2\x80\x9d Wisconsin Dept. of Corrections v. Schacht, \n\n524 U. S. 381, \n390 (1998)\n. At that point, Knowles lacked the authority to concede the amount-in-controversy issue for the absent class members. The Federal District Court, therefore, wrongly concluded that Knowles\xe2\x80\x99 precertification stipulation could overcome its finding that the CAFA jurisdictional threshold had been met.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tKnowles concedes that \xe2\x80\x9c[f]ederal jurisdiction cannot be based on contingent future events.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 20. Yet the two legal principles to which we have just referred\xe2\x80\x94that stipulations must be binding and that a named plaintiff cannot bind precertification class members\xe2\x80\x94mean that the amount to which Knowles has stipulated is in effect contingent.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIf, for example, as Knowles\xe2\x80\x99 complaint asserts, \xe2\x80\x9chundreds, and possibly thousands\xe2\x80\x9d of persons in Arkansas have similar claims, App. to Pet. for Cert. 66, and if each of those claims places a significant sum in controversy, the state court might certify the class and permit the case to proceed, but only on the condition that the stipulation be excised. Or a court might find that Knowles is an inadequate representative due to the artificial cap he purports to impose on the class\xe2\x80\x99 recovery. E.g., Back Doctors Ltd. v. Metropolitan Property & Cas. Ins. Co., 637 F.\xc2\xa03d 827, 830\xe2\x80\x93831 (CA7 2011) (noting a class representative\xe2\x80\x99s fiduciary duty not to \xe2\x80\x9cthrow away what could be a major component of the class\xe2\x80\x99s recovery\xe2\x80\x9d). Similarly, another class mem- ber could intervene with an amended complaint (without a stipulation), and the District Court might permit the action to proceed with a new representative. See 5 A. Conte & H. Newberg, Class Actions \xc2\xa716:7, p.\xc2\xa0154 (4th ed. 2002) (\xe2\x80\x9c[M]embers of a class have a right to intervene if their interests are not adequately represented by existing parties\xe2\x80\x9d). Even were these possibilities remote in Knowles\xe2\x80\x99 own case, there is no reason to think them farfetched in other cases where similar stipulations could have more dramatic amount-lowering effects.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe strongest counterargument, we believe, takes a syl-logistic form: First, this complaint contains a presently nonbinding stipulation that the class will seek damages that amount to less than $5 million. Second, if the state court eventually certifies that class, the stipulation will bind those who choose to remain as class members. Third, if the state court eventually insists upon modification of the stipulation (thereby permitting class members to obtain more than $5 million), it will have in effect created a new, different case. Fourth, CAFA, however, permits the federal court to consider only the complaint that the plaintiff has filed, i.e., this complaint, not a new, modified (or amended) complaint that might eventually emerge.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur problem with this argument lies in its conclusion. We do not agree that CAFA forbids the federal court to consider, for purposes of determining the amount in controversy, the very real possibility that a nonbinding, amount-limiting, stipulation may not survive the class certification process. This potential outcome does not re-sult in the creation of a new case not now before the federal court. To hold otherwise would, for CAFA jurisdictional purposes, treat a nonbinding stipulation as if it were binding, exalt form over substance, and run directly counter to CAFA\xe2\x80\x99s primary objective: ensuring \xe2\x80\x9cFederal court consideration of interstate cases of national impor-tance.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72(b)(2), \n\n119Stat.\n5. It would also have the ef- fect of allowing the subdivision of a $100 million action into 21 just-below-$5-million state-court actions simply by including nonbinding stipulations; such an outcome would squarely conflict with the statute\xe2\x80\x99s objective.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe agree with Knowles that a federal district court might find it simpler to value the amount in controversy on the basis of a stipulation than to aggregate the value of the individual claims of all who meet the class description. We also agree that, when judges must decide jurisdictional matters, simplicity is a virtue. See Hertz Corp. v. Friend, \n\n559 U.\xc2\xa0S. 77, \n94 (2010)\n. But to ignore a nonbinding stipulation does no more than require the federal judge to do what she must do in cases without a stipulation and what the statute requires, namely \xe2\x80\x9caggregat[e]\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9cclaims of the individual class members.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71332(d)(6).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tKnowles also points out that federal courts permit individual plaintiffs, who are the masters of their complaints, to avoid removal to federal court, and to obtain a remand to state court, by stipulating to amounts at issue that fall below the federal jurisdictional requirement. That is so. See St. Paul Mercury Indemnity Co. v. Red Cab Co., \n\n303 U.\xc2\xa0S. 283, \n294 (1938)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cIf [a plaintiff] does not desire to try his case in the federal court he may resort to the expedi-ent of suing for less than the jurisdictional amount, and though he would be justly entitled to more, the defendant cannot remove\xe2\x80\x9d). But the key characteristic about those stipulations is that they are legally binding on all plaintiffs. See 14AA C. Wright, A. Miller, & E. Cooper, Fed-eral Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa73702.1, p.\xc2\xa0335 (4th ed. 2011) (federal court, as condition for remand, can insist on a \xe2\x80\x9cbinding affidavit or stipulation that the plaintiff will continue to claim less than the jurisdictional amount\xe2\x80\x9d (em-phasis added)). That essential feature is missing here, as Knowles cannot yet bind the absent class.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tKnowles argues in the alternative that a stipulation is binding to the extent it limits attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees so that the amount in controversy remains below the CAFA threshold. We do not consider this issue because Knowles\xe2\x80\x99 stipulation did not provide for that option.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn sum, the stipulation at issue here can tie Knowles\xe2\x80\x99 hands, but it does not resolve the amount-in-controversy question in light of his inability to bind the rest of the class. For this reason, we believe the District Court, when following the statute to aggregate the proposed class members\xe2\x80\x99 claims, should have ignored that stipulation. Because it did not, we vacate the judgment below and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIt is so ordered.\n\t\t\t
