BETTERMAN v. MONTANA

\n

certiorari to the supreme court of montana

\n

No. 14\xe2\x80\x931457.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 28, 2016\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 19, 2016

\n

Petitioner Brandon Betterman pleaded guilty to bail jumping after failing to appear in court on domestic assault charges. He was then jailed for over 14 months awaiting sentence, in large part due to institutional delay. He was eventually sentenced to seven years\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment, with four of the years suspended. Arguing that the 14-month gap between conviction and sentencing violated his speedy trial right, Betterman appealed, but the Montana Supreme Court affirmed the conviction and sentence, ruling that the\n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Speedy Trial Clause does not apply to postconviction, presentencing delay.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The\n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s speedy trial guarantee does not apply once a defendant has been found guilty at trial or has pleaded guilty to criminal charges. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0Criminal proceedings generally unfold in three discrete phases. First, the State investigates to determine whether to arrest and charge a suspect. Once charged, the suspect is presumed innocent until conviction upon trial or guilty plea. After conviction, the court imposes sentence. There are checks against delay geared to each particular phase. P.\xc2\xa03.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0Statutes of limitations provide the primary protection against delay in the first stage, when the suspect remains at liberty, with the Due Process Clause safeguarding against fundamentally unfair prosecutorial conduct. United States v. Lovasco,\n\n431 U.\xc2\xa0S. 783\n. P.\xc2\xa03.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0The Speedy Trial Clause right attaches when the second phase begins, that is, upon a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s arrest or formal accusation. United States v. Marion,\n\n404 U.\xc2\xa0S. 307\n\xe2\x80\x93321. The right detaches upon conviction, when this second stage ends. Before conviction, the accused is shielded by the presumption of innocence, Reed v. Ross,\n\n468 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n, which the Speedy Trial Clause implements by minimizing the likelihood of lengthy incarceration before trial, lessening the anxiety and concern associated with a public accusation, and limiting the effects of long delay on the accused\xe2\x80\x99s ability to mount a defense, Marion, 404 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 320. The Speedy Trial Clause thus loses force upon conviction.

\n

This reading comports with the historical understanding of the speedy trial right. It \xe2\x80\x9chas its roots at the very foundation of our English law heritage,\xe2\x80\x9d Klopfer v. North Carolina,\n\n386 U.\xc2\xa0S. 213\n, and it was the contemporaneous understanding of the\n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s language that \xe2\x80\x9caccused\xe2\x80\x9d described a status preceding \xe2\x80\x9cconvicted\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ctrial\xe2\x80\x9d meant a discrete episode after which judgment (i.e., sentencing) would follow. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent aligns with the text and history of the Speedy Trial Clause. See Barker v. Wingo,\n\n407 U.\xc2\xa0S. 514\n\xe2\x80\x93533. Just as the right to speedy trial does not arise prearrest, Marion, 404 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 320\xe2\x80\x93322, adverse consequences of postconviction delay are outside the purview of the Speedy Trial Clause. The sole remedy for a violation of the speedy trial right\xe2\x80\x94dismissal of the charges\xe2\x80\x94fits the preconviction focus of the Clause, for it would be an unjustified windfall to remedy sentencing delay by vacating validly obtained convictions. This reading also finds support in the federal Speedy Trial Act of 1974 and numerous state analogs, which impose time limits for charging and trial but say nothing about sentencing. The prevalence of guilty pleas and the resulting scarcity of trials in today\xe2\x80\x99s justice system do not bear on the presumption-of-innocence protection at the heart of the Speedy Trial Clause. Moreover, a central feature of contemporary sentencing\xe2\x80\x94the preparation and review of a presentence investigation report\xe2\x80\x94requires some amount of wholly reasonable presentencing delay. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

(d)\xc2\xa0Although the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s presumption-of-innocence-protective speedy trial right is not engaged in the sentencing phase, statutes and rules offer defendants recourse. Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 32(b)(1), for example, directs courts to \xe2\x80\x9cimpose sentence without unnecessary delay.\xe2\x80\x9d Further, as at the prearrest stage, due process serves as a backstop against exorbitant delay. Because Betterman advanced no due process claim here, however, the Court expresses no opinion on how he might fare under that more pliable standard. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

378 Mont. 182, 342 P.\xc2\xa03d 971, affirmed.

BRANDON THOMAS BETTERMAN, PETITIONER v. MONTANA

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of montana

\n

[May 19, 2016]

\n

\n

Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

The\n \nSixth Amendment to the U.\xc2\xa0S. Constitution provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n all criminal prosecutions, the accused shall enjoy the right to a speedy and public trial, by an impartial jury .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Does the\n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s speedy trial guarantee apply to the sentencing phase of a criminal prosecution? That is the sole question this case presents. We hold that the guarantee protects the accused from arrest or indictment through trial, but does not apply once a defendant has been found guilty at trial or has pleaded guilty to criminal charges. For inordinate delay in sentencing, although the Speedy Trial Clause does not govern, a defendant may have other recourse, including, in appropriate circumstances, tailored relief under the Due Process Clauses of the Fifth and\n \nFourteenth Amendments. Petitioner Brandon Betterman, however, advanced in this Court only a\n \nSixth Amendment speedy trial claim. He did not preserve a due process challenge. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 19. We, therefore, confine this opinion to his\n \nSixth Amendment challenge.

\n

I

\n

Ordered to appear in court on domestic assault charges, Brandon Betterman failed to show up and was therefore charged with bail jumping. 378 Mont. 182, 184, 342 P.\xc2\xa03d 971, 973 (2015). After pleading guilty to the bail-jumping charge, he was jailed for over 14 months awaiting sentence on that conviction. Id., at 184\xe2\x80\x93185, 342 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 973\xe2\x80\x93974. The holdup, in large part, was due to institutional delay: the presentence report took nearly five months to complete; the trial court took several months to deny two presentence motions (one seeking dismissal of the charge on the ground of delay); and the court was slow in setting a sentencing hearing. Id., at 185, 195, 342 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 973\xe2\x80\x93974, 980. Betterman was eventually sentenced to seven years\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment, with four of those years suspended. Id., at 185, 342 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 974.

\n

Arguing that the 14-month gap between conviction and sentencing violated his speedy trial right, Betterman appealed. The Montana Supreme Court affirmed his conviction and sentence, ruling that the\n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Speedy Trial Clause does not apply to postconviction, presentencing delay. Id., at 188\xe2\x80\x93192, 342 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 975\xe2\x80\x93978.

\n

We granted certiorari, 577 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015), to resolve a split among courts over whether the Speedy Trial Clause applies to such delay.[1] Holding that the Clause does not apply to delayed sentencing, we affirm the Montana Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment.

\n

II

\n

Criminal proceedings generally unfold in three discrete phases. First, the State investigates to determine whether to arrest and charge a suspect. Once charged, the suspect stands accused but is presumed innocent until conviction upon trial or guilty plea. After conviction, the court imposes sentence. There are checks against delay throughout this progression, each geared to its particular phase.

\n

In the first stage\xe2\x80\x94before arrest or indictment, when the suspect remains at liberty\xe2\x80\x94statutes of limitations provide the primary protection against delay, with the Due Process Clause as a safeguard against fundamentally unfair prosecutorial conduct. United States v. Lovasco,\n\n431 U.\xc2\xa0S. 783,\n789 (1977)\n; see id., at 795, n.\xc2\xa017 (Due ProcessClause may be violated, for instance, by prosecutorial delay that is \xe2\x80\x9ctactical\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9creckless\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).

\n

The\n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Speedy Trial Clause homes in on the second period: from arrest or indictment through conviction. The constitutional right, our precedent holds, does not attach until this phase begins, that is, when a defendant is arrested or formally accused. United States v. Marion,\n\n404 U.\xc2\xa0S. 307\n\xe2\x80\x93321 (1971). Today we hold that the right detaches upon conviction, when this second stage ends.[2]

\n

Prior to conviction, the accused is shielded by the presumption of innocence, the \xe2\x80\x9cbedrock[,] axiomatic and elementary principle whose enforcement lies at the foundation of the administration of our criminal law.\xe2\x80\x9d Reed v. Ross,\n\n468 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1,\n4 (1984)\n (internal quotation marks omitted). The Speedy Trial Clause implements that presumption by \xe2\x80\x9cprevent[ing] undue and oppressive incarceration prior to trial, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. minimiz[ing] anxiety and concern accompanying public accusation[,] and .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. limit[ing] the possibilities that long delay will impair the ability of an accused to defend himself.\xe2\x80\x9d Marion, 404 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 320 (internal quotation marks omitted). See also Barker v. Wingo,\n\n407 U.\xc2\xa0S. 514\n\xe2\x80\x93533 (1972). As a measure protecting the presumptively innocent, the speedy trial right\xe2\x80\x94like other similarly aimed measures\xe2\x80\x94loses force upon conviction. Compare In re Winship,\n\n397 U.\xc2\xa0S. 358,\n364 (1970)\n (requiring \xe2\x80\x9cproof beyond a reasonable doubt of every fact necessary to constitute the crime\xe2\x80\x9d), with United States v. O\xe2\x80\x99Brien,\n\n560 U.\xc2\xa0S. 218,\n224 (2010)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cSentencing factors .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. can be proved .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. by a preponderance of the evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d). Compare also\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73142(b) (bail presumptively available for accused awaiting trial) with \xc2\xa73143(a) (bail presumptively unavailable for those convicted awaiting sentence).

\n

Our reading comports with the historical understanding. The speedy trial right, we have observed, \xe2\x80\x9chas its roots at the very foundation of our English law heritage. Its first articulation in modern jurisprudence appears to have been made in Magna Carta (1215) .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Klopfer v. North Carolina,\n\n386 U.\xc2\xa0S. 213,\n223 (1967)\n. Regarding the Framers\xe2\x80\x99 comprehension of the right as it existed at the founding, we have cited Sir Edward Coke\xe2\x80\x99s Institutes of the Laws of England. See id., at 223\xe2\x80\x93225, and nn.\xc2\xa08, 12\xe2\x80\x9314, 18. Coke wrote that \xe2\x80\x9cthe innocent shall not be worn and wasted by long imprisonment, but .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. speedily come to his tria[l].\xe2\x80\x9d 1 E. Coke, Second Part of the Institutes of the Laws of England 315 (1797) (emphasis added).

\n

Reflecting the concern that a presumptively innocent person should not languish under an unresolved charge, the Speedy Trial Clause guarantees \xe2\x80\x9cthe accused\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cthe right to a speedy .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. trial.\xe2\x80\x9d U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Amdt. 6 (emphasis added). At the founding, \xe2\x80\x9caccused\xe2\x80\x9d described a status preceding \xe2\x80\x9cconvicted.\xe2\x80\x9d See, e.g., 4 W. Blackstone, Commentaries on the Laws of England 322 (1769) (commenting on process in which \xe2\x80\x9cpersons accused of felony .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. were tried .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and convicted\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). And \xe2\x80\x9ctrial\xe2\x80\x9d meant a discrete episode after which judgment (i.e., sentencing) would follow. See, e.g., id., at 368 (\xe2\x80\x9cWe are now to consider the next stage of criminal prosecution, after trial and conviction are past .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. : which is that of judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d).[3]

\n

This understanding of the\n \nSixth Amendment language\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9caccused\xe2\x80\x9d as distinct from \xe2\x80\x9cconvicted,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ctrial\xe2\x80\x9d as separate from \xe2\x80\x9csentencing\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94endures today. See, e.g., Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 26 (10th ed. 2014) (defining \xe2\x80\x9caccused\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9ca person who has been arrested and brought before a magistrate or who has been formally charged\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); Fed. Rule Crim. Proc. 32 (governing \xe2\x80\x9cSentencing and Judgment,\xe2\x80\x9d the rule appears in the chapter on \xe2\x80\x9cPost-Conviction Procedures,\xe2\x80\x9d which follows immediately after the separate chapter headed \xe2\x80\x9cTrial\xe2\x80\x9d).[4]

\n

This Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent aligns with the text and history of the Speedy Trial Clause. Detaining the accused pretrial, we have said, disadvantages him, and the imposition is \xe2\x80\x9cespecially unfortunate\xe2\x80\x9d as to those \xe2\x80\x9cultimately found to be innocent.\xe2\x80\x9d Barker, 407 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 532\xe2\x80\x93533. And in Marion, 404 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 320, addressing \xe2\x80\x9cthe major evils protected against by the speedy trial guarantee,\xe2\x80\x9d we observed: \xe2\x80\x9cArrest is a public act that may seriously interfere with the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s liberty, whether he is free on bail or not, and that may disrupt his employment, drain his financial resources, curtail his associations, subject him to public obloquy, and create anxiety in him, his family and his friends.\xe2\x80\x9d We acknowledged in Marion that even pre-arrest\xe2\x80\x94a stage at which the right to a speedy trial does not arise\xe2\x80\x94the passage of time \xe2\x80\x9cmay impair memories, cause evidence to be lost, deprive the defendant of witnesses, and otherwise interfere with his ability to defend himself.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 321. Nevertheless, we determined, \xe2\x80\x9cthis possibility of prejudice at trial is not itself sufficient reason to wrench the\n \nSixth Amendment from its proper [arrest or charge triggered] context.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 321\xe2\x80\x93322. Adverse consequences of postconviction delay, though subject to other checks, see infra, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311, are similarly outside the purview of the Speedy Trial Clause.[5]

\n

The sole remedy for a violation of the speedy trial right\xe2\x80\x94dismissal of the charges, see Strunk v. United States,\n\n412 U.\xc2\xa0S. 434,\n440 (1973)\n; Barker, 407 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 522\xe2\x80\x94fits the preconviction focus of the Clause. It would be an unjustified windfall, in most cases, to remedy sentencing delay by vacating validly obtained convictions. Betterman concedes that a dismissal remedy ordinarily would not be in order once a defendant has been convicted. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 5\xe2\x80\x936; cf. Bozza v. United States,\n\n330 U.\xc2\xa0S. 160,\n166 (1947)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]n error in passing the sentence\xe2\x80\x9d does not permit a convicted defendant \xe2\x80\x9cto escape punishment altogether.\xe2\x80\x9d).[6]

\n

The manner in which legislatures have implemented the speedy trial guarantee matches our reading of the Clause. Congress passed the Speedy Trial Act of 1974,\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73161 et\xc2\xa0seq., \xe2\x80\x9cto give effect to the sixth amendment right.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. MacDonald,\n\n456 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n, n.\xc2\xa07 (1982) (quoting S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 93\xe2\x80\x931021, p.\xc2\xa01 (1974)). \xe2\x80\x9cThe more stringent provisions of the Speedy Trial Act have mooted much litigation about the requirements of the Speedy Trial Clause .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Loud Hawk,\n\n474 U.\xc2\xa0S. 302\n, n.\xc2\xa01 (1986) (citation omitted). With certain exceptions, the Act directs\xe2\x80\x94on pain of dismissal of the charges, \xc2\xa73162(a)\xe2\x80\x94that no more than 30 days pass between arrest and indictment, \xc2\xa73161(b), and that no more than 70 days pass between indictment and trial, \xc2\xa73161(c)(1). The Act says nothing, however, about the period between conviction and sentencing, suggesting that Congress did not regard that period as falling within the\n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s compass. Numerous state analogs similarly impose precise time limits for charging and trial; they, too, say nothing about sentencing.[7]

\n

Betterman asks us to take account of the prevalence of guilty pleas and the resulting scarcity of trials in today\xe2\x80\x99s justice system. See Lafler v. Cooper, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 11) (\xe2\x80\x9c[C]riminal justice today is for the most part a system of pleas, not a system of trials.\xe2\x80\x9d). The sentencing hearing has largely replaced the trial as the forum for dispute resolution, Betterman urges. Therefore, he maintains, the concerns supporting the right to a speedy trial now recommend a speedy sentencing hear-ing. The modern reality, however, does not bear on the presumption-of-innocence protection at the heart of the Speedy Trial Clause. And factual disputes, if any there be, at sentencing, do not go to the question of guilt;they are geared, instead, to ascertaining the proper sentence within boundaries set by statutory minimums and maximums.

\n

Moreover, a central feature of contemporary sentencing in both federal and state courts is preparation by the probation office, and review by the parties and the court, of a presentence investigation report. See\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73552; Fed. Rule Crim. Proc. 32(c)\xe2\x80\x93(g); 6 W. LaFave, J. Israel, N. King, & O. Kerr, Criminal Procedure \xc2\xa726.5(b), pp. 1048\xe2\x80\x931049 (4th ed. 2015) (noting reliance on presentence reports in federal and state courts). This aspect of the system requires some amount of wholly reasonable presentencing delay.[8] Indeed, many\xe2\x80\x94if not most\xe2\x80\x94disputes are resolved, not at the hearing itself, but rather through the presentence-report process. See N. Demleitner, D. Berman, M. Miller, & R. Wright, Sentencing Law and Policy 443 (3d ed. 2013) (\xe2\x80\x9cCriminal justice is far more commonly negotiated than adjudicated; defendants and their attorneys often need to be more concerned about the charging and plea bargaining practices of prosecutors and the presentence investigations of probation offices than .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. about the sentencing procedures of judges or juries.\xe2\x80\x9d); cf. Bierschbach & Bibas, Notice-and-Comment Sentencing, 97 Minn. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1, 15 (2012) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]oday\xe2\x80\x99s sentencing hearings .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. rubber-stamp plea-bargained sentences.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

As we have explained, at the third phase of thecriminal-justice process, i.e., between conviction and sentencing, the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s presumption-of-innocence-protective speedy trial right is not engaged.[9] That does not mean, however, that defendants lack any protection against undue delay at this stage. The primary safeguard comes from statutes and rules. The federal rule on point directs the court to \xe2\x80\x9cimpose sentence without unnecessary delay.\xe2\x80\x9d Fed. Rule Crim. Proc. 32(b)(1). Many States have provisions to the same effect,[10] and some States prescribe numerical time limits.[11] Further, as at the prearrest stage, due process serves as a backstop against exorbitant delay. See supra, at 3. After conviction, a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s due process right to liberty, while diminished, is still present. He retains an interest in a sentencing proceeding that is fundamentally fair. But because Betterman advanced no due process claim here, see supra, at 1, we express no opinion on how he might fare under that more pliable standard. See, e.g., United States v. $8,850,\n\n461 U.\xc2\xa0S. 555\n\xe2\x80\x93565 (1983).[12]

\n

The course of a criminal prosecution is composed of discrete segments. During the segment between accusation and conviction, the\n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Speedy Trial Clause protects the presumptively innocent from long enduring unresolved criminal charges. The\n \nSixth Amendment speedy trial right, however, does not extend beyond conviction, which terminates the presumption of innocence. The judgment of the Supreme Court of Montana is therefore

BRANDON THOMAS BETTERMAN, PETITIONER v. MONTANA

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of montana

\n

[May 19, 2016]

\n

\n

Justice Thomas, with whom Justice Alito joins, concurring.

\n

I agree with the Court that the\n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Speedy Trial Clause does not apply to sentencing proceedings, except perhaps to bifurcated sentencing proceedings where sentencing enhancements operate as functional elements of a greater offense. See ante, at 2\xe2\x80\x933, and n.\xc2\xa02. I also agree with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to reserve judgment on whether sentencing delays might violate the Due Process Clause. Ante, at 11. Brandon Betterman\xe2\x80\x99s counsel repeatedly disclaimed that he was raising in this Court a challenge under the Due Process Clause. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 7\xe2\x80\x938 (\xe2\x80\x9cWe haven\xe2\x80\x99t included that. We didn\xe2\x80\x99t include that in the question presented, Your Honor\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 8 (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e are not advancing that claim here\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 19 (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e didn\xe2\x80\x99t preserve a\xe2\x80\x94a due process challenge. Our challenge is solely under the\n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

We have never decided whether the Due Process Clause creates an entitlement to a reasonably prompt sentencing hearing. Today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion leaves us free to decide the proper analytical framework to analyze such claims if and when the issue is properly before us.

\n

Justice Sotomayor suggests that, for such claims, we should adopt the factors announced in Barker v. Wingo,\n\n407 U.\xc2\xa0S. 514\n\xe2\x80\x93533 (1972). Post, at 2 (concurring opinion). I would not prejudge that matter. The factors listed in Barker may not necessarily translate to the delayed sentencing context. The Due Process Clause can be satisfied where a State has adequate procedures to redress an improper deprivation of liberty or property. See Parratt v. Taylor,\n\n451 U.\xc2\xa0S. 527,\n537 (1981)\n. In unusual cases where trial courts fail to sentence a defendant within a reasonable time, a State might fully satisfy due process by making traditional extraordinary legal remedies, such as mandamus, available. Or, much like the federal Speedy Trial Act regulates trials, see\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73161, a State might remedy improper sentencing delay by statute.[1] And a person who sleeps on these remedies, as Betterman did, may simply have no right to complain that his sentencing was delayed. We should await a proper presentation, full briefing, and argument before taking a position on this issue.

\n

The Court thus correctly \xe2\x80\x9cexpress[es] no opinion on how [Betterman] might fare\xe2\x80\x9d under the Due Process Clause. Ante, at 11.

Justice Sotomayor, concurring.

\n

I agree with the Court that petitioner cannot bring a claim under the Speedy Trial Clause for a delay between his guilty plea and his sentencing. As the majority notes, however, a defendant may have \xe2\x80\x9cother recourse\xe2\x80\x9d for such a delay, \xe2\x80\x9cincluding, in appropriate circumstances, tailored relief under the Due Process Clauses of the Fifth and\n \nFourteenth Amendments.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 1. The Court has no reason to consider today the appropriate test for such a Due Process Clause challenge because petitioner has forfeited any such claim. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 19. I write separately to emphasize that the question is an open one.

\n

The Due Process Clause is \xe2\x80\x9cflexible and calls for such procedural protections as the particular situation demands.\xe2\x80\x9d Morrissey v. Brewer,\n\n408 U.\xc2\xa0S. 471,\n481 (1972)\n. This Court thus uses different tests to consider whether different kinds of delay run afoul of the Due Process Clause. In evaluating whether a delay in instituting judicial proceedings following a civil forfeiture violated the Due Process Clause, the Court applied the test from Barker v. Wingo,\n\n407 U.\xc2\xa0S. 514 (1972)\n\xe2\x80\x94the same test that the Court applies to violations of the Speedy Trial Clause. See United States v. $8,850,\n\n461 U.\xc2\xa0S. 555,\n564 (1983)\n. Under the Barker test, courts consider four factors\xe2\x80\x94the length of the delay, the reason for the delay, the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s assertion of his right, and prejudice to the defendant. Ibid. None of the four factors is \xe2\x80\x9ceither necessary or sufficient,\xe2\x80\x9d and no one factor has a \xe2\x80\x9ctalismanic qualit[y].\xe2\x80\x9d Barker, 407 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 533.

\n

The Montana Supreme Court did not use the Barker test in evaluating petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s Due Process Clause claim. 378 Mont. 182, 193\xe2\x80\x93194, 342 P.\xc2\xa03d 971, 979 (2015). But it seems to me that the Barker factors capture many of the concerns posed in the sentencing delay context and that because the Barker test is flexible, it will allow courts to take account of any differences between trial and sentencing delays. See 407 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 531. The majority of the Circuits in fact use the Barker test for that purpose. See United States v. Sanders, 452 F.\xc2\xa03d 572, 577 (CA6 2006) (collecting cases).

\n

In the appropriate case, I would thus consider the correct test for a Due Process Clause delayed sentencing challenge.
