ENVIRONMENTAL PROTECTION AGENCY et\xc2\xa0al. v. EME HOMER CITY GENERATION, L. P., et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the district of columbia circuit

\n

No. 12\xe2\x80\x931182.\xe2\x80\x83Argued December 10, 2013\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 29, 2014[1]

\n

Congress and the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA or Agency) have, over the course of several decades, made many efforts to deal with the complex challenge of curtailing air pollution emitted in upwind States, but causing harm in other, downwind States. As relevant here, the Clean Air Act (CAA or Act) directs EPA to establish national ambient air quality standards (NAAQS) for pollutants at levels that will protect public health. 42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77408, 7409. Once EPA settles on a NAAQS, the Agency must designate \xe2\x80\x9cnonattainment\xe2\x80\x9d areas, i.e., locations where the concentration of a regulated pollutant exceeds the NAAQS. \xc2\xa77407(d). Each State must submit a State Implementation Plan, or SIP, to EPA within three years of any new or revised NAAQS. \xc2\xa77410(a)(1). From the date EPA determines that a State SIP is inadequate, the Agency has two years to promulgate a Federal Implementation Plan, or FIP. \xc2\xa77410(c)(1). Among other components, the CAA mandates SIP compliance with the Good Neighbor Provision, which requires SIPs to \xe2\x80\x9ccontain adequate provisions .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. prohibiting .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any source or other type of emissions activity within the State from emitting any air pollutant in amounts which will .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. contribute significantly to nonattainment in, or interfere with maintenance by, any other State with respect to any .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [NAAQS].\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa77410(a)(2)(D)(i).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Several times over the past two decades, EPA has attempted to delineate the Good Neighbor Provision\xe2\x80\x99s scope by identifying when upwind States \xe2\x80\x9ccontribute significantly\xe2\x80\x9d to nonattainment downwind. The D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit found fault with the Agency\xe2\x80\x99s 2005 attempt, the Clean Air Interstate Rule, or CAIR, which regulated both nitrogen oxide (NOX) and sulfur dioxide (SO2) emissions, the gasses at issue here. The D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit nevertheless left CAIR temporarily in place, while encouraging EPA to act with dispatch in dealing with problems the court had identified. \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n EPA\xe2\x80\x99s response to that decision is the Cross-State Air Pollution Rule (Transport Rule), which curbs NOX and SO2 emissions in 27 upwind States to achieve downwind attainment of three NAAQS. Under the Transport Rule, an upwind State \xe2\x80\x9ccontribute[d] significantly\xe2\x80\x9d to downwind nonattainment to the extent its exported pollution both (1) produced one percent or more of a NAAQS in at least one downwind State and (2) could be eliminated cost-effectively, as determined by EPA. Upwind States are obliged to eliminate only emissions meeting both of these criteria. Through complex modeling, EPA created an annual emissions \xe2\x80\x9cbudget\xe2\x80\x9d for each regulated State upwind, representing the total quantity of pollution an upwind State could produce in a given year under the Transport Rule. Having earlier determined each regulated State\xe2\x80\x99s SIP to be inadequate, EPA, contemporaneous with the Transport Rule, promulgated FIPs allocating each State\xe2\x80\x99s emissions budgets among its in-state pollution sources.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n A group of state and local governments (State respondents), joined by industry and labor groups (Industry respondents), petitioned for review of the Transport Rule in the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit. The court vacated the rule in its entirety, holding that EPA\xe2\x80\x99s actions exceeded the Agency\xe2\x80\x99s statutory authority in two respects. Acknowledging that EPA\xe2\x80\x99s FIP authority is generally triggered when the Agency disapproves a SIP, the court was nevertheless concerned that States would be incapable of fulfilling the Good Neighbor Provision without prior EPA guidance. The court thus concluded that EPA must give States a reasonable opportunity to allocate their emission budgets before issuing FIPs. The court also found the Agency\xe2\x80\x99s two-part interpretation of the Good Neighbor Provision unreasonable, concluding that EPA must disregard costs and consider exclusively each upwind State\xe2\x80\x99s physically proportionate responsibility for air quality problems downwind.

\n

Held:

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n 1.\xc2\xa0The CAA does not command that States be given a second opportunity to file a SIP after EPA has quantified the State\xe2\x80\x99s interstate pollution obligations. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (a)\xc2\xa0The State respondents do not challenge EPA\xe2\x80\x99s disapproval of any particular SIP. Instead, they argue that, notwithstanding these disapprovals, the Agency was still obliged to grant upwind States anadditional opportunity to promulgate adequate SIPs after EPA had set the State\xe2\x80\x99s emission budget. This claim does not turn on the validity of the prior SIP disapprovals, but on whether the CAA requires EPA do more than disapprove a SIP to trigger the Agency\xe2\x80\x99s authority to issue a FIP. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (b)\xc2\xa0The CAA\xe2\x80\x99s plain text supports the Agency: Disapproval of a SIP, without more, triggers EPA\xe2\x80\x99s obligation to issue a FIP. The statute sets precise deadlines for the States and EPA. Once EPA issues any new or revised NAAQS, a State \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d propose a SIP within three years,\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77410(a)(1), and that SIP \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d include, inter alia, provisions adequate to satisfy the Good Neighbor Provision, \xc2\xa77410(a)(2). If the EPA finds a SIP inadequate, the Agency has a statutory duty to issue a FIP \xe2\x80\x9cat any time\xe2\x80\x9d within two years. \xc2\xa77410(c)(1). However sensible the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s exception to this strict time prescription may be, a reviewing court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ctask is to apply the text [of the statute], not to improve upon it.\xe2\x80\x9d Pavelic & LeFlore v. Marvel Entertainment Group, Div. of Cadence Industries Corp.,\n\n493 U.\xc2\xa0S. 120\n. Nothing in the Act differentiates the Good Neighbor Provision from the several other matters a State must address in its SIP. Nor does the Act condition the duty to promulgate a FIP on EPA\xe2\x80\x99s having first quantified an upwind State\xe2\x80\x99s good neighbor obligations. By altering Congress\xe2\x80\x99 SIP and FIP schedule, the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit allowed a delay Congress did not order and placed an information submission obligation on EPA Congress did not impose. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (c)\xc2\xa0The fact that EPA had previously accorded upwind States a chance to allocate emission budgets among their in-state sources does not show that the Agency acted arbitrarily by refraining to do so here. EPA retained discretion to alter its course provided it gave a reasonable explanation for doing so. Motor Vehicle Mfrs. Assn. of United States, Inc. v. State Farm Mut. Automobile Ins. Co.,\n\n463 U.\xc2\xa0S. 29\n. Here, the Agency had been admonished by the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit to act with dispatch in amending or replacing CAIR. Endeavoring to satisfy that directive, EPA acted speedily, issuing FIPs and the Transport Rule contemporaneously. Pp.\xc2\xa017\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n 2.\xc2\xa0EPA\xe2\x80\x99s cost-effective allocation of emission reductions among upwind States is a permissible, workable, and equitable interpretation of the Good Neighbor Provision. Pp.\xc2\xa018\xe2\x80\x9331.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (a)\xc2\xa0Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 attack on EPA\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the Good Neighbor Provision is not foreclosed by \xc2\xa77607(d)(7)(B), which provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[o]nly an objection to a rule .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. raised with reasonable specificity during the period for public comment .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. may be raised during judicial review.\xe2\x80\x9d Even assuming that respondents failed to object to the Transport Rule with \xe2\x80\x9creasonable specificity,\xe2\x80\x9d that lapse is not jurisdictional. Section 7607(d)(7)(B) is a \xe2\x80\x9cmandatory,\xe2\x80\x9d but not\xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional,\xe2\x80\x9d rule, see Arbaugh v. Y & H Corp.,\n\n546 U.\xc2\xa0S. 500\n, which speaks to a party\xe2\x80\x99s procedural obligations, not a court\xe2\x80\x99s authority, see Kontrick v. Ryan,\n\n540 U.\xc2\xa0S. 443\n. Because EPA did not press this argument unequivocally before the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit, it does not pose an impassable hindrance to this Court\xe2\x80\x99s review. Pp.\xc2\xa018\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (b)\xc2\xa0This Court routinely accords dispositive effect to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s reasonable interpretation of ambiguous statutory language. The Good Neighbor Provision delegates authority to EPA at least as certainly as the CAA provisions involved in Chevron U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc.,\n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 837\n. EPA\xe2\x80\x99s authority to reduce upwind pollution extends only to those \xe2\x80\x9camounts\xe2\x80\x9d of pollution that \xe2\x80\x9ccontribute significantly to nonattainment\xe2\x80\x9d in downwind States. \xc2\xa77410(a)(2)(D)(i). Because a downwind State\xe2\x80\x99s excess pollution is often caused by multiple upwind States, however, EPA must address how to allocate responsibility among multiple contributors. The Good Neighbor Provision does not dictate a method of apportionment. Nothing in the provision, for example, directs the proportional allocation method advanced by the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit, a method that works neither mathematically nor in practical application. Under Chevron, Congress\xe2\x80\x99 silence effectively delegates authority to EPA to select from among reasonable options. See United States v. Mead Corp.,\n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 218\n.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n EPA\xe2\x80\x99s chosen allocation method is a \xe2\x80\x9cpermissible construction of the statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Chevron, 467 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 843. The Agency, tasked with choosing which among equal \xe2\x80\x9camounts\xe2\x80\x9d to eliminate, has chosen sensibly to reduce the amount easier, i.e., less costly, to eradicate. The Industry respondents argue that the final calculation cannot rely on costs, but nothing in the Good Neighbor Provision\xe2\x80\x99s text precludes that choice. And using costs in the Transport Rule calculus is an efficient and equitable solution to the allocation problem the Good Neighbor Provision compels the Agency to address. Efficient because EPA can achieve the same levels of attainment, i.e., of emission reductions, the proportional approach aims to achieve, but at a much lower overall cost. Equitable because, by imposing uniform cost thresholds on regulated States, EPA\xe2\x80\x99s rule subjects to stricter regulation those States that have done less in the past to control their pollution. Pp.\xc2\xa020\xe2\x80\x9328.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (c)\xc2\xa0Wholesale invalidation of the Transport Rule is not justified by either of the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s remaining objections: that the Transport Rule leaves open the possibility that a State might be compelled to reduce emissions beyond the point at which every affected downwind State is in attainment, so-called \xe2\x80\x9cover-control\xe2\x80\x9d; and that EPA\xe2\x80\x99s use of costs does not foreclose the possibility that an upwind State would be required to reduce its emissions by so muchthat the State would be placed below the one-percent mark EPA set as the initial threshold of \xe2\x80\x9csignifican[ce].\xe2\x80\x9d First, instances of \xe2\x80\x9cover-control\xe2\x80\x9d in particular downwind locations may be incidental to reductions necessary to ensure attainment elsewhere. As the Good Neighbor Provision seeks attainment in every downwind State, however, exceeding attainment in one State cannot rank as \xe2\x80\x9cover-control\xe2\x80\x9d unless unnecessary to achieving attainment in any downwind State. Second, the EPA must have leeway in fulfilling its statutory mandate to balance the possibilities of over-control and \xe2\x80\x9cunder-control,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., to maximize achievement of attainment downwind. Finally, in a voluminous record, involving thousands of upwind-to-downwind linkages, respondents point to only a few instances of \xe2\x80\x9cunnecessary\xe2\x80\x9d emission reductions, and even those are contested by EPA. Pp.\xc2\xa028\xe2\x80\x9331.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n These cases concern the efforts of Congress and the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA or Agency) to cope with a complex problem: air pollution emitted in one State, but causing harm in other States. Left unregulated, the emitting or upwind State reaps the benefits of the economic activity causing the pollution without bearing all the costs. See Revesz, Federalism and Interstate Environmental Externalities, 144 U.\xc2\xa0Pa. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 2341, 2343 (1996). Conversely, downwind States to which the pollution travels are unable to achieve clean air because of the influx of out-of-state pollution they lack authority to control. See S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 101\xe2\x80\x93228, p. 49 (1989). To tackle the problem, Congress included a Good Neighbor Provision in the Clean Air Act (Act or CAA). That provision, in its current phrasing, instructs States to prohibit in-state sources \xe2\x80\x9cfrom emitting any air pollutant in amounts which will .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. contribute significantly\xe2\x80\x9d to downwind States\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cnonattainment .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. , or interfere with maintenance,\xe2\x80\x9d of any EPA-promulgated national air quality standard.\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77410(a)(2)(D)(i).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Interpreting the Good Neighbor Provision, EPA adopted the Cross-State Air Pollution Rule (commonly and hereinafter called the Transport Rule). The rule calls for consideration of costs, among other factors, when determining the emission reductions an upwind State must make to improve air quality in polluted downwind areas. The Court of Appeals for the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit vacated the rule in its entirety. It held, 2 to 1, that the Good Neighbor Provision requires EPA to consider only each upwind State\xe2\x80\x99s physically proportionate responsibility for each downwind State\xe2\x80\x99s air quality problem. That reading is demanded, according to the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit, so that no State will be required to decrease its emissions by more than its ratable share of downwind-state pollution.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In Chevron U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc.,\n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 837 (1984)\n, we reversed a D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit decision that failed to accord deference to EPA\xe2\x80\x99s reasonable interpretation of an ambiguous Clean Air Act provision. Satisfied that the Good Neighbor Provision does not command the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 cost-blind construction, and that EPA reasonably interpreted the provision, we reverse the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Air pollution is transient, heedless of state boundaries. Pollutants generated by upwind sources are often transported by air currents, sometimes over hundreds of miles, to downwind States. As the pollution travels out of state, upwind States are relieved of the associated costs. Those costs are borne instead by the downwind States, whose ability to achieve and maintain satisfactory air quality is hampered by the steady stream of infiltrating pollution.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n For several reasons, curtailing interstate air pollution poses a complex challenge for environmental regulators. First, identifying the upwind origin of downwind air pollution is no easy endeavor. Most upwind States propel pollutants to more than one downwind State, many downwind States receive pollution from multiple upwind States, and some States qualify as both upwind and downwind. See Brief for Federal Petitioners 6. The overlapping and interwoven linkages between upwind and downwind States with which EPA had to contend number in the thousands.[1]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Further complicating the problem, pollutants do not emerge from the smokestacks of an upwind State and uniformly migrate downwind. Some pollutants stay within upwind States\xe2\x80\x99 borders, the wind carries others to downwind States, and some subset of that group drifts to States without air quality problems. \xe2\x80\x9cThe wind bloweth where it listeth, and thou hearest the sound thereof, but canst not tell whence it cometh, and whither it goeth.\xe2\x80\x9d The Holy Bible, John 3:8 (King James Version). In crafting a solution to the problem of interstate air pollution, regulators must account for the vagaries of the wind.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Finally, upwind pollutants that find their way downwind are not left unaltered by the journey. Rather, as the gases emitted by upwind polluters are carried downwind, they are transformed, through various chemical processes, into altogether different pollutants. The offending gases at issue in these cases\xe2\x80\x94nitrogen oxide (NOX) and sulfur dioxide (SO2)\xe2\x80\x94often develop into ozone and fine particulate matter (PM2.5) by the time they reach the atmospheres of downwind States. See 76 Fed. Reg. 48222\xe2\x80\x9348223 (2011). See also 69 Fed. Reg. 4575\xe2\x80\x934576 (2004) (describing the components of ozone and PM2.5). Downwind air quality must therefore be measured for ozone and PM2.5 concentrations. EPA\xe2\x80\x99s chore is to quantify the amount of upwind gases (NOX and SO2) that must be reduced to enable downwind States to keep their levels of ozone and PM2.5 in check.

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Over the past 50 years, Congress has addressed interstate air pollution several times and with increasing rigor. In 1963, Congress directed federal authorities to \xe2\x80\x9cencourage cooperative activities by the States and local governments for the prevention and control of air pollution.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n77Stat.\n393,\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71857a (1964 ed.). In 1970, Congress made this instruction more concrete, introducing features still key to the Act. For the first time, Congress directed EPA to establish national ambient air quality standards (NAAQS) for pollutants at levels that will protect public health. See\n\n84Stat.\n1679\xe2\x80\x931680, as amended, 42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77408, 7409 (2006 ed.). Once EPA settles on a NAAQS, the Act requires the Agency to designate \xe2\x80\x9cnonattainment\xe2\x80\x9d areas, i.e., locations where the concentration of a regulated pollutant exceeds the NAAQS. \xc2\xa77407(d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Act then shifts the burden to States to propose plans adequate for compliance with the NAAQS. Each State must submit a State Implementation Plan, or SIP, to EPA within three years of any new or revised NAAQS. \xc2\xa77410(a)(1). If EPA determines that a State has failed to submit an adequate SIP, either in whole or in part, the Act requires the Agency to promulgate a Federal Implementation Plan, or FIP, within two years of EPA\xe2\x80\x99s determina-tion, \xe2\x80\x9cunless the State corrects the deficiency\xe2\x80\x9d before a FIP is issued. \xc2\xa77410(c)(1).[2]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Act lists the matters a SIP must cover. Among SIP components, the 1970 version of the Act required SIPs to include \xe2\x80\x9cadequate provisions for intergovernmental cooperation\xe2\x80\x9d concerning interstate air pollution. \xc2\xa7110(a)(2)(E),\n\n84Stat.\n1681,\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71857c\xe2\x80\x935(a)(2)(E). This statutory requirement, with its text altered over time, has cometo be called the Good Neighbor Provision.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In 1977, Congress amended the Good Neighbor Provision to require more than \xe2\x80\x9ccooperation.\xe2\x80\x9d It directed States to submit SIPs that included provisions \xe2\x80\x9cadequate\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9cprohibi[t] any stationary source within the State from emitting any air pollutant in amounts which will .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. prevent attainment or maintenance [of air quality standards] by any other State.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7108(a)(4),\n\n91Stat.\n693,\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77410(a)(2)(E) (1976 ed., Supp. II). The amended provision thus explicitly instructed upwind States to reduce emissions to account for pollution exported beyond their borders. As then written, however, the provision regulated only individual sources that, considered alone, emitted enough pollution to cause nonattainment in a downwind State. Because it is often \xe2\x80\x9cimpossible to say that any single source or group of sources is the one which actually prevents attainment\xe2\x80\x9d downwind, S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 101\xe2\x80\x93228, p.\xc2\xa021 (1989), the 1977 version of the Good Neighbor Provision proved ineffective, see ibid. (noting the provision\xe2\x80\x99s inability to curb the collective \xe2\x80\x9cemissions [of] multiple sources\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Congress most recently amended the Good Neighbor Provision in 1990. The statute, in its current form, requires SIPs to \xe2\x80\x9ccontain adequate provisions .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. prohibiting .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any source or other type of emissions activity within the State from emitting any air pollutant in amounts which will .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. contribute significantly to nonattainment in, or interfere with maintenance by, any other State with respect to any .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [NAAQS].\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77410(a)(2)(D)(i) (2006 ed.). The controversy before us centers on EPA\xe2\x80\x99s most recent attempt to construe this provision.

\n

C

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Three times over the past two decades, EPA has attempted to delineate the Good Neighbor Provision\xe2\x80\x99s scope by identifying when upwind States \xe2\x80\x9ccontribute significantly\xe2\x80\x9d to nonattainment downwind. In 1998, EPA issued arule known as the \xe2\x80\x9cNOX SIP Call.\xe2\x80\x9d That regulation limited NOX emissions in 23 upwind States to the extent such emissions contributed to nonattainment of ozone standards in downwind States. See 63 Fed. Reg. 57356, 57358. In Michigan v. EPA, 213 F. 3d 663 (2000), the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit upheld the NOX SIP Call, specifically affirming EPA\xe2\x80\x99s use of costs to determine when an upwind State\xe2\x80\x99s contribution was \xe2\x80\x9csignifican[t]\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning of the statute. Id., at 674\xe2\x80\x93679.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In 2005, EPA issued the Clean Air Interstate Rule, or CAIR. 70 Fed. Reg. 25162. CAIR regulated both NOX and SO2 emissions, insofar as such emissions contributed to downwind nonattainment of two NAAQS, both set in 1997, one concerning the permissible annual measure of PM2.5, and another capping the average ozone level gauged over an 8-hour period. See id., at 25171. The D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit initially vacated CAIR as arbitrary and capricious. See North Carolina v. EPA, 531 F.\xc2\xa03d 896, 921 (2008) (per curiam). On rehearing, the court decided to leave the rule in place, while encouraging EPA to act with dispatch in dealing with problems the court had identified. See North Carolina v. EPA, 550 F.\xc2\xa03d 1176, 1178 (2008) (per curiam).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The rule challenged here\xe2\x80\x94the Transport Rule\xe2\x80\x94is EPA\xe2\x80\x99s response to the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s North Carolina decision. Finalized in August 2011, the Transport Rule curtails NOX and SO2 emissions of 27 upwind States to achieve downwind attainment of three different NAAQS: the two 1997 NAAQS previously addressed by CAIR, and the 2006 NAAQS for PM2.5 levels measured on a daily basis. See 76 Fed. Reg. 48208\xe2\x80\x9348209.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Under the Transport Rule, EPA employed a \xe2\x80\x9ctwo-step approach\xe2\x80\x9d to determine when upwind States \xe2\x80\x9ccontribute[d] significantly to nonattainment,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 48254, and therefore in \xe2\x80\x9camounts\xe2\x80\x9d that had to be eliminated. At step one, called the \xe2\x80\x9cscreening\xe2\x80\x9d analysis, the Agency excluded as de minimis any upwind State that contributed less than one percent of the three NAAQS[3] to any downwind State \xe2\x80\x9creceptor,\xe2\x80\x9d a location at which EPA measures air quality. See id., at 48236\xe2\x80\x9348237.[4] If all of an upwind State\xe2\x80\x99s contributions fell below the one-percent threshold, that State would be considered not to have \xe2\x80\x9ccontribute[d] signifi-cantly\xe2\x80\x9d to the nonattainment of any downwind State. Id., at 48236. States in that category were screened out and exempted from regulation under the rule.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The remaining States were subjected to a second inquiry, which EPA called the \xe2\x80\x9ccontrol\xe2\x80\x9d analysis. At this stage, the Agency sought to generate a cost-effective allocation of emission reductions among those upwind States \xe2\x80\x9cscreened in\xe2\x80\x9d at step one.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The control analysis proceeded this way. EPA first calculated, for each upwind State, the quantity of emissions the State could eliminate at each of several cost thresholds. See id., at 48248\xe2\x80\x9348249. Cost for these purposes is measured as cost per ton of emissions prevented, for instance, by installing scrubbers on powerplant smokestacks.[5] EPA estimated, for example, the amount each upwind State\xe2\x80\x99s NOX emissions would fall if all pollution sources within each State employed every control measure available at a cost of $500 per ton or less. See id., at 48249\xe2\x80\x9348251. The Agency then repeated that analysis at ascending cost thresholds. See ibid.[6]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Armed with this information, EPA conducted complex modeling to establish the combined effect the upwind reductions projected at each cost threshold would have on air quality in downwind States. See id., at 48249. The Agency then identified \xe2\x80\x9csignificant cost threshold[s],\xe2\x80\x9d points in its model where a \xe2\x80\x9cnoticeable change occurred in downwind air quality, such as .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. where large upwind emission reductions become available because a certain type of emissions control strategy becomes cost-effective.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. For example, reductions of NOX sufficient to resolve or significantly curb downwind air quality problems could be achieved, EPA determined, at a cost threshold of $500 per ton (applied uniformly to all regulated upwind States). \xe2\x80\x9cMoving beyond the $500 cost threshold,\xe2\x80\x9d EPA concluded, \xe2\x80\x9cwould result in only minimal additional .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. reductions [in emissions].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 48256.[7]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Finally, EPA translated the cost thresholds it had se-lected into amounts of emissions upwind States would be required to eliminate. For each regulated upwind State, EPA created an annual emissions \xe2\x80\x9cbudget.\xe2\x80\x9d These budgets represented the quantity of pollution an upwind State would produce in a given year if its in-state sources implemented all pollution controls available at the chosen cost thresholds. See id., at 48249.[8] If EPA\xe2\x80\x99s projected improvements to downwind air quality were to be realized, an upwind State\xe2\x80\x99s emissions could not exceed the level this budget allocated to it, subject to certain adjustments not relevant here.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Taken together, the screening and control inquiries defined EPA\xe2\x80\x99s understanding of which upwind emissions were within the Good Neighbor Provision\xe2\x80\x99s ambit. In short, under the Transport Rule, an upwind State \xe2\x80\x9ccontribute[d] significantly\xe2\x80\x9d to downwind nonattainment to the extent its exported pollution both (1) produced one percent or more of a NAAQS in at least one downwind State (step one) and (2) could be eliminated cost-effectively, as determined by EPA (step two). See id., at 48254. Upwind States would be obliged to eliminate all and only emissions meeting both of these criteria.[9]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n For each State regulated by the Transport Rule, EPA contemporaneously promulgated a FIP allocating that State\xe2\x80\x99s emission budget among its in-state sources. See id., at 48271, 48284\xe2\x80\x9348287.[10] For each of these States, EPA had determined that the State had failed to submita SIP adequate for compliance with the Good Neighbor Provision. These determinations regarding SIPs became final after 60 days, see\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77607(b)(1)(2006 ed., Supp. V\xc2\xa0), and many went unchallenged.[11] EPA views the SIP determinations as having triggered its statutory obligation to promulgate a FIP within two years, see \xc2\xa77410(c), a view contested by respondents, see Part II, infra.

\n

D

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n A group of state and local governments (State respondents), joined by industry and labor groups (Industry respondents), petitioned for review of the Transport Rule in the U.\xc2\xa0S. Court of Appeals for the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit. Over the dissent of Judge Rogers, the Court of Appeals vacated the rule in its entirety. See 696 F.\xc2\xa03d 7, 37 (2012).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n EPA\xe2\x80\x99s actions, the appeals court held, exceeded the Agency\xe2\x80\x99s statutory authority in two respects. By promulgating FIPs before giving States a meaningful opportunity to adopt their own implementation plans, EPA had, in the court\xe2\x80\x99s view, upset the CAA\xe2\x80\x99s division of responsibility between the States and the Federal Government. In the main, the Court of Appeals acknowledged, EPA\xe2\x80\x99s FIP authority is triggered at the moment the Agency disapproves a SIP. See id., at 30. Thus, when a State proposes a SIP inadequate to achieve a NAAQS, EPA could promulgate a FIP immediately after disapproving that SIP. See id., at 32.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n But the Court of Appeals ruled that a different regime applies to a State\xe2\x80\x99s failure to meet its obligations under the Good Neighbor Provision. While a NAAQS was a \xe2\x80\x9cclear numerical target,\xe2\x80\x9d a State\xe2\x80\x99s good neighbor obligation remained \xe2\x80\x9cnebulous and unknown,\xe2\x80\x9d the court observed, until EPA calculated the State\xe2\x80\x99s emission budget. Ibid. Without these budgets, the Court of Appeals said, upwind States would be compelled to take a \xe2\x80\x9cstab in the dark\xe2\x80\x9d at calculating their own significant contribution to interstate air pollution. Id., at 35. The D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit read the Act to avoid putting States in this position: EPA had an implicit statutory duty, the court held, to give upwind States a reasonable opportunity to allocate their emission budgets among in-state sources before the Agency\xe2\x80\x99s authority to issue FIPs could be triggered. Id., at 37.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit also held that the Agency\xe2\x80\x99s two-part interpretation of the Good Neighbor Provision ignored three \xe2\x80\x9cred lines .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. cabin[ing the] EPA\xe2\x80\x99s authority.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 19. First, the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit interpreted the Good Neighbor Provision to require upwind States to reduce emissions in \xe2\x80\x9ca manner proportional to their contributio[n]\xe2\x80\x9d to pollution in downwind States. Id., at 21. The Transport Rule, however, treated all regulated upwind States alike, regardless of their relative contribution to the overall problem. See id., at 23. It required all upwind States \xe2\x80\x9cscreened in\xe2\x80\x9d at step one to reduce emissions in accord with the uniform cost thresholds set during the step two control analysis. Imposing these uniform cost thresholds, the Court of Appeals observed, could force some upwind States to reduce emissions by more than their \xe2\x80\x9cfair share.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 27.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n According to the Court of Appeals, EPA had also failed to ensure that the Transport Rule did not mandate up-wind States to reduce pollution unnecessarily. The Good Neighbor Provision, the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit noted, \xe2\x80\x9ctargets [only] those emissions from upwind States that \xe2\x80\x98contribute significantly to nonattainment\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of a NAAQS in downwind States. Id., at 22. Pollution reduction beyond that goal was \xe2\x80\x9cunnecessary over-control,\xe2\x80\x9d outside the purview of the Agency\xe2\x80\x99s statutory mandate. Ibid. Because the emission budgets were calculated by reference to cost alone, the court concluded that EPA had done nothing to guard against, or even measure, the \xe2\x80\x9cover-control\xe2\x80\x9d potentially imposed by the Transport Rule. See ibid.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Finally, by deciding, at the screening analysis, that upwind contributions below the one-percent threshold were insignificant, EPA had established a \xe2\x80\x9cfloor\xe2\x80\x9d on the Agency\xe2\x80\x99s authority to act. See id., at 20, and n.\xc2\xa013. Again pointing to the rule\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on costs, the Court of Appeals held that EPA had failed to ensure that upwind States were not being forced to reduce emissions below the one-percent threshold. See ibid.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In dissent, Judge Rogers criticized the majority for deciding two questions that were not, in her view, properly before the court. See id., at 40\xe2\x80\x9346, 51\xe2\x80\x9358. First, she addressed the majority\xe2\x80\x99s insistence that FIPs abide a State\xe2\x80\x99s opportunity to allocate its emission budget among in-state sources. She regarded the respondents\xe2\x80\x99 plea to that effect as an untimely attack on EPA\xe2\x80\x99s previous SIP disapprovals. See id., at 40\xe2\x80\x9346. Second, in Judge Rogers\xe2\x80\x99 assessment, the respondents had failed to raise their substantive objections to the Transport Rule with the specificity necessary to preserve them for review. See id., at 51\xe2\x80\x9358. On the merits, Judge Rogers found nothing in the Act to require, or even suggest, that EPA must quan-tify a State\xe2\x80\x99s good neighbor obligations before it promulgated a FIP. See id., at 46\xe2\x80\x9351. She also disagreed with the court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that the Transport Rule unreasonably interpreted the Act. See id., at 58\xe2\x80\x9360.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We granted certiorari to decide whether the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit had accurately construed the limits the CAA places on EPA\xe2\x80\x99s authority. See 570 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2013).

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Once EPA has calculated emission budgets, the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit held, the Agency must give upwind States the opportunity to propose SIPs allocating those budgets among in-state sources before issuing a FIP. 696 F. 3d, at 37. As the State respondents put it, a FIP allocating a State\xe2\x80\x99s emission budget \xe2\x80\x9cmust issue after EPA has quantified the States\xe2\x80\x99 good-neighbor obligations [in an emission budget] and given the States a reasonable opportunity to meet those obligations in SIPs.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for State Respondents 20.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Before reaching the merits of this argument, we first reject EPA\xe2\x80\x99s threshold objection that the claim is untimely. According to the Agency, this argument\xe2\x80\x94and the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s opinion accepting it\xe2\x80\x94rank as improper collateral attacks on EPA\xe2\x80\x99s prior SIP disapprovals. As earlier recounted, see supra, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310, EPA, by the time it issued the Transport Rule, had determined that each regulated upwind State had failed to submit a SIP adequate to satisfy the Good Neighbor Provision. Many of those determinations, because unchallenged, became final after 60 days, see\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77607(b)(1), and did so before the petitions here at issue were filed. EPA argues that the Court cannot question exercise of the Agency\xe2\x80\x99s FIP authority without subjecting these final SIP disapprovals to untimely review.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We disagree. The gravamen of the State respondents\xe2\x80\x99 challenge is not that EPA\xe2\x80\x99s disapproval of any particular SIP was erroneous. Rather, respondents urge that, notwithstanding these disapprovals, the Agency was obliged to grant an upwind State a second opportunity to promul-gate adequate SIPs once EPA set the State\xe2\x80\x99s emission budget. This claim does not depend on the validity of the prior SIP disapprovals. Even assuming the legitimacy of those disapprovals, the question remains whether EPA was required to do more than disapprove a SIP, as the State respondents urge, to trigger the Agency\xe2\x80\x99s statutory authority to issue a FIP.[12]

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Turning to the merits, we hold that the text of the statute supports EPA\xe2\x80\x99s position. As earlier noted, see supra, at 4\xe2\x80\x935, the CAA sets a series of precise deadlines to which the States and EPA must adhere. Once EPA issues any new or revised NAAQS, a State has three years to adopt a SIP adequate for compliance with the Act\xe2\x80\x99s requirements. See\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77410(a)(1). Among those requirements is the Act\xe2\x80\x99s mandate that SIPs \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d include provisions sufficient to satisfy the Good Neighbor Provision. \xc2\xa77410(a)(2).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n If EPA determines a SIP to be inadequate, the Agency\xe2\x80\x99s mandate to replace it with a FIP is no less absolute:

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \xe2\x80\x9c[EPA] shall promulgate a [FIP] at any time within 2 years after the [Agency]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \xe2\x80\x9c(A) finds that a State has failed to make a required submission or finds that the plan or plan revision submitted by the State does not satisfy the minimum [relevant] criteria .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. , or

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \xe2\x80\x9c(B) disapproves a [SIP] in whole or in part,

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cunless the State corrects the deficiency, and [EPA] approves the plan or plan revision, before the [Agency] promulgates such [FIP].\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa77410(c)(1).

\n

In other words, once EPA has found a SIP inadequate, the Agency has a statutory duty to issue a FIP \xe2\x80\x9cat any time\xe2\x80\x9d within two years (unless the State first \xe2\x80\x9ccorrects the deficiency,\xe2\x80\x9d which no one contends occurred here).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit, however, found an unwritten exception to this strict time prescription for SIPs aimed at implementing the Good Neighbor Provision. Expecting any one State to develop a \xe2\x80\x9ccomprehensive solution\xe2\x80\x9d to the \xe2\x80\x9ccollective problem\xe2\x80\x9d of interstate air pollution without first receiving EPA\xe2\x80\x99s guidance was, in the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 assessment, \xe2\x80\x9cset[ting] the States up to fail.\xe2\x80\x9d 696 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 36\xe2\x80\x9337. The D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit therefore required EPA, after promulgating each State\xe2\x80\x99s emission budget, to give the State a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable\xe2\x80\x9d period of time to propose SIPs implementing its budget. See id., at 37.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n However sensible (or not) the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 position,[13] a reviewing court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ctask is to apply the text [of the statute], not to improve upon it.\xe2\x80\x9d Pavelic & LeFlore v. Marvel Entertainment Group, Div. of Cadence Industries Corp.,\n\n493 U.\xc2\xa0S. 120,\n126 (1989)\n. Nothing in the Act dif-ferentiates the Good Neighbor Provision from the several other matters a State must address in its SIP. Rather, the statute speaks without reservation: Once a NAAQS has been issued, a State \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d propose a SIP within three years, \xc2\xa77410(a)(1), and that SIP \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d include, among other components, provisions adequate to satisfy the Good Neighbor Provision, \xc2\xa77410(a)(2).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Nor does the Act condition the duty to promulgate a FIP on EPA\xe2\x80\x99s having first quantified an upwind State\xe2\x80\x99s good neighbor obligations. As Judge Rogers observed in her dissent from the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision, the Act does not require EPA to furnish upwind States with information of any kind about their good neighbor obligations before a FIP issues. See 696 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 47. Instead, a SIP\xe2\x80\x99s failure to satisfy the Good Neighbor Provision, without more, triggers EPA\xe2\x80\x99s obligation to issue a federal plan within two years. \xc2\xa77410(c). After EPA has disapproved a SIP, the Agency can wait up to two years to issue a FIP, during which time the State can \xe2\x80\x9ccorrec[t] the deficiency\xe2\x80\x9d on its own. Ibid. But EPA is not obliged to wait two years or postpone its action even a single day: The Act empowers the Agency to promulgate a FIP \xe2\x80\x9cat any time\xe2\x80\x9d within the two-year limit. Ibid. Carving out an exception to the Act\xe2\x80\x99s precise deadlines, as the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit did, \xe2\x80\x9crewrites a decades-old statute whose plain text and structure establish a clear chronology of federal and State responsibilities.\xe2\x80\x9d 696 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 47 (Rogers, J., dissenting).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The practical difficulties cited by the Court of Appeals do not justify departure from the Act\xe2\x80\x99s plain text. See Barnhart v. Sigmon Coal Co.,\n\n534 U.\xc2\xa0S. 438\n\xe2\x80\x93462 (2002) (We \xe2\x80\x9cmust presume that a legislature says in a statute what it means and means in a statute what it says there.\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). When Congress elected to make EPA\xe2\x80\x99s input a prerequisite to state action under the Act, it did so expressly. States developing vehicle inspection and maintenance programs under the CAA, for example, must await EPA guidance before issuing SIPs.\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77511a(c)(3)(B). A State\xe2\x80\x99s obligation to adopt a SIP, moreover, arises only after EPA has first set the NAAQS the State must meet. \xc2\xa77410(a)(1). Had Congress intended similarly to defer States\xe2\x80\x99 discharge of their obligations under the Good Neighbor Provision, Congress, we take it, would have included a similar direction in that section. See Jama v. Immigration and Customs Enforcement,\n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 335,\n341 (2005)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cWe do not lightly assume that Congress has omitted from its adopted text requirements that it nonetheless intends to apply, and our reluctance is even greater when Congress has shown elsewhere in the same statute that it knows how to make such a requirement manifest.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In short, nothing in the statute places EPA under an obligation to provide specific metrics to States before they undertake to fulfill their good neighbor obligations. By altering the schedule Congress provided for SIPs and FIPs, the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit stretched out the process. It allowed a delay Congress did not order and placed an information submission obligation on EPA Congress did not impose. The D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit, we hold, had no warrant thus to revise the CAA\xe2\x80\x99s action-ordering prescriptions.

\n

C

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n At oral argument, the State respondents emphasized EPA\xe2\x80\x99s previous decisions, in the NOX SIP Call and CAIR, to quantify the emission reductions required of upwind States before the window to propose a SIP closed. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 37\xe2\x80\x9339, 42\xe2\x80\x9343, 45\xe2\x80\x9346. In their view, by failing to accord States a similar grace period after issuing States\xe2\x80\x99 emission budgets, EPA acted arbitrarily. See ibid.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Whatever pattern the Agency followed in its NOX SIP call and CAIR proceedings, EPA retained discretion to alter its course provided it gave a reasonable explanation for doing so. Motor Vehicle Mfrs. Assn. of United States, Inc. v. State Farm Mut. Automobile Ins. Co.,\n\n463 U.\xc2\xa0S. 29,\n42 (1983)\n. The Agency presented such an explanation in the Transport Rule. As noted, see supra, at 6, the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s North Carolina decision admonished EPA to act with dispatch in amending or replacing CAIR, the Transport Rule\xe2\x80\x99s predecessor. See 550 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1178 (warning EPA that the stay of the court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to vacate CAIR would not persist \xe2\x80\x9cindefinite[ly]\xe2\x80\x9d). Given North Carolina\xe2\x80\x99s stress on expeditious action to cure the infir-mities the court identified in CAIR, EPA thoughtit \xe2\x80\x9c[in]appropriate to establish [the] lengthy transition period\xe2\x80\x9d entailed in allowing States time to propose new or amended SIPs implementing the Transport Rule emission budgets. See 76 Fed. Reg. 48220 (citing North Carolina, 550 F.\xc2\xa03d 1176). Endeavoring to satisfy the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s directive, EPA acted speedily, issuing FIPs contemporaneously with the Transport Rule. In light of the firm deadlines imposed by the Act, which we hold the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit lacked authority to alter, we cannot condemn EPA\xe2\x80\x99s decision as arbitrary or capricious.[14]

\n

III

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit also held that the Transport Rule\xe2\x80\x99s two-step interpretation of the Good Neighbor Provision conflicts with the Act. Before addressing this holding, we take up a jurisdictional objection raised by EPA.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The CAA directs that \xe2\x80\x9c[o]nly an objection to a rule .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. raised with reasonable specificity during the period for public comment .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. may be raised during judicial review.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77607(d)(7)(B). Respondents failed to state their objections to the Transport Rule during the comment period with the \xe2\x80\x9cspecificity\xe2\x80\x9d required for preservation, EPA argues. See Brief for Federal Petitioners 34\xe2\x80\x9342. This failure at the administrative level, EPA urges, forecloses judicial review. Id., at 34.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Assuming, without deciding, that respondents did not meet the Act\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9creasonable specificity\xe2\x80\x9d requirement during the comment period, we do not regard that lapse as \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x9d This Court has cautioned against \xe2\x80\x9cprofligate use\xe2\x80\x9d of the label \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x9d Sebelius v. Auburn Regional Medical Center, 568 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2013) (slip op., at 6). A rule may be \xe2\x80\x9cmandatory,\xe2\x80\x9d yet not \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional,\xe2\x80\x9d we have explained. See Arbaugh v. Y & H Corp.,\n\n546 U.\xc2\xa0S. 500,\n510 (2006)\n. Section 7607(d)(7)(B), we hold, is of that character. It does not speak to a court\xe2\x80\x99s authority, but only to a party\xe2\x80\x99s procedural obligations. See Kontrick v. Ryan,\n\n540 U.\xc2\xa0S. 443,\n455 (2004)\n. Had EPA pursued the \xe2\x80\x9creasonable specificity\xe2\x80\x9d argument vigorously before the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit, we would be obligated to address the merits of the argument. See Gonzalez v. Thaler, 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 10). But EPA did not press the argument unequivocally. Before the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit, it indicated only that the \xe2\x80\x9creasonable specificity\xe2\x80\x9d prescription might bar judicial review. Brief for Respondent EPA et\xc2\xa0al. in No. 11\xe2\x80\x931302 (CADC), p.\xc2\xa030. See also id., at 32. We therefore do not count the prescription an impassable hindrance to our adjudication of the respondents\xe2\x80\x99 attack on EPA\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the Transport Rule. We turn to that attack mindful of the importance of the issues respondents raise to the ongoing implementation of the Good Neighbor Provision.

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We routinely accord dispositive effect to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s reasonable interpretation of ambiguous statutory language. Chevron U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc.,\n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 837 (1984)\n, is the pathmarking decision, and it bears a notable resemblance to the cases before us. Chevron concerned EPA\xe2\x80\x99s definition of the term \xe2\x80\x9csource,\xe2\x80\x9d as used in the 1977 Amendments to the CAA. Id., at 840, n. 1. Those amendments placed additional restrictions on companies\xe2\x80\x99 liberty to add new pollution \xe2\x80\x9csources\xe2\x80\x9d to their factories. See id., at 840. Although \xe2\x80\x9csource\xe2\x80\x9d might have been interpreted to refer to an individual smokestack, EPA construed the term to refer to an entire plant, thereby \xe2\x80\x9ctreat[ing] all of the pollution-emitting devices within the [plant] as though they were encased within a single \xe2\x80\x98bubble.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Under the Agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation, a new pollution-emitting device would not subject a plant to the additional restrictions if the \xe2\x80\x9calteration [did] not increase the total emissions [produced by] the plant.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This Court held EPA\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of \xe2\x80\x9csource\xe2\x80\x9d a reasonable construction of an ambiguous statutory term. When \xe2\x80\x9cCongress has not directly addressed the precise [interpretative] question at issue,\xe2\x80\x9d we cautioned, a reviewing court cannot \xe2\x80\x9csimply impose its own construction o[f] the statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 843. Rather, the agency is charged with filling the \xe2\x80\x9cgap left open\xe2\x80\x9d by the ambiguity. Id., at 866. Because \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a full understanding of the force of the statutory policy .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. depend[s] upon more than ordinary knowledge\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of the situation, the administering agency\xe2\x80\x99s construction is to be accorded \xe2\x80\x9ccontrolling weight unless .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. arbitrary, capricious, or manifestly contrary to the statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 844 (quoting United States v. Shimer,\n\n367 U.\xc2\xa0S. 374,\n382 (1961)\n). Determining that none of those terms fit EPA\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of \xe2\x80\x9csource,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court deferred to the Agency\xe2\x80\x99s judgment.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We conclude that the Good Neighbor Provision delegates authority to EPA at least as certainly as the CAA provisions involved in Chevron. The statute requires States to eliminate those \xe2\x80\x9camounts\xe2\x80\x9d of pollution that \xe2\x80\x9ccontribute significantly to nonattainment\xe2\x80\x9d in downwind States.\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77410(a)(2)(D)(i) (emphasis added). Thus, EPA\xe2\x80\x99s task[15] is to reduce upwind pollution, but only in \xe2\x80\x9camounts\xe2\x80\x9d that push a downwind State\xe2\x80\x99s pollution concentrations above the relevant NAAQS. As noted earlier, however, the nonattainment of downwind States results from the collective and interwoven contributions of multiple upwind States. See supra, at 3. The statute therefore callsupon the Agency to address a thorny causation problem: How should EPA allocate among multiple contributing up-wind States responsibility for a downwind State\xe2\x80\x99s excess pollution?

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n A simplified example illustrates the puzzle EPA faced. Suppose the Agency sets a NAAQS, with respect to a particular pollutant, at 100 parts per billion (ppb), and that the level of the pollutant in the atmosphere of downwind State A is 130 ppb. Suppose further that EPA has determined that each of three upwind States\xe2\x80\x94X, Y, and Z\xe2\x80\x94contributes the equivalent of 30 ppb of the relevant pollutant to State A\xe2\x80\x99s airspace. The Good Neighbor Provision, as just observed, prohibits only upwind emissions that contribute significantly to downwind nonattainment. EPA\xe2\x80\x99s authority under the provision is therefore limited to eliminating a total of 30 ppb,[16] i.e., the overage caused by the collective contribution of States X, Y, and Z.[17]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n How is EPA to divide responsibility among the three States? Should the Agency allocate reductions proportionally (10 ppb each), on a per capita basis, on the basis of the cost of abatement, or by some other metric? See Brief for Federal Petitioners 50 (noting EPA\xe2\x80\x99s consideration of different approaches). The Good Neighbor Provision does not answer that question for EPA. Cf. Chevron, 467 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 860 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he language of [the CAA] simply does not compel any given interpretation of the term \xe2\x80\x98source.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). Under Chevron, we read Congress\xe2\x80\x99 silence as a delegation of authority to EPA to select from among reasonable options. See United States v. Mead Corp.,\n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 218,\n229 (2001)\n.[18]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Yet the Court of Appeals believed that the Act speaks clearly, requiring EPA to allocate responsibility for reducing emissions in \xe2\x80\x9ca manner proportional to\xe2\x80\x9d each State\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccontributio[n]\xe2\x80\x9d to the problem. 696 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 21. Nothing in the text of the Good Neighbor Provision propels EPA down this path. Understandably so, for as EPA notes, the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s proportionality approach could scarcely be satisfied in practice. See App. in No. 11\xe2\x80\x931302 etc. (CADC), p.\xc2\xa02312 (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hile it is possible to determine an emission reduction percentage if there is a single downwind [receptor], most upwind states contribute to multiple downwind [receptors] (in multiple states) and would have a different reduction percentage for each one.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n To illustrate, consider a variation on the example set out above. Imagine that States X and Y now contribute air pollution to State A in a ratio of one to five, i.e., State Y contributes five times the amount of pollution to State A than does State X. If State A were the only downwind State to which the two upwind States contributed, the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s proportionality requirement would be easy to meet: EPA could require State Y to reduce its emissions by five times the amount demanded of State X.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The realities of interstate air pollution, however, are not so simple. Most upwind States contribute pollution to multiple downwind States in varying amounts. See 76 Fed. Reg. 48239\xe2\x80\x9348246. See also Brief for Respondent Calpine Corp. et\xc2\xa0al. in Support of Petitioners 48\xe2\x80\x9349 (offering examples). Suppose then that States X and Y also contribute pollutants to a second downwind State (State B), this time in a ratio of seven to one. Though State Y contributed a relatively larger share of pollution to State A, with respect to State B, State X is the greater offender. Following the proportionality approach with respect to State B would demand that State X reduce its emissions by seven times as much as State Y. Recall, however, that State Y, as just hypothesized, had to effect five times as large a reduction with respect to State A. The Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 proportionality edict with respect to both State A and State B appears to work neither mathematically nor in practical application. Proportionality as to one down-wind State will not achieve proportionality as to others. Quite the opposite. And where, as is generally true, upwind States contribute pollution to more than two downwind receptors, proportionality becomes all the more elusive.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Neither the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit nor respondents face up to this problem. The dissent, for its part, strains to give meaning to the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s proportionality constraint as applied to a world in which multiple upwind States contribute emissions to multiple downwind locations. In the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s view, upwind States must eliminate emissions by \xe2\x80\x9cwhatever minimum amount reduces\xe2\x80\x9d their share of the overage in each and every one of the downwind States to which they are linked. See post, at 8. In practical terms, this means each upwind State will be required to reduce emissions by the amount necessary to eliminate that State\xe2\x80\x99s largest downwind contribution. The dissent\xe2\x80\x99s formulation, however, does not account for the combined and cumu-lative effect of each upwind State\xe2\x80\x99s reductions on attainment in multiple downwind locations. See ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9cUnder a proportional-reduction approach, State X would be required to eliminate emissions of that pollutant by whatever minimum amount reduces both State A\xe2\x80\x99s level by 0.2 unit and State B\xe2\x80\x99s by 0.7 unit.\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). The result would be costly overregulation unnecessary to, indeedin conflict with, the Good Neighbor Provision\xe2\x80\x99s goal of attainment.[19]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In response, the dissent asserts that EPA will \xe2\x80\x9csimply be required to make allowance for\xe2\x80\x9d the overregulation caused by its \xe2\x80\x9cproportional-reduction\xe2\x80\x9d approach. Post, at 11. What criterion should EPA employ to determine which States will have to make those \xe2\x80\x9callowance[s]\xe2\x80\x9d and by how much? The dissent admits there are \xe2\x80\x9cmultiple ways\xe2\x80\x9d EPA might answer those questions. Ibid. But proportionality cannot be one of those ways, for theproportional-reduction approach is what led to the overregulation in the first place. And if a nonproportional approach can play a role in setting the final allocation of reduction obligations, then it is hardly apparent why EPA, free to depart from proportionality at the back end, cannot do so at the outset.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Persuaded that the Good Neighbor Provision does not dictate the particular allocation of emissions among contributing States advanced by the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit, we must next decide whether the allocation method chosen by EPA is a \xe2\x80\x9cpermissible construction of the statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Chevron, 467 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 843. As EPA interprets the statute, upwind emissions rank as \xe2\x80\x9camounts [that] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. contribute significantly to nonattainment\xe2\x80\x9d if they (1) constitute one percent or more of a relevant NAAQS in a nonattaining downwind State and (2) can be eliminated under the cost threshold set by the Agency. See 76 Fed. Reg. 48254. In other words, to identify which emissions were to be eliminated, EPA considered both the magnitude of upwind States\xe2\x80\x99 contributions and the cost associated with eliminating them.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Industry respondents argue that, however EPA ultimately divides responsibility among upwind States, the final calculation cannot rely on costs. The Good Neighbor Provision, respondents and the dissent emphasize, \xe2\x80\x9crequires each State to prohibit only those \xe2\x80\x98amounts\xe2\x80\x99 of air pollution emitted within the State that \xe2\x80\x98contribute significantly\xe2\x80\x99 to another State\xe2\x80\x99s nonattaintment.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Industry Respondents 23 (emphasis added). See also post, at 6. The cost of preventing emissions, they urge, iswholly unrelated to the actual \xe2\x80\x9camoun[t]\xe2\x80\x9d of air pollution an upwind State contributes. Brief for Industry Respondents 23. Because the Transport Rule considers costs, respondents argue, \xe2\x80\x9cStates that contribute identical \xe2\x80\x98amounts\xe2\x80\x99 .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. may be deemed [by EPA] to have [made] substantially different\xe2\x80\x9d contributions. Id., at 30.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n But, as just explained, see supra, at 21\xe2\x80\x9322, the Agency cannot avoid the task of choosing which among equal \xe2\x80\x9camounts\xe2\x80\x9d to eliminate. The Agency has chosen, sensibly in our view, to reduce the amount easier, i.e., less costly, to eradicate, and nothing in the text of the Good Neighbor Provision precludes that choice.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Using costs in the Transport Rule calculus, we agree with EPA, also makes good sense. Eliminating those amounts that can cost-effectively be reduced is an efficient and equitable solution to the allocation problem the Good Neighbor Provision requires the Agency to address. Efficient because EPA can achieve the levels of attainment, i.e., of emission reductions, the proportional approach aims to achieve, but at a much lower overall cost. Equita-ble because, by imposing uniform cost thresholds on regulated States, EPA\xe2\x80\x99s rule subjects to stricter regulation those States that have done relatively less in the past to control their pollution. Upwind States that have not yet implemented pollution controls of the same stringency as their neighbors will be stopped from free riding on their neighbors\xe2\x80\x99 efforts to reduce pollution. They will have to bring down their emissions by installing devices of the kind in which neighboring States have already invested.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Suppose, for example, that the industries of upwind State A have expended considerable resources installing modern pollution-control devices on their plants. Factories in upwind State B, by contrast, continue to run old, dirty plants. Yet, perhaps because State A is more populous and therefore generates a larger sum of pollution overall, the two States\xe2\x80\x99 emissions have equal effects on downwind attainment. If State A and State B are required to eliminate emissions proportionally (i.e., equally), sources in State A will be compelled to spend far more per ton of reductions because they have already utilized lower cost pollution controls. State A\xe2\x80\x99s sources will also have to achieve greater reductions than would have been required had they not made the cost-effective reductions in the first place. State A, in other words, will be tolled for having done more to reduce pollution in the past.[20] EPA\xe2\x80\x99s cost-based allocation avoids these anomalies.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Obligated to require the elimination of only those \xe2\x80\x9camounts\xe2\x80\x9d of pollutants that contribute to the nonattainment of NAAQS in downwind States, EPA must decide how to differentiate among the otherwise like contributions of multiple upwind States. EPA found decisive the difficulty of eliminating each \xe2\x80\x9camount,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., the cost incurred in doing so. Lacking a dispositive statutory instruction to guide it, EPA\xe2\x80\x99s decision, we conclude, is a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable\xe2\x80\x9d way of filling the \xe2\x80\x9cgap left open by Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Chevron, 467 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 866.[21]

\n

C

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit stated two further objections to EPA\xe2\x80\x99s cost-based method of defining an upwind State\xe2\x80\x99s contribution. Once a State was screened in at step one of EPA\xe2\x80\x99s analysis, its emission budget was calculated solely with reference to the uniform cost thresholds the Agency selected at step two. The Transport Rule thus left open thepossibility that a State might be compelled to reduce emissions beyond the point at which every affected downwind State is in attainment, a phenomenon the Court of Appeals termed \xe2\x80\x9cover-control.\xe2\x80\x9d 696 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 22; see supra, at 12. Second, EPA\xe2\x80\x99s focus on costs did not foreclose, as the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit accurately observed, the possibility that an upwind State would be required to reduce its emissions by so much that the State no longer contributed one per-cent or more of a relevant NAAQS to any downwind State. This would place the State below the mark EPA had set, during the screening phase, as the initial threshold of \xe2\x80\x9csignifican[ce].\xe2\x80\x9d See id., at 20, and n.\xc2\xa013.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We agree with the Court of Appeals to this extent: EPA cannot require a State to reduce its output of pollution by more than is necessary to achieve attainment in every downwind State or at odds with the one-percent threshold the Agency has set. If EPA requires an upwind State to reduce emissions by more than the amount necessary to achieve attainment in every downwind State to which it is linked, the Agency will have overstepped its authority, under the Good Neighbor Provision, to eliminate those \xe2\x80\x9camounts [that] contribute .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to nonattainment.\xe2\x80\x9d Nor can EPA demand reductions that would drive an upwind State\xe2\x80\x99s contribution to every downwind State to which it is linked below one percent of the relevant NAAQS. Doing so would be counter to step one of the Agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the Good Neighbor Provision. See 76 Fed. Reg. 48236 (\xe2\x80\x9c[S]tates whose contributions are below th[e] thresholds do not significantly contribute to nonattainment .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of the relevant NAAQS.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Neither possibility, however, justifies wholesale invalidation of the Transport Rule. First, instances of \xe2\x80\x9cover-control\xe2\x80\x9d in particular downwind locations, the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit acknowledged, see 696 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 22, may be incidental to reductions necessary to ensure attainment elsewhere. Because individual upwind States often \xe2\x80\x9ccontribute significantly\xe2\x80\x9d to nonattainment in multiple downwind locations, the emissions reduction required to bring one linked downwind State into attainment may well be large enough to push other linked downwind States over the attainment line.[22] As the Good Neighbor Provision seeks attainment in every downwind State, however, exceeding attainment in one State cannot rank as \xe2\x80\x9cover-control\xe2\x80\x9d unless unnecessary to achieving attainment in any downwind State. Only reductions unnecessary to downwind attainment anywhere fall outside the Agency\xe2\x80\x99s statutory authority.[23]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Second, while EPA has a statutory duty to avoid over-control, the Agency also has a statutory obligation to avoid \xe2\x80\x9cunder-control,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., to maximize achievement of attainment downwind. For reasons earlier explained, see supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x934, a degree of imprecision is inevitable in tackling the problem of interstate air pollution. Slight changes in wind patterns or energy consumption, for example, may vary downwind air quality in ways EPA might not have anticipated. The Good Neighbor Provision requires EPA to seek downwind attainment of NAAQS notwithstanding the uncertainties. Hence, some amount of over-control, i.e., emission budgets that turn out to be more demanding than necessary, would not be surprising. Required to balance the possibilities of under-control and over-control, EPA must have leeway in fulfilling its statutory mandate.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Finally, in a voluminous record, involving thousands of upwind-to-downwind linkages, respondents point to only a few instances of \xe2\x80\x9cunnecessary\xe2\x80\x9d emission reductions, and even those are contested by EPA. Compare Brief for Industry Respondents 19 with Reply Brief for Federal Petitioners 21\xe2\x80\x9322. EPA, for its part, offers data, contested by respondents, purporting to show that few (if any) upwind States have been required to limit emissions below the one-percent threshold of significance. Compare Brief for Federal Petitioners 37, 54\xe2\x80\x9355, with Brief for Industry Respondents 40.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n If any upwind State concludes it has been forced to regulate emissions below the one-percent threshold or beyond the point necessary to bring all downwind States into attainment, that State may bring a particularized, as-applied challenge to the Transport Rule, along with any other as-applied challenges it may have. Cf. Babbitt v. Sweet Home Chapter, Communities for Great Ore.,\n\n515 U.\xc2\xa0S. 687\n\xe2\x80\x93700 (1995) (approving agency\xe2\x80\x99s reasonable interpretation of statute despite possibility of improper applications); American Hospital Assn. v. NLRB,\n\n499 U.\xc2\xa0S. 606,\n619 (1991)\n (rejecting facial challenge to National Labor Relations Board rule despite possible arbitrary applications). Satisfied that EPA\xe2\x80\x99s cost-based methodol-ogy, on its face, is not \xe2\x80\x9carbitrary, capricious, or manifestly contrary to the statute,\xe2\x80\x9d Chevron, 467 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 844, we uphold the Transport Rule. The possibility that the rule, in uncommon particular applications, might exceed EPA\xe2\x80\x99s statutory authority does not warrant judicial condemnation of the rule in its entirety.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In sum, we hold that the CAA does not command that States be given a second opportunity to file a SIP after EPA has quantified the State\xe2\x80\x99s interstate pollution obligations. We further conclude that the Good Neighbor Provision does not require EPA to disregard costs and consider exclusively each upwind State\xe2\x80\x99s physically proportionate responsibility for each downwind air quality problem. EPA\xe2\x80\x99s cost-effective allocation of emission reductions among upwind States, we hold, is a permissible, work-able, and equitable interpretation of the Good Neighbor Provision.

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Too many important decisions of the Federal Government are made nowadays by unelected agency officials exercising broad lawmaking authority, rather than by the people\xe2\x80\x99s representatives in Congress. With the statute involved in the present cases, however, Congress did it right. It specified quite precisely the responsibility of an upwind State under the Good Neighbor Provision: to eliminate those amounts of pollutants that it contributes to downwind problem areas. But the Environmental Protection Agency was unsatisfied with this system. Agency personnel, perhaps correctly, thought it more efficient to require reductions not in proportion to the amounts of pollutants for which each upwind State is responsible, but on the basis of how cost-effectively each can decrease emissions.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Today, the majority approves that undemocratic revision of the Clean Air Act. The Agency came forward with a textual justification for its action, relying on a farfetched meaning of the word \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly\xe2\x80\x9d in the statutory text. That justification is so feeble that today\xe2\x80\x99s majority does not even recite it, much less defend it. The majority reaches its result (\xe2\x80\x9cLook Ma, no hands!\xe2\x80\x9d) without benefit of text, claiming to have identified a remarkable \xe2\x80\x9cgap\xe2\x80\x9d in the statute, which it proceeds to fill (contrary to the plain logic of the statute) with cost-benefit analysis\xe2\x80\x94and then, with no pretended textual justification at all, simply extends cost-benefit analysis beyond the scope of the alleged gap.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Additionally, the majority relieves EPA of any obligation to announce novel interpretations of the Good Neighbor Provision before the States must submit plans that are required to comply with those interpretations. By according the States primacy in deciding how to attain the governing air-quality standards, the Clean Air Act is pregnant with an obligation for the Agency to set those standards before the States can be expected to achieve them. The majority nonetheless approves EPA\xe2\x80\x99s promulgation of federal plans implementing good-neighbor benchmarks before the States could conceivably have met those benchmarks on their own.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n I would affirm the judgment of the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit that EPA violated the law both in crafting the Transport Rule and in implementing it.[1]

\n

I. The Transport Rule

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \xe2\x80\x9cIt is axiomatic that an administrative agency\xe2\x80\x99s power to promulgate legislative regulations is limited to the authority delegated by Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Bowen v. Georgetown Univ. Hospital,\n\n488 U.\xc2\xa0S. 204,\n208 (1988)\n. Yet today the major-ity treats the text of the Clean Air Act not as the source and ceiling of EPA\xe2\x80\x99s authority to regulate interstate air pollution, but rather as a difficulty to be overcome in pursuit of the Agency\xe2\x80\x99s responsibility to \xe2\x80\x9ccraf[t] a solution to the problem of interstate air pollution.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 3. In reality, Congress itself has crafted the solution. The Good Neighbor Provision requires each State to eliminate whatever \xe2\x80\x9camounts\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9cair pollutant[s]\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ccontribute significantly to nonattainment\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cinterfere with maintenance\xe2\x80\x9d of national ambient air-quality standards (NAAQS) in other States.\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77410(a)(2)(D)(i)(I). The statute addresses solely the environmental consequences of emissions, not the facility of reducing them; and it requires States to shoulder burdens in proportion to the size of their contributions, not in proportion to the ease of bearing them. EPA\xe2\x80\x99s utterly fanciful \xe2\x80\x9cfrom each according to its ability\xe2\x80\x9d construction sacrifices democratically adopted text to bureaucratically favored policy. It deserves no deference under Chevron U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc.,\n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 837 (1984)\n.

\n

A. Alleged Textual Support: \xe2\x80\x9cSignificantly\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In the Government\xe2\x80\x99s argument here, the asserted tex-tual support for the efficient-reduction approach adopted by EPA in the Transport Rule is the ambiguity of the word \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly\xe2\x80\x9d in the statutory requirement that each State eliminate those \xe2\x80\x9camounts\xe2\x80\x9d of pollutants that \xe2\x80\x9ccontribute significantly to nonattainment\xe2\x80\x9d in downwind States. \xc2\xa77410(a)(2)(D)(i)(I) (emphasis added). As described in the Government\xe2\x80\x99s briefing:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he term \xe2\x80\x98significantly\xe2\x80\x99 .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is ambiguous, and .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. EPA may permissibly determine the amount of a State\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x98significant\xe2\x80\x99 contribution by reference to the amount of emissions reductions achievable through application of highly cost-effective controls.\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief for Federal Petitioners 15\xe2\x80\x9316 (emphasis added; some internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

And as the Government stated at oral argument:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[I]n terms of the language, \xe2\x80\x98contribute significantly,\xe2\x80\x99 .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. EPA reasonably construed that term to include a component of difficulty of achievement [i.e., cost]; that is, in common parlance, we might say that dunking a basketball is a more significant achievement for somebody who is 5 feet 10 than for somebody who is 6 feet 10.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 9 (emphasis added).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n But of course the statute does not focus on whether the upwind State has \xe2\x80\x9cachieved significantly\xe2\x80\x9d; it asks whether the State has \xe2\x80\x9ccontributed significantly\xe2\x80\x9d to downwind pollution. The provision addresses the physical effects of physical causes, and it is only the magnitude of the relationship sufficient to trigger regulation that admits of some vagueness. Stated differently, the statute is ambiguous as to how much of a contribution to downwind pollution is \xe2\x80\x9csignificant,\xe2\x80\x9d but it is not at all ambiguous as to whether factors unrelated to the amounts of pollutants that make up a contribution affect the analysis. Just as \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t does not matter whether the word \xe2\x80\x98yellow\xe2\x80\x99 is ambiguous when the agency has interpreted it to mean \xe2\x80\x98purple,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Home Concrete & Supply, LLC, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, n.\xc2\xa01 (2012) (Scalia, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment) (slip op., at 2, n.\xc2\xa01), it does not matter whether the phrase \xe2\x80\x9camounts which .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. contribute significantly [to downwind NAAQS nonattainment]\xe2\x80\x9d is ambiguous when EPA has interpreted it to mean \xe2\x80\x9camounts which are inexpensive to eliminate.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n It would be extraordinary for Congress, by use of the single word \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly,\xe2\x80\x9d to transmogrify a statute that assigns responsibility on the basis of amounts of pollut-ants emitted into a statute authorizing EPA to reduce interstate pollution in the manner that it believes most efficient. We have repeatedly said that Congress \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not alter the fundamental details of a regulatory scheme in vague terms or ancillary provisions\xe2\x80\x94it does not, one might say, hide elephants in mouseholes.\xe2\x80\x9d Whitman v. American Trucking Assns., Inc.,\n\n531 U. S. 457,\n468 (2001)\n (citing MCI Telecommunications Corp. v. American Telephone & Telegraph Co.,\n\n512 U.\xc2\xa0S. 218,\n231 (1994)\n; FDA v. Brown & Williamson Tobacco Corp.,\n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 120\n\xe2\x80\x93160 (2000)).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The statute\xe2\x80\x99s history demonstrates that \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly\xe2\x80\x9d is not code for \xe2\x80\x9cfeel free to consider compliance costs.\xe2\x80\x9d The previous version of the Good Neighbor Provision required each State to prohibit emissions that would \xe2\x80\x9cprevent attainment or maintenance by any other State of any [NAAQS].\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n91Stat.\n693 (emphasis added). It is evident that the current reformulation (targeting \xe2\x80\x9cany air pollut-ant in amounts which will .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. contribute significantly to nonattainment in, or interfere with maintenance by, any other State with respect to any [NAAQS]\xe2\x80\x9d) was meant simply to eliminate any implication that the polluting State had to be a but-for rather than merely a contributing cause of the downwind nonattainment or maintenance problem\xe2\x80\x94not to allow cost concerns to creep in through the back door.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In another respect also EPA\xe2\x80\x99s reliance upon the word \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly\xe2\x80\x9d is plainly mistaken. The Good Neighbor Provision targets for elimination not only those emissions that \xe2\x80\x9ccontribute significantly to nonattainment [of NAAQS] in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any other State,\xe2\x80\x9d but also those that \xe2\x80\x9cinterfere with maintenance [of NAAQS] by .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any otherState.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa77410(a)(2)(D)(i)(I). The wiggle-word \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly\xe2\x80\x9dis absent from the latter phrase. EPA does not\xe2\x80\x94cannot\xe2\x80\x94provide any textual justification for the conclusion that, when the same amounts of a pollutant travel downwind from States X and Y to a single area in State A, the emissions from X but not Y can be said to \xe2\x80\x9cinterfere with maintenance\xe2\x80\x9d of the NAAQS in A just because they are cheaper to eliminate. Yet EPA proposes to use the \xe2\x80\x9cfrom each according to its ability\xe2\x80\x9d approach for nonattainment areas and maintenance areas.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n To its credit, the majority does not allude to, much less try to defend, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly\xe2\x80\x9d argument. But there is a serious downside to this. The sky-hook of \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly\xe2\x80\x9d was called into service to counter the criterion of upwind-state responsibility plainly provided in the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text: amounts of pollutants contributed to downwind problem areas. See Brief for Federal Petitioners 42\xe2\x80\x9345. Having forsworn reliance on \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly\xe2\x80\x9d to convert responsibility for amounts of pollutants into responsibility for easy reduction of pollutants, the majority is impaled upon the statutory text.

\n

B. The Alleged \xe2\x80\x9cGap\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n To fill the void created by its abandonment of EPA\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly\xe2\x80\x9d argument, the majority identifies a sup-posed gap in the text, which EPA must fill: While the text says that each upwind State must be responsible for its own contribution to downwind pollution, it does not say how responsibility is to be divided among multiple States when the total of their combined contribution to downwind pollution in a particular area exceeds the reduction that the relevant NAAQS requires. In the example given by the majority, ante, at 21\xe2\x80\x9322, when each of three upwind States contributes 30 units of a pollutant to a downwind State but the reduction required for that State to comply with the NAAQS is only 30 units, how will responsibility for that 30 units be apportioned? Wow, that\xe2\x80\x99s a hard one\xe2\x80\x94almost the equivalent of asking who is buried in Grant\xe2\x80\x99s Tomb. If the criterion of responsibility is amounts of pollutants, then surely shared responsibility must be based upon relative amounts of pollutants\xe2\x80\x94in the major-ity\xe2\x80\x99s example, 10 units for each State. The statute makes no sense otherwise. The Good Neighbor Provision contains a gap only for those who blind themselves to the obvious in order to pursue a preferred policy.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n But not only does the majority bring in cost-benefit analysis to fill a gap that does not really exist. Having filled that \xe2\x80\x9cgap,\xe2\x80\x9d it then extends the efficiency-based principle to situations beyond the imaginary gap\xe2\x80\x94that is, situations where no apportionment is required. Even where an upwind State contributes pollutants to only a single downwind State, its annual emissions \xe2\x80\x9cbudget\xe2\x80\x9d will be based not upon the amounts of pollutants it contributes, but upon what \xe2\x80\x9cpollution controls [are] available at the chosen cost thresholds.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9. EPA\xe2\x80\x99s justification was its implausible (and only half-applicable) notion that \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly\xe2\x80\x9d imports cost concerns into the provision. The majority, having abandoned that absurdity, is left to deal with the no-apportionment situation with no defense\xe2\x80\x94not even an imaginary gap\xe2\x80\x94against a crystal-clear statutory text.

\n

C. The Majority\xe2\x80\x99s Criticisms of Proportional Reduction

\n

1. Impossibility

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The majority contends that a proportional-reduction approach \xe2\x80\x9ccould scarcely be satisfied in practice\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cappears to work neither mathematically nor in practical application, ante, at 23\xe2\x80\x94in essence, that the approach is impossible of application. If that were true, I know of no legal authority and no democratic principle that would derive from it the consequence that EPA could rewrite the statute, rather than the consequence that the statute would be inoperative. \xe2\x80\x9cThere are sometimes statutes which no rule or canon of interpretation can make effective or applicable to the situations of fact which they purport to govern. In such cases the statute must simply fail.\xe2\x80\x9d 3 R. Pound, Jurisprudence 493 (1959) (footnote omitted). In other words, the impossibility argument has no independent force: It is relevant only if the majority\xe2\x80\x99s textual interpretation is permissible. But in any event, the argument is wrong.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The impossibility theorem rests upon the following scenario: \xe2\x80\x9cImagine that States X and Y .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. contribute air pollution to State A in a ratio of one to five\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 23. And suppose that \xe2\x80\x9cStates X and Y also contribute pollutants to a second downwind State (State B), this time in a ratio of seven to one.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The majority concludes that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 proportionality edict with respect to both State A and State B appears to work neither mathematically nor in practical application.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. But why not? The majority\xe2\x80\x99s model relies on two faulty premises\xe2\x80\x94one an oversimplification and the other a misapprehension.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n First, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s formulation suggests that EPA measures the comparative downwind drift of pollutants in free-floating proportions between States. In reality, however, EPA assesses quantities (in physical units), not proportions. So, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s illustration of a 1-to-5 ratio describing the relative contributions of States X and Y to State A\xe2\x80\x99s pollution might mean (for example) that X is responsible for 0.2 unit of some pollutant above the NAAQS in A and that Y is responsible for 1 unit. Andthe second example, assuming a 7-to-1 ratio underlying State X\xe2\x80\x99s and Y\xe2\x80\x99s contributions to State B\xe2\x80\x99s pollution, might mean that State X supplies 0.7 unit of the same pol-lutant above the NAAQS and State Y, 0.1 unit. Under a proportional-reduction approach, State X would be required to eliminate emissions of that pollutant by what-ever minimum amount reduces both State A\xe2\x80\x99s level by 0.2 unit and State B\xe2\x80\x99s by 0.7 unit. State Y, in turn, would be required to curtail its emissions by whatever minimum amount decreases both State A\xe2\x80\x99s measure by 1 unit and State B\xe2\x80\x99s by 0.1 unit.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n But, the majority objects, the reductions that State X must make to help bring State B into compliance may be more than those necessary for it to help bring State A into compliance, resulting in \xe2\x80\x9cover-control\xe2\x80\x9d of X with respect to A. See ante, at 23\xe2\x80\x9325, and n. 19. This objection discloses the second flaw in the impossibility theorem. Echoing EPA, see Brief for Federal Petitioners 47\xe2\x80\x9348, the majority believes that the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the Good Neighbor Provision forbids over-control with respect to even a single downwind receptor. That is the only way in which the proportional-reduction approach could be deemed \xe2\x80\x9cto work neither mathematically nor in practical application\xe2\x80\x9d on its face. Ante, at 23. But the premise is incorrect. Although some of the D. C. Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s simplified examples might support that conclusion, its opinion explicitly acknowledged that the complexity of real-world conditions demands the contrary: \xe2\x80\x9cTo be sure, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. there may be some truly unavoidable over-control in some downwind States that occurs as a byproduct of the necessity of reducing upwind States\xe2\x80\x99 emissions enough to meet the NAAQS in other downwind States.\xe2\x80\x9d 696 F.\xc2\xa03d 7, 22 (2012). Moreover, the majority itself recognizes that the Good Neighbor Provision does not categorically prohibit over-control. \xe2\x80\x9cAs the Good Neighbor Provision seeks attainment in every downwind State, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. exceeding attainment in one State cannot rank as \xe2\x80\x98over-control\xe2\x80\x99 unless unnecessary to achieving attainment in any downwind State.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 29\xe2\x80\x9330. The majority apparently fails to appreciate that, having cleared up that potential point of confusion, nothing stands in the way of the proportional-reduction approach.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The majority relies on an EPA document preceding the Transport Rule to establish the Agency\xe2\x80\x99s supposed belief that the proportional-reduction approach \xe2\x80\x9ccould scarcely be satisfied in practice.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 23. But the document says no such thing. Rather, it shows that the Agency rejected a proportion-based, \xe2\x80\x9cair[-]quality-only\xe2\x80\x9d methodology not because it was impossible of application, but be-cause it failed to account for costs. See App. in No. 11\xe2\x80\x931302 etc. (CADC), pp. 2311\xe2\x80\x932312. The document labels as a \xe2\x80\x9ctechnical difficulty\xe2\x80\x9d (not an impossibility) the fact that \xe2\x80\x9cmost upwind states contribute to multiple downwind [receptors] (in multiple states) and would have a different reduction percentage for each one.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 2312. The Clean Air Act is full of technical difficulties, and this one is overcome by requiring each State to make the greatest reduction necessary with respect to any downwind area.

\n

2. Over-Control

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Apparently conceding that the proportional-reduction approach may not be impossible of application after all, the majority alternatively asserts that it would cause \xe2\x80\x9ccostly overregulation unnecessary to, indeed in conflict with, the Good Neighbor Provision\xe2\x80\x99s goal of attainment.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 24. This assertion of massive overregulation assumes that a vast number of downwind States will be the accidental beneficiaries of collateral pollution reductions\xe2\x80\x94that is, nontargeted reductions that occur as a consequence of required reductions targeted at neighboring downwind States. (Collateral pollution reduction is the opposite of collateral damage, so to speak.) The majority contends that the collateral pollution reductions enjoyed by a downwind State will cause the required upwind reductions actually targeting that State to exceed the level necessary to assure attainment or maintenance, thus producing unnecessary over-control. I have no reason to believe that the problem of over-control is as extensive and thus \xe2\x80\x9ccostly\xe2\x80\x9d as the majority alleges, and the majority provides none.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n But never mind that. It suffices to say that over-control is no more likely to occur when the required reductions are apportioned among upwind States on the basis of amounts of pollutants contributed than when they are apportioned on the basis of cost. There is no conceivable reason why the efficient-reduction States that bear the brunt of the majority\xe2\x80\x99s (and EPA\xe2\x80\x99s) approach are less likely to be over-controlled than the major-pollution-causing States that would bear the brunt of my (and the statute\xe2\x80\x99s) approach. Indeed, EPA never attempted to establish that the Transport Rule did not produce gross over-control. See 696 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 27. What causes the problem of over-control is not the manner of apportioning the required reductions, but the composite volume of the required reductions in each downwind State. If the majority\xe2\x80\x99s approach reduces over-control (it admittedly does not entirely eliminate it), that is only because EPA applies its cost-effectiveness principle not just to determining the proportions of required reductions that each upwind State must bear, but to determining the volume of those required reductions. See supra, at 7.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In any case, the solution to over-control under a proportional-reduction system is not difficult to discern. In calculating good-neighbor responsibilities, EPA would simply be required to make allowance for what I have called collateral pollution reductions. The Agency would set upwind States\xe2\x80\x99 obligations at levels that, after taking into account those reductions, suffice to produce attainment in all downwind States. Doubtless, there are multiple ways for the Agency to accomplish that task in accordance with the statute\xe2\x80\x99s amounts-based, proportional focus.[2] The majority itself invokes an unexplained device to prevent over-control \xe2\x80\x9cin uncommon particular applications\xe2\x80\x9d of its scheme. Ante, at 31. Whatever that device is, it can serve just as well to prevent over-control under the approach I have outlined.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n I fully acknowledge that the proportional-reduction approach will demand some complicated computations where one upwind State is linked to multiple downwind States and vice versa. I am confident, however, that EPA\xe2\x80\x99s skilled number-crunchers can adhere to the statute\xe2\x80\x99s quantitative (rather than efficiency) mandate by crafting quantitative solutions. Indeed, those calculations can be performed at the desk, whereas the \xe2\x80\x9cfrom each according to its ability\xe2\x80\x9d approach requires the unwieldy field examination of many pollution-producing sources with many sorts of equipment.

\n

D. Plus \xc3\x87a Change:EPA\xe2\x80\x99s Continuing Quest for Cost-Benefit Authority

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The majority agrees with EPA\xe2\x80\x99s assessment that \xe2\x80\x9c[u]sing costs in the Transport Rule calculus .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. makes good sense.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 26. Its opinion declares that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]liminating those amounts that can cost-effectively be reduced is an efficient and equitable solution to the allocation problem the Good Neighbor Provision requires the Agency to address.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Efficient, probably. Equitable? Perhaps so, but perhaps not. See Brief for Industry Respondents 35\xe2\x80\x9336. But the point is that whether efficiency should have a dominant or subordinate role is not for EPA or this Court to determine.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This is not the first time EPA has sought to convert the Clean Air Act into a mandate for cost-effective regulation. Whitman v. American Trucking Assns., Inc.,\n\n531 U.\xc2\xa0S. 457 (2001)\n, confronted EPA\xe2\x80\x99s contention that it could consider costs in setting NAAQS. The provision at issue there, like this one, did not expressly bar cost-based decisionmaking\xe2\x80\x94and unlike this one, it even contained words that were arguably ambiguous in the relevant respect. Specifically, \xc2\xa77409(b)(1) instructed EPA to set primary NAAQS \xe2\x80\x9cthe attainment and maintenance of which .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. are requisite to protect the public health\xe2\x80\x9d with \xe2\x80\x9can adequate margin of safety.\xe2\x80\x9d One could hardly overstate the capaciousness of the word \xe2\x80\x9cadequate,\xe2\x80\x9d and the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cpublic health\xe2\x80\x9d was at least equally susceptible (indeed, much more susceptible) of permitting cost-benefit analysis as the word \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly\xe2\x80\x9d is here. As the respondents in American Trucking argued, setting NAAQS without considering costs may bring about failing industries and fewer jobs, which in turn may produce poorer and less healthy citizens. See id., at 466. But we concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cin the context of\xe2\x80\x9d the entire provision, that interpretation \xe2\x80\x9cma[de] no sense.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. As quoted earlier, we said that Congress \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not alter the fundamental details of a regulatory scheme in vague terms or ancillary provisions\xe2\x80\x94it does not .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. hide elephants in mouseholes.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 468.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In American Trucking, the Court \xe2\x80\x9crefused to find im-plicit in ambiguous sections of the [Clear Air Act] an authorization to consider costs that has elsewhere, and so often, been expressly granted,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 467, citing a tradition dating back to Union Elec. Co. v. EPA,\n\n427 U.\xc2\xa0S. 246\n, and n.\xc2\xa05 (1976). There are, indeed, numerous Clean Air Act provisions explicitly permitting costs to be taken into account. See, e.g., \xc2\xa77404(a)(1); \xc2\xa77521(a)(2); \xc2\xa77545(c)(2); \xc2\xa77547(a)(3); \xc2\xa77554(b)(2); \xc2\xa77571(b); \xc2\xa77651c(f)(1)(A). American Trucking thus demanded \xe2\x80\x9ca textual commitment of authority to the EPA to consider costs,\xe2\x80\x9d 531 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 468\xe2\x80\x94a hurdle that the Good Neighbor Provision comes nowhere close to clearing. Today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion turns its back upon that case and is incompatible with that opinion.[3]

\n

II. Imposition of Federal Implementation Plans

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit vacated the Transport Rule for the additional reason that EPA took the reins in allocating emissions budgets among pollution-producing sources through Federal Implementation Plans (FIPs) without first providing the States a meaningful opportunity to perform that task through State Implementation Plans (SIPs). The majority rejects that ruling on the ground that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Act does not require EPA to furnish upwind States with information of any kind about their good neighbor obligations before a FIP issues.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16. \xe2\x80\x9c[N]othing in the statute,\xe2\x80\x9d the majority says, \xe2\x80\x9cplaces EPA under an obligation to provide specific metrics to States before they undertake to fulfill their good neighbor obligations.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 17. This remarkably expansive reasoning makes a hash of the Clean Air Act, transforming it from a program based on cooperative federalism to one of centralized federal control. Nothing in the Good Neighbor Provision suggests such a stark departure from the Act\xe2\x80\x99s fundamental structure.

\n

A. Implications of State Regulatory Primacy

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Down to its very core, the Clean Air Act sets forth a federalism-focused regulatory strategy. The Act begins by declaring that \xe2\x80\x9cair pollution prevention (that is, the reduction or elimination, through any measures, of the amount of pollutants produced or created at the source) and air pollution control at its source is the primary responsibility of States and local governments.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa77401(a)(3) (emphasis added). State primacy permeates Title I, which addresses the promulgation and implementation of NAAQS, in particular. Under \xc2\xa77409(a), EPA must promulgate NAAQS for each pollutant for which air-quality criteria have been issued pursuant to \xc2\xa77408. Section 7410(a)(1), in turn, requires each State, usually within three years of each new or revised NAAQS, to submit a SIP providing for its \xe2\x80\x9cimplementation, maintenance, and enforcement.\xe2\x80\x9d EPA may step in to take over that responsibility if, and only if, a State discharges it inadequately. Specifically, if the Agency finds that a State has failed to make a required or complete submission or disapproves a SIP, it \xe2\x80\x9cshall promulgate a [FIP] at any time within 2 years .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0, unless the State corrects the deficiency, and [EPA] approves the [SIP] or [SIP] revision.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa77410(c)(1).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n To describe the effect of this statutory scheme in simple terms: After EPA sets numerical air-quality benchmarks, \xe2\x80\x9cCongress plainly left with the States .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the power to determine which sources would be burdened by regulation and to what extent.\xe2\x80\x9d Union Elec. Co., 427 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 269. The States are to present their chosen means of achieving EPA\xe2\x80\x99s benchmarks in SIPs, and only if a SIP fails to meet those goals may the Agency commandeer a State\xe2\x80\x99s authority by promulgating a FIP. \xe2\x80\x9c[S]o long as the ultimate effect of a State\xe2\x80\x99s choice of emission limitations is compliance with the [NAAQS], the State is at liberty to adopt whatever mix of emission limitations it deems best suited to its particular situation.\xe2\x80\x9d Train v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc.,\n\n421 U.\xc2\xa0S. 60,\n79 (1975)\n. EPA, we have emphasized, \xe2\x80\x9cis relegated by the Act to a secondary role in the process of determining and enforcing the spe-cific, source-by-source emission limitations which are necessary if the [NAAQS] are to be met.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Good Neighbor Provision is one of the requirements with which SIPs must comply. \xc2\xa77410(a)(2)(D)(i)(I). The statutory structure described above plainly demands that EPA afford States a meaningful opportunity to allocate reduction responsibilities among the sources within their borders. But the majority holds that EPA may in effect force the States to guess at what those responsibilities might be by requiring them to submit SIPs before learning what the Agency regards as a \xe2\x80\x9csignifican[t]\xe2\x80\x9d contribution\xe2\x80\x94with the consequence of losing their regulatory primacy if they guess wrong. EPA asserts that the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit \xe2\x80\x9cwas wrong as a factual matter\xe2\x80\x9d in reasoning that States cannot feasibly implement the Good Neighbor Provision without knowing what the Agency considers their obligations to be. Brief for Federal Petitioners 29. That is literally unbelievable. The only support that EPA can muster are the assertions that \xe2\x80\x9cStates routinely undertake technically complex air quality determinations\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9cemissions information from all States is publicly available.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. As respondents rightly state: \xe2\x80\x9cAll the scientific knowledge in the world is useless if the States are left to guess the way in which EPA might ultimately quantify \xe2\x80\x98significan[ce].\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for State Respondents 50.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Call it \xe2\x80\x9cpunish[ing] the States for failing to meet a standard that EPA had not yet announced and [they] did not yet know,\xe2\x80\x9d 696 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 28; asking them \xe2\x80\x9cto hit the target .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. before EPA defines [it],\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 32; requiring them \xe2\x80\x9cto take [a] stab in the dark,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 35; or \xe2\x80\x9cset[ting] the States up to fail,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 37. Call it \xe2\x80\x9chid[ing] the ball,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for State Respondents 20; or a \xe2\x80\x9cshell game,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 54. Call it \xe2\x80\x9cpin the tail on the donkey.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 24. As we have recently explained:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIt is one thing to expect regulated parties to conform their conduct to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretations once the agency announces them; it is quite another to require regulated parties to divine the agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpre-tations in advance or else be held liable when the agency announces its interpretations for the firsttime .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and demands deference.\xe2\x80\x9d Christopher v. SmithKline Beecham Corp., 567 U. S. ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 14).

\n

That principle applies a\xc2\xa0fortiori to a regulatory regime that rests on principles of cooperative federalism.

\n

B. Past EPA Practice

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n EPA itself has long acknowledged the proposition that it is nonsensical to expect States to comply with the Good Neighbor Provision absent direction about what constitutes a \xe2\x80\x9csignifican[t]\xe2\x80\x9d contribution to interstate pollution.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Agency consistently adopted that position prior to the Transport Rule. In 1998, when it issued the NOX SIP Call under \xc2\xa77410(k)(5), EPA acknowledged that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]ithout determining an acceptable level of NOX reductions, the upwind State would not have guidance as to what is an acceptable submission.\xe2\x80\x9d 63 Fed. Reg. 57370. EPA deemed it \xe2\x80\x9cmost efficient\xe2\x80\x94indeed necessary\xe2\x80\x94for the Federal government to establish the overall emissions levels for the various States.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Accordingly, the Agency quantified good-neighbor responsibilities and then allowed States a year to submit SIPs to implement them. Id., at 57450\xe2\x80\x9357451.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Similarly, when EPA issued the Clean Air Interstate Rule (CAIR) in 2005 under \xc2\xa77410(c), it explicitly \xe2\x80\x9crecognize[d] that States would face great difficulties in developing transport SIPs to meet the requirements of today\xe2\x80\x99s action without th[e] data and policies\xe2\x80\x9d provided by the Rule, including \xe2\x80\x9cjudgments from EPA concerning the appropriate criteria for determining whether upwind sources contribute significantly to downwind nonattainment under [\xc2\xa774]10(a)(2)(D).\xe2\x80\x9d 70 id., at 25268\xe2\x80\x9325269. The Agency thus gave the States 18 months to submit SIPs implementing their new good-neighbor responsibilities. See id., at 25166\xe2\x80\x9325167, 25176. Although EPA published FIPs before that window closed, it specified that they were meant to serve only as a \xe2\x80\x9cFederal backstop\xe2\x80\x9d and would not become effective unless necessary \xe2\x80\x9ca year after the CAIR SIP submission deadline.\xe2\x80\x9d 71 id., at 25330\xe2\x80\x9325331 (2006).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Even since promulgating the Transport Rule, EPA has repeatedly reaffirmed that States cannot be expected to read the Agency\xe2\x80\x99s mind. In other proceedings, EPA has time and again stated that although \xe2\x80\x9c[s]ome of the elements of the [SIP-submission process] are relatively straightforward, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. others clearly require interpretation by EPA through rulemaking, or recommendations through guidance, in order to give specific meaning for a particular NAAQS.\xe2\x80\x9d 76 id., at 58751 (2011). As an example of the latter, the Agency has remarked that the Good Neighbor Provision \xe2\x80\x9ccontains numerous terms that require substantial rulemaking by EPA in order to determine such basic points as what constitutes significant contribution,\xe2\x80\x9d citing CAIR. Ibid., n.\xc2\xa06. In fact, EPA repeated those precise statements not once, not twice, but 30 times following promulgation of the Transport Rule.[4]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Notwithstanding what parties may have argued in other litigation many years ago, it is beyond responsible debate that the States cannot possibly design FIP-proof SIPs without knowing the EPA-prescribed targets at which they must aim. EPA insists that it enjoys significant discretion\xe2\x80\x94indeed, that it can consider essentially whatever factors it wishes\xe2\x80\x94to determine what constitutes a \xe2\x80\x9csignifican[t]\xe2\x80\x9d contribution to interstate pollution; and it simultaneously asserts that the States ought to know what quantities it will choose. The Agency\xe2\x80\x94and the majority\xe2\x80\x94cannot have it both ways.

\n

C. Abuse of Discretion

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The majority attempts to place the blame for hollowing out the core of the Clean Air Act on \xe2\x80\x9cthe Act\xe2\x80\x99s plain text.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16. The first textual element to which it refers is \xc2\xa77410(c)\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that after EPA has disapproved a SIP, it \xe2\x80\x9cshall promulgate a [FIP] at any time within 2 years.\xe2\x80\x9d That is to say, the Agency has discretion whether to act at once or to defer action until some later point during the 2-year period. But it also has discretion to work within the prescribed timetable to respect the rightful role of States in the statutory scheme by delaying the issuance or enforcement of FIPs pending the resubmission and approval of SIPs\xe2\x80\x94as EPA\xe2\x80\x99s conduct surrounding CAIR clearly demonstrates. And all of this assumes that the Agency insists on disapproving SIPs before promulgating the applicable good-neighbor standards\xe2\x80\x94though in fact EPA has discretion to publicize those metrics before the window to submit SIPs closes in the first place.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The majority states that the Agency \xe2\x80\x9cretained discretion to alter its course\xe2\x80\x9d from the one pursued in the NOX SIP Call and CAIR, ante, at 17, but that misses the point.The point is that EPA has discretion to arrange things so as to preserve the Clean Air Act\xe2\x80\x99s core principle of state primacy\xe2\x80\x94and that it is an abuse of discretion to refuse to do so. See \xc2\xa77607(d)(9)(A); see also\n\n5 U. S. C. \xc2\xa7706(2)(A) (identical text in the Administrative Procedure Act). Indeed, the proviso in \xc2\xa77410(c)(1) that the Agency\xe2\x80\x99s authority to promulgate a FIP within the 2-year period terminates if \xe2\x80\x9cthe State corrects the deficiency, and [EPA] approves the [SIP] or [SIP] revision\xe2\x80\x9d explicitly contemplates just such an arrangement.[5]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The majority\xe2\x80\x99s conception of administrative discretion is so sprawling that it would allow EPA to subvert state primacy not only with respect to the interstate-pollution concerns of the Good Neighbor Provision, but with respect to the much broader concerns of the NAAQS program more generally. States must submit SIPs \xe2\x80\x9cwithin 3 years\xe2\x80\x9d of each new or revised NAAQS \xe2\x80\x9cor such shorter period as [EPA] may prescribe.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa77410(a)(1) (emphasis added). Because there is no principled reason to read that scheduling provision in a less malleable manner than the one at issue here, under the majority\xe2\x80\x99s view EPA could demand that States submit SIPs within a matter of days\xe2\x80\x94or even hours\xe2\x80\x94after a NAAQS publication or else face the immediate imposition of FIPs.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The second element of \xe2\x80\x9cplain text\xe2\x80\x9d on which the majority relies is small beer indeed. The Good Neighbor Provision does not expressly state that EPA must publish target quantities before the States are required to submit SIPs\xe2\x80\x94even though the Clean Air Act does so for NAAQS more generally and for vehicle inspection and maintenance programs, see \xc2\xa77511a(c)(3)(B). From that premise, the majority reasons that \xe2\x80\x9c[h]ad Congress intended similarly to defer States\xe2\x80\x99 discharge of their obligations under the Good Neighbor Provision, Congress .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. would have included a similar direction in that section.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 17. Per-haps so. But EPA itself read the statute differently when it declared in the NOX SIP Call that \xe2\x80\x9c[d]etermining the overall level of air pollutants allowed to be emitted in a State is comparable to determining [NAAQS], which the courts have recognized as EPA\xe2\x80\x99s responsibility, and is distinguishable from determining the particular mix of controls among individual sources to attain those standards, which the caselaw identifies as a State responsibility.\xe2\x80\x9d 63 Fed. Reg. 57369 (emphasis added).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The negative implication suggested by a statute\xe2\x80\x99s failure to use consistent terminology can be a helpful guide to determining meaning, especially when all the provisions in question were enacted at the same time (which is not the case here). But because that interpretive canon, like others, is just one clue to aid construction, it can be overcome by more powerful indications of meaning elsewhere in the statute. It is, we have said, \xe2\x80\x9cno more than a rule of thumb that can tip the scales when a statute could be read in multiple ways.\xe2\x80\x9d Sebelius v. Auburn Regional Medical Center, 568 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2013) (slip op., at 9) (internal quotation marks and brackets omitted). The Clean Air Act simply cannot be read to make EPA the primary regulator in this context. The negative-implication canon is easily overcome by the statute\xe2\x80\x99s state-respecting structure\xe2\x80\x94not to mention the sheer impossibility of submitting a sensible SIP without EPA guidance. Negative implication is the tiniest mousehole in which the majority discovers the elephant of federal control.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Addressing the problem of interstate pollution in the manner Congress has prescribed\xe2\x80\x94or in any other manner, for that matter\xe2\x80\x94is a complex and difficult enterprise. But \xe2\x80\x9c[r]egardless of how serious the problem an administrative agency seeks to address, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. it may not exercise its authority \xe2\x80\x98in a manner that is inconsistent with the administrative structure that Congress enacted into law.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brown & Williamson, 529 U. S., at 125 (quoting ETSI Pipeline Project v. Missouri,\n\n484 U. S. 495,\n517 (1988)\n). The majority\xe2\x80\x99s approval of EPA\xe2\x80\x99s approach to the Clean Air Act violates this foundational principle of popular government.

\n

\nToo many important decisions of the Federal Govern\xc2\xad ment are made nowadays by unelected agency officials exercising broad lawmaking authority, rather than by the people\xe2\x80\x99s representatives in Congress. With the statute involved in the present cases, however, Congress did it right. It specified quite precisely the responsibility of an upwind State under the Good Neighbor Provision: to eliminate those amounts of pollutants that it contributes to downwind problem areas. But the Environmental Protection Agency was unsatisfied with this system. Agency personnel, perhaps correctly, thought it more efficient to require reductions not in proportion to the amounts of pollutants for which each upwind State is responsible, but on the basis of how cost-effectively each can decrease emissions.\n

\n

\nToday, the majority approves that undemocratic revi\xc2\xad sion of the Clean Air Act. The Agency came forward with a textual justification for its action, relying on a farfetched meaning of the word \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly\xe2\x80\x9d in the statutory text. That justification is so feeble that today\xe2\x80\x99s majority does not even recite it, much less defend it. The majority reaches its result (\xe2\x80\x9cLook Ma, no hands!\xe2\x80\x9d) without benefit of text, claiming to have identified a remarkable \xe2\x80\x9cgap\xe2\x80\x9d in the statute, which it proceeds to fill (contrary to the plain logic of the statute) with cost-benefit analysis\xe2\x80\x94and then, with no pretended textual justification at all, simply ex\xc2\xad tends cost-benefit analysis beyond the scope of the alleged gap.\n

\n

\nAdditionally, the majority relieves EPA of any obligation to announce novel interpretations of the Good Neighbor Provision before the States must submit plans that are required to comply with those interpretations. By accord\xc2\xad ing the States primacy in deciding how to attain the gov\xc2\xad erning air-quality standards, the Clean Air Act is preg\xc2\xad nant with an obligation for the Agency to set those standards before the States can be expected to achieve them. The majority nonetheless approves EPA\xe2\x80\x99s promul\xc2\xad gation of federal plans implementing good-neighbor benchmarks before the States could conceivably have met those benchmarks on their own.\n

\n

\nI. The Transport Rule\n

\n

\n\xe2\x80\x9cIt is axiomatic that an administrative agency\xe2\x80\x99s power to promulgate legislative regulations is limited to the author\xc2\xad ity delegated by Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Bowen v. Georgetown Univ. Hospital, 488 U. S. 204, 208 (1988). Yet today the major- ity treats the text of the Clean Air Act not as the source and ceiling of EPA\xe2\x80\x99s authority to regulate interstate air pollution, but rather as a difficulty to be overcome in pursuit of the Agency\xe2\x80\x99s responsibility to \xe2\x80\x9ccraf[t] a solution to the problem of interstate air pollution.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 3. In reality, Congress itself has crafted the solution. The Good Neighbor Provision requires each State to eliminate what\xc2\xad ever \xe2\x80\x9camounts\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9cair pollutant[s]\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ccontribute significantly to nonattainment\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cinterfere with maintenance\xe2\x80\x9d of national ambient air-quality standards (NAAQS) in other States. 42 U. S. C. \xc2\xa77410(a)(2)(D)(i)(I). The statute ad\xc2\xad dresses solely the environmental consequences of emis\xc2\xad sions, not the facility of reducing them; and it requires States to shoulder burdens in proportion to the size of their contributions, not in proportion to the ease of bearing them. EPA\xe2\x80\x99s utterly fanciful \xe2\x80\x9cfrom each according to its ability\xe2\x80\x9d construction sacrifices democratically adopted text to bureaucratically favored policy. It deserves no defer\xc2\xad ence under Chevron U. S. A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., 467 U. S. 837 (1984).\n

\n

\nA. Alleged Textual Support: \xe2\x80\x9cSignificantly\xe2\x80\x9d\n

\n

\nIn the Government\xe2\x80\x99s argument here, the asserted tex\xc2\xad tual support for the efficient-reduction approach adopted by EPA in the Transport Rule is the ambiguity of the word \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly\xe2\x80\x9d in the statutory requirement that each State eliminate those \xe2\x80\x9camounts\xe2\x80\x9d of pollutants that \xe2\x80\x9ccon\xc2\xad tribute significantly to nonattainment\xe2\x80\x9d in downwind States. \xc2\xa77410(a)(2)(D)(i)(I) (emphasis added). As de\xc2\xad scribed in the Government\xe2\x80\x99s briefing:\n

\n

\n\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he term \xe2\x80\x98significantly\xe2\x80\x99 . . . is ambiguous, and . . . EPA may permissibly determine the amount of a State\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x98significant\xe2\x80\x99 contribution by reference to the amount of emissions reductions achievable through application of highly cost-effective controls.\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief for Federal Petitioners 15\xe2\x80\x9316 (emphasis added; some internal quotation marks omitted).\n

\n

\nAnd as the Government stated at oral argument:\n

\n

\n\xe2\x80\x9c[I]n terms of the language, \xe2\x80\x98contribute significantly,\xe2\x80\x99 . . . EPA reasonably construed that term to include a component of difficulty of achievement [i.e., cost]; that is, in common parlance, we might say that dunking a basketball is a more significant achievement for somebody who is 5 feet 10 than for somebody who is 6 feet 10.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 9 (emphasis added).\n

\n

\nBut of course the statute does not focus on whether the upwind State has \xe2\x80\x9cachieved significantly\xe2\x80\x9d; it asks whether the State has \xe2\x80\x9ccontributed significantly\xe2\x80\x9d to downwind pollution. The provision addresses the physical effects of physical causes, and it is only the magnitude of the rela\xc2\xad tionship sufficient to trigger regulation that admits of some vagueness. Stated differently, the statute is ambig\xc2\xad uous as to how much of a contribution to downwind pollu\xc2\xad tion is \xe2\x80\x9csignificant,\xe2\x80\x9d but it is not at all ambiguous as to whether factors unrelated to the amounts of pollutants that make up a contribution affect the analysis. Just as \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t does not matter whether the word \xe2\x80\x98yellow\xe2\x80\x99 is ambigu\xc2\xad ous when the agency has interpreted it to mean \xe2\x80\x98purple,\xe2\x80\x99\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Home Concrete & Supply, LLC, 566 U. S. ___, ___, n. 1 (2012) (SCALIA, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment) (slip op., at 2, n. 1), it does not matter whether the phrase \xe2\x80\x9camounts which . . . contribute significantly [to downwind NAAQS nonattainment]\xe2\x80\x9d is ambiguous when EPA has interpreted it to mean \xe2\x80\x9camounts which are inexpensive to eliminate.\xe2\x80\x9d\n

\n

\nIt would be extraordinary for Congress, by use of the single word \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly,\xe2\x80\x9d to transmogrify a statute that assigns responsibility on the basis of amounts of pollut\xc2\xad ants emitted into a statute authorizing EPA to reduce interstate pollution in the manner that it believes most efficient. We have repeatedly said that Congress \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not alter the fundamental details of a regulatory scheme in vague terms or ancillary provisions\xe2\x80\x94it does not, one might say, hide elephants in mouseholes.\xe2\x80\x9d Whitman v. American Trucking Assns., Inc., 531 U. S. 457, 468 (2001) (citing MCI Telecommunications Corp. v. American Tele- phone & Telegraph Co., 512 U. S. 218, 231 (1994); FDA v. Brown & Williamson Tobacco Corp., 529 U. S. 120, 159\xe2\x80\x93 160 (2000)).\n

\n

\nThe statute\xe2\x80\x99s history demonstrates that \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly\xe2\x80\x9d is not code for \xe2\x80\x9cfeel free to consider compliance costs.\xe2\x80\x9d The previous version of the Good Neighbor Provision required each State to prohibit emissions that would \xe2\x80\x9cprevent at\xc2\xad tainment or maintenance by any other State of any [NAAQS].\xe2\x80\x9d 91 Stat. 693 (emphasis added). It is evident that the current reformulation (targeting \xe2\x80\x9cany air pollut- ant in amounts which will . . . contribute significantly to nonattainment in, or interfere with maintenance by, any other State with respect to any [NAAQS]\xe2\x80\x9d) was meant simply to eliminate any implication that the polluting State had to be a but-for rather than merely a contributing cause of the downwind nonattainment or maintenance problem\xe2\x80\x94not to allow cost concerns to creep in through the back door.\n

\n

\nIn another respect also EPA\xe2\x80\x99s reliance upon the word \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly\xe2\x80\x9d is plainly mistaken. The Good Neighbor Provision targets for elimination not only those emissions that \xe2\x80\x9ccontribute significantly to nonattainment [of NAAQS] in . . . any other State,\xe2\x80\x9d but also those that \xe2\x80\x9cinter\xc2\xad fere with maintenance [of NAAQS] by . . . any other State.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa77410(a)(2)(D)(i)(I). The wiggle-word \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly\xe2\x80\x9d is absent from the latter phrase. EPA does not\xe2\x80\x94 cannot\xe2\x80\x94provide any textual justification for the conclu\xc2\xad sion that, when the same amounts of a pollutant travel downwind from States X and Y to a single area in State A, the emissions from X but not Y can be said to \xe2\x80\x9cinterfere with maintenance\xe2\x80\x9d of the NAAQS in A just because they are cheaper to eliminate. Yet EPA proposes to use the \xe2\x80\x9cfrom each according to its ability\xe2\x80\x9d approach for nonat\xc2\xad tainment areas and maintenance areas.\n

\n

\nTo its credit, the majority does not allude to, much less try to defend, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly\xe2\x80\x9d argument. But there is a serious downside to this. The sky-hook of \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly\xe2\x80\x9d was called into service to counter the crite\xc2\xad rion of upwind-state responsibility plainly provided in the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text: amounts of pollutants contributed to down\xc2\xad wind problem areas. See Brief for Federal Petitioners 42\xe2\x80\x93 45. Having forsworn reliance on \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly\xe2\x80\x9d to convert responsibility for amounts of pollutants into responsibility for easy reduction of pollutants, the majority is impaled upon the statutory text.\n

\n

\nB. The Alleged \xe2\x80\x9cGap\xe2\x80\x9d\n

\n

\nTo fill the void created by its abandonment of EPA\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly\xe2\x80\x9d argument, the majority identifies a sup\xc2\xad posed gap in the text, which EPA must fill: While the text says that each upwind State must be responsible for its own contribution to downwind pollution, it does not say how responsibility is to be divided among multiple States when the total of their combined contribution to downwind pollution in a particular area exceeds the reduction that the relevant NAAQS requires. In the example given by the majority, ante, at 21\xe2\x80\x9322, when each of three upwind States contributes 30 units of a pollutant to a downwind State but the reduction required for that State to comply with the NAAQS is only 30 units, how will responsibility for that 30 units be apportioned? Wow, that\xe2\x80\x99s a hard one\xe2\x80\x94almost the equivalent of asking who is buried in Grant\xe2\x80\x99s Tomb. If the criterion of responsibility is amounts of pollutants, then surely shared responsibility must be based upon relative amounts of pollutants\xe2\x80\x94in the major\xc2\xad ity\xe2\x80\x99s example, 10 units for each State. The statute makes no sense otherwise. The Good Neighbor Provision con- tains a gap only for those who blind themselves to the obvious in order to pursue a preferred policy.\n

\n

\nBut not only does the majority bring in cost-benefit analysis to fill a gap that does not really exist. Having filled that \xe2\x80\x9cgap,\xe2\x80\x9d it then extends the efficiency-based prin- ciple to situations beyond the imaginary gap\xe2\x80\x94that is, situations where no apportionment is required. Even where only a single upwind State contributes pollutants to a downwind State, its annual emissions \xe2\x80\x9cbudget\xe2\x80\x9d will be based not upon the amounts of pollutants it contributes, but upon what \xe2\x80\x9cpollution controls [are] available at the chosen cost thresholds.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9. EPA\xe2\x80\x99s justification was its implausible (and only half-applicable) notion that \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly\xe2\x80\x9d imports cost concerns into the provision. The majority, having abandoned that absurdity, is left to deal with the no-apportionment situation with no de- fense\xe2\x80\x94not even an imaginary gap\xe2\x80\x94against a crystal-clear statutory text.\n

\n

\nC. The Majority\xe2\x80\x99s Criticisms of Proportional Reduction 1. Impossibility\n

\n

\nThe majority contends that a proportional-reduction approach \xe2\x80\x9ccould scarcely be satisfied in practice\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cappears to work neither mathematically nor in practical application,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 23\xe2\x80\x94in essence, that the approach is impossible of application. If that were true, I know of no legal authority and no democratic principle that would derive from it the consequence that EPA could rewrite the statute, rather than the consequence that the statute would be inoperative. \xe2\x80\x9cThere are sometimes statutes which no rule or canon of interpretation can make effec- tive or applicable to the situations of fact which they purport to govern. In such cases the statute must simply fail.\xe2\x80\x9d 3 R. Pound, Jurisprudence 493 (1959) (footnote omitted). In other words, the impossibility argument has no independent force: It is relevant only if the majority\xe2\x80\x99s textual interpretation is permissible. But in any event, the argument is wrong.\n

\n

\nThe impossibility theorem rests upon the following scenario: \xe2\x80\x9cImagine that States X and Y . . . contribute air pollutiontoStateAinaratioofonetofive....\xe2\x80\x9d Ante,at 23. And suppose that \xe2\x80\x9cStates X and Y also contribute pollutants to a second downwind State (State B), this time in a ratio of seven to one.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The majority concludes that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 proportionality edict with respect to both State A and State B appears to work nei\xc2\xad ther mathematically nor in practical application.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. But why not? The majority\xe2\x80\x99s model relies on two faulty premises\xe2\x80\x94one an oversimplification and the other a mis\xc2\xad apprehension.\n

\n

\nFirst, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s formulation suggests that EPA measures the comparative downwind drift of pollutants in free-floating proportions between States. In reality, how\xc2\xad ever, EPA assesses quantities (in physical units), not proportions. So, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s illustration of a 1-to-5 ratio describing the relative contributions of States X and Y to State A\xe2\x80\x99s pollution might mean (for example) that X is responsible for 0.2 unit of some pollutant above the NAAQS in A and that Y is responsible for 1 unit. And the second example, assuming a 7-to-1 ratio underlying State X\xe2\x80\x99s and Y\xe2\x80\x99s contributions to State B\xe2\x80\x99s pollution, might mean that State X supplies 0.7 unit of the same pol\xc2\xad lutant above the NAAQS and State Y, 0.1 unit. Under a proportional-reduction approach, State X would be re\xc2\xad quired to eliminate emissions of that pollutant by what- ever minimum amount reduces both State A\xe2\x80\x99s level by 0.2 unit and State B\xe2\x80\x99s by 0.7 unit. State Y, in turn, would be required to curtail its emissions by whatever minimum amount decreases both State A\xe2\x80\x99s measure by 1 unit and State B\xe2\x80\x99s by 0.1 unit.\n

\n

\nBut, the majority objects, the reductions that State X must make to help bring State B into compliance may be more than those necessary for it to help bring State A into compliance, resulting in \xe2\x80\x9cover-control\xe2\x80\x9d of X with respect to A. See ante, at 23\xe2\x80\x9325, and n. 19. This objection discloses the second flaw in the impossibility theorem. Echoing EPA, see Brief for Federal Petitioners 47\xe2\x80\x9348, the majority believes that the D. C. Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the Good Neighbor Provision forbids over-control with respect to even a single downwind receptor. That is the only way in which the proportional-reduction approach could be deemed \xe2\x80\x9cto work neither mathematically nor in practical application\xe2\x80\x9d on its face. Ante, at 23. But the premise is incorrect. Although some of the D. C. Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s simplified examples might support that conclusion, its opinion explic\xc2\xad itly acknowledged that the complexity of real-world condi\xc2\xad tions demands the contrary: \xe2\x80\x9cTo be sure, . . . there may be some truly unavoidable over-control in some downwind States that occurs as a byproduct of the necessity of reduc\xc2\xad ing upwind States\xe2\x80\x99 emissions enough to meet the NAAQS in other downwind States.\xe2\x80\x9d 696 F. 3d 7, 22 (2012). More\xc2\xad over, the majority itself recognizes that the Good Neighbor Provision does not categorically prohibit over-control. \xe2\x80\x9cAs the Good Neighbor Provision seeks attainment in every downwind State, . . . exceeding attainment in one State cannot rank as \xe2\x80\x98over-control\xe2\x80\x99 unless unnecessary to achiev\xc2\xad ing attainment in any downwind State.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 29\xe2\x80\x9330. The majority apparently fails to appreciate that, having cleared up that potential point of confusion, nothing stands in the way of the proportional-reduction approach.\n

\n

\nThe majority relies on an EPA document preceding the Transport Rule to establish the Agency\xe2\x80\x99s supposed belief that the proportional-reduction approach \xe2\x80\x9ccould scarcely be satisfied in practice.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 23. But the document says no such thing. Rather, it shows that the Agency rejected a proportion-based, \xe2\x80\x9cair[-]quality-only\xe2\x80\x9d methodol\xc2\xad ogy not because it was impossible of application, but because it failed to account for costs. See App. in No. 11\xe2\x80\x93 1302 etc. (CADC), pp. 2311\xe2\x80\x932312. The document labels as a \xe2\x80\x9ctechnical difficulty\xe2\x80\x9d (not an impossibility) the fact that \xe2\x80\x9cmost upwind states contribute to multiple downwind [receptors] (in multiple states) and would have a different reduction percentage for each one.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 2312. The Clean Air Act is full of technical difficulties, and this one is overcome by requiring each State to make the greatest reduction necessary with respect to any downwind area.\n

\n

\n2. Over-Control\n

\n

\nApparently conceding that the proportional-reduction approach may not be impossible of application after all, the majority alternatively asserts that it would cause \xe2\x80\x9ccostly overregulation unnecessary to, indeed in conflict with, the Good Neighbor Provision\xe2\x80\x99s goal of attainment.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 24. This assertion of massive overregulation assumes that a vast number of downwind States will be the accidental beneficiaries of collateral pollution reduc\xc2\xad tions\xe2\x80\x94that is, nontargeted reductions that occur as a consequence of required reductions targeted at neighbor\xc2\xad ing downwind States. (Collateral pollution reduction is the opposite of collateral damage, so to speak.) The major\xc2\xad ity contends that the collateral pollution reductions en\xc2\xad joyed by a downwind State will cause the required upwind reductions actually targeting that State to exceed the level necessary to assure attainment or maintenance, thus producing unnecessary over-control. I have no reason to believe that the problem of over-control is as extensive and thus \xe2\x80\x9ccostly\xe2\x80\x9d as the majority alleges, and the majority provides none.\n

\n

\nBut never mind that. It suffices to say that over-control is no more likely to occur when the required reductions are apportioned among upwind States on the basis of amounts of pollutants contributed than when they are apportioned on the basis of cost. There is no conceivable reason why the efficient-reduction States that bear the brunt of the majority\xe2\x80\x99s (and EPA\xe2\x80\x99s) approach are less likely to be over\xc2\xad controlled than the major-pollution-causing States that would bear the brunt of my (and the statute\xe2\x80\x99s) approach. Indeed, EPA never attempted to establish that the Transport Rule did not produce gross over-control. See 696 F. 3d, at 27. What causes the problem of over-control is not the manner of apportioning the required reductions, but the composite volume of the required reductions in each downwind State. If the majority\xe2\x80\x99s approach reduces over-control (it admittedly does not entirely eliminate it), that is only because EPA applies its cost-effectiveness principle not just to determining the proportions of re\xc2\xad quired reductions that each upwind State must bear, but to determining the volume of those required reductions. See supra, at 7.\n

\n

\nIn any case, the solution to over-control under a propor\xc2\xad tional-reduction system is not difficult to discern. In calculating good-neighbor responsibilities, EPA would simply be required to make allowance for what I have called collateral pollution reductions. The Agency would set upwind States\xe2\x80\x99 obligations at levels that, after taking into account those reductions, suffice to produce attain\xc2\xad ment in all downwind States. Doubtless, there are multi\xc2\xad ple ways for the Agency to accomplish that task in accord\xc2\xad ance with the statute\xe2\x80\x99s amounts-based, proportional focus.2 The majority itself invokes an unexplained device to prevent over-control \xe2\x80\x9cin uncommon particular applica\xc2\xad tions\xe2\x80\x9d of its scheme. Ante, at 31. Whatever that device is, it can serve just as well to prevent over-control under the approach I have outlined.\n

\n

\nI fully acknowledge that the proportional-reduction approach will demand some complicated computations where one upwind State is linked to multiple downwind States and vice versa. I am confident, however, that EPA\xe2\x80\x99s skilled number-crunchers can adhere to the stat- ute\xe2\x80\x99s quantitative (rather than efficiency) mandate by crafting quantitative solutions. Indeed, those calculations can be performed at the desk, whereas the \xe2\x80\x9cfrom each according to its ability\xe2\x80\x9d approach requires the unwieldy field examination of many pollution-producing sources with many sorts of equipment.\n

\n

\nD. Our Precedent\n

\n

\nThe majority agrees with EPA\xe2\x80\x99s assessment that \xe2\x80\x9c[u]sing costs in the Transport Rule calculus . . . makes good sense.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 26. Its opinion declares that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]liminating those amounts that can cost-effectively be reduced is an efficient and equitable solution to the alloca- tion problem the Good Neighbor Provision requires the Agency to address.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Efficient, probably. Equitable? Perhaps so, but perhaps not. See Brief for Industry Re- spondents 35\xe2\x80\x9336. But the point is that whether efficiency should have a dominant or subordinate role is for Con- gress, not this Court, to determine.\n

\n

\nThis is not the first time parties have sought to convert the Clean Air Act into a mandate for cost-effective regula- tion. Whitman v. American Trucking Assns., Inc., 531 U.S. 457 (2001), confronted the contention that EPA should consider costs in setting NAAQS. The provision at issue there, like this one, did not expressly bar cost-based decisionmaking\xe2\x80\x94and unlike this one, it even contained words that were arguably ambiguous in the relevant respect. Specifically, \xc2\xa77409(b)(1) instructed EPA to set primary NAAQS \xe2\x80\x9cthe attainment and maintenance of which . . . are requisite to protect the public health\xe2\x80\x9d with \xe2\x80\x9can adequate margin of safety.\xe2\x80\x9d One could hardly overstate the capaciousness of the word \xe2\x80\x9cadequate,\xe2\x80\x9d and the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cpublic health\xe2\x80\x9d was at least equally susceptible (indeed, much more susceptible) of permitting cost-benefit analysis as the word \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly\xe2\x80\x9d is here. As the re- spondents in American Trucking argued, setting NAAQS without considering costs may bring about failing indus- tries and fewer jobs, which in turn may produce poorer and less healthy citizens. See id., at 466. But we conclud- ed that \xe2\x80\x9cin the context of \xe2\x80\x9d the entire provision, that inter- pretation \xe2\x80\x9cma[de] no sense.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. As quoted earlier, we said that Congress \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not alter the fundamental details of a regulatory scheme in vague terms or ancillary provi- sions\xe2\x80\x94it does not . . . hide elephants in mouseholes.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 468.

\n

\nIn American Trucking, the Court \xe2\x80\x9crefused to find im- plicit in ambiguous sections of the [Clear Air Act] an authorization to consider costs that has elsewhere, and so often, been expressly granted,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 467, citing a tradition dating back to Union Elec. Co. v. EPA, 427 U. S. 246, 257, and n. 5 (1976). There are, indeed, numerous Clean Air Act provisions explicitly permitting costs to be taken into account. See, e.g., \xc2\xa77404(a)(1); \xc2\xa77521(a)(2); \xc2\xa77545(c)(2); \xc2\xa77547(a)(3); \xc2\xa77554(b)(2); \xc2\xa77571(b); \xc2\xa77651c(f)(1)(A). Ameri- can Trucking thus demanded \xe2\x80\x9ca textual commitment of authority to the EPA to consider costs,\xe2\x80\x9d 531 U. S., at 468\xe2\x80\x94 a hurdle that the Good Neighbor Provision comes nowhere close to clearing. Today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion turns its back upon that case and is incompatible with that opinion.3\n

\n

\nII. Imposition of Federal Implementation Plans\nThe D. C. Circuit vacated the Transport Rule for the additional reason that EPA took the reins in allocating emissions budgets among pollution-producing sources through Federal Implementation Plans (FIPs) without first providing the States a meaningful opportunity to perform that task through State Implementation Plans (SIPs). The majority rejects that ruling on the ground that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Act does not require EPA to furnish upwind States with information of any kind about their good neighbor obligations before a FIP issues.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16. \xe2\x80\x9c[N]othing in the statute,\xe2\x80\x9d the majority says, \xe2\x80\x9cplaces EPA under an obligation to provide specific metrics to States before they undertake to fulfill their good neighbor obliga\xc2\xad tions.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 17. This remarkably expansive reasoning makes a hash of the Clean Air Act, transforming it from a program based on cooperative federalism to one of central\xc2\xad ized federal control. Nothing in the Good Neighbor Provi\xc2\xad sion suggests such a stark departure from the Act\xe2\x80\x99s fun\xc2\xad damental structure.\n

\n

\nA. Implications of State Regulatory Primacy\n

\n

\nDown to its very core, the Clean Air Act sets forth a federalism-focused regulatory strategy. The Act begins by declaring that \xe2\x80\x9cair pollution prevention (that is, the reduc\xc2\xad tion or elimination, through any measures, of the amount of pollutants produced or created at the source) and air pollution control at its source is the primary responsibility of States and local governments.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa77401(a)(3) (emphasis added). State primacy permeates Title I, which addresses the promulgation and implementation of NAAQS, in par\xc2\xad ticular. Under \xc2\xa77409(a), EPA must promulgate NAAQS for each pollutant for which air-quality criteria have been issued pursuant to \xc2\xa77408. Section 7410(a)(1), in turn, requires each State, usually within three years of each new or revised NAAQS, to submit a SIP providing for its \xe2\x80\x9cimplementation, maintenance, and enforcement.\xe2\x80\x9d EPA may step in to take over that responsibility if, and only if, a State discharges it inadequately. Specifically, if the Agency finds that a State has failed to make a required or complete submission or disapproves a SIP, it \xe2\x80\x9cshall prom\xc2\xad ulgate a [FIP] at any time within 2 years . . . , unless the State corrects the deficiency, and [EPA] approves the [SIP] or [SIP] revision.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa77410(c)(1).\n

\n

\nTo describe the effect of this statutory scheme in simple terms: After EPA sets numerical air-quality benchmarks, \xe2\x80\x9cCongress plainly left with the States . . . the power to determine which sources would be burdened by regulation and to what extent.\xe2\x80\x9d Union Elec. Co., 427 U. S., at 269. The States are to present their chosen means of achieving EPA\xe2\x80\x99s benchmarks in SIPs, and only if a SIP fails to meet those goals may the Agency commandeer a State\xe2\x80\x99s author\xc2\xad ity by promulgating a FIP. \xe2\x80\x9c[S]o long as the ultimate effect of a State\xe2\x80\x99s choice of emission limitations is compli\xc2\xad ance with the [NAAQS], the State is at liberty to adopt whatever mix of emission limitations it deems best suited to its particular situation.\xe2\x80\x9d Train v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., 421 U. S. 60, 79 (1975). EPA, we have emphasized, \xe2\x80\x9cis relegated by the Act to a secondary role in the process of determining and enforcing the spe- cific, source-by-source emission limitations which are necessary if the [NAAQS] are to be met.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n

\n

\nThe Good Neighbor Provision is one of the requirements with which SIPs must comply. \xc2\xa77410(a)(2)(D)(i)(I). The statutory structure described above plainly demands that EPA afford States a meaningful opportunity to allocate reduction responsibilities among the sources within their borders. But the majority holds that EPA may in effect force the States to guess at what those responsibilities might be by requiring them to submit SIPs before learning what the Agency regards as a \xe2\x80\x9csignifican[t]\xe2\x80\x9d contribu\xc2\xad tion\xe2\x80\x94with the consequence of losing their regulatory primacy if they guess wrong. EPA asserts that the D. C. Circuit \xe2\x80\x9cwas wrong as a factual matter\xe2\x80\x9d in reasoning that States cannot feasibly implement the Good Neighbor Provision without knowing what the Agency considers their obligations to be. Brief for Federal Petitioners 29. That is literally unbelievable. The only support that EPA can muster are the assertions that \xe2\x80\x9cStates routinely un\xc2\xad dertake technically complex air quality determinations\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9cemissions information from all States is publicly available.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. As respondents rightly state: \xe2\x80\x9cAll the scientific knowledge in the world is useless if the States are left to guess the way in which EPA might ultimately quantify \xe2\x80\x98significan[ce].\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9d Brief for State Respondents 50.\n

\n

\nCall it \xe2\x80\x9cpunish[ing] the States for failing to meet a standard that EPA had not yet announced and [they] did not yet know,\xe2\x80\x9d 696 F. 3d, at 28; asking them \xe2\x80\x9cto hit the target . . . before EPA defines [it],\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 32; requiring them \xe2\x80\x9cto take [a] stab in the dark,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 35; or \xe2\x80\x9cset[ting] the States up to fail,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 37. Call it \xe2\x80\x9chid[ing] the ball,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for State Respondents 20; or a \xe2\x80\x9cshell game,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 54. Call it \xe2\x80\x9cpin the tail on the donkey.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 24. As we have recently explained:\n

\n

\n\xe2\x80\x9cIt is one thing to expect regulated parties to conform their conduct to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretations once the agency announces them; it is quite another to require regulated parties to divine the agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpre\xc2\xad tations in advance or else be held liable when the agency announces its interpretations for the first time . . . and demands deference.\xe2\x80\x9d Christopher v. SmithKline Beecham Corp., 567 U. S. ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 14).\n

\n

That principle applies a fortiori to a regulatory regime that rests on principles of cooperative federalism.

\n

B. Past EPA Practice

\n

EPA itself has long acknowledged the proposition that it is nonsensical to expect States to comply with the Good Neighbor Provision absent direction about what consti\xc2\xad tutes a \xe2\x80\x9csignifican[t]\xe2\x80\x9d contribution to interstate pollution.

\n

The Agency consistently adopted that position prior to the Transport Rule. In 1998, when it issued the NOX SIP Call under \xc2\xa77410(k)(5), EPA acknowledged that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]ithout determining an acceptable level of NOX reductions, the upwind State would not have guidance as to what is an acceptable submission.\xe2\x80\x9d 63 Fed. Reg. 57370. EPA deemed it \xe2\x80\x9cmost efficient\xe2\x80\x94indeed necessary\xe2\x80\x94for the Federal government to establish the overall emissions levels for the various States.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Accordingly, the Agency quanti\xc2\xad fied good-neighbor responsibilities and then allowed States a year to submit SIPs to implement them. Id., at 57450\xe2\x80\x9357451.

\n

Similarly, when EPA issued the Clean Air Interstate Rule (CAIR) in 2005 under \xc2\xa77410(c), it explicitly \xe2\x80\x9crecog\xc2\xad nize[d] that States would face great difficulties in develop\xc2\xad ing transport SIPs to meet the requirements of today\xe2\x80\x99s action without th[e] data and policies\xe2\x80\x9d provided by the Rule, including \xe2\x80\x9cjudgments from EPA concerning the appropriate criteria for determining whether upwind sources contribute significantly to downwind nonattain\xc2\xad ment under [\xc2\xa774]10(a)(2)(D).\xe2\x80\x9d 70 id., at 25268\xe2\x80\x9325269. The Agency thus gave the States 18 months to submit SIPs implementing their new good-neighbor responsibili\xc2\xad ties. See id., at 25166\xe2\x80\x9325167, 25176. Although EPA published FIPs before that window closed, it specified that they were meant to serve only as a \xe2\x80\x9cFederal backstop\xe2\x80\x9d and would not become effective unless necessary \xe2\x80\x9ca year after the CAIR SIP submission deadline.\xe2\x80\x9d 71 id., at 25330\xe2\x80\x93 25331 (2006).

\n

Even since promulgating the Transport Rule, EPA has repeatedly reaffirmed that States cannot be expected to read the Agency\xe2\x80\x99s mind. In other proceedings, EPA has time and again stated that although \xe2\x80\x9c[s]ome of the ele\xc2\xad ments of the [SIP-submission process] are relatively straightforward, . . . others clearly require interpretation by EPA through rulemaking, or recommendations through guidance, in order to give specific meaning for a particular NAAQS.\xe2\x80\x9d 76 id., at 58751 (2011). As an example of the latter, the Agency has remarked that the Good Neighbor Provision \xe2\x80\x9ccontains numerous terms that require substan\xc2\xad tial rulemaking by EPA in order to determine such basic points as what constitutes significant contribution,\xe2\x80\x9d citing CAIR. Ibid., n. 6. In fact, EPA repeated those precise statements not once, not twice, but 30 times following promulgation of the Transport Rule.4

\n

Notwithstanding what parties may have argued in other litigation many years ago, it is beyond responsible debate that the States cannot possibly design FIP-proof SIPs without knowing the EPA-prescribed targets at which they must aim. EPA insists that it enjoys significant discretion\xe2\x80\x94indeed, that it can consider essentially what\xc2\xad ever factors it wishes\xe2\x80\x94to determine what constitutes a \xe2\x80\x9csignifican[t]\xe2\x80\x9d contribution to interstate pollution; and it simultaneously asserts that the States ought to know what quantities it will choose. The Agency\xe2\x80\x94and the majority\xe2\x80\x94cannot have it both ways.

\n

C. Abuse of Discretion

\n

The majority attempts to place the blame for hollowing out the core of the Clean Air Act on \xe2\x80\x9cthe Act\xe2\x80\x99s plain text.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16. The first textual element to which it refers is \xc2\xa77410(c)\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that after EPA has disapproved a SIP, it \xe2\x80\x9cshall promulgate a [FIP] at any time within 2 years.\xe2\x80\x9d That is to say, the Agency has discretion whether to act at once or to defer action until some later point during the 2-year period. But it also has discretion to work within the prescribed timetable to respect the right\xc2\xad ful role of States in the statutory scheme by delaying the issuance or enforcement of FIPs pending the resubmission and approval of SIPs\xe2\x80\x94as EPA\xe2\x80\x99s conduct surrounding CAIR clearly demonstrates. And all of this assumes that the Agency insists on disapproving SIPs before promulgat\xc2\xad ing the applicable good-neighbor standards\xe2\x80\x94though in fact EPA has discretion to publicize those metrics before the window to submit SIPs closes in the first place.

\n

The majority states that the Agency \xe2\x80\x9cretained discretion to alter its course\xe2\x80\x9d from the one pursued in the NOX SIP Call and CAIR, ante, at 17, but that misses the point. The point is that EPA has discretion to arrange things so as to preserve the Clean Air Act\xe2\x80\x99s core principle of state primacy\xe2\x80\x94and that it is an abuse of discretion to refuse to do so. See \xc2\xa77607(d)(9)(A); see also 5 U. S. C. \xc2\xa7706(2)(A) (identical text in the Administrative Procedure Act). Indeed, the proviso in \xc2\xa77410(c)(1) that the Agency\xe2\x80\x99s au\xc2\xad thority to promulgate a FIP within the 2-year period terminates if \xe2\x80\x9cthe State corrects the deficiency, and [EPA] approves the [SIP] or [SIP] revision\xe2\x80\x9d explicitly contem\xc2\xad plates just such an arrangement.5

\n

The majority\xe2\x80\x99s conception of administrative discretion is so sprawling that it would allow EPA to subvert state primacy not only with respect to the interstate-pollution concerns of the Good Neighbor Provision, but with respect to the much broader concerns of the NAAQS program more generally. States must submit SIPs \xe2\x80\x9cwithin 3 years\xe2\x80\x9d of each new or revised NAAQS \xe2\x80\x9cor such shorter period as [EPA] may prescribe.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa77410(a)(1) (emphasis added). Because there is no principled reason to read that schedul\xc2\xad ing provision in a less malleable manner than the one at issue here, under the majority\xe2\x80\x99s view EPA could demand that States submit SIPs within a matter of days\xe2\x80\x94or even hours\xe2\x80\x94after a NAAQS publication or else face the imme\xc2\xad diate imposition of FIPs.

\n

The second element of \xe2\x80\x9cplain text\xe2\x80\x9d on which the majority relies is small beer indeed. The Good Neighbor Provision does not expressly state that EPA must publish target quantities before the States are required to submit SIPs\xe2\x80\x94 even though the Clean Air Act does so for NAAQS more generally and for vehicle inspection and maintenance programs, see \xc2\xa77511a(c)(3)(B). From that premise, the majority reasons that \xe2\x80\x9c[h]ad Congress intended similarly to defer States\xe2\x80\x99 discharge of their obligations under the Good Neighbor Provision, Congress . . . would have included a similar direction in that section.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 17. Per\xc2\xad haps so. But EPA itself read the statute differently when it declared in the NOX SIP Call that \xe2\x80\x9c[d]etermining the overall level of air pollutants allowed to be emitted in a State is comparable to determining [NAAQS], which the courts have recognized as EPA\xe2\x80\x99s responsibility, and is distinguishable from determining the particular mix of controls among individual sources to attain those stand\xc2\xad ards, which the caselaw identifies as a State responsibil\xc2\xad ity.\xe2\x80\x9d 63 Fed. Reg. 57369 (emphasis added).

\n

The negative implication suggested by a statute\xe2\x80\x99s failure to use consistent terminology can be a helpful guide to determining meaning, especially when all the provisions in question were enacted at the same time (which is not the case here). But because that interpretive canon, like others, is just one clue to aid construction, it can be over\xc2\xad come by more powerful indications of meaning elsewhere in the statute. It is, we have said, \xe2\x80\x9cno more than a rule of thumb that can tip the scales when a statute could be read in multiple ways.\xe2\x80\x9d Sebelius v. Auburn Regional Medical Center, 568 U. S. ___, ___ (2013) (slip op., at 9) (internal quotation marks and brackets omitted). The Clean Air Act simply cannot be read to make EPA the primary regu\xc2\xad lator in this context. The negative-implication canon is easily overcome by the statute\xe2\x80\x99s state-respecting struc\xc2\xad ture\xe2\x80\x94not to mention the sheer impossibility of submitting a sensible SIP without EPA guidance. Negative implica\xc2\xad tion is the tiniest mousehole in which the majority discov\xc2\xad ers the elephant of federal control.

\n

Addressing the problem of interstate pollution in the manner Congress has prescribed\xe2\x80\x94or in any other manner, for that matter\xe2\x80\x94is a complex and difficult enterprise. But \xe2\x80\x9c[r]egardless of how serious the problem an administrative agency seeks to address, . . . it may not exercise its author\xc2\xad ity \xe2\x80\x98in a manner that is inconsistent with the administra\xc2\xad tive structure that Congress enacted into law.\xe2\x80\x99\xe2\x80\x9d Brown & Williamson, 529 U. S., at 125 (quoting ETSI Pipeline Project v. Missouri, 484 U. S. 495, 517 (1988)).
