Syllabus

\n

United States v. Sanchez-Gomez et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 17\xe2\x80\x93312.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 26, 2018\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 14, 2018

\n

The judges of the United States District Court for the Southern District of California adopted a districtwide policy permitting the use of full restraints\xe2\x80\x94handcuffs connected to a waist chain, with legs shackled\xe2\x80\x94on most in-custody defendants produced in court for nonjury proceedings by the United States Marshals Service. Respondents Jasmin Morales, Rene Sanchez-Gomez, Moises Patricio-Guzman, and Mark Ring challenged the use of such restraints in their respective cases and the restraint policy as a whole. The District Court denied their challenges, and respondents appealed to the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit. Before that court could issue a decision, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 underlying criminal cases ended. The court\xe2\x80\x94viewing the case as a \xe2\x80\x9cfunctional class action\xe2\x80\x9d involving \xe2\x80\x9cclass-like claims\xe2\x80\x9d seeking \xe2\x80\x9cclass-like relief,\xe2\x80\x9d 859 F.\xc2\xa03d 649, 655, 657\xe2\x80\x93658\xe2\x80\x94held that this Court\xe2\x80\x99s civil class action precedents saved the case from mootness. On the merits, the Court of Appeals held the policy unconstitutional.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0This case is moot. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0The federal judiciary may adjudicate only \xe2\x80\x9cactual and concrete disputes, the resolutions of which have direct consequences on the parties involved.\xe2\x80\x9d Genesis HealthCare Corp. v. Symczyk, \n\n569 U.\xc2\xa0S. 66, 71. Such a dispute \xe2\x80\x9cmust be extant at all stages of review, not merely at the time the complaint is filed.\xe2\x80\x9d Preiser v. Newkirk,\n\n422 U.\xc2\xa0S. 395, 401. A case that becomes moot at any point during the proceedings is thus outside the jurisdiction of the federal courts. See Already, LLC v. Nike, Inc., \n\n568 U.\xc2\xa0S. 85, 91. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x934.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0In concluding that this case was not moot, the Court of Appeals relied upon this Court\xe2\x80\x99s class action precedents, most prominently Gerstein v. Pugh,\n\n420 U.\xc2\xa0S. 103. That reliance was misplaced. Gerstein was a class action respecting pretrial detention brought under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23. The named class representatives\xe2\x80\x99 individual claims had apparently become moot before class certification. This Court held that the case could nonetheless proceed, explaining that due to the inherently temporary nature of pretrial detention, no named representative might be in custody long enough for a class to be certified. Gerstein does not support a freestanding exception to mootness outside the class action context. It belongs to a line of cases that this Court has described as turning on the particular traits of Rule 23 class actions. See, e.g., Sosna v. Iowa,\n\n419 U. S. 393; United States Parole Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Geraghty,\n\n445 U. S. 388; Genesis HealthCare,\n\n569 U. S. 66. The Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure establish for criminal cases no vehicle comparable to the civil class action, and this Court has never permitted criminal defendants to band together to seek prospective relief in their individual cases on behalf of a class. Here, the mere presence of allegations that might, if resolved in respondents\xe2\x80\x99 favor, benefit other similarly situated individuals cannot save their case from mootness. See id., at 73. That conclusion is unaffected by the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 decision to recast respondents\xe2\x80\x99 appeals as petitions for supervisory mandamus. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0Respondents do not defend the reasoning of the Court of Appeals, and instead argue that the claims of two respondents\xe2\x80\x94Sanchez-Gomez and Patricio-Guzman\xe2\x80\x94fall within the \xe2\x80\x9cexception to the mootness doctrine for a controversy that is capable of repetition, yet evading review.\xe2\x80\x9d Kingdomware Technologies, Inc. v. United States, 579 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (internal quotation marks omitted). Respondents claim that the exception applies because Sanchez-Gomez and Patricio-Guzman will again violate the law, be apprehended, and be returned to pretrial custody. But this Court has consistently refused to \xe2\x80\x9cconclude that the case-or-controversy requirement is satisfied by\xe2\x80\x9d the possibility that a party \xe2\x80\x9cwill be prosecuted for violating valid criminal laws.\xe2\x80\x9d O\xe2\x80\x99Shea v. Littleton,\n\n414 U.\xc2\xa0S. 488, 497. Respondents argue that this usual refusal to assume future criminal conduct is unwarranted here given the particular circumstances of Sanchez-Gomez\xe2\x80\x99s and Patricio-Guzman\xe2\x80\x99s offenses. They cite two civil cases\xe2\x80\x94Honig v. Doe,\n\n484 U.\xc2\xa0S. 305, and Turner v. Rogers,\n\n564 U.\xc2\xa0S. 431\xe2\x80\x94in which this Court concluded that the expectation that a litigant would repeat the misconduct that gave rise to his claims rendered those claims capable of repetition. But Honig and Turner are inapposite because they concerned litigants unable, for reasons beyond their control, to prevent themselves from transgressing and avoid recurrence of the challenged conduct. Sanchez-Gomez and Patricio-Guzman, in contrast, are \xe2\x80\x9cable\xe2\x80\x94and indeed required by law\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94to refrain from further criminal conduct. Lane v. Williams, \n\n455 U.\xc2\xa0S. 624, 633, n.\xc2\xa013. No departure from the settled rule is warranted. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

859 F. 3d 649, vacated and remanded.

SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

No. 17\xe2\x80\x93312

\n

\n

UNITED STATES, PETITIONER v. RENE SANCHEZ-GOMEZ, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[May 14, 2018]

\n

\n

Chief Justice Roberts delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

Four criminal defendants objected to being bound by full restraints during pretrial proceedings in their cases, but the District Court denied relief. On appeal, the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit held that the use of such restraints was unconstitutional, even though each of the four criminal cases had ended prior to its decision. The question presented is whether the appeals were saved from mootness either because the defendants sought \xe2\x80\x9cclass-like relief\xe2\x80\x9d in a \xe2\x80\x9cfunctional class action,\xe2\x80\x9d or because the challenged practice was \xe2\x80\x9ccapable of repetition, yet evading review.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

I

\n

It is the responsibility of the United States Marshals Service to \xe2\x80\x9cprovide for the security .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of the United States District Courts.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7566(a). To fulfill that duty, the United States Marshal for the Southern District of California requested that the judges of that district permit the use of full restraints on all in-custody defendants during nonjury proceedings. When \xe2\x80\x9cfull restraints\xe2\x80\x9d are applied, \xe2\x80\x9ca defendant\xe2\x80\x99s hands are closely handcuffed together, these handcuffs are connected by chain to another chain running around the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s waist, and the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s feet are shackled and chained together.\xe2\x80\x9d 859 F.\xc2\xa03d 649, 653 (CA9 2017) (en banc). In support of his proposal, the Marshal cited safety concerns arising from understaffing, past incidents of violence, and the high volume of in-custody defendants produced in the Southern District. The judges agreed to the Marshal\xe2\x80\x99s request, with modifications providing that a district or magistrate judge may require a defendant to be produced without restraints, and that a defendant can request that this be done. See App. 78\xe2\x80\x9379.

\n

Respondents Jasmin Morales, Rene Sanchez-Gomez, Moises Patricio-Guzman, and Mark Ring were among the defendants produced by the Marshals Service for pretrial proceedings in full restraints. They raised constitutional objections to the use of such restraints in their respective cases, and to the restraint policy as a whole. They noted that the policy had resulted in the imposition of full restraints on, for example, a woman with a fractured wrist, a man with a severe leg injury, a blind man, and a wheelchair-bound woman. The District Court denied their challenges.

\n

Respondents appealed to the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit, but before the court could issue a decision, their underlying criminal cases came to an end. Morales, Sanchez-Gomez, and Patricio-Guzman each pled guilty to the offense for which they were charged: Morales, to felony importation of a controlled substance, in violation of 21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7952 and 960; Sanchez-Gomez, to felony misuse of a passport, in violation of\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71544; and Patricio-Guzman, to misdemeanor illegal entry into the United States, in violation of\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71325. The charges against Ring\xe2\x80\x94for making an interstate threat in violation of\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7875(c)\xe2\x80\x94were dismissed pursuant to a deferred-prosecution agreement.

\n

A panel of the Court of Appeals nonetheless concluded that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims were not moot, and went on to strike down the restraint policy as violating the Due Process Clause of the\n \nFifth Amendment. 798 F.\xc2\xa03d 1204 (CA9 2015). Those rulings were reaffirmed on rehearing en banc. 859 F.\xc2\xa03d 649. The en banc court understood the \xe2\x80\x9cmain dispute\xe2\x80\x9d before it to be a challenge to the policy itself, not just to the application of that policy to respondents. Id., at 655. The court then construed respondents\xe2\x80\x99 notices of appeal as petitions for mandamus, which invoked the court\xe2\x80\x99s supervisory authority over the Southern District. Id., at 657. The case was, in the court\xe2\x80\x99s view, a \xe2\x80\x9cfunctional class action\xe2\x80\x9d involving \xe2\x80\x9cclass-like claims\xe2\x80\x9d seeking \xe2\x80\x9cclass-like relief.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 655, 657\xe2\x80\x93658. In light of that understanding, the Court of Appeals held that this Court\xe2\x80\x99s civil class action precedents kept the case alive, even though respondents were no longer subject to the restraint policy. Id., at 657\xe2\x80\x93659 (citing Gerstein v. Pugh,\n\n420 U.\xc2\xa0S. 103, 110\xe2\x80\x93111, n. 11 (1975)). On the merits, the Court of Appeals concluded that the restraint policy violated the Constitution. 859 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 666.

\n

Judge Ikuta, writing in dissent for herself and four colleagues, rejected the majority\xe2\x80\x99s application of class action precedents to the individual criminal cases before the court and would have held the case moot. Id., at 675. She also disagreed with the majority on the merits, concluding that the restraint policy did not violate the Constitution. Id., at 683.

\n

We granted certiorari. 583 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2017).

\n

II

\n

To invoke federal jurisdiction, a plaintiff must show a \xe2\x80\x9cpersonal stake\xe2\x80\x9d in the outcome of the action. Genesis HealthCare Corp. v. Symczyk,\n\n569 U.\xc2\xa0S. 66, 71 (2013). \xe2\x80\x9cThis requirement ensures that the Federal Judiciary confines itself to its constitutionally limited role of adjudicating actual and concrete disputes, the resolutions of which have direct consequences on the parties involved.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Such a dispute \xe2\x80\x9cmust be extant at all stages of review, not merely at the time the complaint is filed.\xe2\x80\x9d Preiser v. Newkirk,\n\n422 U.\xc2\xa0S. 395, 401 (1975). A case that becomes moot at any point during the proceedings is \xe2\x80\x9cno longer a \xe2\x80\x98Case\xe2\x80\x99 or \xe2\x80\x98Controversy\xe2\x80\x99 for purposes of Article III,\xe2\x80\x9d and is outside the jurisdiction of the federal courts. Already, LLC v. Nike, Inc.,\n\n568 U.\xc2\xa0S. 85, 91 (2013).

\n

A

\n

In concluding that this case was not moot, the Court of Appeals relied upon our class action precedents, most prominently Gerstein v. Pugh. That reliance was misplaced.[1]*

\n

Gerstein, a class action brought under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23, involved a certified class of detainees raising claims concerning their pretrial detention. 420 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 106\xe2\x80\x93107. By the time this Court heard the case, the named representatives\xe2\x80\x99 claims were moot, and the record suggested that their interest might have lapsed even before the District Court certified the class. See id., at 110\xe2\x80\x93111, n.\xc2\xa011. Normally a class action would be moot if no named class representative with an unexpired claim remained at the time of class certification. See ibid. (citing Sosna v. Iowa,\n\n419 U.\xc2\xa0S. 393, 402, n. 11 (1975)). The Court nevertheless held that the case remained live. As we explained, pretrial custody was inherently temporary and of uncertain length, such that we could not determine \xe2\x80\x9cthat any given individual, named as plaintiff, would be in pretrial custody long enough for a district judge to certify the class.\xe2\x80\x9d Gerstein, 420 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 110\xe2\x80\x93111, n.\xc2\xa011. At the same time, it was certain that there would always be some group of detainees subject to the challenged practice. Ibid. Given these circumstances, the Court determined that the class action could proceed. Ibid.; see Swisher v. Brady,\n\n438 U.\xc2\xa0S. 204, 213\xe2\x80\x93214, n. 11 (1978) (employing same analysis in a class action challenging juvenile court procedures).

\n

The Court of Appeals interpreted Gerstein to cover all \xe2\x80\x9ccases sufficiently similar to class actions\xe2\x80\x9d in which, \xe2\x80\x9cbecause of the inherently transitory nature of the claims,\xe2\x80\x9d the claimant\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cinterests would expire before litigation could be completed.\xe2\x80\x9d 859 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 658. Gerstein was an action brought under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23, but the Court of Appeals decided that such \xe2\x80\x9ca procedural mechanism to aggregate the claims\xe2\x80\x9d was not a \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary prerequisite\xe2\x80\x9d for application of the Gerstein rule. 859 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 659 (alteration omitted). Respondents, the court noted, sought \xe2\x80\x9crelief [from the restraint policy] not merely for themselves, but for all in-custody defendants in the district.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 655. Those \xe2\x80\x9cclass-like claims\xe2\x80\x9d seeking \xe2\x80\x9cclass-like relief\xe2\x80\x9d were sufficient to trigger the application of Gerstein and save the case from mootness, despite the termination of respondents\xe2\x80\x99 criminal cases. 859 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 655.

\n

We reject the notion that Gerstein supports a freestanding exception to mootness outside the class action context. The class action is a creature of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure. See generally 7A C. Wright, A. Miller & M. Kane, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa71751 et\xc2\xa0seq. (3d ed. 2005). It is an \xe2\x80\x9cexception to the usual rule that litigation is conducted by and on behalf of the individual named parties only,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cprovides a procedure by which the court may exercise .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. jurisdiction over the various individual claims in a single proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d Califano v. Yamasaki,\n\n442 U.\xc2\xa0S. 682, 700\xe2\x80\x93701 (1979). \xe2\x80\x9cThe certification of a suit as a class action has important consequences for the unnamed members of the class.\xe2\x80\x9d Sosna, 419 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 399, n. 8. Those class members may be \xe2\x80\x9cbound by the judgment\xe2\x80\x9d and are considered parties to the litigation in many important respects. Devlin v. Scardelletti,\n\n536 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1, 7, 9\xe2\x80\x9310 (2002). A certified class thus \xe2\x80\x9cacquires a legal status separate from the interest asserted by the named plaintiff.\xe2\x80\x9d Genesis HealthCare, 569 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 74 (quoting Sosna, 419 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 399; alterations omitted).

\n

Gerstein belongs to a line of cases that we have described as turning on the particular traits of civil class actions. The first case in this line, Sosna v. Iowa, held that when the claim of the named plaintiff becomes moot after class certification, a \xe2\x80\x9clive controversy may continue to exist\xe2\x80\x9d based on the ongoing interests of the remaining unnamed class members. Genesis HealthCare, 569 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 74 (citing Sosna, 419 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 399\xe2\x80\x93402); see Franks v. Bowman Transp. Co.,\n\n424 U.\xc2\xa0S. 747, 755\xe2\x80\x93756 (1976). The \xe2\x80\x9cfact that a putative class acquires an independent legal status once it is certified\xe2\x80\x9d was, we later explained, \xe2\x80\x9cessential to our decision[ ] in Sosna.\xe2\x80\x9d Genesis HealthCare, 569 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 75; see Kremens v. Bartley,\n\n431 U.\xc2\xa0S. 119, 131\xe2\x80\x93133 (1977) (explaining that, under Sosna\xe2\x80\x99s rule, \xe2\x80\x9conly a \xe2\x80\x98properly certified\xe2\x80\x99 class .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. may succeed to the adversary position of a named representative whose claim becomes moot\xe2\x80\x9d); Alvarez v. Smith,\n\n558 U.\xc2\xa0S. 87, 92\xe2\x80\x9393 (2009) (same).

\n

Gerstein, announced one month after Sosna, provides a limited exception to Sosna\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that a named plaintiff with a live claim exist at the time of class certification. The exception applies when the pace of litigation and the inherently transitory nature of the claims at issue conspire to make that requirement difficult to fulfill. See Sosna, 419 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 402, n.\xc2\xa011 (anticipating the Gerstein rule as an exception); Gerstein, 420 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 110\xe2\x80\x93111, n.\xc2\xa011 (describing its holding as \xe2\x80\x9ca suitable exception\xe2\x80\x9d to Sosna). We have repeatedly tied Gerstein\xe2\x80\x99s rule to the class action setting from which it emerged. See, e.g., Genesis HealthCare, 569 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 71, n.\xc2\xa02 (describing Gerstein\xe2\x80\x99s rule as \xe2\x80\x9cdeveloped in the context of class actions under Rule 23 to address the circumstance in which a named plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s claim becomes moot prior to certification of the class\xe2\x80\x9d); United States Parole Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Geraghty,\n\n445 U.\xc2\xa0S. 388, 397\xe2\x80\x93399 (1980) (highlighting Gerstein as an example of the Court \xe2\x80\x9cconsider[ing] the application of the \xe2\x80\x98personal stake\xe2\x80\x99 requirement in the class-action context\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

In concluding that Gerstein reaches further, the Court of Appeals looked to our recent decision in Genesis HealthCare Corp. v. Symczyk. But in that case the Court refused to extend Gerstein beyond the class action context, even with respect to a procedural device bearing many features similar to a class action. Genesis HealthCare addressed whether a \xe2\x80\x9ccollective action\xe2\x80\x9d brought under the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) by a plaintiff on behalf of herself \xe2\x80\x9cand other \xe2\x80\x98similarly situated\xe2\x80\x99 employees\xe2\x80\x9d remained \xe2\x80\x9cjusticiable when the lone plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s individual claim bec[ame] moot.\xe2\x80\x9d 569 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 69. In an effort to continue her case on behalf of others, the plaintiff turned to Sosna and its progeny, including Gerstein. But those cases, we explained, were \xe2\x80\x9cinapposite,\xe2\x80\x9d not least because \xe2\x80\x9cRule 23 actions are fundamentally different from collective actions under the FLSA.\xe2\x80\x9d Genesis HealthCare, 569 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 74. Such collective actions, we stressed, do not \xe2\x80\x9cproduce a class with an independent legal status, or join additional parties to the action.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 75.

\n

This case, which does not involve any formal mechanism for aggregating claims, is even further removed from Rule 23 and Gerstein. The Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure establish for criminal cases no vehicle comparable to the FLSA collective action, much less the class action. And we have never permitted criminal defendants to band to- gether to seek prospective relief in their individual criminal cases on behalf of a class. As we said when declining to apply nonparty preclusion outside the formal class action context, courts may not \xe2\x80\x9crecognize .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a common-law kind of class action\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ccreate de\xc2\xa0facto class actions at will.\xe2\x80\x9d Taylor v. Sturgell,\n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 880, 901 (2008) (alterations omitted); see Smith v. Bayer Corp.,\n\n564 U.\xc2\xa0S. 299, 315\xe2\x80\x93316 (2011) (same); Pasadena City Bd. of Ed. v. Spangler,\n\n427 U.\xc2\xa0S. 424, 430 (1976) (rejecting in mootness context the idea that \xe2\x80\x9cthe failure to obtain the class certification required under Rule 23 is merely the absence of a meaningless \xe2\x80\x98verbal recital\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

The court below designated respondents\xe2\x80\x99 case a \xe2\x80\x9cfunctional class action\xe2\x80\x9d because respondents were pursuing relief \xe2\x80\x9cnot merely for themselves, but for all in-custody defendants in the district.\xe2\x80\x9d 859 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 655, 657\xe2\x80\x93658. But as explained in Genesis HealthCare, the \xe2\x80\x9cmere presence of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. allegations\xe2\x80\x9d that might, if resolved in respondents\xe2\x80\x99 favor, benefit other similarly situated individuals cannot \xe2\x80\x9csave [respondents\xe2\x80\x99] suit from mootness once the[ir] individual claim[s]\xe2\x80\x9d have dissipated. 569 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 73.

\n

Our conclusion is unaffected by the decision of the court below to recast respondents\xe2\x80\x99 appeals as petitions for \xe2\x80\x9csupervisory mandamus.\xe2\x80\x9d See 859 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 659 (viewing such a petition, like the civil class action, as a procedural vehicle to which the Gerstein rule applies). Supervisory mandamus refers to the authority of the Courts of Appeals to exercise \xe2\x80\x9csupervisory control of the District Courts\xe2\x80\x9d through their \xe2\x80\x9cdiscretionary power to issue writs of mandamus.\xe2\x80\x9d La Buy v. Howes Leather Co.,\n\n352 U.\xc2\xa0S. 249, 259\xe2\x80\x93260 (1957). There is no sign in our scant supervisory mandamus precedents that such cases are exempt from the normal mootness rules. See generally Will v. United States,\n\n389 U.\xc2\xa0S. 90 (1967); Schlagenhauf v. Holder,\n\n379 U.\xc2\xa0S. 104 (1964); La Buy,\n\n352 U.\xc2\xa0S. 249. Indeed, as the court below acknowledged, \xe2\x80\x9c[s]upervisory mandamus cases require live controversies.\xe2\x80\x9d 859 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 657.

\n

B

\n

Respondents do not defend the reasoning of the Court of Appeals. See Brief for Respondents 58 (arguing that this Court need not reach the functional class action issue and should \xe2\x80\x9cdiscard[ ]\xe2\x80\x9d that label); Tr. of Oral Arg. 43 (respondents\xe2\x80\x99 counsel agreeing that they \xe2\x80\x9chave not made any effort to defend\xe2\x80\x9d the functional class action approach). In respondents\xe2\x80\x99 view, functional class actions and Gerstein\xe2\x80\x99s rule are beside the point because two respondents\xe2\x80\x94Sanchez-Gomez and Patricio-Guzman\xe2\x80\x94retain a personal stake in the outcome of their appeals.

\n

Sanchez-Gomez and Patricio-Guzman are no longer in pretrial custody. Their criminal cases, arising from their illegal entry into the United States, ended in guilty pleas well before the Court of Appeals issued its decision. Respondents contend, however, that the claims brought by Sanchez-Gomez and Patricio-Guzman fall within the \xe2\x80\x9cexception to the mootness doctrine for a controversy that is capable of repetition, yet evading review.\xe2\x80\x9d Kingdomware Technologies, Inc. v. United States, 579 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2016) (slip op., at 7) (internal quotation marks omitted). A dispute qualifies for that exception only \xe2\x80\x9cif (1) the challenged action is in its duration too short to be fully liti- gated prior to its cessation or expiration, and (2) there is a reasonable expectation that the same complaining party will be subjected to the same action again.\xe2\x80\x9d Turner v. Rogers,\n\n564 U.\xc2\xa0S. 431, 439\xe2\x80\x93440 (2011) (alterations and internal quotation marks omitted). The parties do not contest that the claims at issue satisfy the first prong of that test, but they sharply disagree as to the second.

\n

Respondents argue that Sanchez-Gomez and Patricio-Guzman meet the second prong because they will again violate the law, be apprehended, and be returned to pretrial custody. But we have consistently refused to \xe2\x80\x9cconclude that the case-or-controversy requirement is satisfied by\xe2\x80\x9d the possibility that a party \xe2\x80\x9cwill be prosecuted for violating valid criminal laws.\xe2\x80\x9d O\xe2\x80\x99Shea v. Littleton,\n\n414 U.\xc2\xa0S. 488, 497 (1974). We have instead \xe2\x80\x9cassume[d] that [litigants] will conduct their activities within the law and so avoid prosecution and conviction as well as exposure to the challenged course of conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; see, e.g., Spencer v. Kemna,\n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1, 15 (1998) (reasoning that a claim regarding a parole revocation order was moot following release from custody because any continuing consequences of the order were \xe2\x80\x9ccontingent upon [the claimant] violating the law, getting caught, and being convicted\xe2\x80\x9d); Honig v. Doe,\n\n484 U.\xc2\xa0S. 305, 320 (1988) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e generally have been unwilling to assume that the party seeking relief will repeat the type of misconduct that would once again place him or her at risk of that injury.\xe2\x80\x9d); Lane v. Williams,\n\n455 U.\xc2\xa0S. 624, 632\xe2\x80\x93633, n.\xc2\xa013 (1982) (concluding that case was moot where the challenged parole revocation could not \xe2\x80\x9caffect a subsequent parole determination unless respondents again violate state law, are returned to prison, and become eligible for parole\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Respondents argue that this usual refusal to assume future criminal conduct is unwarranted here given the particular circumstances of Sanchez-Gomez\xe2\x80\x99s and Patricio-Guzman\xe2\x80\x99s offenses. They cite two civil cases\xe2\x80\x94Honig v. Doe and Turner v. Rogers\xe2\x80\x94in which this Court concluded that the expectation that a litigant would repeat the misconduct that gave rise to his claims rendered those claims capable of repetition. Neither case, however, supports a departure from the settled rule.

\n

Honig involved a disabled student\xe2\x80\x99s challenge to his suspension from school for disruptive behavior. We found that given his \xe2\x80\x9cinability to conform his conduct to socially acceptable norms\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cgovern his aggressive, impulsive behavior,\xe2\x80\x9d it was \xe2\x80\x9creasonable to expect that [the student would] again engage in the type of misconduct that precipitated this suit\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cbe subjected to the same unilateral school action for which he initially sought relief.\xe2\x80\x9d 484 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 320\xe2\x80\x93321. In Turner, we determined that an indigent person repeatedly held in civil contempt for failing to make child support payments, who was at the time over $13,000 in arrears, and whose next hearing was only five months away, was destined to find himself in civil contempt proceedings again. The challenged denial of appointed counsel at his contempt hearing was thus capable of repetition. See 564 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 440.

\n

Respondents contend that Sanchez-Gomez and Patricio-Guzman, like the challengers in Honig and Turner, are likely to find themselves right back where they started if we dismiss their case as moot. Respondents cite a Sentencing Commission report finding that in 2013 thirty-eight percent of those convicted and sentenced for an illegal entry or illegal reentry offense \xe2\x80\x9cwere deported and subsequently illegally reentered at least one time.\xe2\x80\x9d United States Sentencing Commission, Illegal Reentry Offenses 15 (2015) (cited by Brief for Respondents 51). Respondents emphasize the economic and familial pressures that often compel individuals such as Sanchez-Gomez and Patricio-Guzman to repeatedly attempt to enter the United States. And respondents note that both men, after their release, actually did cross the border into the United States, were apprehended again, and were charged with new illegal entry offenses. All this, respondents say, adds up to a sufficient showing that Sanchez-Gomez and Patricio-Guzman satisfy the \xe2\x80\x9ccapable of repetition\xe2\x80\x9d require- ment. Because the Court of Appeals was not aware that Sanchez-Gomez and Patricio-Guzman had subsequently reentered the United States illegally, respondents invite us to remand this case for further proceedings.

\n

We decline to do so because Honig and Turner are inapposite. Our decisions in those civil cases rested on the litigants\xe2\x80\x99 inability, for reasons beyond their control, to prevent themselves from transgressing and avoid recurrence of the challenged conduct. In Honig, such incapacity was the very reason the school sought to expel the student. And in Turner, the indigent individual\xe2\x80\x99s large outstanding debt made him effectively incapable of satisfying his imminent support obligations. Sanchez-Gomez and Patricio-Guzman, in contrast, are \xe2\x80\x9cable\xe2\x80\x94and indeed required by law\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94to refrain from further criminal conduct. Lane, 455 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 633, n.\xc2\xa013. Their personal incentives to return to the United States, plus the elevated rate of recidivism associated with illegal entry offenses, do not amount to an inability to obey the law. We have consistently refused to find the case or controversy requirement satisfied where, as here, the litigants simply \xe2\x80\x9canticipate violating lawful criminal statutes.\xe2\x80\x9d O\xe2\x80\x99Shea, 414 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 496.

\n

III

\n

None of this is to say that those who wish to challenge the use of full physical restraints in the Southern District lack any avenue for relief. In the course of this litigation the parties have touched upon several possible options. See, e.g., Tr. of Oral Arg. 12 (indicating circumstances under which detainees could bring a civil suit). Because we hold this case moot, we take no position on the question.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

We vacate the judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit and remand the case to that court with instructions to dismiss as moot.

\n

It is so ordered.
