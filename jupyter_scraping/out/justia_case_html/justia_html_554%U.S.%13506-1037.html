KENTUCKY RETIREMENT SYSTEMS et\xc2\xa0al. v. EQUAL EMPLOYMENT OPPORTUNITY COMMISSION

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

No. 06\xe2\x80\x931037.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 9, 2008\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 19, 2008

\n

Kentucky permits \xe2\x80\x9chazardous position\xe2\x80\x9d workers, e.g., policemen, to receive normal retirement benefits after working either 20 years or 5 years and attaining age 55 and pays \xe2\x80\x9cdisability retirement\xe2\x80\x9d benefits to workers meeting specified requirements. Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cPlan\xe2\x80\x9d calculates normal retirement benefits based on actual years of service. The Plan calculates disability benefits by adding to an employee\xe2\x80\x99s actual years of service the number of years that the employee would have had to continue working in order to become eligible for normal retirement benefits, adding no more than the number of years the employee had previously worked. Charles Lickteig, who continued working after becoming eligible for retirement at age 55, became disabled and retired at age 61. He filed an age discrimination complaint with respondent (EEOC) after the Plan based his pension on his actual years of service without imputing any additional years. The EEOC filed suit against Kentucky and others (collectively Kentucky), arguing that the Plan failed to impute years solely because Lickteig became disabled after age 55. The District Court granted Kentucky summary judgment, holding that the EEOC could not establish age discrimination, but the Sixth Circuit ultimately reversed on the ground that the Plan violated the Age Discrimination in Employment Act of 1967 (ADEA).

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s system does not discriminate against workers who become disabled after becoming eligible for retirement based on age. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The ADEA forbids an employer to \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminate against any individual with respect to his compensation, terms, conditions, or privileges of employment, because of such individual\xe2\x80\x99s age.\xe2\x80\x9d 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7623(a)(1) (emphasis added). A plaintiff claiming age-related \xe2\x80\x9cdisparate treatment\xe2\x80\x9d (i.e., intentional discrimination) must prove that age \xe2\x80\x9cactually motivated the employer\xe2\x80\x99s decision.\xe2\x80\x9d Hazen Paper Co. v. Biggins, 507 U. S. 604, 610 (emphasis added). In Hazen Paper, the Court found that, without evidence of intent, a dismissal based on pension status was not a dismissal \xe2\x80\x9cbecause \xe2\x80\xa6 of age,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 611\xe2\x80\x93612, noting that, though pension status depended upon years of service, and years of service typically go hand in hand with age, the two concepts are \xe2\x80\x9canalytically distinct,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 611. And the dismissal at issue there, if based purely on pension status, would not embody the evils prompting the ADEA: It was not based on a \xe2\x80\x9cprohibited stereotype\xe2\x80\x9d of older workers, did not produce any \xe2\x80\x9cattendant stigma\xe2\x80\x9d to those workers, and was not \xe2\x80\x9cthe result of an inaccurate and denigrating generalization about age.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 612. However, the Court noted that discrimination based on pension status could violate the ADEA if pension status was a \xe2\x80\x9cproxy for age.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 613. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Applying Hazen Paper, the circumstances here, taken together, show that the differences in treatment in this particular instance were not \xe2\x80\x9cactually motivated\xe2\x80\x9d by age. (1) Age and pension status remain \xe2\x80\x9canalytically distinct\xe2\x80\x9d concepts. (2) Here, several background circumstances eliminate the possibility that pension status serves as a \xe2\x80\x9cproxy for age.\xe2\x80\x9d Rather than an individual employment decision, at issue here are complex systemwide rules involving not wages, but pensions\xe2\x80\x94a benefit the ADEA treats somewhat more flexibly and leniently in respect to age. Further, Congress has otherwise approved programs, such as Social Security Disability Insurance, that calculate disability benefits using a formula that expressly takes account of age. (3) The disparity here has a clear non-age-related rationale. The Plan\xe2\x80\x99s disability rules track Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cnormal retirement\xe2\x80\x9d rules by imputing only those additional years of service needed to bring the disabled worker\xe2\x80\x99s total to 20 or to the number of years that the individual would have worked had he worked to age 55. Thus, the disability rules\xe2\x80\x99 purpose is to treat a disabled worker as though he had become disabled after, rather than before, he had become eligible for \xe2\x80\x9cnormal retirement\xe2\x80\x9d benefits. Age factors into the disability calculation only because the normal retirement rules themselves permissibly consider age. The Plan simply seeks to treat disabled employees as if they had worked until the point at which they would be eligible for a normal pension. Thus, the disparity turns upon pension eligibility and nothing more. (4) Although the Plan placed an older worker at a disadvantage here, in other cases, the rules can work to the advantage of older workers, who may get a bigger boost of imputed years than younger workers. (5) Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s system does not rely on the sorts of stereotypical assumptions, e.g., the work capacity of \xe2\x80\x9colder\xe2\x80\x9d workers relative to \xe2\x80\x9cyounger\xe2\x80\x9d workers, that the ADEA sought to eradicate. The Plan\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cassumptions\xe2\x80\x9d that no disabled worker would have continued to work beyond the point at which he was both disabled and pension eligible do not involve age-related stereotypes, but apply equally to all workers regardless of age. (6) The nature of the Plan\xe2\x80\x99s eligibility requirements means that, unless Kentucky were severely to cut the benefits to disabled workers who are not yet pension eligible, it would have to increase the benefits available to disabled, pension-eligible workers, while lacking any clear criteria for determining how many extra years to impute for those already 55 or older. The difficulty of finding a remedy that can both correct the disparity and achieve the Plan\xe2\x80\x99s legitimate objective\xe2\x80\x94providing each disabled worker with a sufficient retirement benefit\xe2\x80\x94further suggests that this objective, not age, \xe2\x80\x9cactually motivated\xe2\x80\x9d the Plan.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in no way unsettles the rule that a statute or policy that facially discriminates based on age suffices to show disparate treatment under the ADEA. The Court is dealing with the quite special case of differential treatment based on pension status, where pension status\xe2\x80\x94with the explicit blessing of the ADEA\xe2\x80\x94itself turns, in part, on age. Further, the rule for dealing with this sort of case is clear:\xc2\xa0Where an employer adopts a pension plan that includes age as a factor, and that employer then treats employees differently based on pension status, a plaintiff, to state a claim under the ADEA, must adduce sufficient evidence to show that the differential treatment was \xe2\x80\x9cactually motivated\xe2\x80\x9d by age, not pension status. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0The Federal Government\xe2\x80\x99s additional arguments are rejected. Since Hazen Paper provides the relevant precedent here, an ADEA amendment made in light of Public Employees Retirement System of Ohio v. Betts, 492 U. S. 158, is beside the point. And a contrary interpretation contained in an EEOC regulation and its compliance manual does not lead to a different conclusion. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

467 F.\xc2\xa03d 571, reversed.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Breyer delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Commonwealth of Kentucky permits policemen, firemen, and other \xe2\x80\x9chazardous position\xe2\x80\x9d workers to retire and to receive \xe2\x80\x9cnormal retirement\xe2\x80\x9d benefits after either (1) working for 20 years; or (2) working for 5 years and attaining the age of 55. See Ky. Rev. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa716.576, 16.577(2) (Lexis 2003), 61.592(4) (Lexis Supp. 2003). It permits those who become seriously disabled but have not otherwise become eligible for retirement to retire immediately and receive \xe2\x80\x9cdisability retirement\xe2\x80\x9d benefits. See \xc2\xa716.582(2)(b) (Lexis 2003). And it treats some of those disabled individuals more generously than it treats some of those who became disabled only after becoming eligible for retirement on the basis of age. The question before us is whether Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s system consequently discriminates against the latter workers \xe2\x80\x9cbecause of \xe2\x80\xa6 age.\xe2\x80\x9d Age Discrimination in Employment Act of 1967 (ADEA or Act), \xc2\xa74(a)(1), 81 Stat. 603, 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7623(a)(1). We conclude that it does not.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Kentucky has put in place a special retirement plan (Plan) for state and county employees who occupy \xe2\x80\x9c[h]azardous position[s],\xe2\x80\x9d e.g., active duty law enforcement officers, firefighters, paramedics, and workers in correctional systems. See Ky. Rev. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa761.592(1)(a) (Lexis Supp. 2003). The Plan sets forth two routes through which such an employee can become eligible for what is called \xe2\x80\x9cnormal retirement\xe2\x80\x9d benefits. The first makes an employee eligible for retirement after 20 years of service. The second makes an employee eligible after only 5 years of service provided that the employee has attained the age of 55. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa716.576, 16.577(2), 61.592(4). An employee eligible under either route will receive a pension calculated in the same way: Kentucky multiplies years of service times 2.5% times final preretirement pay. See \xc2\xa716.576(3).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s Plan has special provisions for hazardous position workers who become disabled but are not yet eligible for normal retirement. Where such an employee has worked for five years or became disabled in the line of duty, the employee can retire at once. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa716.576(1), 16.582(2) (Lexis 2003). In calculating that employee\xe2\x80\x99s benefits Kentucky will add a certain number of (\xe2\x80\x9cimputed\xe2\x80\x9d) years to the employee\xe2\x80\x99s actual years of service. The number of imputed years equals the number of years that the disabled employee would have had to continue working in order to become eligible for normal retirement benefits, i.e., the years necessary to bring the employee up to 20 years of service or to at least 5 years of service when the employee would turn 55 (whichever number of years is lower). See \xc2\xa716.582(5)(a) (Lexis 2003). Thus, if an employee with 17 years of service becomes disabled at age 48, the Plan adds 3 years and calculates the benefits as if the employee had completed 20 years of service. If an employee with 17 years of service becomes disabled at age 54, the Plan adds 1 year and calculates the benefits as if the employee had retired at age 55 with 18 years of service.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Plan also imposes a ceiling on imputed years equal to the number of years the employee has previously worked (i.e., an employee who has worked eight years cannot receive more than eight additional imputed years), see \xc2\xa716.582(5)(a); it provides for a certain minimum payment, see \xc2\xa716.582(6) (Lexis 2003); and it contains various other details, none of which is challenged here.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Charles Lickteig, a hazardous position worker in the Jefferson County Sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s Department, became eligible for retirement at age 55, continued to work, became disabled, and then retired at age 61. The Plan calculated his annual pension on the basis of his actual years of service\n(18 years) times 2.5% times his final annual pay. Be-\ncause Lickteig became disabled after he had already\nbecome eligible for normal retirement benefits, the Plan did not impute any additional years for purposes of the calculation.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Lickteig complained of age discrimination to the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC); and the EEOC then brought this age discrimination lawsuit against the Commonwealth of Kentucky, Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s Plan administrator, and other state entities (to whom we shall refer collectively as \xe2\x80\x9cKentucky\xe2\x80\x9d). The EEOC pointed out that, if Lickteig had become disabled before he reached the age of 55, the Plan, in calculating Lickteig\xe2\x80\x99s benefits would have imputed a number of additional years. And the EEOC argued that the Plan failed to impute years solely because Lickteig became disabled after he reached age 55.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court, making all appropriate evidence-related assumptions in the EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s favor, see Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 56, held that the EEOC could not establish age discrimination; and it granted summary judgment in the defendants\xe2\x80\x99 favor. A panel of the Sixth Circuit affirmed that judgment. EEOC v. Jefferson Cty. Sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s Dept., 424 F.\xc2\xa03d 467 (2005). The Sixth Circuit then granted rehearing en banc, held that Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s Plan did violate the ADEA, and reversed and remanded for further proceedings. 467 F.\xc2\xa03d 571 (2006).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Kentucky sought certiorari. In light of the potentially serious impact of the Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision upon pension benefits provided under plans in effect in many States, we granted the writ. See, e.g., Ind. Code \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa736\xe2\x80\x938\xe2\x80\x938\xe2\x80\x933.3(b) and (c) (West 2004); Mich. Comp. Laws Ann. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa738.23 and 38.556(2)(d) (West 2005); N. C. Gen. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7135\xe2\x80\x931 and 135\xe2\x80\x935 (Lexis 2007); Pa. Stat. Ann., Tit. 7, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa75102 and 5704 (Purdon Supp. 2007), Tenn. Code Ann. \xc2\xa78\xe2\x80\x9336\xe2\x80\x93501(c)(3) (Supp. 2007). See also Reply Brief for Petitioners 20\xe2\x80\x9321 (predicting, inter alia, large increase in pension liabilities, potential reduction in benefits for all disabled persons, or both); Brief for National Association of State Retirement Administrators et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 8\xe2\x80\x9314 (same).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The ADEA forbids an employer to \xe2\x80\x9cfail or refuse to hire or to discharge any individual or otherwise discriminate against any individual with respect to his compensation, terms, conditions, or privileges of employment, because of such individual\xe2\x80\x99s age.\xe2\x80\x9d 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7623(a)(1) (emphasis added). In Hazen Paper Co. v. Biggins, 507 U. S. 604 (1993), the Court explained that where, as here, a plaintiff claims age-related \xe2\x80\x9cdisparate treatment\xe2\x80\x9d (i.e., intentional discrimination \xe2\x80\x9cbecause of \xe2\x80\xa6 age\xe2\x80\x9d) the plaintiff must prove that age \xe2\x80\x9cactually motivated the employer\xe2\x80\x99s decision.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 610 (emphasis added); see also Reeves v. Sanderson Plumbing Products, Inc., 530 U. S. 133, 141 (2000). The Court noted that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he employer may have relied upon a formal, facially discriminatory policy requiring adverse treatment\xe2\x80\x9d because of age, or \xe2\x80\x9cthe employer may have been motivated by [age] on an ad\xc2\xa0hoc, informal basis.\xe2\x80\x9d Hazen Paper, 507 U. S., at 610. But \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hatever the employer\xe2\x80\x99s decisionmaking process,\xe2\x80\x9d a plaintiff alleging disparate treatment cannot succeed unless the employee\xe2\x80\x99s age \xe2\x80\x9cactually played a role in that process and had a determinative influence on the outcome.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added). Cf. Smith v. City of Jackson, 544 U. S. 228, 239\xe2\x80\x93240 (2005) (plurality opinion) (describing \xe2\x80\x9cdisparate-impact\xe2\x80\x9d theory, not here at issue, which focuses upon unjustified discriminatory results).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Hazen Paper the Court considered a disparate treatment claim that an employer had unlawfully dismissed a 62-year-old employee with over 912 years of service in order to avoid paying pension benefits that would have vested after 10 years. The Court held that, without more evidence of intent, the ADEA would not forbid dismissal of the claim. A dismissal based on pension status was not a dismissal \xe2\x80\x9cbecause . . . of age.\xe2\x80\x9d 507 U. S., at 611\xe2\x80\x93612. Of course, pension status depended upon years of service, and years of service typically go hand in hand with age. Id., at 611. But the two concepts were nonetheless \xe2\x80\x9canalytically distinct.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. An employer could easily \xe2\x80\x9ctake account of one while ignoring the other.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. And the dismissal in question, if based purely upon pension status (related to years of service), would not embody the evils that led Congress to enact the ADEA in the first place: The dismissal was not based on a \xe2\x80\x9cprohibited stereotype\xe2\x80\x9d of older workers, did not produce any \xe2\x80\x9cattendant stigma\xe2\x80\x9d to those workers, and was not \xe2\x80\x9cthe result of an inaccurate and denigrating generalization about age.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 612.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the same time, Hazen Paper indicated that discrimination on the basis of pension status could sometimes be unlawful under the ADEA, in particular where pension status served as a \xe2\x80\x9cproxy for age.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 613. Suppose, for example, an employer \xe2\x80\x9ctarget[ed] employees with a particular pension status on the assumption that these employees are likely to be older.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 612\xe2\x80\x93613. In such a case, Hazen Paper suggested, age, not pension status, would have \xe2\x80\x9cactually motivated\xe2\x80\x9d the employer\xe2\x80\x99s decisionmaking. Hazen Paper also left open \xe2\x80\x9cthe special case where an employee is about to vest in pension benefits as a result of his age, rather than years of service.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 613. We here consider a variation on this \xe2\x80\x9cspecial case\xe2\x80\x9d theme.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s Plan turns normal pension eligibility either upon the employee\xe2\x80\x99s having attained 20 years of service alone or upon the employees having attained 5 years of service and reached the age of 55. The ADEA permits an employer to condition pension eligibility upon age. See 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0A. \xc2\xa7623(l)(1)(A)(i) (Supp. 2007). Thus we must decide whether a plan that (1) lawfully makes age in part a condition of pension eligibility, and (2) treats workers differently in light of their pension status, (3) automatically discriminates because of age. The Government argues \xe2\x80\x9cyes.\xe2\x80\x9d But, following Hazen Paper\xe2\x80\x99s approach, we come to a different conclusion. In particular, the following circumstances, taken together, convince us that, in this particular instance, differences in treatment were not \xe2\x80\x9cactually motivated\xe2\x80\x9d by age.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, as a matter of pure logic, age and pension status remain \xe2\x80\x9canalytically distinct\xe2\x80\x9d concepts. Hazen Paper, 507 U. S., at 611. That is to say, one can easily conceive of decisions that are actually made \xe2\x80\x9cbecause of\xe2\x80\x9d pension status and not age, even where pension status is itself based on age. Suppose, for example that an employer pays all retired workers a pension, retirement eligibility turns on age, say 65, and a 70-year-old worker retires. Nothing in language or in logic prevents one from concluding that the employer has begun to pay the worker a pension, not because the worker is over 65, but simply because the worker has retired.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, several background circumstances eliminate the possibility that pension status, though analytically distinct from age, nonetheless serves as a \xe2\x80\x9cproxy for age\xe2\x80\x9d in Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s Plan. Cf. id., at 613. We consider not an individual employment decision, but a set of complex systemwide rules. These systemic rules involve, not wages, but pensions\xe2\x80\x94a benefit that the ADEA treats somewhat more flexibly and leniently in respect to age. See, e.g., 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0A. \xc2\xa7623(l)(1)(A)(i) (Supp. 2007) (explicitly allowing pension eligibility to turn on age); 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7623(l)(2)(A) (allowing employer to consider (age-related) pension benefits in determining level of severance pay); \xc2\xa7623(l)(3) (allowing employer to consider (age-related) pension benefits in determining level of long-term disability benefits). And the specific benefit at issue here is offered to all hazardous position workers on the same nondiscriminatory terms ex ante. That is to say, every such employee, when hired, is promised disability retirement benefits should he become disabled prior to the time that he is eligible for normal retirement benefits.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Furthermore, Congress has otherwise approved of programs that calculate permanent disability benefits using a formula that expressly takes account of age. For example, the Social Security Administration now uses such a formula in calculating Social Security Disability Insurance benefits. See, e.g., 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7415(b)(2)(B)(iii); 20 CFR \xc2\xa7404.211(e) (2007). And until (and in some cases after) 1984, federal employees received permanent disability benefits based on a formula that, in certain circumstances, did not just consider age, but effectively imputed years of service only to those disabled workers younger than 60. See 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa78339(g) (2006 ed.); see also Office of Personnel Management, Disability Retirement Under the Civil Service Retirement System, Retirement Facts 4, p.\xc2\xa03 (rev. Nov. 1997), on line at http://www.opm.gov/forms/\npdfimage/RI83-4.pdf (as visited June 16, 2008, and available in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Third, there is a clear non-age-related rationale for the disparity here at issue. The manner in which Kentucky calculates disability retirement benefits is in every important respect but one identical to the manner in which Kentucky calculates normal retirement benefits. The one significant difference consists of the fact that the Plan imputes additional years of service to disabled individuals. But the Plan imputes only those years needed to bring the disabled worker\xe2\x80\x99s years of service to 20 or to the number of years that the individual would have worked had he worked to age 55. The disability rules clearly track Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s normal retirement rules.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is obvious, then, that the whole purpose of the disability rules is, as Kentucky claims, to treat a disabled worker as though he had become disabled after, rather than before, he had become eligible for normal retirement benefits. Age factors into the disability calculation only because the normal retirement rules themselves permissibly include age as a consideration. No one seeking to help disabled workers in the way that Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s rules seek to help those workers would care whether Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s normal system turned eligibility in part upon age or upon other, different criteria.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That this is so is suggested by the fact that one can readily construct a plan that produces an identical disparity but is age neutral. Suppose that Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s Plan made eligible for a pension (a) day-shift workers who have 20 years of service, and (b) night-shift workers who have 15 years of service. Suppose further that the Plan calculates the amount of the pension the same way in either case, which method of calculation depends solely upon years of service (say, giving the worker a pension equal to $1,000 for each year of service). If the Plan were then to provide workers who become disabled prior to pension eligibility the same pension the workers would have received had they worked until they became pension eligible, the plan would create a disparity between disabled day-shift and night-shift workers: A day-shift worker who becomes disabled before becoming pension eligible would, in many instances, end up receiving a bigger pension than a night-shift worker who becomes disabled after becoming pension eligible. For example, a day-shift worker who becomes disabled prior to becoming pension-eligible would receive an annual pension of $20,000, while a night-shift worker who becomes disabled after becoming pension-eligible, say, after 16 years of service, would receive an annual pension of $16,000.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The disparity in this example is not \xe2\x80\x9cactually motivated\xe2\x80\x9d by bias against night-shift workers. Rather, such a disparity, like the disparity in the case before us, is simply an artifact of Plan rules that treat one set of workers more generously in respect to the timing of their eligibility for normal retirement benefits but which do not treat them more generously in respect to the calculation of the amount of their normal retirement benefits. The example helps to show that the Plan at issue in this case simply seeks to treat disabled employees as if they had worked until the point at which they would be eligible for a normal pension. The disparity turns upon pension eligibility and nothing more.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Fourth, although Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s Plan placed an older worker at a disadvantage in this case, in other cases, it can work to the advantage of older workers. Consider, for example, two disabled workers, one of whom is aged 45 with 10 years of service, one of whom is aged 40 with 15 years of service. Under Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s scheme, the older worker would actually get a bigger boost of imputed years than the younger worker (10 years would be imputed to the former, while only 5 years would be imputed to the latter). And that fact helps to confirm that the underlying motive is not an effort to discriminate \xe2\x80\x9cbecause of \xe2\x80\xa6 age.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Fifth, Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s system does not rely on any of the sorts of stereotypical assumptions that the ADEA sought to eradicate. It does not rest on any stereotype about the work capacity of \xe2\x80\x9colder\xe2\x80\x9d workers relative to \xe2\x80\x9cyounger\xe2\x80\x9d workers. See, e.g., General Dynamics Land Systems, Inc. v. Cline, 540 U. S. 581, 590 (2004) (noting that except on one point, all the findings and statements of objectives in the ADEA are \xe2\x80\x9ceither cast in terms of the effects of age as intensifying over time, or are couched in terms that refer to \xe2\x80\x98older\xe2\x80\x99 workers, explicitly or implicitly relative to \xe2\x80\x98younger\xe2\x80\x99 ones\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). The Plan does assume that all disabled workers would have worked to the point at which they would have become eligible for a pension. It also assumes that no disabled worker would have continued working beyond the point at which he was both (1) disabled; and (2) pension eligible. But these \xe2\x80\x9cassumptions\xe2\x80\x9d do not involve age-related stereotypes, and they apply equally to all workers, regardless of age.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Sixth, the nature of the Plan\xe2\x80\x99s eligibility requirements means that, unless Kentucky were severely to cut the benefits given to disabled workers who are not yet pension eligible (which Kentucky claims it will do if its present Plan is unlawful), Kentucky would have to increase the benefits available to disabled, pension-eligible workers, while lacking any clear criteria for determining how many extra years to impute for those pension-eligible workers who already are 55 or older. The difficulty of finding a remedy that can both correct the disparity and achieve the Plan\xe2\x80\x99s legitimate objective\xe2\x80\x94providing each disabled worker with a sufficient retirement benefit, namely, the normal retirement benefit that the worker would receive if he were pension eligible at the time of disability\xe2\x80\x94further suggests that this objective and not age \xe2\x80\x9cactually motivated\xe2\x80\x9d the Plan.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The above factors all taken together convince us that the Plan does not, on its face, create treatment differences that are \xe2\x80\x9cactually motivated\xe2\x80\x9d by age. And, for present purposes, we accept the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s finding that the Government has pointed to no additional evidence that might permit a factfinder to reach a contrary conclusion. See App. 28\xe2\x80\x9330.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It bears emphasizing that our opinion in no way unsettles the rule that a statute or policy that facially discriminates based on age suffices to show disparate treatment under the ADEA. We are dealing today with the quite special case of differential treatment based on pension status, where pension status\xe2\x80\x94with the explicit blessing of the ADEA\xe2\x80\x94itself turns, in part, on age. Further, the rule we adopt today for dealing with this sort of case is clear: Where an employer adopts a pension plan that includes age as a factor, and that employer then treats employees differently based on pension status, a plaintiff, to state a disparate treatment claim under the ADEA, must adduce sufficient evidence to show that the differential treatment was \xe2\x80\x9cactually motivated\xe2\x80\x9d by age, not pension status. And our discussion of the factors that lead us to conclude that the Government has failed to make the requisite showing in this case provides an indication of what a plaintiff might show in other cases to meet his burden of proving that differential treatment based on pension status is in fact discrimination \xe2\x80\x9cbecause of\xe2\x80\x9d age.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government makes two additional arguments. First, it looks for support to an amendment that Congress made to the ADEA after this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Public Employees Retirement System of Ohio v. Betts, 492 U. S. 158 (1989). In Betts, the employer denied a worker disability benefits on the ground that its bona fide benefit program provided disability benefits only to workers who became disabled prior to age 60, and the worker in that case became disabled at age 61. Id., at 163. The ADEA at that time exempted from its prohibitions employment decisions taken pursuant to the terms of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98any bona fide employee benefit plan . . . which is not a subterfuge to evade the purposes of\xe2\x80\x99 the Act.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 161 (quoting 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7623(f)(2) (1982 ed., Supp. V)). And the Court held that the employer\xe2\x80\x99s decision fell within that exception. 492 U. S., at 182. Subsequently Congress amended the ADEA to make clear that it covered age-based discrimination in respect to all employee benefits. See Older Workers Benefit Protection Act, \xc2\xa7102, 104 Stat. 978, 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7630(l) (2000 ed.). Congress replaced the \xe2\x80\x9cnot a subterfuge\xe2\x80\x9d exception with a provision stating that age-based disparities in the provision of benefits are lawful only when they are justified in respect to cost savings. Id., at 978\xe2\x80\x93979; 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7623(f)(2)(B)(i).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We agree with the Government that the amendment broadened the field of employer actions subject to antidiscrimination rules and it narrowed the statutorily available justifications for age-related differences. But these facts cannot help the Government here. We do not dispute that ADEA prohibitions apply to the Plan at issue, and our basis for finding the Plan lawful does not rest upon amendment-related justifications. Rather, we find that the discrimination is not \xe2\x80\x9cactually motivated\xe2\x80\x9d by age. Thus Hazen Paper, not Betts, provides relevant precedent. And the amendment cited by the Government is beside the point.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, the Government says that we must defer to a contrary EEOC interpretation contained in an EEOC regulation and compliance manual. The regulation, however, says only that providing \xe2\x80\x9cthe same level of benefits to older workers as to younger workers\xe2\x80\x9d does not violate the Act. 29 CFR \xc2\xa71625.10(a)(2) (2007). The Government\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of this language is not entitled to deference because, on its face, the regulation \xe2\x80\x9cdoes little more than restate the terms of the statute itself.\xe2\x80\x9d Gonzales v. Oregon, 546 U. S. 243, 257 (2006) (denying deference to an agency interpretation of its own regulation in light of the \xe2\x80\x9cnear equivalence\xe2\x80\x9d of the statute and regulation).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Compliance Manual provides more explicitly that benefits are not \xe2\x80\x9cequal\xe2\x80\x9d insofar as a plan \xe2\x80\x9creduces or eliminates benefits based on a criterion that is explicitly defined (in whole or in part) by age.\xe2\x80\x9d 2 EEOC Compliance Manual \xc2\xa73, p. 627:00041 (2001) (bold typeface deleted). And the Compliance Manual further provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[b]asing disability retirement benefits on the number of years a disabled employee would have worked until normal retirement age by definition gives more constructive years of service to younger than to older employees\xe2\x80\x9d and thus violates the Act. See id., at 627:0010.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These statements, while important, cannot lead us to a different conclusion. See National Railroad Passenger Corporation v. Morgan, 536 U. S. 101, 111, n. 6 (2002) (noting that compliance manuals are \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9centitled to respect\xe2\x80\x9d under our decision in Skidmore v. Swift & Co., 323 U. S. 134, 140 (1944)\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Christensen v. Harris County, 529 U. S. 576, 587 (2000). Following Hazen Paper, we interpret the Act as requiring a showing that the discrimination at issue \xe2\x80\x9cactually motivated\xe2\x80\x9d the employer\xe2\x80\x99s decision. Given the reasons set forth in Part III, supra, we conclude that evidence of that motivation was lacking here. And the EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s statement in the Compliance Manual that it automatically reaches a contrary conclusion\xe2\x80\x94a statement that the Manual itself makes little effort to justify\xe2\x80\x94lacks the necessary \xe2\x80\x9cpower to persuade\xe2\x80\x9d us. Skidmore v. Swift & Co., 323 U. S. 134, 140 (1944).

\n

V

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment of the Court of Appeals is reversed.

\n

It is so ordered.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Kennedy, with whom Justice Scalia, Justice Ginsburg, and Justice Alito join, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court today ignores established rules for interpreting and enforcing one of the most important statutes Congress has enacted to protect the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s work force from age discrimination, the Age Discrimination in Employment Act of 1967 (ADEA or Act), 81 Stat. 602, as amended, 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7621 et seq. That Act prohibits employment actions that \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminate against any individual with respect to his compensation, terms, conditions, or privileges of employment, because of such individual\xe2\x80\x99s age.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7623(a)(1). In recent years employers and employees alike have been advised by this Court, by most Courts of Appeals, and by the agency charged with enforcing the Act, the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC), that the most straightforward reading of the statute is the correct one: When an employer makes age a factor in an employee benefit plan in a formal, facial, deliberate, and explicit manner, to the detriment of older employees, this is a violation of the Act. Disparate treatment on the basis of age is prohibited unless some exemption or defense provided in the Act applies.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court today undercuts this basic framework. In doing so it puts the Act and its enforcement on a wrong course. The decision of the en banc panel of the Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit, which the Court reverses, brought that Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s case law into line with that of its sister Circuits. See EEOC v. Jefferson Cty. Sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s Dept., 467 F.\xc2\xa03d 571, 573 (2006) (overturning Lyon v. Ohio Ed. Assn. and Professional Staff Union, 53 F.\xc2\xa03d 135 (1995)); see also, e.g., Jankovitz v. Des Moines Independent Community School Dist., 421 F.\xc2\xa03d 649, 653\xe2\x80\x93655 (CA8 2005); Abrahamson v. Board of Ed. of Wappingers Falls Century School Dist., 374 F.\xc2\xa03d 66, 72\xe2\x80\x9373 (CA2 2004); Arnett v. California Public Employees Retirement System, 179 F.\xc2\xa03d 690, 695\xe2\x80\x93697 (CA9 1999); Auerbach v. Board of Ed. of Harborfields Central School Dist. of Greenlawn, 136 F.\xc2\xa03d 104, 109\xe2\x80\x93114 (CA2 1998); Huff v. UARCO, Inc., 122 F.\xc2\xa03d 374, 387\xe2\x80\x93388 (CA7 1997). By embracing the approach rejected by the en banc panel and all other Courts of Appeals that have addressed this issue, this Court creates unevenness in administration, unpredictability in litigation, and uncertainty as to employee rights once thought well settled. These consequences, and the Court\xe2\x80\x99s errors in interpreting the statute and our cases, require this respectful dissent.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even were the Court correct that Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s facially discriminatory disability benefits plan can be justified by a proper motive, the employer\xe2\x80\x99s own submission to us reveals that the plan\xe2\x80\x99s discriminatory classification rests upon a stereotypical assumption that itself violates the Act and the Court\xe2\x80\x99s own analytical framework.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As a threshold matter, all should concede that the paradigm offered to justify the statute is a powerful one: The young police officer or firefighter with a family is disabled in the heroic performance of his or her duty. Disability payments are increased to account for unworked years of service. What the Court overlooks, however, is that a 61-year-old officer or firefighter who is disabled in the same heroic action receives, in many instances, a lower payment and for one reason alone: By explicit command of Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s disability plan age is an express disadvantage in calculating the disability payment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This is a straightforward act of discrimination on the basis of age. Though the Commonwealth is entitled by the law, in some instances, to defend an age-based differential as cost justified, 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7623(f)(2)(B)(ii), that has yet to be established here. What an employer cannot do, and what the Court ought not to do, is to pretend that this explicit discrimination based on age is somehow consistent with the broad statutory and regulatory prohibition against disparate treatment based on age.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The following appears to be common ground for both sides of the dispute: Kentucky operates dual retirement systems for employees in hazardous occupations. An employee is eligible for normal retirement if he or she has accumulated 20 years of service with the Commonwealth, or is over age 55 and has accumulated at least 5 years of service. If the employee can no longer work as a result of a disability, however, he or she is entitled to receive disability retirement. Employees who are eligible for normal retirement benefits are ineligible for disability retirement. See Ky. Rev. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa716.576, 16.577(2) (Lexis 2003), 61.592(4) (Lexis Cum. Supp. 2003).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The distinction between normal and disability retirement is not just a difference of nomenclature. Under the normal retirement system benefits are calculated by multiplying a percentage of the employee\xe2\x80\x99s pay at retirement by years of service. See \xc2\xa716.576(3) (Lexis 2003). Under the disability system the years-of-service multiplier includes not only the employee\xe2\x80\x99s actual years of service but also the number of years it would have taken the employee to become eligible for normal retirement (subject to a cap equal to the number of actual years served). See \xc2\xa716.582(5)(a). In other words employees in the normal retirement system are compensated based solely on their actual years of service; but employees in the disability retirement system get a bonus, which accounts for the number of years the employee would have worked had he or she remained healthy until becoming eligible to receive normal retirement benefits.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Whether intended or not, the result of these divergent benefits formulae is a system that, in some cases, compensates otherwise similarly situated individuals differently on the basis of age. Consider two covered workers, one 45 and one 55, both with five years of service with the Commonwealth and an annual salary of $60,000. If we assume both become disabled in the same accident, the 45-year-old will be entitled to receive $1,250 in monthly benefits; the 55-year-old will receive $625, just half as much. The benefit disparity results from the Commonwealth\xe2\x80\x99s decision, under the disability retirement formula, to credit the 45-year-old with 5 years of unworked service (thereby increasing the appliable years-service-multipler to 10 years), while the 55-year-old\xe2\x80\x99s benefits are based only on actual years of service (5 years). In that instance age is the only factor that accounts for the disparate treatment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0True, age is not a factor that reduces benefits in every case. If a worker has accumulated 20 years of service with the Commonwealth before he or she becomes disabled, age plays no role in the benefits calculation. But there is no question that, in many cases, a disabled worker over the age of 55 who has accumulated fewer than 20 years of service receives a lower monthly stipend than otherwise similarly situated workers who are under 55. The Court concludes this result is something other than discrimination on the basis of age only by ignoring the statute and our past opinions.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is difficult to find a clear rule of law in the list of policy arguments the Court makes to justify its holding. The difficulty is compounded by the Court\xe2\x80\x99s own analysis. The Court concedes that, in this case, Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s plan \xe2\x80\x9cplaced an older worker at a disadvantage,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 9; yet it proceeds to hold that the Commonwealth\xe2\x80\x99s disparate treatment of its workers was not \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98actually motivated\xe2\x80\x99 by age,\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 10. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s apparent rationale is that, even when it is evident that a benefits plan discriminates on its face on the basis of age, an ADEA plaintiff still must provide additional evidence that the employer acted with an \xe2\x80\x9cunderlying motive,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 9, to treat older workers less favorably than younger workers.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court finds no support in the text of the statute. In the wake of Public Employees Retirement System of Ohio v. Betts, 492 U. S. 158 (1989), where the Court held that bona fide employee benefit plans were exempt from the coverage of the ADEA, Congress amended the Act to provide that an employee benefit plan that discriminates on the basis of age is unlawful, except when the employer establishes entitlement to one of the affirmative defenses Congress has provided. See Older Workers Benefit Protection Act (OWBPA), 104 Stat. 978, codified at 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7623(f). As a result of the OWBPA, an employer cannot operate an employee benefit plan in a manner that \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminate[s] against any individual \xe2\x80\xa6 because of such individual\xe2\x80\x99s age,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7623(a), except when the plan is a \xe2\x80\x9cvoluntary early retirement incentive plan\xe2\x80\x9d or when \xe2\x80\x9cthe actual amount of payment made or cost incurred on behalf of an older worker is no less than that made or incurred on behalf of a younger worker,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7623(f)(2)(B)(i)\xe2\x80\x93(ii); see generally B. Lindemann & D. Kadue, Age Discrimination in Employment Law 175 (2003). Under any common understanding of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s terms a disability plan that pays older workers less than younger workers on the basis of age \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminate[s] \xe2\x80\xa6 because of \xe2\x80\xa6 age.\xe2\x80\x9d That is how the agency that administers the statute, the EEOC, understands it. See 2 EEOC Compliance Manual \xc2\xa73, p.\xc2\xa0627:0004 (2001) (\xe2\x80\x9c[B]enefits will not be equal where a plan reduces or eliminates benefits based on a criterion that is explicitly defined (in whole or in part) by age\xe2\x80\x9d). And the employer here has not shown that any of the affirmative defenses or exemptions to the Act applies. That should be the end of the matter; the employer is liable unless it can make such a showing.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding stems, it asserts, from a statement in Hazen Paper Co. v. Biggins, 507 U. S. 604 (1993), that an employment practice discriminates only if it is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98actually motivated\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d by the protected trait. Ante, at 4 (quoting Hazen Paper, 507 U. S., at 610; emphasis deleted). If this phrase had been used without qualification, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of it might have been justified. If one reads the relevant passage in full (with particular emphasis on the second sentence), however, Hazen Paper makes quite clear that no additional proof of motive is required in an ADEA case once the employment policy at issue is deemed discriminatory on its face. The Court said this:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIn a disparate treatment case, liability depends on whether the protected trait (under the ADEA, age) actually motivated the employer\xe2\x80\x99s decision. See, e.g., United States Postal Service Bd. of Governors v. Aikens, 460 U. S. 711 (1983); Texas Dept. of Community Affairs v. Burdine, 450 U. S. 248, 252\xe2\x80\x93256 (1981); Furnco Constr. Corp. v. Waters, 438 U. S. 567, 576\xe2\x80\x93578 (1978). The employer may have relied upon a formal, facially discriminatory policy requiring adverse treatment of employees with that trait. See, e.g., [Trans World Airlines, Inc. v.] Thurston, [469 U. S. 111 (1985)]; Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power v. Manhart, 435 U. S. 702, 704\xe2\x80\x93718 (1978). Or the employer may have been motivated by the protected trait on an ad hoc, informal basis. See, e.g., Anderson v. Bessemer City, 470 U. S. 564 (1985); Teamsters [v. United States, 431 U. S. 324, 334\xe2\x80\x93343 (1977)]. Whatever the employer\xe2\x80\x99s decisionmaking process, a disparate treatment claim cannot succeed unless the employee\xe2\x80\x99s protected trait actually played a role in that process and had a determinative influence on the outcome.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\nIn context the paragraph identifies a decision made in reliance on a \xe2\x80\x9cfacially discriminatory policy requiring adverse treatment of employees with [a protected] trait\xe2\x80\x9d as a type of employment action that is \xe2\x80\x9cactually motivated\xe2\x80\x9d by that trait. By interpreting Hazen Paper to say that a formal, facial, explicit, mandated, age-based differential does not suffice to establish a disparate-treatment violation (subject to statutory defenses and exemptions), it misconstrues the precedent upon which its entire theory of this case is built. The Court was right in Hazen Paper and is wrong here.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At a minimum the Court should not cite Hazen Paper to support what it now holds. Its conclusion that no disparate-treatment violation has been established here conflicts with the longstanding rule in ADEA cases. The rule\xe2\x80\x94confirmed by the quoted text in Hazen Paper\xe2\x80\x94is that once the plaintiff establishes that a policy discriminates on its face, no additional proof of a less-than-benign motive for the challenged employment action is required. For if the plan discriminates on its face, it is obvious that decisions made pursuant to the plan are \xe2\x80\x9cactually motivated\xe2\x80\x9d by age. The EEOC (or the employee) must prevail unless the employer can justify its action under one of the enumerated statutory defenses or exemptions.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Two cases cited in Hazen Paper as examples of \xe2\x80\x9cformal, facially discriminatory polic[ies],\xe2\x80\x9d stand for this proposition. See Trans World Airlines, Inc. v. Thurston, 469 U. S. 111 (1985); Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power v. Manhart, 435 U. S. 702 (1978).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Thurston, the Court considered whether Trans World Airlines\xe2\x80\x99 transfer policy for older pilots violated the ADEA. The policy allowed pilots to continue working for the airline past the mandatory retirement age of 60 if they transferred to the position of flight engineer. 469 U. S., at 115\xe2\x80\x93116. But the 60-year-old pilot had to bid for the position. Under the bid procedures a pilot who became ineligible to remain at the controls on account of a disability (or even outright incompetence) had priority over a pilot forced out due to age. Id., at 116\xe2\x80\x93117. The Court held the burden-shifting framework of McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green, 411 U. S. 792 (1973), which is used to determine whether there was a discriminatory motive at play, had no application because the policy was \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminatory on its face.\xe2\x80\x9d 469 U. S., at 121.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Manhart, a Title VII case, involved a municipal employees\xe2\x80\x99 retirement plan that forced female employees to make larger contributions than their male counterparts. The Court noted that even if there were no evidence that the policy had a discriminatory \xe2\x80\x9ceffect,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cthat evidence does not defeat the claim that the practice, on its face, discriminated against every individual woman employed by the Department.\xe2\x80\x9d 435 U. S., at 716.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Just as the majority misunderstands Hazen Paper\xe2\x80\x99s reference to employment practices that are \xe2\x80\x9cactually motivated\xe2\x80\x9d by age, so too does it overstate what the Hazen Paper Court meant when it observed that pension status and age are \xe2\x80\x9canalytically distinct.\xe2\x80\x9d 507 U. S.em>., at 611. The Court now reads this language as creating a virtual safe harbor for policies that discriminate on the basis of pension status, even when pension status is tied directly to age and then linked to another type of benefit program. The Hazen Paper Court did not allow, or support, this result. In Hazen Paper pension status and age were \xe2\x80\x9canalytically distinct\xe2\x80\x9d because the employee\xe2\x80\x99s eligibility to receive a pension formally had nothing to do with age; pension status was tied solely to years of service. The Court recognized that age and pension status were correlated (because older workers were more likely to be pension eligible); but the Court found the plan to be facially neutral with regard to age precisely because age and pension status were not expressly linked under the terms of the plan. See id., at 613 (noting that \xe2\x80\x9cwe do not consider the special case where an employee is about to vest in pension benefits as a result of his age, rather than years of service\xe2\x80\x9d). In order to prove disparate-treatment liability the Hazen Paper Court held that the plaintiff needed to provide additional evidence that his termination in fact was motivated by age. Id., at 613\xe2\x80\x93614.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The saving feature that was controlling in Hazen Paper is absent here. This case is the opposite of Hazen Paper. Here the age distinction is active and present, not superseded and absent. Age is a determining factor of pension eligibility for all workers over the age of 55 who have over 5 (but less than 20) years of service; and pension status, in turn, is used to determine eligibility for disability benefits. For these employees, pension status and age are not \xe2\x80\x9canalytically distinct\xe2\x80\x9d in any meaningful sense; they merge into one category. When it treats these employees differently on the basis of pension eligibility, Kentucky facially discriminates on the basis of age. Were this not the case, there would be no facial age discrimination if an employer divided his employees into two teams based upon age\xe2\x80\x94putting all workers over the age of 65 on \xe2\x80\x9cTeam A\xe2\x80\x9d and all other workers on \xe2\x80\x9cTeam B\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94and then paid Team B members twice the salary of their Team A counterparts, not on the basis of age (the employer would declare) but of team designation. Neither Hazen Paper nor the plain text of the ADEA can be read to permit this result.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The closest the Court comes to reconciling its holding with the actual text of the statute is its citation to the Act\xe2\x80\x99s exemption allowing employers to condition pension eligibility on age. Ante, at 7. Of course, the fact that it invokes an exemption is a concession by the Court that the Act otherwise would condemn the age-based classification Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s disability plan makes. But the exemption provides no support for the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding in any event. Its coverage is limited to \xe2\x80\x9cemployee pension benefit plan[s] [that] provid[e] for the attainment of a minimum age as a condition of eligibility for normal or early retirement benefits.\xe2\x80\x9d See 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0A. \xc2\xa7623(l)(1)(A)(i) (Supp. 2007). There is no farther reaching exemption for subsequent employment decisions based upon pension eligibility. And to the extent the Court finds such a loophole to be implicit in the text of the statute, a disability benefits program of the sort at issue here is not the only type of employment policy that fits through it. If the ADEA allows an employer to tie disability benefits to an age-based pension status designation, that same designation can be used to determine wages, hours, heath care benefits, reimbursements, job assignments, promotions, office space, transportation vouchers, parking privileges, and any other conceivable benefit or condition of employment.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court recognizes some of the difficulties with its position and seeks to limit its holding, yet it does so in ways not permitted by statute or our previous employment discrimination cases.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court notes that age is not the sole determining factor of pension eligibility but is instead just one factor embedded in a set of \xe2\x80\x9ccomplex system-wide rules.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 7. There is no suggestion in our prior ADEA cases, however, and certainly none in our related Title VII jurisprudence, that discrimination based on a protected trait is permissible if the protected trait is one among many variables.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This is quite evident when the protected trait is necessarily a controlling, outcome-determinative factor in calculating employee benefits. In Manhart, for instance, sex was not the only factor determining how much an employee was required to contribute to the pension plan on a monthly basis; the employee\xe2\x80\x99s salary, age, and length of service were also variables in the equation. 435 U. S., at 705; Brief for Petitioners in Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power v. Manhart, O.\xc2\xa0T. 1977, No. 76\xe2\x80\x931810, p.\xc2\xa023. And even though the employer\xe2\x80\x99s decision to require higher contributions from female employees was based upon an actuarially sound premise\xe2\x80\x94that women have longer life expectancies than men\xe2\x80\x94the Court held that the plan discriminated on its face. 435 U. S., at 711.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Similarly, we have said that the ADEA\xe2\x80\x99s substantive prohibitions, which were \xe2\x80\x9cderived in haec verba from Title VII,\xe2\x80\x9d Lorillard v. Pons, 434 U. S. 575, 584 (1978), require the employer \xe2\x80\x9cto ignore an employee\xe2\x80\x99s age (absent a statutory exemption or defense),\xe2\x80\x9d Hazen Paper, 507 U. S., at 612. This statement perhaps has been qualified by the Court\xe2\x80\x99s subsequent holding in General Dynamics Land Systems, Inc. v. Cline, 540 U. S. 581 (2004), that the ADEA does not forbid employers from discriminating in favor of older workers to the detriment of younger workers. Reasonable minds may have disagreed about the merits of Cline\xe2\x80\x99s holding. See id., at 601 (Scalia, J., dissenting); see also id., at 602 (Thomas, J., dissenting). But Cline does not dictate the path the Court chooses here. For it is one thing to interpret a statute designed to combat age discrimination in a way that benefits older workers to the detriment of younger workers; it is quite another to do what the Court does in this case, which is to interpret the ADEA to allow a discriminatory employment practice that disfavors older workers while favoring younger ones. The Court, moreover, achieved the result in Cline by reading the word \xe2\x80\x9cage\xe2\x80\x9d to mean \xe2\x80\x9cold age\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94i.e., by reading \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminat[ion] \xe2\x80\xa6 because of [an] individual\xe2\x80\x99s age,\xe2\x80\x9d 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7623(a), to mean discrimination because of an individual\xe2\x80\x99s advanced age. See Cline, supra, at 596. Here the Court seems to adopt a new definition of the term \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminate\xe2\x80\x9d by holding that there is no discrimination on the basis of a protected trait if the trait is one among several factors that bear upon how an employee is treated. There is no principled way to draw this distinction, and the Court does not attempt to do so. Cf. Manhart, supra, at 710 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]here is no reason to believe that Congress intended a special definition of discrimination in the context of employee group insurance coverage\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court recites what it sees as \xe2\x80\x9cseveral background circumstances [that] eliminate the possibility that pension status, though analytically distinct from age, nonetheless serves as a \xe2\x80\x98proxy for age\xe2\x80\x99 in Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s Plan.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 7. Among these is a \xe2\x80\x9cclear non-age-related rationale,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., \xe2\x80\x9cto treat a disabled worker as though he had become disabled after, rather than before, he had become eligible for normal retirement benefits,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 8. There is a difference, however, between a laudable purpose and a rule of law.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0An otherwise discriminatory employment action cannot be rendered lawful because the employer\xe2\x80\x99s motives were benign. In Automobile Workers v. Johnson Controls, Inc., 499 U. S. 187 (1991), the employer had a policy barring all female employees, except those who were infertile, from performing jobs that exposed them to lead. The employer said its policy was designed not to reinforce negative gender stereotypes but to protect female employees\xe2\x80\x99 unborn children against the risk of birth defects. Id., at 191. The argument did not prevail. The plan discriminated on its face on the basis of sex, and the employer did not establish a bona fide occupational qualification defense. As a result, the Court held that the restriction violated Title VII. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he absence of a malevolent motive [did] not convert a facially discriminatory plan into a neutral policy with a discriminatory effect.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 199.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Still, even if our cases allowed the motive qualification the Court puts forth to justify a facial and operative distinction based upon age, the plan at issue here does not survive the Court\xe2\x80\x99s own test. We need look no further than the Commonwealth\xe2\x80\x99s own brief for evidence that its motives are contrary to the ADEA. In its brief the Commonwealth refers to the 61-year-old complainant in this case, Charles Lickteig, as follows:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cAn employee in Mr. Lickteig\xe2\x80\x99s position has had an extra 21 years to devote to making money, providing for himself and his family, saving funds for retirement, and accruing years that will increase his retirement benefits. Thus, the 40-year-old employee is likely to need more of a boost.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 23.

\n

\nThe hypothetical younger worker seems entitled to a boost only if one accepts that the younger worker had more productive years of work left in him at the time of his injury than Lickteig did. As an actuarial matter, this assumption may be sound. It is an impermissible basis for differential treatment under the ADEA, however. As we said in Hazen Paper, the idea that \xe2\x80\x9cproductivity and competence decline with old age\xe2\x80\x9d is the \xe2\x80\x9cvery essence of age discrimination.\xe2\x80\x9d 507 U. S., at 610. By forbidding age discrimination against any \xe2\x80\x9cindividual,\xe2\x80\x9d 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7623(a), the ADEA forbids employers from using the blunt tool of age to assess an employee\xe2\x80\x99s future productivity. Cf. Western Air Lines, Inc. v. Criswell, 472 U. S. 400, 409 (1985) (noting the Labor Department\xe2\x80\x99s findings that \xe2\x80\x9cthe process of psychological and physiological degeneration caused by aging varies with each individual\xe2\x80\x9d). Whether this is good public policy in all instances might be debatable. Until Congress sees fit to change the language of the statute, however, there is no principled basis for upholding Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s disability benefits formula.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As explained in this dissent, Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s disability retirement plan violates the ADEA, an Act intended to promote the interests of older Americans. Yet it is no small irony that it does so, at least in part, because the Commonwealth\xe2\x80\x99s normal retirement plan treats older workers in a particularly generous fashion. Kentucky allows its employees to retire at the age of 55 if they have accumulated only five years of service. But for this provision, which links age and years of service in a way that benefits older workers, pension eligibility would be a function solely of tenure, not age. Accordingly, this case would be more like Hazen Paper, and the EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s case would be much weaker. Similarly, as the Court notes, ante, at 10, Kentucky could avoid any problems by not imputing unworked years of service to any disabled workers, old and young alike. Neither change to the plan would result in more generous treatment for older workers. The only difference would be that, under the first example, older workers would lose the option of early retirement, and, under the second, younger workers would see their benefits cut. These are not the only possible remedies\xe2\x80\x94the Commonwealth could impute unworked years of service to all employees forced into retirement on account of a disability regardless of age.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s desire to avoid construing the ADEA in a way that encourages the Commonwealth to eliminate its early retirement program or to reduce benefits to the policemen and firefighters who are covered under the disability plan is understandable. But, under our precedents, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[a] benefit that is part and parcel of the employment relationship may not be doled out in a discriminatory fashion, even if the employer would be free \xe2\x80\xa6 not to provide the benefit at all.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Thurston, 469 U. S., at 121 (quoting Hishon v. King & Spalding, 467 U. S. 69, 75 (1984)). If Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s facially discriminatory plan is good public policy, the answer is not for this Court to ignore its precedents and the plain text of the statute.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For these reasons, in my view, the judgment of the Court of Appeals should be affirmed and the case remanded for a determination whether the State can assert a cost-justification defense.
