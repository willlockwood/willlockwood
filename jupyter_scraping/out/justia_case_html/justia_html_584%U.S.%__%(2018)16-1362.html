Syllabus

Encino Motorcars, LLC v. Navarro et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 16\xe2\x80\x931362.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 17, 2018\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 2, 2018

\n

Respondents, current and former service advisors for petitioner Encino Motorcars, LLC, sued petitioner for backpay, alleging that petitioner violated the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) by failing to pay them overtime. Petitioner moved to dismiss, arguing that service advisors are exempt from the FLSA\xe2\x80\x99s overtime-pay requirement under\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7213(b)(10)(A), which applies to \xe2\x80\x9cany salesman, partsman, or mechanic primarily engaged in selling or servicing automobiles, trucks, or farm implements.\xe2\x80\x9d The District Court agreed and dismissed the suit. The Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit reversed. It found the statute ambiguous and the legislative history inconclusive, and it deferred to a 2011 Department of Labor rule that interpreted \xe2\x80\x9csalesman\xe2\x80\x9d to exclude service advisors. This Court vacated the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment, holding that courts could not defer to the procedurally defective 2011 rule, Encino Motorcars, LLC v. Navarro, 579 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (Encino I), but not deciding whether the exemption covers service advisors, id., at ___. On remand, the Ninth Circuit again held that the exemption does not include service advisors.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Because service advisors are \xe2\x80\x9csalesm[e]n .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. primarily engaged in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. servicing automobiles,\xe2\x80\x9d they are exempt from the FLSA\xe2\x80\x99s overtime-pay requirement. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0A service advisor is obviously a \xe2\x80\x9csalesman.\xe2\x80\x9d The ordinary meaning of \xe2\x80\x9csalesman\xe2\x80\x9d is someone who sells goods or services, and service advisors \xe2\x80\x9csell [customers] services for their vehicles,\xe2\x80\x9d Encino I, supra, at ___. P. 6.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0Service advisors are also \xe2\x80\x9cprimarily engaged in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. servicing automobiles.\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cServicing\xe2\x80\x9d can mean either \xe2\x80\x9cthe action of maintaining or repairing a motor vehicle\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he action of providing a service.\xe2\x80\x9d 15 Oxford English Dictionary 39. Service advisors satisfy both definitions because they are integral to the servicing process. They \xe2\x80\x9cmee[t] customers; liste[n] to their concerns about their cars; sugges[t] repair and maintenance services; sel[l] new accessories or replacement parts; recor[d] service orders; follo[w] up with customers as the services are performed (for instance, if new problems are discovered); and explai[n] the repair and maintenance work when customers return for their vehicles.\xe2\x80\x9d Encino I, supra, at ___. While service advisors do not spend most of their time physically repairing automobiles, neither do partsmen, who the parties agree are \xe2\x80\x9cprimarily engaged in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. servicing automobiles.\xe2\x80\x9d Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit invoked the distributive canon\xe2\x80\x94matching \xe2\x80\x9csalesman\xe2\x80\x9d with \xe2\x80\x9cselling\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cpartsman [and] mechanic\xe2\x80\x9d with \xe2\x80\x9c[servicing]\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94to conclude that the exemption simply does not apply to \xe2\x80\x9csalesm[e]n .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. primarily engaged in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. servicing automobiles.\xe2\x80\x9d But the word \xe2\x80\x9cor,\xe2\x80\x9d which connects all of the exemption\xe2\x80\x99s nouns and gerunds, is \xe2\x80\x9calmost always disjunctive.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Woods,\n\n571 U.\xc2\xa0S. 31\n. Using \xe2\x80\x9cor\xe2\x80\x9d to join \xe2\x80\x9cselling\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cservicing\xe2\x80\x9d thus suggests that the exemption covers a salesman primarily engaged in either activity.

\n

Statutory context supports this reading. First, the distributive canon has the most force when one-to-one matching is present, but here, the statute would require matching some of three nouns with one of two gerunds. Second, the distributive canon has the most force when an ordinary, disjunctive reading is linguistically impossible. But here, \xe2\x80\x9csalesman .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. primarily engaged in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. servicing automobiles\xe2\x80\x9d is an apt description of a service advisor. Third, a narrow distributive phrasing is an unnatural fit here because the entire exemption bespeaks breadth, starting with \xe2\x80\x9cany\xe2\x80\x9d and using the disjunctive \xe2\x80\x9cor\xe2\x80\x9d three times. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

(d)\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit also invoked the principle that exemptions to the FLSA should be construed narrowly. But the Court rejects this principle as a guide to interpreting the FLSA. Because the FLSA gives no textual indication that its exemptions should be construed narrowly, they should be given a fair reading. P.\xc2\xa09.

\n

(e)\xc2\xa0Finally, the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on two extraneous sources to support its interpretation\xe2\x80\x94the 1966\xe2\x80\x931967 Occupational Outlook Handbook and the FLSA\xe2\x80\x99s legislative history\xe2\x80\x94is unavailing. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

845 F.\xc2\xa03d 925, reversed and remanded.

ENCINO MOTORCARS, LLC, PETITIONER v. HECTOR NAVARRO, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[April 2, 2018]

\n

\n

Justice Thomas delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

The Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA),\n\n52Stat.\n1060, as amended,\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7201 et\xc2\xa0seq., requires employers to pay overtime compensation to covered employees. The FLSA exempts from the overtime-pay requirement \xe2\x80\x9cany salesman, partsman, or mechanic primarily engaged in selling or servicing automobiles\xe2\x80\x9d at a covered dealership. \xc2\xa7213(b)(10)(A). We granted certiorari to decide whether this exemption applies to service advisors\xe2\x80\x94employees at car dealerships who consult with customers about their servicing needs and sell them servicing solutions. We conclude that service advisors are exempt.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

Enacted in 1938, the FLSA requires employers to pay overtime to covered employees who work more than 40 hours in a week.\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7207(a). But the FLSA exempts many categories of employees from this requirement. See \xc2\xa7213. Employees at car dealerships have long been among those exempted.

\n

Congress initially exempted all employees at car dealerships from the overtime-pay requirement. See Fair Labor Standards Amendments of 1961, \xc2\xa79,\n\n75Stat.\n73. Congress then narrowed that exemption to cover \xe2\x80\x9cany salesman, partsman, or mechanic primarily engaged in selling or servicing automobiles, trailers, trucks, farm implements, or aircraft.\xe2\x80\x9d Fair Labor Standards Amendments of 1966, \xc2\xa7209,\n\n80Stat.\n836. In 1974, Congress enacted the version of the exemption at issue here. It provides that the FLSA\xe2\x80\x99s overtime-pay requirement does not apply to \xe2\x80\x9cany salesman, partsman, or mechanic primarily engaged in selling or servicing automobiles, trucks, or farm implements, if he is employed by a nonmanufacturing establishment primarily engaged in the business of selling such vehicles or implements to ultimate purchasers.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7213(b)(10)(A).

\n

This language has long been understood to cover service advisors. Although the Department of Labor initially interpreted it to exclude them, 35 Fed. Reg. 5896 (1970) (codified at 29 CFR \xc2\xa7779.372(c)(4) (1971)), the federal courts rejected that view, see Brennan v. Deel Motors, Inc., 475 F.\xc2\xa02d 1095 (CA5 1973); Brennan v. North Bros. Ford, Inc., 76 CCH LC \xc2\xb633, 247 (ED Mich. 1975), aff\xe2\x80\x99d sub\xc2\xa0nom. Dunlop v. North Bros. Ford, Inc., 529 F.\xc2\xa02d 524 (CA6 1976) (table). After these decisions, the Department issued an opinion letter in 1978, explaining that service advisors are exempt in most cases. See Dept. of Labor, Wage & Hour Div., Opinion Letter No. 1520 (WH\xe2\x80\x93467) (1978), [1978\xe2\x80\x931981 Transfer Binder] CCH Wages\xe2\x80\x93Hours Administrative Rulings \xc2\xb631,207. From 1978 to 2011, Congress made no changes to the exemption, despite amending \xc2\xa7213 nearly a dozen times. The Department also continued to acquiesce in the view that service advisors are exempt. See Dept. of Labor, Wage & Hour Div., Field Operations Handbook, Insert No. 1757, 24L04(k) (Oct. 20, 1987), online at https://perma.cc/5GHD-KCJJ (as last visited Mar. 28, 2018).

\n

In 2011, however, the Department reversed course. It issued a rule that interpreted \xe2\x80\x9csalesman\xe2\x80\x9d to exclude service advisors. 76 Fed. Reg. 18832, 18859 (2011) (codified at 29 CFR \xc2\xa7779.372(c)). That regulation prompted this litigation.

\n

B

\n

Petitioner Encino Motorcars, LLC, is a Mercedes-Benz dealership in California. Respondents are current and former service advisors for petitioner. Service advisors \xe2\x80\x9cinteract with customers and sell them services for their vehicles.\xe2\x80\x9d Encino Motorcars, LLC v. Navarro, 579 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2016) (Encino I) (slip op., at 2). They \xe2\x80\x9cmee[t] customers; liste[n] to their concerns about their cars; sugges[t] repair and maintenance services; sel[l] new accessories or replacement parts; recor[d] service orders; follo[w] up with customers as the services are performed (for instance, if new problems are discovered); and explai[n] the repair and maintenance work when customers return for their vehicles.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

In 2012, respondents sued petitioner for backpay. Relying on the Department\xe2\x80\x99s 2011 regulation, respondents alleged that petitioner had violated the FLSA by failing to pay them overtime. Petitioner moved to dismiss, arguing that service advisors are exempt under \xc2\xa7213(b)(10)(A). The District Court agreed with petitioner and dismissed the complaint, but the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit reversed. Finding the text ambiguous and the legislative history \xe2\x80\x9cinconclusive,\xe2\x80\x9d the Ninth Circuit deferred to the Department\xe2\x80\x99s 2011 rule under Chevron U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc.,\n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 837 (1984)\n. Encino, 780 F.\xc2\xa03d 1267, 1275 (2015).

\n

We granted certiorari and vacated the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment. We explained that courts cannot defer to the 2011 rule because it is procedurally defective. See Encino I, 579 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 8\xe2\x80\x9312). Specifically, the regulation undermined significant reliance interests in the automobile industry by changing the treatment of service advisors without a sufficiently reasoned explanation. Id., at ___ (slip op., at 10). But we did not decide whether, without administrative deference, the exemption covers service advisors. Id., at ___ (slip op., at 12). We remanded that issue for the Ninth Circuit to address in the first instance. Ibid.

\n

C

\n

On remand, the Ninth Circuit again held that the exemption does not include service advisors. The Court of Appeals agreed that a service advisor is a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98salesman\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in a \xe2\x80\x9cgeneric sense,\xe2\x80\x9d 845 F.\xc2\xa03d 925, 930 (2017), and is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98primarily engaged in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. servicing automobiles\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in a \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral sense,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 931. Nonetheless, it concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress did not intend to exempt service advisors.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 929.

\n

The Ninth Circuit began by noting that the Department\xe2\x80\x99s 1966\xe2\x80\x931967 Occupational Outlook Handbook listed 12 job titles in the table of contents that could be found at a car dealership, including \xe2\x80\x9cautomobile mechanics,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cautomobile parts countermen,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cautomobile salesmen,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cautomobile service advisors.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 930. Because the FLSA exemption listed three of these positions, but not service advisors, the Ninth Circuit concluded that service advisors are not exempt. Ibid. The Ninth Circuit also determined that service advisors are not primarily engaged in \xe2\x80\x9cservicing\xe2\x80\x9d automobiles, which it defined to mean \xe2\x80\x9conly those who are actually occupied in the repair and maintenance of cars.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 931. And the Ninth Circuit further concluded that the exemption does not cover salesmen who are primarily engaged in servicing. Id., at 933. In reaching this conclusion, the Ninth Circuit invoked the distributive canon. See A. Scalia & B. Garner, Reading Law 214 (2012) (\xe2\x80\x9cDistributive phrasing applies each expression to its appropriate referent\xe2\x80\x9d). It reasoned that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress intended the gerunds\xe2\x80\x94selling and servicing\xe2\x80\x94to be distributed to their appropriate subjects\xe2\x80\x94salesman, partsman, and mechanic. A salesman sells; a partsman services; and a mechanic services.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 934. Finally, the Court of Appeals noted that its interpretation was supported by the principle that exemptions to the FLSA should be construed narrowly, id., at 935, and the lack of any \xe2\x80\x9cmention of service advisors\xe2\x80\x9d in the legislative history, id., at 939.

\n

We granted certiorari, 582 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2017), and now reverse.

\n

II

\n

The FLSA exempts from its overtime-pay requirement \xe2\x80\x9cany salesman, partsman, or mechanic primarily engaged in selling or servicing automobiles, trucks, or farm implements, if he is employed by a nonmanufacturing establishment primarily engaged in the business of selling such vehicles or implements to ultimate purchasers.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7213(b)(10)(A). The parties agree that petitioner is a \xe2\x80\x9cnonmanufacturing establishment primarily engaged in the business of selling [automobiles] to ultimate purchasers.\xe2\x80\x9d The parties also agree that a service advisor is not a \xe2\x80\x9cpartsman\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cmechanic,\xe2\x80\x9d and that a service advisor is not \xe2\x80\x9cprimarily engaged .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in selling automobiles.\xe2\x80\x9d The question, then, is whether service advisors are \xe2\x80\x9csalesm[e]n .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. primarily engaged in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. servicing automobiles.\xe2\x80\x9d We conclude that they are. Under the best reading of the text, service advisors are \xe2\x80\x9csalesm[e]n,\xe2\x80\x9d and they are \xe2\x80\x9cprimarily engaged in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. servicing automobiles.\xe2\x80\x9d The distributive canon, the practice of construing FLSA exemptions narrowly, and the legislative history do not persuade us otherwise.

\n

A

\n

A service advisor is obviously a \xe2\x80\x9csalesman.\xe2\x80\x9d The term \xe2\x80\x9csalesman\xe2\x80\x9d is not defined in the statute, so \xe2\x80\x9cwe give the term its ordinary meaning.\xe2\x80\x9d Taniguchi v. Kan Pacific Saipan, Ltd.,\n\n566 U.\xc2\xa0S. 560,\n566 (2012)\n. The ordinary meaning of \xe2\x80\x9csalesman\xe2\x80\x9d is someone who sells goods or services. See 14 Oxford English Dictionary 391 (2d ed. 1989) (\xe2\x80\x9c[a] man whose business it is to sell goods or conduct sales\xe2\x80\x9d); Random House Dictionary of the English Language 1262 (1966) (\xe2\x80\x9ca man who sells goods, services, etc.\xe2\x80\x9d). Service advisors do precisely that. As this Court previously explained, service advisors \xe2\x80\x9csell [customers] services for their vehicles.\xe2\x80\x9d Encino I, 579 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 2).

\n

B

\n

Service advisors are also \xe2\x80\x9cprimarily engaged in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. servicing automobiles.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7213(b)(10)(A). The word \xe2\x80\x9cservicing\xe2\x80\x9d in this context can mean either \xe2\x80\x9cthe action of maintaining or repairing a motor vehicle\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he action of providing a service.\xe2\x80\x9d 15 Oxford English Dictionary, at 39; see also Random House Dictionary of the English Language, at 1304 (\xe2\x80\x9cto make fit for use; repair; restore to condition for service\xe2\x80\x9d). Service advisors satisfy both definitions. Service advisors are integral to the servicing process. They \xe2\x80\x9cmee[t] customers; liste[n] to their concerns about their cars; sugges[t] repair and maintenance services; sel[l] new accessories or replacement parts; recor[d] service orders; follo[w] up with customers as the services are performed (for instance, if new problems are discovered); and explai[n] the repair and maintenance work when customers return for their vehicles.\xe2\x80\x9d Encino I, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 2). If you ask the average customer who services his car, the primary, and perhaps only, person he is likely to identify is his service advisor.

\n

True, service advisors do not spend most of their time physically repairing automobiles. But the statutory language is not so constrained. All agree that partsmen, for example, are \xe2\x80\x9cprimarily engaged in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. servicing automobiles.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 40; Brief for Respondents 41\xe2\x80\x9344. But partsmen, like service advisors, do not spend most of their time under the hood. Instead, they \xe2\x80\x9cobtain the vehicle parts .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and provide those parts to the mechanics.\xe2\x80\x9d Encino I, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 2); see also 1 Dept. of Labor, Dictionary of Occupational Titles 33 (3d ed. 1965) (defining \xe2\x80\x9cpartsman\xe2\x80\x9d as someone who \xe2\x80\x9c[p]urchases, stores, and issues spare parts for automotive and industrial equipment\xe2\x80\x9d). In other words, the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cprimarily engaged in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. servicing automobiles\xe2\x80\x9d must include some individuals who do not physically repair automobiles themselves but who are integrally involved in the servicing process. That description applies to partsmen and service advisors alike.

\n

C

\n

The Ninth Circuit concluded that service advisors are not covered because the exemption simply does not apply to \xe2\x80\x9csalesm[e]n .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. primarily engaged in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. servicing automobiles.\xe2\x80\x9d The Ninth Circuit invoked the distributive canon to reach this conclusion. Using that canon, it matched \xe2\x80\x9csalesman\xe2\x80\x9d with \xe2\x80\x9cselling\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cpartsma[n] [and] mechanic\xe2\x80\x9d with \xe2\x80\x9cservicing.\xe2\x80\x9d We reject this reasoning.

\n

The text of the exemption covers \xe2\x80\x9cany salesman, partsman, or mechanic primarily engaged in selling or servicing automobiles, trucks, or farm implements.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7213(b)(10)(A). The exemption uses the word \xe2\x80\x9cor\xe2\x80\x9d to connect all of its nouns and gerunds, and \xe2\x80\x9cor\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9calmost always disjunctive.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Woods, \n\n571 U.\xc2\xa0S. 31,\n45 (2013)\n. Thus, the use of \xe2\x80\x9cor\xe2\x80\x9d to join \xe2\x80\x9cselling\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cservicing\xe2\x80\x9d suggests that the exemption covers a salesman primarily engaged in either activity.

\n

Unsurprisingly, statutory context can overcome the ordinary, disjunctive meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cor.\xe2\x80\x9d The distributive canon, for example, recognizes that sometimes \xe2\x80\x9c[w]here a sentence contains several antecedents and several consequents,\xe2\x80\x9d courts should \xe2\x80\x9cread them distributively and apply the words to the subjects which, by context, they seem most properly to relate.\xe2\x80\x9d 2A N. Singer & S. Singer, Sutherland Statutes and Statutory Construction \xc2\xa747:26, p.\xc2\xa0448 (rev. 7th ed. 2014).

\n

But here, context favors the ordinary disjunctive meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cor\xe2\x80\x9d for at least three reasons. First, the distributive canon has the most force when the statute allows for one-to-one matching. But here, the distributive canon would mix and match some of three nouns\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9csalesman, partsman, or mechanic\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94with one of two gerunds\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cselling or servicing.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7213(b)(10)(A). We doubt that a legislative drafter would leave it to the reader to figure out the precise combinations. Second, the distributive canon has the most force when an ordinary, disjunctive reading is linguistically impossible. Cf., e.g., Huidekoper\xe2\x80\x99s Lessee v. Douglass, 3 Cranch 1, 67 (1805) (Marshall, C.\xc2\xa0J.) (applying the distributive canon when a purely disjunctive reading \xe2\x80\x9cwould involve a contradiction in terms\xe2\x80\x9d). But as explained above, the phrase \xe2\x80\x9csalesman .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. primarily engaged in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. servicing automobiles\xe2\x80\x9d not only makes sense; it is an apt description of a service advisor. Third, a narrow distributive phrasing is an unnatural fit here because the entire exemption bespeaks breadth. It begins with the word \xe2\x80\x9cany.\xe2\x80\x9d See Ali v. Federal Bureau of Prisons,\n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 214,\n219 (2008)\n (noting the \xe2\x80\x9cexpansive meaning\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9cany\xe2\x80\x9d). And it uses the disjunctive word \xe2\x80\x9cor\xe2\x80\x9d three times. In fact, all agree that the third list in the exemption\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cautomobiles, trucks, or farm implements\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94modifies every other noun and gerund. But it would be odd to read the exemption as starting with a distributive phrasing and then, halfway through and without warning, switching to a disjunctive phrasing\xe2\x80\x94all the while using the same word (\xe2\x80\x9cor\xe2\x80\x9d) to signal both meanings. See Brown v. Gardner,\n\n513 U.\xc2\xa0S. 115,\n118 (1994)\n (noting the \xe2\x80\x9cvigorous\xe2\x80\x9d presumption that, \xe2\x80\x9cwhen a term is repeated within a given sentence,\xe2\x80\x9d it \xe2\x80\x9cis used to mean the same thing\xe2\x80\x9d). The more natural reading is that the exemption covers any combination of its nouns, gerunds, and objects.

\n

D

\n

The Ninth Circuit also invoked the principle that exemptions to the FLSA should be construed narrowly. 845 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 935\xe2\x80\x93936. We reject this principle as a useful guidepost for interpreting the FLSA. Because the FLSA gives no \xe2\x80\x9ctextual indication\xe2\x80\x9d that its exemptions should be construed narrowly, \xe2\x80\x9cthere is no reason to give [them] anything other than a fair (rather than a \xe2\x80\x98narrow\xe2\x80\x99) interpretation.\xe2\x80\x9d Scalia, Reading Law, at 363. The narrow-construction principle relies on the flawed premise that the FLSA \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98pursues\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d its remedial purpose \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98at all costs.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d American Express Co. v. Italian Colors Restaurant,\n\n570 U.\xc2\xa0S. 228,\n234 (2013)\n (quoting Rodriguez v. United States,\n\n480 U.\xc2\xa0S. 522\n\xe2\x80\x93526 (1987) (per curiam)); see also Henson v. Santander Consumer USA Inc., 582 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2017) (slip op., at 9) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is quite mistaken to assume .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that whatever might appear to further the statute\xe2\x80\x99s primary objective must be the law\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks and alterations omitted)). But the FLSA has over two dozen exemptions in \xc2\xa7213(b) alone, including the one at issue here. Those exemptions are as much a part of the FLSA\xe2\x80\x99s purpose as the overtime-pay requirement. See id., at ___ (slip op., at 9) (\xe2\x80\x9cLegislation is, after all, the art of compromise, the limitations expressed in statutory terms often the price of passage\xe2\x80\x9d). We thus have no license to give the exemption anything but a fair reading.

\n

E

\n

Finally, the Ninth Circuit relied on two extraneous sources to support its interpretation: the Department\xe2\x80\x99s 1966\xe2\x80\x931967 Occupational Outlook Handbook and the FLSA\xe2\x80\x99s legislative history. We find neither persuasive.

\n

1

\n

The Ninth Circuit first relied on the Department\xe2\x80\x99s 1966\xe2\x80\x931967 Occupational Outlook Handbook. It identified 12 jobs from the Handbook\xe2\x80\x99s table of contents that it thought could be found at automobile dealerships. See 845 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 930. The Ninth Circuit then stressed that the exemption aligns with three of those job titles\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9c[a]utomobile mechanics,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c[a]utomobile parts countermen,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c[a]utomobile salesmen\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94but not \xe2\x80\x9c[a]utomobile service advisors.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

The Ninth Circuit cited nothing, however, suggesting that the exemption was meant to align with the job titles listed in the Handbook. To the contrary, the exemption applies to \xe2\x80\x9cany salesman .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. primarily engaged in selling or servicing automobiles.\xe2\x80\x9d It is not limited, like the term in the Handbook, to \xe2\x80\x9cautomobile salesmen.\xe2\x80\x9d And the ordinary meaning of \xe2\x80\x9csalesman\xe2\x80\x9d plainly includes service advisors.

\n

2

\n

The Ninth Circuit also relied on legislative history to support its interpretation. See id., at 936\xe2\x80\x93939. Specifi- cally, it noted that the legislative history discusses \xe2\x80\x9cautomobile salesmen, partsmen, and mechanics\xe2\x80\x9d but never discusses service advisors. Id., at 939. Although the Ninth Circuit had previously found that same legislative history \xe2\x80\x9cinconclusive,\xe2\x80\x9d Encino, 780 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1275, on remand it was \xe2\x80\x9cfirmly persuaded\xe2\x80\x9d that the legislative history demonstrated Congress\xe2\x80\x99 desire to exclude service advisors, 845 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 939.

\n

The Ninth Circuit was right the first time. As we have explained, the best reading of the statute is that service advisors are exempt. Even for those Members of this Court who consider legislative history, silence in the legislative history, \xe2\x80\x9cno matter how \xe2\x80\x98clanging,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d cannot defeat the better reading of the text and statutory context. Sedima, S. P. R. L. v. Imrex Co.,\n\n473 U.\xc2\xa0S. 479\n, n.\xc2\xa013 (1985). If the text is clear, it needs no repetition in the legislative history; and if the text is ambiguous, silence in the legislative history cannot lend any clarity. See Avco Corp. v. Department of Justice, 884 F.\xc2\xa02d 621, 625 (CADC 1989). Even if Congress did not foresee all of the applications of the statute, that is no reason not to give the statutory text a fair reading. See Union Bank v. Wolas,\n\n502 U.\xc2\xa0S. 151,\n158 (1991)\n.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

In sum, we conclude that service advisors are exempt from the overtime-pay requirement of the FLSA because they are \xe2\x80\x9csalesm[e]n .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. primarily engaged in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. servicing automobiles.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7213(b)(10)(A). Accordingly, we reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeals and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

\n

It is so ordered.

ENCINO MOTORCARS, LLC, PETITIONER v. HECTOR NAVARRO, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[April 2, 2018]

\n

\n

Justice Ginsburg, with whom Justice Breyer, Justice Sotomayor, and Justice Kagan join, dissenting.

\n

Diverse categories of employees staff automobile dealerships. Of employees so engaged, Congress explicitly exempted from the Fair Labor Standards Act hours requirements only three occupations: salesmen, partsmen, and mechanics. The Court today approves the exemption of a fourth occupation: automobile service advisors. In accord with the judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit, I would not enlarge the exemption to include service advisors or other occupations outside Congress\xe2\x80\x99 enumeration.

\n

Respondents are service advisors at a Mercedes-Benz automobile dealership in the Los Angeles area. They work regular hours, 7 a.m. to 6 p.m., at least five days per week, on the dealership premises. App. 54. Their weekly minimum is 55 hours. Maximum hours, for workers covered by the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA or Act), are 40 per week.\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7207(a)(1). In this action, respondents seek time-and-a-half compensation for hours worked beyond the 40 per week maximum prescribed by the FLSA.

\n

The question presented: Are service advisors exempt from receipt of overtime compensation under\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7213(b)(10)(A)? That exemption covers \xe2\x80\x9cany salesman, partsman, or mechanic primarily engaged in selling or servicing automobiles.\xe2\x80\x9d Service advisors, such as respondents, neither sell automobiles nor service (i.e., repair or maintain) vehicles. Rather, they \xe2\x80\x9cmeet and greet [car] owners\xe2\x80\x9d; \xe2\x80\x9csolicit and sugges[t]\xe2\x80\x9d repair services \xe2\x80\x9cto remedy the [owner\xe2\x80\x99s] complaints\xe2\x80\x9d; \xe2\x80\x9csolicit and suggest .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. supplemental [vehicle] service[s]\xe2\x80\x9d; and provide owners with cost estimates. App. 55. Because service advisors neither sell nor repair automobiles, they should remain outside the exemption and within the Act\xe2\x80\x99s coverage.

\n

I

\n

In 1961, Congress exempted all automobile-dealership employees from the Act\xe2\x80\x99s overtime-pay requirements. See Fair Labor Standards Amendments of 1961, \xc2\xa79,\n\n75Stat.\n73.[1] Five years later, in 1966, Congress confined the dealership exemption to three categories of employees: automobile salesmen, mechanics, and partsmen. See Fair Labor Standards Amendments of 1966, \xc2\xa7209,\n\n80Stat.\n836. At the time, it was well understood that mechanics perform \xe2\x80\x9cpreventive maintenance\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9crepairs,\xe2\x80\x9d Dept. of Labor, Occupational Outlook Handbook 477 (1966\xe2\x80\x931967 ed.) (Handbook), while partsmen requisition parts, \xe2\x80\x9csuppl[y] [them] to mechanics,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 312, and, at times, have \xe2\x80\x9cmechanical responsibilities in repairing parts,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for International Association of Machinists and Aerospace Workers, AFL\xe2\x80\x93CIO, as Amicus Curiae 30; see Handbook, at 312\xe2\x80\x93313 (partsmen may \xe2\x80\x9cmeasure parts for interchangeability,\xe2\x80\x9d test parts for \xe2\x80\x9cdefect[s],\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9crepair parts\xe2\x80\x9d). Congress did not exempt numerous other categories of dealership employees, among them, automobile painters, upholsterers, bookkeeping workers, cashiers, janitors, purchasing agents, shipping and receiving clerks, and, most relevant here, service advisors. These positions and their duties were well known at the time, as documented in U.\xc2\xa0S. Government catalogs of American jobs. See Handbook, at XIII, XV, XVI (table of contents); Brief for International Association of Machinists and Aerospace Workers, AFL\xe2\x80\x93CIO, as Amicus Curiae 34 (noting \xe2\x80\x9cmore than twenty distinct [job] classifications\xe2\x80\x9d in the service department alone).

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cWhere Congress explicitly enumerates certain exceptions .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. , additional exceptions are not to be implied, in the absence of evidence of a contrary legislative intent.\xe2\x80\x9d TRW Inc. v. Andrews,\n\n534 U.\xc2\xa0S. 19,\n28 (2001)\n (internal quotation marks omitted). The Court thus has no warrant to add to the three explicitly exempt categories (salesmen, partsmen, and mechanics) a fourth (service advisors) for which the Legislature did not provide. The reach of today\xe2\x80\x99s ruling is uncertain, troublingly so: By expansively reading the exemption to encompass all salesmen, partsmen, and mechanics who are \xe2\x80\x9cintegral to the servicing process,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 6, the Court risks restoring much of what Congress intended the 1966 amendment to terminate, i.e., the blanket exemption of all dealership employees from overtime-pay requirements.

\n

II

\n

Had the \xc2\xa7213(b)(10)(A) exemption covered \xe2\x80\x9cany salesman or mechanic primarily engaged in selling or servicing automobiles,\xe2\x80\x9d there could be no argument that service advisors fit within it. Only \xe2\x80\x9csalesmen\xe2\x80\x9d primarily engaged in \xe2\x80\x9cselling\xe2\x80\x9d automobiles and \xe2\x80\x9cmechanics\xe2\x80\x9d primarily engaged in \xe2\x80\x9cservicing\xe2\x80\x9d them would fall outside the Act\xe2\x80\x99s coverage. Service advisors, defined as \xe2\x80\x9csalesmen primarily engaged in the selling of services,\xe2\x80\x9d Encino Motorcars, LLC v. Navarro, 579 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2016) (Thomas, J., dissenting) (slip op., at 2) (emphasis added), plainly do not belong in either category. Moreover, even if the exemption were read to reach \xe2\x80\x9csalesmen\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cprimarily engaged in servicing automobiles,\xe2\x80\x9d not just selling them, service advisors would not be exempt. The ordinary meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cservicing\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cthe action of maintaining or repairing a motor vehicle.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 6 (quoting 15 Oxford English Dictionary 39 (2d ed. 1989)). As described above, see supra, at 2, service advisors neither maintain nor repair automobiles.[2]

\n

Petitioner stakes its case on Congress\xe2\x80\x99 addition of the \xe2\x80\x9cpartsman\xe2\x80\x9d job to the exemption. See Reply Brief 6\xe2\x80\x9310. That inclusion, petitioner urges, has a vacuum effect: It draws into the exemption job categories other than the three for which Congress provided, in particular, service advisors. Because partsmen, like service advisors, neither \xe2\x80\x9csell\xe2\x80\x9d nor \xe2\x80\x9cservice\xe2\x80\x9d automobiles in the conventional sense, petitioner reasons, Congress must have intended the word \xe2\x80\x9cservice\xe2\x80\x9d to mean something broader than repair and maintenance.

\n

To begin with, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s premise is flawed. Unlike service advisors, partsmen \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98get their hands dirty\xe2\x80\x99 by \xe2\x80\x98working as a mechanic\xe2\x80\x99s right-hand man or woman.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Encino Motorcars, 579 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___, n.\xc2\xa01 (Ginsburg, J., concurring) (slip op., at 1, n.\xc2\xa01) (quoting Brief for Respondents in No. 15\xe2\x80\x93415, p.\xc2\xa011; alterations omitted); see supra, at 2\xe2\x80\x933 (describing duties of partsmen). As the Solicitor General put it last time this case was before the Court, a mechanic \xe2\x80\x9cmight be able to obtain the parts to complete a repair without the real-time assistance of a partsman by his side.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae in No. 15\xe2\x80\x93415, p.\xc2\xa023. But dividing the \xe2\x80\x9ckey [repair] tasks .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. between two individuals\xe2\x80\x9d only \xe2\x80\x9creinforces\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cthat both the mechanic and the partsman are .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. involved in repairing (\xe2\x80\x98servicing\xe2\x80\x99) the vehicle.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Service advisors, in contrast, \xe2\x80\x9csell .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. services [to customers] for their vehicles,\xe2\x80\x9d Encino Motorcars, 579 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 2) (emphasis added)\xe2\x80\x94services that are later performed by mechanics and partsmen.

\n

Adding partsmen to the exemption, moreover, would be an exceptionally odd way for Congress to have indicated that \xe2\x80\x9cservicing\xe2\x80\x9d should be given a meaning deviating from its ordinary usage. There is a more straightforward explanation for Congress\xe2\x80\x99 inclusion of partsmen alongside salesmen and mechanics: Common features of the three enumerated jobs make them unsuitable for overtime pay.

\n

Both salesmen and mechanics work irregular hours, including nights and weekends, not uncommonly offsite, rendering time worked not easily tracked.[3] As noted in the 1966 Senate floor debate, salesmen \xe2\x80\x9cgo out at unusual hours, trying to earn commissions.\xe2\x80\x9d 112 Cong. Rec. 20504 (1966) (remarks of Sen. Bayh). See also ibid. (remarks of Sen. Yarborough) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he salesman .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [can] sell an Oldsmobile, a Pontiac, or a Buick all day long and all night. He is not under any overtime.\xe2\x80\x9d). Mechanics\xe2\x80\x99 work may involve similar \xe2\x80\x9cdifficult[ies] [in] keeping regular hours.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. For example, mechanics may be required to \xe2\x80\x9canswe[r] calls in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. rural areas,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., or to \xe2\x80\x9cgo out on the field where there is a harvesting of sugarbeets,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 20505 (remarks of Sen. Clark).[4] And, like salesmen, mechanics may be \xe2\x80\x9csubject to substantial seasonal variations in business.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 20502 (remarks of Sen. Hruska).

\n

Congress added \xe2\x80\x9cpartsman\xe2\x80\x9d to the exemption because it believed that job, too, entailed irregular hours. See ibid. This is \xe2\x80\x9cespecially true,\xe2\x80\x9d several Senators emphasized, \xe2\x80\x9cin the farm equipment business where farmers, during planting, cultivating and harvesting seasons, may call on their dealers for parts at any time during the day or evening and on weekends.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (remarks of Sen. Bayh). See also id., at 20503 (remarks of Sen. Mansfield). In Senator Bayh\xe2\x80\x99s experience, for instance, a mechanic who \xe2\x80\x9ccould not find [a] necessary part\xe2\x80\x9d after hours might \xe2\x80\x9ccall the partsman, get him out of bed, and get him to come down to the store.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 20504. See also id., at 20503 (remarks of Sen. Hruska) (\xe2\x80\x9cAre we going to say to the farmer who needs a part .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. on Sunday: You cannot get a spark plug .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. because the partsman is not exempt, but you can have machinery repaired by a mechanic who is exempt[?]\xe2\x80\x9d). Although some Senators opposed adding partsmen to the exemption because, as they understood the job\xe2\x80\x99s demands, partsmen did not work irregular hours, e.g., id., at 20505 (remarks of Sen. Clark), the crux of the debate underscores the exemption\xe2\x80\x99s rationale.

\n

That rationale has no application here. Unlike salesmen, partsmen, and mechanics, service advisors \xe2\x80\x9cwor[k] ordinary, fixed schedules on-site.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 47 (citing Handbook, at 316). Respondents, for instance, work regular 11-hour shifts, at all times of the year, for a weekly minimum of 55 hours. See App. 54. Service advisors thus do not implicate the concerns underlying the \xc2\xa7213(b)(10)(A) exemption. Indeed, they are precisely the type of workers Congress intended the FLSA to shield \xe2\x80\x9cfrom the evil of overwork,\xe2\x80\x9d Barrentine v. Arkansas-Best Freight System, Inc.,\n\n450 U.\xc2\xa0S. 728,\n739 (1981)\n (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

I note, furthermore, that limiting the exemption to the three delineated jobs\xe2\x80\x94salesman, partsman, and mechanic\xe2\x80\x94does not leave the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cprimarily engaged in selling or servicing,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7213(b)(10)(A), without utility. Congress included that language to ensure that only employees who actually perform the tasks commonly associated with the enumerated positions would be covered. Otherwise, for example, a worker who acts as a \xe2\x80\x9csalesman\xe2\x80\x9d in name only could lose the FLSA\xe2\x80\x99s protections merely because of the formal title listed on the employer\xe2\x80\x99s payroll records. See Bowers v. Fred Haas Toyota World, 2017 WL 5127289, *4 (SD Tex., June 21, 2017) (\xe2\x80\x9c[An employee\xe2\x80\x99s] title alone is not dispositive of whether he meets the .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. exemption.\xe2\x80\x9d). Thus, by partsmen \xe2\x80\x9cprimarily engaged in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. servicing automobiles,\xe2\x80\x9d Congress meant nothing more than partsmen primarily engaged in the ordinary duties of a partsman, i.e., requisitioning, supplying, and repairing parts. See supra, at 2\xe2\x80\x933, 4\xe2\x80\x935. The inclusion of \xe2\x80\x9cpartsman\xe2\x80\x9d therefore should not result in the removal of service advisors from the Act\xe2\x80\x99s protections.

\n

III

\n

Petitioner contends that \xe2\x80\x9caffirming the decision below would disrupt decades of settled expectations\xe2\x80\x9d while exposing \xe2\x80\x9cemployers to substantial retroactive liability.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 51. \xe2\x80\x9c[M]any dealerships,\xe2\x80\x9d petitioner urges, \xe2\x80\x9chave offered compensation packages based primarily on sales commissions,\xe2\x80\x9d in reliance on court decisions and agency guidance ranking service advisors as exempt. Id., at 51\xe2\x80\x9352. Respondents here, for instance, are compensated on a \xe2\x80\x9cpure commission basis.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 55. Awarding retroactive overtime pay to employees who were \xe2\x80\x9cfocused on earning commissions,\xe2\x80\x9d not \xe2\x80\x9cworking a set number of hours,\xe2\x80\x9d petitioner argues, would yield an \xe2\x80\x9cunjustified windfal[l].\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 53.

\n

Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s concerns are doubly overstated. As the Court previously acknowledged, see Encino Motorcars, 579 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 11), the FLSA provides an affirmative defense that explicitly protects regulated parties from retroactive liability for actions taken in good-faith reliance on superseded agency guidance. See\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7259(a). Given the Department of Labor\xe2\x80\x99s longstanding view that service advisors fit within the \xc2\xa7213(b)(10)(A) exemption, see ante, at 2, the reliance defense would surely shield employers from retroactive liability were the Court to construe the exemption properly.

\n

Congress, moreover, has spoken directly to the treatment of commission-based workers. The FLSA exempts from its overtime directives any employee of a \xe2\x80\x9cretail or service establishment\xe2\x80\x9d who receives more than half of his or her pay on commission, so long as the employee\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cregular rate of pay\xe2\x80\x9d is more than 1\xc2\xbd times the minimum wage. \xc2\xa7207(i). Thus, even without the \xc2\xa7213(b)(10)(A) exemption, many service advisors compensated on commission would remain ineligible for overtime remuneration.[5]

\n

In crafting the commission-pay exemption, Congress struck a deliberate balance: It exempted higher paid commissioned employees, perhaps in recognition of their potentially irregular hours, see Mechmet v. Four Seasons Hotels, Ltd., 825 F.\xc2\xa02d 1173, 1176\xe2\x80\x931177 (CA7 1987); cf. supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x937, but it maintained protection for lower paid employees, to vindicate the Act\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cprincipal .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. purpose\xe2\x80\x9d of shielding \xe2\x80\x9cworkers from substandard wages and oppressive working hours,\xe2\x80\x9d Barrentine, 450 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 739.[6] By stretching the \xc2\xa7213(b)(10)(A) exemption to encompass even the lowest income service advisors compensated on commission, the Court upsets Congress\xe2\x80\x99 careful balance, while stripping away protection for the most vulnerable workers in this occupation.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

This Court once recognized that the \xe2\x80\x9cparticularity\xe2\x80\x9d of FLSA exemptions \xe2\x80\x9cpreclude[s] their enlargement by implication.\xe2\x80\x9d Addison v. Holly Hill Fruit Products, Inc.,\n\n322 U.\xc2\xa0S. 607,\n617 (1944)\n. Employees outside the Act\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cnarrow and specific\xe2\x80\x9d exemptions, the Court affirmed, \xe2\x80\x9cremain within the Act.\xe2\x80\x9d Powell v. United States Cartridge Co.,\n\n339 U.\xc2\xa0S. 497,\n517 (1950)\n.[7] The Court today, in adding an exemption of its own creation, veers away from that comprehension of the FLSA\xe2\x80\x99s mission. I would instead resist, as the Ninth Circuit did, diminishment of the Act\xe2\x80\x99s overtime strictures.
