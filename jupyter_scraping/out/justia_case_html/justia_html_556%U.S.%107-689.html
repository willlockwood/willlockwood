\nBARTLETT V. STRICKLAND
\n
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\n
\n

BARTLETT, EXECUTIVE DIRECTOR OF NORTH CAROLINA STATE BOARD OF ELECTIONS, et\xc2\xa0al. v. STRICKLAND et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the supreme court of north carolina

\n

No. 07\xe2\x80\x93689.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 14, 2008\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 9, 2009

\n

Despite the North Carolina Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cWhole County Provision\xe2\x80\x9d prohibiting the General Assembly from dividing counties when drawing its own legislative districts, in 1991 the legislature drew House District 18 to include portions of four counties, including Pender County, for the asserted purpose of satisfying \xc2\xa72 of the Voting Rights Act of 1965. At that time, District 18 was a geographically compact majority-minority district. By the time the district was to be redrawn in 2003, the African-American voting-age population in District 18 had fallen below 50 percent. Rather than redrawing the district to keep Pender County whole, the legislators split portions of it and another county. District 18\xe2\x80\x99s African-American voting-age population is now 39.36 percent. Keeping Pender County whole would have resulted in an African-American voting-age population of 35.33 percent. The legislators\xe2\x80\x99 rationale was that splitting Pender County gave African-American voters the potential to join with majority voters to elect the minority group\xe2\x80\x99s candidate of choice, while leaving Pender County whole would have violated \xc2\xa72 of the Voting Rights Act.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Pender County and others filed suit, alleging that the redistricting plan violated the Whole County Provision. The state-official defendants answered that dividing Pender County was required by \xc2\xa72. The trial court first considered whether the defendants had established the three threshold requirements for \xc2\xa72 liability under Thornburg v. Gingles, 478 U. S. 30, 51, only the first of which is relevant here: whether the minority group \xe2\x80\x9cis sufficiently large and geographically compact to constitute a majority in a single-member district.\xe2\x80\x9d The court concluded that although African-Americans were not a majority of District 18\xe2\x80\x99s voting-age population, the district was a \xe2\x80\x9cde facto\xe2\x80\x9d majority-minority district because African-Americans could get enough support from crossover majority voters to elect their preferred candidate. The court ultimately determined, based on the totality of the circumstances, that \xc2\xa72 required that Pender County be split, and it sustained District 18\xe2\x80\x99s lines on that rationale. The State Supreme Court reversed, holding that a minority group must constitute a numerical majority of the voting-age population in an area before \xc2\xa72 requires the creation of a legislative district to prevent dilution of that group\xe2\x80\x99s votes. Because African-Americans did not have such a numerical majority in District 18, the court ordered the legislature to redraw the district.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The judgment is affirmed.

\n

361 N.\xc2\xa0C. 491, 649 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 364, affirmed.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Kennedy, joined by The Chief Justice and Justice Alito, concluded that \xc2\xa72 does not require state officials to draw election-district lines to allow a racial minority that would make up less than 50 percent of the voting-age population in the redrawn district to join with crossover voters to elect the minority\xe2\x80\x99s candidate of choice. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9321.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0As amended in 1982, \xc2\xa72 provides that a violation \xe2\x80\x9cis established if, based on the totality of circumstances, it is shown that the [election] processes \xe2\x80\xa6 in the State or political subdivision are not equally open to participation by members of a [protected] class [who] have less opportunity than other members of the electorate to participate in the political process and to elect representatives of their choice.\xe2\x80\x9d 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71973(b). Construing the amended \xc2\xa72 in Gingles, supra, at 50\xe2\x80\x9351, the Court identified three \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary preconditions\xe2\x80\x9d for a claim that the use of multimember districts constituted actionable vote dilution. It later held that those requirements apply equally in \xc2\xa72 cases involving single-member districts. Growe v. Emison, 507 U. S. 25, 40\xe2\x80\x9341. Only when a party has established the requirements does a court proceed to analyze whether a \xc2\xa72 violation has occurred based on the totality of the circumstances. See, e.g., Johnson v. De Grandy, 512 U. S. 997, 1013. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0Only when a geographically compact group of minority voters could form a majority in a single-member district has the first Gingles requirement been met. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9321.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0A party asserting \xc2\xa72 liability must show by a preponderance of the evidence that the minority population in the potential election district is greater than 50 percent. The Court has held both that \xc2\xa72 can require the creation of a \xe2\x80\x9cmajority-minority\xe2\x80\x9d district, in which a minority group composes a numerical, working majority of the voting-age population, see, e.g., Voinovich v. Quilter, 507 U. S. 146, 154\xe2\x80\x93155, and that \xc2\xa72 does not require the creation of an \xe2\x80\x9cinfluence\xe2\x80\x9d district, in which a minority group can influence the outcome of an election even if its preferred candidate cannot be elected, see League of United Latin American Citizens v. Perry, 548 U. S. 399, ___ (LULAC). This case involves an intermediate, \xe2\x80\x9ccrossover\xe2\x80\x9d district, in which the minority makes up less than a majority of the voting-age population, but is large enough to elect the candidate of its choice with help from majority voters who cross over to support the minority\xe2\x80\x99s preferred candidate. Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 theory that such districts satisfy the first Gingles requirement is contrary to \xc2\xa72, which requires a showing that minorities \xe2\x80\x9chave less opportunity than other members of the electorate to \xe2\x80\xa6 elect representatives of their choice,\xe2\x80\x9d 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71973(b). Because they form only 39 percent of District 18\xe2\x80\x99s voting-age population, African-Americans standing alone have no better or worse opportunity to elect a candidate than any other group with the same relative voting strength. Recognizing a \xc2\xa72 claim where minority voters cannot elect their candidate of choice based on their own votes and without assistance from others would grant special protection to their right to form political coalitions that is not authorized by the section. Nor does the reasoning of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases support petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims. In Voinovich, for example, the Court stated that the first Gingles requirement \xe2\x80\x9cwould have to be modified or eliminated\xe2\x80\x9d to allow crossover-district claims. 507 U. S., at 158. Indeed, mandatory recognition of such claims would create serious tension with the third Gingles requirement, that the majority votes as a bloc to defeat minority-preferred candidates, see 478 U. S., at 50\xe2\x80\x9351, and would call into question the entire Gingles framework. On the other hand, the Court finds support for the clear line drawn by the majority-minority requirement in the need for workable standards and sound judicial and legislative administration. By contrast, if \xc2\xa72 required crossover districts, determining whether a \xc2\xa72 claim would lie would require courts to make complex political predictions and tie them to race-based assumptions. Heightening these concerns is the fact that because \xc2\xa72 applies nationwide to every jurisdiction required to draw election-district lines under state or local law, crossover-district claims would require courts to make predictive political judgments not only about familiar, two-party contests in large districts but also about regional and local elections. Unlike any of the standards proposed to allow crossover claims, the majority-minority rule relies on an objective, numerical test: Do minorities make up more than 50 percent of the voting-age population in the relevant geographic area? Given \xc2\xa72\xe2\x80\x99s text, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases interpreting that provision, and the many difficulties in assessing \xc2\xa72 claims without the restraint and guidance provided by the majority-minority rule, all of the federal courts of appeals that have interpreted the first Gingles factor have required a majority-minority standard. The Court declines to depart from that uniform interpretation, which has stood for more than 20 years. Because this case does not involve allegations of intentional and wrongful conduct, the Court need not consider whether intentional discrimination affects the Gingles analysis. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Arguing for a less restrictive interpretation, petitioners point to \xc2\xa72\xe2\x80\x99s guarantee that political processes be \xe2\x80\x9cequally open to participation\xe2\x80\x9d to protect minority voters\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9copportunity \xe2\x80\xa6 to elect representatives of their choice,\xe2\x80\x9d 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71973(b), and assert that such \xe2\x80\x9copportunit[ies]\xe2\x80\x9d occur in crossover districts and require protection. But petitioners emphasize the word \xe2\x80\x9copportunity\xe2\x80\x9d at the expense of the word \xe2\x80\x9cequally.\xe2\x80\x9d The statute does not protect any possible opportunity through which minority voters could work with other constituencies to elect their candidate of choice. Section 2 does not guarantee minority voters an electoral advantage. Minority groups in crossover districts have the same opportunity to elect their candidate as any other political group with the same relative voting strength. The majority-minority rule, furthermore, is not at odds with \xc2\xa72\xe2\x80\x99s totality-of-the-circumstances test. See, e.g., Growe, supra, at 40. Any doubt as to whether \xc2\xa72 calls for this rule is resolved by applying the canon of constitutional avoidance to steer clear of serious constitutional concerns under the Equal Protection Clause. See Clark v. Martinez, 543 U. S. 371, 381\xe2\x80\x93382. Such concerns would be raised if \xc2\xa72 were interpreted to require crossover districts throughout the Nation, thereby \xe2\x80\x9cunnecessarily infus[ing] race into virtually every redistricting.\xe2\x80\x9d LULAC, supra, at 446. Pp.\xc2\xa016\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0This holding does not consider the permissibility of crossover districts as a matter of legislative choice or discretion. Section 2 allows States to choose their own method of complying with the Voting Rights Act, which may include drawing crossover districts. See Georgia v. Ashcroft, 539 U. S. 461, 480\xe2\x80\x93482. Moreover, the holding should not be interpreted to entrench majority-minority districts by statutory command, for that, too, could pose constitutional concerns. See, e.g., Miller v. Johnson, 515 U. S. 900. Such districts are only required if all three Gingles factors are met and if \xc2\xa72 applies based on the totality of the circumstances. A claim similar to petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 assertion that the majority-minority rule is inconsistent with \xc2\xa75 was rejected in LULAC, supra, at ___. Pp.\xc2\xa019\xe2\x80\x9321.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Thomas, joined by Justice Scalia, adhered to his view in Holder v. Hall, 512 U. S. 874, 891, 893 (opinion concurring in judgment), that the text of \xc2\xa72 of the Voting Rights Act of 1965 does not authorize any vote dilution claim, regardless of the size of the minority population in a given district. The Thornburg v. Gingles, 478 U. S. 30, framework for analyzing such claims has no basis in \xc2\xa72\xe2\x80\x99s text and \xe2\x80\x9chas produced \xe2\x80\xa6 a disastrous misadventure in judicial policymaking,\xe2\x80\x9d Holder, supra, at 893. P.\xc2\xa01.

GARY BARTLETT, EXECUTIVE DIRECTOR OF THE\nNORTH CAROLINA STATE BOARD OF ELECTIONS,\net\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. DWIGHT\nSTRICKLAND et\xc2\xa0al.

[March 9, 2009]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Kennedy announced the judgment of the Court and delivered an opinion, in which The Chief Justice and Justice Alito join.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case requires us to interpret \xc2\xa72 of the Voting Rights Act of 1965, 79 Stat. 437, as amended, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71973 (2000 ed.). The question is whether the statute can be invoked to require state officials to draw election-district lines to allow a racial minority to join with other voters to elect the minority\xe2\x80\x99s candidate of choice, even where the racial minority is less than 50 percent of the voting-age population in the district to be drawn. To use election-law terminology: In a district that is not a majority-minority district, if a racial minority could elect its candidate of choice with support from crossover majority voters, can \xc2\xa72 require the district to be drawn to accommodate this potential?

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The case arises in a somewhat unusual posture. State authorities who created a district now invoke the Voting Rights Act as a defense. They argue that \xc2\xa72 required them to draw the district in question in a particular way, despite state laws to the contrary. The state laws are provisions of the North Carolina Constitution that prohibit the General Assembly from dividing counties when drawing legislative districts for the State House and Senate. Art. II, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73, 5. We will adopt the term used by the state courts and refer to both sections of the state constitution as the Whole County Provision. See Pender County v. Bartlett, 361 N.\xc2\xa0C. 491, 493, 649 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 364, 366 (2007) (case below).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is common ground that state election-law requirements like the Whole County Provision may be superseded by federal law\xe2\x80\x94for instance, the one-person, one-vote principle of the Equal Protection Clause of the United States Constitution. See Reynolds v. Sims, 377 U. S. 533 (1964). Here the question is whether \xc2\xa72 of the Voting Rights Act requires district lines to be drawn that otherwise would violate the Whole County Provision. That, in turn, depends on how the statute is interpreted.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We begin with the election district. The North Carolina House of Representatives is the larger of the two chambers in the State\xe2\x80\x99s General Assembly. District 18 of that body lies in the southeastern part of North Carolina. Starting in 1991, the General Assembly drew District 18 to include portions of four counties, including Pender County, in order to create a district with a majority African-American voting-age population and to satisfy the Voting Rights Act. Following the 2000 census, the North Carolina Supreme Court, to comply with the Whole County Provision, rejected the General Assembly\xe2\x80\x99s first two statewide redistricting plans. See Stephenson v. Bartlett, 355 N.\xc2\xa0C. 354, 375, 562 S.\xc2\xa0E.\xc2\xa02d 377, 392, stay denied, 535 U. S. 1301 (2002) (Rehnquist, C.\xc2\xa0J., in chambers); Stephenson v. Bartlett, 357 N.\xc2\xa0C. 301, 314, 582 S.\xc2\xa0E.\xc2\xa02d 247, 254 (2003).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0District 18 in its present form emerged from the General Assembly\xe2\x80\x99s third redistricting attempt, in 2003. By that time the African-American voting-age population had fallen below 50 percent in the district as then drawn, and the General Assembly no longer could draw a geographically compact majority-minority district. Rather than draw District 18 to keep Pender County whole, however, the General Assembly drew it by splitting portions of Pender and New Hanover counties. District 18 has an African-American voting-age population of 39.36 percent. App. 139. Had it left Pender County whole, the General Assembly could have drawn District 18 with an African-American voting-age population of 35.33 percent. Id., at 73. The General Assembly\xe2\x80\x99s reason for splitting Pender County was to give African-American voters the potential to join with majority voters to elect the minority group\xe2\x80\x99s candidate of its choice. Ibid. Failure to do so, state officials now submit, would have diluted the minority group\xe2\x80\x99s voting strength in violation of \xc2\xa72.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In May 2004, Pender County and the five members of its Board of Commissioners filed the instant suit in North Carolina state court against the Governor of North Carolina, the Director of the State Board of Elections, and other state officials. The plaintiffs alleged that the 2003 plan violated the Whole County Provision by splitting Pender County into two House districts. App. 5\xe2\x80\x9314. The state-official defendants answered that dividing Pender County was required by \xc2\xa72. Id., at 25. As the trial court recognized, the procedural posture of this case differs from most \xc2\xa72 cases. Here the defendants raise \xc2\xa72 as a defense. As a result, the trial court stated, they are \xe2\x80\x9cin the unusual position\xe2\x80\x9d of bearing the burden of proving that a \xc2\xa72 violation would have occurred absent splitting Pender County to draw District 18. App. to Pet. for Cert. 90a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The trial court first considered whether the defendant state officials had established the three threshold requirements for \xc2\xa72 liability under Thornburg v. Gingles, 478 U. S. 30, 50\xe2\x80\x9351 (1986)\xe2\x80\x94namely, (1) that the minority group \xe2\x80\x9cis sufficiently large and geographically compact to constitute a majority in a single-member district,\xe2\x80\x9d (2) that the minority group is \xe2\x80\x9cpolitically cohesive,\xe2\x80\x9d and (3) \xe2\x80\x9cthat the white majority votes sufficiently as a bloc to enable it \xe2\x80\xa6 usually to defeat the minority\xe2\x80\x99s preferred candidate.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As to the first Gingles requirement, the trial court concluded that, although African-Americans were not a majority of the voting-age population in District 18, the district was a \xe2\x80\x9cde facto\xe2\x80\x9d majority-minority district because African-Americans could get enough support from crossover majority voters to elect the African-Americans\xe2\x80\x99 preferred candidate. The court ruled that African-Americans in District 18 were politically cohesive, thus satisfying the second requirement. And later, the plaintiffs stipulated that the third Gingles requirement was met. App. to Pet. for Cert. at 102a\xe2\x80\x93103a, 130a. The court then determined, based on the totality of the circumstances, that \xc2\xa72 required the General Assembly to split Pender County. The court sustained the lines for District 18 on that rationale. Id., at 116a\xe2\x80\x93118a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Three of the Pender County Commissioners appealed the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling that the defendants had established the first Gingles requirement. The Supreme Court of North Carolina reversed. It held that a \xe2\x80\x9cminority group must constitute a numerical majority of the voting population in the area under consideration before Section 2 \xe2\x80\xa6 requires the creation of a legislative district to prevent dilution of the votes of that minority group.\xe2\x80\x9d 361 N.\xc2\xa0C., at 502, 649 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 371. On that premise the State Supreme Court determined District 18 was not mandated by \xc2\xa72 because African-Americans do not \xe2\x80\x9cconstitute a numerical majority of citizens of voting age.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 507, 649 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 374. It ordered the General Assembly to redraw District 18. Id., at 510, 649 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 376.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari, 552 U. S. ___ (2008), and now affirm.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Passage of the Voting Rights Act of 1965 was an important step in the struggle to end discriminatory treatment of minorities who seek to exercise one of the most fundamental rights of our citizens: the right to vote. Though the Act as a whole was the subject of debate and controversy, \xc2\xa72 prompted little criticism. The likely explanation for its general acceptance is that, as first enacted, \xc2\xa72 tracked, in part, the text of the Fifteenth Amendment. It prohibited practices \xe2\x80\x9cimposed or applied by any State or political subdivision to deny or abridge the right of any citizen of the United States to vote on account of race or color.\xe2\x80\x9d 79 Stat. 437; cf. U. S. Const., Amdt. 15 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe right of citizens of the United States to vote shall not be denied or abridged by the United States or by any State on account of race, color, or previous condition of servitude\xe2\x80\x9d); see also S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 162, 89th Cong., 1st Sess., pt. 3, pp. 19\xe2\x80\x9320 (1965). In Mobile v. Bolden, 446 U. S. 55, 60\xe2\x80\x9361 (1980), this Court held that \xc2\xa72, as it then read, \xe2\x80\x9cno more than elaborates upon \xe2\x80\xa6 the Fifteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x9d and was \xe2\x80\x9cintended to have an effect no different from that of the Fifteenth Amendment itself.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1982, after the Mobile ruling, Congress amended \xc2\xa72, giving the statute its current form. The original Act had employed an intent requirement, prohibiting only those practices \xe2\x80\x9cimposed or applied \xe2\x80\xa6 to deny or abridge\xe2\x80\x9d the right to vote. 79 Stat. 437. The amended version of \xc2\xa72 requires consideration of effects, as it prohibits practices \xe2\x80\x9cimposed or applied \xe2\x80\xa6 in a manner which results in a denial or abridgment\xe2\x80\x9d of the right to vote. 96 Stat. 134, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71973(a) (2000 ed.). The 1982 amendments also added a subsection, \xc2\xa72(b), providing a test for determining whether a \xc2\xa72 violation has occurred. The relevant text of the statute now states:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(a)\xe2\x80\x82No voting qualification or prerequisite to voting or standard, practice, or procedure shall be imposed or applied by any State or political subdivision in a manner which results in a denial or abridgement of the right of any citizen of the United States to vote on account of race or color [or membership in a language minority group], as provided in subsection (b) of this section.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(b)\xe2\x80\x82A violation of subsection (a) of this section is established if, based on the totality of circumstances, it is shown that the political processes leading to nomination or election in the State or political subdivision are not equally open to participation by members of a class of citizens protected by subsection (a) of this section in that its members have less opportunity than other members of the electorate to participate in the political process and to elect representatives of their choice.\xe2\x80\x9d 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71973.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court first construed the amended version of \xc2\xa72 in Thornburg v. Gingles, 478 U. S. 30 (1986). In Gingles, the plaintiffs were African-American residents of North Carolina who alleged that multimember districts diluted minority voting strength by submerging black voters into the white majority, denying them an opportunity to elect a candidate of their choice. The Court identified three \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary preconditions\xe2\x80\x9d for a claim that the use of multimember districts constituted actionable vote dilution under \xc2\xa72: (1) The minority group must be \xe2\x80\x9csufficiently large and geographically compact to constitute a majority in a single-member district,\xe2\x80\x9d (2) the minority group must be \xe2\x80\x9cpolitically cohesive,\xe2\x80\x9d and (3) the majority must vote \xe2\x80\x9csufficiently as a bloc to enable it \xe2\x80\xa6 usually to defeat the minority\xe2\x80\x99s preferred candidate.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 50\xe2\x80\x9351.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court later held that the three Gingles requirements apply equally in \xc2\xa72 cases involving single-member districts, such as a claim alleging vote dilution because a geographically compact minority group has been split between two or more single-member districts. Growe v. Emison, 507 U. S. 25, 40\xe2\x80\x9341 (1993). In a \xc2\xa72 case, only when a party has established the Gingles requirements does a court proceed to analyze whether a violation has occurred based on the totality of the circumstances. Gingles, supra, at 79; see also Johnson v. De\xc2\xa0Grandy, 512 U. S. 997, 1013 (1994).

\n

III

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case turns on whether the first Gingles requirement can be satisfied when the minority group makes up less than 50 percent of the voting-age population in the potential election district. The parties agree on all other parts of the Gingles analysis, so the dispositive question is: What size minority group is sufficient to satisfy the first Gingles requirement?

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the outset the answer might not appear difficult to reach, for the Gingles Court said the minority group must \xe2\x80\x9cdemonstrate that it is sufficiently large and geographically compact to constitute a majority in a single-member district.\xe2\x80\x9d 478 U. S., at 50. This would seem to end the matter, as it indicates the minority group must demonstrate it can constitute \xe2\x80\x9ca majority.\xe2\x80\x9d But in Gingles and again in Growe the Court reserved what it considered to be a separate question\xe2\x80\x94whether, \xe2\x80\x9cwhen a plaintiff alleges that a voting practice or procedure impairs a minority\xe2\x80\x99s ability to influence, rather than alter, election results, a showing of geographical compactness of a minority group not sufficiently large to constitute a majority will suffice.\xe2\x80\x9d Growe, supra, at 41, n.\xc2\xa05; see also Gingles, supra, at 46\xe2\x80\x9347, n.\xc2\xa012. The Court has since applied the Gingles requirements in \xc2\xa72 cases but has declined to decide the minimum size minority group necessary to satisfy the first requirement. See Voinovich v. Quilter, 507 U. S. 146, 154 (1993); De\xc2\xa0Grandy, supra, at 1009; League of United Latin American Citizens v. Perry, 548 U. S. 399, 443 (2006) (opinion of Kennedy, J.) (LULAC). We must consider the minimum-size question in this case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is appropriate to review the terminology often used to describe various features of election districts in relation to the requirements of the Voting Rights Act. In majority-minority districts, a minority group composes a numerical, working majority of the voting-age population. Under present doctrine, \xc2\xa72 can require the creation of these districts. See, e.g., Voinovich, supra, at 154 (\xe2\x80\x9cPlacing black voters in a district in which they constitute a sizeable and therefore \xe2\x80\x98safe\xe2\x80\x99 majority ensures that they are able to elect their candidate of choice\xe2\x80\x9d); but see Holder v. Hall, 512 U. S. 874, 922\xe2\x80\x93923 (1994) (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment). At the other end of the spectrum are influence districts, in which a minority group can influence the outcome of an election even if its preferred candidate cannot be elected. This Court has held that \xc2\xa72 does not require the creation of influence districts. LULAC, supra, at 445 (opinion of Kennedy,\xc2\xa0J.).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The present case involves an intermediate type of district\xe2\x80\x94a so-called crossover district. Like an influence district, a crossover district is one in which minority voters make up less than a majority of the voting-age population. But in a crossover district, the minority population, at least potentially, is large enough to elect the candidate of its choice with help from voters who are members of the majority and who cross over to support the minority\xe2\x80\x99s preferred candidate. 361 N.\xc2\xa0C., at 501\xe2\x80\x93502, 649 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 371 (case below). This Court has referred sometimes to crossover districts as \xe2\x80\x9ccoalitional\xe2\x80\x9d districts, in recognition of the necessary coalition between minority and crossover majority voters. See Georgia v. Ashcroft, 539 U. S. 461, 483 (2003); see also Pildes, Is Voting Rights Law Now at War with Itself? Social Science and Voting Rights in the 2000s, 80 N.\xc2\xa0C. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1517, 1539 (2002) (hereinafter Pildes). But that term risks confusion with coalition-district claims in which two minority groups form a coalition to elect the candidate of the coalition\xe2\x80\x99s choice. See, e.g., Nixon v. Kent County, 76 F.\xc2\xa03d 1381, 1393 (CA6 1996) (en banc). We do not address that type of coalition district here. The petitioners in the present case (the state officials who were the defendants in the trial court) argue that \xc2\xa72 requires a crossover district, in which minority voters might be able to persuade some members of the majority to cross over and join with them.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners argue that although crossover districts do not include a numerical majority of minority voters, they still satisfy the first Gingles requirement because they are \xe2\x80\x9ceffective minority districts.\xe2\x80\x9d Under petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 theory keeping Pender County whole would have violated \xc2\xa72 by cracking the potential crossover district that they drew as District 18. See Gingles, 478 U. S., at 46, n.\xc2\xa011 (vote dilution \xe2\x80\x9cmay be caused by the dispersal of blacks into districts in which they constitute an ineffective minority of voters\xe2\x80\x9d). So, petitioners contend, \xc2\xa72 required them to override state law and split Pender County, drawing District 18 with an African-American voting-age population of 39.36 percent rather than keeping Pender County whole and leaving District 18 with an African-American voting-age population of 35.33 percent. We reject that claim.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, we conclude, the petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 theory is contrary to the mandate of \xc2\xa72. The statute requires a showing that minorities \xe2\x80\x9chave less opportunity than other members of the electorate to \xe2\x80\xa6 elect representatives of their choice.\xe2\x80\x9d 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71973(b) (2000 ed.). But because they form only 39 percent of the voting-age population in District 18, African-Americans standing alone have no better or worse opportunity to elect a candidate than does any other group of voters with the same relative voting strength. That is, African-Americans in District 18 have the opportunity to join other voters\xe2\x80\x94including other racial minorities, or whites, or both\xe2\x80\x94to reach a majority and elect their preferred candidate. They cannot, however, elect that candidate based on their own votes and without assistance from others. Recognizing a \xc2\xa72 claim in this circumstance would grant minority voters \xe2\x80\x9ca right to preserve their strength for the purposes of forging an advantageous political alliance.\xe2\x80\x9d Hall v. Virginia, 385 F.\xc2\xa03d 421, 431 (CA4 2004); see also Voinovich, supra, at 154 (minorities in crossover districts \xe2\x80\x9ccould not dictate electoral outcomes independently\xe2\x80\x9d). Nothing in \xc2\xa72 grants special protection to a minority group\xe2\x80\x99s right to form political coalitions. \xe2\x80\x9c[M]inority voters are not immune from the obligation to pull, haul, and trade to find common political ground.\xe2\x80\x9d De\xc2\xa0Grandy, 512 U. S., at 1020.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although the Court has reserved the question we confront today and has cautioned that the Gingles requirements \xe2\x80\x9ccannot be applied mechanically,\xe2\x80\x9d Voinovich, supra, at 158, the reasoning of our cases does not support petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims. Section 2 does not impose on those who draw election districts a duty to give minority voters the most potential, or the best potential, to elect a candidate by attracting crossover voters. In setting out the first requirement for \xc2\xa72 claims, the Gingles Court explained that \xe2\x80\x9c[u]nless minority voters possess the potential to elect representatives in the absence of the challenged structure or practice, they cannot claim to have been injured by that structure or practice.\xe2\x80\x9d 478 U. S., at 50, n.\xc2\xa017. The Growe Court stated that the first Gingles requirement is \xe2\x80\x9cneeded to establish that the minority has the potential to elect a representative of its own choice in some single-member district.\xe2\x80\x9d 507 U. S., at 40. Without such a showing, \xe2\x80\x9cthere neither has been a wrong nor can be a remedy.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 41. There is a difference between a racial minority group\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cown choice\xe2\x80\x9d and the choice made by a coalition. In Voinovich, the Court stated that the first Gingles requirement \xe2\x80\x9cwould have to be modified or eliminated\xe2\x80\x9d to allow crossover-district claims. 507 U. S., at 158. Only once, in dicta, has this Court framed the first Gingles requirement as anything other than a majority-minority rule. See De\xc2\xa0Grandy, 512 U. S., at 1008 (requiring \xe2\x80\x9ca sufficiently large minority population to elect candidates of its choice\xe2\x80\x9d). And in the same case, the Court rejected the proposition, inherent in petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claim here, that \xc2\xa72 entitles minority groups to the maximum possible voting strength:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[R]eading \xc2\xa72 to define dilution as any failure to maximize tends to obscure the very object of the statute and to run counter to its textually stated purpose. One may suspect vote dilution from political famine, but one is not entitled to suspect (much less infer) dilution from mere failure to guarantee a political feast.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1016\xe2\x80\x931017.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Allowing crossover-district claims would require us to revise and reformulate the Gingles threshold inquiry that has been the baseline of our \xc2\xa72 jurisprudence. Mandatory recognition of claims in which success for a minority depends upon crossover majority voters would create serious tension with the third Gingles requirement that the majority votes as a bloc to defeat minority-preferred candidates. It is difficult to see how the majority-bloc-voting requirement could be met in a district where, by definition, white voters join in sufficient numbers with minority voters to elect the minority\xe2\x80\x99s preferred candidate. (We are skeptical that the bloc-voting test could be satisfied here, for example, where minority voters in District 18 cannot elect their candidate of choice without support from almost 20 percent of white voters. We do not confront that issue, however, because for some reason respondents conceded the third Gingles requirement in state court.)

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the Gingles Court explained, \xe2\x80\x9cin the absence of significant white bloc voting it cannot be said that the ability of minority voters to elect their chosen representatives is inferior to that of white voters.\xe2\x80\x9d 478 U. S., at 49, n.\xc2\xa015. Were the Court to adopt petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 theory and dispense with the majority-minority requirement, the ruling would call in question the Gingles framework the Court has applied under \xc2\xa72. See LULAC, 548 U. S., at 490, n.\xc2\xa08. (Souter, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part) (\xe2\x80\x9cAll aspects of our established analysis for majority-minority districts in Gingles and its progeny may have to be rethought in analyzing ostensible coalition districts\xe2\x80\x9d); cf. Metts v. Murphy, 363 F.\xc2\xa03d 8, 12 (CA1 2004) (en banc) (per curiam) (allowing influence-district claim to survive motion to dismiss but noting \xe2\x80\x9cthere is tension in this case for plaintiffs in any effort to satisfy both the first and third prong of Gingles\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We find support for the majority-minority requirement in the need for workable standards and sound judicial and legislative administration. The rule draws clear lines for courts and legislatures alike. The same cannot be said of a less exacting standard that would mandate crossover districts under \xc2\xa72. Determining whether a \xc2\xa72 claim would lie\xe2\x80\x94i.e., determining whether potential districts could function as crossover districts\xe2\x80\x94would place courts in the untenable position of predicting many political variables and tying them to race-based assumptions. The judiciary would be directed to make predictions or adopt premises that even experienced polling analysts and political experts could not assess with certainty, particularly over the long term. For example, courts would be required to pursue these inquiries: What percentage of white voters supported minority-preferred candidates in the past? How reliable would the crossover votes be in future elections? What types of candidates have white and minority voters supported together in the past and will those trends continue? Were past crossover votes based on incumbency and did that depend on race? What are the historical turnout rates among white and minority voters and will they stay the same? Those questions are speculative, and the answers (if they could be supposed) would prove elusive. A requirement to draw election districts on answers to these and like inquiries ought not to be inferred from the text or purpose of \xc2\xa72. Though courts are capable of making refined and exacting factual inquiries, they \xe2\x80\x9care inherently ill-equipped\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9cmake decisions based on highly political judgments\xe2\x80\x9d of the sort that crossover-district claims would require. Holder, 512 U. S., at 894 (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment). There is an underlying principle of fundamental importance: We must be most cautious before interpreting a statute to require courts to make inquiries based on racial classifications and race-based predictions. The statutory mandate petitioners urge us to find in \xc2\xa72 raises serious constitutional questions. See infra, at 16\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Heightening these concerns even further is the fact that \xc2\xa72 applies nationwide to every jurisdiction that must draw lines for election districts required by state or local law. Crossover-district claims would require courts to make predictive political judgments not only about familiar, two-party contests in large districts but also about regional and local jurisdictions that often feature more than two parties or candidates. Under petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 view courts would face the difficult task of discerning crossover patterns in nonpartisan contests for a city commission, a school board, or a local water authority. The political data necessary to make such determinations are nonexistent for elections in most of those jurisdictions. And predictions would be speculative at best given that, especially in the context of local elections, voters\xe2\x80\x99 personal affiliations with candidates and views on particular issues can play a large role.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Unlike any of the standards proposed to allow crossover-district claims, the majority-minority rule relies on an objective, numerical test: Do minorities make up more than 50 percent of the voting-age population in the relevant geographic area? That rule provides straightforward guidance to courts and to those officials charged with drawing district lines to comply with \xc2\xa72. See LULAC, supra, at 485 (opinion of Souter, J.) (recognizing need for \xe2\x80\x9cclear-edged rule\xe2\x80\x9d). Where an election district could be drawn in which minority voters form a majority but such a district is not drawn, or where a majority-minority district is cracked by assigning some voters elsewhere, then\xe2\x80\x94assuming the other Gingles factors are also satisfied\xe2\x80\x94denial of the opportunity to elect a candidate of choice is a present and discernible wrong that is not subject to the high degree of speculation and prediction attendant upon the analysis of crossover claims. Not an arbitrary invention, the majority-minority rule has its foundation in principles of democratic governance. The special significance, in the democratic process, of a majority means it is a special wrong when a minority group has 50 percent or more of the voting population and could constitute a compact voting majority but, despite racially polarized bloc voting, that group is not put into a district.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Given the text of \xc2\xa72, our cases interpreting that provision, and the many difficulties in assessing \xc2\xa72 claims without the restraint and guidance provided by the majority-minority rule, no federal court of appeals has held that \xc2\xa72 requires creation of coalition districts. Instead, all to consider the question have interpreted the first Gingles factor to require a majority-minority standard. See Hall, 385 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 427\xe2\x80\x93430 (CA4 2004), cert. denied, 544 U. S. 961 (2005); Valdespino v. Alamo Heights Independent School Dist., 168 F.\xc2\xa03d 848, 852\xe2\x80\x93853 (CA5 1999), cert. denied, 528 U. S. 1114 (2000); Cousin v. Sundquist, 145 F.\xc2\xa03d 818, 828\xe2\x80\x93829 (CA6 1998), cert. denied, 525 U. S. 1138 (1999); Sanchez v. Colorado, 97 F.\xc2\xa03d 1303, 1311\xe2\x80\x931312 (CA10 1996), cert. denied, 520 U. S. 1229 (1997); Romero v. Pomona, 883 F.\xc2\xa02d 1418, 1424, n.\xc2\xa07, 1425\xe2\x80\x931426 (CA9 1989), overruled on other grounds, 914 F.\xc2\xa02d 1136, 1141 (CA9 1990); McNeil v. Springfield Park Dist., 851 F.\xc2\xa02d 937, 947 (CA7 1988), cert. denied, 490 U. S. 1031 (1989). Cf. Metts, 363 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 11 (expressing unwillingness \xe2\x80\x9cat the complaint stage to foreclose the possibility\xe2\x80\x9d of influence-district claims). We decline to depart from the uniform interpretation of \xc2\xa72 that has guided federal courts and state and local officials for more than 20 years.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To be sure, the Gingles requirements \xe2\x80\x9ccannot be applied mechanically and without regard to the nature of the claim.\xe2\x80\x9d Voinovich, 507 U. S., at 158. It remains the rule, however, that a party asserting \xc2\xa72 liability must show by a preponderance of the evidence that the minority population in the potential election district is greater than 50 percent. No one contends that the African-American voting-age population in District 18 exceeds that threshold. Nor does this case involve allegations of intentional and wrongful conduct. We therefore need not consider whether intentional discrimination affects the Gingles analysis. Cf. Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 14 (evidence of discriminatory intent \xe2\x80\x9ctends to suggest that the jurisdiction is not providing an equal opportunity to minority voters to elect the representative of their choice, and it is therefore unnecessary to consider the majority-minority requirement before proceeding to the ultimate totality-of-the-circumstances analysis\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Garza v. County of Los Angeles, 918 F.\xc2\xa02d 763, 771 (CA9 1990). Our holding does not apply to cases in which there is intentional discrimination against a racial minority.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In arguing for a less restrictive interpretation of the first Gingles requirement petitioners point to the text of \xc2\xa72 and its guarantee that political processes be \xe2\x80\x9cequally open to participation\xe2\x80\x9d to protect minority voters\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9copportunity \xe2\x80\xa6 to elect representatives of their choice.\xe2\x80\x9d 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71973(b) (2000 ed.). An \xe2\x80\x9copportunity,\xe2\x80\x9d petitioners argue, occurs in crossover districts as well as majority-minority districts; and these extended opportunities, they say, require \xc2\xa72 protection.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But petitioners put emphasis on the word \xe2\x80\x9copportunity\xe2\x80\x9d at the expense of the word \xe2\x80\x9cequally.\xe2\x80\x9d The statute does not protect any possible opportunity or mechanism through which minority voters could work with other constituencies to elect their candidate of choice. Section 2 does not guarantee minority voters an electoral advantage. Minority groups in crossover districts cannot form a voting majority without crossover voters. In those districts minority voters have the same opportunity to elect their candidate as any other political group with the same relative voting strength.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority-minority rule, furthermore, is not at odds with \xc2\xa72\xe2\x80\x99s totality-of-the-circumstances test. The Court in De\xc2\xa0Grandy confirmed \xe2\x80\x9cthe error of treating the three Gingles conditions as exhausting the enquiry required by \xc2\xa72.\xe2\x80\x9d 512 U. S., at 1013. Instead the Gingles requirements are preconditions, consistent with the text and purpose of \xc2\xa72, to help courts determine which claims could meet the totality-of-the-circumstances standard for a \xc2\xa72 violation. See Growe, 507 U. S., at 40 (describing the \xe2\x80\x9cGingles threshold factors\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To the extent there is any doubt whether \xc2\xa72 calls for the majority-minority rule, we resolve that doubt by avoiding serious constitutional concerns under the Equal Protection Clause. See Clark v. Martinez, 543 U. S. 371, 381\xe2\x80\x93382 (2005) (canon of constitutional avoidance is \xe2\x80\x9ca tool for choosing between competing plausible interpretations of a statutory text, resting on the reasonable presumption that Congress did not intend the alternative which raises serious constitutional doubts\xe2\x80\x9d). Of course, the \xe2\x80\x9cmoral imperative of racial neutrality is the driving force of the Equal Protection Clause,\xe2\x80\x9d and racial classifications are permitted only \xe2\x80\x9cas a last resort.\xe2\x80\x9d Richmond v. J. A. Croson Co., 488 U. S. 469, 518, 519 (1989) (Kennedy, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment). \xe2\x80\x9cRacial classifications with respect to voting carry particular dangers. Racial gerrymandering, even for remedial purposes, may balkanize us into competing racial factions; it threatens to carry us further from the goal of a political system in which race no longer matters\xe2\x80\x94a goal that the Fourteenth and Fifteenth Amendments embody, and to which the Nation continues to aspire.\xe2\x80\x9d Shaw v. Reno, 509 U. S. 630, 657 (1993). If \xc2\xa72 were interpreted to require crossover districts throughout the Nation, \xe2\x80\x9cit would unnecessarily infuse race into virtually every redistricting, raising serious constitutional questions.\xe2\x80\x9d LULAC, 548 U. S., at 446 (opinion of Kennedy, J.); see also Ashcroft, 539 U. S., at 491 (Kennedy, J., concurring). That interpretation would result in a substantial increase in the number of mandatory districts drawn with race as \xe2\x80\x9cthe predominant factor motivating the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s decision.\xe2\x80\x9d Miller v. Johnson, 515 U. S. 900, 916 (1995).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 view of the case courts and legislatures would need to scrutinize every factor that enters into districting to gauge its effect on crossover voting. Injecting this racial measure into the nationwide districting process would be of particular concern with respect to consideration of party registration or party influence. The easiest and most likely alliance for a group of minority voters is one with a political party, and some have suggested using minority voters\xe2\x80\x99 strength within a particular party as the proper yardstick under the first Gingles requirement. See, e.g., LULAC, supra, at 485\xe2\x80\x93486 (opinion of Souter, J.) (requiring only \xe2\x80\x9cthat minority voters \xe2\x80\xa6 constitute a majority of those voting in the primary of \xe2\x80\xa6 the party tending to win in the general election\xe2\x80\x9d). That approach would replace an objective, administrable rule with a difficult \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial inquiry into party rules and local politics\xe2\x80\x9d to determine whether a minority group truly \xe2\x80\x9ccontrols\xe2\x80\x9d the dominant party\xe2\x80\x99s primary process. McLoughlin, Gingles in Limbo: Coalitional Districts, Party Primaries and Manageable Vote Dilution Claims, 80 N.\xc2\xa0Y.\xc2\xa0U. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 312, 349 (2005). More troubling still is the inquiry\xe2\x80\x99s fusion of race and party affiliation as a determinant when partisan considerations themselves may be suspect in the drawing of district lines. See Vieth v. Jubelirer, 541 U. S. 267, 317 (2004) (Stevens, J., dissenting); id., at 316 (Kennedy, J., concurring in judgment); see also Pildes 1565 (crossover-district requirement would essentially result in political party \xe2\x80\x9centitlement to \xe2\x80\xa6 a certain number of seats\xe2\x80\x9d). Disregarding the majority-minority rule and relying on a combination of race and party to presume an effective majority would involve the law and courts in a perilous enterprise. It would rest on judicial predictions, as a matter of law, that race and party would hold together as an effective majority over time\xe2\x80\x94at least for the decennial apportionment cycles and likely beyond. And thus would the relationship between race and party further distort and frustrate the search for neutral factors and principled rationales for districting.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 approach would reverse the canon of avoidance. It invites the divisive constitutional questions that are both unnecessary and contrary to the purposes of our precedents under the Voting Rights Act. Given the consequences of extending racial considerations even further into the districting process, we must not interpret \xc2\xa72 to require crossover districts.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our holding that \xc2\xa72 does not require crossover districts does not consider the permissibility of such districts as a matter of legislative choice or discretion. Assuming a majority-minority district with a substantial minority population, a legislative determination, based on proper factors, to create two crossover districts may serve to diminish the significance and influence of race by encouraging minority and majority voters to work together toward a common goal. The option to draw such districts gives legislatures a choice that can lead to less racial isolation, not more. And as the Court has noted in the context of \xc2\xa75 of the Voting Rights Act, \xe2\x80\x9cvarious studies have suggested that the most effective way to maximize minority voting strength may be to create more influence or [crossover] districts.\xe2\x80\x9d Ashcroft, 539 U. S., at 482. Much like \xc2\xa75, \xc2\xa72 allows States to choose their own method of complying with the Voting Rights Act, and we have said that may include drawing crossover districts. See id., at 480\xe2\x80\x93483. When we address the mandate of \xc2\xa72, however, we must note it is not concerned with maximizing minority voting strength, De\xc2\xa0Grandy, 512 U. S., at 1022; and, as a statutory matter, \xc2\xa72 does not mandate creating or preserving crossover districts.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our holding also should not be interpreted to entrench majority-minority districts by statutory command, for that, too, could pose constitutional concerns. See Miller v. Johnson, supra; Shaw v. Reno, supra. States that wish to draw crossover districts are free to do so where no other prohibition exists. Majority-minority districts are only required if all three Gingles factors are met and if \xc2\xa72 applies based on a totality of the circumstances. In areas with substantial crossover voting it is unlikely that the plaintiffs would be able to establish the third Gingles precondition\xe2\x80\x94bloc voting by majority voters. See supra, at 11. In those areas majority-minority districts would not be required in the first place; and in the exercise of lawful discretion States could draw crossover districts as they deemed appropriate. See Pildes 1567 (\xe2\x80\x9cDistricts could still be designed in such places that encouraged coalitions across racial lines, but these districts would result from legislative choice, not \xe2\x80\xa6 obligation\xe2\x80\x9d). States can\xe2\x80\x94and in proper cases should\xe2\x80\x94defend against alleged \xc2\xa72 violations by pointing to crossover voting patterns and to effective crossover districts. Those can be evidence, for example, of diminished bloc voting under the third Gingles factor or of equal political opportunity under the \xc2\xa72 totality-of-the-circumstances analysis. And if there were a showing that a State intentionally drew district lines in order to destroy otherwise effective crossover districts, that would raise serious questions under both the Fourteenth and Fifteenth Amendments. See Reno v. Bossier Parish School Bd., 520 U. S. 471, 481\xe2\x80\x93482 (1997); Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 13\xe2\x80\x9314. There is no evidence of discriminatory intent in this case, however. Our holding recognizes only that there is no support for the claim that \xc2\xa72\ncan require the creation of crossover districts in the first\ninstance.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners claim the majority-minority rule is inconsistent with \xc2\xa75, but we rejected a similar argument in LULAC, 548 U. S., at 446 (opinion of Kennedy,\xc2\xa0J.). The inquiries under \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72 and 5 are different. Section 2 concerns minority groups\xe2\x80\x99 opportunity \xe2\x80\x9cto elect representatives of their choice,\xe2\x80\x9d 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71973(b) (2000 ed.), while the more stringent \xc2\xa75 asks whether a change has the purpose or effect of \xe2\x80\x9cdenying or abridging the right to vote,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71973c. See LULAC, supra, at 446; Bossier Parish, supra, at 476\xe2\x80\x93480. In LULAC, we held that although the presence of influence districts is relevant for the \xc2\xa75 retrogression analysis, \xe2\x80\x9cthe lack of such districts cannot establish a \xc2\xa72 violation.\xe2\x80\x9d 548 U. S., at 446 (opinion of Kennedy,\xc2\xa0J.); see also Ashcroft, 539 U. S., at 482\xe2\x80\x93483. The same analysis applies for crossover districts: Section 5 \xe2\x80\x9cleaves room\xe2\x80\x9d for States to employ crossover districts, id., at 483, but \xc2\xa72 does not require them.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Some commentators suggest that racially polarized voting is waning\xe2\x80\x94as evidenced by, for example, the election of minority candidates where a majority of voters are white. See Note, The Future of Majority-Minority Districts in Light of Declining Racially Polarized Voting, 116 Harv. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 2208, 2209 (2003); see also id., at 2216\xe2\x80\x932222; Pildes 1529\xe2\x80\x931539; Bullock & Dunn, The Demise of Racial Districting and the Future of Black Representation, 48 Emory L.\xc2\xa0J. 1209 (1999). Still, racial discrimination and racially polarized voting are not ancient history. Much remains to be done to ensure that citizens of all races have equal opportunity to share and participate in our democratic processes and traditions; and \xc2\xa72 must be interpreted to ensure that continued progress.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It would be an irony, however, if \xc2\xa72 were interpreted to entrench racial differences by expanding a \xe2\x80\x9cstatute meant to hasten the waning of racism in American politics.\xe2\x80\x9d De\xc2\xa0Grandy, supra, at 1020. Crossover districts are, by definition, the result of white voters joining forces with minority voters to elect their preferred candidate. The Voting Rights Act was passed to foster this cooperation. We decline now to expand the reaches of \xc2\xa72 to require, by force of law, the voluntary cooperation our society has achieved. Only when a geographically compact group of minority voters could form a majority in a single-member district has the first Gingles requirement been met.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment of the Supreme Court of North Carolina is affirmed.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I join Justice Souter\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in full. I write separately in light of the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s claim that a bright-line 50% rule (used as a Gingles gateway) serves administrative objectives. In the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s view, that rule amounts to a relatively simple administrative device that will help separate at the outset those cases that are more likely meritorious from those that are not. Even were that objective as critically important as the plurality believes, however, it is not difficult to find other numerical gateway rules that would work better.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Assume that a basic purpose of a gateway number is to separate (1) districts where a minority group can \xe2\x80\x9celect representatives of their choice,\xe2\x80\x9d from (2) districts where the minority, because of the need to obtain majority crossover votes, can only \xe2\x80\x9celect representatives\xe2\x80\x9d that are consensus candidates. 42 U. S. C. \xc2\xa71973(b) (2000 ed.); League of United Latin American Citizens v. Perry, 548 U. S. 399, 445 (2006) (plurality opinion). At first blush, one might think that a 50% rule will work in this respect. After all, if a 50% minority population votes as a bloc, can it not always elect the candidate of its choice? And if a minority population constitutes less than 50% of a district, is not any candidate elected from that district always a consensus choice of minority and majority voters? The realities of voting behavior, however, make clear that the answer to both these questions is \xe2\x80\x9cno.\xe2\x80\x9d See, e.g., Brief for Nathaniel Persily et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 5\xe2\x80\x936. (\xe2\x80\x9cFifty percent is seen as a magic number by some because under conditions of complete racial polarization and equal rates of voting eligibility, registration, and turnout, the minority community will be able to elect its candidate of choice. In practice, such extreme conditions are never present. . . . [S]ome districts must be more than 50% minority, while others can be less than 50% minority, in order for the minority community to have an equal opportunity to elect its candidate of choice\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); see also ante, at 8 (Souter, J., dissenting).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0No voting group is 100% cohesive. Except in districts with overwhelming minority populations, some crossover votes are often necessary. The question is how likely it is that the need for crossover votes will force a minority to reject its \xe2\x80\x9cpreferred choice\xe2\x80\x9d in favor of a \xe2\x80\x9cconsensus candidate.\xe2\x80\x9d A 50% number does not even try to answer that question. To the contrary, it includes, say 51% minority districts, where imperfect cohesion may, in context, prevent election of the \xe2\x80\x9cminority-preferred\xe2\x80\x9d candidate, while it excludes, say, 45% districts where a smaller but more cohesive minority can, with the help of a small and reliable majority crossover vote, elect its preferred candidate.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Why not use a numerical gateway rule that looks more directly at the relevant question: Is the minority bloc large enough, is it cohesive enough, is the necessary majority crossover vote small enough, so that the minority (tending to vote cohesively) can likely vote its preferred candidate (rather than a consensus candidate) into office? See ante, at 7 (Souter, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9c[E]mpirical studies confirm[] that . . . minority groups\xe2\x80\x9d constituting less than 50% of the voting population \xe2\x80\x9cregularly elect their preferred candidates with the help of modest crossover by members of the majority\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Pildes, Is Voting-Rights Law Now at War With Itself? Social Science and Voting Rights in the 2000s, 80 N. C. L. Rev. 1517, 1529\xe2\x80\x931535 (2002) (reviewing studies showing small but reliable crossover voting by whites in districts where minority voters have demonstrated the ability to elect their preferred candidates without constituting 50% of the population in that district). We can likely find a reasonably administrable mathematical formula more directly tied to the factors in question.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To take a possible example: Suppose we pick a numerical ratio that requires the minority voting age population to be twice as large as the percentage of majority crossover votes needed to elect the minority\xe2\x80\x99s preferred candidate. We would calculate the latter (the percentage of majority crossover votes the minority voters need) to take account of both the percentage of minority voting age population in the district and the cohesiveness with which they vote. Thus, if minority voters account for 45% of the voters in a district and 89% of those voters tend to vote cohesively as a group, then the minority needs a crossover vote of about 20% of the majority voters to elect its preferred candidate. (Such a district with 100 voters would have 45 minority voters and 55 majority voters; 40 minority voters would vote for the minority group\xe2\x80\x99s preferred candidate at election time; the minority voters would need 11 more votes to elect their preferred candidate; and 11 is about 20% of the majority\xe2\x80\x99s 55.) The larger the minority population, the greater its cohesiveness, and thus the smaller the crossover vote needed to assure success, the greater the likelihood that the minority can elect its preferred candidate and the smaller the likelihood that the cohesive minority, in order to find the needed majority crossover vote, must support a consensus, rather than its preferred, candidate.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In reflecting the reality that minority voters can elect the candidate of their choice when they constitute less than 50% of a district by relying on a small majority crossover vote, this approach is in no way contradictory, or even in tension with, the third Gingles requirement. Since Gingles itself, we have acknowledged that the requirement of majority-bloc voting can be satisfied even when some small number of majority voters crossover to support a minority-preferred candidate. See Thornburg v. Gingles, 478 U. S. 30, 59 (1986) (finding majority-bloc voting where the majority group supported African-American candidates in the general election at a rate of between 26% and 49%, with an average support of one-third). Given the difficulty of obtaining totally accurate statistics about cohesion, or even voting age population, the district courts should administer the numerical ratio flexibly, opening (or closing) the Gingles gate (in light of the probable merits of a case) where only small variances are at issue (e.g., where the minority group is 39% instead of 40% of a district). But the same is true with a 50% number (e.g., where the minority group is 49% instead of 50% of a district). See, e.g., Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 15.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I do not claim that the 2-to-1 ratio is a perfect rule; I claim only that it is better than the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s 50% rule. After all, unlike 50%, a 2-to-1 ratio (of voting age minority population to necessary non-minority crossover votes) focuses directly upon the problem at hand, better reflects voting realities, and consequently far better separates at the gateway likely sheep from likely goats. See Gingles, supra, at 45 (The Section 2 inquiry depends on a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98functional\xe2\x80\x99 view of the political process\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a searching practical evaluation of the past and present reality.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting S. Rep. No. 97\xe2\x80\x93417, p.\xc2\xa030, and n. 120 (1982)); Gingles, supra, at 94\xe2\x80\x9395 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]here is no indication that Congress intended to mandate a single, universally applicable standard for measuring undiluted minority voting strength, regardless of local conditions . . . \xe2\x80\x9d). In most cases, the 50% rule and the 2-to-1 rule would have roughly similar effects. Most districts where the minority voting age population is greater than 50% will almost always satisfy the 2-to-1 rule; and most districts where the minority population is below 40% will almost never satisfy the 2-to-1 rule. But in districts with minority voting age populations that range from 40% to 50%, the divergent approaches of the two standards can make a critical difference\xe2\x80\x94as well they should.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In a word, Justice Souter well explains why the majority\xe2\x80\x99s test is ill suited to the statute\xe2\x80\x99s objectives. I add that the test the majority adopts is ill suited to its own administrative ends. Better gateway tests, if needed, can be found.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0With respect, I dissent.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The question in this case is whether a minority with under 50% of the voting population of a proposed voting district can ever qualify under \xc2\xa72 of the Voting Rights Act of 1965 (VRA) as residents of a putative district whose minority voters would have an opportunity \xe2\x80\x9cto elect representatives of their choice.\xe2\x80\x9d 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71973(b) (2000 ed.). If the answer is no, minority voters in such a district will have no right to claim relief under \xc2\xa72 from a statewide districting scheme that dilutes minority voting rights. I would hold that the answer in law as well as in fact is sometimes yes: a district may be a minority-opportunity district so long as a cohesive minority population is large enough to elect its chosen candidate when combined with a reliable number of crossover voters from an otherwise polarized majority.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s view, only a district with a minority population making up 50% or more of the citizen voting age population (CVAP) can provide a remedy to minority voters lacking an opportunity \xe2\x80\x9cto elect representatives of their choice.\xe2\x80\x9d This is incorrect as a factual matter if the statutory phrase is given its natural meaning; minority voters in districts with minority populations under 50% routinely \xe2\x80\x9celect representatives of their choice.\xe2\x80\x9d The effects of the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s unwillingness to face this fact are disturbing by any measure and flatly at odds with the obvious purpose of the Act. If districts with minority populations under 50% can never count as minority-opportunity districts to remedy a violation of the States\xe2\x80\x99 obligation to provide equal electoral opportunity under \xc2\xa72, States will be required under the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s rule to pack black voters into additional majority-minority districts, contracting the number of districts where racial minorities are having success in transcending racial divisions in securing their preferred representation. The object of the Voting Rights Act will now be promoting racial blocs, and the role of race in districting decisions as a proxy for political identification will be heightened by any measure.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Recalling the basic premises of vote-dilution claims under \xc2\xa72 will show just how far astray the plurality has gone. Section 2 of the VRA prohibits districting practices that \xe2\x80\x9cresul[t] in a denial or abridgement of the right of any citizen of the United States to vote on account of race.\xe2\x80\x9d 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71973(a). A denial or abridgment is established if, \xe2\x80\x9cbased on the totality of circumstances,\xe2\x80\x9d it is shown that members of a racial minority \xe2\x80\x9chave less opportunity than other members of the electorate to participate in the political process and to elect representatives of their choice.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71973(b).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Since \xc2\xa72 was amended in 1982, 96 Stat. 134, we have read it to prohibit practices that result in \xe2\x80\x9cvote dilution,\xe2\x80\x9d see Thornburg v. Gingles, 478 U. S. 30 (1986), understood as distributing politically cohesive minority voters through voting districts in ways that reduce their potential strength. See id., at 47\xe2\x80\x9348. There are two classic patterns. Where voting is racially polarized, a districting plan can systemically discount the minority vote either \xe2\x80\x9cby the dispersal of blacks into districts in which they constitute an ineffective minority of voters\xe2\x80\x9d or from \xe2\x80\x9cthe concentration of blacks into districts where they constitute an excessive majority,\xe2\x80\x9d so as to eliminate their influence in neighboring districts. Id., at 46, n. 11. Treating dilution as a remediable harm recognizes that \xc2\xa72 protects not merely the right of minority voters to put ballots in a box, but to claim a fair number of districts in which their votes can be effective. See id., at 47.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Three points follow. First, to speak of a fair chance to get the representation desired, there must be an identifiable baseline for measuring a group\xe2\x80\x99s voting strength. Id., at 88 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9cIn order to evaluate a claim that a particular multimember district or single-member district has diluted the minority group\xe2\x80\x99s voting strength to a degree that violates \xc2\xa72, \xe2\x80\xa6 it is \xe2\x80\xa6 necessary to construct a measure of \xe2\x80\x98undiluted\xe2\x80\x99 minority voting strength\xe2\x80\x9d). Several baselines can be imagined; one could, for example, compare a minority\xe2\x80\x99s voting strength under a particular districting plan with the maximum strength possible under any alternative.[Footnote 1] Not surprisingly, we have conclusively rejected this approach; the VRA was passed to guarantee minority voters a fair game, not a killing. See Johnson v. De Grandy, 512 U. S. 997, 1016\xe2\x80\x931017 (1994). We have held that the better baseline for measuring opportunity to elect under \xc2\xa72, although not dispositive, is the minority\xe2\x80\x99s rough proportion of the relevant population. Id., at 1013\xe2\x80\x931023. Thus, in assessing \xc2\xa72 claims under a totality of the circumstances, including the facts of history and geography, the starting point is a comparison of the number of districts where minority voters can elect their chosen candidate with the group\xe2\x80\x99s population percentage. Ibid; see also League of United Latin American Citizens v. Perry, 548 U. S. 399, 436 (2006) (LULAC) (\xe2\x80\x9cWe proceed now to the totality of the circumstances, and first to the proportionality inquiry, comparing the percentage of total districts that are [minority] opportunity districts with the [minority] share of the citizen voting-age population\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 2]\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, the significance of proportionality means that a \xc2\xa72 claim must be assessed by looking at the overall effect of a multidistrict plan. A State with one congressional seat cannot dilute a minority\xe2\x80\x99s congressional vote, and only the systemic submergence of minority votes where a number of single-member districts could be drawn can be treated as harm under \xc2\xa72. So a \xc2\xa72 complaint must look to an entire districting plan (normally, statewide), alleging that the challenged plan creates an insufficient number of minority-opportunity districts in the territory as a whole. See id., at 436\xe2\x80\x93437.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Third, while a \xc2\xa72 violation ultimately results from the dilutive effect of a districting plan as a whole, a \xc2\xa72 plaintiff must also be able to place himself in a reasonably compact district that could have been drawn to improve upon the plan actually selected. See, e.g., De Grandy, supra, at 1001\xe2\x80\x931002. That is, a plaintiff must show both an overall deficiency and a personal injury open to redress.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our first essay at understanding these features of statutory vote dilution was Thornburg v. Gingles, which asked whether a multimember district plan for choosing representatives by at-large voting deprived minority voters of an equal opportunity to elect their preferred candidates. In answering, we set three now-familiar conditions that a \xc2\xa72 claim must meet at the threshold before a court will analyze it under the totality of circumstances:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cFirst, the minority group must be able to demonstrate that it is sufficiently large and geographically compact to constitute a majority in a single-member district\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. Second, the minority group must be able to show that it is politically cohesive\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. Third, the minority must be able to demonstrate that the white majority votes sufficiently as a bloc to enable it \xe2\x80\xa6 usually to defeat the minority\xe2\x80\x99s preferred candidate.\xe2\x80\x9d 478 U. S., at 50\xe2\x80\x9351.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As we have emphasized over and over, the Gingles conditions do not state the ultimate standard under \xc2\xa72, nor could they, since the totality of the circumstances standard has been set explicitly by Congress. See LULAC, supra, at 425\xe2\x80\x93426; De Grandy, supra, at 1011. Instead, each condition serves as a gatekeeper, ensuring that a plaintiff who proceeds to plenary review has a real chance to show a redressable violation of the ultimate \xc2\xa72 standard. The third condition, majority racial bloc voting, is necessary to establish the premise of vote-dilution claims: that the minority as a whole is placed at a disadvantage owing to race, not the happenstance of independent politics. Gingles, 478 U. S., at 51. The second, minority cohesion, is there to show that minority voters will vote together to elect a distinct representative of choice. Ibid. And the first, a large and geographically compact minority population, is the condition for demonstrating that a dilutive plan injures the \xc2\xa72 plaintiffs by failing to draw an available remedial district that would give them a chance to elect their chosen candidate. Growe v. Emison, 507 U. S. 25, 40\xe2\x80\x9341 (1993); Gingles, supra, at 50.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Though this case arose under the Constitution of North Carolina, the dispositive issue is one of federal statutory law: whether a district with a minority population under 50%, but large enough to elect its chosen candidate with the help of majority voters disposed to support the minority favorite, can ever count as a district where minority voters have the opportunity \xe2\x80\x9cto elect representatives of their choice\xe2\x80\x9d for purposes of \xc2\xa72. I think it clear from the nature of a vote-dilution claim and the text of \xc2\xa72 that the answer must be yes. There is nothing in the statutory text to suggest that Congress meant to protect minority opportunity to elect solely by the creation of majority-minority districts. See Voinovich v. Quilter, 507 U. S. 146, 155 (1993) (\xe2\x80\x9c[Section 2] says nothing about majority-minority districts\xe2\x80\x9d). On the contrary, \xc2\xa72 \xe2\x80\x9cfocuses exclusively on the consequences of apportionment,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., as Congress made clear when it explicitly prescribed the ultimate functional approach: a totality of the circumstances test. See 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71973(b) (\xe2\x80\x9cA violation \xe2\x80\xa6 is established if, based on the totality of circumstances, it is shown \xe2\x80\xa6\xe2\x80\x9d). And a functional analysis leaves no doubt that crossover districts vindicate the interest expressly protected by \xc2\xa72: the opportunity to elect a desired representative.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It has been apparent from the moment the Court first took up \xc2\xa72 that no reason exists in the statute to treat a crossover district as a less legitimate remedy for dilution than a majority-minority one (let alone to rule it out). See Gingles, supra, at 90, n.\xc2\xa01 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]f a minority group that is not large enough to constitute a voting majority in a single-member district can show that white support would probably \xe2\x80\xa6 enable the election of the candidates its members prefer, that minority group would appear to have demonstrated that, at least under this measure of its voting strength, it would be able to elect some candidates of its choice\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Pildes, Is Voting-Rights Law Now at War with Itself? Social Science and Voting Rights in the 2000s, 80 N.\xc2\xa0C. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1517, 1553 (2002) (hereinafter Pildes) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhat should be so magical, then, about whether there are enough black voters to become a formal majority so that a conventional \xe2\x80\x98safe\xe2\x80\x99 district can be created? If a safe and a coalition district have the same probability of electing a black candidate, are they not functionally identical, by definition, with respect to electing such candidates?\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As these earlier comments as much as say, whether a district with a minority population under 50% of CVAP may redress a violation of \xc2\xa72 is a question of fact with an obvious answer: of course minority voters constituting less than 50% of the voting population can have an opportunity to elect the candidates of their choice, as amply shown by empirical studies confirming that such minority groups regularly elect their preferred candidates with the help of modest crossover by members of the majority. See, e.g., id., at 1531\xe2\x80\x931534, 1538. The North Carolina Supreme Court for example, determined that voting districts with a black voting age population of as little as 38.37% have an opportunity to elect black candidates, Pender Cty. v. Bartlett, 361 N.\xc2\xa0C. 491, 494\xe2\x80\x93495, 649 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 364, 366\xe2\x80\x93367 (2007), a factual finding that has gone unchallenged and is well supported by electoral results in North Carolina. Of the nine House districts in which blacks make up more than 50% of the voting age population (VAP), all but two elected a black representative in the 2004 election. See App. 109. Of the 12 additional House districts in which blacks are over 39% of the VAP, all but one elected a black representative in the 2004 election. Ibid. It would surely surprise legislators in North Carolina to suggest that black voters in these 12 districts cannot possibly have an opportunity to \xe2\x80\x9celect [the] representatives of their choice.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is of course true that the threshold population sufficient to provide minority voters with an opportunity to elect their candidates of choice is elastic, and the proportions will likely shift in the future, as they have in the past. See Pildes 1527\xe2\x80\x931532 (explaining that blacks in the 1980s required well over 50% of the population in a district to elect the candidates of their choice, but that this number has gradually fallen to well below 50%); id., at 1527, n.\xc2\xa026 (stating that some courts went so far as to refer to 65% \xe2\x80\x9cas a \xe2\x80\x98rule of thumb\xe2\x80\x99 for the black population required to constitute a safe district\xe2\x80\x9d). That is, racial polarization has declined, and if it continues downward the first Gingles condition will get easier to satisfy.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But this is no reason to create an arbitrary threshold; the functional approach will continue to allow dismissal of claims for districts with minority populations too small to demonstrate an ability to elect, and with \xe2\x80\x9ccrossovers\xe2\x80\x9d too numerous to allow an inference of vote dilution in the first place. No one, for example, would argue based on the record of experience in this case that a district with a 25% black population would meet the first Gingles condition. And the third Gingles requirement, majority-bloc voting, may well provide an analytical limit to claims based on crossover districts. See LULAC, 548 U. S., at 490, n. 8 (Souter, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part) (noting the interrelationship of the first and third Gingles factors); see also post, at 1\xe2\x80\x935 (Breyer, J., dissenting) (looking to the third Gingles condition to suggest a mathematical limit to the minority population necessary for a cognizable crossover district). But whatever this limit may be, we have no need to set it here, since the respondent state officials have stipulated to majority-bloc voting, App. to Pet. for Cert. 130a. In sum, \xc2\xa72 addresses voting realities, and for practical purposes a 39%-minority district in which we know minorities have the potential to elect their preferred candidate is every bit as good as a 50%-minority district.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In fact, a crossover district is better. Recognizing crossover districts has the value of giving States greater flexibility to draw districting plans with a fair number of minority-opportunity districts, and this in turn allows for a beneficent reduction in the number of majority-minority districts with their \xe2\x80\x9cquintessentially race-conscious calculus,\xe2\x80\x9d De Grandy, 512 U. S., at 1020, thereby moderating reliance on race as an exclusive determinant in districting decisions, cf. Shaw v. Reno, 509 U. S. 630 (1993). See also Pildes 1547\xe2\x80\x931548 (\xe2\x80\x9cIn contrast to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s concerns with bizarrely designed safe districts, it is hard to see how coalitional districts could \xe2\x80\x98convey the message that political identity is, or should be, predominantly racial.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6 Coalitional districts would seem to encourage and require a kind of integrative, cross-racial political alliance that might be thought consistent with, even the very ideal of, both the VRA and the U. S. Constitution\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Bush v. Vera, 517 U. S. 952, 980 (1996))). A crossover is thus superior to a majority-minority district precisely because it requires polarized factions to break out of the mold and form the coalitions that discourage racial divisions.

\n

III

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The plurality\xe2\x80\x99s contrary conclusion that \xc2\xa72 does not recognize a crossover claim is based on a fundamental misunderstanding of vote-dilution claims, a mistake epitomized in the following assessment of the crossover district in question:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[B]ecause they form only 39 percent of the voting-age population in District 18, African-Americans standing alone have no better or worse opportunity to elect a candidate than does any other group of voters with the same relative voting strength [in District 18].\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\nSee also ante, at 16 (\xe2\x80\x9c[In crossover districts,] minority voters have the same opportunity to elect their candidate as any other political group with the same relative voting strength\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The claim that another political group in a particular district might have the same relative voting strength as the minority if it had the same share of the population takes the form of a tautology: the plurality simply looks to one district and says that a 39% group of blacks is no worse off than a 39% group of whites would be. This statement might be true, or it might not be, and standing alone it demonstrates nothing.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even if the two 39% groups were assumed to be comparable in fact because they will attract sufficient crossover (and so should be credited with satisfying the first Gingles condition), neither of them could prove a \xc2\xa72 violation without looking beyond the 39% district and showing a disproportionately small potential for success in the State\xe2\x80\x99s overall configuration of districts. As this Court has explained before, the ultimate question in a \xc2\xa72 case (that is, whether the minority group in question is being denied an equal opportunity to participate and elect) can be answered only by examining the broader pattern of districts to see whether the minority is being denied a roughly proportionate opportunity. See LULAC, 548 U. S., at 436\xe2\x80\x93437. Hence, saying one group\xe2\x80\x99s 39% equals another\xe2\x80\x99s, even if true in particular districts where facts are known, does not mean that either, both, or neither group could show a \xc2\xa72 violation. The plurality simply fails to grasp that an alleged \xc2\xa72 violation can only be proved or disproved by looking statewide.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The plurality\xe2\x80\x99s more specific justifications for its counterfactual position are no more supportable than its 39% tautology.

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The plurality seems to suggest that our prior cases somehow require its conclusion that a minority population under 50% will never support a \xc2\xa72 remedy, emphasizing that Gingles spoke of a majority and referred to the requirement that minority voters have \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the potential to elect\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d their chosen representatives. Ante, at 10 (quoting, Gingles, 478 U. S., at 50, n.\xc2\xa017). It is hard to know what to make of this point since the plurality also concedes that we have explicitly and repeatedly reserved decision on today\xe2\x80\x99s question. See LULAC, supra, at 443 (plurality opinion); De Grandy, 512 U. S., at 1009; Voinovich, 507 U. S., at 154; Growe, 507 U. S., at 41, n.\xc2\xa05; Gingles, supra, at 46\xe2\x80\x9347, n. 12. In fact, in our more recent cases applying \xc2\xa72, Court majorities have formulated the first Gingles prong in a way more consistent with a functional approach. See LULAC, supra, at 430 (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]n the context of a challenge to the drawing of district lines, \xe2\x80\x98the first Gingles condition requires the possibility of creating more than the existing number of reasonably compact districts with a sufficiently large minority population to elect candidates of its choice\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting De Grandy, supra, at 1008)). These Court majorities get short shrift from today\xe2\x80\x99s plurality.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In any event, even if we ignored Gingles\xe2\x80\x99s reservation of today\xe2\x80\x99s question and looked to Gingles\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cpotential to elect\xe2\x80\x9d as if it were statutory text, I fail to see how that phrase dictates that a minority\xe2\x80\x99s ability to compete must be singlehanded in order to count under \xc2\xa72. As explained already, a crossover district serves the same interest in obtaining representation as a majority-minority district; the potential of 45% with a 6% crossover promises the same result as 51% with no crossover, and there is nothing in the logic of \xc2\xa72 to allow a distinction between the two types of district.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In fact, the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s distinction is artificial on its own terms. In the past, when black voter registration and black voter turnout were relatively low, even black voters with 55% of a district\xe2\x80\x99s CVAP would have had to rely on crossover voters to elect their candidate of choice. See Pildes 1527\xe2\x80\x931528. But no one on this Court (and, so far as I am aware, any other court addressing it) ever suggested that reliance on crossover voting in such a district rendered minority success any less significant under \xc2\xa72, or meant that the district failed to satisfy the first Gingles factor. Nor would it be any answer to say that black voters in such a district, assuming unrealistic voter turnout, theoretically had the \xe2\x80\x9cpotential\xe2\x80\x9d to elect their candidate without crossover support; that would be about as relevant as arguing in the abstract that a black CVAP of 45% is potentially successful, on the assumption that black voters could turn out en masse to elect the candidate of their choice without reliance on crossovers if enough majority voters stay home.

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The plurality is also concerned that recognizing the \xe2\x80\x9cpotential\xe2\x80\x9d of anything under 50% would entail an exponential expansion of special minority districting; the plurality goes so far as to suggest that recognizing crossover districts as possible minority-opportunity districts would inherently \xe2\x80\x9centitl[e] minority groups to the maximum possible voting strength.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11. But this conclusion again reflects a confusion of the gatekeeping function of the Gingles conditions with the ultimate test for relief under \xc2\xa72. See ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310 (\xe2\x80\x9cAfrican-Americans standing alone have no better or worse opportunity to elect a candidate than does any other group of voters with the same relative voting strength\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As already explained, supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x936, the mere fact that all threshold Gingles conditions could be met and a district could be drawn with a minority population sufficiently large to elect the candidate of its choice does not require drawing such a district. This case simply is about the first Gingles condition, not about the number of minority-opportunity districts needed under \xc2\xa72, and accepting Bartlett\xe2\x80\x99s position would in no way imply an obligation to maximize districts with minority voter potential. Under any interpretation of the first Gingles factor, the State must draw districts in a way that provides minority voters with a fair number of districts in which they have an opportunity to elect candidates of their choice; the only question here is which districts will count toward that total.

\n

3

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The plurality\xe2\x80\x99s fear of maximization finds a parallel in the concern that treating crossover districts as minority-opportunity districts would \xe2\x80\x9ccreate serious tension\xe2\x80\x9d with the third Gingles prerequisite of majority-bloc voting. Ante, at 11. The plurality finds \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t \xe2\x80\xa6 difficult to see how the majority-bloc-voting requirement could be met in a district where, by definition, white voters join in sufficient numbers with minority voters to elect the minority\xe2\x80\x99s preferred candidate.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is not difficult to see. If a minority population with 49% of the CVAP can elect the candidate of its choice with crossover by 2% of white voters, the minority \xe2\x80\x9cby definition\xe2\x80\x9d relies on white support to elect its preferred candidate. But this fact alone would raise no doubt, as a matter of definition or otherwise, that the majority-bloc-voting requirement could be met, since as much as 98% of the majority may have voted against the minority\xe2\x80\x99s candidate of choice. As explained above, supra, at 8, the third Gingles condition may well impose an analytical floor to the minority population and a ceiling on the degree of crossover allowed in a crossover district; that is, the concept of majority-bloc voting requires that majority voters tend to stick together in a relatively high degree. The precise standard for determining majority-bloc voting is not at issue in this case, however; to refute the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s 50% rule, one need only recognize that racial cohesion of 98% would be bloc voting by any standard.[Footnote 3]

\n

4

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The plurality argues that qualifying crossover districts as minority-opportunity districts would be less administrable than demanding 50%, forcing courts to engage with the various factual and predictive questions that would come up in determining what percentage of majority voters would provide the voting minority with a chance at electoral success. Ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313. But claims based on a State\xe2\x80\x99s failure to draw majority-minority districts raise the same issues of judicial judgment; even when the 50% threshold is satisfied, a court will still have to engage in factually messy enquiries about the \xe2\x80\x9cpotential\xe2\x80\x9d such a district may afford, the degree of minority cohesion and majority-bloc voting, and the existence of vote-dilution under a totality of the circumstances. See supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x936, 8. The plurality\xe2\x80\x99s rule, therefore, conserves an uncertain amount of judicial resources, and only at the expense of ignoring a class of \xc2\xa72 claims that this Court has no authority to strike from the statute\xe2\x80\x99s coverage.

\n

5

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The plurality again misunderstands the nature of \xc2\xa72 in suggesting that its rule does not conflict with what the Court said in Georgia v. Ashcroft, 539 U. S. 461, 480\xe2\x80\x93482 (2003): that crossover districts count as minority-opportunity districts for the purpose of assessing whether minorities have the opportunity \xe2\x80\x9cto elect their preferred candidates of choice\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa75 of the VRA, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0A. \xc2\xa71973c(b) (Supp. 2008). While the plurality is, of course, correct that there are differences between the enquiries under \xc2\xa72 and \xc2\xa75, ante, at 20, those differences do not save today\xe2\x80\x99s decision from inconsistency with the prior pronouncement. A districting plan violates \xc2\xa75 if it diminishes the ability of minority voters to \xe2\x80\x9celect their preferred candidates of choice,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71973c(b), as measured against the minority\xe2\x80\x99s previous electoral opportunity, Ashcroft, supra, at 477. A districting plan violates \xc2\xa72 if it diminishes the ability of minority voters to \xe2\x80\x9celect representatives of their choice,\xe2\x80\x9d 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71973(b) (2000 ed.), as measured under a totality of the circumstances against a baseline of rough proportionality. It makes no sense to say that a crossover district counts as a minority-opportunity district when comparing the past and the present under \xc2\xa75, but not when comparing the present and the possible under \xc2\xa72.

\n

6

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, the plurality tries to support its insistence on a 50% threshold by invoking the policy of constitutional avoidance, which calls for construing a statute so as to avoid a possibly unconstitutional result. The plurality suggests that allowing a lower threshold would \xe2\x80\x9crequire crossover districts throughout the Nation,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 17, thereby implicating the principle of Shaw v. Reno that districting with an excessive reliance on race is unconstitutional (\xe2\x80\x9cexcessive\xe2\x80\x9d now being equated by the plurality with the frequency of creating opportunity districts). But the plurality has it precisely backwards. A State will inevitably draw some crossover districts as the natural byproduct of districting based on traditional factors. If these crossover districts count as minority-opportunity districts, the State will be much closer to meeting its \xc2\xa72 obligation without any reference to race, and fewer minority-opportunity districts will, therefore, need to be created purposefully. But if, as a matter of law, only majority-minority districts provide a minority seeking equality with the opportunity to elect its preferred candidates, the State will have much further to go to create a sufficient number of minority-opportunity districts, will be required to bridge this gap by creating exclusively majority-minority districts, and will inevitably produce a districting plan that reflects a greater focus on race. The plurality, however, seems to believe that any reference to race in districting poses a constitutional concern, even a State\xe2\x80\x99s decision to reduce racial blocs in favor of crossover districts. A judicial position with these consequences is not constitutional avoidance.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0More serious than the plurality opinion\xe2\x80\x99s inconsistency with prior cases construing \xc2\xa72 is the perversity of the results it portends. Consider the effect of the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s rule on North Carolina\xe2\x80\x99s districting scheme. Black voters make up approximately 20% of North Carolina\xe2\x80\x99s VAP[Footnote 4] and are distributed throughout 120 State House districts, App. to Pet. for Cert. 58a. As noted before, black voters constitute more than 50% of the VAP in 9 of these districts and over 39% of the VAP in an additional 12. Supra, at 7\xe2\x80\x938. Under a functional approach to \xc2\xa72, black voters in North Carolina have an opportunity to elect (and regularly do elect) the representative of their choice in as many as 21 House districts, or 17.5% of North Carolina\xe2\x80\x99s total districts. See App. 109\xe2\x80\x93110. North Carolina\xe2\x80\x99s districting plan is therefore close to providing black voters with proportionate electoral opportunity. According to the plurality, however, the remedy of a crossover district cannot provide opportunity to minority voters who lack it, and the requisite opportunity must therefore be lacking for minority voters already living in districts where they must rely on crossover. By the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s reckoning, then, black voters have an opportunity to elect representatives of their choice in, at most, nine North Carolina House districts. See ibid. In the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s view, North Carolina must have a long way to go before it satisfies the \xc2\xa72 requirement of equal electoral opportunity.[Footnote 5]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A State like North Carolina faced with the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, whether it wants to comply with \xc2\xa72 or simply to avoid litigation, will, therefore, have no reason to create crossover districts. Section 2 recognizes no need for such districts, from which it follows that they can neither be required nor be created to help the State meet its obligation of equal electoral opportunity under \xc2\xa72. And if a legislature were induced to draw a crossover district by the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s encouragement to create them voluntarily, ante, at 20\xe2\x80\x9321, it would open itself to attack by the plurality based on the pointed suggestion that a policy favoring crossover districts runs counter to Shaw. The plurality has thus boiled \xc2\xa72 down to one option: the best way to avoid suit under \xc2\xa72, and the only way to comply with \xc2\xa72, is by drawing district lines in a way that packs minority voters into majority-minority districts, probably eradicating crossover districts in the process.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Perhaps the plurality recognizes this aberrant implication, for it eventually attempts to disavow it. It asserts that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa72 allows States to choose their own method of complying with the Voting Rights Act, and we have said that may include drawing crossover districts. . . . [But] \xc2\xa72 does not mandate creating or preserving crossover districts.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 19. See also, ante, at 20 (crossover districts \xe2\x80\x9ccan be evidence \xe2\x80\xa6 of equal political opportunity \xe2\x80\xa6\xe2\x80\x9d). But this is judicial fiat, not legal reasoning; the plurality does not even attempt to explain how a crossover district can be a minority-opportunity district when assessing the compliance of a districting plan with \xc2\xa72, but cannot be one when sought as a remedy to a \xc2\xa72 violation. The plurality cannot have it both ways. If voluntarily drawing a crossover district brings a State into compliance with \xc2\xa72, then requiring creation of a crossover district must be a way to remedy a violation of \xc2\xa72, and eliminating a crossover district must in some cases take a State out of compliance with the statute. And when the elimination of a crossover district does cause a violation of \xc2\xa72, I cannot fathom why a voter in that district should not be able to bring a claim to remedy it.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In short, to the extent the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s holding is taken to control future results, the plurality has eliminated the protection of \xc2\xa72 for the districts that best vindicate the goals of the statute, and has done all it can to force the States to perpetuate racially concentrated districts, the quintessential manifestations of race consciousness in American politics.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I respectfully dissent.
