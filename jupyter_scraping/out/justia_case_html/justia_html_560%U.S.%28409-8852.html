\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Per Curiam.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner Lawrence Jefferson, who has been sentenced to death, claimed in both state and federal courts that his lawyers were constitutionally inadequate because they failed to investigate a traumatic head injury that he suffered as a child. The state court rejected that claim after making a finding that the attorneys were advised by an expert that such investigation was unnecessary. Under the governing federal statute, that factual finding is presumed correct unless any one of eight exceptions applies. See 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72254(d)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(8) (1994 ed.). But the Court of Appeals considered only one of those exceptions (specifically \xc2\xa72254(d)(8)). And on that basis, it considered itself \xe2\x80\x9cduty-bound\xe2\x80\x9d to accept the state court\xe2\x80\x99s finding, and rejected Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s claim. Because the Court of Appeals did not fully consider several remaining potentially applicable exceptions, we vacate its judgment and remand.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When Jefferson was a child, he \xe2\x80\x9csuffered a serious injury to his head.\xe2\x80\x9d Jefferson v. Terry, 490 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1261, 1326 (ND Ga. 2007); see id., at 1320 (quoting Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s mother\xe2\x80\x99s testimony that \xe2\x80\x9ca car ran over the top of his head\xe2\x80\x9d when he was two years old). The accident left his skull swollen and misshapen and his forehead visibly scarred. Jefferson v. Hall, 570 F.\xc2\xa03d 1283, 1311, 1315, n.\xc2\xa04 (CA11 2009) (Carnes, J., dissenting). During the District Court proceedings below, uncontroverted experts testified that, as a result of his head injury, Jefferson has \xe2\x80\x9cpermanent brain damage\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9ccauses abnormal behavior\xe2\x80\x9d over which he \xe2\x80\x9chas no or substantially limited control.\xe2\x80\x9d 490 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1321\xe2\x80\x931322. According to these experts, Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s condition causes \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98emotional dullness,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98restless or aggressive characteristics,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98impulsiveness,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98temper outbursts,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98markedly diminished impulse control,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98impaired social judgment,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98transient outbursts of rage which are totally inconsistent with his normal behavioral pattern.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1322, 1327.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The experts further testified that Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98severe cognitive disabilities\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98profoundly alter\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d his \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98ability to plan and coordinate his actions, to be aware of the consequences of his behavior, and to engage in premeditated or intentional acts.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1327. But they testified he is neither psychotic nor retarded. Id., at 1319. Thus, they said, to a lay observer or even to a professional psychologist, Jefferson does not outwardly appear mentally impaired. Indeed, according to the experts, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the behavior that may result from\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d his condition \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98could, without the administration of proper testing, be mistaken for volitional.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1322.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Jefferson faced a death sentence for killing his co-worker while the two men were fishing. Id., at 1271\xe2\x80\x931272. Prior to trial, he was examined by a psychologist named Dr. Gary Dudley, who prepared a formal report in which he concluded that Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s mental deficiencies do not impair \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98his judgment or decision-making capacity.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 570 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1294 (quoting report). But Dr. Dudley\xe2\x80\x99s report included a caveat: \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98One possibility that could not be explored because of [Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s] incarceration has to do with the sequelae,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., pathologies, related to a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98head injury experienced during childhood.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98In my opinion,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d he wrote, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98it would be worthwhile to conduct neuropsychological evaluation of this individual to rule out an organic etiology,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., to rule out brain damage. Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although \xe2\x80\x9cit is undisputed that the testing\xe2\x80\x9d Dr. Dudley recommended \xe2\x80\x9ccould have easily been performed,\xe2\x80\x9d 490 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1322, and that Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s attorneys possessed police reports and hospital records recounting his head injury, id., at 1323, the attorneys did not have Jefferson tested. At sentencing, they presented only testimony from two prison guards, who stated that Jefferson was an unproblematic inmate, and from three members of Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s family, who testified that he is a \xe2\x80\x9cresponsible, generous, gentle, and kind\xe2\x80\x9d person and \xe2\x80\x9ca good father.\xe2\x80\x9d 570 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1290\xe2\x80\x931291. And while Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s mother briefly mentioned the car accident, \xe2\x80\x9cshe was not questioned and did not offer any testimony regarding the impact, if any, that the accident had on him.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1291. Thus, \xe2\x80\x9c[a]s far as the jury knew, Jefferson did not suffer from brain damage or neurological impairment; he had no organic disorders\xe2\x80\x9d; and \xe2\x80\x9chis emotional stability, impulse control, and judgment were perfectly normal.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1311 (Carnes, J., dissenting).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Jefferson sought habeas relief in state court, arguing that his two trial attorneys unreasonably failed to pursue brain-damage testing. In response, the trial attorneys testified that they did not pursue such testing because, after delivering his formal written report, Dr. Dudley later told them that further investigation \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98may be a waste of time because the rest of [his] report\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d had \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98said that [Jefferson] was non psychotic.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 570 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1295 (quoting testimony). Dr. Dudley did not testify in person at the hearing, but he submitted a sworn affidavit denying that he had ever made such statements. He said \xe2\x80\x9cit had always been his expert opinion \xe2\x80\x98that neuropsychological testing was necessary\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and that when he wrote as much in his formal report \xe2\x80\x9che \xe2\x80\x98meant it.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1312 (Carnes, J., dissenting) (quoting affidavit). He added, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98I never, before or after that report, suggested to [Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s attorneys] that such an evaluation was not necessary or that it would not be worthwhile.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; cf. Pet. for Cert. 17, n.\xc2\xa012.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Jefferson contends, and the State has not disputed, that after the hearing concluded the state-court judge contacted the attorneys for the State ex parte. And in a private conversation that included neither Jefferson nor his attorneys, the judge asked the State\xe2\x80\x99s attorneys to draft the opinion of the court. See id., at 3, 12. According to Jefferson, no such request was made of him, nor was he informed of the request made to opposing counsel. Id., at 12, n.\xc2\xa08, 13; see also Jefferson v. Zant, 263 Ga. 316, 431 S.\xc2\xa0E.\xc2\xa02d 110, 111 (1993) (\xe2\x80\x9cJefferson contends [the order] amounts to no more and no less than a reply brief to which [he] has not had a chance to respond\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The attorneys for the State prepared an opinion finding that \xe2\x80\x9cDr. Dudley led [Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s trial attorneys] to believe that further investigation would simply be a waste of time because Petitioner [i]s not psychotic.\xe2\x80\x9d Jefferson v. Zant, Civ. Action No. 87\xe2\x80\x93V\xe2\x80\x931241 (Super. Ct. Butts Cty., Ga., Oct. 7, 1992), p.\xc2\xa016, App. 4 to Pet. for Cert. 16 (hereinafter State Order); see also id., at 37. The opinion \xe2\x80\x9cspecifically credits the testimony of [the trial attorneys] with regard to their efforts to investigate Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s mental condition.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 18; see also id., at 36. And relying on these findings, it concludes that Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s attorneys \xe2\x80\x9cmade a reasonable investigation into [his] mental health\xe2\x80\x9d and were thus not ineffective. Id., at 37.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Notably, as the Georgia Supreme Court acknowledged, the State\xe2\x80\x99s opinion discusses statements purportedly made on Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s behalf by a witness \xe2\x80\x9cwho did not testify\xe2\x80\x9d or participate in the proceedings. 263 Ga., at 318, 431 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 112; see State Order 24\xe2\x80\x9325. Nonetheless, the opinion \xe2\x80\x9cwas adopted verbatim by the [state] court.\xe2\x80\x9d 263 Ga., at 316, 431 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 111. And while the State Supreme Court recognized that we have \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98criticized\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d such a practice, it affirmed the judgment. Id., at 317, 320, 431 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 112, 114 (quoting Anderson v. Bessemer City, 470 U. S. 564, 572 (1985)).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Jefferson next sought federal habeas relief in the District Court. In his opening brief, he argued that \xe2\x80\x9cthere is no reason under principles of comity or otherwise to give any deference to the findings of the State Habeas Corpus Court.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner in No. 1:96\xe2\x80\x93CV\xe2\x80\x93989\xe2\x80\x93CC (ND Ga.), Doc. 105, p.\xc2\xa04, and n.\xc2\xa01 (hereinafter District Court Brief). In support of that argument, he claimed that the state court \xe2\x80\x9cmerely signed an order drafted by the State without revision of a single word,\xe2\x80\x9d even though the order \xe2\x80\x9cdescribed witnesses who never testified.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. And he said that such a process \xe2\x80\x9crais[es] serious doubts as to whether [the judge] even read, much less carefully considered, the proposed order submitted by the State.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court ruled in Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s favor. It noted that under the relevant statute \xe2\x80\x9cfactual findings of state courts are presumed to be correct unless one of \xe2\x80\xa6 eight enumerated exceptions \xe2\x80\xa6 applies.\xe2\x80\x9d 490 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1280; see also id., at 1280, n.\xc2\xa05 (listing the exceptions). And it acknowledged \xe2\x80\x9cthe state habeas corpus court\xe2\x80\x99s failure to explain the basis\xe2\x80\x9d for its credibility findings. Id., at 1324, n.\xc2\xa017. But it accepted Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s claim of ineffective assistance of counsel without disturbing the state court\xe2\x80\x99s factual findings because it believed he should prevail even accepting those findings as true. Id., at 1324\xe2\x80\x931325.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On appeal, Jefferson defended the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment primarily on its own terms. But he also argued that the state court\xe2\x80\x99s factfinding was \xe2\x80\x9cdubious at best\xe2\x80\x9d in light of the process that court employed, and that the Court of Appeals therefore \xe2\x80\x9cshould harbor serious doubts about the findings of fact and credibility determinations in the state court record.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner/Appellee in No. 07\xe2\x80\x9312502 (CA11), pp. 31\xe2\x80\x9332, n.\xc2\xa010 (hereinafter Appeals Brief).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A divided Court of Appeals panel reversed, and Jefferson filed this petition for certiorari asking us to review his claim of ineffective assistance of counsel. And, in so doing, he challenges\xe2\x80\x94as he did in the State Supreme Court, the District Court, and the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cthe fact findings of the state court,\xe2\x80\x9d given what he describes as the deficient procedure employed by that court while reviewing his claim. Pet. for Cert. 11\xe2\x80\x9313, 17, n.\xc2\xa012, 18, n.\xc2\xa013 (recounting \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98reason[s] to doubt\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the state court\xe2\x80\x99s findings). Cf. Lebron v. National Railroad Passenger Corporation, 513 U. S. 374, 379 (1995) (stating standard for preserving an issue for review in this Court).

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This habeas application was filed prior to the enactment of the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 and is therefore governed by federal habeas law as it existed prior to that point. Lindh v. Murphy, 521 U. S. 320, 326\xe2\x80\x93336 (1997). In 1963, we set forth the \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate standard\xe2\x80\x9d to be applied by a \xe2\x80\x9cfederal court in habeas corpus\xe2\x80\x9d when \xe2\x80\x9cthe facts\xe2\x80\x9d pertinent to a habeas application \xe2\x80\x9care in dispute.\xe2\x80\x9d Townsend v. Sain, 372 U. S. 293, 312. We held that when \xe2\x80\x9cthe habeas applicant was afforded a full and fair hearing by the state court resulting in reliable findings\xe2\x80\x9d the district court \xe2\x80\x9cordinarily should \xe2\x80\xa6 accept the facts as found\xe2\x80\x9d by the state-court judge. Id., at 318. However, \xe2\x80\x9cif the habeas applicant did not receive a full and fair evidentiary hearing in a state court, either at the time of the trial or in a collateral proceeding,\xe2\x80\x9d we held that the federal court \xe2\x80\x9cmust hold an evidentiary hearing\xe2\x80\x9d to resolve any facts that \xe2\x80\x9care in dispute.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 312. We further \xe2\x80\x9cexplain[ed] the controlling criteria\xe2\x80\x9d by enumerating six circumstances in which such an evidentiary hearing would be required:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(1) the merits of the factual dispute were not resolved in the state hearing; (2) the state factual determination is not fairly supported by the record as a whole; (3) the fact-finding procedure employed by the state court was not adequate to afford a full and fair hearing; (4) there is a substantial allegation of newly discovered evidence; (5) the material facts were not adequately developed at the state-court hearing; or (6) for any reason it appears that the state trier of fact did not afford the habeas applicant a full and fair fact hearing.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 313 (emphasis added).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Three years later, in 1966, Congress enacted an amendment to the federal habeas statute that \xe2\x80\x9cwas an almost verbatim codification of the standards delineated in Townsend v. Sain.\xe2\x80\x9d Miller v. Fenton, 474 U. S. 104, 111 (1985). That codification read in relevant part as follows:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cIn any proceeding instituted in a Federal court by an application for a writ of habeas corpus by a person in custody pursuant to the judgment of a State court, a determination \xe2\x80\xa6 of a factual issue, made by a State court of competent jurisdiction \xe2\x80\xa6 , shall be presumed to be correct, unless the applicant shall establish or it shall otherwise appear, or the respondent shall admit\xe2\x80\x94

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(1) that the merits of the factual dispute were not resolved in the State court hearing;

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(2) that the factfinding procedure employed by the State court was not adequate to afford a full and fair hearing;

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(3) that the material facts were not adequately developed at the State court hearing;

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(4) that the State court lacked jurisdiction of the subject matter or over the person of the applicant in the State court proceeding;

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(5) that the applicant was an indigent and the State court, in deprivation of his constitutional right, failed to appoint counsel to represent him in the State court proceeding;

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(6) that the applicant did not receive a full, fair, and adequate hearing in the State court proceeding; or

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(7) that the applicant was otherwise denied due process of law in the State court proceeding;

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(8) or unless \xe2\x80\xa6 the Federal court on a consideration of [the relevant] part of the record as a whole concludes that such factual determination is not fairly supported by the record.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72254(d) (emphasis added).

\n

\nAs is clear from the statutory text quoted above, and as the District Court correctly stated, if any \xe2\x80\x9cone of the eight enumerated exceptions \xe2\x80\xa6 applies\xe2\x80\x9d then \xe2\x80\x9cthe state court\xe2\x80\x99s factfinding is not presumed correct.\xe2\x80\x9d 490 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1280; accord, Miller, supra, at 105 (\xe2\x80\x9cUnder 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d), state-court findings of fact \xe2\x80\x98shall be presumed to be correct\xe2\x80\x99 in a federal habeas corpus proceeding unless one of eight enumerated exceptions applies\xe2\x80\x9d); see also 1 R. Hertz & J. Liebman, Federal Habeas Corpus Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa720.2c, pp. 915\xe2\x80\x93918 (5th ed. 2005).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Jefferson has consistently argued that the federal courts \xe2\x80\x9cshould harbor serious doubts about\xe2\x80\x9d and should not \xe2\x80\x9cgive any deference to\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9cfindings of fact and credibility determinations\xe2\x80\x9d made by the state habeas court because those findings were drafted exclusively by the attorneys for the State pursuant to an ex parte request from the state-court judge, who made no such request of Jefferson, failed to notify Jefferson of the request made to opposing counsel, and adopted the State\xe2\x80\x99s proposed opinion verbatim even though it recounted evidence from a nonexistent witness. See, e.g., Appeals Brief 32, n.\xc2\xa010; District Court Brief 4, n.\xc2\xa01; Pet. for Cert. 12. These are arguments that the state court\xe2\x80\x99s process was deficient. In other words, they are arguments that Jefferson \xe2\x80\x9cdid not receive a full and fair evidentiary hearing in \xe2\x80\xa6 state court.\xe2\x80\x9d Townsend, supra, at 312. Or, to use the statutory language, they are arguments that the state court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cfactfinding procedure,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9chearing,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cproceeding\xe2\x80\x9d were not \xe2\x80\x9cfull, fair, and adequate.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72254(d)(2), (6), (7).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But the Court of Appeals did not consider the state court\xe2\x80\x99s process when it applied the statutory presumption of correctness. Instead, it invoked Circuit precedent that applied only paragraph (8) of \xc2\xa72254(d), which, codifying the second Townsend exception, 372 U. S., at 313, lifts the presumption of correctness for findings that are \xe2\x80\x9cnot fairly supported by the record.\xe2\x80\x9d See 570 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1300 (quoting Jackson v. Herring, 42 F.\xc2\xa03d 1350, 1366 (CA11 1995), in turn quoting 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(8)). And even though the Court of Appeals \xe2\x80\x9crecognize[d]\xe2\x80\x9d that Jefferson had argued that the state court\xe2\x80\x99s process had produced factual findings that were \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98dubious at best,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and that federal courts should therefore \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98harbor serious doubts about\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the state court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98findings of fact and credibility,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the Court of Appeals nonetheless held that the state court\xe2\x80\x99s findings are \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98entitled to a presumption of correctness\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d that it was \xe2\x80\x9cduty-bound\xe2\x80\x9d to apply. 570 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1304, n.\xc2\xa08 (quoting Appeals Brief 32, n.\xc2\xa010). The Court of Appeals explicitly stated that it considered itself \xe2\x80\x9cduty-bound\xe2\x80\x9d to defer to the state court\xe2\x80\x99s findings because \xe2\x80\x9cJefferson has not argued that any of the state courts\xe2\x80\x99 factual findings were \xe2\x80\x98not fairly supported by the record,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d a direct reference to \xc2\xa72254(d)(8) and to the second Townsend exception. 570 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1304, n.\xc2\xa08 (emphasis added). And it then concluded: \xe2\x80\x9cBased on these factual findings of the state habeas courts\xe2\x80\x94all of which are fairly supported by the record\xe2\x80\x94we believe that Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s counsel were reasonable in deciding not to pursue neuropsychological testing.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1304 (emphasis added).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In our view, the Court of Appeals did not properly consider the legal status of the state court\xe2\x80\x99s factual findings. Under Townsend, as codified by the governing statute, a federal court is not \xe2\x80\x9cduty-bound\xe2\x80\x9d to accept any and all state-court findings that are \xe2\x80\x9cfairly supported by the record.\xe2\x80\x9d Those words come from \xc2\xa72254(d)(8), which is only one of eight enumerated exceptions to the presumption of correctness. But there are seven others, see \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72254(d)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(7), none of which the Court of Appeals considered when addressing Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s claim. To be sure, we have previously stated in cases applying \xc2\xa72254(d)(8) that \xe2\x80\x9ca federal court\xe2\x80\x9d may not overturn a state court\xe2\x80\x99s factual conclusion \xe2\x80\x9cunless the conclusion is not \xe2\x80\x98fairly supported by the record.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Parker v. Dugger, 498 U. S. 308, 320 (1991) (granting federal habeas relief after rejecting state court\xe2\x80\x99s finding under \xc2\xa72254(d)(8)); see also Demosthenes v. Baal, 495 U. S. 731 (1990) (per curiam) (applying \xc2\xa72254(d)(8)); cf. post, at 10 (Scalia, J., dissenting). But in those cases there was no suggestion that any other provisions enumerated in \xc2\xa72254(d) were at issue. That is not the case here. In treating \xc2\xa72254(d)(8) as the exclusive statutory exception, and by failing to address Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the state court\xe2\x80\x99s procedures deprived its findings of deference, the Court of Appeals applied the statute and our precedents incorrectly.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although we have stated that a court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cverbatim adoption of findings of fact prepared by prevailing parties\xe2\x80\x9d should be treated as findings of the court, we have also criticized that practice. Anderson, 470 U. S., at 572. And we have not considered the lawfulness of, nor the application of the habeas statute to, the use of such a practice where (1) a judge solicits the proposed findings ex parte, (2) does not provide the opposing party an opportunity to criticize the findings or to submit his own, or (3) adopts findings that contain internal evidence suggesting that the judge may not have read them. Cf. id., at 568; Ga. Code of Judicial Conduct, Canon 3(A)(4) (1993) (prohibiting ex parte judicial communications).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We decline to determine in the first instance whether any of the exceptions enumerated in \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72254(d)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(8) apply in this case, see, e.g., Cutter v. Wilkinson, 544 U. S. 709, 718, n.\xc2\xa07 (2005), especially given that the facts surrounding the state habeas court\xe2\x80\x99s process are undeveloped. Respondent has conceded that it drafted the state court\xe2\x80\x99s final order at that court\xe2\x80\x99s request and that the order was adopted verbatim, 263 Ga., at 317, 431 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 111, and has not disputed in this Court that the state court solicited the order \xe2\x80\x9cex parte and without prior notice\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cdid not seek a proposed order from Petitioner,\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Cert. 12, and n.\xc2\xa08. But the precise nature of what transpired during the state-court proceedings is not fully known. See 263 Ga. at 316\xe2\x80\x93317, 431 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 111 (noting dispute as to whether Jefferson \xe2\x80\x9chad a chance to respond\xe2\x80\x9d to the final order); see also Pet. for Cert. 13.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, we believe it necessary for the lower courts to determine on remand whether the state court\xe2\x80\x99s factual findings warrant a presumption of correctness, and to conduct any further proceedings as may be appropriate in light of their resolution of that issue. See Townsend, supra, at 313\xe2\x80\x93319; Keeney v. Tamayo-Reyes, 504 U. S. 1 (1992). In so holding, we express no opinion as to whether Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s Sixth Amendment rights were violated assuming the state court\xe2\x80\x99s factual findings to be true.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The petition for a writ of certiorari and motion to proceed in forma pauperis are granted. The judgment of the Court of Appeals is vacated, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

LAWRENCE JOSEPH JEFFERSON v. STEPHEN\nUPTON, WARDEN

on petition for writ of certiorari to the united states\ncourt of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia, with whom Justice Thomas joins, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The question presented by Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s petition for writ of certiorari is whether his trial attorneys rendered ineffective assistance of counsel when they declined to pursue further investigation of Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s childhood head injury. In my view the Court should either answer that question or (as I would prefer) deny the petition. Instead, it summarily vacates the judgment of the Court of Appeals on an altogether different ground that was neither raised nor passed upon below and that is not fairly included within the sole question presented. To make matters worse, the Court conjures up an \xe2\x80\x9cerror\xe2\x80\x9d with respect to that ground by misquoting and mischaracterizing the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 opinion, ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310, and by overlooking relevant authority from this Court. I respectfully dissent.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The prior version of 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d) (1994 ed.) applicable in this case provided that in federal habeas proceedings the factual determinations of a state court \xe2\x80\x9cshall be presumed to be correct,\xe2\x80\x9d unless the applicant proves, the respondent admits, or a federal court determines that one of eight exceptions set forth in \xc2\xa72254(d)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(8) applies. The Court concludes that the Eleventh Circuit misapplied that provision and our precedents by treating one of those exceptions, \xc2\xa72254(d)(8), \xe2\x80\x9cas the exclusive statutory exception\xe2\x80\x9d to the presumption of correctness, and by failing to address whether \xc2\xa72254(d)(2), (6), or (7) might also bar application of that presumption.[Footnote 1] Ante, at 10.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, however, is the first anyone (including Jefferson) has heard of this argument. Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s briefs below contain no discussion or even citation of subsection (d)\xe2\x80\x94let alone of paragraphs (2), (6), or (7)\xe2\x80\x94and the courts below understandably never passed upon the application of those provisions. Under our longstanding practice, that should be the end of the matter. See, e.g., Pennsylvania Dept. of Corrections v. Yeskey, 524 U. S. 206, 212\xe2\x80\x93213 (1998).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But the Court insists, ante, at 5\xe2\x80\x936, 8\xe2\x80\x939, that if we squint at them long enough we can see in Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s briefs below a challenge to the state court\xe2\x80\x99s fact-finding process cognizable under \xc2\xa72254(d)(2), (6), and (7). But the handful of isolated, vague statements it musters (buried in hundreds of pages of briefs) show no such thing. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s only evidence that Jefferson presented the point to the District Court, ante, at 5, 8, consists of a single sentence of text (and an accompanying two-sentence footnote) in the \xe2\x80\x9cPrior Proceedings\xe2\x80\x9d section of his 180-page brief. Final Evidentiary Brief and Proposed Findings of Fact and Conclusions of Law, Record in No. 1:96\xe2\x80\x93CV\xe2\x80\x93989\xe2\x80\x93CC (ND Ga.), Doc. 105 (hereinafter District Court Brief). The sentence is: \xe2\x80\x9cIn entering the State Habeas Corpus Order Judge Newton merely signed an order drafted by the State without revision of a single word.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 4. The footnote adds:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he order signed by Judge Newton described witnesses who never testified, raising serious doubt as to whether he even read, much less carefully considered, the proposed order submitted by the State. In this circumstance, there is no reason under principles of comity or otherwise to give any deference to the findings of the State Habeas Court, because there was apparently no serious consideration or deliberation of the factual and legal issues raised.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid., n.\xc2\xa01.

\n

\nThis passing suggestion that deference would be unwarranted is, to put it mildly, an elliptical way to argue that the state fact-finding procedure was inadequate, \xc2\xa72254(d)(2), that Jefferson was denied a full, fair, and adequate hearing, \xc2\xa72254(d)(6), or that Jefferson was denied due process of law, \xc2\xa72254(d)(7). And it only appeared, I emphasize again, in the \xe2\x80\x9cPrior Proceedings\xe2\x80\x9d section of the brief. The argument section of Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s District Court Brief, consisting of 164 pages and containing separate assignments of error from III to XLIV (44), makes no mention of the ground upon which the Court today relies. And the assignment of error that is the basis for the question presented in Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s petition, VI, id., at 47\xe2\x80\x9380, did not dispute the state courts\xe2\x80\x99 factual findings under \xc2\xa72254(d), but only challenged the state courts\xe2\x80\x99 legal conclusion that his attorneys\xe2\x80\x99 failure to conduct a fuller investigation into the head injury he suffered as a child was not deficient performance under Strickland v. Washington, 466 U. S. 668 (1984).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Jefferson also did not raise the point in the Eleventh Circuit. His brief to that court acknowledged that the state courts\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9c[f]indings of fact and credibility determinations are reviewed for clear error.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner/Appellee, No. 07\xe2\x80\x9312502, pp. 16\xe2\x80\x9317 (hereinafter Appeals Brief). It declared that \xe2\x80\x9cThe District Court Correctly Deferred to the Fact Findings of the State Court\xe2\x80\x9d in adjudicating his ineffective-assistance-of-counsel claim. Id., at 21 (capitalization and boldface type deleted); see also id., at 29, and n.\xc2\xa07, 31. And it conceded that with respect to the ineffective-assistance claim, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he relevant facts are not in dispute.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 24. Jefferson did characterize the state habeas court\xe2\x80\x99s factual findings as generally \xe2\x80\x9cdubious\xe2\x80\x9d and suggested there were \xe2\x80\x9cserious doubts\xe2\x80\x9d about them, id., at 32, n.\xc2\xa010. But not once did he argue that the dubiousness of the findings was the consequence of a failure to meet the requirements of \xc2\xa72254(d)(2), (6), or (7)\xe2\x80\x94or even more generally that the findings should not be presumed correct under \xc2\xa72254(d). Instead, he pressed the same argument he made in the District Court: Even if the state courts\xe2\x80\x99 factual findings were correct, his trial attorneys rendered ineffective assistance in deciding to forgo further investigation of his childhood head injury. Id., at 31\xe2\x80\x9333, 50\xe2\x80\x9351.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor did the courts below pass upon the argument the Court now addresses. The District Court did not dispute the state courts\xe2\x80\x99 factual findings. Jefferson v. Terry, 490 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1261, 1319\xe2\x80\x931320 (ND Ga. 2007). It accepted those findings as true, including the state habeas court\xe2\x80\x99s credibility findings, id., at 1323\xe2\x80\x931324, and n.\xc2\xa017, but held \xe2\x80\x9cas a matter of law\xe2\x80\x9d that it was objectively unreasonable for Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s attorneys \xe2\x80\x9cnot to investigate\xe2\x80\x9d further into the effect, if any, of the accident on Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s mental capacity and health, id., at 1324. Concluding that Jefferson was thereby prejudiced, the court ordered a new sentencing hearing. Id., at 1328.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals disagreed with that determination and reversed, holding that his trial attorneys\xe2\x80\x99 performance was not objectively unreasonable under Strickland. Jefferson v. Hall, 570 F.\xc2\xa03d 1283, 1301\xe2\x80\x931309 (CA11 2009). That court correctly stated the applicable framework under \xc2\xa72254(d):

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cPre-AEDPA, questions of law and mixed questions of law and fact resolved by state habeas courts are reviewed de novo, while the state courts\xe2\x80\x99 factual findings are \xe2\x80\x98subject to the presumption of correctness.\xe2\x80\x99 Freund v. Butterworth, 165 F.\xc2\xa03d 839, 861 (11th Cir. 1999). Although these findings may be disregarded if, for example, they are not \xe2\x80\x98fairly supported by the record,\xe2\x80\x99 Jackson v. Herring, 42 F.\xc2\xa03d 1350, 1366 (11th Cir. 1995) (quoting 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(8)), this Court has construed the \xe2\x80\x98presumption of correctness\xe2\x80\x99 standard to be the same as the \xe2\x80\x98clear error\xe2\x80\x99 standard of review.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1300 (emphasis added; footnote omitted).

\n

\nConfronted with no argument that \xc2\xa72254(d)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(7) applied or that it must disregard the state courts\xe2\x80\x99 factual findings, the Court of Appeals understandably did not pass upon those questions.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals did consider the record on its own, as required by \xc2\xa72254(d)(8), to determine whether the state courts\xe2\x80\x99 factual determinations were fairly supported by the record. Id., at 1303\xe2\x80\x931304, and n. 8. In doing so, the court \xe2\x80\x9cspecifically note[d] that neither Jefferson nor the district court questioned the state court\xe2\x80\x99s factual finding that [the defense\xe2\x80\x99s psychiatric expert] led [one of Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s attorney\xe2\x80\x99s] to believe that further investigation would simply be a waste of time, . . . despite [his attorney\xe2\x80\x99s] testimony that [the expert] told him it \xe2\x80\x98may\xe2\x80\x99 be a waste of time.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1303, n.\xc2\xa08. It added that Jefferson did not \xe2\x80\x9cpoint to any particular finding that was clearly erroneous,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 1304, n. 8\xe2\x80\x94applying the same standard Jefferson had proposed in his brief, see supra, at 3. Even the dissent agreed that the court was \xe2\x80\x9cobliged to accept\xe2\x80\x9d the state courts\xe2\x80\x99 credibility determination, despite the \xe2\x80\x9creasons to doubt it.\xe2\x80\x9d 570 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1312 (opinion of Carnes, J.). The dissent did not cite \xc2\xa72254(d)(2), (6), or (7), but instead focused on the same question of constitutional law that occupied Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s briefs, the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, and the majority\xe2\x80\x99s opinion: whether, accepting the factual findings and credibility determination of the state courts as true, Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s attorneys rendered ineffective assistance of counsel. That is only the question that occupied the courts and the parties below.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is bad enough that the Court decides an issue not raised or resolved in the lower courts. It is much worse that it decides an issue Jefferson has not even asked us to address. Under this Court\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 14.1(a), \xe2\x80\x9c[o]nly the questions set forth in the petition, or fairly included therein, will be considered by the Court.\xe2\x80\x9d We apply that rule in all but \xe2\x80\x9cthe most exceptional cases, where reasons of urgency or of economy suggest the need to address the unpresented question under consideration.\xe2\x80\x9d Yee v. Escondido, 503 U. S. 519, 535 (1992) (citation and internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s petition for writ of certiorari presents a single question:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hether the majority opinion, in affording trial counsel\xe2\x80\x99s decision to limit the scope of investigation in a death penalty case \xe2\x80\x98higher-than-strong presumption of reasonableness\xe2\x80\x99 [sic] conflicts with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent as announced in Williams v. Taylor, 529 U. S. 362 (2000), Wiggins v. Smith, 539 U. S. 510 (2003), Rompilla v. Beard, 545 U. S. 374 (2005), and Porter v. McCollum, 130 S.\xc2\xa0Ct. 447 (2009).\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Cert. i.

\n

\nThis is a straightforward request for error correction on a constitutional claim in light of those four decisions, and neither the request nor those cases have anything to do with the pre-AEDPA version of \xc2\xa72254(d). Nor does that question necessarily encompass whether the Court of Appeals misapplied that version of \xc2\xa72254(d) in determining the deference due to the state courts\xe2\x80\x99 factual findings. The statutory question may be \xe2\x80\x9crelated to,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cperhaps complementary to the one petitioner presented,\xe2\x80\x9d but it is not \xe2\x80\x9cfairly included therein.\xe2\x80\x9d Yee, supra, at 537 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As for the body of Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s petition: Far from invoking \xc2\xa72254(d)\xe2\x80\x99s exceptions to the presumption of correctness to support the Sixth Amendment claim, the petition does not even mention subsection (d), let alone paragraphs (2), (6), or (7). There is no argument, anywhere in the section entitled \xe2\x80\x9cReasons for Granting the Writ,\xe2\x80\x9d that the state courts\xe2\x80\x99 factual findings are not entitled to a presumption of correctness.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court claims, ante, at 6, that Jefferson sufficiently presented the statutory issue by his characterizations of the state courts\xe2\x80\x99 factual findings in the \xe2\x80\x9cStatement of the Case\xe2\x80\x9d section of his petition, see Pet. for Cert. 11\xe2\x80\x9313, 17, n.\xc2\xa012, 18, n.\xc2\xa013. Even if that were so, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the fact that [petitioner] discussed this issue in the text of [his] petition for certiorari does not bring it before us. Rule 14.1(a) requires that a subsidiary question be fairly included in the question presented for our review.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Wood v. Allen, 558 U. S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 13) (quoting Izumi Seimitsu Kogyo Kabushiki Kaisha v. U. S. Philips Corp., 510 U. S. 27, 31, n.\xc2\xa05 (1993) (per curiam)). But in any event, the cited passages do not remotely present the statutory issue. They contain no argument that \xc2\xa72254(d)\xe2\x80\x99s presumption is inapplicable because of \xc2\xa72254(d)(2), (6), or (7), but merely describe the proceedings below, see Pet. for Cert. 11\xe2\x80\x9313, and assert that there might be reasons to doubt the state-court findings (but for the \xc2\xa72254(d) presumption), see id., at 17, n.\xc2\xa012, 18, n. 13.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cThe premise of our adversarial system is that appellate courts do not sit as self-directed boards of legal inquiry and research, but essentially as arbiters of legal questions presented and argued by the parties before them.\xe2\x80\x9d Carducci v. Regan, 714 F.\xc2\xa02d 171, 177 (CADC 1983) (opinion of Scalia, J.). Our refusal to abide by standard rules of appellate practice is unfair to the Eleventh Circuit, whose judgment the Court vacates, and especially to the respondent here, who suffers a loss in this Court without ever having an opportunity to address the merits of the statutory question the Court decides.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s approach would be objectionable even if it were correct that the Court of Appeals went astray. But it is not. The Court of Appeals did not treat \xc2\xa72254(d)(8) as \xe2\x80\x9cthe exclusive statutory exception\xe2\x80\x9d to the presumption of correctness. Ante, at 10. It is true that the majority\xe2\x80\x99s opinion\xe2\x80\x94as well as the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s\xe2\x80\x94discussed only \xc2\xa72254(d)(8). But that is because only \xc2\xa72254(d)(8), and not \xc2\xa72254(d)(2), (6), or (7), was ever brought to the court\xe2\x80\x99s attention. On the fair reading we owe the Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, there simply was no error in its application of \xc2\xa72254(d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court asserts, however, that the Eleventh Circuit ignored the other seven paragraphs in \xc2\xa72254(d) when it \xe2\x80\x9cinvoked Circuit precedent that applied only paragraph (8) of \xc2\xa72254(d).\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9. It did nothing of the sort. The Court of Appeals said that a state court\xe2\x80\x99s factual findings \xe2\x80\x9cmay be disregarded if, for example, they are not \xe2\x80\x98fairly supported by the record,\xe2\x80\x99 Jackson v. Herring, 42 F.\xc2\xa03d 1350, 1366 (11th Cir. 1995) (quoting 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(8)).\xe2\x80\x9d 570 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1300 (emphasis added). The Court of Appeals thus expressly acknowledged that \xc2\xa72254(d)(8) was but one example of the grounds for disregarding a state court\xe2\x80\x99s factual findings. And the Circuit precedent it cited, Jackson v. Herring, similarly did not imply, much less hold, that \xc2\xa72254(d)(8) provided the only grounds for setting aside a state court\xe2\x80\x99s factual findings under \xc2\xa72254(d). See 42 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1366.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Next, the Court states:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cAnd even though the Court of Appeals \xe2\x80\x98recognize[d]\xe2\x80\x99 that Jefferson had argued that the state court\xe2\x80\x99s process had produced factual findings that were \xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cdubious at best,\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99 and that federal courts should therefore \xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9charbor serious doubts about\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99 the state court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cfindings of fact and credibility,\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99 the Court of Appeals nonetheless held that the state court\xe2\x80\x99s findings are \xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9centitled to a presumption of correctness\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99 that it was \xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cduty-bound\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99 to apply. 570 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1304, n.\xc2\xa08 (quoting Appeals Brief 32, n.\xc2\xa010).\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9.

\n

\nAgain, the Court has plucked isolated language from here and there in the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 opinion, to produce a reading which suggests that the Court of Appeals agreed with, or at least did not contest, Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s claim of \xe2\x80\x9cserious doubts.\xe2\x80\x9d That is not so. In the first paragraph of footnote eight of its opinion, the panel reasoned that it was \xe2\x80\x9cduty-bound to accept\xe2\x80\x9d the state courts\xe2\x80\x99 factual findings because it concluded they \xe2\x80\x9care clear, unambiguous, and fairly supported by the record.\xe2\x80\x9d 570 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1303\xe2\x80\x931304, n.\xc2\xa08. \n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Generally speaking, we have no power to set aside the duly entered judgment of a lower federal court unless we find it to have been in error. More specifically, except where there has been an intervening legal development (such as a subsequently announced opinion of ours) that might alter the judgment below, we cannot grant a petition for certiorari, vacate the judgment below, and remand the case (GVR) simply to obtain a re-do. Webster v. Cooper, 558 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (Scalia, J., dissenting) (slip op., at 3). Yet today the Court vacates the judgment of the Eleventh Circuit on the basis of an error that court did not commit, with respect to a statutory issue that had never previously been raised, and remands for more extensive consideration of a new argument that might affect the judgment. Under the taxonomy of our increasingly unprincipled GVR practice, this creature is of the same genus as the \xe2\x80\x9cSummary Remand for a More Extensive Opinion than Petitioner Requested\xe2\x80\x9d (SRMEOPR). Id., at ___ (slip op., at 4). But it is a distinctly odious species, deserving of its own name: Summary Remand to Ponder a Point Raised Neither Here nor Below (SRPPRNHB).
