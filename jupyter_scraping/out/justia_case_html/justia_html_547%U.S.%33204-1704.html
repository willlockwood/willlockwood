\nDAIMLERCHRYSLER CORP. V. CUNO

\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

\n

DAIMLERCHRYSLER CORP. et\xc2\xa0al. v. CUNO et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

No. 04\xe2\x80\x931704.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 1, 2006\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 15, 2006

\n

The city of Toledo and State of Ohio sought to encourage DaimlerChrysler Corp. to expand its Toledo operations by offering it local property tax exemptions and a state franchise tax credit. A group of plaintiffs including Toledo residents who pay state and local taxes sued in state court, alleging that the tax breaks violated the Commerce Clause. The taxpayer plaintiffs claimed injury because the tax breaks depleted the state and local treasuries to which they contributed. Defendants removed the action to District Court. Plaintiffs moved to remand to state court because, inter alia, they doubted whether they satisfied either the constitutional or prudential limitations on standing in federal court. The District Court declined to remand the case, concluding that plaintiffs had standing under the \xe2\x80\x9cmunicipal taxpayer standing\xe2\x80\x9d rule articulated in Massachusetts v. Mellon, 262 U. S. 447. On the merits, the court found that neither tax benefit violated the Commerce Clause. Without addressing standing, the Sixth Circuit agreed as to the municipal tax exemption, but held that the state franchise tax credit violated the Commerce Clause. Defendants sought certiorari to review the invalidation of the franchise tax credit, and plaintiffs sought certiorari to review the upholding of the property tax exemption. This Court granted review to consider whether the franchise tax credit violates the Commerce Clause, and directed the parties to address the issue of standing.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Plaintiffs have not established their standing to challenge the state franchise tax credit. Because they have no standing to challenge that credit, the lower courts erred by considering their claims on the merits. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0State taxpayers have no standing under Article III to challenge state tax or spending decisions simply by virtue of their status as taxpayers. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Before this Court can address the merits of plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 challenge, it has an obligation to assure itself that the merits question is presented in a proper Article III \xe2\x80\x9ccase\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ccontroversy.\xe2\x80\x9d Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife, 504 U. S. 555, 560. The case-or-controversy limitation is crucial in maintaining the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98tripartite allocation of power\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d set forth in the Constitution. Valley Forge Christian College v. Americans United for Separation of Church and State, Inc., 454 U. S. 464, 474. \xe2\x80\x9cArticle III standing \xe2\x80\xa6 enforces the \xe2\x80\xa6 case-or-controversy requirement.\xe2\x80\x9d Elk Grove Unified School Dist. v. Newdow, 542 U. S. 1, 11. The requisite elements of standing are familiar: \xe2\x80\x9cA plaintiff must allege personal injury fairly traceable to the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s allegedly unlawful conduct and likely to be redressed by the requested relief.\xe2\x80\x9d Allen v. Wright, 468 U. S. 737, 751. Plaintiffs, as the parties now asserting federal jurisdiction, must carry the burden of establishing their standing. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 principal claim that the franchise tax credit depletes state funds to which they contribute through their taxes, and thus diminishes the total funds available for lawful uses and imposes disproportionate burdens on them, is insufficient to establish standing under Article III. This Court has denied federal taxpayers standing under Article III to object to a particular expenditure of federal funds simply because they are taxpayers. See, e.g., Valley Forge Christian College, supra, at 476\xe2\x80\x93482. The animating principle behind cases such as Valley Forge was announced in Frothingham v. Mellon, decided with Massachusetts v. Mellon, 262 U. S. 447, in which the Court observed that a federal taxpayer\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cinterest in the moneys of the Treasury \xe2\x80\xa6 is shared with millions of others; is comparatively minute and indeterminable; and the effect upon future taxation, of any payment out of the funds, so remote, fluctuating and uncertain, that no basis is afforded for an appeal to the preventive powers of a court of equity.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 486\xe2\x80\x93487. This rationale applies with undiminished force to state taxpayers who allege simply that a state fiscal decision will deplete the fisc and \xe2\x80\x9cimpose disproportionate burdens on them.\xe2\x80\x9d See Doremus v. Board of Ed. of Hawthorne, 342 U. S. 429, 433\xe2\x80\x93434. Because state budgets frequently have an array of tax and spending provisions that may be challenged on a variety of bases, affording state taxpayers standing to press such challenges simply because their tax burden gives them an interest in the state treasury would interpose the federal courts as \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98virtually continuing monitors of the wisdom and soundness\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of state fiscal administration, contrary to the more modest role Article III envisions for federal courts. See Allen, supra,\xc2\xa0at 760\xe2\x80\x93761. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Also rejected is plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 argument that they have state taxpayer standing on the ground that their Commerce Clause challenge is just like the Establishment Clause challenge this Court permitted in Flast v. Cohen, 392 U. S. 83, 105\xe2\x80\x93106. Flast allowed an Establishment Clause challenge by federal taxpayers to a congressional action under Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa78. Although Flast held out the possibility that \xe2\x80\x9cspecific [constitutional] limitations\xe2\x80\x9d other than the Establishment Clause might support federal taxpayer standing, id., at 105, 85, only the Establishment Clause has been held to do so since Flast, see, e.g., Bowen v. Kendrick, 487 U. S. 589, 618. Plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 reliance on Flast is misguided: Whatever rights plaintiffs have under the Commerce Clause, they are fundamentally unlike the right not to contribute even \xe2\x80\x9cthree pence\xe2\x80\x9d to support a religious establishment that was upheld in Flast, 392 U. S., at 103. Indeed, plaintiffs compare the two Clauses at such a high level of generality that almost any constitutional constraint on government power could be likened to the Establishment Clause as interpreted in Flast. Id., at 105. And a finding that the Commerce Clause satisfies the Flast test because it often implicates governments\xe2\x80\x99 fiscal decisions would leave no principled way of distinguishing other constitutional provisions that also constrain governments\xe2\x80\x99 taxing and spending decisions. See, e.g., Arkansas Writers\xe2\x80\x99 Project, Inc. v. Ragland, 481 U. S. 221. Yet such a broad application of Flast\xe2\x80\x99s exception to the general prohibition on taxpayer standing would be at odds with Flast\xe2\x80\x99s own promise that it would not transform federal courts into forums for taxpayers\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cgeneralized grievances.\xe2\x80\x9d 392 U. S., at 106. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0Plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 status as municipal taxpayers does not give them standing to challenge the state franchise tax credit at issue.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court has noted with approval the standing of municipal taxpayers to enjoin the illegal use of a municipal corporation\xe2\x80\x99s funds. See, e.g., Frothingham, supra, at 486\xe2\x80\x93487. But plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 attempts to leverage the notion of municipal taxpayer standing into standing to challenge the state tax credit are unavailing. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Plaintiffs argue that because state law requires revenues from the franchise tax to be distributed to local governments, the award of a credit to DaimlerChrysler reduced such distributions and thus depleted the funds of local governments to which plaintiffs pay taxes. But plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 challenge is still to the state law and state decision, not those of plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 municipality. Their argument thus suffers from the same defects that the claim of state taxpayer standing exhibits. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Also rejected is plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 claim that their standing to challenge the municipal property tax exemption supports jurisdiction over their challenge to the franchise tax credit under the \xe2\x80\x9csupplemental jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d recognized in Mine Workers v. Gibbs, 383 U. S. 715. Gibbs held that federal-question jurisdiction over a claim may authorize a federal court to exercise jurisdiction over state-law claims that may be viewed as part of the same case because they \xe2\x80\x9cderive from a common nucleus of operative fact\xe2\x80\x9d as the federal claim. Id., at 725. Plaintiffs assume that Gibbs stands for the proposition that federal jurisdiction extends to all claims sufficiently related to a claim within Article III to be part of the same case, regardless of the deficiency that would keep the former claims out of federal court if presented on their own. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s general approach to the application of Gibbs has been markedly more cautious. See, e.g., Exxon Mobil Corp. v. Allapattah Services, Inc., 545 U. S. ___, ___. The Court has never applied Gibbs\xe2\x80\x99 rationale to permit a federal court to exercise supplemental jurisdiction over a claim that does not itself satisfy those elements of the Article III inquiry, such as constitutional standing, that \xe2\x80\x9cserv[e] to identify those disputes which are appropriately resolved through the judicial process.\xe2\x80\x9d Whitmore v. Arkansas, 495 U. S. 149, 155. There is no reason to read Gibbs\xe2\x80\x99 language as broadly as plaintiffs urge, particularly since the Court\xe2\x80\x99s standing cases confirm that a plaintiff must demonstrate standing for each claim he seeks to press, see, e.g., Allen, supra, at 752. If standing were commutative, as plaintiffs claim, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s insistence that a plaintiff must demonstrate standing separately for each form of relief sought, see, e.g., Friends of Earth, Inc. v. Laidlaw Environmental Services (TOC), Inc., 528 U. S. 167, 185, would make little sense when all claims for relief derive from a \xe2\x80\x9ccommon nucleus of operative fact,\xe2\x80\x9d as they appear to have in cases like Laidlaw.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Such a reading of Gibbs would have remarkable implications. The doctrines of mootness, ripeness, and political question all originate in Article III\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccase\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ccontroversy\xe2\x80\x9d language, no less than standing does. See, e.g., National Park Hospitality Assn. v. Department of Interior, 538 U. S. 803, 808. Yet if Gibbs\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9ccommon nucleus\xe2\x80\x9d formulation announced a new definition of \xe2\x80\x9ccase\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ccontroversy\xe2\x80\x9d for all Article III purposes, a federal court would be free to entertain moot or unripe claims, or claims presenting a political question, if they \xe2\x80\x9cderived from\xe2\x80\x9d the same \xe2\x80\x9coperative fact[s]\xe2\x80\x9d as another federal claim suffering from none of these defects. Plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 reading of Gibbs, therefore, would amount to a significant revision of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent interpreting Article III. With federal courts thus deciding issues they would not otherwise be authorized to decide, the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98tripartite allocation of power\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d that Article III is designed to maintain, Valley Forge, supra, at 474, would quickly erode, and the Court\xe2\x80\x99s emphasis on the standing requirement\xe2\x80\x99s role in maintaining this separation would be rendered hollow rhetoric, see Lewis v. Casey, 518 U. S. 343, 357. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

386 F.\xc2\xa03d 738, vacated in part and remanded.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Chief Justice Roberts delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Jeeps were first mass-produced in 1941 for the U. S. Army by the Willys-Overland Motor Company in Toledo, Ohio. Nearly 60 years later, the city of Toledo and State of Ohio sought to encourage the current manufacturer of Jeeps\xe2\x80\x94DaimlerChrysler\xe2\x80\x94to expand its Jeep operation in Toledo, by offering local and state tax benefits for new investment. Taxpayers in Toledo sued, alleging that their local and state tax burdens were increased by the tax breaks for DaimlerChrysler, tax breaks that they asserted violated the Commerce Clause. The Court of Appeals agreed that a state tax credit offered under Ohio law violated the Commerce Clause, and state and local officials and DaimlerChrysler sought review in this Court. We are obligated before reaching this Commerce Clause question to determine whether the taxpayers who objected to the credit have standing to press their complaint in federal court. We conclude that they do not, and we therefore can proceed no further.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Ohio levies a franchise tax \xe2\x80\x9cupon corporations for the privilege of doing business in the state, owning or using a part or all of its capital or property in [the] state, or holding a certificate of compliance authorizing it to do business in [the] state.\xe2\x80\x9d Wesnovtek Corp. v. Wilkins, 105 Ohio St. 3d 312, 313, 2005\xe2\x80\x93Ohio\xe2\x80\x931826, \xc2\xb62, 825 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 1099, 1100; see Ohio Rev. Code Ann. \xc2\xa75733.01 (Lexis 2005). A taxpayer that purchases \xe2\x80\x9cnew manufacturing machinery and equipment\xe2\x80\x9d and installs it at sites in the State receives a credit against the franchise tax. See \xc2\xa75733.33(B)(1) (Lexis 1999).[Footnote 1] Municipalities in Ohio may also offer partial property tax waivers to businesses that agree to invest\nin qualifying areas. See \xc2\xa75709.62(C)(1)(a) (Lexis 2005). With consent from local school districts, the partial property tax waiver can be increased to a complete exemption. See \xc2\xa75709.62(D)(1).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1998, DaimlerChrysler entered into a contract with the city of Toledo. Under the contract, DaimlerChrysler agreed to expand its Jeep assembly plant at Stickney Avenue in Toledo. In exchange, the city agreed to waive the property tax for the plant, with the consent of the two school districts in which the plant is located. Because DaimlerChrysler undertook to purchase and install \xe2\x80\x9cnew manufacturing machinery and equipment,\xe2\x80\x9d it was also entitled to a credit against the state franchise tax. See \xc2\xa75733.33(B)(1) (Lexis 1999).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Plaintiffs filed suit against various state and local officials and DaimlerChrysler in state court, alleging that these tax benefits violated the Commerce Clause. Most of the plaintiffs were residents of Toledo, who paid taxes to both the city of Toledo and State of Ohio. They claimed that they were injured because the tax breaks for DaimlerChrysler diminished the funds available to the city and State, imposing a \xe2\x80\x9cdisproportionate burden\xe2\x80\x9d on plaintiffs. App. 18a, 23a, 28a.[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Defendants removed the action to the United States District Court for the Northern District of Ohio. See 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71441. Plaintiffs filed motions to remand the case to state court. See \xc2\xa71447(c). One of the grounds on which they sought remand concerned their standing. They professed \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial doubts about their ability to satisfy either the constitutional or the prudential limitations on standing in the federal court,\xe2\x80\x9d and urged the District Court to avoid the issue entirely by remanding. Plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 Supplemental Motion for Remand to State Court in No. 3:00cv7247, p.\xc2\xa013, Record Doc. 17 (footnote omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court declined to remand the case, concluding that, \xe2\x80\x9c[a]t the bare minimum, the Plaintiffs who are taxpayers have standing to object to the property tax exemption and franchise tax credit statutes under the \xe2\x80\x98municipal taxpayer standing\xe2\x80\x99 rule articulated in Massachusetts v. Mellon, 262 U. S. 447 (1923).\xe2\x80\x9d App. 78a (citations omitted). On the merits, the District Court found that neither tax benefit violated the Commerce Clause. See 154 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1196 (2001). The Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit agreed with the District Court as to the municipal property tax exemption, but held that the state franchise tax credit violated the Commerce Clause. See 386 F.\xc2\xa03d 738 (2004). The Court of Appeals did not address the issue of standing.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Defendants sought certiorari to review the Sixth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s invalidation of the franchise tax credit and plaintiffs sought certiorari to review the upholding of the property tax exemption. We granted certiorari to consider whether the franchise tax credit violates the Commerce Clause, 545 U. S. ___ (2005); the Michigan Supreme Court had decided a similar question contrary to the Sixth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s analysis here. See Caterpillar, Inc. v. Dept. of Treasury, 440 Mich. 400, 488 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 182 (1992). We also asked the parties to address whether plaintiffs have standing to challenge the franchise tax credit in this litigation.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have \xe2\x80\x9can obligation to assure ourselves\xe2\x80\x9d of litigants\xe2\x80\x99 standing under Article III. Friends of Earth, Inc. v. Laidlaw Environmental Services (TOC), Inc., 528 U. S. 167, 180 (2000). We therefore begin by addressing plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 claims that they have standing as taxpayers to challenge the franchise tax credit.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0 Chief Justice Marshall, in Marbury v. Madison, 1 Cranch 137 (1803), grounded the Federal Judiciary\xe2\x80\x99s authority to exercise judicial review and interpret the Constitution on the necessity to do so in the course of carrying out the judicial function of deciding cases. As Marshall explained, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]hose who apply the rule to particular cases, must of necessity expound and interpret that rule.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 177. Determining that a matter before the federal courts is a proper case or controversy under Article III therefore assumes particular importance in ensuring that the Federal Judiciary respects \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the proper\xe2\x80\x94and properly limited\xe2\x80\x94role of the courts in a democratic society,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Allen v. Wright, 468 U. S. 737, 750 (1984) (quoting Warth v. Seldin, 422 U. S. 490, 498 (1975)). If a dispute is not a proper case or controversy, the courts have no business deciding it, or expounding the law in the course of doing so.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court has recognized that the case-or-controversy limitation is crucial in maintaining the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98tripartite allocation of power\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d set forth in the Constitution. Valley Forge Christian College v. Americans United for Separation of Church and State, Inc., 454 U. S. 464, 474 (1982) (quoting Flast v. Cohen, 392 U. S. 83, 95 (1968)). Marshall again made the point early on, this time in a speech in the House of Representatives. \xe2\x80\x9cA case in law or equity,\xe2\x80\x9d Marshall remarked,

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cwas a term \xe2\x80\xa6 of limited signification. It was a controversy between parties which had taken a shape for judicial decision. If the judicial power extended to every question under the constitution it would involve almost every subject proper for legislative discussion and decision; if to every question under the laws and treaties of the United States it would involve almost every subject on which the executive could act. The division of power [among the branches of government] could exist no longer, and the other departments would be swallowed up by the judiciary.\xe2\x80\x9d 4 Papers of John Marshall 95 (C. Cullen ed. 1984).

\n

\nAs this Court has explained, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[n]o principle is more fundamental to the judiciary\xe2\x80\x99s proper role in our system of government than the constitutional limitation of federal-court jurisdiction to actual cases or controversies.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Raines v. Byrd, 521 U. S. 811, 818 (1997) (quoting Simon v. Eastern Ky. Welfare Rights Organization, 426 U. S. 26, 37 (1976)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The case-or-controversy requirement thus plays a critical role, and \xe2\x80\x9cArticle III standing \xe2\x80\xa6 enforces the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s case-or-controversy requirement.\xe2\x80\x9d Elk Grove Unified School Dist. v. Newdow, 542 U. S. 1, 11 (2004). The \xe2\x80\x9ccore component\xe2\x80\x9d of the requirement that a litigant have standing to invoke the authority of a federal court \xe2\x80\x9cis an essential and unchanging part of the case-or-controversy requirement of Article III.\xe2\x80\x9d Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife, 504 U. S. 555, 560 (1992). The requisite elements of this \xe2\x80\x9ccore component derived directly from the Constitution\xe2\x80\x9d are familiar: \xe2\x80\x9cA plaintiff must allege personal injury fairly traceable to the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s allegedly unlawful conduct and likely to be redressed by the requested relief.\xe2\x80\x9d Allen, supra, at 751. We have been asked to decide an important question of constitutional law concerning the Commerce Clause. But before we do so, we must find that the\nquestion is presented in a \xe2\x80\x9ccase\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ccontroversy\xe2\x80\x9d that\nis, in James Madison\xe2\x80\x99s words, \xe2\x80\x9cof a Judiciary Nature.\xe2\x80\x9d 2\nRecords of the Federal Convention of 1787, p. 430\n(M. Farrand ed. 1966). That requires plaintiffs, as the parties now asserting federal jurisdiction, to carry the burden of establishing their standing under Article III.[Footnote 3]

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Plaintiffs principally claim standing by virtue of their status as Ohio taxpayers, alleging that the franchise tax credit \xe2\x80\x9cdepletes the funds of the State of Ohio to which the Plaintiffs contribute through their tax payments\xe2\x80\x9d and thus \xe2\x80\x9cdiminish[es] the total funds available for lawful uses and impos[es] disproportionate burdens on\xe2\x80\x9d them. App. 28a; see also Brief for Respondents 24. On several occasions, this Court has denied federal taxpayers standing under Article III to object to a particular expenditure of federal funds simply because they are taxpayers. Thus the alleged \xe2\x80\x9cdeprivation of the fair and constitutional use of [a federal taxpayer\xe2\x80\x99s] tax dollar\xe2\x80\x9d cannot support a challenge to the conveyance of Government land to a private religious college, Valley Forge, supra, at 476\xe2\x80\x93482 (internal quotation marks and some brackets omitted), and \xe2\x80\x9cthe interest of a taxpayer in the moneys of the federal treasury furnishes no basis\xe2\x80\x9d to argue that a federal agency\xe2\x80\x99s loan practices are unconstitutional, Alabama Power Co. v. Ickes, 302 U. S. 464, 478 (1938); see also Schlesinger v. Reservists Comm. to Stop the War, 418 U. S. 208 (1974); United States v. Richardson, 418 U. S. 166 (1974).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The animating principle behind these cases was announced in their progenitor, Frothingham v. Mellon, decided with Massachusetts v. Mellon, 262 U. S. 447 (1923). In rejecting a claim that improper federal appropriations would \xe2\x80\x9cincrease the burden of future taxation and thereby take [the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s] property without due process of law,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court observed that a federal taxpayer\xe2\x80\x99s

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cinterest in the moneys of the Treasury \xe2\x80\xa6 is shared with millions of others; is comparatively minute and indeterminable; and the effect upon future taxation, of any payment out of the funds, so remote, fluctuating and uncertain, that no basis is afforded for an appeal to the preventive powers of a court of equity.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 486\xe2\x80\x93487.

\n

\nThis logic is equally applicable to taxpayer challenges to expenditures that deplete the treasury, and to taxpayer challenges to so-called \xe2\x80\x9ctax expenditures,\xe2\x80\x9d which reduce amounts available to the treasury by granting tax credits or exemptions. In either case, the alleged injury is based on the asserted effect of the allegedly illegal activity on public revenues, to which the taxpayer contributes.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Standing has been rejected in such cases because the alleged injury is not \xe2\x80\x9cconcrete and particularized,\xe2\x80\x9d Defenders of Wildlife, supra, at 560, but instead a grievance the taxpayer \xe2\x80\x9csuffers in some indefinite way in common with people generally,\xe2\x80\x9d Frothingham, supra, at 488. In addition, the injury is not \xe2\x80\x9cactual or imminent,\xe2\x80\x9d but instead \xe2\x80\x9cconjectural or hypothetical.\xe2\x80\x9d Defenders of Wildlife, supra, at 560 (internal quotation marks and citations omitted). As an initial matter, it is unclear that tax breaks of the sort at issue here do in fact deplete the treasury: The very point of the tax benefits is to spur economic activity, which in turn increases government revenues. In this very action, the Michigan plaintiffs claimed that they were injured because they lost out on the added revenues that would have accompanied DaimlerChrysler\xe2\x80\x99s decision to expand facilities in Michigan. See n.\xc2\xa02, supra.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 alleged injury is also \xe2\x80\x9cconjectural or hypothetical\xe2\x80\x9d in that it depends on how legislators respond to a reduction in revenue, if that is the consequence of the credit. Establishing injury requires speculating that elected officials will increase a taxpayer-plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s tax bill to make up a deficit; establishing redressability requires speculating that abolishing the challenged credit will redound to the benefit of the taxpayer because legislators will pass along the supposed increased revenue in the form of tax reductions. Neither sort of speculation suffices to support standing. See ASARCO Inc. v. Kadish, 490 U. S. 605, 614 (1989) (opinion of Kennedy, J.) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is pure speculation whether the lawsuit would result in any actual tax relief for respondents\xe2\x80\x9d); Warth, 422 U. S., at 509 (criticizing a taxpayer standing claim for the \xe2\x80\x9cconjectural nature of the asserted injury\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A taxpayer-plaintiff has no right to insist that the government dispose of any increased revenue it might experience as a result of his suit by decreasing his tax liability or bolstering programs that benefit him. To the contrary, the decision of how to allocate any such savings is the very epitome of a policy judgment committed to the \xe2\x80\x9cbroad and legitimate discretion\xe2\x80\x9d of lawmakers, which \xe2\x80\x9cthe courts cannot presume either to control or to predict.\xe2\x80\x9d ASARCO, supra, at 615 (opinion of Kennedy, J.). Under such circumstances, we have no assurance that the asserted injury is \xe2\x80\x9cimminent\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94that it is \xe2\x80\x9ccertainly impending.\xe2\x80\x9d Whitmore v. Arkansas, 495 U. S. 149, 158 (1990) (internal quotation marks omitted); see Defenders of Wildlife, 504 U. S., at 564\xe2\x80\x93565, n.\xc2\xa02.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The foregoing rationale for rejecting federal taxpayer standing applies with undiminished force to state taxpayers. We indicated as much in Doremus v. Board of Ed. of Hawthorne, 342 U. S. 429 (1952). In that case, we noted our earlier holdings that \xe2\x80\x9cthe interests of a taxpayer in the moneys of the federal treasury are too indeterminable, remote, uncertain and indirect\xe2\x80\x9d to support standing to challenge \xe2\x80\x9ctheir manner of expenditure.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 433. We then \xe2\x80\x9creiterate[d]\xe2\x80\x9d what we had said in rejecting a federal taxpayer challenge to a federal statute \xe2\x80\x9cas equally true when a state Act is assailed: \xe2\x80\x98The [taxpayer] must be able to show \xe2\x80\xa6 that he has sustained \xe2\x80\xa6 some direct injury \xe2\x80\xa6 and not merely that he suffers in some indefinite way in common with people generally.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 433\xe2\x80\x93434 (quoting Frothingham, supra, at 488); see ASARCO, supra, at 613\xe2\x80\x93614 (opinion of Kennedy, J.) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e have likened state taxpayers to federal taxpayers\xe2\x80\x9d for purposes of taxpayer standing (citing Doremus, supra, at 434)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The allegations of injury that plaintiffs make in their complaint furnish no better basis for finding standing than those made in the cases where federal taxpayer standing was denied. Plaintiffs claim that DaimlerChrysler\xe2\x80\x99s tax credit depletes the Ohio fisc and \xe2\x80\x9cimpos[es] disproportionate burdens on [them].\xe2\x80\x9d App. 28a. This is no different from similar claims by federal taxpayers we have already rejected under Article III as insufficient to establish standing. See, e.g., Frothingham, 262 U. S., at 486 (allegation of injury that the effect of government spending \xe2\x80\x9cwill be to increase the burden of future taxation and thereby take [plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s] property without due process of law\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0State policymakers, no less than their federal counterparts, retain broad discretion to make \xe2\x80\x9cpolicy decisions\xe2\x80\x9d concerning state spending \xe2\x80\x9cin different ways \xe2\x80\xa6 depending on their perceptions of wise state fiscal policy and myriad other circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d ASARCO, supra, at 615 (opinion of Kennedy, J.). Federal courts may not assume a particular exercise of this state fiscal discretion in establishing standing; a party seeking federal jurisdiction cannot rely on such \xe2\x80\x9c[s]peculative inferences \xe2\x80\xa6 to connect [his] injury to the challenged actions of [the defendant],\xe2\x80\x9d Simon, 426 U. S., at 45; see also Allen, 468 U. S., at 759. Indeed, because state budgets frequently contain an array of tax and spending provisions, any number of which may be challenged on a variety of bases, affording state taxpayers standing to press such challenges simply because their tax burden gives them an interest in the state treasury would interpose the federal courts as \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98virtually continuing monitors of the wisdom and soundness\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of state fiscal administration, contrary to the more modest role Article III envisions for federal courts. See id., at 760\xe2\x80\x93761 (quoting Laird v. Tatum, 408 U. S. 1, 15 (1972)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For the foregoing reasons, we hold that state taxpayers have no standing under Article III to challenge state tax or spending decisions simply by virtue of their status as taxpayers.[Footnote 4]

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Plaintiffs argue that an exception to the general prohibition on taxpayer standing should exist for Commerce Clause challenges to state tax or spending decisions, analogizing their Commerce Clause claim to the Establishment Clause challenge we permitted in Flast v. Cohen, 392 U. S. 83. Flast held that because \xe2\x80\x9cthe Establishment Clause \xe2\x80\xa6 specifically limit[s] the taxing and spending power conferred by Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa78,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ca taxpayer will have standing consistent with Article III to invoke federal judicial power when he alleges that congressional action under the taxing and spending clause is in derogation of\xe2\x80\x9d the Establishment Clause. Id., at 105\xe2\x80\x93106. Flast held out the possibility that \xe2\x80\x9cother specific [constitutional] limitations\xe2\x80\x9d on Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa78, might surmount the \xe2\x80\x9cbarrier to suits against Acts of Congress brought by individuals who can assert only the interest of federal taxpayers.\xe2\x80\x9d 392 U. S., at 105, 85. But as plaintiffs candidly concede, \xe2\x80\x9conly the Establishment Clause\xe2\x80\x9d has supported federal taxpayer suits since Flast. Brief for Respondents 12; see Bowen v. Kendrick, 487 U. S. 589, 618 (1988) (\xe2\x80\x9cAlthough we have considered the problem of standing and Article III limitations on federal jurisdiction many times since [Flast], we have consistently adhered to Flast and the narrow exception it created to the general rule against taxpayer standing\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Quite apart from whether the franchise tax credit is analogous to an exercise of congressional power under Art. I, \xc2\xa78, plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 reliance on Flast is misguided: Whatever rights plaintiffs have under the Commerce Clause, they are fundamentally unlike the right not to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98contribute three pence \xe2\x80\xa6 for the support of any one [religious] establishment.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 392 U. S., at 103 (quoting 2 Writings of James Madison 186 (G. Hunt ed. 1901)). Indeed, plaintiffs compare the Establishment Clause to the Commerce Clause at such a high level of generality that almost any constitutional constraint on government power would \xe2\x80\x9cspecifically limit\xe2\x80\x9d a State\xe2\x80\x99s taxing and spending power for Flast purposes. 392 U. S., at 105; see Brief for Respondents 14 (\xe2\x80\x9cIn each case, the harm to be avoided by [the two clauses] is the loss of governmental neutrality\xe2\x80\x9d). And even if the two clauses are similar in that they often implicate governments\xe2\x80\x99 fiscal decisions, see id., at 13\xe2\x80\x9314, a finding that the Commerce Clause satisfies the Flast test would leave no principled way of distinguishing those other constitutional provisions that we have recognized constrain governments\xe2\x80\x99 taxing and spending decisions. See, e.g., Arkansas Writers\xe2\x80\x99 Project, Inc. v. Ragland, 481 U. S. 221 (1987) (invalidating state sales tax under the Free Press Clause). Yet such a broad application of Flast\xe2\x80\x99s exception to the general prohibition on taxpayer standing would be quite at odds with its narrow application in our precedent and Flast\xe2\x80\x99s own promise that it would not transform federal courts into forums for taxpayers\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cgeneralized grievances.\xe2\x80\x9d 392 U. S., at 106.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Flast is consistent with the principle, underlying the Article III prohibition on taxpayer suits, that a litigant may not assume a particular disposition of government funds in establishing standing. The Flast Court discerned in the history of the Establishment Clause \xe2\x80\x9cthe specific evils feared by [its drafters] that the taxing and spending power would be used to favor one religion over another or to support religion in general.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 103. The Court therefore understood the \xe2\x80\x9cinjury\xe2\x80\x9d alleged in Establishment Clause challenges to federal spending to be the very \xe2\x80\x9cextract[ion] and spen[ding]\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9ctax money\xe2\x80\x9d in aid of religion alleged by a plaintiff. Id., at 106. And an injunction against the spending would of course redress that injury, regardless of whether lawmakers would dispose of the savings in a way that would benefit the taxpayer-plaintiffs personally. See Valley Forge, 454 U. S., at 514 (Stevens, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 invocation of the Establishment Clause was of decisive importance in resolving the standing issue in [Flast]\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Plaintiffs thus do not have state taxpayer standing on the ground that their Commerce Clause challenge is just like the Establishment Clause challenge in Flast.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Plaintiffs also claim that their status as municipal taxpayers gives them standing to challenge the state franchise tax credit at issue here. The Frothingham Court noted with approval the standing of municipal residents to enjoin the \xe2\x80\x9cillegal use of the moneys of a municipal corporation,\xe2\x80\x9d relying on \xe2\x80\x9cthe peculiar relation of the corporate taxpayer to the corporation\xe2\x80\x9d to distinguish such a case from the general bar on taxpayer suits. 262 U. S., at 486\xe2\x80\x93487; see ASARCO, 490 U. S., at 613\xe2\x80\x93614 (opinion of Kennedy, J.) (reiterating distinction). Plaintiffs here challenged the municipal property tax exemption as municipal taxpayers. That challenge was rejected by the Court of Appeals on the merits, and no issue regarding plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 standing to bring it has been raised. In plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 challenge to the state franchise tax credit, however, they identify no municipal action contributing to any claimed injury. Instead, they try to leverage the notion of municipal taxpayer standing beyond challenges to municipal action, in two ways.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, plaintiffs claim that because state law requires revenues from the franchise tax to be distributed to local governments, Ohio Rev. Code Ann. \xc2\xa75733.12 (Lexis 2005), the award of a credit to DaimlerChrysler reduced such distributions and thus depleted the funds of \xe2\x80\x9clocal governments to which Respondents pay taxes.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 16. But plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 challenge is still to the state law and state decision, not those of their municipality. We have already explained why a state taxpayer lacks standing to challenge a state fiscal decision on the grounds that it might affect his tax liability. All plaintiffs have done in recasting their claims as ones brought by municipal taxpayers whose municipalities receive funding from the State\xe2\x80\x94the level of which might be affected by the same state fiscal decision\xe2\x80\x94is introduce yet another level of conjecture to their already hypothetical claim of injury.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0And in fact events have highlighted the peril of assuming that any revenue increase resulting from a taxpayer suit will be put to a particular use. Ohio\xe2\x80\x99s General Assembly suspended the statutory budget mechanism that distributes franchise tax revenues to local governments in 2001 and again in its subsequent biennial budgets.\nSee Amended Substitute H.\xc2\xa0B. 94, 124th General Assembly \xc2\xa7140 (2001), available at http://www.legislature.state.\noh.us/BillText124/124_HB_94_ENR.pdf (all Internet ma-\nterials as visited May 12, 2006, and available in\nClerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file); Amended Substitute H.\xc2\xa0B.\n95, 125th General Assembly \xc2\xa7139 (2003), available at http://\xc2\xa0www.legislature.state.oh.us/BillText125/125_HB_95_EN2_N.pdf; Amended Substitute H.\xc2\xa0B. 66, 126th Gen-\neral Assembly \xc2\xa7557.12 (2005), available at http://www.\nlegislature.state.oh.us/BillText126/126_HB_66_EN2d.pdf. Any effect that enjoining DaimlerChrysler\xe2\x80\x99s credit will have on municipal funds, therefore, will not result from automatic operation of a statutory formula, but from a hypothesis that the state government will choose to direct the supposed revenue from the restored franchise tax to municipalities. This is precisely the sort of conjecture we may not entertain in assessing standing. See ASARCO, supra, at 614 (opinion of Kennedy, J.).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The second way plaintiffs seek to leverage their standing to challenge the municipal property tax exemption into a challenge to the franchise tax credit is by relying on Mine Workers v. Gibbs, 383 U. S. 715 (1966). According to plaintiffs, the \xe2\x80\x9csupplemental jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d recognized in that case supports jurisdiction over all their claims, once the District Court determined they had standing to challenge the property tax exemption. Brief for Respondents 17\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Gibbs held that federal-question jurisdiction over a claim may authorize a federal court to exercise jurisdiction over state-law claims that may be viewed as part of the same case because they \xe2\x80\x9cderive from a common nucleus of operative fact\xe2\x80\x9d as the federal claim. 383 U. S., at 725. Plaintiffs assume that Gibbs stands for the proposition that federal jurisdiction extends to all claims sufficiently related to a claim within Article III to be part of the same case, regardless of the nature of the deficiency that would keep the former claims out of federal court if presented on their own.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our general approach to the application of Gibbs, however, has been markedly more cautious. For example, as a matter of statutory construction of the pertinent jurisidictional provisions, we refused to extend Gibbs to allow claims to be asserted against nondiverse parties when jurisdiction was based on diversity, see Owen Equipment & Erection Co. v. Kroger, 437 U. S. 365 (1978), and we refused to extend Gibbs to authorize supplemental jurisdiction over claims that do not satisfy statutory amount-in-controversy requirements, see Finley v. United States, 490 U. S. 545 (1989). As the Court explained just last Term, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]e have not \xe2\x80\xa6 applied Gibbs\xe2\x80\x99 expansive interpretive approach to other aspects of the jurisdictional statutes.\xe2\x80\x9d Exxon Mobil Corp. v. Allapattah Services, Inc., 545 U. S. ___, ___ (2005) (slip op., at 5) (applying 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71367, enacted in 1990, to allow a federal court in a diversity action to exercise supplemental jurisdiction over additional diverse plaintiffs whose claims failed to meet the amount-in-controversy threshold).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0What we have never done is apply the rationale of Gibbs to permit a federal court to exercise supplemental jurisdiction over a claim that does not itself satisfy those elements of the Article III inquiry, such as constitutional standing, that \xe2\x80\x9cserv[e] to identify those disputes which are appropriately resolved through the judicial process.\xe2\x80\x9d Whitmore, 495 U. S., at 155. We see no reason to read the language of Gibbs so broadly, particularly since our standing cases confirm that a plaintiff must demonstrate standing for each claim he seeks to press. See Allen, 468 U. S., at 752 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he standing inquiry requires careful judicial examination of a complaint\xe2\x80\x99s allegations to ascertain whether the particular plaintiff is entitled to an adjudication of the particular claims asserted\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). We have insisted, for instance, that \xe2\x80\x9ca plaintiff must demonstrate standing separately for each form of relief sought.\xe2\x80\x9d Laidlaw, 528 U. S., at 185; see Los Angeles v. Lyons, 461 U. S. 95, 109 (1983). But if standing were commutative, as plaintiffs claim, this insistence would make little sense when all claims for relief derive from a \xe2\x80\x9ccommon nucleus of operative fact,\xe2\x80\x9d as they certainly appear to have in both Laidlaw, supra, at 175\xe2\x80\x93179, and Lyons, supra, at 97\xe2\x80\x9398.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 reading of Gibbs to allow standing as to one claim to suffice for all claims arising from the same \xe2\x80\x9cnucleus of operative fact\xe2\x80\x9d would have remarkable implications. The doctrines of mootness, ripeness, and political question all originate in Article III\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccase\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ccontroversy\xe2\x80\x9d language, no less than standing does. See, e.g., National Park Hospitality Assn. v. Department of Interior, 538 U. S. 803, 808 (2003) (ripeness); Arizonans for Official English v. Arizona, 520 U. S. 43, 67 (1997) (mootness); Reservists Comm. to Stop the War, 418 U. S., at 215 (political question). Yet if Gibbs\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9ccommon nucleus\xe2\x80\x9d formulation announced a new definition of \xe2\x80\x9ccase\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ccontroversy\xe2\x80\x9d for all Article III purposes, a federal court would be free to entertain moot or unripe claims, or claims presenting a political question, if they \xe2\x80\x9cderived from\xe2\x80\x9d the same \xe2\x80\x9coperative fact[s]\xe2\x80\x9d as another federal claim suffering from none\nof these defects. Plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 reading of Gibbs, therefore, would amount to a significant revision of our precedent interpreting Article III. \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0With federal courts thus deciding issues they would not otherwise be authorized to decide, the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98tripartite allocation of power\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d that Article III is designed to maintain, Valley Forge, 454 U. S., at 474, would quickly erode; our emphasis on the standing requirement\xe2\x80\x99s role in maintaining this separation would be rendered hollow rhetoric. As we have explained, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he actual-injury requirement would hardly serve the purpose \xe2\x80\xa6 of preventing courts from undertaking tasks assigned to the political branches[,] if once a plaintiff demonstrated harm from one particular inadequacy in government administration, the court were authorized to remedy all inadequacies in that administration.\xe2\x80\x9d Lewis v. Casey, 518 U. S. 343, 357 (1996).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Lewis emphasized that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he remedy must of course be limited to the inadequacy that produced the injury in fact that the plaintiff has established.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 theory of ancillary standing would contravene this principle. Plaintiffs failed to establish Article III injury with respect to their state taxes, and even if they did do so with respect to their municipal taxes, that injury does not entitle them to seek a remedy as to the state taxes. As the Court summed up the point in Lewis, \xe2\x80\x9cstanding is not dispensed in gross.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 358, n.\xc2\xa06.[Footnote 5]

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0All the theories plaintiffs have offered to support their standing to challenge the franchise tax credit are unavailing. Because plaintiffs have no standing to challenge that credit, the lower courts erred by considering their claims against it on the merits. The judgment of the Sixth Circuit is therefore vacated in part, and the cases are remanded for dismissal of plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 challenge to the franchise tax credit.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision, the Court rightly points out, is solidly grounded in longstanding precedent, Frothingham v. Mellon (decided with Massachusetts v. Mellon), 262 U. S. 447 (1923), and Doremus v. Board of Ed. of Hawthorne, 342 U. S. 429 (1952), decisions that antedate current jurisprudence on standing to sue. See ante, at 7, 9. Frothingham held nonjusticiable a federal taxpayer\xe2\x80\x99s suit challenging a federal-spending program. See 262 U. S., at 487 (describing taxpayer\xe2\x80\x99s interest as \xe2\x80\x9cminute and indeterminable\xe2\x80\x9d). Doremus applied Frothingham\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning to a state taxpayer\xe2\x80\x99s suit. 342 U. S., at 434. These decisions exclude from federal-court cognizance claims, not delineated by Congress, presenting generalized grievances. An exception to Frothingham\xe2\x80\x99s rule, recognized post-Doremus in Flast v. Cohen, 392 U. S. 83 (1968), covers certain alleged violations of the Establishment Clause. The Flast exception has not been extended to other areas. See Bowen v. Kendrick, 487 U. S. 589, 618 (1988); cf. Enrich, Saving the States from Themselves: Commerce Clause Constraints on State Tax Incentives for Business, 110 Harv. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 377, 417\xe2\x80\x93418 (1996).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0One can accept, as I do, the nonjusticiability of Frothingham-type federal and state taxpayer suits in federal court without endorsing as well the limitations on standing later declared in Simon v. Eastern Ky. Welfare Rights Organization, 426 U. S. 26 (1976) (EKWRO), Valley Forge Christian College v. Americans United for Separation of Church and State, Inc., 454 U. S. 464 (1982), Allen v. Wright, 468 U. S. 737 (1984), and Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife, 504 U. S. 555 (1992). See EKWRO, 426 U. S., at 54\xe2\x80\x9366 (Brennan, J., concurring in judgment); Valley Forge, 454 U. S., at 513\xe2\x80\x93515 (Stevens, J., dissenting); Allen, 468 U. S., at 783\xe2\x80\x93795 (Stevens, J., dissenting), and the overturned Court of Appeals opinion, Wright v. Regan, 656 F.\xc2\xa02d 820, 828\xe2\x80\x93832 (CADC 1981) (Ginsburg, J.); Defenders of Wildlife, 504 U. S., at 582\xe2\x80\x93585 (Stevens, J., concurring in judgment); Sunstein, What\xe2\x80\x99s Standing after Lujan? Of Citizen Suits, \xe2\x80\x9cInjuries,\xe2\x80\x9d and Article III, 91 Mich. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 163, 203\xe2\x80\x93205, 228\xe2\x80\x93229 (1992) (contrasting Lujan, Allen, and EKWRO with Regents of Univ. of Cal. v. Bakke, 438 U. S. 265 (1978)); Fletcher, The Structure of Standing, 98 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J. 221, 267\xe2\x80\x93270 (1988) (commenting on Flast and Valley Forge). Noting this large reservation, I concur in the judgment, and in the balance of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion.
