\n\t\t\t\tCOLEMAN v. COURT OF APPEALS OF MARYLAND et\xc2\xa0al.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fourth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 10\xe2\x80\x931016.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 11, 2012\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 20, 2012\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe Family and Medical Leave Act of 1993 (FMLA) entitles an employee to take up to 12 work weeks of unpaid leave per year for (A) the care of a newborn son or daughter; (B) the adoption or foster-care placement of a child; (C) the care of a spouse, son, daughter, or parent with a serious medical condition; and (D) the employee\xe2\x80\x99s own serious health condition when the condition interferes with the employee\xe2\x80\x99s ability to perform at work. \n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72612(a)(1). The FMLA also creates a private right of action for equitable relief and damages \xe2\x80\x9cagainst any employer (including a public agency) in any Federal or State court.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72617(a)(2). For present purposes, subparagraphs (A), (B), and (C) are referred to as the family-care provisions, and subparagraph (D) as the self-care provision. In Nevada Dept. of Human Resources v. Hibbs, \n\n538 U.\xc2\xa0S. 721\n\xe2\x88\x92732, this Court held that Congress could subject States to suit for violations of subparagraph (C) based on evidence of family-leave policies that discriminated on the basis of sex.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioner filed suit, alleging that his employer, the Maryland Court of Appeals, an instrumentality of the State, violated the FMLA by denying him self-care leave. The Federal District Court dismissed the suit on sovereign immunity grounds. The Fourth Circuit affirmed, holding that unlike the family-care provision in Hibbs, the self-care provision was not directed at an identified pattern of gender-based discrimination and was not congruent and proportional to any pattern of sex-based discrimination on the part of States.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0The judgment is affirmed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t626 F.\xc2\xa03d 187, affirmed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Kennedy, joined by The Chief Justice, Justice Thomas, and Justice Alito, concluded that suits against States under the self-care provision are barred by sovereign immunity. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x88\x9212.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0Under the federal system, States, as sovereigns, are immune from damages suits, unless they waive that defense. See, e.g., Kimel v. Florida Bd. of Regents, \n\n528 U.\xc2\xa0S. 62\n\xe2\x88\x9273. Congress may also abrogate the States\xe2\x80\x99 immunity pursuant to its powers under \xc2\xa75 of the \n \nFourteenth Amendment, but it must make that intention \xe2\x80\x9cunmistakably clear in the language of the statute,\xe2\x80\x9d Hibbs, supra, at 726. It did so in the FMLA. Congress also \xe2\x80\x9cmust tailor\xe2\x80\x9d legislation enacted under \xc2\xa75 \xe2\x80\x9cto remedy or prevent\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cconduct transgressing the \n \nFourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s substantive provisions.\xe2\x80\x9d Florida Prepaid Postsecondary Ed. Expense Bd. v. College Savings Bank, \n\n527 U.\xc2\xa0S. 627\n. \xe2\x80\x9cThere must be a congruence and proportionality between the injury to be prevented or remedied and the means adopted to that end.\xe2\x80\x9d City of Boerne v. Flores, \n\n521 U.\xc2\xa0S. 507\n. Pp. 3\xe2\x88\x925.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0The sex-based discrimination that supported allowing subparagraph (C) suits against States is absent with respect to the self-care provision. Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s three arguments to the contrary are unpersuasive. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9312.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(1)\xc2\xa0Petitioner maintains that the self-care provision addresses sex discrimination and sex stereotyping. But the provision, standing alone, is not a valid abrogation of the States\xe2\x80\x99 immunity from suit. At the time the FMLA was enacted, there was no evidence of such discrimination or stereotyping in sick-leave policies. Congress was concerned about the economic burdens imposed by illness-related job loss on employees and their families and about discrimination based on illness, not sex. Although the self-care provision offers some women a benefit by allowing them to take leave for pregnancy-related illnesses, the provision, as a remedy, is not congruent and proportional to any identified constitutional violations. When the FMLA was enacted, Congress had no evidence that States were excluding pregnancy-related illnesses from their leave policies. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x937.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(2)\xc2\xa0Petitioner also argues that the self-care provision is a necessary adjunct to the family-care provision sustained in Hibbs. But his claim\xe2\x80\x94that the provisions work in tandem to ensure the equal availability of total FMLA leave time to women and men despite their different leave-usage patterns\xe2\x80\x95is unconvincing and does not comply with the requirements of City of Boerne. Also, there are no congressional findings of, or evidence on, how the self-care provision is necessary to the family-care provisions or how it reduces employer discrimination against women. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9311.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(3)\xc2\xa0Finally, petitioner contends that the self-care provision helps single parents keep their jobs when they get ill. The fact that most single parents happen to be women demonstrates, at most, that the self-care provision was directed at remedying neutral leave restrictions that have a disparate effect on women. However, \xe2\x80\x9c[a]lthough disparate impact may be relevant evidence of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. discrimination .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. such evidence is insufficient [to prove a constitutional violation] even where the \n \nFourteenth Amendment subjects state action to strict scrutiny.\xe2\x80\x9d Board of Trustees of Univ. of Ala. v. Garrett, \n\n531 U.\xc2\xa0S. 356\n. Because it is unlikely that many of the neutral leave policies affected by the self-care provision are unconstitutional, the scope of the self-care provision is out of proportion to its supposed remedial or preventive objectives. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x88\x9212. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Scalia adhered to his view that the Court should abandon the \xe2\x80\x9ccongruence and proportionality\xe2\x80\x9d approach in favor of one that is properly tied to the text of \xc2\xa75, which grants Congress the power \xe2\x80\x9cto enforce, by appropriate legislation,\xe2\x80\x9d the other provisions of the \n \nFourteenth Amendment. Outside the context of racial discrimination, Congress\xe2\x80\x99s \xc2\xa75 power should be limited to the regulation of conduct that itself violates the \n \nFourteenth Amendment and thus would not reach a State\xe2\x80\x99s failure to grant self-care leave to its employees. Pp.\xc2\xa01\xe2\x88\x922.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Kennedy announced the judgment of the Court and delivered an opinion, in which The Chief Justice, Justice Thomas, and Justice Alito joined.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe question in this case is whether a state employee is allowed to recover damages from the state entity that employs him by invoking one of the provisions of a federal statute that, in express terms, seeks to abrogate the States\xe2\x80\x99 immunity from suits for damages. The statute in question is the Family and Medical Leave Act of 1993, \n\n107Stat.\n6, \n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72601 et seq. The provision at issue requires employers, including state employers, to grant unpaid leave for self care for a serious medical condition, provided other statutory requisites are met, particularly requirements that the total amount of annual leave taken under all the Act\xe2\x80\x99s provisions does not exceed a stated maximum. \xc2\xa72612(a)(1)(d). In agreement with every Court of Appeals to have addressed this question, this Court now holds that suits against States under this provision are barred by the States\xe2\x80\x99 immunity as sovereigns in our federal system. See 626 F.\xc2\xa03d 187 (CA4 2010) (case below); Nelson v. University of Tex., 535 F.\xc2\xa03d 318 (CA5 2008); Miles v. Bellfontaine Habilitation Center, 481 F.\xc2\xa03d 1106 (CA8 2007) (per\xc2\xa0curiam); Toeller v. Wisconsin Dept. of Corrections, 461 F.\xc2\xa03d 871 (CA7 2006); Touvell v. Ohio Dept. of Mental Retardation & Developmental Disabilities, 422 F.\xc2\xa03d 392 (CA6 2005); Brockman v. Wyoming Dept. of Family Servs., 342 F.\xc2\xa03d 1159 (CA10 2003); Laro v. New Hampshire, 259 F.\xc2\xa03d 1 (CA1 2001).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Family and Medical Leave Act of 1993 (FMLA or Act) entitles eligible employees to take up to 12 work weeks of unpaid leave per year. An employee may take leave under the FMLA for: (A) \xe2\x80\x9cthe birth of a son or daughter .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in order to care for such son or daughter,\xe2\x80\x9d (B) the adoption or foster-care placement of a child with the employee, (C) the care of a \xe2\x80\x9cspouse .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. son, daughter, or parent\xe2\x80\x9d with \xe2\x80\x9ca serious health condition,\xe2\x80\x9d and (D) the employee\xe2\x80\x99s own serious health condition when the condition interferes with the employee\xe2\x80\x99s ability to perform at work. \n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72612(a)(1). The Act creates a private right of action to seek both equitable relief and money damages \xe2\x80\x9cagainst any employer (including a public agency) in any Federal or State court of competent jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72617(a)(2). As noted, subparagraph (D) is at issue here.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis Court considered subparagraph (C) in Nevada Dept. of Human Resources v. Hibbs, \n\n538 U.\xc2\xa0S. 721 (2003)\n. Subparagraph (C), like (A) and (B), grants leave for reasons related to family care, and those three provisions are referred to here as the family-care provisions. Hibbs held that Congress could subject the States to suit for violations of subparagraph (C), \xc2\xa72612(a)(1)(C). That holding rested on evidence that States had family-leave policies that differentiated on the basis of sex and that States administered even neutral family-leave policies in ways that discriminated on the basis of sex. See id., at 730\xe2\x80\x93732. Subparagraph (D), the self-care provision, was not at issue in Hibbs.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioner Daniel Coleman was employed by the Court of Appeals of the State of Maryland. When Coleman requested sick leave, he was informed he would be terminated if he did not resign. Coleman then sued the state court in the United States District Court for the District of Maryland, alleging, inter alia, that his employer violated the FMLA by failing to provide him with self-care leave.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe District Court dismissed the suit on the basis that the Maryland Court of Appeals, as an entity of a sovereign State, was immune from the suit for damages. The parties do not dispute the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling that the Maryland Court of Appeals is an entity or instrumentality of the State for purposes of sovereign immunity. The District Court concluded the FMLA\xe2\x80\x99s self-care provision did not validly abrogate the State\xe2\x80\x99s immunity from suit. App. to Pet. for Cert. 15\xe2\x80\x9320. The Court of Appeals for the Fourth Circuit affirmed, reasoning that, unlike the family-care provision at issue in Hibbs, the self-care provision was not directed at an identified pattern of gender-based discrimination and was not congruent and proportional to any pattern of sex-based discrimination on the part of States. 626 F.\xc2\xa03d 187. Certiorari was granted. 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2011).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA foundational premise of the federal system is that States, as sovereigns, are immune from suits for damages, save as they elect to waive that defense. See Kimel v. Florida Bd. of Regents, \n\n528 U.\xc2\xa0S. 62\n\xe2\x80\x9373 (2000); Alden v. Maine, \n\n527 U.\xc2\xa0S. 706 (1999)\n. As an exception to this principle, Congress may abrogate the States\xe2\x80\x99 immunity from suit pursuant to its powers under \xc2\xa75 of the \n \nFourteenth Amendment. See, e.g., Fitzpatrick v. Bitzer, \n\n427 U.\xc2\xa0S. 445 (1976)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress must \xe2\x80\x9cmak[e] its intention to abrogate unmistakably clear in the language of the statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Hibbs, 538 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 726. On this point the Act does express the clear purpose to abrogate the States\xe2\x80\x99 immunity. Ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9cThe clarity of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent\xe2\x80\x9d to abrogate the States\xe2\x80\x99 immunity from suits for damages under the FMLA \xe2\x80\x9cis not fairly debatable\xe2\x80\x9d). Congress subjected any \xe2\x80\x9cpublic agency\xe2\x80\x9d to suit under the FMLA, \n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72617(a)(2), and a \xe2\x80\x9cpublic agency\xe2\x80\x9d is defined to include both \xe2\x80\x9cthe government of a State or political subdivision thereof\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cany agency of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a State, or a political subdivision of a State,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7203(x), 2611(4)(A)(iii).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe question then becomes whether the self-care provision and its attempt to abrogate the States\xe2\x80\x99 immunity are a valid exercise of congressional power under \xc2\xa75 of the \n \nFourteenth Amendment. Section 5 grants Congress the power \xe2\x80\x9cto enforce\xe2\x80\x9d the substantive guarantees of \xc2\xa71 of the Amendment by \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate legislation.\xe2\x80\x9d The power to enforce \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98includes the authority both to remedy and to deter violation[s] of rights guaranteed\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d by \xc2\xa71. See Board of Trustees of Univ. of Ala. v. Garrett, \n\n531 U.\xc2\xa0S. 356, \n365 (2001)\n (quoting Kimel, supra, at 81). To ensure Congress\xe2\x80\x99 enforcement powers under \xc2\xa75 remain enforcement powers, as envisioned by the ratifiers of the Amendment, rather than powers to redefine the substantive scope of \xc2\xa71, Congress \xe2\x80\x9cmust tailor\xe2\x80\x9d legislation enacted under \xc2\xa75 \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98to rem- edy or prevent\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cconduct transgressing the \n \nFourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s substantive provisions.\xe2\x80\x9d Florida Prepaid Postsecondary Ed. Expense Bd. v. College Savings Bank, \n\n527 U.\xc2\xa0S. 627, \n639 (1999)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhether a congressional Act passed under \xc2\xa75 can impose monetary liability upon States requires an assessment of both the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98evil\xe2\x80\x99 or \xe2\x80\x98wrong\xe2\x80\x99 that Congress intended to remedy,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., and the means Congress adopted to address that evil, see City of Boerne v. Flores, \n\n521 U.\xc2\xa0S. 507, \n520 (1997)\n. Legislation enacted under \xc2\xa75 must be targeted at \xe2\x80\x9cconduct transgressing the \n \nFourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s substantive provisions.\xe2\x80\x9d Florida Prepaid, supra, at 639; see Kimel, supra, at 88; City of Boerne, 521 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 525. And \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here must be a congruence and proportionality between the injury to be prevented or remedied and the means adopted to that end.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 520.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUnder this analysis Hibbs permitted employees to recover damages from States for violations of subparagraph (C). In enacting the FMLA, Congress relied upon evidence of a well-documented pattern of sex-based discrimination in family-leave policies. States had facially discriminatory leave policies that granted longer periods of leave to women than to men. 538 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 730\xe2\x80\x93731. States also administered facially neutral family-leave policies in gender-biased ways. Id., at 732. These practices reflected what Congress found to be a \xe2\x80\x9cpervasive sex-role stereotype that caring for family members is women\xe2\x80\x99s work,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 731, a stereotype to which even this Court had succumbed in earlier times, id., at 729. Faced with \xe2\x80\x9cthe States\xe2\x80\x99 record of unconstitutional participation in, and fostering of, gender-based discrimination in the administration of leave benefits,\xe2\x80\x9d Hibbs concluded that requiring state employers to give all employees the opportunity to take family-care leave was \xe2\x80\x9cnarrowly targeted at the faultline between work and family\xe2\x80\x94precisely where sex-based overgeneralization has been and remains strongest.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 735, 738.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe same cannot be said for requiring the States to give all employees the opportunity to take self-care leave. Petitioner advances three arguments for allowing employees to recover damages from States that violate the FMLA\xe2\x80\x99s self-care provision: The self-care provision standing alone addresses sex discrimination and sex stereotyping; the provision is a necessary adjunct to the family-care provision sustained in Hibbs; and the provision eases the burden on single parents. But what the family-care provisions have to support them, the self-care provision lacks, namely evidence of a pattern of state constitutional violations accompanied by a remedy drawn in narrow terms to address or prevent those violations.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tStanding alone, the self-care provision is not a valid abrogation of the States\xe2\x80\x99 immunity from suit. When the FMLA was enacted, \xe2\x80\x9cninety-five percent of full-time state- and local-government employees were covered by paid sick leave plans and ninety-six percent of such employees likewise enjoyed short-term disability protection.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for States of Texas et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 13\xe2\x80\x9314 (hereinafter Texas Brief) (citing Bureau of Labor Statistics, U.\xc2\xa0S. Dept. of Labor, Employee Benefits in State and Local Governments 17\xe2\x80\x9326 (1994) (hereinafter BLS Rept.)). The evidence did not suggest States had facially discriminatory self-care leave policies or that they administered neutral self-care leave policies in a discriminatory way. And there is scant evidence in the legislative history of a purported stereotype harbored by employers that women take self-care leave more often than men. Congress considered evidence that \xe2\x80\x9cmen and women are out on medical leave approximately equally.\xe2\x80\x9d H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 101\xe2\x80\x9328, pt. 1, p. 15 (1989) (hereinafter H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep.). Nothing in the record shows employers formulated self-care leave policies based on a contrary view.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWithout widespread evidence of sex discrimination or sex stereotyping in the administration of sick leave, it is apparent that the congressional purpose in enacting the self-care provision is unrelated to these supposed wrongs. The legislative history of the self-care provision reveals a concern for the economic burdens on the employee and the employee\xe2\x80\x99s family resulting from illness-related job loss and a concern for discrimination on the basis of illness, not sex. See, e.g., S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 103\xe2\x80\x933, pp. 11\xe2\x80\x9312 (1993); H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep., at 23. In the findings pertinent to the self-care provision, the statute makes no reference to any distinction on the basis of sex. See \n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72601(a)(4) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]here is inadequate job security for employees who have serious health conditions that prevent them from working for temporary periods\xe2\x80\x9d). By contrast, with regard to family care Congress invoked concerns related to gender. See \xc2\xa72601(a)(5) (\xe2\x80\x9c[D]ue to the nature of the roles of men and women in our society, the primary responsibility for family caretaking often falls on women, and such responsibility affects the working lives of women more than it affects the working lives of men\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is true the self-care provision offers some women a benefit by allowing them to take leave for pregnancy-related illnesses; but as a remedy, the provision is not congruent and proportional to any identified constitutional violations. At the time of the FMLA\xe2\x80\x99s enactment, \xe2\x80\x9cninety-five percent\xe2\x80\x9d of state employees had paid sick-leave plans at work, and \xe2\x80\x9cninety-six percent\xe2\x80\x9d had short-term disability protection. Texas Brief 13\xe2\x80\x9314 (citing BLS Rept. 17\xe2\x80\x9326). State employees presumably could take leave for pregnancy-related illnesses under these policies, and Congress did not document any pattern of States excluding pregnancy-related illnesses from sick-leave or disability-leave policies. \xe2\x80\x9cCongress .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. said nothing about the existence or adequacy of state remedies.\xe2\x80\x9d Florida Prepaid, 527 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 644. It follows that abrogating the States\xe2\x80\x99 immunity from suits for damages for failure to give self-care leave is not a congruent and proportional remedy if the existing state leave policies would have sufficed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs an alternative justification for the self-care provision, it has been suggested that the provision is a necessary adjunct to the family-care provisions. Petitioner argues that employers may assume women are more likely to take family-care leave than men and that the FMLA therefore offers up to 12 weeks of leave for family care and self care combined. According to petitioner, when the self-care provision is coupled with the family-care provisions, the self-care provision could reduce the difference in the expected number of weeks of FMLA leave that different employees take for different reasons.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe fact that self-care leave could have this effect does not mean that it would. If, for example, women are expected to take 20 days of family-care leave per year and men to take 10, and women and men are each expected to take 5 days of self-care leave per year, the difference in the expected number of days of leave and cost to the employer remains the same regardless of the availability of self-care leave. Congress made no findings, and received no specific testimony, to suggest the availability of self-care leave equalizes the expected amount of FMLA leave men and women will take. Even if women take family-care leave more often than men, men do not take self-care leave more often than women; and there is little evidence that employers assume they do. See H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep., at 15. Petitioner suggests that some women will be expected to take all 12 weeks of leave under the FMLA for family-care purposes, and therefore that any amount of self-care leave taken by men will diminish the difference in the amount of FMLA leave taken by men and women. But there is little evidence to support petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s assumption about the magnitude of women\xe2\x80\x99s expected FMLA leave for family-care purposes. And men are only expected to take five days of sick leave per year, see ibid., so the self-care provision diminishes the difference in expected leave time by a maximum of five days. And that is only to the extent women use all their available FMLA leave for family-care reasons. Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s overly complicated argument about how the self-care provision works in tandem with the family-care provisions is unconvincing and in the end does not comply with the clear requirements of City of Boerne.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn addition petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s first defense of the self-care provision contradicts his second defense of the provision. In the first defense, the Court is told employers assume women take more self-care leave than men. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 10\xe2\x80\x9312. In the second defense, the Court is told the self-care provision provides an incentive to hire women that will counteract the incentives created by the family-care provisions because employers assume women take more family-care leave than men. But if the first defense is correct, the second defense is wrong. In other words, if employers assume women take self-care leave more often than men (the first defense), a self-care provision will not provide an incentive to hire women. To the contrary, the self-care provision would provide an incentive to discriminate against women.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThere is \xe2\x80\x9clittle support in the record for the concerns that supposedly animated\xe2\x80\x9d the self-care provision. Florida Prepaid, supra, at 639. Only supposition and conjecture support the contention that the self-care provision is necessary to make the family-care provisions effective. The evidence documented in support of the self-care provision is, to a large degree, unrelated to sex discrimination, or to the administration of the family-care provisions. See supra, at 7. Congress made no findings and did not cite specific or detailed evidence to show how the self-care provision is necessary to the family-care provisions or how it reduces an employer\xe2\x80\x99s incentives to discriminate against women. And \xe2\x80\x9cCongress .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. said nothing about the existence or adequacy of state\xe2\x80\x9d sick-leave policies. Florida Prepaid, supra, at 644; see Garrett, 531 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 373. Under this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents, more is required to sub- ject unconsenting States to suits for damages, particularly where, as here, it is for violations of a provision (the self-care provision) that is a supposedly preventive step in aid of already preventive provisions (the family-care provisions). See Florida Prepaid, 527 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 642 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he legislative record still provides little support for the prop- osition that Congress sought to remedy a \n \nFourteenth Amendment violation in enacting the Patent Remedy Act\xe2\x80\x9d); Kimel, 528 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 88 (\xe2\x80\x9cOne means by which we have made such a determination .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is by examining the legislative record containing the reasons for Congress\xe2\x80\x99 action\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe \xe2\x80\x9cfew fleeting references\xe2\x80\x9d to how self-care leave is inseparable from family-care leave fall short of what is required for a valid abrogation of States\xe2\x80\x99 immunity from suits for damages. Florida Prepaid, supra, at 644. These \xe2\x80\x9cisolated sentences clipped from floor debates\xe2\x80\x9d and testimony, Kimel, supra, at 89, are stated as conclusions, unsupported by evidence or findings about how the self-care provision interrelates to the family-care provisions to counteract employers\xe2\x80\x99 incentives to discriminate against women. Congress must rely on more than abstract generalities to subject the States to suits for damages. Otherwise, Congress could choose to combat the purported effects of the family-care provisions by allowing employees to sue States that do not permit employees to take vacation time under the FMLA. There is nothing in particular about self-care leave, as opposed to leave for any personal reason, that connects it to gender discrimination. And when the issue, as here, is whether subparagraph (D) can abrogate a State\xe2\x80\x99s immunity from damages, there is no sufficient nexus, or indeed any demonstrated nexus, between self-care leave and gender discrimination by state employers. Documented discrimination against women in the general workplace is a persistent, unfortunate reality, and, we must assume, a still prevalent wrong. An explicit purpose of the Congress in adopting the FMLA was to improve workplace conditions for women. See 29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72601(b)(4), (5). But States may not be subject to suits for damages based on violations of a comprehensive statute unless Congress has identified a specific pattern of constitutional violations by state employers. See City of Boerne, 521 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 532.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s last defense of the self-care provision is that the provision helps single parents retain their jobs when they become ill. This, however, does not explain how the provision remedies or prevents constitutional violations. The fact that most single parents happen to be women, see, e.g., S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 103\xe2\x80\x933, at 7, demonstrates, at most, that the self-care provision was directed at remedying employers\xe2\x80\x99 neutral leave restrictions which have a disparate effect on women. \xe2\x80\x9cAlthough disparate impact may be relevant evidence of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. discrimination .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. such evidence alone is insufficient [to prove a constitutional violation] even where the \n \nFourteenth Amendment subjects state action to strict scrutiny.\xe2\x80\x9d Garrett, supra, at 372\xe2\x80\x93373; see Tuan Anh Nguyen v. INS, \n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 53\n\xe2\x80\x9383 (2001) (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., dissenting); Washington v. Davis, \n\n426 U.\xc2\xa0S. 229, \n239 (1976)\n. To the extent, then, that the self-care provision addresses neutral leave policies with a disparate impact on women, it is not directed at a pattern of constitutional violations. Because, moreover, it is \xe2\x80\x9cunlikely that many of the [neutral leave policies] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. affected by\xe2\x80\x9d the self-care provision are unconstitutional, \xe2\x80\x9cthe scope of the [self-care provision is] out of proportion to its supposed remedial or preventive objectives.\xe2\x80\x9d Kimel, supra, at 82; see City of Boerne, supra, at 519.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOf course, a State need not assert its \n \nEleventh Amendment immunity from suits for damages. See, e.g., Sossamon v. Texas, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 5) (\xe2\x80\x9cA State .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. may choose to waive its immunity in federal court at its pleasure\xe2\x80\x9d). Discrimination against women is contrary to the public policy of the State of Maryland, see, e.g., Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s Fair Employment Practices Act, Md. State Govt. Code Ann. \xc2\xa720\xe2\x80\x93606 (Lexis 2009), and the State has conceded that the Act is good social policy, see Tr. of Oral Arg. 35. If the State agrees with petitioner that damages liability for violations of the self-care provision is necessary to combat discrimination against women, the State may waive its immunity or create a parallel state law cause of action. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs a consequence of our constitutional design, money damages are the exception when sovereigns are defendants. See, e.g., Pennhurst State School and Hospital v. Halderman, \n\n451 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1, \n29 (1981)\n. Subjecting States to suits for damages pursuant to \xc2\xa75 requires more than a theory for why abrogating the States\xe2\x80\x99 immunity aids in, or advances, a stated congressional purpose. To abrogate the States\xe2\x80\x99 immunity from suits for damages under \xc2\xa75, Congress must identify a pattern of constitutional violations and tailor a remedy congruent and proportional to the documented violations. It failed to do so when it allowed employees to sue States for violations of the FMLA\xe2\x80\x99s self-care provision. The judgment of the Court of Appeals is affirmed.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSection 1 of the \n \nFourteenth Amendment provides: \xe2\x80\x9cNo State shall .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. deny to any person within its jurisdiction the equal protection of the laws.\xe2\x80\x9d Section 5 grants Congress the \xe2\x80\x9cpower to enforce, by appropriate legislation, the provisions of this article.\xe2\x80\x9d Congress\xe2\x80\x99 \xc2\xa75 enforcement power includes the authority to remedy and deter violations of \xc2\xa71\xe2\x80\x99s substantive guarantees by prohibiting conduct \xe2\x80\x9cnot itself forbidden by the Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s text.\xe2\x80\x9d Kimel v. Flor- ida Bd. of Regents, \n\n528 U.\xc2\xa0S. 62, \n81 (2000)\n. \xe2\x80\x9cIn other words, Congress may enact so-called prophylactic leg- islation that proscribes facially constitutional conduct, in order to prevent and deter unconstitutional conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d Nevada Dept. of Human Resources v. Hibbs, \n\n538 U.\xc2\xa0S. 721\n\xe2\x80\x93728 (2003).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Family and Medical Leave Act of 1993 (FMLA or Act) entitles eligible employees to 12 weeks of job-secured leave during any 12-month period: (A) to care for a newborn son or daughter; (B) to care for a newly adopted son or daughter; (C) to care for a spouse, child, or parent with a serious health condition; or (D) because the employee has a serious health condition that makes her unable to perform the functions of her position. \n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72612(a)(1).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEven accepting this Court\xe2\x80\x99s view of the scope of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 power under \xc2\xa75 of the \n \nFourteenth Amendment, I would hold that the self-care provision, \xc2\xa72612(a)(1)(D), validly enforces the right to be free from gender discrimination in the workplace.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSection 5 legislation \xe2\x80\x9cmust be targeted at conduct transgressing the \n \nFourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s substantive provisions,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 5 (internal quotation marks omitted), \xe2\x80\x9c[a]nd \xe2\x80\x98[t]here must be a congruence and proportionality between the injury to be prevented or remedied and the means adopted to that end.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting City of Boerne v. Flores, \n\n521 U.\xc2\xa0S. 507, \n520 (1997)\n). The first step of the now-familiar Boerne inquiry calls for identification of the constitutional right Congress sought to enforce. See, e.g., Tennessee v. Lane, \n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 509, \n522 (2004)\n. The FMLA\xe2\x80\x99s self-care provision, Maryland asserts, trains not on the right to be free from gender discrimination, but on an \xe2\x80\x9cequal protection right to be free from irrational state employment discrimination based on a medical condition.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 14. The plurality agrees, concluding that the self-care provision reveals \xe2\x80\x9ca concern for discrimination on the basis of illness, not sex.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 7. In so declaring, the plurality undervalues the language, purpose, and history of the FMLA, and the self-care provision\xe2\x80\x99s important role in the statutory scheme. As well, the plurality underplays the main theme of our decision in Hibbs: \xe2\x80\x9cThe FMLA aims to protect the right to be free from gender-based discrimination in the workplace.\xe2\x80\x9d 538 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 728.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI begin with the text of the statute, which repeatedly emphasizes gender discrimination. One of the FMLA\xe2\x80\x99s stated purposes is to \xe2\x80\x9centitle employees to take reasonable leave,\xe2\x80\x9d \n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72601(b)(2), \xe2\x80\x9cin a manner that, consistent with the Equal Protection Clause of the \n \nFourteenth Amendment, minimizes the potential for employment dis- crimination on the basis of sex by ensuring generally that leave is available for eligible medical reasons (including maternity-related disability) and for compelling family reasons, on a gender-neutral basis.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72601(b)(4). Another identified aim is \xe2\x80\x9cto promote the goal of equal employment opportunity for women and men, pursuant to [the Equal Protection Clause].\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72601(b)(5). \xe2\x80\x9c[E]mployment standards that apply to one gender only,\xe2\x80\x9d Congress expressly found, \xe2\x80\x9chave serious potential for encouraging employers to discriminate against employees and applicants for employment who are of that gender.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72601(a)(6).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe FMLA\xe2\x80\x99s purpose and legislative history reinforce the conclusion that the FMLA, in its entirety, is directed at sex discrimination. Indeed, the FMLA was originally envisioned as a way to guarantee\xe2\x80\x94without singling out women or pregnancy\xe2\x80\x94that pregnant women would not lose their jobs when they gave birth. The self-care provision achieves that aim.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA brief history is in order. In his 1982 congressional campaign, then-candidate Howard Berman pledged to introduce legislation similar to the California law challenged in California Fed. Sav. & Loan Assn. v. Guerra, \n\n479 U.\xc2\xa0S. 272 (1987)\n. S. Wisensale, Family Leave Policy: The Political Economy of Work and Family in America 134 (2001) (hereinafter Wisensale). \n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe California law sharply divided women\xe2\x80\x99s rights ad- vocates. \xe2\x80\x9cEqual-treatment\xe2\x80\x9d feminists asserted it violated the Pregnancy Discrimination Act\xe2\x80\x99s (PDA) commitment to treating pregnancy the same as other disabilities.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t It did so by requiring leave only for disability caused by pregnancy and childbirth, thereby treating pregnancy as sui generis. See also Williams, Equality\xe2\x80\x99s Riddle: Pregnancy and the Equal Treatment/Special Treatment Debate, 13 N. Y. U. Rev. L. & Soc. Change 325, 326\xe2\x80\x93328 (1984\xe2\x80\x931985) (hereinafter Williams) (discussing disagreement).\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhile California Fed. moved through the lower federal courts, equal-treatment feminists began work on a gender-neutral leave model, which eventually became the FMLA. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThough this Court, in California Fed., eventually upheld California\xe2\x80\x99s pregnancy-only leave policy as not preempted by the PDA, equal-treatment feminists continued to believe that viewing pregnancy as sui generis perpetuated widespread discrimination against women.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t They therefore maintained their commitment to gender-neutral leave. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress agreed. See infra, at 14\xe2\x80\x9315. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBoerne next asks \xe2\x80\x9cwhether Congress had evidence of a pattern of constitutional violations on the part of the States.\xe2\x80\x9d Hibbs, 538 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 729. \n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c[A] state\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to provide pregnancy leave to its employees,\xe2\x80\x9d Maryland responds, is \xe2\x80\x9cnot unconstitutional.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 23 (citing Geduldig v. Aiello, \n\n417 U.\xc2\xa0S. 484, \n495 (1974)\n). Aiello\xe2\x80\x99s footnote 20 proclaimed that discrimination on the basis of pregnancy is not discrimi- nation on the basis of sex. In my view, this case is a fit occasion to revisit that conclusion. Footnote 20 reads:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cThe dissenting opinion to the contrary, this case is .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a far cry from cases like Reed v. Reed, \n\n404 U.\xc2\xa0S. 71 (1971)\n, and Frontiero v. Richardson, \n\n411 U.\xc2\xa0S. 677 (1973)\n, involving discrimination based upon gender as such. The California insurance program does not exclude anyone from benefit eligibility because of gender but merely removes one physical condition\xe2\x80\x94pregnancy\xe2\x80\x94from the list of compensable disabili- ties. While it is true that only women can become pregnant, it does not follow that every legislative classification concerning pregnancy is a sex-based classification .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cThe lack of identity between the excluded disability and gender as such under this insurance program becomes clear upon the most cursory analysis. The program divides potential recipients into two groups\xe2\x80\x94pregnant women and nonpregnant persons. While the first group is exclusively female, the second includes members of both sexes. The fiscal and actuarial benefits of the program thus accrue to members of both sexes.\xe2\x80\x9d 417 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 496, n.\xc2\xa020.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFirst, \xe2\x80\x9c[a]s an abstract statement,\xe2\x80\x9d it is \xe2\x80\x9csimply false\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9ca classification based on pregnancy is gender-neutral.\xe2\x80\x9d Bray v. Alexandria Women\xe2\x80\x99s Health Clinic, \n\n506 U.\xc2\xa0S. 263, \n327 (1993)\n (Stevens, J., dissenting). Rather, discriminating on the basis of pregnancy \xe2\x80\x9c[b]y definition .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. discriminates on account of sex; for it is the capacity to become pregnant which primarily differentiates the fe- male from the male.\xe2\x80\x9d General Elec. Co. v. Gilbert, \n\n429 U.\xc2\xa0S. 125\n\xe2\x80\x93162 (1976) (Stevens, J., dissenting). See also Issacharoff & Rosenblum, Women and the Workplace: Accommodating the Demands of Pregnancy, 94 Colum. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 2154, 2180 (1994) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is precisely because pregnancy is a condition unique to women that the exclusion of pregnancy from disability coverage is a sex-based classification\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis reality is well illustrated by the facts of Aiello. The California disability-insurance program at issue granted disability benefits for virtually any conceivable work disability, including those arising from cosmetic surgery, skiing accidents, and alcoholism. See Brief for EEOC as Amicus Curiae in Aiello, O. T. 1973, No. 73\xe2\x80\x93640, p.\xc2\xa07. It also compensated men for disabilities caused by ailments and procedures that affected men alone: for example, vasectomies, circumcision, and prostatectomies. See Brief for American Civil Liberties Union et al. as Amici Curiae in id., at 17\xe2\x80\x9318. Only pregnancy was excluded from the definition of disability. See Cal. Un. Ins. Code Ann. \xc2\xa72626 (West 1972); Aiello, 417 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 489. As Justice Brennan insightfully concluded in dissent, \xe2\x80\x9ca limitation is imposed upon the disabilities for which women workers may re- cover, while men receive full compensation for all disabilities suffered .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. Such dissimilar treatment of men and women, on the basis of physical characteristics inextricably linked to one sex, inevitably constitutes sex discrimination.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 501.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSecond, pregnancy provided a central justification for the historic discrimination against women this Court chronicled in Hibbs. See 538 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 729 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] proper discharge of [a woman\xe2\x80\x99s] maternal functions\xe2\x80\x94having in view not merely her own health, but the well-being of the race\xe2\x80\x94justif[ies] legislation to protect her from the greed as well as the passion of man.\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Muller v. Oregon, \n\n208 U.\xc2\xa0S. 412, \n422 (1908)\n; 2d and 3d alterations in Hibbs)). See also Siegel, Employment Equality Under the Preg- nancy Discrimination Act of 1978, 94 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J. 929, 942 (1985) (Pregnancy \xe2\x80\x9cis a biological difference central to the definition of gender roles, one traditionally believed to render women unfit for employment.\xe2\x80\x9d). Relatedly, discrimination against pregnant employees was often \xe2\x80\x9cbased not on the pregnancy itself but on predictions concerning the future behavior of the pregnant woman when her child was born or on views about what her behavior should be.\xe2\x80\x9d Williams 355. See also S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 95\xe2\x80\x93331, p.\xc2\xa03 (1977) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he assumption that women will become pregnant and leave the labor market is at the core of the sex stereotyping resulting in unfavorable disparate treatment of women in the workplace.\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn sum, childbearing is not only a biological function unique to women. It is also inextricably intertwined with employers\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cstereotypical views about women\xe2\x80\x99s commitment to work and their value as employees.\xe2\x80\x9d Hibbs, 538 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 736. Because pregnancy discrimination is in- evitably sex discrimination, and because discrimination against women is tightly interwoven with society\xe2\x80\x99s beliefs about pregnancy and motherhood, I would hold that Aiello was egregiously wrong to declare that discrimination on the basis of pregnancy is not discrimination on the basis of sex.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tC\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBoerne\xe2\x80\x99s third step requires \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a congruence and proportionality between the injury to be prevented or remedied and the means adopted to that end.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 5 (quoting 521 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 520). Section 2612(a)(1)(D), I would conclude, is an appropriate response to pervasive discriminatory treatment of pregnant women. In separating self-care leave for the physical disability following childbirth, \xc2\xa72612(a)(1)(D), which affects only women, from family-care leave for parenting a newborn baby, \xc2\xa72612(a)(1)(A), for which men and women are equally suited, Congress could attack gender discrimination and challenge stereotypes of women as lone childrearers. Cf. Hibbs, 538 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 731 (States\xe2\x80\x99 extended \xe2\x80\x9cmaternity\xe2\x80\x9d leaves, far exceeding a woman\xe2\x80\x99s physical disability following childbirth, were attributable \xe2\x80\x9cto the pervasive sex-role stereotype that caring for family members is women\xe2\x80\x99s work.\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt would make scant sense to provide job-protected leave for a woman to care for a newborn, but not for her recovery from delivery, a miscarriage, or the birth of a stillborn baby. And allowing States to provide no pregnancy-disability leave at all, given that only women can become pregnant, would obviously \xe2\x80\x9cexclude far more women than men from the workplace.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 738.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe plurality\xe2\x80\x99s statement that Congress lacked \xe2\x80\x9cwidespread evidence of sex discrimination .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in the administration of sick leave,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 6, misses the point. So too does the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s observation that state employees likely \xe2\x80\x9ccould take leave for pregnancy-related illnesses\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94presumably severe morning sickness, toxemia, etc.\xe2\x80\x94under paid sick-leave plans, ante, at 7. Congress heard evidence that existing sick-leave plans were inadequate to ensure that women were not fired when they needed to take time out to recover their strength and stamina after childbirth. The self-care provision responds to that evidence by requiring employers to allow leave for \xe2\x80\x9congoing pregnancy, miscarriages, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the need for prenatal care, childbirth, and recovery from childbirth.\xe2\x80\x9d S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 103\xe2\x80\x933, p.\xc2\xa029 (1993).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThat \xc2\xa72612(a)(1)(D) entitles all employees to up to 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave for a serious health condition, rather than singling out pregnancy or childbirth, does not mean that the provision lacks the requisite congruence and proportionality to the identified constitutional violations. As earlier noted, supra, at 6\xe2\x80\x937, Congress made plain its rationale for the prescription\xe2\x80\x99s broader compass: Congress sought to ward off the unconstitutional discrimination it believed would attend a pregnancy-only leave requirement. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTwo additional points. First, this Court reached a different conclusion than the one I reach here in Board of Trustees of Univ. of Ala. v. Garrett, \n\n531 U.\xc2\xa0S. 356 (2001)\n, and Kimel, \n\n528 U.\xc2\xa0S. 62\n. In those cases, as we observed in Hibbs, we reviewed statutes targeting disability and age discrimination, respectively. Neither disability nor age is a suspect classification under this Court\xe2\x80\x99s Equal Protection Clause jurisprudence; States may discriminate on the basis of disability or age as long as the classification is rationally related to a legitimate state interest. See Garrett, 531 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 366\xe2\x80\x93367; Kimel, 528 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 83\xe2\x80\x9384. Therefore, for the statutes to be responsive to or designed to prevent unconstitutional discrimination, Congress needed to rely on a pattern of irrational state discrimination on the basis of disability or age. See Garrett, 531 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 368; Kimel, 528 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 89. Here, however, Congress homed in on gender discrimination, which triggers heightened review. See United States v. Virginia, \n\n518 U.\xc2\xa0S. 515, \n531 (1996)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cParties who seek to defend gender-based government action must demonstrate an exceedingly persuasive justification for that action.\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). \xe2\x80\x9c[I]t was [therefore] easier for Congress to show a pattern of state constitutional violations.\xe2\x80\x9d Hibbs, 538 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 736.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe plurality pays scant attention to the overarching aim of the FMLA: to make it feasible for women to work while sustaining family life. Over the course of eight years, Congress considered the problem of workplace discrimination against women, and devised the FMLA to reduce sex-based inequalities in leave programs. Essential to its design, Congress assiduously avoided a legislative package that, overall, was or would be seen as geared to women only. The self-care provision is a key part of that endeavor, and, in my view, a valid exercise of congressional power under \xc2\xa75 of the \n \nFourteenth Amendment.
