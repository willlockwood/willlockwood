MARRAMA v. CITIZENS BANK OF MASSACHUSETTS et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the first circuit

\n

No. 05\xe2\x80\x93996.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 6, 2006\xe2\x80\x94Decided February 21, 2007

\n

In filing his petition under Chapter 7 of the Bankruptcy Code, petitioner Marrama misrepresented the value of his Maine property and that he had not transferred it during the preceding year. Respondent DeGiacomo, the trustee of Marrama\xe2\x80\x99s estate, stated his intention to recover the Maine property as an estate asset. Thereafter, Marrama sought to convert the proceeding to Chapter 13, but the trustee and respondent bank, Marrama\xe2\x80\x99s principal creditor, objected, contending that the request to convert was made in bad faith and would constitute an abuse of the bankruptcy process. The Bankruptcy Judge denied Marrama\xe2\x80\x99s request, finding bad faith. Affirming, the First Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s Bankruptcy Appellate Panel rejected Marrama\xe2\x80\x99s argument that he had an absolute right to convert under \xc2\xa7706(a) of the Bankruptcy Code, which provides that a Chapter 7 debtor \xe2\x80\x9cmay convert a case\xe2\x80\x9d so long as it has not been converted previously, and that a waiver of the right to convert is unenforceable. The First Circuit also rejected that argument, emphasizing, inter alia, that a bankruptcy court has the authority to dismiss a Chapter 13 petition based on a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s bad faith, and that a first-time motion to convert a Chapter 7 case to Chapter 13 should not be treated differently from the filing of a Chapter 13 petition in the first instance.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Marrama forfeited his right to proceed under Chapter 13. The broad description of the right to convert as \xe2\x80\x9cabsolute\xe2\x80\x9d in Senate and House Committee Reports fails to give full effect to the express limitation of \xc2\xa7706(d), which provides that \xe2\x80\x9ca case may not be converted to a case under another chapter of this title unless the debtor may be a debtor under such chapter.\xe2\x80\x9d That text expressly conditioned Marrama\xe2\x80\x99s right to convert on his ability to qualify as a Chapter 13 \xe2\x80\x9cdebtor.\xe2\x80\x9d Marrama does not qualify as such a debtor under \xc2\xa71307(c), which provides that a Chapter 13 proceeding may be either dismissed or converted to a Chapter 7 proceeding \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause.\xe2\x80\x9d Bankruptcy courts routinely treat dismissal for prepetition bad-faith conduct as implicitly authorized by the words \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause,\xe2\x80\x9d and a ruling that an individual\xe2\x80\x99s Chapter 13 case should be dismissed or converted to Chapter 7 because of bad faith is tantamount to a ruling that the individual does not qualify as a Chapter 13 debtor. Congress gave \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98honest but unfortunate debtor[s]\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Grogan v. Garner, 498 U. S. 279, 287, the chance to repay their debts should they acquire the means to do so, and \xc2\xa7706(a) protects a debtor from being forced to waive that right. However, a provision protecting a borrower from waiver is not a shield against forfeiture. Neither \xc2\xa7706 nor \xc2\xa71307(c) limits a court\xe2\x80\x99s authority to take appropriate action in response to fraudulent conduct by the atypical litigant who has demonstrated that he is not entitled to the relief available to the typical debtor. On the contrary, bankruptcy judges\xe2\x80\x99 broad authority to take necessary or appropriate action \xe2\x80\x9cto prevent an abuse of process\xe2\x80\x9d described in Code \xc2\xa7105(a) is adequate to authorize an immediate denial of a \xc2\xa7706 motion to convert in lieu of a conversion order that merely postpones the allowance of equivalent relief and may give a debtor an opportunity to take action prejudicial to creditors. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

430 F.\xc2\xa03d 474, affirmed.

ROBERT LOUIS MARRAMA, PETITIONER v. CITIZENS\nBANK OF MASSACHUSETTS et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the first circuit

\n

[February 21, 2007]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The principal purpose of the Bankruptcy Code is to grant a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98fresh start\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98honest but unfortunate debtor.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Grogan v. Garner, 498 U. S. 279, 286, 287 (1991). Both Chapter 7 and Chapter 13 of the Code permit an insolvent individual to discharge certain unpaid debts toward that end. Chapter 7 authorizes a discharge of prepetition debts following the liquidation of the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s assets by a bankruptcy trustee, who then distributes the proceeds to creditors. Chapter 13 authorizes an individual with regular income to obtain a discharge after the successful completion of a payment plan approved by the bankruptcy court. Under Chapter 7 the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s non-exempt assets are controlled by the bankruptcy trustee; under Chapter 13 the debtor retains possession of his property. A proceeding that is commenced under Chapter 7 may be converted to a Chapter 13 proceeding and vice versa. 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7706(a), 1307(a) and (c).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0An issue that has arisen with disturbing frequency is whether a debtor who acts in bad faith prior to, or in the course of, filing a Chapter 13 petition by, for example, fraudulently concealing significant assets, thereby forfeits his right to obtain Chapter 13 relief. The issue may arise at the outset of a Chapter 13 case in response to a motion by creditors or by the United States trustee either to dismiss the case or to convert it to Chapter 7, see \xc2\xa71307(c). It also may arise in a Chapter 7 case when a debtor files a motion under \xc2\xa7706(a) to convert to Chapter 13. In the former context, despite the absence of any statutory provision specifically addressing the issue, the federal courts are virtually unanimous that prepetition bad-faith conduct may cause a forfeiture of any right to proceed with a Chapter 13 case.[Footnote 1] In the latter context, however, some courts have suggested that even a bad-faith debtor has an absolute right to convert at least one Chapter 7 proceeding into a Chapter 13 case even though the case will thereafter be dismissed or immediately returned to Chapter 7.[Footnote 2] We granted certiorari to decide whether the Code mandates that procedural anomaly. 547 U. S. ____ (2006).

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On March 11, 2003, petitioner, Robert Marrama, filed a voluntary petition under Chapter 7, thereby creating an estate consisting of all his property \xe2\x80\x9cwherever located and by whomever held.\xe2\x80\x9d 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7541(a). Respondent Mark DeGiacomo is the trustee of that estate. Respondent Citizens Bank of Massachusetts (hereinafter Bank) is the principal creditor.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In verified schedules attached to his petition, Marrama made a number of statements about his principal asset, a house in Maine, that were misleading or inaccurate. For instance, while he disclosed that he was the sole beneficiary of the trust that owned the property, he listed its value as zero. He also denied that he had transferred any property other than in the ordinary course of business during the year preceding the filing of his petition. Neither statement was true. In fact, the Maine property had substantial value, and Marrama had transferred it into the newly created trust for no consideration seven months prior to filing his Chapter 13 petition. Marrama later admitted that the purpose of the transfer was to protect the property from his creditors.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After Marrama\xe2\x80\x99s examination at the meeting of creditors, see 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7341, the trustee advised Marrama\xe2\x80\x99s counsel that he intended to recover the Maine property as an asset of the estate. Thereafter, Marrama filed a \xe2\x80\x9cVerified Notice of Conversion to Chapter 13.\xe2\x80\x9d Pursuant to Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 1017(c)(2), the notice of conversion was treated as a motion to convert, to which both the trustee and the Bank filed objections. Relying primarily on Marrama\xe2\x80\x99s attempt to conceal the Maine property from his creditors,[Footnote 3] the trustee contended that the request to convert was made in bad faith and would constitute an abuse of the bankruptcy process. The Bank opposed the conversion on similar grounds.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the hearing on the conversion issue, Marrama explained through counsel that his misstatements about the Maine property were attributable to \xe2\x80\x9cscrivener\xe2\x80\x99s error,\xe2\x80\x9d that he had originally filed under Chapter 7 rather than Chapter 13 because he was then unemployed, and that he had recently become employed and was therefore eligible to proceed under Chapter 13.[Footnote 4] The Bankruptcy Judge rejected these arguments, ruling that there is no \xe2\x80\x9cOops\xe2\x80\x9d defense to the concealment of assets and that the facts established a \xe2\x80\x9cbad faith\xe2\x80\x9d case. App. 34a\xe2\x80\x9335a. The judge denied the request for conversion.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Marrama\xe2\x80\x99s principal argument on appeal to the Bankruptcy Appellate Panel for the First Circuit[Footnote 5] was that he had an absolute right to convert his case from Chapter 7 to Chapter 13 under the plain language of \xc2\xa7706(a) of the Code. The panel affirmed the decision of the Bankruptcy Court. It construed \xc2\xa7706(a), when read in connection with other provisions of the Code and the Bankruptcy Rules, as creating a right to convert a case from Chapter 7 to Chapter 13 that \xe2\x80\x9cis absolute only in the absence of extreme circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d In\xc2\xa0re Marrama, 313 B.\xc2\xa0R. 525, 531 (2004). In concluding that the record disclosed such circumstances, the panel relied on Marrama\xe2\x80\x99s failure to describe the transfer of the Maine residence into the revocable trust, his attempt to obtain a homestead exemption on rental property in Massachusetts, and his nondisclosure of an anticipated tax refund.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On appeal from the panel, the Court of Appeals for the First Circuit also rejected the argument that \xc2\xa7706(a) gives a Chapter 7 debtor an absolute right to convert to Chapter 13. In addition to emphasizing that the statute uses the word \xe2\x80\x9cmay\xe2\x80\x9d rather than \xe2\x80\x9cshall,\xe2\x80\x9d the court added:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIn construing subsection 706(a), it is important to bear in mind that the bankruptcy court has unquestioned authority to dismiss a chapter 13 petition\xe2\x80\x94as distinguished from converting the case to chapter 13\xe2\x80\x94based upon a showing of \xe2\x80\x98bad faith\xe2\x80\x99 on the part of the debtor. We can discern neither a theoretical nor a practical reason that Congress would have chosen to treat a first-time motion to convert a chapter 7 case to chapter 13 under subsection 706(a) differently from the filing of a chapter 13 petition in the first instance.\xe2\x80\x9d In\xc2\xa0re Marrama, 430 F.\xc2\xa03d 474, 479 (2005) (citations omitted).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While other Courts of Appeals and bankruptcy appellate panels have refused to recognize any \xe2\x80\x9cbad faith\xe2\x80\x9d exception to the conversion right created by \xc2\xa7706(a), see n.\xc2\xa02, supra, we conclude that the courts in this case correctly held that Marrama forfeited his right to proceed under Chapter 13.

\n

II

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The two provisions of the Bankruptcy Code most relevant to our resolution of the issue are subsections (a) and (d) of 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7706, which provide:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(a) The debtor may convert a case under this chapter to a case under chapter 11, 12, or 13 of this title at any time, if the case has not been converted under section 1112, 1208, or 1307 of this title. Any waiver of the right to convert a case under this subsection is unenforceable.

\n

\n\n \xe2\x80\x9c(d) Notwithstanding any other provision of this section, a case may not be converted to a case under another chapter of this title unless the debtor may be a debtor under such chapter.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner contends that subsection (a) creates an unqualified right of conversion. He seeks support from language in both the House and Senate Committee Reports on the provision. The Senate Report stated:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cSubsection (a) of this section gives the debtor the one-time absolute right of conversion of a liquidation case to a reorganization or individual repayment plan case. If the case has already once been converted from chapter 11 or 13 to chapter 7, then the debtor does not have that right. The policy of the provision is that the debtor should always be given the opportunity to repay his debts, and a waiver of the right to convert a case is unenforceable.\xe2\x80\x9d S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 95\xe2\x80\x93989, p. 94 (1978); see also H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 95\xe2\x80\x93595, p. 380 (1977) (using nearly identical language).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Committee Reports\xe2\x80\x99 reference to an \xe2\x80\x9cabsolute right\xe2\x80\x9d of conversion is more equivocal than petitioner suggests. Assuming that the described debtor\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9copportunity to repay his debts\xe2\x80\x9d is a short-hand reference to a right to proceed under Chapter 13, the statement that he should \xe2\x80\x9calways\xe2\x80\x9d have that right is inconsistent with the earlier recognition that it is only a one-time right that does not survive a previous conversion to, or filing under, Chapter 13. More importantly, the broad description of the right as \xe2\x80\x9cabsolute\xe2\x80\x9d fails to give full effect to the express limitation in subsection (d). The words \xe2\x80\x9cunless the debtor may be a debtor under such chapter\xe2\x80\x9d expressly conditioned Marrama\xe2\x80\x99s right to convert on his ability to qualify as a \xe2\x80\x9cdebtor\xe2\x80\x9d under Chapter 13.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There are at least two possible reasons why Marrama may not qualify as such a debtor, one arising under \xc2\xa7109(e) of the Code, and the other turning on the construction of the word \xe2\x80\x9ccause\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa71307(c). The former provision imposes a limit on the amount of indebtedness that an individual may have in order to qualify for Chapter 13 relief.[Footnote 6] More pertinently,[Footnote 7] the latter provision, \xc2\xa71307(c), provides that a Chapter 13 proceeding may be either dismissed or converted to a Chapter 7 proceeding \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause\xe2\x80\x9d and includes a nonexclusive list of 10 causes justifying that relief.[Footnote 8] None of the specified causes mentions prepetition bad-faith conduct (although subparagraph 10 does identify one form of Chapter 7 error\xe2\x80\x94which is necessarily prepetition conduct\xe2\x80\x94that would justify dismissal of a Chapter 13 case).[Footnote 9] Bankruptcy courts nevertheless routinely treat dismissal for prepetition bad-faith conduct as implicitly authorized by the words \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause.\xe2\x80\x9d See n.\xc2\xa01, supra. In practical effect, a ruling that an individual\xe2\x80\x99s Chapter 13 case should be dismissed or converted to Chapter 7 because of prepetition bad-faith conduct, including fraudulent acts committed in an earlier Chapter 7 proceeding, is tantamount to a ruling that the individual does not qualify as a debtor under Chapter 13. That individual, in other words, is not a member of the class of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98honest but unfortunate debtor[s]\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d that the bankruptcy laws were enacted to protect. See Grogan v. Garner, 498 U. S., at 287. The text of \xc2\xa7706(d) therefore provides adequate authority for the denial of his motion to convert.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The class of honest but unfortunate debtors who do possess an absolute right to convert their cases from Chapter 7 to Chapter 13 includes the vast majority of the hundreds of thousands of individuals who file Chapter 7 petitions each year.[Footnote 10] Congress sought to give these individuals the chance to repay their debts should they acquire the means to do so. Moreover, as the Court of Appeals observed, the reference in \xc2\xa7706(a) to the unenforceability of a waiver of the right to convert functions \xe2\x80\x9cas a consumer protection provision against adhesion contracts, whereby a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s creditors might be precluded from attempting to prescribe a waiver of the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s right to convert to chapter 13 as a non-negotiable condition of its contractual agreements.\xe2\x80\x9d 430 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 479.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A statutory provision protecting a borrower from waiver is not a shield against forfeiture. Nothing in the text of either \xc2\xa7706 or \xc2\xa71307(c) (or the legislative history of either provision) limits the authority of the court to take appropriate action in response to fraudulent conduct by the atypical litigant who has demonstrated that he is not entitled to the relief available to the typical debtor.[Footnote 11] On the contrary, the broad authority granted to bankruptcy judges to take any action that is necessary or appropriate \xe2\x80\x9cto prevent an abuse of process\xe2\x80\x9d described in \xc2\xa7105(a) of the Code,[Footnote 12] is surely adequate to authorize an immediate denial of a motion to convert filed under \xc2\xa7706 in lieu of a conversion order that merely postpones the allowance of equivalent relief and may provide a debtor with an opportunity to take action prejudicial to creditors.[Footnote 13]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Indeed, as the Solicitor General has argued in his brief amicus curiae, even if \xc2\xa7105(a) had not been enacted, the inherent power of every federal court to sanction \xe2\x80\x9cabusive litigation practices,\xe2\x80\x9d see Roadway Express, Inc. v. Piper, 447 U. S. 752, 765 (1980), might well provide an adequate justification for a prompt, rather than a delayed, ruling on an unmeritorious attempt to qualify as a debtor under Chapter 13.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, the judgment of the Court of Appeals is affirmed.

\n

It is so ordered.

No provision of this title providing for the raising of an issue by a party in interest shall be construed to preclude the court from, sU. S.onte, taking any action or making any determination necessary or appropriate to enforce or implement court orders or rules, or to prevent an abuse of process.\xe2\x80\x9d

\nFootnote 13

\xc2\xa0Both the Chapter 7 trustee and the United States as amicus curiae argue in their briefs that in the interval between the allowance of a motion to convert under \xc2\xa7706(a) and the subsequent granting of a motion to dismiss under \xc2\xa71307(c), the fact that the debtor would have possession of the property formerly under the control of the trustee would create an opportunity for the debtor to take actions that would impair the rights of creditors. Whether or not that risk is significant, under our understanding of the Code, the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s prior misconduct may provide a sufficient justification for a denial of his motion to convert.

ROBERT LOUIS MARRAMA, PETITIONER v. CITIZENS\nBANK OF MASSACHUSETTS et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the first circuit

\n

[February 21, 2007]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Alito, with whom The Chief Justice, Justice Scalia, and Justice Thomas join, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under the clear terms of the Bankruptcy Code, a debtor who initially files a petition under Chapter 7 has the right to convert the case to another chapter under which the case is eligible to proceed. The Court, however, holds that a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s conversion right is conditioned upon a bankruptcy judge\xe2\x80\x99s finding of \xe2\x80\x9cgood faith.\xe2\x80\x9d Because the imposition of this condition is inconsistent with the Bankruptcy Code, I respectfully dissent.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Bankruptcy Code unambiguously provides that a debtor who has filed a bankruptcy petition under Chapter 7 has a broad right to convert the case to another chapter. Title 11 \xc2\xa7706(a) states:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[A] debtor may convert a case under this chapter to a case under chapter 11, 12, or 13 of this title at any time, if the case has not been converted under section 1112, 1208, or 1307 of this title.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Code restricts a Chapter 7 debtor\xe2\x80\x99s conversion right in two\xe2\x80\x94and only two\xe2\x80\x94ways. First, \xc2\xa7706(a) makes clear that the right to convert is available only once: A debtor may convert so long as \xe2\x80\x9cthe case has not been converted [to Chapter 7] under section 1112, 1208, or 1307 of this title.\xe2\x80\x9d Second, \xc2\xa7706(d) provides that a debtor wishing to convert to another chapter must meet the conditions that are needed in order to \xe2\x80\x9cbe a debtor under such chapter.\xe2\x80\x9d Nothing in \xc2\xa7706(a) or any other provision of the Code suggests that a bankruptcy judge has the discretion to override a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of the \xc2\xa7706(a) conversion right on a ground not set out in the Code. Thus, a straightforward reading of the Code suggests that a Chapter 7 debtor has the right to convert the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s case to Chapter 13 (or another chapter) provided that the two express statutory conditions contained in \xc2\xa7706 are satisfied.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This reading of the Code is buttressed by the contrast between the terms of \xc2\xa7706 and the language employed in other Code provisions that give bankruptcy judges the discretion to deny conversion requests. As noted, \xc2\xa7706(a) says that a Chapter 7 debtor \xe2\x80\x9cmay convert\xe2\x80\x9d the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s case to another chapter. Chapters 11, 12, and 13 contain similar provisions stating that debtors under those chapters \xe2\x80\x9cmay convert\xe2\x80\x9d their cases to other chapters. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71112(a), 1208(a), and 1307(a) (2000 ed. and Supp IV). Chapters 11, 12, and 13 also contain separate provisions governing conversion requests by other parties in interest. For example, the applicable provision in Chapter 11\nprovides:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cOn request of a party in interest and after notice and a hearing, the court may convert a case under this chapter to a case under chapter 11 of this title at any time.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7706(b) (emphasis added).

\n

\nSee also \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71112(b), 1208(b), (d), and 1307(c).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In these sections, parties in interest are not given a right to convert. Rather, parties in interest are authorized to request conversion. And the authority to convert, after notice and a hearing, is expressly left to the discretion of the bankruptcy court, which \xe2\x80\x9cmay convert\xe2\x80\x9d the case if the general standard of \xe2\x80\x9ccause\xe2\x80\x9d is found to have been met. If the Code had been meant to give a bankruptcy court similar authority when a Chapter 7 debtor wishes to convert, the Code would have used language similar to that in \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71112(b), 1208(b), (d), and 1307(c). Congress knew how to limit conversion authority in this way, and it did not do so in \xc2\xa7706(a).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Chapter 7, Congress did directly address the consequences of the sort of conduct complained of in this case. In \xc2\xa7727(a)(3), Congress specified that a debtor may be denied a discharge of debts if \xe2\x80\x9cthe debtor has concealed . . . records, and papers, from which the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition or business transactions might be ascertained.\xe2\x80\x9d The Code further provides that discharge may be denied if the debtor has \xe2\x80\x9cmade a false oath or account\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cpresented or used a false claim.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7727(a)(4). In addition to blocking discharge, Congress could easily have deemed such conduct sufficient to bar conversion to another chapter, but Congress did not do so.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Instead of taking that approach, Congress included in the statutory scheme several express means to redress a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s bad faith. First, if a bankruptcy court finds that there is \xe2\x80\x9ccause,\xe2\x80\x9d the court may convert or reconvert a Chapter 11 or Chapter 13 restructuring to a Chapter 7 liquidation. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71112(b), 1307(c). Second, a Chapter 13 debtor must propose a repayment plan to satisfy the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s creditors\xe2\x80\x94a plan that is subject to court approval and must be proposed in good faith. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71325(a)(3), (4); accord, \xc2\xa71328(b)(2). Third, a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s asset schedules are filed under penalty of perjury. 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71746; Fed. Rule Bkrtcy. Proc. 1008. Fourth, a Chapter 13 case is overseen by a trustee who is empowered to investigate the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial affairs, to furnish information regarding the bankruptcy estate to parties in interest, and to oppose discharge if necessary. 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7704(4), (6) and (9). See also \xc2\xa71302(b) (defining the powers of a Chapter 13 trustee in part by reference to the powers of a Chapter 7 trustee). These measures, as opposed to the \xe2\x80\x9cgood faith\xe2\x80\x9d requirement crafted by the Court, represent the Code\xe2\x80\x99s strategy for dealing with debtors who engage in the type of abusive tactics that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion targets.[Footnote 1]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, the Code expressly gives a debtor who initially files under Chapter 7 the right to convert the case to another chapter so long as the debtor satisfies the requirements of the destination chapter. By contrast, the Code pointedly does not give the bankruptcy courts the authority to deny conversion based on a finding of \xe2\x80\x9cbad faith.\xe2\x80\x9d There is no justification for disregarding the Code\xe2\x80\x99s scheme.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In reaching the conclusion that a bankruptcy judge may override a Chapter 7 debtor\xe2\x80\x99s conversion right based on a finding of \xe2\x80\x9cbad faith,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court reasons as follows. Under \xc2\xa7706(d), a Chapter 7 debtor may not convert to another chapter \xe2\x80\x9cunless the debtor may be a debtor under such chapter.\xe2\x80\x9d Under \xc2\xa71307(c), a Chapter 13 proceeding may be dismissed or converted to Chapter 7 \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause.\xe2\x80\x9d One such \xe2\x80\x9ccause\xe2\x80\x9d recognized by bankruptcy courts is \xe2\x80\x9cbad faith.\xe2\x80\x9d Therefore, a Chapter 7 debtor who has proceeded in \xe2\x80\x9cbad faith\xe2\x80\x9d and wishes to convert his or her case to Chapter 13 is not eligible to \xe2\x80\x9cbe a debtor\xe2\x80\x9d under Chapter 13 because the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s case would be subject to dismissal or reconversion to Chapter 7 pursuant to \xc2\xa71307(c). I cannot agree with this strained reading of the Code.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The requirements that must be met in order to \xe2\x80\x9cbe a debtor\xe2\x80\x9d under Chapter 13 are set forth in 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0A. \xc2\xa7109 (main ed. and Supp. 2006), which is appropriately titled \xe2\x80\x9cWho may be a debtor.\xe2\x80\x9d The two requirements that are specific to Chapter 13 appear in subsection (e). First, Chapter 13 is restricted to individuals, with or without their spouses, with regular income. Second, a debtor may not proceed under Chapter 13 if specified debt limits are exceeded.[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the Court of Appeals below correctly understood, \xc2\xa7706(d)\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that a debtor may convert only if \xe2\x80\x9cthe debtor may be a debtor under such chapter\xe2\x80\x9d obviously refers to the chapter-specific requirements of \xc2\xa7109. In\xc2\xa0re Marrama, 430 F.\xc2\xa03d 474, 479, n.\xc2\xa03 (CA1 2005).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Rather than reading \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7109(e) and 706(d) together, the Court puts \xc2\xa7109(e) aside and treats \xc2\xa7706(d) as a separate repository of additional requirements (namely, the absence of the grounds for dismissal or reconversion under \xc2\xa71307(c)) that a Chapter 7 debtor must satisfy before conversion to Chapter 13. But \xc2\xa71307(c) plainly does not set out requirements that an individual must meet in order to \xe2\x80\x9cbe a debtor\xe2\x80\x9d under Chapter 13. Instead, \xc2\xa71307(c) sets out the standard (\xe2\x80\x9ccause\xe2\x80\x9d) that a bankruptcy court must apply in deciding whether, in its discretion, an already filed Chapter 13 case should be dismissed or converted to Chapter 7. Thus, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding in this case finds no support in the terms of the Bankruptcy Code.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In holding that a bankruptcy judge may deny conversion based on \xe2\x80\x9cbad faith,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court of Appeals appears to have been influenced by the belief that following the literal terms of the Code would be pointless. Id., at 479\xe2\x80\x93481. Specifically, the Court of Appeals observed that if a debtor who wishes to convert from Chapter 7 to Chapter 13 has exhibited such \xe2\x80\x9cbad faith\xe2\x80\x9d that the bankruptcy court would immediately convert the case back to Chapter 7 under \xc2\xa71307(c), then no purpose would be served by requiring the parties and the court to go through the process of conversion and prompt reconversion. Id., at 481.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is by no means clear, however, that conversion under \xc2\xa7706(a) followed by a reconversion proceeding under \xc2\xa71307(c) would be an empty exercise. The immediate practical effect of following the statutory scheme is compliance with Bankruptcy Rule 1017(f), which applies Bankruptcy Rule 9014 to the reconversion. Fed. Rule Bkrtcy. Proc. 1017(e)(1). Rule 9014 (a), in turn, requires that the request be made by motion and that \xe2\x80\x9creasonable notice and opportunity for hearing . . . be afforded the party against whom relief is sought.\xe2\x80\x9d The Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision circumvents this process and forecloses the right that a Chapter 13 debtor would otherwise possess to file a Chapter 13 repayment and reorganization plan, 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71321, which must be filed in good faith and which must demonstrate that creditors will receive no less than they would under an immediate Chapter 7 liquidation, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71325(a)(3) and (4); accord, \xc2\xa71328(b)(2). While the plan must be filed no later than 15 days after filing the petition or conversion, the debtor may file the plan at the time of conversion, i.e., before the reconversion hearing. Fed. Rule Bkrtcy. Proc. 3015(b).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, it is not clear whether, in\xc2\xa0converting a case \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa71307(c), a bankruptcy court must consider the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s plan (if already filed) and, if the plan must be considered, whether the court must take into account whether the plan was filed in good faith, whether it honestly discloses the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s assets, whether it demonstrates that creditors would in fact fare better under the plan than under a liquidation, and whether the plan in some sense \xe2\x80\x9ccures\xe2\x80\x9d prior bad faith. Today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion renders these questions academic, and little is left to guide what a bankruptcy court must consider, or may disregard, in blocking a \xc2\xa7706(a) conversion.[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court notes that the Bankruptcy Code is intended to give a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cfresh start\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9chonest but unfortunate debtor.\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 1, 9 (quoting Grogan v. Garner, 498 U. S. 279, 286, 287 (1991)). But compliance with the statutory scheme\xe2\x80\x94conversion to Chapter 13 followed by notice and a hearing on the question of reconversion\xe2\x80\x94would at least provide some structure to the process of identifying those debtors whose \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98bad faith\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d meets the Court\xe2\x80\x99s standard for consignment to liquidation, i.e., \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98bad faith\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d conduct that is \xe2\x80\x9catypical\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cextraordinary.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10, n.\xc2\xa011.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, the Court notes two alternative bases for its holding. First, the Court points to 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7105(a), which governs a bankruptcy court\xe2\x80\x99s general powers.[Footnote 4] Second, the Court suggests that even without a textual basis, a bankruptcy court\xe2\x80\x99s inherent power may empower it to deny a \xc2\xa7706(a) conversion request for bad faith. Obviously, however, neither of these sources of authority authorizes a bankruptcy court to contravene the Code. On the contrary, a bankruptcy court\xe2\x80\x99s general and equitable powers \xe2\x80\x9cmust and can only be exercised within the confines of the Bankruptcy Code.\xe2\x80\x9d Norwest Bank Worthington v. Ahlers, 485 U. S. 197, 206 (1988); accord, Sec v. United States Realty & Improvement Co., 310 U. S. 434, 455 (1940) (\xe2\x80\x9cA bankruptcy court . . . is guided by equitable doctrines and principles except in so far as they are inconsistent with the Act\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Ultimately, \xc2\xa7105(a) and a bankruptcy court\xe2\x80\x99s inherent powers may have a role to play in a case such as this. The problem the Court identifies is a real one. A debtor who is convinced that he or she can successfully conceal assets has a significant incentive to pursue Chapter 7 liquidation in lieu of a Chapter 13 restructuring. If successful, the debtor preserves wealth; if unsuccessful, the debtor can convert to Chapter 13 and land largely where the debtor would have been if he or she had fully disclosed all assets and proceeded in Chapter 13 in the first instance.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Bankruptcy courts have used their statutory and equitable authority to craft various remedies for a range of bad faith conduct: requiring accountings or reporting of assets\xc2\xa0[Footnote 5]; enjoining debtors from alienating estate property\xc2\xa0[Footnote 6]; penalizing counsel\xc2\xa0[Footnote 7]; assessing costs and fees\xc2\xa0[Footnote 8]; or holding the debtor in contempt\xc2\xa0[Footnote 9]. But whatever steps a bankruptcy court may take pursuant to \xc2\xa7105(a) or its general equitable powers, a bankruptcy court cannot contravene the provisions of the Code.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because the provisions of the Code rule out the procedure that was followed in this case by the bankruptcy court, I would reverse the judgment of the Court of\nAppeals.
