\n\t\t\t\tKURNS, executrix of the ESTATE OF CORSON, DECEASED, et\xc2\xa0al. v. RAILROAD FRICTION PRODUCTS CORP. et\xc2\xa0al.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the third circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 10\xe2\x80\x93879.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 9, 2011\xe2\x80\x94Decided February 29, 2012\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tGeorge Corson worked as a welder and machinist for a railroad carrier. After retirement, Corson was diagnosed with mesothelioma. He and his wife, a petitioner here, sued respondents Railroad Friction Products Corporation and Viad Corp in state court, claiming injury from Corson\xe2\x80\x99s exposure to asbestos in locomotives and locomotive parts distributed by respondents. The Corsons alleged state-law claims of defective design and failure to warn of the dangers posed by asbestos. After Corson died, petitioner Kurns, executrix of his estate, was substituted as a party. Respondents removed the case to the Federal District Court, which granted them summary judgment, ruling that the state-law claims were pre-empted by the Locomotive Inspection Act (LIA), \n49 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa720701 et seq. The Third Circuit affirmed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 state-law design-defect and failure-to-warn claims fall within the field of locomotive equipment regulation pre-empted by the LIA, as that field was defined in Napier v. Atlantic Coast Line R.\xc2\xa0Co., \n\n272 U.\xc2\xa0S. 605\n. Pp.\xc2\xa02\xe2\x88\x9211.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0The LIA provides that a railroad carrier may use or allow to be used a locomotive or tender on its railroad line only when the locomotive or tender and its parts or appurtenances are in proper condition and safe to operate without unnecessary danger of personal injury, have been inspected as required by the LIA and regulations prescribed thereunder by the Secretary of Transportation, and can withstand every test prescribed under the LIA by the Secretary. See \xc2\xa720701. Pp.\xc2\xa02\xe2\x80\x933.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0Congress may expressly pre-empt state law. But even without an express pre-emption provision, state law must yield to a congressional Act to the extent of any conflict with a federal statute, see Crosby v. National Foreign Trade Council, \n\n530 U.\xc2\xa0S. 363\n, or when the federal statute\xe2\x80\x99s scope indicates that Congress intended federal law to occupy a field exclusively, see Freightliner Corp. v. Myrick, \n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 280\n. This case involves only the latter, so-called \xe2\x80\x9cfield pre-emption.\xe2\x80\x9d Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x934.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0In Napier, this Court held two state laws prescribing the use of locomotive equipment pre-empted by the LIA, concluding that the broad power conferred by the LIA on the Interstate Commerce Commission (the agency then vested with authority to carry out the LIA\xe2\x80\x99s requirements) was a \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral one\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cextends to the design, the construction and the material of every part of the locomotive and tender and of all appurtenances.\xe2\x80\x9d 272 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 611. The Court rejected the States\xe2\x80\x99 contention that the scope of the pre-empted field was to \xe2\x80\x9cbe determined by the object sought through legislation, rather than the physical elements affected by it,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 612, and found it dispositive that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he federal and state statutes are directed to the same subject\xe2\x80\x95the equipment of locomotives.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x88\x925.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(d)\xc2\xa0The Federal Railroad Safety Act of 1970 (FRSA) did not alter the LIA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emptive scope. By its terms, the FRSA\xe2\x80\x94which instructs that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Secretary of Transportation .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall prescribe regulations and issue orders for every area of railroad safety supplementing laws and regulations in effect on October 16, 1970,\xe2\x80\x9d \n49 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa720103(a)\xe2\x80\x94does not alter pre-existing federal railroad safety statutes. Rather, it leaves those statutes intact\xc2\xa0and authorizes the Secretary to fill interstitial areas of railroad safety with supplemental regulation. Because the LIA was already in effect when the FRSA was enacted, the FRSA left the LIA, and its pre-emptive scope as defined by Napier, intact. P.\xc2\xa06.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(e)\xc2\xa0Petitioners do not argue that Napier should be overruled. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x937. Instead, petitioners contend that their claims fall outside the LIA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-empted field, as it was defined in Napier. Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 arguments are unpersuasive. First, the argument that the pre-empted field does not extend to state-law claims arising from the repair or maintenance of locomotives is inconsistent with Napier\xe2\x80\x99s holding that Congress, in enacting the LIA, \xe2\x80\x9cmanifest[ed] the intention to occupy the entire field of regulating locomotive equipment.\xe2\x80\x9d 272 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 611. Second, the argument that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 failure-to-warn claims are not pre-empted because they do not base liability on the design or manufacture of a product ignores that a failure-to-warn claim alleges that the product itself is defective unless accompanied by sufficient warnings or instructions. Because petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 failure-to-warn claims are therefore directed at the equipment of locomotives, they fall within the pre-empted field defined by Napier. Third, the argument that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims are not pre-empted because manufacturers were not regulated under the LIA when Corson was exposed to asbestos is inconsistent with Napier, which defined the pre-empted field on the basis of the physical elements regulated, not on the basis of the entity directly subject to regulation. Finally, contrary to petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 argument, the LIA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emptive scope is not limited to state legislation or regulation but extends to state common-law duties and standards of care directed to the subject of locomotive equipment. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x88\x9211.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t620 F.\xc2\xa03d 392, affirmed.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Thomas delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis case requires us to determine whether petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 state-law tort claims for defective design and failure to warn are pre-empted by the Locomotive Inspection Act (LIA), \n49 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa720701 et\xc2\xa0seq. The United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit determined that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims fall within the field pre-empted by that Act, as that field was defined by this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Napier v. Atlantic Coast Line R. Co., \n\n272 U.\xc2\xa0S. 605 (1926)\n. We agree.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tGeorge Corson was employed as a welder and machinist by the Chicago, Milwaukee, St. Paul & Pacific Railroad from 1947 until 1974. Corson worked in locomotive repair and maintenance facilities, where his duties included installing brakeshoes on locomotives and stripping insulation from locomotive boilers. In 2005, Corson was diagnosed with malignant mesothelioma.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 2007, Corson and his wife filed suit in Pennsylvania state court against 59 defendants, including respondents Railroad Friction Products Corporation (RFPC) and Viad Corp (Viad). According to the complaint, RFPC distributed locomotive brakeshoes containing asbestos, and Viad was the successor-in-interest to a company that manufactured and sold locomotives and locomotive engine valves containing asbestos. Corson alleged that he handled this equipment and that he was injured by exposure to asbestos. The complaint asserted state-law claims that the equipment was defectively designed because it contained asbestos, and that respondents failed to warn of the dangers of asbestos or to provide instructions regarding its safe use. After the complaint was filed, Corson passed away, and the executrix of his estate, Gloria Kurns, was substituted as a party. Corson\xe2\x80\x99s widow and the executrix are petitioners here.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondents removed the case to the United States District Court for the Eastern District of Pennsylvania and moved for summary judgment. Respondents argued that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 state-law claims were pre-empted by the LIA. The District Court agreed and granted summary judgment for respondents. See Kurns v. A. W. Chesterton, Civ. Action No. 08\xe2\x80\x932216 (ED Pa., Feb. 3, 2009), App. to Pet. for Cert. 39a. The Third Circuit affirmed. See Kurns v. A. W. Chesterton, Inc., 620 F.\xc2\xa03d 392 (2010). We granted certiorari. 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2011).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress enacted the predecessor to the LIA, the Boiler Inspection Act (BIA), in 1911. The BIA made it unlawful to use a steam locomotive \xe2\x80\x9cunless the boiler of said locomotive and appurtenances thereof are in proper condition and safe to operate .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. without unnecessary peril to life or limb.\xe2\x80\x9d Act of Feb. 17, 1911, ch. 103, \xc2\xa72, \n\n36Stat.\n913\xe2\x80\x93914. In 1915, Congress amended the BIA to apply to \xe2\x80\x9cthe entire locomotive and tender and all parts and appurtenances thereof.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Act of Mar. 4, 1915, ch. 169, \xc2\xa71, \n\n38Stat.\n1192. The BIA as amended became commonly known as the Locomotive Inspection Act. As relevant here, the LIA provides:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cA railroad carrier may use or allow to be used a locomotive or tender on its railroad line only when the locomotive or tender and its parts and appurtenances\xe2\x80\x94\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c(1) are in proper condition and safe to operate without unnecessary danger of personal injury;\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c(2) have been inspected as required under this chapter and regulations prescribed by the Secretary of Transportation under this chapter; and\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c(3) can withstand every test prescribed by the Secretary under this chapter.\xe2\x80\x9d \n49 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa720701.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe issue presented in this case is whether the LIA pre-empts petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 state-law claims that respondents defectively designed locomotive parts and failed to warn Corson of dangers associated with those parts. In light of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s prior decision in Napier, supra, we conclude that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims are pre-empted.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Supremacy Clause provides that federal law \xe2\x80\x9cshall be the supreme Law of the Land .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any Thing in the Constitution or Laws of any State to the Contrary notwithstanding.\xe2\x80\x9d U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Art. VI, cl. 2. Pre-emption of state law thus occurs through the \xe2\x80\x9cdirect operation of the Supremacy Clause.\xe2\x80\x9d Brown v. Hotel Employees, \n\n468 U.\xc2\xa0S. 491, \n501 (1984)\n. Congress may, of course, expressly pre-empt state law, but \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ven without an express provision for preemption, we have found that state law must yield to a congressional Act in at least two circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Crosby v. National Foreign Trade Council, \n\n530 U.\xc2\xa0S. 363, \n372 (2000)\n. First, \xe2\x80\x9cstate law is naturally preempted to the extent of any conflict with a federal statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Second, we have deemed state law pre-empted \xe2\x80\x9cwhen the scope of a [federal] statute indicates that Congress intended federal law to occupy a field exclusively.\xe2\x80\x9d Freightliner Corp. v. Myrick, \n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 280, \n287 (1995)\n. We deal here only with the latter, so-called field pre-emption.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe do not, however, address the LIA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emptive ef- fect on a clean slate, because this Court addressed that issue 85 years ago in Napier. In that case, railroads challenged two state laws that \xe2\x80\x9cprohibit[ed] use within the State of locomotives not equipped with\xe2\x80\x9d certain prescribed devices, on the ground that the Interstate Commerce Commission (ICC), the agency then vested with the authority to carry out the LIA\xe2\x80\x99s requirements, had not required the devices in question.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t 272 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 607, 609. In response, the States argued that their requirements were not pre-empted because they were directed at a different objective than the LIA. Id., at 612. According to the States, their regulations were intended to protect railroad workers from sickness and disease, whereas \xe2\x80\x9cthe federal regulation endeavors solely to prevent accidental injury in the operation of trains.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo determine whether the state requirements were pre-empted, this Court asked whether the LIA \xe2\x80\x9cmanifest[s] the intention to occupy the entire field of regulating locomotive equipment[.]\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 611. The Court answered that question in the affirmative, stating that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he broad scope of the authority conferred upon the [ICC]\xe2\x80\x9d by Congress in the LIA led to that conclusion. Id., at 613. The power delegated to the ICC, the Court explained, was a \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral one\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cextends to the design, the construction and the material of every part of the locomotive and tender and of all appurtenances.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 611.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court rejected the States\xe2\x80\x99 contention that the scope of the pre-empted field was to \xe2\x80\x9cbe determined by the object sought through the legislation, rather than the physical elements affected by it.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 612. The Court found it dispositive that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he federal and the state statutes are directed to the same subject\xe2\x80\x94the equipment of locomotives.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Because the States\xe2\x80\x99 requirements operated upon the same physical elements as the LIA, the Court held that the state laws, \xe2\x80\x9chowever commendable or however different their purpose,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 613, fell within the LIA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-empted field.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIV\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAgainst the backdrop of Napier, petitioners advance two arguments in support of their position that their state-law claims related to the use of asbestos in locomotive equipment do not fall within the LIA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-empted field. Petitioners first contend that Napier no longer defines the scope of the LIA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-empted field because that field has been narrowed by a subsequently enacted federal statute. Alternatively, petitioners argue that their claims do not fall within the LIA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-empted field, even as that field was defined by Napier. We address each of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 arguments in turn.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFirst, petitioners suggest that the Federal Railroad Safety Act of 1970 (FRSA), \n\n84Stat.\n971 (codified at \n49 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa720102 et\xc2\xa0seq.), altered the LIA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emptive scope. The FRSA grants the Secretary of Transportation broad regulatory authority over railroad safety. See \xc2\xa720103(a). Petitioners point to the FRSA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emption provision, which provides in part that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] State may adopt or continue in force a law, regulation, or order related to railroad safety .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. until the Secretary of Transportation .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. prescribes a regulation or issues an order covering the subject matter of the State requirement.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa720106(a)(2) (2006 ed., Supp. III). According to petitioners, the FRSA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emption provision supplanted the LIA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emption of the field, with the result that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims are not pre-empted because the Secretary has not issued a regulation or order addressing the use of asbestos in locomotives or locomotive parts.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners\xe2\x80\x99 reliance on the FRSA is misplaced. The FRSA instructs that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Secretary of Transportation, as necessary, shall prescribe regulations and issue orders for every area of railroad safety supplementing laws and regulations in effect on October 16, 1970.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa720103(a) (2006 ed.) (emphasis added). By its terms, the FRSA does not alter pre-existing federal statutes on railroad safety. \xe2\x80\x9cRather, it leaves existing statutes intact,\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and authorizes the Secretary to fill interstitial areas of railroad safety with supplemental regulation.\xe2\x80\x9d Marshall v. Burlington Northern, Inc., 720 F.\xc2\xa02d 1149, 1152\xe2\x80\x931153 (CA9 1983) (Kennedy, J.). Because the LIA was already in effect when the FRSA was enacted, we conclude that the FRSA left the LIA, and its pre-emptive scope as defined by Napier, intact.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSince the LIA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emptive scope remains unaltered, petitioners must contend with Napier. Petitioners do not ask us to overrule Napier and thus do not seek to overcome the presumption of stare decisis that attaches to this 85-year-old precedent. See Global-Tech Appliances, Inc. v. SEB S.\xc2\xa0A., 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 9) (noting the \xe2\x80\x9cspecial force of the doctrine of stare decisis with regard to questions of statutory interpretation\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). Instead, petitioners advance several arguments aimed at demonstrating that their claims fall outside of the field pre-empted by the LIA, as it was defined in Napier. Each is unpersuasive.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners, along with the Solicitor General as amicus curiae, first argue that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims do not fall within the LIA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-empted field because the claims arise out of the repair and maintenance of locomotives, rather than the use of locomotives on a railroad line. Specifically, they contend that the scope of the field pre-empted by the LIA is coextensive with the scope of the Federal Government\xe2\x80\x99s regulatory authority under the LIA, which, they argue, does not extend to the regulation of hazards arising from the repair or maintenance of locomotives. Therefore, the argument goes, state-law claims arising from repair or maintenance\xe2\x80\x94as opposed to claims arising from use on the line\xe2\x80\x94do not fall within the pre-empted field.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe reject this attempt to redefine the pre-empted field. In Napier, the Court held that Congress, in enacting the LIA, \xe2\x80\x9cmanifest[ed] the intention to occupy the entire field of regulating locomotive equipment,\xe2\x80\x9d and the Court did not distinguish between hazards arising from repair and maintenance as opposed to those arising from use on the line. 272 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 611. The pre-empted field as defined by Napier plainly encompasses the claims at issue here. Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 common-law claims for defective design and failure to warn are aimed at the equipment of locomotives. Because those claims \xe2\x80\x9care directed to the same subject\xe2\x80\x9d as the LIA, Napier dictates that they fall within the pre-empted field. Id., at 612.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners further argue that, even if their design-defect claims are pre-empted, their failure-to-warn claims do not suffer the same fate. In their complaint, petitioners alleged in closely related claims (1) that respondents negligently failed to warn of the risks associated with asbestos and to provide instructions concerning safe- guards for working with asbestos; and (2) that the asbestos- containing products were defective because respondents failed to give sufficient warnings or instructions con- cerning the \xe2\x80\x9crisks, dangers, and harm inherent in said asbestos products.\xe2\x80\x9d See App. 20\xe2\x80\x9327 (\xc2\xb6\xc2\xb67\xe2\x80\x9310, 12), 42 (\xc2\xb68); see also Brief for Petitioners 11. According to petitioners, these claims do not fall within the LIA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-empted field because \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he basis of liability for failure to warn .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is not the \xe2\x80\x98design\xe2\x80\x99 or \xe2\x80\x98manufacture\xe2\x80\x99 of a product,\xe2\x80\x9d but is instead \xe2\x80\x9cthe failure to provide adequate warnings regarding the product\xe2\x80\x99s risks.\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief for Petitioners 16.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe disagree. A failure-to-warn claim alleges that the product itself is unlawfully dangerous unless accompanied by sufficient warnings or instructions. Restatement (Third) of Torts: Products Liability \xc2\xa72(c) (1997) (A failure-to-warn claim alleges that a product is defective \xe2\x80\x9cwhen the foreseeable risks of harm posed by the product could have been reduced or avoided by the provision of reasonable instructions or warnings by the seller or other distributor,\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and the omission of the instructions or warnings renders the product not reasonably safe\xe2\x80\x9d); see also id., Comment l, at 33 (\xe2\x80\x9cReasonable designs and instructions or warnings both play important roles in the production and distribution of reasonably safe products\xe2\x80\x9d). Thus, the \xe2\x80\x9cgravamen\xe2\x80\x9d of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 failure-to-warn claims \xe2\x80\x9cis still that [Corson] suffered harmful consequences as a result of his exposure to asbestos contained in locomotive parts and appurtenances.\xe2\x80\x9d 620 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 398, n.\xc2\xa08. Because petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 failure-to-warn claims are therefore directed at the equipment of locomotives, they fall within the pre-empted field defined by Napier. 272 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 612.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners also contend that their state-law claims against manufacturers of locomotives and locomotive parts fall outside of the LIA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-empted field because manufacturers were not regulated under the LIA at the time that Corson was allegedly exposed to asbestos. Petitioners point out that the LIA, as originally enacted in the BIA, subjected only common carriers to civil penalties. Act of Feb. 17, 1911, \xc2\xa79, \n\n36Stat.\n916. It was not until 1988, well after the events of this case, that the LIA\xe2\x80\x99s penalty provision was revised to apply to \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny person\xe2\x80\x9d violating the LIA. Rail Safety Improvement Act of 1988, \xc2\xa714(7)(A), \n\n102Stat.\n633; see also \xc2\xa714(7)(B) (amending penalty provision to provide that \xe2\x80\x9can act by an individual that causes a railroad to be in violation .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall be deemed a violation\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis argument fails for the same reason as the two preceding arguments: It is inconsistent with Napier. Napier defined the field pre-empted by the LIA on the basis of the physical elements regulated\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cthe equipment of locomotives\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94not on the basis of the entity directly subject to regulation. 272 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 612. Because petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims are directed at the equipment of locomotives, they fall within the pre-empted field.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners\xe2\x80\x99 proposed rule is also contrary to common sense. Under petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 approach, a State could not require railroads to equip their locomotives with parts meeting state-imposed specifications, but could require manufacturers of locomotive parts to produce only parts meeting those state-imposed specifications. We rejected a similar approach in an express pre-emption context in Engine Mfrs. Assn. v. South Coast Air Quality Management Dist., \n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 246 (2004)\n. There, a state entity argued that its rules prohibiting the purchase or lease of vehicles that failed to meet stringent emissions requirements were not pre-empted by the Clean Air Act, \n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77543(a), because the rules in question were aimed at the purchase of vehicles, rather than their manufacture or sale. 541 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 248. We observed, however, that \xe2\x80\x9ctreating sales restrictions and purchase restrictions differently for pre-emption purposes would make no sense,\xe2\x80\x9d because the \xe2\x80\x9cmanufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s right to sell federally approved vehicles is meaningless in the absence of a purchaser\xe2\x80\x99s right to buy them.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 255. Similarly, a railroad\xe2\x80\x99s ability to equip its fleet of locomotives in compliance with federal standards is meaningless if manufacturers are not allowed to produce locomotives and locomotive parts that meet those standards. Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims thus do not avoid pre-emption simply because they are aimed at the manufacturers of locomotives and locomotive parts.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, petitioners contend that the LIA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emptive scope does not extend to state common-law claims, as opposed to state legislation or regulation. Petitioners note that \xe2\x80\x9ca preempted field does not necessarily include state common law.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 38\xe2\x80\x9339 (citing Silkwood v. Kerr-McGee Corp., \n\n464 U.\xc2\xa0S. 238 (1984)\n; Sprietsma v. Mercury Marine, \n\n537 U.\xc2\xa0S. 51 (2002)\n). Napier, however, held that the LIA \xe2\x80\x9coccup[ied] the entire field of regulating locomotive equipment\xe2\x80\x9d to the exclusion of state regulation. 272 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 611\xe2\x80\x93612. That categorical conclusion admits of no exception for state common-law duties and standards of care. As we have recognized, state \xe2\x80\x9cregulation can be .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. effectively exerted through an award of damages,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he obligation to pay compensation can be, indeed is designed to be, a potent method of governing conduct and controlling policy.\xe2\x80\x9d San Diego Building Trades Council v. Garmon, \n\n359 U.\xc2\xa0S. 236, \n247 (1959)\n. Cf. Riegel v. Med- tronic, Inc., \n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 312, \n324 (2008)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cAbsent other in- dication, reference to a State\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x98requirements\xe2\x80\x99 [in a federal express pre-emption provision] includes its common-law duties\xe2\x80\x9d). We therefore conclude that state common-law duties and standards of care directed to the subject of locomotive equipment are pre-empted by the LIA.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor the foregoing reasons, we hold that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 state-law design-defect and failure-to-warn claims fall within the field of locomotive equipment regulation pre-empted by the LIA, as that field was defined in Napier. Accordingly, the judgment of the Court of Appeals is affirmed.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI concur in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding that the Locomotive Inspection Act (LIA), \n49 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa720701 et\xc2\xa0seq., pre-empts petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 tort claims for defective design, but I respectfully dissent from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding that the same is true of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims for failure to warn. In my view, the latter escape pre-emption because they impose no state-law requirements in the field reserved for federal regulation: \xe2\x80\x9cthe equipment of locomotives.\xe2\x80\x9d Napier v. Atlantic Coast Line R. Co., \n\n272 U.\xc2\xa0S. 605, \n612 (1926)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tStatutory stare decisis compels me to agree that the LIA occupies \xe2\x80\x9cthe field of regulating locomotive equipment used on a highway of interstate commerce.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 607. Perhaps this Court might decide Napier differently today. The LIA lacks an express pre-emption clause, and \xe2\x80\x9cour recent cases have frequently rejected field pre-emption in the absence of statutory language expressly requiring it.\xe2\x80\x9d Camps Newfound/Owatonna, Inc. v. Town of Harrison, \n\n520 U.\xc2\xa0S. 564, \n617 (1997)\n (Thomas, J., dissenting). The LIA contains no substantive regulations, let alone a \xe2\x80\x9cscheme of federal regulation .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. so pervasive as to make reasonable the inference that Congress left no room for the States to supplement it.\xe2\x80\x9d Rice v. Santa Fe Elevator Corp., \n\n331 U.\xc2\xa0S. 218, \n230 (1947)\n. Instead of relying on such indications of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent to oust state law, Napier implied field pre-emption from the LIA\xe2\x80\x99s mere delegation of regulatory authority to the Interstate Commerce Commission. Compare 272 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 612\xe2\x80\x93613, with, e.g., Hillsborough County v. Automated Medical Laboratories, Inc., \n\n471 U.\xc2\xa0S. 707, \n717 (1985)\n, and New York State Dept. of Social Servs. v. Dublino, \n\n413 U.\xc2\xa0S. 405, \n415 (1973)\n. Nonetheless, Napier\xe2\x80\x99s construction of the LIA has been settled law for 85 years, and \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[c]onsiderations of stare decisis have special force in the area of statutory interpretation.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Hilton v. South Carolina Public Railways Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, \n\n502 U.\xc2\xa0S. 197, \n202 (1991)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tConsistent with the values served by statutory stare decisis, however, it is important to be precise about what Napier held: Napier defined the pre-empted field as the physical composition of locomotive equipment. See 272 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 611 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he power delegated .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. by the [LIA] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. extends to the design, the construction, and the material of every part of the locomotive and tender and of all appurtenances\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 612 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe federal and the state statutes are directed to the same subject\xe2\x80\x94the equipment of locomotives. They operate upon the same object\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Act of June 7, 1924, \xc2\xa72, \n\n43Stat.\n659 (making the LIA\xe2\x80\x99s standard of care applicable to the \xe2\x80\x9clocomotive, its boiler, tender, and all parts and appurtenances thereof\xe2\x80\x9d). Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 defective-design claims fall within the pre-empted field because they would impose state-law requirements on a locomotive\xe2\x80\x99s physical makeup. See ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x938.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners\xe2\x80\x99 failure-to-warn claims, by contrast, proceed on a fundamentally different theory of tort liability that does not implicate a product\xe2\x80\x99s physical composition at all. A failure-to-warn claim asks nothing of a product\xe2\x80\x99s de- sign, but requires instead that a manufacturer caution of nonobvious dangers and provide instructions for safe use. Indeed, a product may be flawlessly designed and still subject its manufacturer or seller to liability for lack of adequate instructions or warnings. See, e.g., Madden, The Duty To Warn in Products Liability: Contours and Criticism, 89 W.\xc2\xa0Va. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 221 (1987) (\xe2\x80\x9cAlthough a product is unerringly designed, manufactured and assembled, injury or damage occasioned by its intended or reasonably foreseeable use may subject the seller to liability. Such liability may be found if the product has a potential for injury that is not readily apparent to the user\xe2\x80\x9d (cited in Restatement (Third) of Torts: Products Liability \xc2\xa72, Reporter\xe2\x80\x99s Note, Comment i, n. 1 (1997) (hereinafter Restatement)); see also Madden, 89 W.\xc2\xa0Va. L.\xc2\xa0Rev., at 221, n.\xc2\xa01 (collecting cases). Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 complaint embodies just this conceptual distinction. Compare App. 22\xe2\x80\x9323, \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb610(c)\xe2\x80\x93(e), (g), with id., at 25, \xc2\xb610(p).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn the jurisdictions relevant to this suit, failure to warn is \xe2\x80\x9ca distinct cause of action under the theory of strict products liability.\xe2\x80\x9d Riley v. American Honda Motor Co., 259 Mont. 128, 132, 856 P.\xc2\xa02d 196, 198 (1993). Thus, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a failure to warn of an injury[-]causing risk associated with the use of a technically pure and fit product can render such product unreasonably dangerous.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; see also, e.g., Jahnig v. Coisman, 283 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 557, 560 (S.\xc2\xa0D. 1979) (\xe2\x80\x9cIn products liability suits based upon strict liability, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the product itself need not be defective. Where a manufacturer or seller has reason to anticipate that danger may result from a particular use of his product, and he fails to give adequate warning of such a danger, the product sold without such warning is in a defective condition within the strict liability doctrine\xe2\x80\x9d); Greiner v. Volkswagenwerk Aktiengeselleschaft, 540 F.\xc2\xa02d 85, 92\xe2\x80\x9393 (CA3 1976) (finding that \xe2\x80\x9cfailure to adequately warn of inherent or latent limitations in a product, which do not necessarily amount to a design defect\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9can independent basis of liability\xe2\x80\x9d under Pennsylvania law).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSimilarly, this Court has explained that a failure-to-warn claim is \xe2\x80\x9cnarrower\xe2\x80\x9d than a claim that alleges a defect in the underlying product. Wyeth v. Levine, \n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 555, \n565 (2009)\n. Thus in Wyeth, this Court affirmed a state damages award based on a drug manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s failure to provide sufficient warnings to clinicians against intravenous administration of the drug, but noted that it was unnecessary to decide \xe2\x80\x9cwhether a state rule proscribing intravenous administration would be pre-empted.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Cf. Bates v. Dow Agrosciences LLC, \n\n544 U.\xc2\xa0S. 431, \n444 (2005)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cRules that require manufacturers to design reasonably safe products .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. plainly do not qualify as requirements for \xe2\x80\x98labeling or packaging.\xe2\x80\x99 None of these common-law rules requires that manufacturers label or package their products in any particular way\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority treats defective-design and failure-to-warn claims as congruent, reasoning that each asserts a product defect. See ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939 (citing Restatement \xc2\xa72(c) and Comment l). That may be true at a high level of gener- ality, but \xe2\x80\x9c[d]esign and failure-to-warn claims .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. rest on different factual allegations and distinct legal concepts.\xe2\x80\x9d Restatement \xc2\xa72, at 35, Comment n. For example, a manufacturer or seller cannot escape liability for an unreasonably unsafe design merely by issuing a warning. See id., at 33, Comment l (\xe2\x80\x9cWarnings are not .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a substitute for the provision of a reasonably safe design\xe2\x80\x9d). In a fundamental sense, therefore, a failure-to-warn claim proceeds by taking a product\xe2\x80\x99s physical design as a given. A failure-to-warn claim alleges a \xe2\x80\x9cdefect\xe2\x80\x9d by asserting that a product, as designed, is safe for use only when accompanied by a warning\xe2\x80\x94not that a product must be designed differently.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority further conflates defective-design and failure-to-warn claims by noting that each is \xe2\x80\x9cdirected at\xe2\x80\x9d locomotive equipment. Ante, at 9. That is insufficient. Not every state law that \xe2\x80\x9ccould be said to affect tangentially\xe2\x80\x9d matters within the regulated field is pre-empted. English v. General Elec. Co., \n\n496 U.\xc2\xa0S. 72, \n85 (1990)\n. Rather, \xe2\x80\x9cfor a state law to fall within the pre-empted zone, it must have some direct and substantial effect\xe2\x80\x9d on the primary conduct of entities subject to federal regulation. Ibid. As explained above, the LIA regulates the physical equipment of locomotives. But petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 failure-to-warn claims, if successful, would have no necessary effect on the physical equipment of locomotives at all, as respondents themselves acknowledge. See Brief for Respondents 55 (petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 failure-to-warn claims \xe2\x80\x9cmay not themselves literally mandate physical alteration of the locomotive\xe2\x80\x99s design or construction\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn the majority\xe2\x80\x99s view, a \xe2\x80\x9cduty to warn and the accompanying threat of liability will inevitably influence\xe2\x80\x9d a manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s design choices. Ante, at 9, n.\xc2\xa04. But an \xe2\x80\x9cinfluence\xe2\x80\x9d is not the same as an \xe2\x80\x9ceffect,\xe2\x80\x9d and not every state law with some imaginable impact on matters within a federally regulated field is, for that reason alone, pre-empted. See English, 496 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 85\xe2\x80\x9386; Silkwood v. Kerr-McGee Corp., \n\n464 U.\xc2\xa0S. 238, \n256 (1984)\n. Indeed, the majority elides the distinction between indirect and direct regulation, even though this Court has explained that the two are not equivalent for pre-emption purposes. See Goodyear Atomic Corp. v. Miller, \n\n486 U.\xc2\xa0S. 174, \n186 (1988)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress may reasonably determine that incidental reg- ulatory pressure is acceptable, whereas direct regulatory authority is not\xe2\x80\x9d). State wage-and-hour laws, work- place safety standards, or tax credits for green technology, for example, could all \xe2\x80\x9cinfluence\xe2\x80\x9d the means and materials of locomotive equipment manufacture without imposing direct obligations. Nor does the majority substantiate its assertion that the \xe2\x80\x9cinfluence\xe2\x80\x9d exerted by a duty to warn need be \xe2\x80\x9cinevitabl[e]\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9, n.\xc2\xa04. To the contrary, the requirements imposed by such a duty could be light, and the corresponding liability negligible, in comparison to the commercial value of retaining an existing design.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondents could have complied with state-law duties to warn by providing instructions for the safe maintenance of asbestos-containing locomotive parts in equipment man- uals. See, e.g., Baldwin-Lima-Hamilton Corp., Engine Manual for 600 Series Diesel Engines (1951), online at http://www.rr-fallenflags.org/manual/blh-6em.html (last visited Feb. 27, 2012, and available in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file). Or respondents could have ensured that repair shops posted signs. See Restatement \xc2\xa72, at 29\xe2\x80\x9330, Comment i (duty to warn \xe2\x80\x9cmay require that instructions and warnings be given not only to purchasers, users, and consumers, but also to others who a reasonable seller should know will be in a position to reduce or avoid the risk of harm\xe2\x80\x9d); see also, e.g., Patch v. Hillerich & Bradsby Co., 361 Mont. 241, 246, 257 P.\xc2\xa03d 383, 388 (2011) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhile placing a warning directly on a product is one method of warning, other methods of warning exist, including, but not limited to, issuing oral warnings and placing warnings in advertisements, posters, and media releases\xe2\x80\x9d). Neither step would encroach on the pre-empted field of locomotives\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cphysical elements.\xe2\x80\x9d Napier, 272 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 612. The majority is therefore wrong to say that \xe2\x80\x9cthe \xe2\x80\x98gravamen\xe2\x80\x99 of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 failure-to-warn claims \xe2\x80\x98is still that [Corson] suffered harmful consequences as a result of his exposure to asbestos contained in locomotive parts and appurtenances.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939 (quoting Kurns v. A. W. Chesteron, Inc., 620 F.\xc2\xa03d 392, 398, n.\xc2\xa08 (CA3 2010)). Rather, the \xe2\x80\x9cgravamen\xe2\x80\x9d of these claims is that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 decedent George Corson could have avoided the harmful consequences of exposure to asbestos while repairing precisely the same locomotive parts had respondents cautioned him, for example, to wear a mask.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, preserving petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 failure-to-warn claims coheres with the LIA\xe2\x80\x99s regulatory regime. Neither the Interstate Commerce Commission, to which Congress first delegated authority under the LIA, nor the Federal Railroad Administration (FRA), to whom that authority now belongs, has ever regulated locomotive repair and maintenance. To the contrary, the FRA takes the position that it lacks power under the LIA to regulate within locomotive maintenance and repair facilities. Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae in John Crane, Inc. v. Atwell, O.\xc2\xa0T. 2010, No. 10\xe2\x80\x93272, p.\xc2\xa010 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he field covered by the LIA does not include requirements concerning the repair of locomotives that are not in use\xe2\x80\x9d); Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 13 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe preempted field .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. does not include tort claims based on injuries arising when locomotives are not in use\xe2\x80\x9d). The FRA has determined that the Occupational Safety and Health Administration, not itself, bears primary responsibility for workplace safety, including with respect to hazardous materials. 43 Fed. Reg. 10583\xe2\x80\x9310590 (1978); cf., e.g., English, 496 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 83, and n.\xc2\xa06. And the FRA has not promulgated regulations that address warnings specific to maintenance and repair. Because the pre-empted field is congruent with the regulated field, see, e.g., United States v. Locke, \n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 89, \n112 (2000)\n, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s decision sweeps far too broadly.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn short, the majority affords the LIA field-pre-emptive effect well beyond what Napier requires, leaving petitioners without a remedy for what they allege was fatal ex- posure to asbestos in repair facilities. \xe2\x80\x9cIt is difficult to believe that Congress would, without comment, remove all means of judicial recourse for those injured by illegal conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d Silkwood, 464 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 251. That is doubly true in light of the LIA\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cpurpose .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of facilitating employee recovery, not of restricting such recovery or making it impossible.\xe2\x80\x9d Urie v. Thompson, \n\n337 U.\xc2\xa0S. 163, \n189 (1949)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tLike Justice Sotomayor, post, at 1 (opinion concurring in part and dissenting in part), I doubt this Court would decide Napier v. Atlantic Coast Line R. Co., \n\n272 U.\xc2\xa0S. 605 (1926)\n, in the same way today. The Napier Court concluded that Congress had \xe2\x80\x9cmanifest[ed] the intention to occupy the entire field of regulating locomotive equipment,\xe2\x80\x9d based on nothing more than a statute granting regulatory authority over that subject matter to a federal agency. Id., at 611. Under our more recent cases, Congress must do much more to oust all of state law from a field. See, e.g., New York State Dept. of Social Servs. v. Dublino, \n\n413 U.\xc2\xa0S. 405, \n415 (1973)\n (rejecting preemption even though Congress had enacted a \xe2\x80\x9cdetailed\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ccomprehensive\xe2\x80\x9d regulatory scheme). Viewed through the lens of modern preemption law, Napier is an anachronism.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut Napier governs so long as Congress lets it\xe2\x80\x94and that decision provides a straightforward way to determine whether state laws relating to locomotive equipment are preempted. According to Napier, the scope of the agency\xe2\x80\x99s power under the Locomotive Inspection Act (LIA) determines the boundaries of the preempted field. See 272 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 611 (state regulations were preempted because they fell \xe2\x80\x9cwithin the scope of the authority delegated to the Commission\xe2\x80\x9d); see also ante, at 5 (the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98broad scope of the authority\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d given to the agency \xe2\x80\x9cled to [Napier\xe2\x80\x99s] conclusion\xe2\x80\x9d); post, at 7\xe2\x80\x938 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he pre-empted field is congruent with the regulated field\xe2\x80\x9d). And under that test, none of the state-law claims at issue here can survive.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAll of us agree that the petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 defective-design claims are preempted. Napier recognized the federal agency\xe2\x80\x99s delegated authority over \xe2\x80\x9cthe design, the construction and the material of every part of the locomotive.\xe2\x80\x9d 272 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 611. In doing so, Napier did not distinguish between \xe2\x80\x9chazards arising from repair and maintenance\xe2\x80\x9d of the parts and hazards stemming from their \xe2\x80\x9cuse on the line.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 7. The agency thus has authority to regulate the design of locomotive equipment\xe2\x80\x94like the asbetos-containing brakeshoes here\xe2\x80\x94to prevent either danger. And that fact resolves the preemption question. Because the agency could have banned use of the brakeshoes as designed, the petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 defective-design claims\xe2\x80\x94which would effectively accomplish the identical result\xe2\x80\x94fall within the preempted field.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSo too the petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 failure-to-warn claims, and for the same reason. Napier did not specifically address warnings, because the case in no way involved them. But if an agency has the power to prohibit the use of locomotive equipment, it also has the power to condition the use of that equipment on proper warnings. (And that is so, contrary to Justice Sotomayor\xe2\x80\x99s view, see post, at 8, n. 3, whether the warning is engraved into the part itself or posted on the workshop wall.) Here, for example, the agency need not have chosen between banning asbestos-containing brakeshoes and leaving them entirely unregulated. It could instead have required a warning about how to handle those brakeshoes safely. If, say, a mask would have protected a worker from risk, then the agency could have demanded a notice to that effect. See, e.g., Law v. General Motors Corp., 114 F.\xc2\xa03d 908, 911 (CA9 1997) (\xe2\x80\x9cAs for warning requirements, these too are within the scope of the [agency\xe2\x80\x99s] authority\xe2\x80\x9d); Scheiding v. General Motors Corp., 22 Cal. 4th 471, 484, 993 P.\xc2\xa02d 996, 1004 (2000) (same).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t And because the agency could have required warnings about the equipment\xe2\x80\x99s use, the petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 failure-to-warn claims, no less than their defective-design claims, are preempted under Napier.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI understand these views to comport with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in this case, and I accordingly join it in full. \n\t\t\t
