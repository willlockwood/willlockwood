NIXON, ATTORNEY GENERAL OF MISSOURI v.\nMISSOURI MUNICIPAL LEAGUE et al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eighth circuit

\n

No. 02\xe2\x80\x931238. Argued January 12, 2004\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 24, 2004

\n

After Missouri enacted a statute forbidding its \xe2\x80\x9cpolitical subdivision[s to] provide or offer for sale \xe2\x80\xa6 a telecommunications service or \xe2\x80\xa6 facility,\xe2\x80\x9d the municipal respondents, including municipally owned utilities, petitioned the Federal Communications Commission (FCC) for an order declaring the statute unlawful under 47 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7253, which authorizes preemption of state and local laws and regulations \xe2\x80\x9cthat prohibit or have the effect of prohibiting the ability of any entity\xe2\x80\x9d to provide telecommunications services. Relying on its earlier order resolving a challenge to a comparable Texas law and the affirming opinion of the District of Columbia Circuit, the FCC refused to declare the Missouri statute preempted, concluding that \xe2\x80\x9cany entity\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa7253(a) does not include state political subdivisions, but applies only to independent entities subject to state regulation. The FCC also adverted to the principle of Gregory v. Ashcroft, 501 U. S. 452, that Congress needs to be clear before it constrains traditional state authority to order its government. The Eighth Circuit panel unanimously reversed, explaining that \xc2\xa7253(a)\xe2\x80\x99s word \xe2\x80\x9centity,\xe2\x80\x9d especially when modified by \xe2\x80\x9cany,\xe2\x80\x9d manifested sufficiently clear congressional attention to governmental entities to get past Gregory.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The class of entities contemplated by \xc2\xa7253 does not include the State\xe2\x80\x99s own subdivisions, so as to affect the power of States and localities to restrict their own (or their political inferiors\xe2\x80\x99) delivery of telecommunications services. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Two considerations fall short of supporting the municipal respondents. First, they argue that fencing governmental entities out of the telecommunications business flouts the public interest in promoting competition. It does not follow, however, that preempting state or local barriers to governmental entry into the market would be an effective way to draw municipalities into the business, and in any event the issue here does not turn on the merits of municipal telecommunications services. Second, concentrating on the undefined statutory phrase \xe2\x80\x9cany entity\xe2\x80\x9d does not produce a persuasive answer here. While an \xe2\x80\x9centity\xe2\x80\x9d can be either public or private, there is no convention of omitting the modifiers \xe2\x80\x9cpublic and private\xe2\x80\x9d when both are meant to be covered. Nor is coverage of public entities reliably signaled by speaking of \xe2\x80\x9cany\xe2\x80\x9d entity; \xe2\x80\x9cany\xe2\x80\x9d can and does mean different things depending upon the setting. To get at Congress\xe2\x80\x99s understanding requires a broader frame of reference, and in this litigation it helps to ask how Congress could have envisioned the preemption clause actually working if the FCC applied it at the municipal respondents\xe2\x80\x99 urging. See, e.g., New Jersey Realty Title Ins. Co. v. Division of Tax Appeals of N.\xc2\xa0J., 338 U. S. 665, 673. The strange and indeterminate results of using federal preemption to free public entities from state or local limitations is the key to understanding that Congress used \xe2\x80\x9cany entity\xe2\x80\x9d with a limited reference to any private entity. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The municipal respondents\xe2\x80\x99 position holds sufficient promise of futility and uncertainty to keep this Court from accepting it. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(1)\xc2\xa0In familiar instances of regulatory preemption under the Supremacy Clause, a federal measure preempting state regulation of economic conduct by a private party simply leaves that party free to do anything it chooses consistent with the prevailing federal law. See, e.g., Lorillard Tobacco Co. v. Reilly, 533 U. S. 525, 540\xe2\x80\x93553. But no such simple result would follow from federal preemption meant to unshackle local governments from entrepreneurial limitations. Such a government\xe2\x80\x99s capacity to enter an economic market turns not only on the effect of straightforward economic regulation below the national level (including outright bans), but on the authority and potential will of state or local governments to support entry into the market. Preempting a ban on government utilities would not accomplish much if the government could not point to some law authorizing it to run a utility in the first place. And preemption would make no difference to anyone if the state regulator were left with control over funding needed for any utility operation and declined to pay for it. In other words, when a government regulates itself (or the subdivision through which it acts) there is no clear distinction between the regulator and the entity regulated. Legal limits on what the government itself (including its subdivisions) may do will often be indistinguishable from choices that express what the government wishes to do with the authority and resources it can command. Thus, preempting state or local governmental self-regulation (or regulation of political inferiors) would work so differently from preempting regulation of private players that it is highly unlikely that Congress intended to set off on such uncertain adventures. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(2)\xc2\xa0Several hypothetical examples illustrate the implausibility of the municipal respondents\xe2\x80\x99 reading that Congress intended \xc2\xa7253 to preempt state or local governmental self-regulation. Whether a law prohibiting an entity\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cability\xe2\x80\x9d to provide telecommunications under \xc2\xa7253 means denying the entity a capacity or authority to act in the first place, or whether it means limiting or cutting back on some preexisting authority to go into the telecommunications business (under a different law), the hypotheticals demonstrate that \xc2\xa7253 would not work like a normal preemptive statute if it applied to a governmental unit. It would often accomplish nothing, it would treat States differently depending on the formal structures of their laws authorizing municipalities to function, and it would hold out no promise of a national consistency. That Congress meant \xc2\xa7253 to start down such a road in the absence of any clearer signal than the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cability of any entity\xe2\x80\x9d is farfetched. See, e.g., United States v. American Trucking Assns., Inc., 310 U. S. 534, 543. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(3)\xc2\xa0The practical implication of the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s reading of \xc2\xa7253 to forbid States to withdraw municipalities\xe2\x80\x99 preexisting authority expressly to enter the telecommunications business, but not withdrawals of authority that are competitively neutral in the sense of being couched in general terms that do not expressly target telecommunications, is to read out of \xc2\xa7253 the words \xe2\x80\x9cor has the effect of prohibiting.\xe2\x80\x9d Those words signal Congress\xe2\x80\x99 willingness to preempt laws that produce the unwanted effect, even if they do not advertise their prohibitory agenda on their faces. The dissent\xe2\x80\x99s reading therefore disregards \xc2\xa7253\xe2\x80\x99s plain language and entails a policy consequence that Congress could not possibly have intended. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0A complementary principle would bring the Court to the same conclusion even on the assumption that preemption might operate straightforwardly to provide local choice. Section 253(a) is hardly forthright enough to pass Gregory: \xe2\x80\x9cability of any entity\xe2\x80\x9d is not limited to one reading, and neither statutory structure nor legislative history points unequivocally to a commitment by Congress to treat governmental telecommunications providers on par with private firms. The want of any \xe2\x80\x9cunmistakably clear\xe2\x80\x9d statement to that effect, 501 U. S., at 460, would be fatal to respondents\xe2\x80\x99 reading. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x93\n14.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Souter delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 101(a) of the Telecommunications Act of 1996, 110 Stat. 70, 47 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7253, authorizes preemption of state and local laws and regulations expressly or effectively \xe2\x80\x9cprohibiting the ability of any entity\xe2\x80\x9d to provide telecommunications services. The question is whether the class of entities includes the State\xe2\x80\x99s own subdivisions, so as to affect the power of States and localities to restrict their own (or their political inferiors\xe2\x80\x99) delivery of such services. We hold it does not.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1997, the General Assembly of Missouri enacted the statute codified as \xc2\xa7392.410(7) of the State\xe2\x80\x99s Revised Statutes:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cNo political subdivision of this state shall provide or offer for sale, either to the public or to a telecommunications provider, a telecommunications service or telecommunications facility used to provide a telecommunications service for which a certificate of service authority is required pursuant to this section.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 1]

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On July 8, 1998, the municipal respondents, including municipalities, municipal organizations, and municipally owned utilities, petitioned the Federal Communications Commission (FCC or Commission) for an order declaring the state statute unlawful and preempted under 47 U. S.\xc2\xa0C \xc2\xa7253:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cNo State or local statute or regulation, or other State or local legal requirement, may prohibit or have the effect of prohibiting the ability of any entity to provide any interstate or intrastate telecommunications service.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7253(a).

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9cIf, after notice and an opportunity for public comment, the Commission determines that a State or local government has permitted or imposed any statute, regulation, or legal requirement that violates subsection (a) or (b) of this section, the Commission shall preempt the enforcement of such statute, regulation, or legal requirement to the extent necessary to correct such violation or inconsistency.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7253(d).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After notice and comment, the FCC refused to declare the Missouri statute preempted, In\xc2\xa0re Missouri Municipal League, 16 FCC Rcd. 1157 (2001), relying on its own earlier order resolving a challenge to a comparable Texas law, In\xc2\xa0re Public Utility Commission of Texas, 13 FCC Rcd. 3460 (1997), as well as the affirming opinion of the United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit, Abilene v. FCC, 164 F.\xc2\xa03d 49 (1999). The agency concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthe term \xe2\x80\x98any entity\xe2\x80\x99 in section 253(a) \xe2\x80\xa6 was not intended to include political subdivisions of the state, but rather appears to prohibit restrictions on market entry that apply to independent entities subject to state regulation.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 2] 16 FCC Rcd., at 1162. Like the District of Columbia Circuit in Abilene, the FCC also adverted to the principle of Gregory v. Ashcroft, 501 U. S. 452 (1991), that Congress needs to be clear before it constrains traditional state authority to order its government. 16 FCC Rcd., at 1169. But at the same time the Commission rejected preemption, it also denounced the policy behind the Missouri statute, id., at 1162\xe2\x80\x931163, and the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s order carried two appended statements (one for Chairman William E. Kennard and Commissioner Gloria Tristani, id., at 1172, and one by Commissioner Susan Ness, id., at 1173) to the effect that barring municipalities from providing telecommunications substantially disserved the policy behind the Telecommunications Act.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The municipal respondents appealed to the Eighth Circuit, where a panel unanimously reversed the agency disposition, 299 F.\xc2\xa03d 949 (2002), with the explanation that the plain-vanilla \xe2\x80\x9centity,\xe2\x80\x9d especially when modified by \xe2\x80\x9cany,\xe2\x80\x9d manifested sufficiently clear congressional attention to governmental entities to get past Gregory. 299 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 953\xe2\x80\x93955. The decision put the Eighth Circuit at odds with the District of Columbia Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s Abilene opinion, and we granted certiorari to resolve the conflict. 539 U. S. 941 (2003). We now reverse.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the outset, it is well to put aside two considerations that appear in this litigation but fall short of supporting the municipal respondents\xe2\x80\x99 hopes for prevailing on their generous conception of preemption under \xc2\xa7253. The first is public policy, on which the respondents have at the least a respectable position, that fencing governmental entities out of the telecommunications business flouts the public interest. There are, of course, arguments on the other side, against government participation: in a business substantially regulated at the state level, regulation can turn into a public provider\xe2\x80\x99s weapon against private competitors, see, e.g., Brief for Petitioner Southwestern Bell in No. 02\xe2\x80\x931405 et\xc2\xa0al., pp.\xc2\xa017\xe2\x80\x9318; and (if things turn out bad) government utilities that fail leave the taxpayers with the bills. Still, the Chairman of the FCC and Commissioner Tristani minced no words in saying that participation of municipally owned entities in the telecommunications business would \xe2\x80\x9cfurther the goal of the 1996 Act to bring the benefits of competition to all Americans, particularly those who live in small or rural communities in which municipally-owned utilities have great competitive potential.\xe2\x80\x9d 16 FCC Rcd., at 1172. Commissioner Ness said much the same, and a number of amicus briefs in this litigation argue the competitive advantages of letting municipalities furnish telecommunications services, drawing on the role of government operators in extending the electric power lines early in the last century. Brief for City of Abilene et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 14\xe2\x80\x9318; Brief for Consumer Federation of America as Amicus Curiae 7. As we will try to explain, however, infra, at 6\xe2\x80\x9310, it does not follow that preempting state or local barriers to governmental entry into the market would be an effective way to draw municipalities into the business, and in any event the issue here does not turn on the merits of municipal telecommunications services.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The second consideration that fails to answer the question posed in this litigation is the portion of the text that has received great emphasis. The Eighth Circuit trained its analysis on the words \xe2\x80\x9cany entity,\xe2\x80\x9d left undefined by the statute, with much weight being placed on the modifier \xe2\x80\x9cany.\xe2\x80\x9d But concentration on the writing on the page does not produce a persuasive answer here. While an \xe2\x80\x9centity\xe2\x80\x9d can be either public or private, compare, e.g., 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0A. \xc2\xa79604(k)(1) (Supp. 2003) (defining \xe2\x80\x9celigible entity\xe2\x80\x9d as a state or local government body or its agent) with 26 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7269B(c)(1) (defining \xe2\x80\x9centity\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cany corporation, partnership, trust, association, estate, or other form of carrying on a business or activity\xe2\x80\x9d), there is no convention of omitting the modifiers \xe2\x80\x9cpublic and private\xe2\x80\x9d when both are meant to be covered. See, e.g., 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000d\xe2\x80\x937(a)(2) (exposing States to remedies in antidiscrimination suits comparable to those available \xe2\x80\x9cagainst any public or private entity other than a State\xe2\x80\x9d). Nor is coverage of public entities reliably signaled by speaking of \xe2\x80\x9cany\xe2\x80\x9d entity; \xe2\x80\x9cany\xe2\x80\x9d can and does mean different things depending upon the setting. Compare, e.g., United States v. Gonzales, 520 U. S. 1, 5 (1997) (suggesting an expansive meaning of the term \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98any other term of imprisonment\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to include state as well as federal sentences), with Raygor v. Regents of Univ. of Minnesota, 534 U. S. 533, 542\xe2\x80\x93546 (2002) (implying a narrow interpretation of the phrase \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98any claim asserted\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d so as to exclude certain claims dismissed on Eleventh Amendment grounds). To get at Congress\xe2\x80\x99s understanding, what is needed is a broader frame of reference, and in this litigation it helps if we ask how Congress could have envisioned the preemption clause actually working if the FCC applied it at the municipal respondents\xe2\x80\x99 urging. See, e.g., New Jersey Realty Title Ins. Co. v. Division of Tax Appeals of N.\xc2\xa0J., 338 U. S. 665, 673 (1950) (enquiring into the \xe2\x80\x9cthe practical operation and effect\xe2\x80\x9d of a state tax on federal bonds). We think that the strange and indeterminate results of using federal preemption to free public entities from state or local limitations is the key to understanding that Congress used \xe2\x80\x9cany entity\xe2\x80\x9d with a limited reference to any private entity when it cast the preemption net.

\n

III

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In familiar instances of regulatory preemption under the Supremacy Clause, a federal measure preempting state regulation in some precinct of economic conduct carried on by a private person or corporation simply leaves the private party free to do anything it chooses consistent with the prevailing federal law. If federal law, say, preempts state regulation of cigarette advertising, a cigarette seller is left free from advertising restrictions imposed by a State, which is left without the power to control on that matter. See, e.g., Lorillard Tobacco Co. v. Reilly, 533 U. S. 525, 540\xe2\x80\x93553 (2001). On the subject covered, state law just drops out.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But no such simple result would follow from federal preemption meant to unshackle local governments from entrepreneurial limitations. The trouble is that a local government\xe2\x80\x99s capacity to enter an economic market turns not only on the effect of straightforward economic regulation below the national level (including outright bans), but on the authority and potential will of governments at the state or local level to support entry into the market. Preemption of the state advertising restriction freed a seller who otherwise had the legal authority to advertise and the money to do it if that made economic sense. But preempting a ban on government utilities would not accomplish much if the government could not point to some law authorizing it to run a utility in the first place. And preemption would make no difference to anyone if the state regulator were left with control over funding needed for any utility operation and declined to pay for it. In other words, when a government regulates itself (or the subdivision through which it acts) there is no clear distinction between the regulator and the entity regulated. Legal limits on what may be done by the government itself (including its subdivisions) will often be indistinguishable from choices that express what the government wishes to do with the authority and resources it can command. That is why preempting state or local governmental self-regulation (or regulation of political inferiors) would work so differently from preempting regulation of private players that we think it highly unlikely that Congress intended to set off on such uncertain adventures. A few hypotheticals may bring the point home.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Hypotheticals have to rest on some understanding of what \xc2\xa7253 means when it describes subjects of its preemption as laws or regulations that prohibit, expressly or in effect, \xe2\x80\x9cthe ability of any entity\xe2\x80\x9d to provide telecommunications. The reference to \xe2\x80\x9cability\xe2\x80\x9d complicates things. In customary usage, we speak simply of prohibiting a natural or legal person from doing something. To speak in terms of prohibiting their ability to provide a service may mean something different: it may mean denying the entity a capacity or authority to act in the first place. But this is not clear, and it is possible that a law prohibiting the ability to provide telecommunications means a law that limits or cuts back on some preexisting authority (under a different law) to go into the telecommunications business.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If the scope of law subject to preemption under \xc2\xa7253 has the former, broader, meaning, consider how preemption would apply to a state statute authorizing municipalities to operate specified utilities, to provide water and electricity but nothing else.[Footnote 3] The enumeration would certainly have the effect of prohibiting a municipally owned and operated electric utility from entering the telecommunications business (as Congress clearly meant private electric companies to be able to do, see S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 103\xe2\x80\x93367, p.\xc2\xa055 (1994)) and its implicit prohibition would thus be open to FCC preemption. But what if the FCC did preempt the restriction? The municipality would be free of the statute, but freedom is not authority, and in the absence of some further, authorizing legislation the municipality would still be powerless to enter the telecommunications business. There is, after all, no argument that the Telecommunications Act of 1996 is itself a source of federal authority granting municipalities local power that state law does not.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Now assume that \xc2\xa7253 has the narrower construction (preempting only laws that restrict authority derived from a different legal source). Consider a State with plenary authority itself, under its constitution, to operate any variety of utility.[Footnote 4] Assume that its statutes authorized a state-run utility to deliver electric and water services, but drew the line at telecommunications. The restrictive element of that limited authorization would run afoul of \xc2\xa7253 as respondents would construe it. But if, owing to preemption, the state operating utility authority were suddenly free to provide telecommunications and its administrators were raring to enter this new field, where would the necessary capital come from? Surely there is no contention that the Telecommunications Act of 1996 by its own force entails a state agency\xe2\x80\x99s entitlement to unappropriated funds from the state treasury, or to the exercise of state bonding authority.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Or take the application of \xc2\xa7253 preemption to municipalities empowered by state law to furnish services generally, but forbidden by a special statute to exercise that power for the purpose of providing telecommunications services. If the special statute were preempted, a municipality in that State would have a real option to enter the telecommunications business if its own legislative arm so chose and funded the venture. But in a State next door where municipalities lacked such general authority, a local authority would not be able to, and the result would be a national crazy quilt. We will presumably get a crazy quilt, of course, as a consequence of state and local political choices arrived at in the absence of any preemption under \xc2\xa7253, but the crazy quilt of this hypothetical would result not from free political choices but from the fortuitous interaction of a federal preemption law with the forms of municipal authorization law.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, consider the result if a State that previously authorized municipalities to operate a number of utilities including telecommunications changed its law by narrowing the range of authorization. Assume that a State once authorized municipalities to furnish water, electric, and communications services, but sometime after the passage of \xc2\xa7253 narrowed the authorization so as to leave municipalities authorized to enter only the water business. The repealing statute would have a prohibitory effect on the prior ability to deliver telecommunications service and would be subject to preemption. But that would mean that a State that once chose to provide broad municipal authority could not reverse course. A State next door, however, starting with a legal system devoid of any authorization for municipal utility operation, would at the least be free to change its own course by authorizing its municipalities to venture forth. The result, in other words, would be the federal creation of a one-way ratchet. A State or municipality could give the power, but it could not take it away later. Private counterparts could come and go from the market at will, for after any federal preemption they would have a free choice to compete or not to compete in telecommunications; governmental providers could never leave (or, at least, could not leave by a forthright choice to change policy), for the law expressing the government\xe2\x80\x99s decision to get out would be preempted.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The municipal respondents\xe2\x80\x99 answer to the one-way ratchet, and indeed to a host of the incongruities that would follow from preempting governmental restriction on the exercise of its own power, is to rely on \xc2\xa7253(b), which insulates certain state actions taken \xe2\x80\x9con a competitively neutral basis.\xe2\x80\x9d Respondents contend that a State or municipality would be able to make a competitively neutral change of mind to leave the telecommunications market after deciding earlier to enter it or authorize entry. Tr. of Oral Arg. 32\xe2\x80\x9333.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But we think this is not much of an answer. The FCC has understood \xc2\xa7253(b) neutrality to require a statute or regulation affecting all types of utilities in like fashion, as a law removing only governmental entities from telecommunications could not be. See, e.g., In\xc2\xa0re Federal-State Joint Board on Universal Service, 15 FCC Rcd. 15168, 15175\xe2\x80\x9315178, \xc2\xb6 ;\xc2\xb6 ; 19\xe2\x80\x9324 (2000) (declaratory ruling). An even more fundamental weakness in respondents\xe2\x80\x99 answer is shown in briefs filed by amici City of Abilene and Consumer Federation of America. We have no reason to doubt them when they explain how highly unlikely it is that a state decision to withdraw would be \xe2\x80\x9cneutral\xe2\x80\x9d in any sense of the word. There is every reason to expect just the contrary, that legislative choices in this arena would reflect the intent behind the intense lobbying directed to those choices, manifestly intended to impede, not enhance, competition. See, e.g., Chen, Legal Process and Political Economy of Telecommunications Reform, 97 Colum. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 835, 866\xe2\x80\x93868 (1997). After all, the notion that the legislative process addressing governmental utility authority is susceptible to capture by competition-averse private utilities is fully consistent with (and one reason for) the FCC\xe2\x80\x99s position that statutes like Missouri\xe2\x80\x99s disserve the policy objects of the Telecommunications Act of 1996. Given the unlikely application of \xc2\xa7253(b) to state or local choices driven by policy, not business failure, the fair conclusion is that \xc2\xa7253(a), if read respondents\xe2\x80\x99 way, would allow governments to move solely toward authorizing telecommunications operation, with no alternative to reverse course deliberately later on.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, \xc2\xa7253 would not work like a normal preemptive statute if it applied to a governmental unit. It would often accomplish nothing, it would treat States differently depending on the formal structures of their laws authorizing municipalities to function, and it would hold out no promise of a national consistency. We think it farfetched that Congress meant \xc2\xa7253 to start down such a road in the absence of any clearer signal than the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cability of any entity.\xe2\x80\x9d See, e.g., United States v. American Trucking Assns., Inc., 310 U. S. 534, 543 (1940) (Court will not construe a statute in a manner that leads to absurd or futile results).

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens contends that in our use of the hypothetical examples to illustrate the implausibility of the municipal respondents\xe2\x80\x99 reading of \xc2\xa7253, we read the statute in a way that produces anomalous results unnecessarily, whereas a simpler interpretation carrying fewer unhappy consequences is available. The dissent emphasizes the word \xe2\x80\x9cability\xe2\x80\x9d in the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cprohibits or has the effect of prohibiting the ability of any entity\xe2\x80\x9d to furnish telecommunications. With its focus on this word, the dissent concludes that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa7253 prohibits States from withdrawing municipalities\xe2\x80\x99 pre-existing authority to enter the telecommunications business, but does not command that States affirmatively grant either that authority or the means with which to carry it out.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 5. Thus, if a State leaves an earlier grant of authority on the books while limiting it with a legislative ban on telecommunications, the new statute would be preempted, and presumably preemption would also defeat a State\xe2\x80\x99s attempted withdrawal of municipalities\xe2\x80\x99 authority by repealing the preexisting authorization itself.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But on the very next page, Justice Stevens allows (in the course of disagreeing about the one-way ratchet) that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] State may withdraw comprehensive authorization in favor of enumerating specific municipal powers \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 6. It turns out, in other words, that withdrawals of preexisting authority are not (or not inevitably, at any rate) subject to preemption. The dissent goes on to clarify that it means to distinguish between withdrawals of authority that are competitively neutral in the sense of being couched in general terms (and therefore not properly the subject of preemption), and those in which the repealing law expressly targets telecommunications (and therefore properly preempted). \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he one thing a State may not do,\xe2\x80\x9d the dissent explains, \xe2\x80\x9cis enact a statute or regulation specifically aimed at preventing municipalities or other entities from providing telecommunications services.\xe2\x80\x9d But the practical implication of that interpretation is to read out of \xc2\xa7253 the words \xe2\x80\x9cor ha[s] the effect of prohibiting,\xe2\x80\x9d by which Congress signaled its willingness to preempt laws that produce the unwanted effect, even if they do not advertise their prohibitory agenda on their faces. Even if \xc2\xa7253 permitted such a formalistic distinction between implicit and explicit repeals of authority, the result would be incoherence of policy; whether the issue is viewed through the lens of preventing anticompetitive action or the lens of state autonomy from federal interference, there is no justification for preempting only those laws that self-consciously interfere with the delivery of telecommunications services. In short, instead of supplying a more straightforward interpretation of \xc2\xa7253, the dissent ends up reading it in a way that disregards its plain language and entails a policy consequence that Congress could not possibly have intended.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The municipal respondents\xe2\x80\x99 position holds sufficient promise of futility and uncertainty to keep us from accepting it, but a complementary principle would bring us to the same conclusion even on the assumption that preemption could operate straightforwardly to provide local choice, as in some instances it might. Preemption would, for example, leave a municipality with a genuine choice to enter the telecommunications business when state law provided general authority and a newly unfettered municipality wished to fund the effort. But the liberating preemption would come only by interposing federal authority between a State and its municipal subdivisions, which our precedents teach, \xe2\x80\x9care created as convenient agencies for exercising such of the governmental powers of the State as may be entrusted to them in its absolute discretion.\xe2\x80\x9d Wisconsin Public Intervenor v. Mortier, 501 U. S. 597, 607\xe2\x80\x93608 (1991) (internal quotation marks, citations, and alterations omitted); Columbus v. Ours Garage & Wrecker Service, Inc., 536 U. S. 424, 433 (2002). Hence the need to invoke our working assumption that federal legislation threatening to trench on the States\xe2\x80\x99 arrangements for conducting their own governments should be treated with great skepticism, and read in a way that preserves a State\xe2\x80\x99s chosen disposition of its own power, in the absence of the plain statement Gregory requires. What we have said already is enough to show that \xc2\xa7253(a) is hardly forthright enough to pass Gregory: \xe2\x80\x9cability of any entity\xe2\x80\x9d is not limited to one reading, and neither statutory structure nor legislative history points unequivocally to a commitment by Congress to treat governmental telecommunications providers on par with private firms. The want of any \xe2\x80\x9cunmistakably clear\xe2\x80\x9d statement to that effect, 501 U. S., at 460, would be fatal to respondents\xe2\x80\x99 reading.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit is, accordingly, reversed.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the Telecommunications Act of 1996 (1996 Act), Congress created \xe2\x80\x9ca new telecommunications regime designed to foster competition in local telephone markets.\xe2\x80\x9d Verizon Md. Inc. v. Public Serv. Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of Md., 535 U. S. 635, 638 (2002). Reasonable minds have differed as to whether municipalities\xe2\x80\x99 participation in telecommunications markets serves or disserves the statute\xe2\x80\x99s procompetitive goals. On the one hand, some have argued that municipally owned utilities enjoy unfair competitive advantages that will deter entry by private firms and impair the normal development of healthy, competitive markets.[Footnote 1] On the other hand, members of the Federal Communications Commission, the regulatory agency charged with implementation of the 1996 Act, have taken the view that municipal entry \xe2\x80\x9cwould further the goal of the 1996 Act to bring the benefits of competition to all Americans, particularly those who live in small or rural communities in which municipally-owned utilities have great competitive potential.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 2] The answer to the question presented in these cases does not, of course, turn on which side has the better view in this policy debate. It turns on whether Congress itself intended to take sides when it passed the 1996 Act.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In \xc2\xa7253 of the Communications Act of 1934, as added by \xc2\xa7101 of the 1996 Act, Congress provided that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o State or local statute or regulation, or other State or local legal requirement, may prohibit or have the effect of prohibiting the ability of any entity to provide any interstate or intrastate telecommunications service,\xe2\x80\x9d unless the State or local law is \xe2\x80\x9ccompetitively neutral\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary to \xe2\x80\xa6 protect the public safety and welfare, ensure the continued quality of telecommunications services, and safeguard the rights of consumers.\xe2\x80\x9d 47 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7253(a), (b). It is common ground among the parties that Congress intended to include utilities in the category of \xe2\x80\x9centities\xe2\x80\x9d protected by \xc2\xa7253. See, e.g., Reply Brief for Federal Petitioners in No. 02\xe2\x80\x931238 et\xc2\xa0al., p. 16 (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress clearly did intend to preempt state laws that closed the telecommunications market, including those that closed the market to electric or other utilities\xe2\x80\x9d). The legislative history of \xc2\xa7253 confirms the point: Congress clearly meant for \xc2\xa7253 to pre-empt \xe2\x80\x9cexplicit prohibitions on entry by a utility into telecommunications.\xe2\x80\x9d S. Rep. No. 104\xe2\x80\x93230, p. 127 (1996).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But while petitioners acknowledge the unmistakable clarity of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent to protect utilities\xe2\x80\x99 ability to enter local telephone markets, they contend that Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent to protect the subset of utilities that are owned and operated by municipalities is somehow less than clear. The assertion that Congress could have used the term \xe2\x80\x9cany entity\xe2\x80\x9d to include utilities generally, but not municipally owned utilities, must rest on one of two assumptions: Either Congress was unaware that such utilities exist, or it deliberately ignored their existence when drafting \xc2\xa7253. Both propositions are manifestly implausible, given the sheer number of public utilities in the United States.[Footnote 3] Indeed, elsewhere in the 1996 Act, Congress narrowed the definition of the word \xe2\x80\x9cutility,\xe2\x80\x9d as used in the Pole Attachments Act, 47 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7224, to exclude utilities \xe2\x80\x9cowned by \xe2\x80\xa6 any State,\xe2\x80\x9d including its political subdivisions\xe2\x80\x94a clear indication that Congress was aware that many utilities are in fact owned by States and their political subdivisions. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7224(a)(1), (a)(3). Moreover, the question of municipal participation in local telephone markets was clearly brought to Congress\xe2\x80\x99 attention. In hearings on a predecessor bill, Congress heard from a representative of the American Public Power Association who described public utilities\xe2\x80\x99 unique potential to promote competition, particularly in small cities, towns, and rural communities underserved by private companies. Hearings on S. 1822 before the Senate Committee on Commerce, Science, and Transportation, 103d Cong., 2d Sess., 351\xe2\x80\x93360 (1994) (statement of William J. Ray, General Manager, Glasgow Electric Plant Board).[Footnote 4] In short, there is every reason to suppose that Congress meant precisely what it said: No State or local law shall prohibit or have the effect of prohibiting the ability of any entity, public or private, from entering the telecommunications market.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The question that remains is whether reading the statute to give effect to Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent necessarily will produce the absurd results that the Court suggests. Ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x939. \xe2\x80\x9cAs in all cases[,] our task is to interpret the words of [the statute] in light of the purposes Congress sought to serve.\xe2\x80\x9d Chapman v. Houston Welfare Rights Organization, 441 U. S. 600, 608 (1979). Before nullifying Congress\xe2\x80\x99 evident purpose in an effort to avoid hypothetical absurd results, I would first decide whether the statute can reasonably be read so as to avoid such absurdities, without casting aside congressional intent.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court begins its analysis by asking us to imagine how \xc2\xa7253 might apply to \xe2\x80\x9ca state statute authorizing municipalities to operate specified utilities, to provide water and electricity but nothing else,\xe2\x80\x9d or to a State\xe2\x80\x99s failure to provide the necessary capital to a state-run utility \xe2\x80\x9craring\xe2\x80\x9d to enter the telecommunications market. Ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939. Certainly one might plausibly interpret \xc2\xa7253, as the Court does, to forbid States\xe2\x80\x99 refusals to provide broader authorization or to provide necessary capital as impermissible prohibitions on entry. And as the Court observes, such an interpretation would undeniably produce absurd results; it would leave covered entities in a kind of legal limbo, armed with a federal-law freedom to enter the market but lacking the state-law power to do so. But we need not\xe2\x80\x94and in my opinion, should not\xe2\x80\x94interpret \xc2\xa7253 in this fashion. We should instead read the statute\xe2\x80\x99s reference to state and local laws that \xe2\x80\x9cprohibit or have the effect of prohibiting the ability of any entity,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7253(a), to enter the telecommunications business to embody an implicit understanding that the only \xe2\x80\x9centities\xe2\x80\x9d covered by \xc2\xa7253 are entities otherwise able to enter the business\xe2\x80\x94i.e., entities both authorized to provide telecommunications services and capable of providing such services without the State\xe2\x80\x99s direct assistance. In other words, \xc2\xa7253 prohibits States from withdrawing municipalities\xe2\x80\x99 pre-existing authority to enter the telecommunications business, but does not command that States affirmatively grant either that authority or the means with which to carry it out.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Of course, the Court asserts that still other absurd results would follow from application of \xc2\xa7253 pre-emption to state laws that withdraw a municipality\xe2\x80\x99s pre-existing authority to enter the telecommunications business. But these results are, on closer examination, perhaps not so absurd after all. The Court first contends that reading \xc2\xa7253 in this manner will produce a \xe2\x80\x9cnational crazy quilt\xe2\x80\x9d of public telecommunications authority, where the possibility of municipal participation in the telecommunications market turns on the scope of the authority each State has already granted to its subdivisions. Ante, at 9. But as the Court acknowledges, permitting States such as Missouri to prohibit municipalities from providing telecommunications services hardly will help the cause of national consistency. Ibid. That the \xe2\x80\x9ccrazy quilt\xe2\x80\x9d the Court describes is the product of political choices made by Congress rather than state legislatures, see ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310, renders it no more absurd than the \xe2\x80\x9ccrazy quilt\xe2\x80\x9d that will result from leaving the matter of municipal entry entirely to individual States\xe2\x80\x99 discretion.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court also contends that applying \xc2\xa7253 pre-emption to bar withdrawal of authority to enter the telecommunications market will result in \xe2\x80\x9cthe federal creation of a one-way ratchet\xe2\x80\x9d: \xe2\x80\x9cA State or municipality could give the power, but it could not take it away later.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10. But nothing in \xc2\xa7253 prohibits States from scaling back municipalities\xe2\x80\x99 authority in a general way. A State may withdraw comprehensive authorization in favor of enumerating specific municipal powers, or even abolish municipalities altogether. Such general withdrawals of authority may very well \xe2\x80\x9chave the effect of prohibiting\xe2\x80\x9d municipalities\xe2\x80\x99 ability to enter the telecommunications market, see ante, at 13, just as enforcement of corporate governance and tax laws might \xe2\x80\x9chave the effect of prohibiting\xe2\x80\x9d other entities\xe2\x80\x99 ability to enter. \xc2\xa7253(a). But \xc2\xa7253 clearly does not pre-empt every state law that \xe2\x80\x9chas the effect\xe2\x80\x9d of restraining entry. It pre-empts only those that constitute nonneutral restraints on entry. \xc2\xa7253(b). A general redefinition of municipal authority no more constitutes a prohibited nonneutral restraint on entry than enforcement of other laws of general applicability that, practically speaking, may make it more difficult for certain entities to enter the telecommunications business.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As I read the statute, the one thing a State may not do is enact a statute or regulation specifically aimed at preventing municipalities or other entities from providing telecommunications services. This prohibition would certainly apply to a law like Missouri\xe2\x80\x99s, which \xe2\x80\x9cadvertise[s] [its] prohibitory agenda on [its] fac[e].\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13. But it would also apply to a law that accomplished a similar result by other means\xe2\x80\x94for example, a law that permitted only private telecommunications carriers to receive federal universal service support or access to unbundled network elements.[Footnote 5] As the Court notes, there is little reason to think that legislation that targets municipalities\xe2\x80\x99 ability to provide telecommunications services is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98neutral\xe2\x80\x99 in any sense of the word,\xe2\x80\x9d or that it is designed to do anything other than impede competition, rather than enhance it. Ante, at 11. To the extent that reading \xc2\xa7253 to forbid such protectionist legislation creates a \xe2\x80\x9cone-way ratchet,\xe2\x80\x9d it is one perfectly consistent with the goal of promoting competition in the telecommunications market, while otherwise preserving States\xe2\x80\x99 ability to define the scope of authority held by their political subdivisions.[Footnote 6]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s concern about hypothetical absurd results is particularly inappropriate because the pre-emptive effect of \xc2\xa7253 is not automatic, but requires the FCC\xe2\x80\x99s intervention. \xc2\xa7253(d). Rather than assume that the FCC will apply the statute improperly, and rather than stretch our imaginations to identify possible problems in cases not before the Court, we should confront the problem presented by the cases at hand and endorse the most reasonable interpretation of the statute that both fulfills Congress\xe2\x80\x99 purpose and avoids unnecessary infringement on state prerogatives. I would accordingly affirm the judgment of the Court of Appeals.
