BELL, WARDEN v. THOMPSON

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

No. 04\xe2\x80\x93514.Argued April 26, 2005\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 27, 2005

\n

After respondent Thompson was convicted of murder and sentenced to death, Tennessee state courts denied postconviction relief on his claim that his trial counsel had been ineffective for failing to adequately investigate his mental health. His federal habeas attorneys subsequently retained psychologist Dr. Sultan, whose report and deposition contended that Thompson suffered from serious mental illness at the time of his offense. The District Court dismissed the petition, but apparently Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s habeas counsel had failed to include Sultan\xe2\x80\x99s deposition and report in the record. Upholding the dismissal, the Sixth Circuit, inter alia, found no ineffective assistance and did not discuss Sultan\xe2\x80\x99s report and deposition in detail. That court later denied rehearing, but stayed issuance of its mandate pending disposition of Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s certiorari petition. After this Court denied certiorari on December 1, 2003, the Sixth Circuit stayed its mandate again, pending disposition of a petition for rehearing, which this Court denied on January 20, 2004. A copy of that order was filed with the Sixth Circuit on January 23, but the court did not issue its mandate. The State set Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s execution date, and state and federal proceedings began on his competency to be executed. Competency proceedings were pending in the Federal District Court on June 23, 2004, when the Sixth Circuit issued an amended opinion in the federal habeas case, vacating the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s habeas judgment and remanding the case for an evidentiary hearing on the ineffective-assistance claim. The Sixth Circuit supplemented the record on appeal with Sultan\xe2\x80\x99s deposition and explained that its authority to issue an amended opinion five months after this Court denied rehearing was based on its inherent power to reconsider an opinion before issuance of the mandate.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Assuming that Federal Rule of Appellate Procedure 41 authorizes a stay of a mandate following a denial of certiorari and that a court may stay the mandate without entering an order, the Sixth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision to do so here was an abuse of discretion. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0This Court need not decide the scope of the court of appeals\xe2\x80\x99 Rule 41 authority to withhold a mandate in order to resolve this case. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Prominent among the reasons warranting the result here is that the Sixth Circuit did not release its amended opinion for more than five months after this Court denied rehearing. The consequence of delay for the State\xe2\x80\x99s criminal justice system was compounded by the Sixth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s failure to issue an order or otherwise give notice to the parties that it was reconsidering its earlier opinion. The express terms of the Sixth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s stay state that the mandate would be stayed until this Court acted on the rehearing petition. Thus, once rehearing was denied, the stay dissolved by operation of law. Tennessee, relying on the Sixth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s earlier orders and this Court\xe2\x80\x99s certiorari and rehearing denials could assume that the mandate would issue, especially since Thompson sought no additional stay and the Sixth Circuit gave no indication that it might be revisting its earlier decision. The latter point is important, for it is an open question whether a court may exercise its Rule 41(b) authority to extend the time to issue a mandate through mere inaction. Without a formal docket entry neither the parties nor this Court had, or have, any way to know whether the Sixth Circuit had stayed the mandate or simply made a clerical mistake. That court could have spared the parties and state judicial system considerable time and resources had it notified them that it was reviewing its decision. The scheduling of Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s execution and the resulting competency proceedings were steps taken in reliance on the assumption that the federal habeas case was final. That assumption was all the more reasonable because the delay in issuing the mandate took place after this Court had denied certiorari, which usually signals the end of litigation. See Fed. Rule App. Proc. 41(d)(2)(D). The fact that the Sixth Circuit had the opportunity at the rehearing stage to consider the same arguments it eventually adopted in its amended opinion is yet another factor supporting the determination here. A review of the Sultan deposition also reinforces this conclusion. While the evidence would have been relevant to the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis, it is not of such a character as to warrant the Sixth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s extraordinary departure from standard procedures. Finally, by withholding its mandate for months\xe2\x80\x94based on evidence supporting only an arguable constitutional claim\xe2\x80\x94while the State prepared to carry out Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s sentence, the Sixth Circuit did not accord the appropriate level of respect to the State\xe2\x80\x99s judgment that Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s crimes merit the ultimate punishment. See Calderon v. Thompson, 523 U. S. 538, 554\xe2\x80\x93557. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9319.

RICKY BELL, WARDEN, PETITIONER v. GREGORY \nTHOMPSON

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

[June 27, 2005]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Kennedy delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case requires us to consider whether, after we had denied certiorari and a petition for rehearing, the Court of Appeals had the power to withhold its mandate for more than five months without entering a formal order. We hold that, even assuming a court may withhold its mandate after the denial of certiorari in some cases, the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 decision to do so here was an abuse of\ndiscretion.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1985, Gregory Thompson and Joanna McNamara abducted Brenda Blanton Lane from a store parking lot in Shelbyville, Tennessee. After forcing Lane to drive them to a remote location, Thompson stabbed her to death. Thompson offered no evidence during the guilt phase of trial and was convicted by a jury of first-degree murder.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s defense attorneys concentrated their efforts on persuading the sentencing jury that Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s positive qualities and capacity to adjust to prison life provided good reasons for not imposing the death penalty. Before trial, Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s counsel had explored the issue of his mental condition. The trial judge referred Thompson to a state-run mental health facility for a 30-day evaluation. The resulting report indicated that Thompson was competent at the time of the offense and at the time of the examination. The defense team retained their own expert, Dr. George Copple, a clinical psychologist. At sentencing Copple testified that Thompson was remorseful and still had the ability to work and contribute while in prison. Thompson presented the character testimony of a number of witnesses, including former high school teachers, his grandparents, and two siblings. Arlene Cajulao, Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s girlfriend while he was stationed with the Navy in Hawaii, also testified on his behalf. She claimed that Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s behavior became erratic after he suffered head injuries during an attack by three of his fellow servicemen. In rebuttal the State called Dr. Glenn Watson, a clinical psychologist who led the pretrial evaluation of Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s competence. Watson testified that his examination of Thompson revealed no significant mental illness.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The jury sentenced Thompson to death. His conviction and sentence were affirmed on direct review. State v. Thompson, 768 S.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 239 (Tenn. 1989), cert. denied, 497 U. S. 1031 (1990).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In his state postconviction petition, Thompson claimed his trial counsel had been ineffective for failing to conduct an adequate investigation into his mental health. Thompson argued that his earlier head injuries had diminished his mental capacity and that evidence of his condition should have been presented as mitigating evidence during the penalty phase of trial. Under Tennessee law, mental illness that impairs a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s capacity to appreciate the wrongfulness of his conduct or to conform his conduct to the requirements of the law is a mitigating factor in capital sentencing. Tenn. Code Ann. \xc2\xa739\xe2\x80\x932\xe2\x80\x93203(j)(8) (1982) (repealed); \xc2\xa739\xe2\x80\x9313\xe2\x80\x93204(j)(8) (Lexis 2003). The postconviction court denied relief following an evidentiary hearing, and the Tennessee Court of Criminal Appeals affirmed. Thompson v. State, 958 S. W. 2d 156 (1997). The Tennessee Supreme Court denied discretionary review.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thompson renewed his ineffective-assistance-of-counsel claim on federal habeas. Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s attorneys retained a psychologist, Dr. Faye Sultan, to assist with the proceedings. At this point, 13 years had passed since Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s conviction. Sultan examined and interviewed Thompson three times, questioned his family members, and conducted an extensive review of his legal, military, medical, and prison records, App. 12, before diagnosing him as suffering from schizoaffective disorder, bipolar type, id., at 20. She contended that Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s symptoms indicated he was \xe2\x80\x9csuffering serious mental illness at the time of the 1985 offense for which he has been convicted and sentenced. This mental illness would have substantially impaired Mr. Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s ability to conform his conduct to the requirements of the law.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Sultan prepared an expert report on Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s behalf and was also deposed by the State.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In February 2000, the United States District Court for the Eastern District of Tennessee granted the State\xe2\x80\x99s motion for summary judgment and dismissed the habeas petition. The court held that Thompson failed to show that the state court\xe2\x80\x99s resolution of his claim rested on an unreasonable application of Supreme Court precedent or on an unreasonable determination of the facts in light of the evidence presented in state court. See 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d). The District Court also stated that Thompson had not presented \xe2\x80\x9cany significant probative evidence that [he] was suffering from a significant mental disease that should have been presented to the jury during the punishment phase as mitigation.\xe2\x80\x9d No. 4:98\xe2\x80\x93CV006 (ED Tenn., Feb. 17, 2000), App. to Pet. for Cert. 270. Sultan\xe2\x80\x99s deposition and report, however, had apparently not been included in the District Court record.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s appeal to the Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit was pending, he filed a motion in the District Court under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 60(b) requesting that the court supplement the record with Sultan\xe2\x80\x99s expert report and deposition. Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s habeas counsel at the time explained that the failure to include the Sultan evidence in the summary judgment record was an oversight. Thompson also asked the Court of Appeals to hold his case in abeyance pending a ruling from the District Court and attached the Sultan evidence in support of his motion.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court denied the Rule 60(b) motion as untimely, and the Court of Appeals denied Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s motion to hold his appeal in abeyance. On January 9, 2003, a divided panel of the Court of Appeals affirmed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s denial of habeas relief. 315 F. 3d 566. The lead opinion, authored by Judge Suhrheinrich, reasoned that there was no ineffective assistance of counsel because Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s attorneys were aware of his head injuries and made appropriate inquiries into his mental fitness. Id., at 589\xe2\x80\x93592. In particular, Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s attorneys had requested that the trial court order a competency evaluation. A team of experts at the Middle Tennessee Mental Health Institute, a state-run facility, found \xe2\x80\x9cno mental illness, mental defect, or insanity.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 589. Dr. George Copple, the clinical psychologist retained by Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s attorneys, also \xe2\x80\x9cfound no evidence of mental illness.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Judge Suhrheinrich emphasized that none of the experts retained by Thompson since trial had offered an opinion on his mental condition at the time of the crime. Id., at 589\xe2\x80\x93592. The lead opinion contained a passing reference to Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s unsuccessful Rule 60(b) motion, but did not discuss the Sultan deposition or expert report in any detail. Id., at 583, n.\xc2\xa013. Judge Moore concurred in the result based on Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s failure to present \xe2\x80\x9cevidence that his counsel knew or should have known either that Thompson was mentally ill or that his mental condition was deteriorating at the time of his trial or at the time of his crime.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 595.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thompson filed a petition for rehearing. The petition placed substantial emphasis on the Sultan evidence, quoting from both her deposition and expert report. The Court of Appeals denied the petition for rehearing and stayed the issuance of its mandate pending the disposition of Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s petition for certiorari.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court denied certiorari on December 1, 2003. 540 U. S. 1051. The following day, Thompson filed a motion in the Court of Appeals seeking to extend the stay of mandate pending disposition of his petition for rehearing in this Court. The Court of Appeals granted the motion and \xe2\x80\x9cordered that the mandate be stayed to allow appellant time to file a petition for rehearing from the denial of the writ of certiorari, and thereafter until the Supreme Court disposes of the case.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 348. On January 20, 2004, this Court denied Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s petition for rehearing. 540 U. S. 1158. A copy of the order was filed with the Court of Appeals on January 23, 2004. The Court of Appeals, however, did not issue its mandate.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The State, under the apparent assumption that the federal habeas corpus proceedings had terminated, filed a motion before the Tennessee Supreme Court requesting that an execution date be set. The court scheduled Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s execution for August 19, 2004.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0From February to June 2004, there were proceedings in both state and federal courts related to Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s present competency to be executed under Ford v. Wainwright, 477 U. S. 399 (1986). The state courts, after considering Sultan\xe2\x80\x99s testimony (which was based in part on followup observations after her initial 1998 examination) as well as that of other experts, found Thompson competent to be executed. Thompson v. State, 134 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d 168 (Tenn. 2004). Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s Ford claim was still pending before the Federal District Court when on June 23, 2004, some seven months after this Court denied certiorari, the Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit issued an amended opinion in Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s initial federal habeas case. 373 F. 3d 688. The new decision vacated the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment denying habeas relief and remanded the case for an evidentiary hearing on Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s ineffective-assistance-of-counsel claim. Id., at 691\xe2\x80\x93692. The Court of Appeals relied on its equitable powers to supplement the record on appeal with Dr. Sultan\xe2\x80\x99s 1999 deposition after finding that it was \xe2\x80\x9capparently negligently omitted\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cprobative of Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s mental state at the time of the crime.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 691. The court also explained its authority to issue an amended opinion five months after this Court denied a petition for rehearing: \xe2\x80\x9c[W]e rely on our inherent power to reconsider our opinion prior to the issuance of the mandate, which has not yet issued in this case.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 691\xe2\x80\x93692. Judge Suhrheinrich authored a lengthy separate opinion concurring in part and dissenting in part, which explained that his chambers initiated the sU. S.onte reconsideration of the case. He agreed with the majority about the probative value of the Sultan deposition, referring to the evidence as \xe2\x80\x9ccritical.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 733. Unlike the majority, however, Judge Suhrheinrich would have relied upon fraud on the court to justify the decision to expand the record and issue an amended opinion. Id., at 725\xe2\x80\x93726, 729\xe2\x80\x93742. He found \xe2\x80\x9cimplausible\xe2\x80\x9d the explanation offered by Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s habeas counsel for his failure to include the Sultan deposition in the District Court record, id., at 742, and speculated that counsel \xe2\x80\x9cplanned to unveil Dr. Sultan\xe2\x80\x99s opinion on the eve of Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s execution,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 738, n. 21.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari. 543 U. S. ___ (2005).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At issue in this case is the scope of the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 authority to withhold the mandate pursuant to Federal Rule of Appellate Procedure 41. As relevant, the Rule provides:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(b) When Issued. The court\xe2\x80\x99s mandate must issue 7 calendar days after the time to file a petition for rehearing expires, or 7 calendar days after entry of an order denying a timely petition for panel rehearing, petition for rehearing en banc, or motion for stay of mandate, whichever is later. The court may shorten or extend the time.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(c) Effective Date. The mandate is effective when issued.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(d) Staying the Mandate.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(1) On Petition for Rehearing or Motion. The timely filing of a petition for panel rehearing, petition for rehearing en banc, or motion for stay of mandate, stays the mandate until disposition of the petition or motion, unless the court orders otherwise.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(2) Pending Petition for Certiorari.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(A) A party may move to stay the mandate pending the filing of a petition for a writ of certiorari in the Supreme Court. The motion must be served on all parties and must show that the certiorari petition would present a substantial question and that there is good cause for a stay.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(B) The stay must not exceed 90 days, unless the period is extended for good cause or unless the party who obtained the stay files a petition for the writ and so notifies the circuit clerk in writing within the period of the stay. In that case, the stay continues until the Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s final disposition.

\n

.....

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(D) The court of appeals must issue the mandate immediately when a copy of a Supreme Court order denying the petition for writ of certiorari is filed.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Tennessee argues that the Court of Appeals was required to issue the mandate following this Court\xe2\x80\x99s denial of Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s petition for certiorari. The State\xe2\x80\x99s position rests on Rule 41(d)(2)(D), which states that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he court of appeals must issue the mandate immediately when a copy of a Supreme Court order denying the petition for writ of certiorari is filed.\xe2\x80\x9d This provision, the State points out, admits of no exceptions, so the mandate should have issued on the date that a copy of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s order denying certiorari was filed with the Court of Appeals, i.e., December 8, 2003.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The State further contends that because the mandate should have issued in December 2003, the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 amended opinion was in essence a recall of the mandate. If this view is correct, the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 decision to revisit its earlier opinion must satisfy the standard established by Calderon v. Thompson, 523 U. S. 538 (1998). Calderon held that \xe2\x80\x9cwhere a federal court of appeals sua sponte recalls its mandate to revisit the merits of an earlier decision denying habeas corpus relief to a state prisoner, the court abuses its discretion unless it acts to avoid a miscarriage of justice as defined by our habeas corpus jurisprudence.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 558. See also Schlup v. Delo, 513 U. S. 298 (1995); Sawyer v. Whitley, 505 U. S. 333 (1992).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thompson counters by arguing that Rule 41(d)(2)(D) is determinative only when the court of appeals enters a stay of the mandate to allow the Supreme Court to dispose of a petition for certiorari. The provision, Thompson says, does not affect the court of appeals\xe2\x80\x99 broad discretion to enter a stay for other reasons. He relies on Rule 41(b), which provides the court of appeals may \xe2\x80\x9cshorten or extend the time\xe2\x80\x9d in which to issue the mandate. Because the authority vested by Rule 41(b) is not limited to the period before a petition for certiorari is denied, he argues that the Court of Appeals had the authority to stay its mandate following this Court\xe2\x80\x99s denial of certiorari and rehearing. Although the Court of Appeals failed to issue an order staying the mandate after we denied rehearing, Thompson asserts that the court exercised its Rule 41(b) powers by simply failing to issue it.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To resolve this case, we need not adopt either party\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of Rule 41. Instead, we hold that\xe2\x80\x94assuming, arguendo, both that the Rule authorizes a stay of the mandate following the denial of certiorari and also that a court may stay the mandate without entering an order\xe2\x80\x94here the Court of Appeals abused its discretion in doing so.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We find an abuse of discretion for the following reasons. \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Prominent among our concerns is the length of time between this Court\xe2\x80\x99s denial of certiorari and the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 issuance of its amended opinion. We denied Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s petition for certiorari in December 2003 and his petition for rehearing one month later. From this last denial, however, the Court of Appeals delayed issuing its mandate for over five months, releasing its amended opinion in June.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The consequence of delay for the State\xe2\x80\x99s criminal justice system was compounded by the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 failure to issue an order or otherwise give notice to the parties that the court was reconsidering its earlier opinion. The Court of Appeals had issued two earlier orders staying its mandate. The first order stayed the mandate pending disposition of Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s petition for certiorari. The second order extended the stay to allow Thompson time to file a petition for rehearing with this Court and \xe2\x80\x9cthereafter until the Supreme Court disposes of the case.\xe2\x80\x9d So by the express terms of the second order the mandate was not to be stayed after this Court acted; and when we denied rehearing on January 20, 2004, the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 second stay dissolved by operation of law. Tennessee, acting in reliance on the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 earlier orders and our denial of certiorari and rehearing, could assume that the mandate would\xe2\x80\x94indeed must\xe2\x80\x94issue. While it might have been prudent for the State to verify that the mandate had issued, it is understandable that it proceeded to schedule an execution date. Thompson, after all, had not sought an additional stay of the mandate, and the Court of Appeals had given no indication that it might be revisiting its earlier decision.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This latter point is important. It is an open question whether a court may exercise its Rule 41(b) authority to extend the time for the mandate to issue through mere inaction. Even assuming, however, that a court could effect a stay for a short period of time by withholding the mandate, a delay of five months is different in kind. \xe2\x80\x9cBasic to the operation of the judicial system is the principle that a court speaks through its judgments and orders.\xe2\x80\x9d Murdaugh Volkswagen, Inc. v. First National Bank of South Carolina, 741 F.\xc2\xa02d 41, 44 (CA4 1984). Without a formal docket entry neither the parties nor this Court had, or have, any way to know whether the court had stayed the mandate or simply made a clerical mistake. Cf. Ballard v. Commissioner, 544 U. S. ___, ___ (2005) (slip op., at 17). The dissent claims \xe2\x80\x9cthe failure to notify the parties was likely due to a simple clerical error\xe2\x80\x9d on the part of the Clerk\xe2\x80\x99s office. Post, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313 (opinion of Breyer, J.). The record lends no support to this speculation. The dissent also fails to explain why it is willing to apply a \xe2\x80\x9cpresumption of regularity\xe2\x80\x9d to the panel\xe2\x80\x99s actions but not to the Clerk\xe2\x80\x99s. Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals could have spared the parties and the state judicial system considerable time and resources if it had notified them that it was reviewing its original panel decision. After we denied Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s petition for rehearing, Tennessee scheduled his execution date. This, in turn, led to various proceedings in state and federal court to determine Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s present competency to be executed. See, e.g., Thompson v. State, 134 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d 168 (Tenn. 2004). All of these steps were taken in reliance on the mistaken impression that Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s first federal habeas case was final. The State had begun to \xe2\x80\x9cinvok[e] its entire legal and moral authority in support of executing its judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d Calderon v. Thompson, 523 U. S., at 556\xe2\x80\x93557.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The parties\xe2\x80\x99 assumption that Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s habeas proceedings were complete was all the more reasonable because the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 delay in issuing its mandate took place after we had denied certiorari. As a practical matter, a decision by this Court denying discretionary review usually signals the end of litigation. While Rule 41(b) may authorize a court to stay the mandate after certiorari is denied, the circumstances where such a stay would be warranted are rare. See, e.g., First Gibraltar Bank, FSB v. Morales, 42 F.\xc2\xa03d 895 (CA5 1995); Alphin v. Henson, 552 F.\xc2\xa02d 1033 (CA4 1977). In the typical case, where the stay of mandate is entered solely to allow this Court time to consider a petition for certiorari, Rule 41(d)(2)(D) provides the default: \xe2\x80\x9cThe court of appeals must issue the mandate immediately when a copy of a Supreme Court order denying the petition for writ of certiorari is filed.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0By providing a mechanism for correcting errors in the courts of appeals before Supreme Court review is requested, the Federal Rules of Appellate Procedure ensure that litigation following the denial of certiorari will be infrequent. See Fed. Rule App. Proc. 40(a) (\xe2\x80\x9cUnless the time is shortened or extended by order or local rule, a petition for panel rehearing may be filed within 14 days after entry of judgment\xe2\x80\x9d). See also Fed. Rules App. Proc. 35 (rehearing en banc), 40 (panel rehearing).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Indeed, in this case Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s petition for rehearing and suggestion for rehearing en banc pressed the same arguments that eventually were adopted by the Court of Appeals in its amended opinion. The Sultan evidence, first presented to the Court of Appeals as an attachment to Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s motion to hold his appeal in abeyance, was quoted extensively in the petition for rehearing to the Court of Appeals. Pet. for Rehearing and Suggestion for Rehearing En Banc in No. 2:00\xe2\x80\x935516 (CA6), pp. 12\xe2\x80\x9320, 28\xe2\x80\x9331. After the request for rehearing was denied, the State could have assumed with good reason that the Court of Appeals was not impressed by Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s arguments based on the Sultan evidence. The court\xe2\x80\x99s opportunity to consider these arguments at the rehearing stage is yet another factor supporting our determination that the decision to withhold the mandate was in error. Cf. Calderon v. Thompson, supra, at 551\xe2\x80\x93553 (questioning whether a \xe2\x80\x9cmishandled law clerk transition\xe2\x80\x9d and the \xe2\x80\x9cfailure of another judge to notice the action proposed by the original panel\xe2\x80\x9d would justify recalling the mandate in a nonhabeas case).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dissent\xe2\x80\x99s explanation of how the Sultan evidence was overlooked is inaccurate in several respects. For example, the statements that the \xe2\x80\x9cSultan documents were not in the initial record on appeal,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 9, and that \xe2\x80\x9cthe panel previously had not seen these documents\xe2\x80\x9d before the rehearing stage, id., at 9, convey the wrong impression. Although the Sultan evidence was not part of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s summary judgment record, the documents were included in the certified record on appeal as attachments to Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 60(b) motion. Record 133; Docket Entry 4/5/02 in No. 98\xe2\x80\x93CV\xe2\x80\x936 (ED Tenn.); Docket Entry 4/10/02 in No. 00\xe2\x80\x935516 (CA6). The dissent also argues the petition for rehearing did not adequately bring the Sultan evidence to the attention of the Court of Appeals. Post, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310, 13\xe2\x80\x9314. This is simply untrue. The original panel opinion, which did not discuss the Sultan evidence in any detail, emphasized that Thompson had failed to produce any evidence that he was mentally ill at the time of his offense. 315 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 590; id., at 595\xe2\x80\x93596 (Moore, J., concurring). The petition for rehearing attacked this conclusion in no uncertain terms and placed the Sultan evidence front and center. Here, for example, is an excerpt from the petition\xe2\x80\x99s table of contents:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cII. THE CONCLUSION THAT THERE IS NO EVIDENCE PRESENTED IN THE RECORD OF THOMPSON\xe2\x80\x99S MENTAL ILLESS AT THE TIME OF THE CRIME IS WRONG

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cA. Thompson Has Set Forth Above The Record Facts Demonstrating His Mental Illness At The Time of The Crime

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cB. The Majority Overlooks The Facts And Expert Opinion Set Forth In Dr. Sultan\xe2\x80\x99s Report and Deposition.\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Rehearing and Suggestion for Rehearing En Banc in No. 2:00\xe2\x80\x935516 (CA6), p. ii.

\n

\nSee also id., at 1 (mentioning the Sultan evidence in the second paragraph of the statement in support of panel rehearing). The rehearing petition did not explain why Sultan\xe2\x80\x99s deposition and expert report had been omitted from the summary judgment record but that is beside the point. The petition acknowledged that the Sultan evidence was first presented to the District Court as an attachment to the Rule 60(b) motion, id., at 29, and gave the Sultan evidence a prominent and explicit mention in the table of contents. It is difficult to see how Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s counsel could have been clearer in telling the Court of Appeals that it was wrong. The dissent\xe2\x80\x99s treatment of this issue assumes that judges forget even the basic details of a capital case only one month after issuing a 38-page opinion and that judges cannot be relied upon to read past the first page of a petition for rehearing. The problem is that the dissent cannot have it both ways: If the Sultan evidence is as crucial as the dissent claims, it would not easily have been overlooked by the Court of Appeals at the rehearing stage.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our review of the Sultan deposition reinforces our conclusion that the Court of Appeals abused its discretion by withholding the mandate. Had the Sultan deposition and report been fully considered in the federal habeas proceedings, it no doubt would have been relevant to the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis. Based on the Sultan deposition Thompson could have argued he suffered from mental illness at the time of his crime that would have been a mitigating factor under Tennessee law and that his trial attorneys were constitutionally ineffective for failing to conduct an adequate investigation into his mental health.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Relevant though the Sultan evidence may be, however, it is not of such a character as to warrant the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 extraordinary departure from standard appellate procedures. There are ample grounds to conclude the evidence was unlikely to have altered the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s resolution of Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s ineffective-assistance-of-counsel claim. Sultan examined Thompson for the first time on August 20, 1998, App. 37, some 13 years after Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s crime and conviction. She relied on the deterioration in Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s present mental health\xe2\x80\x94something that obviously was not observable at the time of trial\xe2\x80\x94as evidence of his condition in 1985. (Indeed, there was a marked decline in his condition during the 6-month period between Sultan\xe2\x80\x99s first two visits. Id., at 51\xe2\x80\x9358.) Sultan\xe2\x80\x99s findings regarding Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s condition in 1985 are contradicted by the testimony of two experts who examined him at the time of trial, Dr. Watson and Dr. Copple. Watson performed a battery of tests at the Middle Tennessee Mental Health Institute, where Thompson was referred by the trial court for an examination, and concluded that Thompson \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[did] not appear to be suffering from any complicated mental disorder which would impair his capacity to appreciate the wrongfulness of the alleged offenses, or which would impair his capacity to conform his conduct to the requirements of the law.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 19 Tr. 164. Indeed, Watson presented substantial evidence supporting his conclusion that Thompson was malingering for mental illness. Id., at 151\xe2\x80\x93152; 20 id., at 153\xe2\x80\x93160. For example, Thompson claimed he could not read despite a B average in high school and one year\xe2\x80\x99s college credit. 19 id., at 137; 20 id., at 151. Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s test scores also indicated that he was attempting to fake schizophrenia. 20 id., at 153\xe2\x80\x93154. Copple, the psychologist retained by Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s defense team, agreed with Watson that Thompson was not suffering from mental illness. 19 id., at 58. Had the Sultan deposition been included in the District Court record, Thompson still would have faced an uphill battle to obtaining federal habeas relief. He would have had to argue that his trial attorneys should have continued to investigate his mental health even after both Watson and Copple had opined that there was nothing to uncover.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Sultan\xe2\x80\x99s testimony does not negate Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s responsibility for committing the underlying offense, but it does bear upon an argument that Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s attorneys could have presented at sentencing. Sultan\xe2\x80\x99s ultimate conclusion\xe2\x80\x94that Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s mental illness substantially impaired his ability to conform his conduct to the requirements of the law\xe2\x80\x94is couched in the language of a mitigating factor under Tennessee law. Tenn. Code Ann. \xc2\xa739\xe2\x80\x932\xe2\x80\x93203(j)(8) (1982). See also \xc2\xa739\xe2\x80\x9313\xe2\x80\x93204(j)(8) (Lexis 2003). Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s trial attorneys, however, chose not to pursue a mitigation strategy based on mental illness, stressing instead character evidence from family and friends and expert testimony that he had the capacity to adjust to prison. Thompson v. State, 958 S. W. 2d, at 164\xe2\x80\x93165. This strategic calculation, while ultimately unsuccessful, was based on a reasonable investigation into Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s background. Sultan relied on three witnesses in preparing her report: Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s grandmother, sister, and ex-girlfriend. These witnesses not only were interviewed by the defense attorneys; they testified at sentencing. Consultation with these witnesses, when combined with the opinions of Watson and Copple, provided an adequate basis for Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s attorneys to conclude that focusing on Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s mental health was not the best strategy. As the Tennessee Court of Criminal Appeals noted, \xe2\x80\x9cBecause two experts did not detect brain damage, counsel cannot be faulted for discarding a strategy that could not be supported by a medical opinion.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 165.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Without a single citation to the record, the dissent suggests that Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s attorneys failed to conduct adequate interviews of the defense witnesses on whom Sultan relied in her report. Post, at 14\xe2\x80\x9315. Most of the information on Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s childhood was provided to Sultan by Nora Jean Wharton, Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s older sister. App. 16\xe2\x80\x9318. Setting aside the fact that Thompson did not argue in state court that his counsel\xe2\x80\x99s interview of Wharton was inadequate, Thompson v. State, 958 S. W. 2d, at 160\xe2\x80\x93169, Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s attorneys cannot be faulted for failing to elicit from her any details on Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s difficult home life. After all, Wharton testified at trial that Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s childhood was \xe2\x80\x9cpoor,\xe2\x80\x9d but \xe2\x80\x9cvery happy.\xe2\x80\x9d 18 Tr. 3. The dissent also implies that the experts who examined Thompson lacked information necessary to reach an accurate assessment. The record refutes this assertion. In conducting his examination, Watson had access to Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s social history and military records. 19 id., at 149; 20 id., at 186 (Exh. 102, pp.\xc2\xa011, 27\xe2\x80\x9328). Watson was also aware of the prior head injuries as well as Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s claim that he heard voices. 19 id., at 152; 20 id., at 154\xe2\x80\x93155. Nevertheless, Watson, whose evaluation was contemporaneous with the trial, found no evidence that Thompson was mentally ill at the time of the crime. Watson\xe2\x80\x99s report was unequivocal on this point:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Mr. Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s speech and communication were coherent, rational, organized, relevant, and devoid of circumstantiality, tangentiality, looseness of associations, paranoid ideation, ideas of reference, delusions, and other indicators of a thought disorder. His affect was appropriate to his thought content, and he exhibited no flight of ideas, manic, depressed, or bizarre behaviors, and his speech was not pressured nor rapid. He exhibited none of the signs of an affective illness. His judgment and insight are rather poor. Psychological testing revealed him to be functioning in the average range intellectually, to exhibit no signs of organicity or brain damage on the Bender-Gestalt Test and the Bender Interference Procedure. Personality profiles revealed no evidence of a psychosis, but indicated malingering in the mental illness direction. (For example, the schizophrenic score was at T 120, while clinical observations revealed no evidence of a thought disorder.) Mr. Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s memory for recent and remote events appeared unimpaired.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 20 id., at 159\xe2\x80\x93160.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Sultan\xe2\x80\x99s testimony provides some support for the argument that the strategy of emphasizing Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s positive attributes was a mistake in light of Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s deteriorated condition 13 years after the trial. This evidence, however, would not come close to satisfying the miscarriage of justice standard under Calderon had the Court of Appeals recalled the mandate. Neither, in our view, did this evidence justify the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 decision to withhold the mandate without notice to the parties, which in turn led the State to proceed for five months on the mistaken assumption that the federal habeas proceedings had terminated. The dissent suggests that failing to take account of the Sultan evidence would result in a \xe2\x80\x9cmiscarriage of justice,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 1\xe2\x80\x932, 16, but the dissent uses that phrase in a way that is inconsistent with our precedents. In Sawyer v. Whitley, 505 U. S., at 545\xe2\x80\x93547, this Court held that additional mitigating evidence could not meet the miscarriage of justice standard. Only evidence that affects a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s eligibility for the death penalty\xe2\x80\x94which the Sultan evidence is not\xe2\x80\x94can support a miscarriage of justice claim in the capital sentencing context. Id., at 547; Calderon, 523 U. S., at 559\xe2\x80\x93560.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0One last consideration informs our review of the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 actions. In Calderon, we held that federalism concerns, arising from the unique character of federal habeas review of state-court judgments, and the policies embodied in the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 required an additional presumption against recalling the mandate. This case also arises from federal habeas corpus review of a state conviction. While the State\xe2\x80\x99s reliance interest is not as strong in a case where, unlike Calderon, the mandate has not issued, the finality and comity concerns that animated Calderon are implicated here. Here a dedicated judge discovered what he believed to have been an error, and we are respectful of the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 willingness to correct a decision that it perceived to have been mistaken. A court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion under Rule 41 must be exercised, however, in a way that is consistent with the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98State\xe2\x80\x99s interest in the finality of convictions that have survived direct review within the state court system.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 555 (quoting Brecht v. Abrahamson, 507 U. S. 619, 635 (1993)). Tennessee expended considerable time and resources in seeking to enforce a capital sentence rendered 20 years ago, a sentence that reflects the judgment of the citizens of Tennessee that Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s crimes merit the ultimate punishment. By withholding the mandate for months\xe2\x80\x94based on evidence that supports only an arguable constitutional claim\xe2\x80\x94while the State prepared to carry out Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s sentence, the Court of Appeals did not accord the appropriate level of respect to that judgment. See Calderon v. Thompson, supra, at 554\xe2\x80\x93557.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals may have been influenced by Sultan\xe2\x80\x99s unsettling account of Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s condition during one of her visits. She described Thompson as being in \xe2\x80\x9cterrible psychological condition,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cphysically filthy,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9chighly agitated.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 51. This testimony raised questions about Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s deteriorating mental health and perhaps his competence to be executed, but these concerns were properly addressed in separate proceedings. Based on the most recent state-court decision, which rejected the argument that Thompson is not competent to be executed, it appears that his condition has improved. Thompson v. State, 134 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d, at 184\xe2\x80\x93185. Proceedings on this issue were underway in the District Court when the Court of Appeals issued its second opinion. If those proceedings resume, the District Court will have an opportunity to address these matters again and in light of the current evidence.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Taken together these considerations convince us that the Court of Appeals abused any discretion Rule 41 arguably granted it to stay its mandate, without entering a formal order, after this Court had denied certiorari. The judgment of the Court of Appeals for Sixth Circuit is reversed.

\n

It is so ordered.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Breyer, with whom Justice Stevens, Justice Souter, and Justice Ginsburg join, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This capital case arises out of unusual circumstances\xe2\x80\x94circumstances of a kind that I have not previously experienced in the 25 years I have served on the federal bench. After an appellate court writes and releases an opinion, but before it issues its mandate, the writing judge, through happenstance, comes across a document that (he reasonably believes) shows not only that the court\xe2\x80\x99s initial decision is wrong but that the decision will lead to a serious miscarriage of justice. What is the judge to do?

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0What the judge did here was to spend time\xe2\x80\x94hundreds of hours (while a petition for certiorari was pending before this Court and during the five months following our denial of the petition for rehearing)\xe2\x80\x94reviewing the contents of the vast record with its many affidavits, reports, transcripts, and other documents accumulated in the course of numerous state and federal proceedings during the preceding 20 years. The judge ultimately concluded that his initial instinct about the document was correct. The document was critically important. It could affect the outcome of what is, and has always been, the major issue in the case. To consider the case without reference to it could mean a miscarriage of justice.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judge consequently wrote a lengthy opinion (almost 30,000 words) explaining what had happened. The other members of the panel did not agree with everything in that opinion, but they did agree that their initial decision must be vacated.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court commendably describes what occurred as follows: A \xe2\x80\x9cdedicated judge discovered what he believed to have been an error, and we are respectful of the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 willingness to correct a decision that it perceived to have been mistaken.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 18. The Court, however, does not decide this case in a manner consistent with that observation. A somewhat more comprehensive account of the nature of the \xe2\x80\x9cerror\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94of the matter at stake, of the importance of the document, of the mystery of its late appearance, of the potential for a miscarriage of justice\xe2\x80\x94should help make apparent the difficult circumstance the panel believed it faced. It will also explain why there was no \xe2\x80\x9cabuse\xe2\x80\x9d of discretion in the panel\xe2\x80\x99s effort to \xe2\x80\x9ccorrect a decision that it perceived to have been mistaken.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Judge Suhrheinrich, the panel member who investigated the record, is an experienced federal judge, serving since 1984 as a federal trial court judge and since 1990 as a federal appellate judge. He wrote a lengthy account of the circumstances present here. To understand this case, one must read that full account and then compare it with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s truncated version. I provide a rough summary of the matter based upon my own reading of his opinion. 373 F.\xc2\xa03d 688, 692\xe2\x80\x93742 (CA6 2004).

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The panel\xe2\x80\x99s initial decision, issued on January 9, 2003, focused upon an issue often raised when federal habeas courts review state proceedings in a capital case, namely, the effectiveness of counsel at the original trial. 315 F.\xc2\xa03d 566, 587\xe2\x80\x93594 (CA6 2003). See Strickland v. Washington, 466 U. S. 668 (1984). In this instance, the federal ineffective-assistance claim was that state trial counsel had not sufficiently investigated the background of the defendant, Gregory Thompson. Thompson claimed that an adequate investigation would have shown, to the satisfaction of testifying experts, that he suffered from episodes of schizophrenia at the time of the crime. The schizophrenia\xe2\x80\x94though episodic\xe2\x80\x94would have proved a mitigating circumstance at the penalty phase. 373 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 697\xe2\x80\x93698, and n.\xc2\xa04.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s trial took place in a Tennessee state court, where he was found guilty of murder and sentenced to death. His state-appointed counsel put on no defense at trial. At sentencing, however, counsel sought to show that Thompson was schizophrenic. State forensic psychologists examined Thompson and concluded that Thompson, probably \xe2\x80\x9cmalingering,\xe2\x80\x9d did not show genuine and significant symptoms of schizophrenia at that time and was not mentally ill. A clinical psychologist hired by Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s counsel examined Thompson for eight hours and reached approximately the same conclusion: he said that Thompson was not then mentally ill. Id., at 692, 694\xe2\x80\x93695.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thompson raised the issue of his mental condition in state postconviction proceedings, which he initiated in 1990. His expert witness, Dr. Gillian Blair, testified (with much supportive material) that Thompson was by that time clearly displaying serious schizophrenic symptoms\xe2\x80\x94voice illusions, attempts at physical self-mutilation, and the like. Indeed, the State conceded that he was under a regime of major antipsychotic medication. But Dr. Blair said that she could not determine whether Thompson had been similarly afflicted (i.e., suffering from episodes of schizophrenia) at the time of the crime without a thorough background investigation\xe2\x80\x94funds for which the state court declined to make available. The state court then ruled in the State\xe2\x80\x99s favor. Id., at 694\xe2\x80\x93695.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thompson filed a habeas petition in Federal District Court about eight months after the state court\xe2\x80\x99s denial of postconviction relief became final. As I said above, see supra, at 3, he claimed ineffective assistance of counsel. The Federal District Court appointed counsel, an assistant federal public defender. Counsel then obtained the services of two experts, Dr. Barry Crown and Dr. Faye Sultan. Both examined Thompson, and the latter, Dr. Sultan, conducted the more thorough background investigation that Dr. Blair had earlier sought. The State, after deposing Dr. Sultan, moved for summary judgment. 373 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 696, 700\xe2\x80\x93704, 711.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court granted that motion on the ground that \xe2\x80\x9cThompson has not provided this Court with anything other than factually unsupported allegations that he was incompetent at the time he committed the crime,\xe2\x80\x9d nor \xe2\x80\x9chas Thompson provided this Court with any significant probative evidence that [he] was suffering from a significant mental disease that should have been presented to the jury during the punishment phase as mitigation evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 712\xe2\x80\x93713 (quoting District Court\xe2\x80\x99s memorandum opinion (emphasis and internal quotation marks omitted)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thompson (now with a new public defender as counsel) appealed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s grant of summary judgment in the State\xe2\x80\x99s favor. (A little over a year later, while the appeal was still pending, Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s new counsel, apparently having discovered that Dr. Sultan\xe2\x80\x99s deposition and report had not been included in the record before the District Court, filed a motion in that court for relief from judgment under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 60(b), seeking to supplement the record with those documents. Counsel also filed a motion in the appellate court, with the Sultan deposition attached, requesting that the appeal be held in abeyance while the District Court considered the Rule 60(b) motion. Both motions were denied, and Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s counsel did not take an appeal from the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s denial of the Rule 60(b) motion.) 373 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 714\xe2\x80\x93715, and n.\xc2\xa010, 724\xe2\x80\x93725.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0 The Court of Appeals reviewed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s grant of summary judgment. In doing so, the appellate panel examined the record before that court. It noted that Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s federal habeas counsel had hired two experts (Crown and Sultan), and had told the court (in an offer of proof) that they would provide evidence that Thompson suffered from mental illness at the time of the crime. But the appellate panel found that neither expert had done so. Indeed, said the panel, Thompson had \xe2\x80\x9cnever submitted to any court any proof that he suffered from severe mental illness at the time of the crime.\xe2\x80\x9d 315 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 590 (emphasis altered). Though Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s several attorneys had made the same allegation for many years in several different courts (said the panel), \xe2\x80\x9cat each opportunity, counsel fail[ed] to secure an answer to the critical issue of whether Thompson was mentally ill at the time of the crime.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. That fact, concluded the panel (over a dissent), was fatal to Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s basic ineffective-assistance-of-counsel claim. Obviously \xe2\x80\x9ctrial counsel cannot be deemed ineffective for failing to discover something that does not appear to exist.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; see also id., at 595 (Moore, J., concurring in result) (\xe2\x80\x9cThompson has presented no evidence that his [trial] counsel knew or should have known either that Thompson was mentally ill or that his mental condition was deteriorating at the time of his trial or at the time of his crime\xe2\x80\x9d). The dissenting judge thought Thompson had made out an ineffective-assistance claim by showing that his trial counsel had relied on an inadequate expert, that is, an expert without the necessary qualifications to counter the State\xe2\x80\x99s experts\xe2\x80\x99 conclusions. Id., at 599\xe2\x80\x93605 (opinion of Clay, J.).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The appeals court issued its opinion on January 9, 2003. Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s appointed federal appeals counsel filed a rehearing petition, which the court denied on March 10, 2003. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 346 (Order in No. 00\xe2\x80\x935516 (CA6)). Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s counsel then sought Supreme Court review. This Court denied review (and rehearing) about one year later. 540 U. S. 1051 (2003) (denying certiorari); 540 U. S. 1158 (2004) (denying rehearing).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals, following ordinary appellate-court practice, withheld issuance of its mandate while the case was under review here, namely during calendar year 2003. During that time and in the months that followed, something unusual happened. Judge Suhrheinrich realized that the panel, in reaching its decision, seemed to have overlooked documents provided by Dr. Sultan that likely were relevant. In September 2003, the appellate court called for the entire certified record. Upon reviewing that record, Judge Suhrheinrich found Dr. Sultan\xe2\x80\x99s deposition and accompanying report. 373 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 692\xe2\x80\x93693; App. to Pet. for Cert. 347\xe2\x80\x93348; see also Appendix, infra.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Sultan documents filled the evidentiary gap that underlay the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s and the appellate panel\xe2\x80\x99s determinations. These documents made clear that Dr. Sultan had investigated Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s background in depth and that in her (well-supported) opinion, Thompson had suffered from serious episodic bouts of schizophrenia at the time the crime was committed. Clearly the documents contained evidence supporting Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s claim regarding his mental state at the time of the offense. Why had the District Court denied the existence of any such evidence? Why had Judge Suhrheinrich, and the other members of the panel (and the State, which took Dr. Sultan\xe2\x80\x99s deposition) done the same?

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Judge Suhrheinrich then drafted an opinion that sought to answer three questions:

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Question One: Do these documents actually provide strong evidence that Thompson was schizophrenic (and seriously so) at the time of the crime?

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Question Two: If so, given the many previous opportunities that Thompson has had to raise the issue of his mental health, to what extent would these documents be likely to matter in respect to the legal question raised in Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s federal proceedings, i.e., would they likely lead a federal habeas court to hold that Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s trial counsel was ineffective for failing to undertake a background investigation akin to that performed by Dr. Sultan?

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Question Three: How did these documents previously escape our attention?\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The panel answered the first question\xe2\x80\x94regarding the importance of the documents\xe2\x80\x94unanimously. Sultan\xe2\x80\x99s report and deposition were critically important. As Judge Suhrheinrich\xe2\x80\x99s opinion explains, these documents detail Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s horrendous childhood, his family history of mental illness, his self-destructive schizophrenic behavior (including auditory hallucinations) as a child, his mood swings and bizarre behavior as a young adult, and a worsening of that behavior after a serious beating to his head that he suffered while in the Navy. For example, Dr. Sultan\xe2\x80\x99s examination of Thompson and her interviews with Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s family members and others revealed that as a child Thompson would repeatedly bang his head against the wall to \xe2\x80\x9cknock the Devil out\xe2\x80\x9d after his grandmother yelled at him, \xe2\x80\x9cYou have the Devil in you.\xe2\x80\x9d 373 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 716 (internal quotation marks omitted). These documents explain how Thompson, as a young adult, would talk to himself and scream and cry for no apparent reason. They suggest that he had bouts of paranoia.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The documents provide strong support for the conclusion that Thompson suffered from episodes of schizophrenia at the time of the offense. And they thereby offer significant support for the conclusion that, had earlier testifying experts had this information, they could have countered the State\xe2\x80\x99s experts\xe2\x80\x99 conclusion that Thompson was malingering at the time of trial. Thus, the Sultan materials seriously undermined the foundation of the State\xe2\x80\x99s position in respect to Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s mental condition.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Sultan materials also revealed that trial counsel failed to discover other mitigating evidence of importance. Interviews with family members revealed repeated incidents of violence in the family, including an episode in which, as a young boy, Thompson witnessed his father brutally beat and rape his mother. His grandmother, with whom Thompson and his siblings lived after their mother died, subjected them to abuse and neglect. She would forget to feed the children, leaving them to steal money from under her bed to buy food. These and other circumstances are detailed in sections of the Sultan report and deposition reproduced in the Appendix, infra.

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The panel also responded unanimously and affirmatively to the second question: Would federal-court access to the Sultan documents likely have made a significant difference in respect to the federal legal question at issue in Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s habeas petition, namely, the failure of Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s trial counsel to investigate his background? Trial counsel had had important indications that something was wrong. Indeed, counsel himself had sought an evaluation of Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s mental condition. He also was aware of Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s violent behavior in the military, and knew that Thompson had said he had had auditory hallucinations all his life. He was aware, too, of the changes in Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s behavior. Should counsel not then have investigated further?

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Sultan documents make clear that, had he done so, he would have had a strong answer to the State\xe2\x80\x99s experts. Thus the documents were relevant to the outcome of the federal habeas proceedings. The Federal District Court based its grant of summary judgment on the premise that there was no evidence supporting Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s claim. The documents showed that precisely such evidence was then available.

\n

3

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The panel (while disagreeing about how to allocate blame) agreed in part about the answer to the third question: how these documents previously had escaped the panel\xe2\x80\x99s attention. The judges agreed that the Sultan documents were not in the initial record on appeal. The panel\xe2\x80\x99s original opinion, while mentioning both Dr. Sultan and Dr. Crown, assumed that neither expert had addressed Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s mental condition at the time of the crime. 315 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 583, n.\xc2\xa013 (\xe2\x80\x9cSultan\xe2\x80\x99s affidavit does not discuss Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s mental state at the time of the offense\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); ibid. (explaining that Thompson filed a Rule 60(b) motion to supplement the record with Dr. Sultan\xe2\x80\x99s report, but not mentioning that the report addressed Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s mental condition at the time of the offense); see also supra, at 5.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0How had the panel overlooked the copies of the Sultan deposition attached to (1) the rehearing petition and (2) the (Rule 60(b)-related) motion to hold the appeal in abeyance? As for the rehearing petition, the reason could well lie in the petition\xe2\x80\x99s (incorrect) suggestion that the panel had already considered the appended document as part of the original record. See Pet. for Rehearing and Suggestion for Rehearing En Banc in No. 2:00\xe2\x80\x935516 (CA6), p.\xc2\xa01 (\xe2\x80\x9cA majority of this panel overlooked other proof in the record, including but not limited to, the expert opinion of Dr. Faye E. Sultan\xe2\x80\x9d); see also id., at 28\xe2\x80\x9332. While the petition explains the importance of the documents, it does not explain the circumstances, namely, that the panel previously had not seen these documents. Instead, it gives the impression that counsel was simply reemphasizing a matter the panel had already considered. To that extent, the petition reduced the likelihood that the panel would make the connection it later made and fatally weakened its argument for re-hearing.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As for the motion to hold the appeal in abeyance, the panel\xe2\x80\x99s failure to recognize the significance of the appended Sultan materials is also understandable. The motion gives the impression that the appellate court would have been able to handle any problem arising from the exclusion of these materials in an appeal taken from the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 60(b) decision. The appellate court, however, never had any such opportunity because counsel did not appeal the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s denial of the Rule 60(b) motion.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Once the panel understood the significance of the Sultan report, it had to decide what to do. An appellate court exists to correct legal errors made in the trial court. What legal error had the District Court committed? The appeal concerned its grant of summary judgment in the State\xe2\x80\x99s favor. The District Court made that decision on the basis of the record before it, and that record apparently lacked the relevant documents. How then could an appeals court say that the District Court was wrong to grant the summary judgment motion?

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The panel answered this question by not holding that the District Court had erred. Finding that the Sultan documents had been \xe2\x80\x9capparently negligently omitted\xe2\x80\x9d from the record, it exercised its equitable powers to supplement the record with the deposition. 373 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 691. It also found that, since the State itself had helped to create that document (because the State had taken Sultan\xe2\x80\x99s deposition), the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s reconsideration of the matter would not unfairly prejudice the State. And it noted that this case is a death case. Then, relying on its \xe2\x80\x9cinherent power to reconsider\xe2\x80\x9d an opinion \xe2\x80\x9cprior to the issuance of the mandate,\xe2\x80\x9d the court issued a new opinion, vacating the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s grant of summary judgment to the State and remanding the case to the District Court for further proceedings on the matter. Ibid.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0 The question before us is not whether we, as judges, would have come to the same conclusions as did the panel of the Court of Appeals. It is whether the three members of the appellate panel abused their discretion in reconsidering the matter and, after agreeing unanimously that they would have reached a different result had they considered the overlooked evidence, vacating the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment and remanding the case.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court concludes that the panel\xe2\x80\x99s reconsideration of the matter and decision to vacate the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment amounted to an \xe2\x80\x9cabuse of discretion.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 1. It therefore reverses the panel\xe2\x80\x99s unanimous interlocutory judgment remanding a capital case to the District Court for an evidentiary hearing. The Court lists five reasons why the Court of Appeals \xe2\x80\x9cabused its discretion.\xe2\x80\x9d None of these reasons, whether taken separately or considered together, stands up to examination.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Reason One. During the 5-month period after this Court denied rehearing of Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s certiorari petition, during which time the Court of Appeals was reconsidering the matter, it gave \xe2\x80\x9cno indication that it might be revisiting its earlier decision.\xe2\x80\x9d Had it \xe2\x80\x9cnotified\xe2\x80\x9d the parties, the court \xe2\x80\x9ccould have spared the parties and the state judicial system considerable time and resources.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If this consideration favors the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion, it does so to a very modest degree. For one thing, the Federal Rules themselves neither set an unchangeable deadline for issuance of a mandate nor require notice when the court enlarges the time for issuance. Compare Fed. Rule App. Proc. 41(b) (2005) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe court may shorten or extend the time\xe2\x80\x9d), with Rule 41(b) (1968) (mandate \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d issue \xe2\x80\x9cunless the time is shortened or extended by order\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). The Advisory Committee Notes to Rule 41 expressly contemplate that the parties will themselves check the docket to determine whether the mandate has issued. See Advisory Committee\xe2\x80\x99s 1998 Note on subd. (c) of Rule 41 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he parties can easily calculate the anticipated date of issuance and verify issuance of the mandate[;] the entry of the order on the docket alerts the parties to that fact\xe2\x80\x9d). And Sixth Circuit Rules require the Circuit Clerk to provide all parties with copies of the mandate. See Internal Operating Procedure 41(a) (CA6 2005) (\xe2\x80\x9cCopies of the mandate are distributed to all parties and the district court clerk\xe2\x80\x99s office\xe2\x80\x9d). Thus, the State\xe2\x80\x99s attorneys knew, or certainly should have known, that the mandate had not issued, and, as experienced practitioners, they also knew, or certainly should have known, that a proceeding is not technically over until the court has issued its mandate. And if concerned by the delay (and some delay in such matters is not uncommon), they could have asked the Circuit Clerk why the mandate had not issued. If necessary, they could have filed a motion seeking that information or seeking the mandate\xe2\x80\x99s immediate issuance.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For another thing, since notification is a clerical duty, the panel may have thought the parties had been notified. One of the judges on the panel could well have instructed the Circuit Clerk not to issue the mandate, and then simply have assumed that the Clerk would notify the parties of that fact (though the Clerk, perhaps inadvertently, did not do so). Why would the court want to hide what it was doing from the parties? Once we apply a presumption of regularity to the panel\xe2\x80\x99s actions, we must assume that the failure to notify the parties was likely due to a simple clerical error.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Further, the prejudice to the State that troubles the Court was likely small or nonexistent. The need to reset an execution date is not uncommon, and the state court\xe2\x80\x99s execution order explicitly foresaw that possibility. See 373 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 692 (Tennessee Supreme Court order set Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s execution date for August 19, 2004, \xe2\x80\x9cunless otherwise ordered by this Court or other appropriate authority\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). Moreover, the State has not even argued\xe2\x80\x94despite ample opportunity to do so\xe2\x80\x94that the further proceedings ordered by the panel would actually have required it to set a new date.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, the State did not, by way of a petition for rehearing, make any of its \xe2\x80\x9cfailure to notify\xe2\x80\x9d arguments to the Court of Appeals. Although the law does not require the State to seek rehearing, such a petition would have permitted the panel to explain why the State was not notified and possibly to explore the matter of prejudice. There is no reason to reward the State for not filing a petition by assuming prejudice where none appears to exist.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Given the State\xe2\x80\x99s likely knowledge that the mandate had not issued, the existence of avenues for resolving any uncertainty, and the small likelihood of prejudice, the lack of notice does not significantly advance the Court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cabuse of discretion\xe2\x80\x9d finding. Indeed, if the Court believes that the Court of Appeals could have issued a revised opinion correcting its earlier judgment if only it had given notice to the parties, the sanction it now imposes\xe2\x80\x94outright reversal\xe2\x80\x94is far out of proportion to the crime.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Reason Two. The court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9copportunity to consider\xe2\x80\x9d the Sultan evidence \xe2\x80\x9cat the rehearing stage is yet another factor supporting\xe2\x80\x9d the abuse-of-discretion \xe2\x80\x9cdetermination.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12. I agree that it is unfortunate that, upon review of the rehearing petition, the panel failed to make the connection that would have allowed it, at that time, to reach the same conclusion it reached later. Still, the petition wrongly implied that the Sultan documents were part of the original appeal. Because it did not request rehearing on the ground that the documents were not in the record, it did not offer a genuine \xe2\x80\x9copportunity to consider\xe2\x80\x9d the Sultan evidence.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under these circumstances, I cannot agree that the court\xe2\x80\x99s opportunity to consider these documents at the rehearing stage should militate in favor of finding an abuse of discretion. To the contrary, I believe we should encourage, rather than discourage, an appellate panel, when it learns that it has made a serious mistake, to take advantage of an opportunity to correct it, rather than to ignore the problem.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Reason Three. The \xe2\x80\x9cSultan evidence \xe2\x80\xa6 is not of such a character as to warrant [a] \xe2\x80\xa6 departure from standard appellate procedures\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9cthe evidence was unlikely to have altered the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s resolution of Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s ineffective-assistance-of-counsel claim.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14. That is to say, given the expert testimony in the trial court, the Sultan evidence is unlikely meaningfully to have strengthened Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s claim before the Federal District Court. Ante, at 14\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This conclusion is wrong. \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court argues the following: (1) Dr. Sultan\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion rests in significant part upon interviews with three witnesses, Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s grandmother and sister (with whom Dr. Sultan spoke directly) and his girlfriend (whose interview with a defense investigator Dr. Sultan reviewed); (2) since all three of these witnesses testified at sentencing, Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s counsel must have consulted them at the time; and (3) \xe2\x80\x9c[c]onsultation with these witnesses, when combined with the opinions of [the State\xe2\x80\x99s expert] and [Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s expert], provided an adequate basis for Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s attorneys to conclude that focusing on Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s mental health was not the best strategy.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16. The Court then says that trial counsel\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cstrategy\xe2\x80\x9d may have been \xe2\x80\x9ca mistake,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 17, but apparently not enough of a mistake to amount to inadequate assistance of counsel.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0 But how do the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusions follow from the premises? Dr. Sultan\xe2\x80\x99s interview of the three witnesses apparently turned up new information, indeed, crucial information. Why does that fact not tend show that trial counsel\xe2\x80\x99s own \xe2\x80\x9cconsultation\xe2\x80\x9d with those witnesses was inadequate? Or, if trial counsel was aware of the information, why does that not tend to show that trial counsel hired an expert who was not qualified to assess Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s mental condition, or that counsel failed adequately to convey the critical information to that expert? This Court in Wiggins v. Smith, 539 U. S. 510, 523\xe2\x80\x93525 (2003), found trial counsel inadequate for failing to conduct a reasonable investigation, given notice that such an investigation would likely turn up important mitigating evidence. See also Rompilla v. Beard, ante, p.\xc2\xa0___. Why is the same not true here, where Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s trial counsel was fully aware of the need for a background investigation, and then either did not ask the right questions, or did not hire the right expert, or did not convey the right information to that expert? At the least, is there not a good argument to this effect\xe2\x80\x94an argument that the Sultan documents significantly strengthened? All three judges on the panel thought so: They concluded that they would have reached a different result on Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s ineffective-assistance-of-counsel claim had they been aware of the Sultan documents. The Court does not satisfactorily explain its basis for second-guessing the panel on this point.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Reason Four. The Sultan evidence does \xe2\x80\x9cnot come close to satisfying the miscarriage of justice standard under Calderon.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 17 (referring to Calderon v. Thompson, 523 U. S. 538 (1998)). \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the Court apparently agrees, see ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939, Calderon does not apply here. And the panel\xe2\x80\x99s basic conclusion\xe2\x80\x94that consideration of Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s ineffective-assistance-of-counsel claim without the benefit of the Sultan evidence would constitute a grave miscarriage of justice\xe2\x80\x94survives any plausible standard of review. I can find nothing in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion that explains why the panel\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion is wrong.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Reason Five. The Court of Appeals \xe2\x80\x9cdid not accord the appropriate level of respect\xe2\x80\x9d to the State\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cjudgment.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 19. If by \xe2\x80\x9cjudgment\xe2\x80\x9d the Court means to refer to the state court\xe2\x80\x99s original judgment of conviction, this reason simply repeats Reason Four. The panel carefully examined the entire record and determined that there is a significant likelihood the Sultan evidence would demonstrate a violation of the Federal Constitution.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If the Court means to refer to the state court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment not to set aside the conviction in state postconviction proceedings, the Court is clearly wrong. The state court on collateral review refused to authorize funds for a background investigation, one for which Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s expert then showed a strong need, and which Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s expert now shows could well have demonstrated a significantly mitigating mental condition. How is it disrespectful of the State for a federal habeas court to identify a constitutional error that occurred in state-court proceedings in a capital case, by taking account of a key piece of evidence, mistakenly omitted from the record?

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If the Court means to refer to the State\xe2\x80\x99s decision to proceed with the execution, I cannot possibly agree. The Court could not mean that any exercise by a federal court to correct an inadvertent, and important, evidentiary error is \xe2\x80\x9cdisrespectful\xe2\x80\x9d of a State\xe2\x80\x99s effort to proceed to execution. But if it does not mean \xe2\x80\x9cany\xe2\x80\x9d exercise at all, then how can it say the present exercise is disrespectful? The present exercise embodies as thorough an examination of the record and as significant a piece of evidence as one is likely to find. The process\xe2\x80\x94the detail and care with which the Court of Appeals combed the record\xe2\x80\x94does not show \xe2\x80\x9cdisrespect.\xe2\x80\x9d It shows the contrary.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The upshot is that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s five reasons are unconvincing. The Court simply states those reasons as conclusions. It fails to show how, or why, the unanimous panel erred in reaching diametrically opposite conclusions, all supported with detailed evidence set forth in Judge Suhrheinrich\xe2\x80\x99s opinion. It does not satisfactorily explain the evidentiary basis for its own conclusions. And, in the process, it loses sight of the question before us: again, not whether we, as judges, would have reached the same conclusion that the three judges on the panel reached, but rather whether they, having unanimously agreed that their earlier decision was wrong, abused their discretion in setting it right.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Ultimately this case presents three kinds of question. The first is a narrow legal question. Has the Court of Appeals abused its discretion? For the reasons I have set forth, the answer to that question, legally speaking, must be \xe2\x80\x9cno.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The second is an epistemological question. How, in respect to matters involving the legal impact of the Sultan report and deposition, can the Court replace the panel\xe2\x80\x99s judgment with its own? Judge Suhrheinrich\xe2\x80\x99s opinion demonstrates why any assessment of that legal impact must grow out of thorough knowledge of the record. He spent hundreds of hours with its numerous documents in order to make that assessment. Those of his conclusions that were shared by the other members of the panel are logical, rest upon record-based facts, and are nowhere refuted (in respect to those facts) by anything before us or by anything in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion. How can the Court know that the panel is wrong?

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The third question is about basic jurisprudence. A legal system is based on rules; it also seeks justice in the individual case. Sometimes these ends conflict. To take account of such conflict, the system often grants judges a degree of discretion, thereby providing oil for the rule-based gears. When we tell the Court of Appeals that it cannot exercise its discretion to correct the serious error it discovered here, we tell courts they are not to act to cure serious injustice in similar cases. The consequence is to divorce the rule-based result from the just result. The American judicial system has long sought to avoid that divorce. Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision takes an unfortunate step in the

\n

wrong direction.
