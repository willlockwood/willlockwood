\nUNICODE VALUE="8195">KAWASAKI KISEN KAISHA LTD. V. REGAL-BELOIT CORP.
\n
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\n
\n

KAWASAKI KISEN KAISHA LTD. et\xc2\xa0al. v. REGAL-BELOIT CORP. et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 08\xe2\x80\x931553.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 24, 2010\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 21, 2010*

\n

Respondents (cargo owners) delivered to petitioners in No. 08\xe2\x80\x931553 (\xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line) goods for shipping from China to inland United States destinations. \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line issued them four through bills of lading, i.e., bills of lading covering both the ocean and inland portions of transport in a single document. As relevant here, the bills contain a \xe2\x80\x9cHimalaya Clause,\xe2\x80\x9d which extends the bills\xe2\x80\x99 defenses and liability limitations to subcontractors; permit \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line to subcontract to complete the journey; provide that the entire journey is governed by the Carriage of Goods by Sea Act (COGSA), which regulates bills of lading issued by ocean carriers engaged in foreign trade; and designate a Tokyo court as the venue for any dispute. \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line arranged the journey, subcontracting with petitioner in No. 08\xe2\x80\x931554 (Union Pacific) for rail shipment in the United States. The cargo was shipped in \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line vessels to California and then loaded onto a Union Pacific train. A derailment along the inland route allegedly destroyed the cargo. Ultimately, the Federal District Court granted the motion of Union Pacific and \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line to dismiss the cargo owners\xe2\x80\x99 suits against them based on the parties\xe2\x80\x99 Tokyo forum-selection clause. The Ninth Circuit reversed, concluding that that clause was trumped by the Carmack Amendment governing bills of lading issued by domestic rail carriers, which applied to the inland portion of the shipment.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Because the Carmack Amendment does not apply to a shipment originating overseas under a single through bill of lading, the parties\xe2\x80\x99 agreement to litigate these cases in Tokyo is binding. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9321.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0COGSA, which \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line and Union Pacific contend governs these cases, requires a carrier to issue to the cargo owner a bill containing specified terms. It does not limit the parties\xe2\x80\x99 ability to adopt forum-selection clauses. It only applies to shipments from United States ports to foreign ports and vice versa, but permits parties to extend certain of its terms \xe2\x80\x9cby contract\xe2\x80\x9d to cover \xe2\x80\x9cthe entire period in which [the goods] would be under [a carrier\xe2\x80\x99s] responsibility, including [a] period of inland \xe2\x80\xa6 transport.\xe2\x80\x9d Norfolk Southern R.\xc2\xa0Co. v. James N. Kirby, Pty Ltd., 543 U. S. 14, 29. The Carmack Amendment, on which respondents rely, requires a domestic rail carrier that \xe2\x80\x9creceives [property] for transportation under this part\xe2\x80\x9d to issue a bill of lading. 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa711706(a). \xe2\x80\x9c[T]his part\xe2\x80\x9d refers to the Surface Transportation Board\xe2\x80\x99s (STB\xe2\x80\x99s) jurisdiction over domestic rail transportation. See \xc2\xa710501(b). Carmack assigns liability for damage on the rail route to \xe2\x80\x9creceiving rail carrier[s]\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cdelivering rail carrier[s],\xe2\x80\x9d regardless of which carrier caused the damage. \xc2\xa711706(a). Its purpose is to relieve cargo owners \xe2\x80\x9cof the burden of searching out a particular negligent carrier from among the often numerous carriers handling an interstate shipment of goods.\xe2\x80\x9d Reider v. Thompson, 339 U. S. 113, 119. Thus, it constrains carriers\xe2\x80\x99 ability to limit liability by contract, \xc2\xa711706(c), and limits the parties\xe2\x80\x99 choice of venue to federal and state courts. \xc2\xa711706(d)(1). Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0In Kirby, as in these cases, an ocean shipping company issued a through bill of lading that extended COGSA\xe2\x80\x99s terms to the inland segment, and the property was damaged during the inland rail portion. This Court held that the through bill\xe2\x80\x99s terms governed under federal maritime law, notwithstanding contrary state laws, 543 U. S., at 23\xe2\x80\x9327, explaining that \xe2\x80\x9cso long as a bill of lading requires substantial carriage of goods by sea, its purpose is to effectuate maritime commerce,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 27, and adding that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]pplying state law \xe2\x80\xa6 would undermine the uniformity of general maritime law,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 28, and defeat COGSA\xe2\x80\x99s apparent purpose \xe2\x80\x9cto facilitate efficient contracting in contracts for carriage by sea,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. Here, as in Kirby, \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line issued through bills under COGSA, in maritime commerce, and extended its terms to the journey\xe2\x80\x99s inland domestic segment. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0The Carmack Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s text, history, and purposes make clear that it does not require a different result. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9321.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(1)\xc2\xa0Carmack divides the realm of rail carriers into receiving, delivering, and connecting rail carriers. Its first sentence requires a compliant bill of lading (1) if a rail carrier provid[es] transportation or service subject to the [STB\xe2\x80\x99s] jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d and (2) if that carrier \xe2\x80\x9creceives\xe2\x80\x9d the property \xe2\x80\x9cfor transportation \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d 11706(a). It thus requires the receiving rail carrier\xe2\x80\x94but not the delivering or connecting rail carrier\xe2\x80\x94to issue a bill of lading. This conclusion is consistent with statute\xe2\x80\x99s text and this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent. See St. Louis, I. M. & S. R. Co. v. Starbird, 243 U. S. 592, 595, 604. A receiving rail carrier is the initial carrier, which \xe2\x80\x9creceives\xe2\x80\x9d the property for domestic rail transportation at the journey\xe2\x80\x99s point of origin. If the Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s bill of lading requirement referred not to the initial carrier, but to any carrier \xe2\x80\x9creceiving\xe2\x80\x9d the property from another carrier, then every carrier during the shipment would have to issue its own separate bill. This would be contrary to Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s purpose of making the receiving and delivering carriers liable under a single, initial bill for damage caused by any carrier within a single course of shipment. This conclusion is consistent with Mexican Light & Power Co. v. Texas Mexican R. Co., 331 U. S. 731, where the Court held that a bill of lading issued by a subsequent rail carrier when the \xe2\x80\x9cinitial carrier\xe2\x80\x9d has issued a through bill is \xe2\x80\x9cvoid\xe2\x80\x9d unless it \xe2\x80\x9crepresents the initiation of a new shipment,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 733\xe2\x80\x93734. And Reider, supra, is not to the contrary. There, absent a through bill of lading, the original journey from Argentina terminated at the port of New Orleans, and the first rail carrier in the United States was the receiving rail carrier for Carmack purposes. Id., at 117. Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s second sentence establishes that it applies only to transport of property for which a receiving carrier is required to issue a bill of lading, regardless of whether that carrier actually issues such a bill. See \xc2\xa711706(a). Thus, Carmack applies only if the journey begins with a receiving rail carrier that had to issue a compliant bill of lading, not if the property is received at an overseas location under a through bill that covers transport into an inland location in this country. The initial carrier in that instance receives the property at the shipment\xe2\x80\x99s point of origin for overseas multimodal import transport, not domestic rail transport. Carmack did not require \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line to issue bills of lading because \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line was not a receiving rail carrier. That it chose to use rail transport to complete one segment of the journey under its \xe2\x80\x9cessentially maritime\xe2\x80\x9d contracts, Kirby, supra, at 24, does not put it within Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s reach. Union Pacific, which the cargo owners concede was a mere delivering carrier that did not have to issue its own Carmack bill of lading, was also not a receiving rail carrier under Carmack. Because the Ninth Circuit ignored Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9creceive[d] \xe2\x80\xa6 for transportation\xe2\x80\x9d limitation, it reached the wrong conclusion. Its conclusion is also an awkward fit with Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s venue provisions, which presume that the receiving carrier obtains the property in a judicial district within the United States. If \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line were a receiving carrier in a case with a \xe2\x80\x9cpoint of origin\xe2\x80\x9d in China, there would be no place under Carmack to sue \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line, since China is not within a judicial district \xe2\x80\x9cof the United States or in a State Court.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa711706(d)(1). Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(2)\xc2\xa0Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s statutory history supports this conclusion. None of its legislative versions\xe2\x80\x94the original 1906 statute or the amended 1915, 1978, or 1995 ones\xe2\x80\x94have applied to the inland domestic rail segment of an import shipment from overseas under a through bill. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(3)\xc2\xa0This interpretation also attains the most consistency between Carmack and COGSA. Applying Carmack to the inland segment of an international carriage originating overseas under a through bill would undermine Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s purposes, which are premised on the view that a shipment has a single bill of lading and any damage is the responsibility of both receiving and delivering carriers. Under the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation, there might be no venue in which to sue the receiving carrier. That interpretation would also undermine COGSA and international, container-based multimodal transport: COGSA\xe2\x80\x99s liability and venue rules would apply when cargo is damaged at sea and Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s rules almost always would apply when the damage occurs on land. Moreover, applying Carmack to international import shipping transport would undermine COGSA\xe2\x80\x99s purpose \xe2\x80\x9cto facilitate efficient contracting in contracts for carriage by sea.\xe2\x80\x9d Kirby, supra, at 29. The cargo owners\xe2\x80\x99 contrary policy arguments are unavailing. Pp.\xc2\xa017\xe2\x80\x9320.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Kennedy delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These cases concern through bills of lading covering cargo for the entire course of shipment, beginning in a foreign, overseas country and continuing to a final, inland destination in the United States. The voyage here included ocean transit followed by transfer to a rail carrier in this country. The Court addressed similar factual circumstances in Norfolk Southern R.\xc2\xa0Co. v. James N. Kirby, Pty Ltd., 543 U. S. 14 (2004). In that case the terms of a through bill were controlled by federal maritime law and by a federal statute known as the Carriage of Goods by Sea Act (COGSA), note following 46 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa730701. Kirby held that bill of lading provisions permissible under COGSA can be invoked by a domestic rail carrier, despite contrary state law.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The instant cases present a question neither raised nor addressed in Kirby. It is whether the terms of a through bill of lading issued abroad by an ocean carrier can apply to the domestic part of the import\xe2\x80\x99s journey by a rail carrier, despite prohibitions or limitations in another federal statute. That statute is known as the Carmack Amendment and it governs the terms of bills of lading issued by domestic rail carriers. 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa711706(a).

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents Regal-Beloit Corporation, Victory Fireworks, Inc., PICC Property & Casualty Company Ltd., and Royal & Sun Alliance Insurance Company Ltd. are cargo owners or insurance firms that paid losses to cargo owners and succeeded to their rights, all referred to as \xe2\x80\x9ccargo owners.\xe2\x80\x9d To ship their goods from China to inland destinations in the Midwestern United States, the cargo owners delivered the goods in China to petitioners in No. 08\xe2\x80\x931553, Kawasaki Kisen Kaisha, Ltd., and its agent \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line America, Inc., both referred to as \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line. All agree the relevant contract terms governing the shipment are contained in four through bills of lading \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line issued to the cargo owners. The bills of lading covered the entire course of shipment.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The bills required \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line to arrange delivery of the goods from China to their final destinations in the United States, by any mode of transportation of \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line\xe2\x80\x99s choosing. A bill of lading \xe2\x80\x9crecords that a carrier has received goods from the party that wishes to ship them, states the terms of carriage, and serves as evidence of the contract for carriage.\xe2\x80\x9d Kirby, 543 U. S., at 18\xe2\x80\x9319. A through bill of lading covers both the ocean and inland portions of the transport in a single document. Id., at 25\xe2\x80\x9326.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line\xe2\x80\x99s through bills contain five relevant provisions. First, they include a so-called \xe2\x80\x9cHimalaya Clause,\xe2\x80\x9d which extends the bills\xe2\x80\x99 defenses and limitations on liability to parties that sign subcontracts to perform services contemplated by the bills. See id., at 20, and n.\xc2\xa02. Second, the bills permit \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line \xe2\x80\x9cto sub-contract on any terms whatsoever\xe2\x80\x9d for the completion of the journey. App. 145. Third, the bills provide that COGSA\xe2\x80\x99s terms govern the entire journey. Fourth, the bills require that any dispute will be governed by Japanese law. Fifth, the bills state that any action relating to the carriage must be brought in \xe2\x80\x9cTokyo District Court in Japan.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 144. The forum-selection provision in the last clause gives rise to the dispute here.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line, pursuant to the bills of lading, arranged for the entire journey. It subcontracted with petitioner in No. 08\xe2\x80\x931554, Union Pacific Railroad Company, for rail shipment in the United States. The goods were to be shipped in a \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line vessel to a port in Long Beach, California, and then transferred to Union Pacific for rail carriage to the final destinations.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In March and April 2005, the cargo owners brought four different container shipments to \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line vessels in Chinese ports. All parties seem to assume that \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line safely transported the cargo across the Pacific Ocean to California. The containers were then loaded onto a Union Pacific train and that train, or some other train operated by Union Pacific, derailed in Tyrone, Oklahoma, allegedly destroying the cargo.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The cargo owners filed four separate lawsuits in the Superior Court of California, County of Los Angeles. The suit named \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line and Union Pacific as defendants. Union Pacific removed the suits to the United States District Court for the Central District of California. Union Pacific and \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line then moved to dismiss based on the parties\xe2\x80\x99 Tokyo forum-selection clause. The District Court granted the motion to dismiss. It decided that the forum-selection clause was reasonable and applied to Union Pacific pursuant to the Himalaya Clause in \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line\xe2\x80\x99s bills of lading. 462 F. Supp. 2d 1098, 1102\xe2\x80\x931103 (2006).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit reversed and remanded. 557 F.\xc2\xa03d 985 (2009). The court concluded that the Carmack Amendment applied to the inland portion of an international shipment under a through bill of lading and thus trumped the parties\xe2\x80\x99 forum-selection clause. Id., at 994\xe2\x80\x93995. The court noted that this view was consistent with the position taken by the Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit, see id., at 994 (citing Sompo Japan Ins. Co. of Am. v. Union Pacific R. Co., 456 F. 3d 54 (2006)), but inconsistent with the views of the Courts of Appeals for the Fourth, Sixth, Seventh, and Eleventh Circuits, see 557 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 994 (citing Shao v. Link Cargo (Taiwan) Ltd., 986 F.\xc2\xa02d 700 (CA4 1993); American Road Serv. Co. v. Consolidated Rail Corp., 348 F.\xc2\xa03d 565 (CA6 2003); Capitol Converting Equip., Inc. v. LEP Transp., Inc., 965 F.\xc2\xa02d 391 (CA7 1992); Altadis USA, Inc. ex rel. Fireman\xe2\x80\x99s Fund Ins. Co. v. Sea Star Line, LLC, 458 F.\xc2\xa03d 1288 (CA11 2006)). This Court granted certiorari to address whether Carmack applies to the inland segment of an overseas import shipment under a through bill of lading. 558 U. S. ___ (2009).

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Before turning to Carmack, a brief description of COGSA is in order; for \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line\xe2\x80\x99s and Union Pacific\xe2\x80\x99s primary contention is that COGSA, not Carmack, controls. COGSA governs the terms of bills of lading issued by ocean carriers engaged in foreign trade. 49 Stat. 1207, as amended, note following 46 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa730701, p. 1178. It requires each carrier to issue to the cargo owner a bill that contains certain terms. \xc2\xa73(3)\xe2\x80\x93(8), at 1178\xe2\x80\x931179. Although COGSA imposes some limitations on the parties\xe2\x80\x99 authority to adjust liability, it does not limit the parties\xe2\x80\x99 ability to adopt forum-selection clauses. See Vimar Seguros y Reaseguros, S. A. v. M/V Sky Reefer, 515 U. S. 528, 537\xe2\x80\x93539 (1995). By its terms, COGSA only applies to shipments from United States ports to ports of foreign countries and vice versa. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71(e), 13, at 1178, 1180. The statute, however, allows parties \xe2\x80\x9cthe option of extending [certain COGSA terms] by contract\xe2\x80\x9d to cover \xe2\x80\x9cthe entire period in which [the goods] would be under [a carrier\xe2\x80\x99s] responsibility, including [a] period of \xe2\x80\xa6 inland transport.\xe2\x80\x9d Kirby, 543 U. S., at 29 (citing COGSA \xc2\xa77, at 1180). Ocean carriers, who often must issue COGSA bills of lading, are regulated by the Federal Maritime Commission (Maritime Commission), which is responsible for oversight over \xe2\x80\x9ccommon carriage of goods by water in . . . foreign commerce.\xe2\x80\x9d 46 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa740101(1).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The next statute to consider is the Carmack Amendment, \xc2\xa77, 34 Stat. 595, which governs the terms of bills of lading issued by domestic rail carriers. Carmack was first enacted in 1906 as an amendment to the Interstate Commerce Act, 24 Stat. 379. The Carmack Amendment has been altered and recodified over the last century. It now provides, in relevant part, as follows:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(a) A rail carrier providing transportation or service subject to the jurisdiction of the [Surface Transportation Board (STB)] under this part shall issue a receipt or bill of lading for property it receives for transportation under this part. That rail carrier and any other carrier that delivers the property and is providing transportation or service subject to the jurisdiction of the [STB] under this part are liable to the person entitled to recover under the receipt or bill of lading. The liability imposed under this subsection is for the actual loss or injury to the property caused by\xe2\x80\x94

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(1)\xe2\x80\x82the receiving rail carrier;

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(2)\xe2\x80\x82the delivering rail carrier; or

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(3)\xe2\x80\x82another rail carrier over whose line or route the property is transported in the United States or from a place in the United States to a place in an adjacent foreign country when transported under a through bill of lading.

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9cFailure to issue a receipt or bill of lading does not affect the liability of a rail carrier.\xe2\x80\x9d 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa711706; see also \xc2\xa714706(a) (motor carriers).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Carmack Amendment thus requires a rail carrier that \xe2\x80\x9creceives [property] for transportation under this part\xe2\x80\x9d to issue a bill of lading. \xc2\xa711706(a). The provision \xe2\x80\x9cthis part\xe2\x80\x9d refers to is the STB\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction over rail transportation within the United States. See \xc2\xa710501 (2006 ed. and Supp. II). The STB is the successor to the Interstate Commerce Commission (ICC). The STB has \xe2\x80\x9cexclusive\xe2\x80\x9d jurisdiction to regulate \xe2\x80\x9ctransportation by rail carriers\xe2\x80\x9d between places in the United States as well as between a place \xe2\x80\x9cin the United States and a place in a foreign country.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa710501(a)(2)(F), (b) (2006 ed.). Regulated rail carriers must provide transportation subject to STB rail carrier jurisdiction \xe2\x80\x9con reasonable request,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa711101(a), at reasonable rates, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa710702, 10707(b), 11101(a), (e).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In cases where it applies, Carmack imposes upon \xe2\x80\x9creceiving rail carrier[s]\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cdelivering rail carrier[s]\xe2\x80\x9d liability for damage caused during the rail route under the bill of lading, regardless of which carrier caused the damage. \xc2\xa711706(a). Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s purpose is to relieve cargo owners \xe2\x80\x9cof the burden of searching out a particular negligent carrier from among the often numerous carriers handling an interstate shipment of goods.\xe2\x80\x9d Reider v. Thompson, 339 U. S. 113, 119 (1950). To help achieve this goal, Carmack constrains carriers\xe2\x80\x99 ability to limit liability by contract. \xc2\xa711706(c).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0 Carmack also limits the parties\xe2\x80\x99 ability to choose the venue of their suit:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(d)(1) A civil action under this section may be brought in a district court of the United States or in a State court.

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9c(2)(A) A civil action under this section may only be brought\xe2\x80\x94

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(i) against the originating rail carrier, in the judicial district in which the point of origin is located;

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(ii) against the delivering rail carrier, in the judicial district in which the principal place of business of the person bringing the action is located if the delivering carrier operates a railroad or a route through such judicial district, or in the judicial district in which the point of destination is located; and

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(iii) against the carrier alleged to have caused the loss or damage, in the judicial district in which such loss or damage is alleged to have occurred.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa711706.

\n

\nFor purposes of these cases, it can be assumed that if Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s terms apply to the bills of lading here, the cargo owners would have a substantial argument that the Tokyo forum-selection clause in the bills is pre-empted by Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s venue provisions. The parties argue about whether they may contract out of Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s venue provisions and other requirements, see \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa710502, 10709; but in light of the disposition and ruling to follow, those matters need not be discussed or further explored.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Kirby, an ocean shipping company issued a through bill of lading, agreeing to deliver cargo from Australia to Alabama. Like the through bills in the present cases, the Kirby bill extended COGSA\xe2\x80\x99s terms to the inland segment under a Himalaya Clause. There, as here, the property was damaged by a domestic rail carrier during the inland rail portion. 543 U. S., at 19\xe2\x80\x9320.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Kirby held that the through bill\xe2\x80\x99s terms governed under federal maritime law, notwithstanding contrary state laws. Id., at 23\xe2\x80\x9327. Kirby explained that \xe2\x80\x9cso long as a bill of lading requires substantial carriage of goods by sea, its purpose is to effectuate maritime commerce.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 27. The Court added that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]pplying state law to cases like this one would undermine the uniformity of general maritime law.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 28. \xe2\x80\x9cConfusion and inefficiency will inevitably result if more than one body of law governs a given contract\xe2\x80\x99s meaning.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 29. The Court noted that its conclusion \xe2\x80\x9creinforce[d] the liability regime Congress established in COGSA,\xe2\x80\x9d and explained that COGSA allows parties to extend its terms to an inland portion of a journey under a through bill of lading. Ibid. Finally, the Court concluded that a contrary holding would defeat \xe2\x80\x9cthe apparent purpose of COGSA, to facilitate efficient contracting in contracts for carriage by sea.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Much of what the Court said in Kirby applies to the present cases. \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line issued the through bills under COGSA, in maritime commerce. Congress considered such international through bills and decided to permit parties to extend COGSA\xe2\x80\x99s terms to the inland domestic segment of the journey. The cargo owners and \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line did exactly that in these cases, agreeing in the through bills to require that any suit be brought in Tokyo.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The cargo owners argue that the Carmack Amendment, which has its own venue provisions and was not discussed in Kirby, requires a different result. In particular they argue that Carmack applies to the domestic inland segment of the carriage here, so the Tokyo forum-selection clause is inapplicable. For the reasons set forth below, this contention must be rejected. Instructed by the text, history, and purposes of Carmack, the Court now holds that the amendment does not apply to a shipment originating overseas under a single through bill of lading. As in Kirby, the terms of the bill govern the parties\xe2\x80\x99 rights.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The text of the statute charts the analytic course. Carmack divides the realm of rail carriers into three parts: (1)\xc2\xa0receiving rail carriers; (2) delivering rail carriers; and (3) connecting rail carriers. A \xe2\x80\x9creceiving rail carrier\xe2\x80\x9d is one that \xe2\x80\x9cprovid[es] transportation or service \xe2\x80\xa6 for property it receives for transportation under this part.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa711706(a); see \xc2\xa711706(a)(1). The provision \xe2\x80\x9cthis part\xe2\x80\x9d refers to is the STB\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction over rail transportation within the United States. See \xc2\xa710501. A \xe2\x80\x9cdelivering rail carrier\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cdelivers the property and is providing transportation or service subject to the jurisdiction of the [STB] under this part.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa711706(a); see \xc2\xa711706(a)(2). A connecting rail carrier is \xe2\x80\x9canother rail carrier over whose line or route the property is transported in the United States or from a place in the United States to a place in an adjacent foreign country when transported under a through bill of lading.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa711706(a)(3).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A rail carrier\xe2\x80\x99s obligation to issue a Carmack-compliant bill of lading is determined by Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s first sentence:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cA rail carrier providing transportation or service subject to the jurisdiction of the [STB] under this part shall issue a receipt or bill of lading for property it receives for transportation under this part.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa711706(a).

\n

\nThis critical first sentence requires a Carmack-compliant bill of lading if two conditions are satisfied. First, the rail carrier must \xe2\x80\x9cprovid[e] transportation or service subject to the jurisdiction of the [STB].\xe2\x80\x9d Second, that carrier must \xe2\x80\x9creceiv[e]\xe2\x80\x9d the property \xe2\x80\x9cfor transportation under this part,\xe2\x80\x9d where \xe2\x80\x9cthis part\xe2\x80\x9d is the STB\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction over domestic rail transport. Carmack thus requires the receiving rail carrier\xe2\x80\x94but not the delivering or connecting rail carrier\xe2\x80\x94to issue a bill of lading. As explained below, ascertaining the shipment\xe2\x80\x99s point of origin is critical to deciding whether the shipment includes a receiving rail carrier.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The conclusion that Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s bill of lading requirement only applies to the receiving rail carrier is dictated by the text and is consistent with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent. See St. Louis, I. M. & S. R. Co. v. Starbird, 243 U. S. 592, 604 (1917) (explaining that Carmack \xe2\x80\x9crequires the receiving carrier to issue a through bill of lading\xe2\x80\x9d). A receiving rail carrier is the initial carrier, which \xe2\x80\x9creceives\xe2\x80\x9d the property for domestic rail transportation at the journey\xe2\x80\x99s point of origin. \xc2\xa711706(a). If Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s bill of lading requirement did not refer to the initial carrier, but rather to any rail carrier that in the colloquial sense \xe2\x80\x9creceived\xe2\x80\x9d the property from another carrier, then every carrier during the shipment would have to issue its own separate bill. This would be altogether contrary to Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s purpose of making the receiving and delivering carriers liable under a single, initial bill of lading for damage caused by any carrier within a single course of shipment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Mexican Light & Power Co. v. Texas Mexican R. Co., 331 U. S. 731 (1947), supports the conclusion that only the receiving rail carrier must issue a Carmack bill of lading. There, a subsequent rail carrier in an export shipment from the United States to Mexico issued its own separate bill of lading at the U. S.-Mexico border. The second bill differed from the through bill issued by the \xe2\x80\x9cinitial carrier,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 733, (that is, the receiving carrier) at the inland point of origin. The Court held that Carmack, far from requiring nonreceiving carriers to issue their separate bills of lading, makes any subsequent bill \xe2\x80\x9cvoid\xe2\x80\x9d unless the \xe2\x80\x9cso-called second bill of lading represents the initiation of a new shipment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 734.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Reider v. Thompson, 339 U. S. 113, is not to the contrary. That case involved goods originating in Argentina, bound for an inland location in the United States. The Court in Reider determined that because there was no through bill of lading, the original journey from Argentina terminated at the port of New Orleans. Thus, the first rail carrier in the United States was the receiving rail carrier and had to issue a Carmack bill of lading. Id., at 117. And because that carrier had to issue a separate bill of lading, it was not liable for damage done during the ocean-based portion of the shipment. Id., at 118\xe2\x80\x93119. Notably, neither Mexican Light nor Reider addressed the situation in the present cases, where the shipment originates overseas under a through bill of lading. And, for this reason, neither case discussed COGSA.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Carmack Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s second sentence establishes when Carmack liability applies:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[The receiving rail carrier referred to in the first sentence] and any other carrier that delivers the property and is providing transportation or service subject to the jurisdiction of the [STB] under this part are liable to the person entitled to recover under the receipt or bill of lading.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa711706(a)

\n

\nThus, the receiving and delivering rail carriers are subject to liability only when damage is done to this \xe2\x80\x9cproperty,\xe2\x80\x9d that is to say, to property for which Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s first sentence requires the receiving rail carrier to issue a bill of lading. Ibid. Put another way, Carmack applies only to transport of property for which Carmack requires a receiving carrier to issue a bill of lading, regardless of whether that carrier erroneously fails to issue such a bill. See ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9cFailure to issue a receipt or bill of lading does not affect the liability of a rail carrier\xe2\x80\x9d). The language in some of the Courts of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 decisions, which were rejected by the Court of Appeals in the opinion now under review, could be read to imply that Carmack applies only if a rail carrier actually issued a separate domestic bill of lading. See, e.g., Atladis, 458 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1291\xe2\x80\x931294; American Road, 348 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 568; Shao, 986 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 703; Capitol Converting, 965 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 394. This may have led to some confusion. The decisive question is not whether the rail carrier in fact issued a Carmack bill but rather whether that carrier was required to issue a bill by Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s first sentence.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The above principles establish that for Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s provisions to apply the journey must begin with a receiving rail carrier, which would have to issue a Carmack-compliant bill of lading. It follows that Carmack does not apply if the property is received at an overseas location under a through bill that covers the transport into an inland location in the United States. In such a case, there is no receiving rail carrier that \xe2\x80\x9creceives\xe2\x80\x9d the property \xe2\x80\x9cfor [domestic rail] transportation,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa711706(a), and thus no carrier that must issue a Carmack-compliant bill of lading. The initial carrier in that instance receives the property at the shipment\xe2\x80\x99s point of origin for overseas multimodal import transport, not for domestic rail transport. (Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision need not address the instance where goods are received at a point in the United States for export. Nor is it necessary to decide if Carmack applies to goods initially received in Canada or Mexico, for import into the United States. See infra, at 16.)

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The present cases illustrate the operation of these principles. Carmack did not require \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line to issue bills of lading because \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line was not a receiving rail carrier. \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line obtained the cargo in China for overseas transport across an ocean and then to inland destinations in the United States. \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line shipped this property under COGSA-authorized through bills of lading. See supra, at 4\xe2\x80\x935. That \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line chose to use rail transport to complete one segment of the journey under these \xe2\x80\x9cessentially maritime\xe2\x80\x9d contracts, Kirby, 543 U. S., at 24, does not put \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line within Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s reach and thus does not require it to issue Carmack bills of lading.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As for Union Pacific, it was also not a receiving rail carrier under Carmack. The cargo owners conceded at oral argument that, even under their theory, Union Pacific was a mere delivering carrier, which did not have to issue its own Carmack bill of lading. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 29, 39. This was a necessary concession. A carrier does not become a receiving carrier simply by accepting goods for further transport from another carrier in the middle of an international shipment under a through bill. After all, Union Pacific was not the \xe2\x80\x9cinitial carrier\xe2\x80\x9d for the carriage. Mexican Light, 331 U. S., at 733.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If a carrier like Union Pacific, which acts as a connecting or delivering carrier during an international through shipment, was, counterintuitively, a receiving carrier under Carmack, this would in effect outlaw through shipments under a single bill of lading. This is because a carriage like the one in the present case would require two bills of lading: one that the overseas carrier (here, \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line) issues to the cargo owners under COGSA, and a second one that the first domestic rail carrier (here, Union Pacific) issues to the overseas carrier under Carmack. Kirby noted \xe2\x80\x9cthe popularity of \xe2\x80\x98through\xe2\x80\x99 bills of lading, in which cargo owners can contract for transportation across oceans and to inland destinations in a single transaction.\xe2\x80\x9d 543 U. S., at 25\xe2\x80\x9326. The Court sees no reason to read COGSA and Carmack to outlaw this efficient mode of international shipping by requiring these journeys to have multiple bills of lading. In addition, if Union Pacific had to issue a Carmack bill of lading to \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line, it is unclear whether the cargo owners (the parties Carmack is designed to protect) would be able to sue under the terms governing that bill, especially in light of their different through bill with \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line. These difficulties are reason enough to reject this novel interpretation of Carmack, which was neither urged by any party nor adopted by any authority that has been called to this Court\xe2\x80\x99s attention.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This would be a quite different case if, as in Reider, the bills of lading for the overseas transport ended at this country\xe2\x80\x99s ports and the cargo owners then contracted with Union Pacific to complete a new journey to an inland destination in the United States. Under those circumstances, Union Pacific would have been the receiving rail carrier and would have been required to issue a separate Carmack-compliant bill of lading to the cargo owners. See Reider, 339 U. S., at 117 (\xe2\x80\x9cIf the various parties dealing with this shipment separated the carriage into distinct portions by their contracts, it is not for courts judicially to meld the portions into something they are not\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals interpreted Carmack as applying to any domestic rail segment of an overseas shipment, regardless of whether Carmack required a bill of lading. The court rested on the assumption that \xe2\x80\x9c[STB]\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction \xe2\x80\xa6 is coextensive with Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s coverage.\xe2\x80\x9d 557 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 992. Yet, as explained above, Carmack applies only to shipments for which Carmack requires a bill of lading; that is to say, to shipments that start with a carrier that is both subject to the STB\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction and \xe2\x80\x9creceives [the property] for [domestic rail] transportation.\xe2\x80\x9d The Court of Appeals ignored this \xe2\x80\x9creceive[d] \xe2\x80\xa6 for transportation\xe2\x80\x9d limitation and so reached the wrong conclusion. See, e.g., Reiter v. Sonotone Corp., 442 U. S. 330, 339 (1979) (courts are \xe2\x80\x9cobliged to give effect, if possible, to every word Congress used\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 conclusion is also an awkward fit with Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s venue provisions. Under Carmack, a suit against the \xe2\x80\x9coriginating\xe2\x80\x9d (that is, receiving) rail carrier that has not actually caused the damage to the goods \xe2\x80\x9cmay only be brought \xe2\x80\xa6 in the judicial district in which the point of origin is located.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa711706(d)(2)(A), (A)(i). Suit against either a delivering carrier or any carrier that caused the damage, by contrast, may be brought in various other districts. See \xc2\xa711706(d)(2)(B), (C). \xe2\x80\x9c[J]udicial district\xe2\x80\x9d refers to \xe2\x80\x9cdistrict court of the United States or in a State Court.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa711706(d)(1). Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s venue provisions presume that the receiving carrier obtains the property in a judicial district within the United States. Here, the journey\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cpoint of origin\xe2\x80\x9d was China, so Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s venue provisions reinforce the interpretation that Carmack does not apply to this carriage.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Indeed, if \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line were a receiving carrier in a case where the journey\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cpoint of origin\xe2\x80\x9d was China, there would be no place under Carmack to sue \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line, since China is not within a judicial district \xe2\x80\x9cof the United States or in a State court.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s original premise is that the receiving carrier is liable for damage caused by the other carriers in the delivery chain. This premise would be defeated if there were no venue in which to sue the receiving rail carrier, as opposed to suing a different carrier under one of Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s other venue provisions and then naming the receiving carrier as a codefendant. The far more likely conclusion is that \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line is not a receiving rail carrier at all under Carmack, and thus Carmack, including its venue provisions, does not apply to property shipped under \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line\xe2\x80\x99s through bills. True, if the sole question were one of venue, suit could still be brought against the carrier that caused the damage or the delivering carrier. But the issue need not be explored here, for, as the Court holds, Carmack is inapplicable in these cases.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s statutory history supports the conclusion that it does not apply to a shipment originating overseas under a through bill. None of Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s legislative versions have applied to the inland domestic rail segment of an import shipment from overseas under a through bill.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress enacted Carmack in 1906, as an amendment to the Interstate Commerce Act. At that time, the amendment\xe2\x80\x99s provisions applied only to \xe2\x80\x9cproperty for transportation from a point in one State to a point in another State.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa77, 34 Stat. 595. Congress amended Carmack in 1915, \xc2\xa71, 38 Stat. 1197, and the relevant language remained unchanged until Carmack was recodified in 1978. Under the pre-1978 language, Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s bill of lading provisions applied not only to wholly domestic rail transport but also to cargo \xe2\x80\x9creceive[d] . .\xc2\xa0. for transportation\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cfrom any point in the United States to a point in an adjacent foreign country.\xe2\x80\x9d 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa720(11) (1976 ed.).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even if there could be some argument that the Carmack Amendment before 1978 applied to imports from Canada and Mexico because the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cfrom . . . to\xe2\x80\x9d could also mean \xe2\x80\x9cbetween,\xe2\x80\x9d cf. Reider, supra, at 118 (explicitly not deciding this issue), the Court is unaware of any authority holding that the Carmack Amendment before 1978 applied to cargo originating from nonadjacent overseas countries under a through bill. See, e.g., In re The Cummins Amendment, 33 I.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0C. 682, 693 (1915); Brief for Respondents 8 (effectively conceding this point).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1978, Congress adopted the Carmack Amendment in largely its current form. \xc2\xa71, 92 Stat. 1337. Congress in the statute itself stated that it was recodifying Carmack and instructed that this recodification \xe2\x80\x9cmay not be construed as making a substantive change in the la[w].\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73(a), id., at 1466; see Burlington Northern R. Co. v. Oklahoma Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 481 U. S. 454, 457, n.\xc2\xa01 (1987). By interpreting the current version of the Carmack Amendment to cover cargo originating overseas, the Court of Appeals disregarded this direction and dramatically expanded Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s scope beyond its historical coverage.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, in 1995, Congress reenacted Carmack. But that reenactment evidenced no intent to affect the substantive change that Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 decision would entail. See \xc2\xa7102(a), 109 Stat. 847\xe2\x80\x93849. There is no claim that the 1995 statute altered Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s text in any manner relevant here, as that reenactment merely indented subsections of Carmack for readability. Cf. United States v. O\xe2\x80\x99Brien, 560 U. S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 14) (\xe2\x80\x9c[C]urrent legislative drafting guidelines \xe2\x80\xa6 advise drafters to break lengthy statutory provisions into separate subsections that can be read more easily\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Where the text permits, congressional enactments should be construed to be consistent with one another. And the interpretation of Carmack the Court now adopts attains the most consistency between Carmack and COGSA. First, applying Carmack to the inland segment of an international carriage originating overseas under a through bill would undermine Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s purposes. Carmack is premised on the view that the shipment has a single bill of lading and any damage during the journey is the responsibility of both the receiving and the delivering carrier. See supra, at 6. Yet, under the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 interpretation of Carmack, there would often be no venue in which to sue the receiving carrier. See supra, at 14\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Applying two different bill of lading regimes to the same through shipment would undermine COGSA and international, container-based multimodal transport. As Kirby explained, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he international transportation industry \xe2\x80\x98clearly has moved into a new era\xe2\x80\x94the age of multimodalism, door-to-door transport based on efficient use of all available modes of transportation by air, water, and land.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 543 U. S., at 25 (quoting 1 T.\xc2\xa0Schoenbaum, Admiralty and Maritime Law 589 (4th ed. 2004)). If Carmack applied to an inland segment of a shipment from overseas under a through bill, then one set of liability and venue rules would apply when cargo is damaged at sea (COGSA) and another almost always would apply when the damage occurs on land (Carmack). Rather than making claims by cargo owners easier to resolve, a court would have to decide where the damage occurred to determine which law applied. As a practical matter, this requirement often could not be met; for damage to the content of containers can occur when the contents are damaged by rough handling, seepage, or theft, at some unknown point. See H. Kindred & M. Brooks, Multimodal Transport Rules 143 (1997). Indeed, adopting the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 approach would seem to require rail carriers to open containers at the port to check if damage has been done during the sea voyage. This disruption would undermine international container-based transport. The Court will not read Congress\xe2\x80\x99 nonsubstantive recodification of Carmack in 1978 to create such a drastic sea change in practice in this area.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Applying Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s provisions to international import shipping transport would also undermine the \xe2\x80\x9cpurpose of COGSA, to facilitate efficient contracting in contracts for carriage by sea.\xe2\x80\x9d Kirby, supra, at 29. These cases provide an apt illustration. The sophisticated cargo owners here agreed to maritime bills of lading that applied to the inland segment through the Himalaya Clause and authorized \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line to subcontract for that inland segment \xe2\x80\x9con any terms whatsoever.\xe2\x80\x9d The cargo owners thus made the decision to select \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line as a single company for their through transportation needs, rather than contracting for rail services themselves. The through bills provided the liability and venue rules for the foreseeable event that the cargo was damaged during carriage. Indeed, the cargo owners obtained separate insurance to protect against any excess loss. The forum-selection clause the parties agreed upon is \xe2\x80\x9can indispensable element in international trade, commerce, and contracting\xe2\x80\x9d because it allows parties to \xe2\x80\x9cagre[e] in advance on a forum acceptable\xe2\x80\x9d to them. The Bremen v. Zapata Off-Shore Co., 407 U. S. 1, 13\xe2\x80\x9314 (1972). A clause of this kind is enforced unless it imposes a venue \xe2\x80\x9cso gravely difficult and inconvenient that [the plaintiff] will for all practical purposes be deprived of his day in court.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 18. The parties sensibly agreed that because their bills were governed by Japanese law, Tokyo would be the best venue for any suit relating to the cargo.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The cargo owners\xe2\x80\x99 contrary policy arguments are unavailing. They assert that if Carmack does not apply, the inland segment of international shipments will be \xe2\x80\x9cunregulated.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 2, 21, 24, 64, 91. First, any speculation that not applying Carmack to inland segments of overseas shipments will cause severe problems is refuted by that fact that Carmack even arguably did not govern the inland portion of such shipments from its enactment in 1906 until its nonsubstantive recodification in 1978. See supra, at 15\xe2\x80\x9317. It is true that if the cargo owners\xe2\x80\x99 position were to prevail, the terms of through bills of lading made in maritime commerce would be more restricted in some circumstances. But that does not mean that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding leaves the field unregulated. Ocean-based through bills are governed by COGSA, and ocean vessels like those operated by \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line are overseen by the Federal Maritime Commission. Supra, at 4\xe2\x80\x935. Rail carriers like Union Pacific, furthermore, remain subject to the STB\xe2\x80\x99s regulation to the extent they operate within the United States. See supra, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314. It is notable that although the STB has jurisdiction to regulate the rates of such carriers, even when the carriage is not governed by the Carmack Amendment, STB has exercised its authority to exempt from certain regulations service provided by a rail carrier \xe2\x80\x9cas part of a continuous intermodal freight movement,\xe2\x80\x9d 49 CFR \xc2\xa71090.2 (2009), like the journey at issue in these cases, see ibid. (exercising STB\xe2\x80\x99s deregulation authority under 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa710502(f)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, the cargo owners miss the mark in relying on the recent United Nations Convention on Contracts for the International Carriage of Goods Wholly or Partly by Sea, which has yet to be \xe2\x80\x9cratified by the President with the advice and consent of the Senate.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 11. These so-called \xe2\x80\x9cRotterdam Rules\xe2\x80\x9d would explicitly allow the inland leg of an international shipment to be governed by a different legal regime than the ocean leg, under some circumstances. See G.\xc2\xa0A. Res. 63/122, art. 26, U.\xc2\xa0N. Doc. A/RES/63/122 (Dec. 11, 2008). Nothing in the Rotterdam Rules, however, requires every country to mandate a different regime to govern the inland rail leg of an international through shipment; and, as explained above, Congress, by enacting COGSA, has opted for allowing shipments governed by a single through bill. And if the objection is that today\xe2\x80\x99s decision will undermine the results of these international negotiations in some way, that concern is met by the fact that the United States Government has urged the result the Court adopts today. See Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 13\xe2\x80\x9329.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress has decided to allow parties engaged in international maritime commerce to structure their contracts, to a large extent, as they see fit. It has not imposed Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s regime, textually and historically limited to the carriage of goods received for domestic rail transport, onto what are \xe2\x80\x9cessentially maritime\xe2\x80\x9d contracts. Kirby, 543 U. S., at 24.

\n

V

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line received the goods in China, under through bills for shipment into the United States. \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line was thus not a receiving rail carrier under Carmack and was not required to issue bills of lading under that Amendment. Union Pacific is also not a receiving carrier for this carriage and was thus not required to issue Carmack-compliant bills. Because the journey included no receiving rail carrier that had to issue bills of lading under Carmack, Carmack does not apply. The parties\xe2\x80\x99 agreement to litigate these cases in Tokyo is binding. The cargo owners must abide by the contracts they made.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit is reversed, and the cases are remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In my view, the Carmack Amendment to the Interstate Commerce Act (ICA), \xc2\xa77, 34 Stat. 595, plainly applies to the inland leg of a multimodal shipment traveling on an international through bill of lading. Unless they have permissibly contracted around Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s requirements, rail carriers in the United States such as petitioner Union Pacific are subject to those requirements, even though ocean carriers such as petitioner \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line are not. To avoid this simple conclusion, the Court contorts the statute and our cases, misreads the statutory history, and ascribes to Congress a series of policy choices that Congress manifestly did not make. Because I believe Carmack provides the default legal regime for rail transportation of cargo within the United States, regardless of whether the shipment originated abroad, I would reach the second question presented: whether Union Pacific was free to opt out of Carmack under 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa710709, or whether Union Pacific first had to offer \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line, its contractual counterparty, Carmack-compliant terms under \xc2\xa710502. As to that question, I would hold that opt-out under \xc2\xa710709 was not available and would remand to the District Court to consider in the first instance whether Union Pacific satisfied its obligations under \xc2\xa710502. For these reasons, I respectfully dissent.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s scope is wrong as a matter of text, history, and policy.

\n

A

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I begin with the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text. Two provisions guide my conclusion that Carmack provides the default legal regime for the inland leg of a multimodal shipment traveling on an international through bill of lading: \xc2\xa711706(a), which outlines the basic requirements for liability under Carmack, and \xc2\xa710501(a), which defines the jurisdiction of the Surface Transportation Board (STB or Board), the successor to the Interstate Commerce Commission (ICC), see ante, at 7. Section 11706(a) states as follows:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cA rail carrier providing transportation or service subject to the jurisdiction of the Board under this part shall issue a receipt or bill of lading for property it receives for transportation under this part. That rail carrier and any other carrier that delivers the property and is providing transportation or service subject to the jurisdiction of the Board under this part are liable to the person entitled to recover under the receipt or bill of lading. The liability imposed under this subsection is for the actual loss or injury to the property caused by\xe2\x80\x94

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(1) the receiving rail carrier;

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(2) the delivering rail carrier; or

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(3) another rail carrier over whose line or route the property is transported in the United States or from a place in the United States to a place in an adjacent foreign country when transported under a through bill of lading.

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9cFailure to issue a receipt or bill of lading does not affect the liability of a rail carrier. A delivering rail carrier is deemed to be the rail carrier performing the line-haul transportation nearest the destination but does not include a rail carrier providing only a switching service at the destination.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\nWith respect to the Board\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction, \xc2\xa710501(a) provides as follows:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(1) Subject to this chapter, the Board has jurisdiction over transportation by rail carrier that is\xe2\x80\x94

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(A) only by railroad; or

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(B) by railroad and water, when the transportation is under common control, management, or arrangement for a continuous carriage or shipment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(2) Jurisdiction under paragraph (1) applies only to transportation in the United States between a place in\xe2\x80\x94

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(A) a State and a place in the same or another State as part of the interstate rail network;

\n

.\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83.\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83.\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83.\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(E) the United States and another place in the United States through a foreign country; or

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(F) the United States and a place in a foreign country.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cA simple, straight-forward reading of [these provisions] practically compels the conclusion that the Carmack Amendment applies in a typical multimodal carriage case with inland damage.\xe2\x80\x9d Sturley, Maritime Cases About Train Wrecks: Applying Maritime Law to the Inland Damage of Ocean Cargo, 40 J. Mar. L. & Com. 1, 13 (2009) (hereinafter Train Wrecks). The first sentence of \xc2\xa711706(a) sets forth the circumstances in which a receiving rail carrier must issue a bill of lading: when property is first \xe2\x80\x9creceive[d]\xe2\x80\x9d for domestic transportation. This sentence does not define the full scope of Carmack liability, however, as the penultimate sentence of \xc2\xa711706(a) makes the absence of a bill of lading ultimately immaterial to the question of Carmack liability. Instead, the second sentence of \xc2\xa711706(a) establishes Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s expansive scope, explaining which carriers are subject to Carmack liability: not only the rail carrier that receives the property, but also \xe2\x80\x9cany other carrier that delivers the property and is providing transportation or service subject to the jurisdiction of the Board under this part.\xe2\x80\x9d Critically, that a rail carrier\xe2\x80\x99s provision of \xe2\x80\x9ctransportation or service subject to the jurisdiction of the Board\xe2\x80\x9d is the criterion that establishes liability under Carmack demonstrates that Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s scope must be considered in tandem with the provision describing the Board\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction over rail carriage.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under that provision, the Board has authority \xe2\x80\x9cover transportation by rail carrier,\xe2\x80\x9d either when that transportation is \xe2\x80\x9conly by railroad\xe2\x80\x9d or when it is \xe2\x80\x9cby railroad and water, when the transportation is under common control, management, or arrangement for a continuous carriage or shipment.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa710501(a)(1). Board jurisdiction over transportation by rail carrier \xe2\x80\x9capplies only to transportation in the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d not to transportation abroad. \xc2\xa710501(a)(2). Within the United States, however, Board jurisdiction exists broadly whenever that transportation is \xe2\x80\x9cbetween,\xe2\x80\x9d inter alia, \xe2\x80\x9ca place in \xe2\x80\xa6 a State and a place in the same or another State as part of the interstate rail network,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ca place in \xe2\x80\xa6 the United States and another place in the United States through a foreign country,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ca place in \xe2\x80\xa6 the United States and a place in a foreign country.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa710501(a)(2)(A), (E), (F).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0With the jurisdictional framework in mind, I return to the final sentences of Carmack, \xc2\xa711706. The third sentence clarifies that liability under Carmack is imposed upon (1) \xe2\x80\x9cthe receiving rail carrier\xe2\x80\x9d (which, under the first sentence of \xc2\xa711706(a) and the definition of the Board\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction over domestic rail carriage in \xc2\xa710501(a), is the rail carrier that first receives the property for transportation in the United States); (2) \xe2\x80\x9cthe delivering rail carrier\xe2\x80\x9d (which, under the last sentence of \xc2\xa711706(a) and the Board\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction over domestic rail carriage in \xc2\xa710501(a), is the final rail carrier providing the long-distance transportation \xe2\x80\x9cnearest the destination\xe2\x80\x9d in the United States); and (3) \xe2\x80\x9canother rail carrier over whose line or route the property is transported in the United States or from a place in the United States to a place in an adjacent foreign country when transported under a through bill of lading.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa711706(a). This last phrase in \xc2\xa711706(a)(3) serves two functions. It ensures that, where the entire rail transportation is \xe2\x80\x9c[with]in the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d any connecting rail carrier between the point at which the goods were received and the point at which the goods were delivered is liable under Carmack. It also ensures that, where the final destination of the goods is in Canada or Mexico, such that there is no domestic \xe2\x80\x9cdelivering\xe2\x80\x9d carrier, a connecting carrier taking on the goods in the United States will remain subject to Carmack as it travels toward its foreign destination while still in the United States. (As noted, the jurisdictional provision, incorporated by reference in \xc2\xa711706(a), is limited to \xe2\x80\x9ctransportation in the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa710501(a)(2).)

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The language of Carmack thus announces an expansive intent to provide the liability regime for rail carriage of property within the United States. Once a first domestic rail carrier subject to the Board\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction receives property in the United States, Carmack attaches, regardless of where the property originated. Carmack then applies to any other rail carrier subject to the Board\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction in the chain of transportation, no matter whether the ultimate destination of the property is in the United States or elsewhere, for the period the carrier is traveling within the United States.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It seems to me plain that, under these broadly inclusive provisions, Carmack governs rail carriers such as Union Pacific for any transportation of cargo within the United States, whether or not their domestic transportation is part of a multimodal international shipment, and whether or not they actually issued a domestic bill of lading. There is no question that Union Pacific is a \xe2\x80\x9crail carrier\xe2\x80\x9d that is \xe2\x80\x9csubject to the jurisdiction of the Board.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa711706(a). It \xe2\x80\x9creceive[d]\xe2\x80\x9d the cargo, ibid., in California for domestic transportation to four different domestic inland locations\xe2\x80\x94i.e., \xe2\x80\x9cbetween a place in \xe2\x80\xa6 a State and a place in \xe2\x80\xa6 another State,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa710501(a)(2)(A)\xe2\x80\x94while the shipment itself was transported \xe2\x80\x9cbetween a place in \xe2\x80\xa6 the United States and a place in a foreign country,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa710501(a)(2)(F). Union Pacific should have issued a bill of lading for the cargo it received, but its failure to do so does not shield it from liability, as \xc2\xa711706(a) makes clear. Carmack therefore provides the legal regime governing Union Pacific\xe2\x80\x99s rail transportation in these cases.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Carmack does not, however, govern ocean carriers such as \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line, because such carriers are not \xe2\x80\x9crail carrier[s] providing transportation or service subject to the jurisdiction of the Board.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa711706(a). The ICA defines a \xe2\x80\x9crail carrier\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9ca person providing common carrier railroad transportation for compensation.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa710102(5). To resolve whether \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line meets this definition, I would apply the STB\xe2\x80\x99s well established test and ask whether it \xe2\x80\x9cconduct[s] rail operations\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98hold[s] out\xe2\x80\x99 that service to the public.\xe2\x80\x9d Assoc. of P&C Dock Longshoremen v. Pittsburgh and Conneaut Dock Co., 8 I.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0C. 2d 280, 290 (1992).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents\xe2\x80\x94the owners of cargo that was allegedly damaged during Union Pacific\xe2\x80\x99s train derailment in Oklahoma, ante, at 2\xe2\x80\x933\xe2\x80\x94primarily contend that \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line conducted rail operations by using containers to transport the cargo from China to the United States in conjunction with Union Pacific\xe2\x80\x99s subsequent carriage of those same containers. Brief for Respondents 82\xe2\x80\x9383 (noting that the statutory definition of \xe2\x80\x9crailroad\xe2\x80\x9d includes \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98intermodal equipment used by or in connection with a railroad,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa710102(6)(A)). This interpretation goes too far. Read so literally, the statute would render a truck a railroad simply because the truck transported containers during a journey in which the containers also traveled by rail. Such a reading would gut the separate provisions of the ICA governing motor carriage in subtitle IV, part B of Title 49. The ICA\xe2\x80\x99s broad description of what the term \xe2\x80\x9crailroad\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cincludes,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa710102(6), is better read as ensuring that all services a rail carrier conducts are regulated under the Act \xe2\x80\x9cto prevent overcharges and discriminations from being made under the pretext of performing such additional services.\xe2\x80\x9d Cleveland, C., C. & St. L. R. Co. v. Dettlebach, 239 U. S. 588, 594 (1916).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At oral argument, respondents focused on a separate argument, contending that \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line should be considered a rail carrier because it conducts substantial rail operations at its depot facility in Long Beach, California. Tr. of Oral Arg. 37 (describing transportation between Port of Los Angeles, where \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line\xe2\x80\x99s private chassis transport the containers on the Port\xe2\x80\x99s train tracks to the Los Angeles train depot, where the containers are loaded onto Union Pacific trains for inland transportation). I agree with the Board, however, that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98ownership and operation of private terminal facilities, including rail yards,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d is not sufficient to bring a shipper within the definition of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a rail carrier subject to [Board] jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d where the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98terminal is maintained for [the ocean common carrier\xe2\x80\x99s] exclusive use in interchanging cargo with rail and motor carriers providing inland transportation.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Joint Application of CSX Corp. & Sea-Land Corp. Under 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa711321, 3 I.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0C. 2d 512, 519 (1987).[Footnote 1]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The jurisdictional provisions of the ICA and the Shipping Act of 1984, 46 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa740101 et seq., confirm my view that \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line is not a rail carrier \xe2\x80\x9csubject to the jurisdiction of the Board,\xe2\x80\x9d 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa711706(a), under Carmack. The STB\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction over transportation by rail carriers is \xe2\x80\x9cexclusive,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa710501(b), while ocean carriers are subject to the jurisdiction of the Federal Maritime Commission (FMC), 46 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa740102; see also 46 CFR \xc2\xa7520.1 (2009). In addition, the Board\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction over water carriage is limited to domestic water carriage. 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa713521(a)(3). The Board itself has concluded that ocean carriers providing intermodal transportation jointly with inland rail and motor carriers are subject to the FMC\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction rather than its own. See Improvement of TOFC/COFC Regulations, 3 I.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0C. 2d 869, 883 (1987).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For these reasons, Carmack governs Union Pacific but not \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line for the inland transportation at issue in these cases.

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In finding Carmack inapplicable to the inland transportation in these cases, the majority relies on the fact that Carmack does not govern ocean carriers such as \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line. While I agree that \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line is not a rail carrier, the majority places too much weight on that determination. That the ocean carrier \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line is not subject to Carmack does not affect the determination that the rail carrier Union Pacific is, for the textual reasons I have explained. The majority\xe2\x80\x99s contrary reading of the statute reflects four fundamental errors.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, the majority reads the term \xe2\x80\x9creceiving rail carrier\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa711706(a) too narrowly. There is simply no basis in the text of the statute to support the majority\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that Carmack applies only when the first rail carrier in the chain of transportation accepted the cargo at the shipment\xe2\x80\x99s point of origin. Cf. ante, at 10, 12. The two cases the majority cites for this proposition are inapposite, as neither addresses an international, multimodal shipment in which the first leg of the trip was by ocean.[Footnote 2] In St. Louis, I. M. & S. R. Co. v. Starbird, 243 U. S. 592, 594 (1917), the entire shipment was by rail from Arkansas to New York City. And in Mexican Light & Power Co. v. Texas Mexican R. Co., 331 U. S. 731, 732 (1947), the entire shipment was by rail from Pennsylvania to Mexico. Given that the first rail carrier was in each case the carrier that received the goods from the shipper and issued a through bill of lading, it is unsurprising that the Court, applying Carmack, described that carrier as the \xe2\x80\x9cinitial carrier.\xe2\x80\x9d 243 U. S., at 595; 331 U. S., at 733. But nothing in these cases, and nothing in Carmack itself, requires that the \xe2\x80\x9creceiving carrier\xe2\x80\x9d take the goods from the shipper at the shipment\xe2\x80\x99s point of origin.[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Instead, these cases are compatible with my view that the \xe2\x80\x9creceiving carrier\xe2\x80\x9d is any rail carrier that first receives cargo for transportation in the United States. Union Pacific, which is unquestionably a \xe2\x80\x9crail carrier\xe2\x80\x9d in the normal sense of those words, is also the \xe2\x80\x9creceiving carrier\xe2\x80\x9d subject to liability under Carmack.[Footnote 4] Our opinion in Reider v. Thompson, 339 U. S. 113 (1950), further supports this reading. There we explained that the test for Carmack applicability \xe2\x80\x9cis not where the shipment originated, but where the obligation of the carrier as receiving carrier originated.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 117. Because Carmack applies to domestic rail transport, and the domestic rail carrier\xe2\x80\x99s obligation in that case arose in New Orleans where the rail carrier received the goods, it did not matter that the shipment began overseas in Buenos Aires. Similarly, in the instant cases, because Union Pacific\xe2\x80\x99s obligations to transport by rail originated in California, it does not matter that the shipment began overseas in China.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, the majority errs in suggesting that the issuance of an international through bill of lading precludes the applicability of Carmack. Cf. ante, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311, 13. The cases on which the majority relies do not stand for this proposition. In Reider, the Court found Carmack applicable when the first domestic rail carrier issued a bill of lading from New Orleans to Boston. Although we observed in that opinion that there was no through bill of lading from Buenos Aires to Boston, 339 U. S., at 117, we did not say, and it is not a necessary corollary, that the presence of such a bill of lading would have commanded a different result. The observation is better read as indicating that no law other than Carmack could possibly have applied in that case: Because \xe2\x80\x9cthe shipment \xe2\x80\xa6 could not have moved an inch beyond New Orleans under the ocean bill,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 118, a new domestic bill of lading for domestic transportation was required, and as to that transportation, we held, Carmack unquestionably applied.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For its part, Mexican Light held only that, where the first rail carrier in the chain of transportation issued a bill of lading, a subsequent bill of lading issued by a later rail carrier was void because Carmack contemplates one through bill of lading governing the entire journey by rail. 331 U. S., at 734. A subsequent bill of lading by a connecting rail carrier, however, can be void under Carmack without requiring the conclusion that an international through bill of lading involving initial transportation by ocean carrier would void a subsequent bill of lading issued in the United States by the first rail carrier in the domestic chain of transportation. Because the text of Carmack expressly requires a bill of lading to be issued for property \xe2\x80\x9creceive[d] for transportation under this part,\xe2\x80\x9d and Union Pacific first received the property for rail transportation in the United States, it should have issued a bill of lading. Of course, its failure to do so did not affect its liability under Carmack (or that of a subsequent connecting or delivering carrier), as \xc2\xa711706(a) explicitly states.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Third, the majority errs in giving weight to the difference in scope between Carmack liability and the jurisdiction of the Board. Ante, at 14. I agree with the majority that Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s reach is narrower than the Board\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction. The Board\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction extends over transportation by rail carrier \xe2\x80\x9cin the United States between a place in \xe2\x80\xa6 the United States and a place in a foreign country,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa710501(a)(2)(F), which indicates that it does not matter whether the movement of the transportation is from the United States to the foreign country or from the foreign country to the United States.[Footnote 6] In contrast, Carmack applies only when a rail carrier first receives property in the United States, \xc2\xa711706(a), and therefore would not apply to a rail carrier originating in Canada and delivering in the United States without transferring the property to a domestic rail carrier.[Footnote 7] As long as there is a receiving rail carrier in the United States, however, Carmack attaches. Because the property at issue in these cases was received in the United States for domestic transportation by Union Pacific, Carmack governs the rail carrier\xe2\x80\x99s liability.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, the majority misunderstands the role I believe Carmack liability plays in international shipments to the United States. My reading of the statute would not \xe2\x80\x9coutlaw through shipments under a single bill of lading.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13. To the contrary, an overseas ocean carrier like \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line can still issue a through bill of lading governing the entire international trip to an American destination. That bill of lading reflects the ocean carrier\xe2\x80\x99s agreement with and obligations to the original shipper of the cargo. As the ocean carrier has no independent Carmack obligations of its own, the ocean carrier and the shipper are free to select whatever liability terms they wish to govern their relationship during the entire shipment. See infra, at 20\xe2\x80\x9321. Carmack simply requires an American \xe2\x80\x9creceiving rail carrier\xe2\x80\x9d like Union Pacific to issue a bill of lading to the party from whom it received the goods for shipment\xe2\x80\x94here, \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line. See Norfolk Southern R.\xc2\xa0Co. v. James N. Kirby, Pty Ltd., 543 U. S. 14, 33 (2004) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhen an intermediary contracts with a carrier to transport goods, the cargo owner\xe2\x80\x99s recovery against the carrier is limited by the liability limitation to which the intermediary and carrier agreed\xe2\x80\x9d); Great Northern R. Co. v. O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, 232 U. S. 508, 514\xe2\x80\x93515 (1914) (holding that a railroad company is entitled to treat the intermediary forwarder as the shipper). As to that bill of lading, Carmack provides the legal regime and defines the relationship between the contracting parties (unless they have agreed to contract out of Carmack, see infra, at 23\xe2\x80\x9326). The issuance of this second bill of lading, however, in no way undermines the efficiency of the through bill of lading between the ocean carrier and the original shipper, nor does it require that those parties bind themselves to apply Carmack to the inland leg.[Footnote 8]

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In addition to misreading the text, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion misapplies Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s statutory history. The Court states that no version of Carmack has ever applied to imports originating overseas on a through bill of lading. Ante, at 15. The Court further asserts that, because Congress stated that the 1978 recodification of the ICA effected no \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive change,\xe2\x80\x9d Carmack should be read consistently with this historical practice. Ante, at 16. There are three problems with this analysis.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, if \xe2\x80\x9cCongress intended no substantive change\xe2\x80\x9d to Carmack in the 1978 recodification, \xe2\x80\x9cthat would mean only that the present text is the best evidence of what the law has always meant, and that the language of the prior version cannot be relied upon to support a different reading.\xe2\x80\x9d Keene Corp. v. United States, 508 U. S. 200, 221 (1993) (Stevens, J., dissenting). Because the present text of Carmack indicates that it applies to the domestic inland rail transportation of a multimodal international shipment, there is no reason to rely on Congress\xe2\x80\x99 statement in the recodification.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, there is no necessary conflict between the pre-1978 version of Carmack and my reading of the current text. The pre-1978 text referred to a carrier \xe2\x80\x9creceiving property for transportation from a point in one State or Territory or the District of Columbia to a point in another State, Territory, [or the] District of Columbia, or from any point in the United States to a point in an adjacent foreign country.\xe2\x80\x9d 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa720(11) (1976).[Footnote 9] A rail carrier like Union Pacific who receives property in California for transportation to locations in the American midwest \xe2\x80\x9creceiv[es] property from a point in one State \xe2\x80\xa6 to a point in another State,\xe2\x80\x9d regardless of whether the property originated in California or China. The geographical restriction \xe2\x80\x9cfrom any point in the United States to a point in an adjacent foreign country\xe2\x80\x9d simply reflected agreements between the ICC and its Canadian counterpart to respect each other\xe2\x80\x99s regulation of rail carriage originating in that country. See Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae (hereinafter Brief for United States) 17\xe2\x80\x9318. It does not indicate any rejection of Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s applicability to imports as a whole or exports to a nonadjacent foreign country.[Footnote 10] Instead, the \xe2\x80\x9cadjacent foreign country\xe2\x80\x9d provision was expansive rather than limiting, ensuring that Carmack would apply where a shipment traveled by rail from New York City through to Montreal without stopping at the border of Canada.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Third, to the extent there are meaningful differences between the pre-1978 text of Carmack and its current text, it is the current text that we should interpret, regardless of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 general hortatory statement in the 1978 Public Law applicable to the entire ICA. As we have often observed, \xe2\x80\x9c[a] specific provision controls one of more general application.\xe2\x80\x9d Gozlon-Peretz v. United States, 498 U. S. 395, 407 (1991). The general statement that Congress intended no change to the ICA should not require us to ignore what the current text of the specific Carmack provision says, as both Union Pacific and \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line explicitly ask us to do. See Brief for Petitioner in No. 08\xe2\x80\x931554, at 20 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Pre-1978 Statutory Language Controls This Case\xe2\x80\x9d); Brief for Petitioners in No. 08\xe2\x80\x931553, at 41\xe2\x80\x9349 (arguing for reliance on pre-1978 text). Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 view of statutory interpretation would give rise to an unwieldy\xe2\x80\x94and unjust\xe2\x80\x94system. I would have thought it beyond cavil that litigants are entitled to rely on the currently applicable version of enacted statutes to determine their rights and obligations.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the final analysis, the meaning of the pre-1978 language is murky, and Congress\xe2\x80\x99 instruction that the 1978 recodification effected no substantive change provides no meaningful guidance. The current text does not restrict Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s coverage to trade with adjacent foreign countries, and it makes no distinction between imports and exports. Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s ambiguous history cannot justify reading such atextual limitations into the statute.[Footnote 11]

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion that its interpretation properly effectuates the goals of Carmack and \xe2\x80\x9cattains the most consistency between Carmack and [the Carriage of Goods by Sea Act (COGSA)],\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 17, reflects its fundamental misunderstanding of these statutes and the broader legal context in which the international shipping industry functions. As the mandatory default regime governing the relationship between an American receiving rail carrier and its direct contracting partner (here an overseas ocean carrier), Carmack permits the shippers who contract for a through bill of lading with the ocean carrier to receive the benefit of Carmack through that once-removed relationship. Such a legal regime is entirely consistent with COGSA and industry practice.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As noted, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s position as to Carmack rests on its erroneous belief that the \xe2\x80\x9creceiving carrier\xe2\x80\x9d must receive the goods at the point of the shipment\xe2\x80\x99s origin. Ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9315. Because Carmack provides that suit against the receiving rail carrier \xe2\x80\x9cmay only be brought \xe2\x80\xa6 in the judicial district in which the point of origin is located,\xe2\x80\x9d 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa711706(d)(2)(A)(i), and defines \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial district\xe2\x80\x9d as only a federal or state court, \xc2\xa711706(d)(2)(B), the Court mistakenly concludes that were Carmack to apply to inland transportation of international shipments, \xe2\x80\x9cthere would often be no venue in which to sue the receiving carrier\xe2\x80\x9d because that carrier would have received the goods in a foreign country where no federal or state court exists. Ante, at 14\xe2\x80\x9315, 17. Contrary to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion, however, the proper venue in which to sue a receiving carrier under Carmack is the location in which the first domestic rail carrier received the goods for domestic transportation. Supra, at 4\xe2\x80\x935, 9.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor is it true that Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s focus is on providing a single through bill of lading for an entire shipment. Ante, at 17. Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s purpose in \xc2\xa711706 is to ensure that a single bill of lading, with a single protective liability regime, governs an entire shipment by rail carrier within the United States.[Footnote 12] It does not require the rail carrier to offer Carmack-compliant terms to anyone but the party with whom the rail carrier contracts when it receives the goods. It does not place obligations on the relationship between any overseas carrier and any overseas shipper who operate under their own bill of lading. That Congress expected different liability regimes to govern ocean and rail carriers can be inferred from the different regulatory oversight provided for each type of carrier\xe2\x80\x94the FMC for the former, the STB for the latter, see supra, at 8.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, that Carmack provides certain greater protections than does COGSA demonstrates that one of Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s purposes\xe2\x80\x94beyond simply the fact of a single bill of lading governing all rail transportation\xe2\x80\x94was to specify a protective liability regime for that part of the shipment only. As compared to COGSA, Carmack provides heightened liability rules for rail transportation, compare COGSA \xc2\xa74, 49 Stat. 1207, note following 46 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa730701, p. 1179, with 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa711706(a)\xe2\x80\x93(c); stricter venue requirements, compare Vimar Seguros y Reaseguros, S. A. v. M/V Sky Reefer, 515 U. S. 528, 535 (1995), with \xc2\xa711706(d); and more generous time allowances for filing suit, compare COGSA \xc2\xa73(6), at 1179, with \xc2\xa711706(e). Congress is evidently wary of creating broad exemptions from Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s regime: While Congress has given expansive authority to the STB to deregulate carriers from the requirements of the ICA, it has precluded the STB from excusing carriers from complying with Carmack. See infra, at 25 (discussing \xc2\xa710502). By taking Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s protections out of the picture for goods that travel by rail in the United States whenever the goods first traveled by ocean liner, it is the Court that \xe2\x80\x9cundermine[s] Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s purposes,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 17. Cf. Reider, 339 U. S., at 119 (applying Carmack to domestic rail transportation of goods, even where the goods originated overseas, in order to avoid \xe2\x80\x9cimmuniz[ing] from the beneficial provisions of the [Carmack] Amendment all shipments originating in a foreign country when reshipped via the very transportation chain with which the Amendment was most concerned\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion that its interpretation best comports with the goals of COGSA fares no better. The Court is correct, ante, at 8, that Congress has permitted parties contractually to extend COGSA, which, by its own terms, applies only to the period \xe2\x80\x9cfrom the time when the goods are loaded on to the time when they are discharged from the ship.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71(e), 7, at 1178, 1180. But the Court ignores that COGSA specifically contemplates that there may be \xe2\x80\x9cother law\xe2\x80\x9d that mandatorily governs the inland leg, and makes clear that contractual extension of COGSA does not trump this law. \xc2\xa712, at 1180 (\xe2\x80\x9cNothing in [COGSA] shall be construed as superseding \xe2\x80\xa6 any \xe2\x80\xa6 other law which would be applicable in the absence of [COGSA], insofar as they relate to the duties, responsibilities, and liabilities of the ship or carrier prior to the time when the goods are loaded on or after the time they are discharged from the ship\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Sturley, Freedom of Contract and the Ironic Story of Section 7 of the Carriage of Goods by Sea Act, 4 Benedict\xe2\x80\x99s Mar. Bull. 201, 202 (2006) (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is highly ironic to suggest that section 7 was intended to facilitate the extension of COGSA [inland]. The unambiguous history demonstrates that section 7 was specifically designed to accomplish exactly the opposite result\xe2\x80\x9d). Notably, when it wants to do so, Congress knows how to specify that a contractual extension of COGSA supersedes other law: COGSA elsewhere defines a limited circumstance\xe2\x80\x94the carriage of goods by sea between ports of the United States\xe2\x80\x94in which a contractual extension of COGSA has the force of law. \xc2\xa713, at 1180 (providing that such bills of lading \xe2\x80\x9cshall be subjected hereto as fully as if subject hereto by the express provisions of [COGSA]\xe2\x80\x9d). That Congress did not make the same provision for inland travel is powerful evidence that it meant for Carmack to remain the default regime on land governing the relationship between an inland rail carrier and an overseas carrier with which it directly contracted.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court is also wrong that its interpretation avoids the risk that two sets of rules will apply to the same shipment at different times.[Footnote 13] Ante, at 17\xe2\x80\x9318. Even under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation, two sets of rules may govern, because the parties need not extend COGSA to the inland leg\xe2\x80\x94they may agree on any terms they choose to cover that transportation. \xc2\xa77, at 1180 (permitting the parties to \xe2\x80\x9cente[r] into any agreement \xe2\x80\xa6 as to the responsibility and liability of the carrier or the ship\xe2\x80\x9d for the period before the goods are loaded on and after they are discharged from the ship (emphasis added)); see also Train Wrecks 23 (\xe2\x80\x9c[C]arriers regularly include clauses in their bills of lading to limit their liability [for inland travel] in ways that COGSA prohibits\xe2\x80\x9d); 1 T. Schoenbaum, Admiralty and Maritime Law \xc2\xa710\xe2\x80\x934, p. 599\xe2\x80\x93600 (4th ed. 2004) (describing typical non-COGSA liability rules parties select for the inland leg). In these cases, for example, \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line\xe2\x80\x99s bills of lading include certain terms governing the inland leg that differ from the terms governing the ocean carriage. See, e.g., App. 147 (providing different time frames within which suit must be brought depending on whether the actionable conduct \xe2\x80\x9coccurred during other than Water Carriage\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court relies heavily on Kirby as identifying the relevant policy consideration in these cases, but it takes the wrong lesson from Kirby. In that case, we were concerned about displacing a single federal law, COGSA, with 50 varying state liability regimes.[Footnote 14] 543 U. S., at 28\xe2\x80\x9329. The rule the Court establishes today creates even greater practical difficulties than the regime we criticized in Kirby by displacing Carmack with as many liability rules as there are bills of lading. It would even permit different liability rules to apply to different lawsuits arising out of the same inland accident depending on where each piece of cargo originated. Contrary to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s view, then, the value of uniformity articulated in Kirby is best promoted by application of Carmack to the obligations of the rail carrier during the inland leg in these cases. Cf. ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x938, 17\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, while purporting to effectuate the contractual choices of the parties in the international multimodal shipping industry, ante, at 17\xe2\x80\x9320, the Court ignores the realities of the industry\xe2\x80\x99s operation. The industry has long been accustomed to drafting bills of lading that encompass two legal regimes, one governing ocean transportation and another governing inland transportation, given mandatory law governing road and rail carriage in most of Europe and in certain countries in Asia and North Africa. See generally Convention on the Contract for the International Carriage of Goods by Road, May 19, 1956, 399 U. N. T. S. 189; Uniform Rules Concerning the Contract for International Carriage of Goods by Rail, App. B to the Convention Concerning International Carriage by Rail, May 9, 1980, 1397 U. N. T. S. 112, as amended by Protocol for the Modification of the Convention Concerning International Carriage of Rail of May 9, 1980, June 3, 1999. Indeed, \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line\xe2\x80\x99s own bills of lading evidence this practice, providing that, where an \xe2\x80\x9capplicable international convention or national law\xe2\x80\x9d exists, \xe2\x80\x9ccannot be departed from,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cwould have applied\xe2\x80\x9d if a separate contract for inland carriage had been made between the merchant and the inland carrier, those laws govern \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line\xe2\x80\x99s liability. Brief for Respondents 53.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The recently signed United Nations Convention on Contracts for the International Carriage of Goods Wholly or Partly by Sea, also known as the \xe2\x80\x9cRotterdam Rules,\xe2\x80\x9d provided an opportunity for the international community to adopt rules for multimodal shipments that would be uniform for both the ocean and inland legs. See generally Train Wrecks 36\xe2\x80\x9339. Instead, the final version of the Rotterdam Rules retained the current system in which the inland leg may be governed by a different legal regime than the ocean leg. See United Nations Convention on Contracts for the International Carriage of Goods Wholly or Partly by Sea, G. A. Res. 63/122, art. 26, A/RES/63/122, (Dec. 11, 2008). The Association of American Railroads and the United States, among others, advocated for this outcome.[Footnote 15] See Proposal of the United States of America on the Definition of \xe2\x80\x9cMaritime Performing Party,\xe2\x80\x9d U. N. Doc. A/CN.9/WG.III/WP.84, at \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb61\xe2\x80\x932 (Feb. 28, 2007); Proposal by the United States of America, U. N. Doc. A/CN.9/WG.III/WP.34, \xc2\xb67 (Aug. 7, 2003); Proposals by the International Road Transport Union (IRU), U. N. Doc. A/CN.9/WG.III/WP.90, \xc2\xb61 (Mar. 27, 2007); Preparation of a Draft Instrument on the Carriage of Goods [by Sea], Compilation of Replies to a Questionnaire on Door-to-Door Transport, U. N. Doc. A/CN.9/WG.III/WP.28, at 32\xe2\x80\x9334, 43 (Jan. 31, 2003) (Comments on behalf of the Association of American Railroads and the International Road Transport Union). Thus, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s mistaken interpretation not only upsets domestic law but also disregards industry practice as evidenced by carefully calibrated international negotiations.[Footnote 16]

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because, in my view, Carmack provides the default legal regime governing the relationship between the rail carrier and the ocean carrier during the inland leg of a multimodal shipment traveling on a through bill of lading, I would reach the second question presented by these cases: whether the parties validly contracted out of Carmack. I would hold that where, as here, the STB has exempted rail carriers from Part A of the ICA pursuant to its authority as set forth in 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa710502, such rail carriers may not use \xc2\xa710709 to opt out of Carmack entirely. Instead, such rail carriers must first offer their contractual counterparties Carmack-compliant terms for liability and claims, as \xc2\xa710502(e) requires. Having reached that conclusion, I would remand for consideration of whether the requirements of \xc2\xa710502(e) were met in these cases. I set forth these views only briefly, as the Court\xe2\x80\x99s determination that Carmack does not apply at all makes resolution of these questions moot.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the Staggers Rail Act of 1980, Pub. L. 96\xe2\x80\x93448, 94 Stat. 1895, Congress set forth a national policy of \xe2\x80\x9callow[ing], to the maximum extent possible, competition and the demand for services to establish reasonable rates for transportation by rail\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cminimiz[ing] the need for Federal regulatory control\xe2\x80\x9d of the railroad industry. \xc2\xa7101, id., at 1897. Consistent with these goals, 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa710502 and 10709 provide two options for contracting around the requirements of the ICA.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 10502(a) provides that when certain conditions are met, the Board \xe2\x80\x9cshall exempt,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cto the maximum extent consistent with this part,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ca person, class of persons, or a transaction or service\xe2\x80\x9d from either a particular provision of Part A of the ICA or the entirety of that Part. Section 10502(f) specifies that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Board may exercise its authority under this section to exempt transportation that is provided by a rail carrier as part of a continuous intermodal movement.\xe2\x80\x9d Acting pursuant to this authority, the Board has broadly exempted such transportation \xe2\x80\x9cfrom the requirements of [the ICA].\xe2\x80\x9d 49 CFR \xc2\xa71090.2 (2009). The authority to issue broad exemptions, however, is not unlimited. Under 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa710502(e), \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o exemption order issued pursuant to this section shall operate to relieve any rail carrier from an obligation to provide contractual terms for liability and claims which are consistent with the provisions of [Carmack],\xe2\x80\x9d although, at the same time, \xe2\x80\x9c[n]othing \xe2\x80\xa6 shall prevent rail carriers from offering alternative terms.\xe2\x80\x9d Section 10502(g) further limits the Board from exempting rail carriers from their obligations to comply with certain employee protections under Part A of the ICA.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In turn, under \xc2\xa710709(a), \xe2\x80\x9c[o]ne or more rail carriers providing transportation subject to the jurisdiction of the Board \xe2\x80\xa6 may enter into a contract with one or more purchasers of rail services to provide specified services under specified rates and conditions.\xe2\x80\x9d Having signed such a contract, a rail carrier \xe2\x80\x9cshall have no duty in connection with services provided under such contract other than those duties specified by the terms of the contract.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa710709(b). Once such a contract is made, that contract, \xe2\x80\x9cand transportation under such contract, shall not be subject to this part, and may not be subsequently challenged before the Board or in any court on the grounds that such contract violates a provision of [Part A of the ICA].\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa710709(c)(1).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0According to Union Pacific, \xc2\xa710502(e) limits only the Board\xe2\x80\x99s exemption ability; it does not place any affirmative obligation on rail carriers to offer Carmack-compliant terms. Rail carriers, Union Pacific contends, may opt out of Carmack entirely simply by entering into a contract under \xc2\xa710709, thus escaping any duty imposed by Part A of the ICA. I disagree. I am persuaded by the Government\xe2\x80\x99s view that because the Board\xe2\x80\x99s order in 49 CFR \xc2\xa71090.2 exempted intermodal rail transportation from all of Part A of the ICA, which includes 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa710709, \xe2\x80\x9cUnion Pacific could not properly enter into a contract under Section 10709 to relieve it of its obligations under Section 10502(e).\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 31. Those obligations require \xe2\x80\x9ca rail carrier providing exempt transportation [to] offer the shipper the option of contractual terms for liability and claims consistent with Carmack, presumably at a higher rate,\xe2\x80\x9d and they permit such a rail carrier to \xe2\x80\x9center into a contract with different terms only if the shipper does not select that option.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 30.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Observing that the Board\xe2\x80\x99s exemption order relieves intermodal rail transportation from the \xe2\x80\x9crequirements\xe2\x80\x9d of Part A, Union Pacific contends that \xc2\xa710709 is not a requirement but a privilege and therefore is not included within the exemption. In clarifying its order, however, the Board has described the exemption as one from \xe2\x80\x9cregulation\xe2\x80\x9d under the ICA or \xe2\x80\x9capplication\xe2\x80\x9d of that Act. See, e.g., Improvement of TOFC/COFC Regulations, 3 I.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0C. 2d, at 869\xe2\x80\x93870. Especially in light of this clarification, there seems little reason to ascribe significance to the Board\xe2\x80\x99s use of the word \xe2\x80\x9crequirements,\xe2\x80\x9d instead of the statutory term \xe2\x80\x9cprovision,\xe2\x80\x9d in the exemption order.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government aptly describes the policy concerns that justify this reading of the interplay between \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa710502 and 10709. Brief for United States 31\xe2\x80\x9332. Because a rail carrier\xe2\x80\x99s counterparty to a \xc2\xa710709 contract can ordinarily require a rail carrier to comply with common carriage rates and terms under Part A (including Carmack), such counterparties possess considerable bargaining power. But rail carriers the Board has exempted from Part A under \xc2\xa710502 lack any obligation to comply with that Part. If exempt carriers could escape Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s obligations under \xc2\xa710709, their counterparties would be at a significant disadvantage as compared to counterparties to contracts with nonexempt carriers. Such a disadvantage cannot be squared with Congress\xe2\x80\x99 evident intent, as expressed in \xc2\xa710502(e), to ensure that no carrier may be automatically exempted from Carmack.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This interpretation of \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa710502 and 10709 imposes no unfairness on exempt rail carriers. As the Court of Appeals explained, \xe2\x80\x9ccarriers providing exempt transportation gain the benefits of deregulation, but lose the opportunity to contract for preferable terms under \xc2\xa710709 without first offering Carmack terms.\xe2\x80\x9d 557 F.\xc2\xa03d 985, 1002 (CA9 2009). Given rail carriers\xe2\x80\x99 ability to charge higher rates for full Carmack coverage, see New York, N. H. & H. R. Co. v. Nothnagle, 346 U. S. 128, 135 (1953), and the likelihood that some counterparties will agree to reject Carmack-compliant terms in favor of a lower price, such a trade-off makes eminent sense.

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Whether Union Pacific properly contracted out of Carmack under \xc2\xa710502(e) requires a factual determination better suited for resolution by the District Court in the first instance. Accordingly, I would remand for consideration of that issue. Cf. 557 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1003. Union Pacific also raises a related legal argument not decided by the courts below: that the forum selection clause at issue in these cases is valid because venue is not encompassed within the phrase \xe2\x80\x9ccontractual terms for liability and claims\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa710502(e). To the extent this argument is not waived, it would also be properly considered on remand.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In endorsing a strained reading of the text, history, and purpose of Carmack, the Court is evidently concerned with a perceived need to enforce the COGSA-based contracts that the \xe2\x80\x9csophisticated cargo owners\xe2\x80\x9d here made with \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line. Ante, at 18. But these cases do not require the Court to interpret or examine the contract between the cargo owners and \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line. The Court need consider only the legal relationship between Union Pacific and \xe2\x80\x9cK\xe2\x80\x9d Line as its direct contracting party. As to that relationship, it bears emphasizing that industry actors on all sides are sophisticated and can easily adapt to a regime in which Carmack provides the default rule governing the rail carrier\xe2\x80\x99s liability during the inland leg of a multimodal shipment traveling on an international through bill of lading. See, e.g., Train Wrecks 40 (describing how ocean and rail carriers have drafted their contracts to account for\xe2\x80\x94and permissibly escape\xe2\x80\x94Carmack\xe2\x80\x99s applicability); cf. Kirby, 543 U. S., at 36 (recognizing that \xe2\x80\x9cour decision does no more than provide a legal backdrop against which future bills of lading will be negotiated\xe2\x80\x9d). In disregarding Congress\xe2\x80\x99 commands in both Carmack and COGSA and in discounting the practical realities reflected in the Rotterdam Rules and other international conventions governing the carriage of goods, the Court ignores what we acknowledged in Kirby: \xe2\x80\x9cIt is not \xe2\x80\xa6 this Court\xe2\x80\x99s task to structure the international shipping industry.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. I respectfully dissent.
