FRANCHISE TAX BOARD OF CALIFORNIA v. HYATT

\n

certiorari to the supreme court of nevada

\n

No. 14\xe2\x80\x931175.\xe2\x80\x83Argued December 7, 2015\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 19, 2016

\n

Respondent Hyatt claims that he moved from California to Nevada in 1991, but petitioner Franchise Tax Board of California, a state agency, claims that he actually moved in 1992 and thus owes California millions in taxes, penalties, and interest. Hyatt filed suit in Nevada state court, which had jurisdiction over California under Nevada v. Hall,\n\n440 U.\xc2\xa0S. 410\n, seeking damages for California\xe2\x80\x99s alleged abusive audit and investigation practices. After this Court affirmed the Nevada Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling that Nevada courts, as a matter of comity, would immunize California to the same extent that Nevada law would immunize its own agencies and officials, see Franchise Tax Bd. of Cal. v. Hyatt,\n\n538 U.\xc2\xa0S. 488\n, the case went to trial, where Hyatt was awarded almost $500 million in damages and fees. On appeal, California argued that the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s Full Faith and Credit Clause, Art.\xc2\xa0IV, \xc2\xa71, required Nevada to limit damages to $50,000, the maximum that Nevada law would permit in a similar suit against its own officials. The Nevada Supreme Court, however, affirmed $1 million of the award and ordered a retrial on another damages issue, stating that the $50,000 maximum would not apply on remand.

\n

Held:

\n

1.\xc2\xa0The Court is equally divided on the question whether Nevada v. Hall should be overruled and thus affirms the Nevada courts\xe2\x80\x99 exercise of jurisdiction over California\xe2\x80\x99s state agency. P.\xc2\xa04.

\n

2.\xc2\xa0The Constitution does not permit Nevada to apply a rule of Nevada law that awards damages against California that are greater than it could award against Nevada in similar circumstances. This conclusion is consistent with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents. A statute is a \xe2\x80\x9cpublic Act\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning of the Full Faith and Credit Clause. While a State is not required \xe2\x80\x9cto substitute for its own statute .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the statute of another State reflecting a conflicting and opposed policy,\xe2\x80\x9d Carroll v. Lanza,\n\n349 U.\xc2\xa0S. 408\n, a State\xe2\x80\x99s decision to decline to apply another State\xe2\x80\x99s statute on this ground must not embody a \xe2\x80\x9cpolicy of hostility to the public Acts\xe2\x80\x9d of that other State, id., at 413. Using this approach, the Court found no violation of the Clause in Carroll v. Lanza or in Franchise Tax Bd. the first time this litigation was considered. By contrast, the rule of unlimited damages applied here is not only \xe2\x80\x9copposed\xe2\x80\x9d to California\xe2\x80\x99s law of complete immunity; it is also inconsistent with the general principles of Nevada immunity law, which limit damages awards to $50,000. Nevada explained its departure from those general principles by describing California\xe2\x80\x99s own system of controlling its agencies as an inadequate remedy for Nevada\xe2\x80\x99s citizens. A State that disregards its own ordinary legal principles on this ground employs a constitutionally impermissible \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98policy of hostility to the public Acts\xe2\x80\x99 of a sister State.\xe2\x80\x9d 538 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 499. The Nevada Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision thereby lacks the \xe2\x80\x9chealthy regard for California\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign status\xe2\x80\x9d that was the hallmark of its earlier decision. Ibid. This holding does not indicate a return to a complex \xe2\x80\x9cbalancing-of-interests approach to conflicts of law under the Full Faith and Credit Clause.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 496. Rather, Nevada\xe2\x80\x99s hostility toward California is clearly evident in its decision to devise a special, discriminatory damages rule that applies only to a sister State. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

130 Nev. ___, 335 P.\xc2\xa03d 125, vacated and remanded.

\n

Breyer, J., delivered the opinion of the Court, in which Kennedy, Ginsburg, Sotomayor, and Kagan, JJ., joined. Alito, J., concurred in the judgment. Roberts, C.\xc2\xa0J., filed a dissenting opinion, in which Thomas, J., joined.

FRANCHISE TAX BOARD OF CALIFORNIA, PETITIONER v. GILBERT P. HYATT

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of nevada

\n

[April 19, 2016]

\n

\n

Justice Breyer delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

In Nevada v. Hall,\n\n440 U.\xc2\xa0S. 410 (1979)\n, this Court held that one State (here, Nevada) can open the doors of its courts to a private citizen\xe2\x80\x99s lawsuit against another State (here, California) without the other State\xe2\x80\x99s consent. In this case, a private citizen, a resident of Nevada, has brought a suit in Nevada\xe2\x80\x99s courts against the Franchise Tax Board of California, an agency of the State of California. The board has asked us to overrule Hall and hold that the Nevada courts lack jurisdiction to hear this lawsuit. The Court is equally divided on this question, and we consequently affirm the Nevada courts\xe2\x80\x99 exercise of jurisdiction over California. See, e.g., Exxon Shipping Co. v. Baker,\n\n554 U.\xc2\xa0S. 471,\n484 (2008)\n (citing Durant v. Essex Co., 7 Wall. 107, 112 (1869)).

\n

California also asks us to reverse the Nevada court\xe2\x80\x99s decision insofar as it awards the private citizen greater damages than Nevada law would permit a private citizen to obtain in a similar suit against Nevada\xe2\x80\x99s own agencies. We agree that Nevada\xe2\x80\x99s application of its damages law in this case reflects a special, and constitutionally forbidden, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98policy of hostility to the public Acts\xe2\x80\x99 of a sister State,\xe2\x80\x9d namely, California. U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0IV, \xc2\xa71 (Full Faith and Credit Clause); Franchise Tax Bd. of Cal. v. Hyatt,\n\n538 U.\xc2\xa0S. 488,\n499 (2003)\n (quoting Carroll v. Lanza,\n\n349 U.\xc2\xa0S. 408,\n413 (1955)\n). We set aside the Nevada Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision accordingly.

\n

I

\n

Gilbert P. Hyatt, the respondent here, moved from California to Nevada in the early 1990\xe2\x80\x99s. He says that he moved to Nevada in September 1991. California\xe2\x80\x99s Franchise Tax Board, however, after an investigation and tax audit, claimed that Hyatt moved to Nevada later, in April 1992, and that he consequently owed California more than $10 million in taxes, associated penalties, and interest.

\n

Hyatt filed this lawsuit in Nevada state court against California\xe2\x80\x99s Franchise Tax Board, a California state agency. Hyatt sought damages for what he considered the board\xe2\x80\x99s abusive audit and investigation practices, including rifling through his private mail, combing through his garbage, and examining private activities at his place of worship. See App. 213\xe2\x80\x93245, 267\xe2\x80\x93268.

\n

California recognized that, under Hall, the Constitution permits Nevada\xe2\x80\x99s courts to assert jurisdiction over California despite California\xe2\x80\x99s lack of consent. California nonetheless asked the Nevada courts to dismiss the case on other constitutional grounds. California law, it pointed out, provided state agencies with immunity from lawsuits based upon actions taken during the course of collecting taxes. Cal. Govt. Code Ann. \xc2\xa7860.2 (West 1995); see also \xc2\xa7860.2 (West 2012). It argued that the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s Full Faith and Credit Clause required Nevada to apply California\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign immunity law to Hyatt\xe2\x80\x99s case. Nevada\xe2\x80\x99s Supreme Court, however, rejected California\xe2\x80\x99s claim. It held that Nevada\xe2\x80\x99s courts, as a matter of comity, would immunize California where Nevada law would similarly immunize its own agencies and officials (e.g., for actions taken in the performance of a \xe2\x80\x9cdiscretionary\xe2\x80\x9d function), but they would not immunize California where Nevada law permitted actions against Nevada agencies, say, for acts taken in bad faith or for intentional torts. App. to Pet. for Cert. in Franchise Tax Bd. of Cal. v. Hyatt, O.\xc2\xa0T. 2002, No. 42, p. 12. We reviewed that decision, and we affirmed. Franchise Tax Bd., supra, at 499.

\n

On remand, the case went to trial. A jury found in Hyatt\xe2\x80\x99s favor and awarded him close to $500 million in damages (both compensatory and punitive) and fees (including attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees). California appealed. It argued that the trial court had not properly followed the Nevada Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s earlier decision. California explained that in a similar suit against similar Nevada officials, Nevada statutory law would limit damages to $50,000, and it argued that the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s Full Faith and Credit Clause required Nevada to limit damages similarly here.

\n

The Nevada Supreme Court accepted the premise that Nevada statutes would impose a $50,000 limit in a similar suit against its own officials. See 130 Nev. ___, ___, 335 P.\xc2\xa03d 125, 145\xe2\x80\x93146 (2014); see also Nev. Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa741.035(1) (1995). But the court rejected California\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion. Instead, while setting aside much of the damages award, it nonetheless affirmed $1 million of the award (earmarked as compensation for fraud), and it remanded for a retrial on the question of damages for intentional infliction of emotional distress. In doing so, it stated that \xe2\x80\x9cdamages awarded on remand .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. are not subject to any statutory cap.\xe2\x80\x9d 130 Nev., at ___, 335 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 153. The Nevada Supreme Court explained its holding by stating that California\xe2\x80\x99s efforts to control the actions of its own agencies were inadequate as applied to Nevada\xe2\x80\x99s own citizens. Hence, Nevada\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cpolicy interest in providing adequate redress to Nevada\xe2\x80\x99s citizens [wa]s paramount to providing [California] a statutory cap on damages under comity.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___, 335 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 147.

\n

California petitioned for certiorari. We agreed to decide two questions. First, whether to overrule Hall. And, second, if we did not do so, whether the Constitution permits Nevada to award Hyatt damages against a California state agency that are greater than those that Nevada would award in a similar suit against its own stateagencies.

\n

II

\n

In light of our 4-to-4 affirmance of Nevada\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of jurisdiction over California\xe2\x80\x99s state agency, we must consider the second question: Whether the Constitution permits Nevada to award damages against California agencies under Nevada law that are greater than it could award against Nevada agencies in similar circumstances. We conclude that it does not. The Nevada Supreme Court has ignored both Nevada\xe2\x80\x99s typical rules of immunity and California\xe2\x80\x99s immunity-related statutes (insofar as California\xe2\x80\x99s statutes would prohibit a monetary recovery that is greater in amount than the maximum recovery that Nevada law would permit in similar circumstances). Instead, it has applied a special rule of law that evinces a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98policy of hostility\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d toward California. Franchise Tax Bd., supra, at 499 (quoting Carroll v. Lanza, supra, at 413). Doing so violates the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that \xe2\x80\x9cFull Faith and Credit shall be given in each State to the public Acts, Records and judicial Proceedings of every other State.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0IV, \xc2\xa71.

\n

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents strongly support this conclusion. A statute is a \xe2\x80\x9cpublic Act\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning of the Full Faith and Credit Clause. See, e.g., Carroll v. Lanza, supra, at 411; see also\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71738 (referring to \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Acts of the legislature\xe2\x80\x9d in the full faith and credit context). We have said that the Clause \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not require a State to substitute for its own statute, applicable to persons and events within it, the statute of another State reflecting a conflicting and opposed policy.\xe2\x80\x9d Carroll v. Lanza, 349 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 412. But when affirming a State\xe2\x80\x99s decision to decline to apply another State\xe2\x80\x99s statute on this ground, we have consistently emphasized that the State had \xe2\x80\x9cnot adopt[ed] any policy of hostility to the public Acts\xe2\x80\x9d of that other State. Id., at 413.

\n

In Carroll v. Lanza, the Court considered a negligence action brought by a Missouri worker in Arkansas\xe2\x80\x99 courts. We held that the Arkansas courts need not apply a time limitation contained in Missouri\xe2\x80\x99s (but not in Arkansas\xe2\x80\x99) workman\xe2\x80\x99s compensation law. Id., at 413\xe2\x80\x93414. In doing so, we emphasized both that (1) Missouri law (compared with Arkansas law) embodied \xe2\x80\x9ca conflicting and opposed policy,\xe2\x80\x9d and (2) Arkansas law did not embody \xe2\x80\x9cany policy of hostility to the public Acts of Missouri.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 412\xe2\x80\x93413. This second requirement was well established in earlier law. See, e.g., Broderick v. Rosner,\n\n294 U.\xc2\xa0S. 629\n\xe2\x80\x93643 (1935) (New Jersey may not enforce a jurisdictional statute that would permit enforcement of certain claims under New Jersey law but \xe2\x80\x9cdeny the enforcement\xe2\x80\x9d of similar, valid claims under New York law); Hughes v. Fetter,\n\n341 U.\xc2\xa0S. 609\n\xe2\x80\x93612 (1951) (invalidating a Wisconsin statute that \xe2\x80\x9cclose[d] the doors of its courts\xe2\x80\x9d to an Illinois cause of action while permitting adjudication of similar Wisconsin claims).

\n

We followed this same approach when we considered the litigation now before us for the first time. See Franchise Tax Bd., 538 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 498\xe2\x80\x93499. Nevada had permitted Hyatt to sue California in Nevada courts. See id., at 497 (citing Hall, 440 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 414\xe2\x80\x93421). Nevada\xe2\x80\x99s courts recognized that California\xe2\x80\x99s law of complete immunity would prevent any recovery in this case. The Nevada Supreme Court consequently did not apply California law. It applied Nevada law instead. We upheld that decision as consistent with the Full Faith and Credit Clause. But in doing so, we emphasized both that (1) the Clause does not require one State to apply another State\xe2\x80\x99s law that violates its \xe2\x80\x9cown legitimate public policy,\xe2\x80\x9d Franchise Tax Bd., supra, at 497\xe2\x80\x93498 (citing Hall, supra, at 424), and (2) Nevada\xe2\x80\x99s choice of law did not \xe2\x80\x9cexhibi[t] a \xe2\x80\x98policy of hostility to the public Acts\xe2\x80\x99 of a sister State.\xe2\x80\x9d Franchise Tax Bd., supra, at 499 (quoting Carroll v. Lanza, supra, at 413). Rather, Nevada had evinced \xe2\x80\x9ca healthy regard for California\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign status,\xe2\x80\x9d we said, by \xe2\x80\x9crelying on the contours of Nevada\xe2\x80\x99s own sovereign immunity from suit as a benchmark for its analysis.\xe2\x80\x9d Franchise Tax Bd., supra, at 499.

\n

The Nevada decision before us embodies a critical departure from its earlier approach. Nevada has not applied the principles of Nevada law ordinarily applicable to suits against Nevada\xe2\x80\x99s own agencies. Rather, it has applied a special rule of law applicable only in lawsuits against its sister States, such as California. With respect to damages awards greater than $50,000, the ordinary principles of Nevada law do not \xe2\x80\x9cconflic[t]\xe2\x80\x9d with California law, for both laws would grant immunity. Carroll v. Lanza, 349 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 412. Similarly, in respect to such amounts, the \xe2\x80\x9cpolic[ies]\xe2\x80\x9d underlying California law and Nevada\xe2\x80\x99s usual approach are not \xe2\x80\x9copposed\xe2\x80\x9d; they are consistent. Id., at 412\xe2\x80\x93413.

\n

But that is not so in respect to Nevada\xe2\x80\x99s special rule. That rule, allowing damages awards greater than $50,000, is not only \xe2\x80\x9copposed\xe2\x80\x9d to California law, ibid.; it is also inconsistent with the general principles of Nevada immunity law, see Franchise Tax Bd., supra, at 499. The Nevada Supreme Court explained its departure from those general principles by describing California\xe2\x80\x99s system of controlling its own agencies as failing to provide \xe2\x80\x9cadequate\xe2\x80\x9d recourse to Nevada\xe2\x80\x99s citizens. 130 Nev., at ___, 335 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 147. It expressed concerns about the fact that California\xe2\x80\x99s agencies \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98operat[e] outside\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the systems of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98legislative control, administrative oversight, and public accountability\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d that Nevada applies to its own agencies. Ibid. (quoting Faulkner v. University of Tenn., 627 So.\xc2\xa02d 362 (Ala. 1992)). Such an explanation, which amounts to little more than a conclusory statement disparaging California\xe2\x80\x99s own legislative, judicial, and administrative controls, cannot justify the application of a special and discriminatory rule. Rather, viewed through a full faith and credit lens, a State that disregards its own ordinary legal principles on this ground is hostile to another State. A constitutional rule that would permit this kind of discriminatory hostility is likely to cause chaotic interference by some States into the internal, legislative affairs of others. Imagine, for example, that many or all States enacted such discriminatory, special laws, and justified them on the sole basis that (in their view) a sister State\xe2\x80\x99s law provided inadequate protection to their citizens. Would each affected sister State have to change its own laws? Entirely? Piece-by-piece, in order to respond to the new special laws enacted by every other State? It is difficult to reconcile such a system of special and discriminatory rules with the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s vision of 50 individual and equally dignified States. In light of the \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutional equality\xe2\x80\x9d among the States, Coyle v. Smith,\n\n221 U.\xc2\xa0S. 559,\n580 (1911)\n, Nevada has not offered \xe2\x80\x9csufficient policy considerations\xe2\x80\x9d to justify the application of a special rule of Nevada law that discriminates against its sister States, Carroll v. Lanza, supra, at 413. In our view, Nevada\xe2\x80\x99s rule lacks the \xe2\x80\x9chealthy regard for California\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign status\xe2\x80\x9d that was the hallmark of its earlier decision, and it reflects a constitutionally impermissible \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98policy of hostility to the public Acts\xe2\x80\x99 of a sister State.\xe2\x80\x9d Franchise Tax Bd., supra, at 499 (quoting Carroll v. Lanza, supra, at 413).

\n

In so holding we need not, and do not, intend to return to a complex \xe2\x80\x9cbalancing-of-interests approach to conflicts of law under the Full Faith and Credit Clause.\xe2\x80\x9d Franchise Tax Bd., 538 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 496. Long ago this Court\xe2\x80\x99s efforts to apply that kind of analysis led to results that seemed to differ depending, for example, upon whether the case involved commercial law, a shareholders\xe2\x80\x99 action, insurance claims, or workman\xe2\x80\x99s compensation statutes. See, e.g., Bradford Elec. Light Co. v. Clapper,\n\n286 U.\xc2\xa0S. 145\n\xe2\x80\x93159 (1932); Carroll v. Lanza, supra, at 414\xe2\x80\x93420 (Frankfurter, J., dissenting) (listing, and trying to classify, nearly 50 cases). We have since abandoned that approach, and we continue to recognize that a State need not \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98substitute the statutes of other states for its own statutes dealing with a subject matter concerning which it is competent to legislate.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Franchise Tax Bd., supra, at 496 (quoting Pacific Employers Ins. Co. v. Industrial Accident Comm\xe2\x80\x99n,\n\n306 U. S. 493,\n501 (1939)\n). But here, we can safely conclude that, in devising a special\xe2\x80\x94and hostile\xe2\x80\x94rule for California, Nevada has not \xe2\x80\x9csensitively applied principles of comity with a healthy regard for California\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign status.\xe2\x80\x9d Franchise Tax Bd., supra, at 499; see Thomas v. Washington Gas Light Co.,\n\n448 U.\xc2\xa0S. 261,\n272 (1980)\n (plurality opinion) (Clause seeks to prevent \xe2\x80\x9cparochial entrenchment on the interests of other States\xe2\x80\x9d); Allstate Ins. Co. v. Hague,\n\n449 U.\xc2\xa0S. 302\n, and n. 10 (1981) (Stevens, J., concurring in judgment) (Clause is properly brought to bear when a State\xe2\x80\x99s choice of law \xe2\x80\x9cthreatens the federal interest in national unity by unjustifiably infringing upon the legitimate interests of another State\xe2\x80\x9d); cf. Supreme Court of N.\xc2\xa0H. v. Piper,\n\n470 U.\xc2\xa0S. 274,\n288 (1985)\n (Privileges and Immunities Clause prevents the New Hampshire Supreme Court from promulgating a rule that limits bar admission to state residents, discriminating against out-of-state lawyers); Bendix Autolite Corp. v. Midwesco Enterprises, Inc.,\n\n486 U.\xc2\xa0S. 888,\n894 (1988)\n (Commerce Clause invalidates a statute of limitations that \xe2\x80\x9cimposes a greater burden on out-of-state companies than it does on [in-state] companies\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

For these reasons, insofar as the Nevada Supreme Court has declined to apply California law in favor of a special rule of Nevada law that is hostile to its sister States, we find its decision unconstitutional. We vacate its judgment and remand the case for further proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

FRANCHISE TAX BOARD OF CALIFORNIA, PETITIONER v. GILBERT P. HYATT

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of nevada

\n

[April 19, 2016]

\n

\n

Chief Justice Roberts, with whom Justice Thomas joins, dissenting.

\n

Petitioner Franchise Tax Board is the California agency that collects California\xe2\x80\x99s state income tax. Respondent Gilbert Hyatt, a resident of Nevada, filed suit in Nevada state court against the Board, alleging that it had committed numerous torts in the course of auditing his California tax returns. The Board is immune from such a suit in California courts. The last time this case was before us, we held that the Nevada Supreme Court could apply Nevada law to resolve the Board\xe2\x80\x99s claim that it was immune from suit in Nevada as well. Following our decision, the Nevada Supreme Court upheld a $1 million jury award against the Board after concluding that the Board did not enjoy immunity under Nevada law.

\n

Today the Court shifts course. It now holds that the Full Faith and Credit Clause requires the Nevada Supreme Court to afford the Board immunity to the extent Nevada agencies are entitled to immunity under Nevada law. Because damages in a similar suit against Nevada agencies are capped at $50,000 by Nevada law, the Court concludes that damages against the Board must be capped at that level as well.

\n

That seems fair. But, for better or worse, the word \xe2\x80\x9cfair\xe2\x80\x9d does not appear in the Full Faith and Credit Clause. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision is contrary to our precedent holding that the Clause does not block a State from applying its own law to redress an injury within its own borders. The opinion also departs from the text of the Clause, which\xe2\x80\x94when it applies\xe2\x80\x94requires a State to give full faith and credit to another State\xe2\x80\x99s laws. The Court instead permits partial credit: To comply with the Full Faith and Credit Clause, the Nevada Supreme Court need only afford the Board the same limited immunity that Nevada agencies enjoy.

\n

I respectfully dissent.

\n

I

\n

In 1991 Gilbert Hyatt sold his house in California and rented an apartment, registered to vote, and opened a bank account in Nevada. When he filed his 1991 and 1992 tax returns, he claimed Nevada as his place of residence. Unlike California, Nevada has no state income tax, and the move saved Hyatt millions of dollars in California taxes. California\xe2\x80\x99s Franchise Tax Board was suspicious, and it initiated an audit.

\n

In the course of the audit, employees of the Board traveled to Nevada and allegedly peered through Hyatt\xe2\x80\x99s windows, rummaged around in his garbage, contacted his estranged family members, and shared his personal information not only with newspapers but also with his business contacts and even his place of worship. Hyatt claims that one employee in particular had it in for him, referring to him in antisemitic terms and taking \xe2\x80\x9ctrophy-like pictures\xe2\x80\x9d in front of his home after the audit. Brief for Respondent 3. As a result of the audit, the Board determined that Hyatt was a resident of California for 1991 and part of 1992, and that he accordingly owed over $10 million in unpaid state income taxes, penalties, and interest.

\n

Hyatt protested the audit before the Board, which upheld the audit following an 11-year administrative proceeding. Hyatt is still challenging the audit in California court. In 1998, Hyatt also filed suit against the Board in Nevada state court. In that suit, which is the subject of this case, Hyatt claimed that the Board committed a variety of torts, including fraud, intentional infliction of emotional distress, and invasion of privacy. The Board is immune from suit under California law, and it argued that Nevada was required under the Full Faith and Credit Clause to enforce California\xe2\x80\x99s immunity law.

\n

When the case reached the Nevada Supreme Court, that court held, applying general principles of comity under Nevada law, that the Board was entitled to immunity for its negligent but not intentional torts\xe2\x80\x94the same immu-nity afforded Nevada state agencies. Not satisfied, the Board pursued its claim of complete immunity to this Court, but we affirmed. We ruled that the Full Faith and Credit Clause did not prohibit Nevada from applying its own immunity law to the dispute. Franchise Tax Bd. of Cal. v. Hyatt,\n\n538 U.\xc2\xa0S. 488\n\xe2\x80\x93499 (2003).

\n

On remand, the trial court conducted a four-month jury trial. The jury found for Hyatt, awarding him $1 million for fraud, $52 million for invasion of privacy, $85 million for emotional distress, and $250 million in punitive damages. On appeal, the Nevada Supreme Court significantly reduced the award, concluding that the invasion of privacy claims failed as a matter of law. Applying principles of comity, the Nevada Supreme Court also held that because Nevada state agencies are not subject to punitive dam-ages, the Board was not liable for the $250 million punitive damages award. The court did hold the Board responsible for the $1 million fraud judgment, however, and it remanded for a new trial on damages for the emotional distress claim. Although tort liability for Nevada state agencies was capped at $50,000 under Nevada law, the court held that it was against Nevada\xe2\x80\x99s public policy to apply that cap to the Board\xe2\x80\x99s liability for the fraud and emotional distress claims. The Board sought review by this Court, and we again granted certiorari. 576 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015).

\n

II

\n

A

\n

The Full Faith and Credit Clause provides that \xe2\x80\x9cFull Faith and Credit shall be given in each State to the public Acts, Records, and judicial Proceedings of every other State.\xe2\x80\x9d U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Art. IV, \xc2\xa71. The purpose of the Clause \xe2\x80\x9cwas to alter the status of the several states as independent foreign sovereignties, each free to ignore obligations created under the laws or by the judicial proceedings of the others, and to make them integral parts of a single nation.\xe2\x80\x9d Milwaukee County v. M. E. White Co.,\n\n296 U.\xc2\xa0S. 268\n\xe2\x80\x93277 (1935).

\n

The Full Faith and Credit Clause applies in a straightforward fashion to state court judgments: \xe2\x80\x9cA judgment entered in one State must be respected in another pro-vided that the first State had jurisdiction over the parties and the subject matter.\xe2\x80\x9d Nevada v. Hall,\n\n440 U.\xc2\xa0S. 410,\n421 (1979)\n. The Clause is more difficult to apply to \xe2\x80\x9cpublic Acts,\xe2\x80\x9d which include the laws of other States. See Carroll v. Lanza,\n\n349 U.\xc2\xa0S. 408,\n411 (1955)\n. State courts must give full faith and credit to those laws. But what does that mean in practice?

\n

It is clear that state courts are not always required to apply the laws of other States. State laws frequently conflict, and a \xe2\x80\x9crigid and literal enforcement of the full faith and credit clause, without regard to the statute of the forum, would lead to the absurd result that, wherever the conflict arises, the statute of each state must be enforced in the courts of the other, but cannot be in its own.\xe2\x80\x9d Alaska Packers Assn. v. Industrial Accident Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of Cal.,\n\n294 U.\xc2\xa0S. 532,\n547 (1935)\n. Accordingly, this Court has treated the Full Faith and Credit Clause as a \xe2\x80\x9cconflicts of law\xe2\x80\x9d provision that dictates when a State must apply the laws of another State rather than its own. Franchise Tax Bd., 538 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 496; see also Hall, 440 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 424 (California court is not required to apply Nevada law).

\n

Under the Full Faith and Credit Clause, \xe2\x80\x9cit is frequently the case\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9ca court can lawfully apply either the law of one State or the contrary law of another.\xe2\x80\x9d Franchise Tax Bd., 538 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 496 (internal quotation marks omitted). As we have explained,

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cthe very nature of the federal union of states, to which are reserved some of the attributes of sovereignty, precludes resort to the full faith and credit clause as the means for compelling a state to substitute the statutes of other states for its own statutes dealing with a subject matter concerning which it is competent to legislate.\xe2\x80\x9d Pacific Employers Ins. Co. v. Industrial Accident Comm\xe2\x80\x99n,\n\n306 U.\xc2\xa0S. 493,\n501 (1939)\n.

\n

This Court has generally held that when a State chooses \xe2\x80\x9cto apply its own rule of law to give affirmative relief for an action arising within its borders,\xe2\x80\x9d the Full Faith and Credit Clause is satisfied. Carroll, 349 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 413; see Hall, 440 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 424 (California court may apply California law consistent with the State\xe2\x80\x99s interest in \xe2\x80\x9cproviding full protection to those who are injured on its highways\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).

\n

A State may not apply its own law, however, if doing so reflects a \xe2\x80\x9cpolicy of hostility to the public Acts\xe2\x80\x9d of another State. Carroll, 349 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 413. A State is considered to have adopted such a policy if it has \xe2\x80\x9cno sufficient policy considerations to warrant\xe2\x80\x9d its refusal to apply the other State\xe2\x80\x99s laws. Ibid. For example, when a State \xe2\x80\x9cseeks to exclude from its courts actions arising under a foreign statute\xe2\x80\x9d but permits similar actions under its own laws, the State has adopted a policy of hostility to the \xe2\x80\x9cpublic Acts\xe2\x80\x9d of another State. Ibid.; see Hughes v. Fetter,\n\n341 U.\xc2\xa0S. 609\n\xe2\x80\x93613 (1951). In such cases, this Court has held that the forum State must open its doors and permit the plaintiff to seek relief under another State\xe2\x80\x99s laws. See, e.g., id., at 611 (\xe2\x80\x9cWisconsin cannot escape [its] con-stitutional obligation to enforce the rights and duties validly created under the laws of other states by the simple device of removing jurisdiction from courts otherwise competent\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

B

\n

According to the Court, the Nevada Supreme Court violated the Full Faith and Credit Clause by applying \xe2\x80\x9ca special rule of law that evinces a policy of hostility toward California.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 4 (internal quotation marks omitted). As long as Nevada provides immunity to its state agencies for awards above $50,000, the majority reasons, the State has no legitimate policy rationale for refusing to give similar immunity to the agencies of other States. The Court concludes that the Nevada Supreme Court is accordingly required to rewrite Nevada law to afford the Board the same immunity to which Nevada agencies are entitled. In the majority\xe2\x80\x99s view, that result is \xe2\x80\x9cstrongly\xe2\x80\x9d supported by this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents. Ibid. I disagree.

\n

Carroll explains that the Full Faith and Credit Clause prohibits a State from adopting a \xe2\x80\x9cpolicy of hostility to the public Acts\xe2\x80\x9d of another State. 349 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 413. But it does not stop there. Carroll goes on to describe what adopting a \xe2\x80\x9cpolicy of hostility\xe2\x80\x9d means: A State may not refuse to apply another State\xe2\x80\x99s law where there are \xe2\x80\x9cno sufficient policy considerations to warrant such refusal.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added). Where a State chooses a different rule from a sister State in order \xe2\x80\x9cto give affirmative relief for an action arising within its borders,\xe2\x80\x9d the State has a sufficient policy reason for applying its own law, and the Full Faith and Credit Clause is satisfied. Ibid.

\n

In this case, the Nevada Supreme Court applied Nevada rather than California immunity law in order to uphold the \xe2\x80\x9cstate\xe2\x80\x99s policy interest in providing adequate redress to Nevada citizens.\xe2\x80\x9d 130 Nev. ___, ___, 335 P.\xc2\xa03d 125, 147 (2014). This Court has long recognized that \xe2\x80\x9c[f]ew matters could be deemed more appropriately the concern of the state in which the injury occurs or more completely within its power\xe2\x80\x9d than \xe2\x80\x9cthe bodily safety and economic protection\xe2\x80\x9d of people injured within its borders. Pacific Employers Ins. Co., 306 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 503; see Hall, 440 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 424. Hyatt alleges that the Board committed multiple torts, including fraud and intentional infliction of emotional distress. See 130 Nev., at ___, 335 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 130. Under Pacific Employers Insurance and Carroll, there is no doubt that Nevada has a \xe2\x80\x9csufficient\xe2\x80\x9d policy interest in protecting Nevada residents from such injuries.

\n

The majority, however, does not regard that policy interest as sufficient justification for denying the Board immunity. Despite this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to get out of the business of \xe2\x80\x9cappraising and balancing state interests under the Full Faith and Credit Clause,\xe2\x80\x9d Franchise Tax Bd., 538 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 498, the majority concludes that Nevada cannot really have a state policy to protect its citizens from the kinds of torts alleged here, because the State capped its own liability at $50,000 in similar situations. See ante, at 6\xe2\x80\x937. But that fails to credit the Nevada Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s explanation for why a damages cap for Nevada state agencies is fully consistent with the State\xe2\x80\x99s policy of protecting its citizens.

\n

According to the Nevada Supreme Court, Nevada law treats its own agencies differently from the agencies of other States because Nevada agencies are \xe2\x80\x9csubject to legislative control, administrative oversight, and public accountability\xe2\x80\x9d in Nevada. 130 Nev., at ___, 335 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 147 (internal quotation marks omitted). The same is not true of other litigants, such as the Board, who operate \xe2\x80\x9coutside such controls.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted). The majority may think that Nevada is being unfair, but it cannot be said that the State failed to articulate a sufficient policy explanation for its decision to apply a damages cap to Nevada state agencies, but not to the agencies of other States.

\n

As the Court points out, the Constitution certainly has a \xe2\x80\x9cvision of 50 individual and equally dignified States,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 7, which is why California remains free to adopt a policy similar to that of Nevada, should it wish to do so. See Coyle v. Smith,\n\n221 U.\xc2\xa0S. 559,\n567 (1911)\n (The Union \xe2\x80\x9cwas and is a union of States, equal in power, dignity and authority, each competent to exert that residuum of sovereignty not delegated to the United States by the Constitution itself\xe2\x80\x9d). Nevada is not, however, required to treat its sister State as equally committed to the protection of Nevada citizens.

\n

It is true that this Court in the prior iteration of this case found no Full Faith and Credit Clause violation in part because the \xe2\x80\x9cNevada Supreme Court sensitively applied principles of comity with a healthy regard for California\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign status, relying on the contours of Nevada\xe2\x80\x99s own sovereign immunity from suit as a benchmark for its analysis.\xe2\x80\x9d Franchise Tax Bd., 538 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 499. But the Nevada court adhered to its policy of sensitivity to comity concerns this time around as well. In deference to the Board\xe2\x80\x99s sovereignty, the court threw out a $250 million punitive damages award, on top of its previous decision that the Board was not liable at all for its negligent acts. That is more than a \xe2\x80\x9chealthy regard\xe2\x80\x9d for California\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign status.

\n

Even if the Court is correct that Nevada violated the Full Faith and Credit Clause, however, it is wrong about the remedy. The majority concludes that in the sovereign immunity context, the Full Faith and Credit Clause is not a choice of law provision, but a create-your-own-law provision: The Court does not require the Nevada Supreme Court to apply either Nevada law (no immunity for the Board) or California law (complete immunity for the Board), but instead requires a new hybrid rule, under which the Board enjoys partial immunity.

\n

The majority\xe2\x80\x99s approach is nowhere to be found in the Full Faith and Credit Clause. Where the Clause applies, it expressly requires a State to give full faith and credit to another State\xe2\x80\x99s laws. If the majority is correct that Nevada has no sufficient policy justification for applying Nevada immunity law, then California law applies. And under California law, the Board is entitled to full immunity. Or, if Nevada has a sufficient policy reason to apply its own law, then Nevada law applies, and the Board is subject to full liability.

\n

I respectfully dissent.
