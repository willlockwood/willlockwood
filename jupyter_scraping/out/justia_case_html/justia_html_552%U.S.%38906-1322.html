FEDERAL EXPRESS CORP. v. HOLOWECKI et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the second circuit

\n

No. 06\xe2\x80\x931322.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 6, 2007\xe2\x80\x94Decided February 27, 2008

\n

The Age Discrimination in Employment Act of 1967 (ADEA) requires that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o civil action \xe2\x80\xa6 be commenced \xe2\x80\xa6 until 60 days after a charge alleging unlawful discrimination has been filed with the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission\xe2\x80\x9d (EEOC), 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7626(d), but does not define the term \xe2\x80\x9ccharge.\xe2\x80\x9d After petitioner delivery service (FedEx) initiated programs tying its couriers\xe2\x80\x99 compensation and continued employment to certain performance benchmarks, respondent Kennedy (hereinafter respondent), a FedEx courier over age 40, filed with the EEOC, in December 2001, a Form 283 \xe2\x80\x9cIntake Questionnaire\xe2\x80\x9d and a detailed affidavit supporting her contention that the FedEx programs discriminated against older couriers in violation of the ADEA. In April 2002, respondent and others filed this ADEA suit claiming, inter alia, that the programs were veiled attempts to force out, harass, and discriminate against older couriers. FedEx moved to dismiss respondent\xe2\x80\x99s action, contending she had not filed the \xe2\x80\x9ccharge\xe2\x80\x9d required by \xc2\xa7626(d). Respondent countered that her Form 283 and affidavit constituted a valid charge, but the District Court disagreed and granted FedEx\xe2\x80\x99s motion. The Second Circuit reversed.

\n

Held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0In addition to the information required by the implementing regulations, i.e., an allegation of age discrimination and the name of the charged party, if a filing is to be deemed a \xe2\x80\x9ccharge\xe2\x80\x9d under the ADEA it must be reasonably construed as a request for the agency to take remedial action to protect the employee\xe2\x80\x99s rights or otherwise settle a dispute between the employer and the employee. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0There is little dispute that the EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s regulations\xe2\x80\x94so far as they go\xe2\x80\x94are reasonable constructions of the statutory term \xe2\x80\x9ccharge\xe2\x80\x9d and are therefore entitled to deference under Chevron U. S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., 467 U. S. 837, 843\xe2\x80\x93845. However, while the regulations give some content to the term charge, they fall short of a comprehensive definition. Thus, the issue is the guidance the regulations give. Title 29 CFR \xc2\xa71626.3 says: \xe2\x80\x9ccharge shall mean a statement filed with the [EEOC] which alleges that the named prospective defendant has engaged in or is about to engage in acts in violation of the Act.\xe2\x80\x9d Section 1626.8(a) identifies information a \xe2\x80\x9ccharge should contain,\xe2\x80\x9d including: the employee\xe2\x80\x99s and employer\xe2\x80\x99s names, addresses, and phone numbers; an allegation that the employee was the victim of age discrimination; the number of employees of the charged employer; and a statement indicating whether the charging party has initiated state proceedings. Section 1626.8(b), however, seems to qualify these requirements by stating that a charge is \xe2\x80\x9csufficient\xe2\x80\x9d if it meets the requirements of \xc2\xa71626.6\xe2\x80\x94i.e., if it is \xe2\x80\x9cin writing and \xe2\x80\xa6 name[s] the prospective respondent and \xe2\x80\xa6 generally allege[s] the discriminatory act(s).\xe2\x80\x9d That the meaning of charge remains unclear, even with the regulations, is evidenced by the differing positions of the parties and the Courts of Appeals on the matter. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Just as this Court defers to reasonable statutory interpretations, an agency is entitled to deference when it adopts a reasonable interpretation of its regulations, unless its position is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0plainly erroneous or inconsistent with the regulation,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Auer v. Robbins, 519 U. S. 452, 461. The Court accords such deference to the EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s position that its regulations identify certain requirements for a charge but do not provide an exhaustive definition. It follows that a document meeting \xc2\xa71626.6\xe2\x80\x99s requirements is not a charge in every instance. The language in \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71626.6 and 1626.8 cannot be viewed in isolation from the rest of the regulations. While the regulations\xe2\x80\x99 structure is less than clear, the relevant provisions are grouped under the title, \xe2\x80\x9cProcedures\xe2\x80\x94Age Discrimination in Employment Act.\xe2\x80\x9d A permissible reading is that the regulations identify the procedures for filing a charge but do not state the full contents of a charge. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0That does not resolve this case because the regulations do not state what additional elements are required in a charge. The EEOC submits, in accordance with a position it has adopted in internal directives over the years, that the proper test is whether a filing, taken as a whole, should be construed as a request by the employee for the EEOC to take whatever action is necessary to vindicate her rights. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(d)\xc2\xa0The EEOC acted within its authority in formulating its request-to-act requirement. The agency\xe2\x80\x99s policy statements, embodied in its compliance manual and internal directives, interpret not only its regulations but also the statute itself. Assuming these interpretive statements are not entitled to full Chevron deference, they nevertheless are entitled to a \xe2\x80\x9cmeasure of respect\xe2\x80\x9d under the less deferential standard of Skidmore v. Swift & Co., 323 U. S. 134, see Alaska Dept. of Environmental Conservation v. EPA, 540 U. S. 461, 487, whereby the Court considers whether the agency has consistently applied its position, e.g., United States v. Mead Corp., 533 U. S. 218, 228. Here, the relevant interpretive statement has been binding on EEOC staff for at least five years. True, the agency\xe2\x80\x99s implementation has been uneven; e.g., its field office did not treat respondent\xe2\x80\x99s filing as a charge, and, as a result, she filed suit before the EEOC could initiate conciliation with FedEx. Such undoubted deficiencies are not enough, however, to deprive an agency that processes over 175,000 inquiries a year of all judicial deference. Moreover, the charge must be defined in a way that allows the agency to fulfill its distinct statutory functions of enforcing antidiscrimination laws, see 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7626(d), and disseminating information about those laws to the public, see, e.g., Civil Rights Act of 1964, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7705(i), 705(g)(3). Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(e)\xc2\xa0FedEx\xe2\x80\x99s view that because the EEOC must act \xe2\x80\x9c[u]pon receiving \xe2\x80\xa6 a charge,\xe2\x80\x9d 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7626(d), its failure to do so means the filing is not a charge, is rejected as too artificial a reading of the ADEA. The statute requires the aggrieved individual to file a charge before filing a lawsuit; it does not condition the individual\xe2\x80\x99s right to sue upon the agency taking any action. Cf. Edelman v. Lynchburg College, 535 U. S. 106, 112\xe2\x80\x93113. Moreover, because the filing of a charge determines when the ADEA\xe2\x80\x99s time limits and procedural mechanisms commence, it would be illogical and impractical to make the definition of charge dependent upon a condition subsequent over which the parties have no control. Cf. Logan v. Zimmerman Brush Co., 455 U. S. 422, 444. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0The agency\xe2\x80\x99s determination that respondent\xe2\x80\x99s December 2001 filing was a charge is a reasonable exercise of its authority to apply its own regulations and procedures in the course of the routine administration of the statute it enforces. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s completed Form 283 contained all the information outlined in 29 CFR \xc2\xa71626.8, and, although the form did not itself request agency action, the accompanying affidavit asked the EEOC to \xe2\x80\x9cforce [FedEx] to end [its] age discrimination plan.\xe2\x80\x9d FedEx contends unpersuasively that, in context, the latter statement is ambiguous because the affidavit also stated: \xe2\x80\x9cI have been \xe2\x80\xa6 assur[ed] by [the EEOC] that this Affidavit will be considered confidential \xe2\x80\xa6 and will not be disclosed \xe2\x80\xa6 unless it becomes necessary \xe2\x80\xa6 to produce the affidavit in a formal proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d This argument reads too much into the nondisclosure assurances. Respondent did not request the EEOC to avoid contacting FedEx, but stated only her understanding that the affidavit itself would be kept confidential and, even then, consented to disclosure of the affidavit in a \xe2\x80\x9cformal proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d Furthermore, respondent checked a box on the Form 283 giving consent for the EEOC to disclose her identity to FedEx. The fact that respondent filed a formal charge with the EEOC after she filed her District Court complaint is irrelevant because postfiling conduct does not nullify an earlier, proper charge. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Because the EEOC failed to treat respondent\xe2\x80\x99s filing as a charge in the first instance, both sides lost the benefits of the ADEA\xe2\x80\x99s informal dispute resolution process. The court that hears the merits can attempt to remedy this deficiency by staying the proceedings to allow an opportunity for conciliation and settlement. While that remedy is imperfect, it is unavoidable in this case. However, the ultimate responsibility for establishing a clearer, more consistent process lies with the EEOC, which should determine, in the first instance, what revisions to its forms and processes are necessary or appropriate to reduce the risk of future misunderstandings by those who seek its assistance. Pp.\xc2\xa016\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

440 F.\xc2\xa03d 558, affirmed.

FEDERAL EXPRESS CORPORATION, PETITIONER v.\nPAUL HOLOWECKI et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the second circuit

\n

[February 27, 2008]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Kennedy delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case arises under the Age Discrimination in Employment Act of 1967 (ADEA or Act), 81 Stat. 602, as amended, 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7621 et seq. When an employee files \xe2\x80\x9ca charge alleging unlawful [age] discrimination\xe2\x80\x9d with the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC), the charge sets the Act\xe2\x80\x99s enforcement mechanisms in motion, commencing a waiting period during which the employee cannot file suit. The phrase, \xe2\x80\x9ca charge alleging unlawful discrimination,\xe2\x80\x9d is used in the statute, \xc2\xa7626(d), and \xe2\x80\x9ccharge\xe2\x80\x9d appears in the agency\xe2\x80\x99s implementing regulations; but it has no statutory definition. In deciding what constitutes a charge under the Act the Courts of Appeals have adopted different definitions. As a result, difficulties have arisen in determining when employees may seek relief under the ADEA in courts of competent jurisdiction.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As a cautionary preface, we note that the EEOC enforcement mechanisms and statutory waiting periods for ADEA claims differ in some respects from those pertaining to other statutes the EEOC enforces, such as Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, 78 Stat. 253, as amended, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000e et seq., and the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990, 104 Stat. 327, as amended, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa712101 et seq. While there may be areas of common definition, employees and their counsel must be careful not to apply rules applicable under one statute to a different statute without careful and critical examination. Cf. General Dynamics Land Systems, Inc. v. Cline, 540 U. S. 581, 586\xe2\x80\x93587 (2004). This is so even if the EEOC forms and the same definition of charge apply in more than one type of discrimination case.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner, Federal Express Corporation (FedEx), provides mail pickup and delivery services to customers worldwide. In 1994 and 1995, FedEx initiated two programs, designed, it says, to make its 45,000-strong courier network more productive. The programs, \xe2\x80\x9cBest Practice Pays\xe2\x80\x9d (BPP) and \xe2\x80\x9cMinimum Acceptable Performance Standards\xe2\x80\x9d (MAPS), tied the couriers\xe2\x80\x99 compensation and continued employment to certain performance benchmarks, for instance the number of stops a courier makes per day.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents are 14 current and former FedEx couriers over the age of 40. They filed suit in the United States District Court for the Southern District of New York on April 30, 2002, claiming, inter alia, that BPP and MAPS violate the ADEA. Asserting that their claims were typical of many couriers nationwide, respondents sought to represent a plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 class of all couriers over the age of 40 who were subject to alleged acts of age discrimination by FedEx. The suit maintains that BPP and MAPS were veiled attempts to force older workers out of the company before they would be entitled to receive retirement benefits. FedEx, it is alleged, used the initiatives as a pretext for harassing and discriminating against older couriers in favor of younger ones.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The immediate question before us is the timeliness of the suit filed by one of the plaintiffs below, Patricia Kennedy, referred to here as \xe2\x80\x9crespondent.\xe2\x80\x9d Petitioner moved to dismiss respondent\xe2\x80\x99s action, contending respondent had not filed her charge with the EEOC at least 60 days before filing suit, as required by 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7626(d). Respondent countered that she filed a valid charge on December 11, 2001, by submitting EEOC Form 283.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The agency labels Form 283 an \xe2\x80\x9cIntake Questionnaire.\xe2\x80\x9d Respondent attached to the questionnaire a signed affidavit describing the alleged discriminatory employment practices in greater detail. The District Court determined these documents were not a charge and granted the motion to dismiss. No. 02 Civ. 3355(LMM) (SDNY, Oct. 9, 2002), App. to Pet. for Cert. 39a. An appeal followed, and the Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit reversed. See 440 F.\xc2\xa03d 558, 570 (2006). We granted certiorari to consider whether respondent\xe2\x80\x99s filing was a charge, 551 U. S. ___ (2007), and we now affirm.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case presents two distinct questions: What is a charge as the ADEA uses that term? And were the documents respondent filed in December 2001 a charge?

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The relevant statutory provision states:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cNo civil action may be commenced by an individual under [the ADEA] until 60 days after a charge alleging unlawful discrimination has been filed with the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission. \xe2\x80\xa6

\n

.\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83.\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83.\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83.\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83.

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9cUpon receiving such a charge, the Commission shall promptly notify all persons named in such charge as prospective defendants in the action and shall promptly seek to eliminate any alleged unlawful practice by informal methods of conciliation, conference, and persuasion.\xe2\x80\x9d 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7626(d).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Act does not define charge. While EEOC regulations give some content to the term, they fall short of a comprehensive definition. The agency has statutory authority to issue regulations, see \xc2\xa7628; and when an agency invokes its authority to issue regulations, which then interpret ambiguous statutory terms, the courts defer to its reasonable interpretations. See Chevron U. S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., 467 U. S. 837, 843\xe2\x80\x93845 (1984). The regulations the agency has adopted\xe2\x80\x94so far as they go\xe2\x80\x94are reasonable constructions of the term charge. There is little dispute about this. The issue is the guidance the regulations give.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0One of the regulations, 29 CFR \xc2\xa71626.3 (2007), is entitled \xe2\x80\x9cOther definitions.\xe2\x80\x9d It says: \xe2\x80\x9ccharge shall mean a statement filed with the Commission by or on behalf of an aggrieved person which alleges that the named prospective defendant has engaged in or is about to engage in actions in violation of the Act.\xe2\x80\x9d Section 1626.8(a) identifies five pieces of information a \xe2\x80\x9ccharge should contain\xe2\x80\x9d: (1)\xe2\x80\x93(2) the names, addresses, and telephone numbers of the person making the charge and the charged entity; (3) a statement of facts describing the alleged discriminatory act; (4) the number of employees of the charged employer; and (5) a statement indicating whether the charging party has initiated state proceedings. The next subsection, \xc2\xa71626.8(b), however, seems to qualify these requirements by stating that a charge is \xe2\x80\x9csufficient\xe2\x80\x9d if it meets the requirements of \xc2\xa71626.6\xe2\x80\x94i.e., if it is \xe2\x80\x9cin writing and \xe2\x80\xa6 name[s] the prospective respondent and \xe2\x80\xa6 generally allege[s] the discriminatory act(s).\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even with the aid of the regulations the meaning of charge remains unclear, as is evident from the differing positions of the parties now before us and in the Courts of Appeals. Petitioner contends an Intake Questionnaire cannot be a charge unless the EEOC acts upon it. On the other hand some Courts of Appeals, including the Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit, take a position similar to the Government\xe2\x80\x99s in this case, that an Intake Questionnaire can constitute a charge if it expresses the filer\xe2\x80\x99s intent to activate the EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s enforcement processes. See, e.g., Steffen v. Meridian Life Ins. Co., 859 F.\xc2\xa02d 534, 542 (CA7 1988). A third view, which seems to accord with respondent\xe2\x80\x99s position, is that all completed Intake Questionnaires are charges. See, e.g., Casavantes v. California State Univ., Sacramento, 732 F.\xc2\xa02d 1441, 1443 (CA9 1984).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In support of her position that the Intake Questionnaire she filed, taken together with the attached six-page affidavit, meets the regulatory definition of a charge, respondent places considerable emphasis on what might be described as the regulations\xe2\x80\x99 catchall or savings provision, 29 CFR \xc2\xa71626.8(b). This seems to require only a written document with a general allegation of discriminatory conduct by a named employer. Respondent points out that, when read together, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71626.8(b) and 1626.6 say that a \xe2\x80\x9ccharge is sufficient when the Commission receives \xe2\x80\xa6\na written statement\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cname[s] the [employer] and\n\xe2\x80\xa6 generally allege[s] the discriminatory act(s).\xe2\x80\x9d Re-\nspondent views this language as unequivocal and sees no basis for requiring that a charge contain any additional information.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s view, as expressed in the Government\xe2\x80\x99s amicus brief, however, is that the regulations identify certain requirements for a charge but do not provide an exhaustive definition. As such, not all documents that meet the minimal requirements of \xc2\xa71626.6 are charges.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The question, then, becomes how to interpret the scope of the regulations. Just as we defer to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s reasonable interpretations of the statute when it issues regulations in the first instance, see Chevron, supra, the agency is entitled to further deference when it adopts a reasonable interpretation of regulations it has put in force. See Auer v. Robbins, 519 U. S. 452 (1997). Under Auer, we accept the agency\xe2\x80\x99s position unless it is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0plainly erroneous or inconsistent with the regulation.\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 461 (quoting Robertson v. Methow Valley Citizens Council, 490 U. S. 332, 359 (1989)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In accord with this standard we accept the agency\xe2\x80\x99s position that the regulations do not identify all necessary components of a charge; and it follows that a document meeting the requirements of \xc2\xa71626.6 is not a charge in every instance. The language in \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71626.6 and 1626.8 cannot be viewed in isolation from the rest of the regulations. True, the structure of the regulations is less than clear. But the relevant provisions are grouped under the title, \xe2\x80\x9cProcedures\xe2\x80\x94Age Discrimination in Employment Act.\xe2\x80\x9d A permissible reading is that the regulations identify the procedures for filing a charge but do not state the full contents a charge document must contain. This is the agency\xe2\x80\x99s position, and we defer to it under Auer.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This does not resolve the case. While we agree with the Government that the regulations do not state all the elements a charge must contain, the question of what additional elements are required remains. On this point the regulations are silent.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The EEOC submits that the proper test for determining whether a filing is a charge is whether the filing, taken as a whole, should be construed as a request by the employee for the agency to take whatever action is necessary to vindicate her rights. Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 15. The EEOC has adopted this position in the Government\xe2\x80\x99s amicus brief and in various internal directives it has issued to its field offices over the years. See 1 EEOC Compliance Manual \xc2\xa72.2(b), p. 2:0001 (Aug. 2002); Memorandum from Elizabeth M. Thornton, Director, Office of Field Programs, EEOC, to All District, Area, and Local Office Directors et\xc2\xa0al. (Feb. 21, 2002), on line at\nhttp://www.eeoc.gov/charge/memo-2-21-02.html (hereinafter Thornton Memo) (all Internet materials as visited\nFeb. 21, 2008, and available in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case\nfile); Memorandum from Nicholas M. Inzeo, Director, Office of Field Programs, EEOC, to All District, Field, Area, and Local Office Directors et al. (Aug. 13, 2007),\non line at http://www.eeoc.gov/charge/memo-8-13-07.html. The Government asserts that this request-to-act requirement is a reasonable extrapolation of the agency\xe2\x80\x99s regulations and that, as a result, the agency\xe2\x80\x99s position is dispositive under Auer.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government acknowledges the regulations do not, on their face, speak to the filer\xe2\x80\x99s intent. To the extent the request-to-act requirement can be derived from the text of the regulations, it must spring from the term charge. But, in this context, the term charge is not a construct of the agency\xe2\x80\x99s regulations. It is a term Congress used in the underlying statute that has been incorporated in the regulations by the agency. Thus, insofar as they speak to the filer\xe2\x80\x99s intent, the regulations do so by repeating language from the underlying statute. It could be argued, then, that this case can be distinguished from Auer. See Gonzales v. Oregon, 546 U. S. 243, 257 (2006) (the \xe2\x80\x9cnear equivalence of the statute and regulation belies [the case for] Auer deference\xe2\x80\x9d); Christensen v. Harris County, 529 U. S. 576, 588 (2000) (an agency cannot \xe2\x80\x9cunder the guise of interpreting a regulation \xe2\x80\xa6 create de facto a new\nregulation\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is not necessary to hold that Auer deference applies to the agency\xe2\x80\x99s construction of the term charge as it is used in the regulations, however. For even if Auer deference is inapplicable, we would accept the agency\xe2\x80\x99s proposed construction of the statutory term, and we turn next to the reasons for this conclusion.

\n

D

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In our view the agency\xe2\x80\x99s policy statements, embodied in its compliance manual and internal directives, interpret not only the regulations but also the statute itself. Assuming these interpretive statements are not entitled to full Chevron deference, they do reflect \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a body of experience and informed judgment to which courts and litigants may properly resort for guidance.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Bragdon v. Abbott, 524 U. S. 624, 642 (1998) (quoting Skidmore v. Swift & Co., 323 U. S. 134 (1944)). As such, they are entitled to a \xe2\x80\x9cmeasure of respect\xe2\x80\x9d under the less deferential Skidmore standard. Alaska Dept. of Environmental Conservation v. EPA, 540 U. S. 461, 487, 488 (2004); United States v. Mead Corp., 533 U. S. 218, 227\xe2\x80\x93239 (2001).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under Skidmore, we consider whether the agency has applied its position with consistency. Mead Corp., supra, at 228; Good Samaritan Hospital v. Shalala, 508 U. S. 402, 417 (1993). Here, the relevant interpretive statement, embodied in the compliance manual and memoranda, has been binding on EEOC staff for at least five years. See Thornton Memo, supra. True, as the Government concedes, the agency\xe2\x80\x99s implementation of this policy has been uneven. See Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 25. In the very case before us the EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s Tampa field office did not treat respondent\xe2\x80\x99s filing as a charge, as the Government now maintains it should have done. And, as a result, respondent filed suit before the agency could initiate a conciliation process with the employer.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These undoubted deficiencies in the agency\xe2\x80\x99s administration of the statute and its regulatory scheme are not enough, however, to deprive the agency of all judicial deference. Some degree of inconsistent treatment is unavoidable when the agency processes over 175,000 inquiries a year. Id., at 19, n.\xc2\xa010. And although one of the policy memoranda the Government relies upon was circulated after we granted certiorari, the position the document takes is consistent with the EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s previous directives. We see no reason to assume the agency\xe2\x80\x99s position\xe2\x80\x94that a charge is filed when the employee requests some action\xe2\x80\x94was framed for the specific purpose of aiding a party in this litigation. Cf. Bowen v. Georgetown Univ. Hospital, 488 U. S. 204, 212\xe2\x80\x93213 (1988).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The EEOC, moreover, has drawn our attention to the need to define charge in a way that allows the agency to fulfill its distinct statutory functions of enforcing antidiscrimination laws and disseminating information about those laws to the public. Cf. Barnhart v. Walton, 535 U. S. 212, 225 (2002) (noting that deference is appropriate in \xe2\x80\x9cmatters of detail related to [an agency\xe2\x80\x99s] administration\xe2\x80\x9d of a statute). The agency\xe2\x80\x99s duty to initiate informal dispute resolution processes upon receipt of a charge is mandatory in the ADEA context. See 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7626(d) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he Commission \xe2\x80\xa6 shall promptly seek to eliminate any alleged unlawful practice by informal methods of conciliation, conference, and persuasion\xe2\x80\x9d); Cf. Lopez v. Davis, 531 U. S. 230, 241 (2001) (noting that Congress\xe2\x80\x99 use of the term \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98shall\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d indicates an intent to \xe2\x80\x9cimpose discretionless obligations\xe2\x80\x9d). Yet, at the same time, Congress intended the agency to serve an \xe2\x80\x9ceducational\xe2\x80\x9d function. See Civil Rights Act of 1964, \xc2\xa7705(i), 78 Stat. 259; id., \xc2\xa7705(g)(3) (noting that the Commission shall have the power to \xe2\x80\x9cfurnish to persons subject to this title such technical assistance as they may request\xe2\x80\x9d). Providing answers to the public\xe2\x80\x99s questions is a critical part of the EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s mission; and it accounts for a substantial part of the agency\xe2\x80\x99s work. Of about 175,000 inquiries the agency receives each year, it dockets around 76,000 of these as charges. Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 19, n.\xc2\xa010. Even allowing for errors in the classification of charges and noncharges, it is evident that many filings come from individuals who have questions about their rights and simply want information.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For efficient operations, and to effect congressional intent, the agency requires some mechanism to separate information requests from enforcement requests. Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s proposed standard, that a charge need contain only an allegation of discrimination and the name of the employer, falls short in this regard. Were that stripped-down standard to prevail, individuals who approach the agency with questions could end up divulging enough information to create a charge. This likely would be the case for anyone who completes an Intake Questionnaire\xe2\x80\x94which provides space to indicate the name and address of the offending employer and asks the individual to answer the question, \xe2\x80\x9cWhat action was taken against you that you believe to be discrimination?\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 43a. If an individual knows that reporting this minimal information to the agency will mandate the agency to notify her employer, she may be discouraged from consulting the agency or wait until her employment situation has become so untenable that conciliation efforts would be futile. The result would be contrary to Congress\xe2\x80\x99 expressed desire that the EEOC act as an information provider and try to settle employment disputes through informal means.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For these reasons, the definition of charge respondent advocates\xe2\x80\x94i.e., that it need conform only to 29 CFR \xc2\xa71626.6\xe2\x80\x94is in considerable tension with the structure and purposes of the ADEA. The agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretive position\xe2\x80\x94the request-to-act requirement\xe2\x80\x94provides a reasonable alternative that is consistent with the statutory framework. No clearer alternatives are within our authority or expertise to adopt; and so deference to the agency is appropriate under Skidmore. We conclude as follows: In addition to the information required by the regulations, i.e., an allegation and the name of the charged party, if a filing is to be deemed a charge it must be reasonably construed as a request for the agency to take remedial action to protect the employee\xe2\x80\x99s rights or otherwise settle a dispute between the employer and the employee.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Some Courts of Appeals have referred to a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98manifest intent\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d test, under which, in order to be deemed a charge, the filing must demonstrate \xe2\x80\x9can individual\xe2\x80\x99s intent to have the agency initiate its investigatory and conciliatory processes.\xe2\x80\x9d 440 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 566 (case below); see also Wilkerson v. Grinnell Corp., 270 F.\xc2\xa03d 1314, 1319 (CA11 2001); Steffen, 859 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 543; Bihler v. Singer Co., 710 F.\xc2\xa02d 96, 99 (CA3 1983). If this formulation suggests the filer\xe2\x80\x99s state of mind is somehow determinative, it misses the point. If, however, it means the filing must be examined from the standpoint of an objective observer to determine whether, by a reasonable construction of its terms, the filer requests the agency to activate its machinery and remedial processes, that would be in accord with our conclusion.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is true that under this permissive standard a wide range of documents might be classified as charges. But this result is consistent with the design and purpose of the ADEA. Even in the formal litigation context, pro se litigants are held to a lesser pleading standard than other parties. See Estelle v. Gamble, 429 U. S. 97, 106 (1976) (Pro se pleadings are to be \xe2\x80\x9cliberally construed\xe2\x80\x9d). In the administrative context now before us it appears pro se filings may be the rule, not the exception. The ADEA, like Title VII, sets up a \xe2\x80\x9cremedial scheme in which laypersons, rather than lawyers, are expected to initiate the process.\xe2\x80\x9d EEOC v. Commercial Office Products Co., 486 U. S. 107, 124 (1988); see also Oscar Mayer & Co. v. Evans, 441 U. S. 750, 756 (1979) (noting the \xe2\x80\x9ccommon purpose\xe2\x80\x9d of Title VII and the ADEA). The system must be accessible to individuals who have no detailed knowledge of the relevant statutory mechanisms and agency processes. It thus is consistent with the purposes of the Act that a charge can be a form, easy to complete, or an informal document, easy to draft. The agency\xe2\x80\x99s proposed test implements these purposes.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Reasonable arguments can be made that the agency should adopt a standard giving more guidance to filers, making it clear that the request to act must be stated in quite explicit terms. A rule of that sort might yield more consistent results. This, however, is a matter for the agency to decide in light of its experience and expertise in protecting the rights of those who are covered by the Act. For its decisions in this regard the agency is subject to the oversight of the political branches. Cf. National Cable & Telecommunications Assn. v. Brand X Internet Services, 545 U. S. 967, 980 (2005) (\xe2\x80\x9cFilling these gaps [in ambiguous statutes] involves difficult policy choices that agencies are better equipped to make than courts\xe2\x80\x9d). We find no reason in this case to depart from our usual rule: Where ambiguities in statutory analysis and application are presented, the agency may choose among reasonable alternatives.

\n

E

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Asserting its interest as an employer, petitioner urges us to condition the definition of charge, and hence an employee\xe2\x80\x99s ability to sue, upon the EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s fulfilling its mandatory duty to notify the charged party and initiate a conciliation process. In petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s view, because the Commission must act \xe2\x80\x9c[u]pon receiving such a charge,\xe2\x80\x9d 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7626(d), its failure to do so means the filing is not a charge.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The agency rejects this view, as do we. As a textual matter, the proposal is too artificial a reading of the statute to accept. The statute requires the aggrieved individual to file a charge before filing a lawsuit; it does not condition the individual\xe2\x80\x99s right to sue upon the agency taking any action. Ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9cNo civil action may be commenced by an individual under [the ADEA] until 60 days after a charge alleging unlawful discrimination has been filed with the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission\xe2\x80\x9d); Cf. Edelman v. Lynchburg College, 535 U. S. 106, 112\xe2\x80\x93113 (2002) (rejecting the argument that a charge is not a charge until the filer satisfies Title VII\xe2\x80\x99s oath or affirmation requirement). The filing of a charge, moreover, determines when the Act\xe2\x80\x99s time limits and procedural mechanisms commence. It would be illogical and impractical to make the definition of charge dependent upon a condition subsequent over which the parties have no control. Cf. Logan v. Zimmerman Brush Co., 455 U. S. 422, 444 (1982) (Powell, J., concurring in judgment).

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Having determined that the agency acted within its authority in formulating the rule that a filing is deemed a charge if the document reasonably can be construed to request agency action and appropriate relief on the employee\xe2\x80\x99s behalf, the question is whether the filing here meets this test. The agency says it does, and we agree. The agency\xe2\x80\x99s determination is a reasonable exercise of its authority to apply its own regulations and procedures in the course of the routine administration of the statute it enforces.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s completed intake form contained all of the information outlined in 29 CFR \xc2\xa71626.8, including: the employee\xe2\x80\x99s name, address, and telephone number, as well as those of her employer; an allegation that she and other employees had been the victims of \xe2\x80\x9cage discrimination\xe2\x80\x9d; the number of employees who worked at the Dunedin, Florida, facility where she was stationed; and a statement indicating she had not sought the assistance of any government agency regarding this matter. See App. 265.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner maintains the filing was still deficient because it contained no request for the agency to act. Were the Intake Questionnaire the only document before us we might agree its handwritten statements do not request action. The design of the form in use in 2001, moreover, does not give rise to the inference that the employee requests action against the employer. Unlike EEOC Form 5, the Intake Questionnaire is not labeled a \xe2\x80\x9cCharge of Discrimination,\xe2\x80\x9d see id., at 275. In fact the wording of the questionnaire suggests the opposite: that the form\xe2\x80\x99s purpose is to facilitate \xe2\x80\x9cpre-charge filing counseling\xe2\x80\x9d and to enable the agency to determine whether it has jurisdiction over \xe2\x80\x9cpotential charges.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 265. There might be instances where the indicated discrimination is so clear or pervasive that the agency could infer from the allegations themselves that action is requested and required, but the agency is not required to treat every completed Intake Questionnaire as a charge.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this case, however, the completed questionnaire filed in December 2001 was supplemented with a detailed six-page affidavit. At the end of the last page, respondent asked the agency to \xe2\x80\x9c[p]lease force Federal Express to end their age discrimination plan so we can finish out our careers absent the unfairness and hostile work environment created within their application of Best Practice/High-Velocity Culture Change.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 273. This is properly construed as a request for the agency to act.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner says that, in context, the statement is ambiguous. It points to respondent\xe2\x80\x99s accompanying statement that \xe2\x80\x9cI have been given assurances by an Agent of the U. S. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission that this Affidavit will be considered confidential by the United States Government and will not be disclosed as long as the case remains open unless it becomes necessary for the Government to produce the affidavit in a formal proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 266. Petitioner argues that if respondent intended the affidavit to be kept confidential, she could not have expected the agency to treat it as a charge. This reads too much into the assurance of nondisclosure. Respondent did not request the agency to avoid contacting her employer. She stated only her understanding that the affidavit itself would be kept confidential. Even then, she gave consent for the agency to disclose the affidavit in a \xe2\x80\x9cformal proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d Furthermore, respondent checked a box on the Intake Questionnaire giving consent for the agency to disclose her identity to the employer. Id., at 265. Here the combination of the waiver and respondent\xe2\x80\x99s request in the affidavit that the agency \xe2\x80\x9cforce\xe2\x80\x9d the employer to stop discriminating against her were enough to bring the entire filing within the definition of charge we adopt here.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner notes that respondent did file a Form 5 (a formal charge) with the EEOC but only after she filed her complaint in the District Court. This shows, petitioner argues, that respondent did not intend the earlier December 2001 filing to be a charge; otherwise, there would have been no reason for the later filing. What matters, however, is whether the documents filed in December 2001 should be interpreted as a request for the agency to act. Postfiling conduct does not nullify an earlier, proper charge.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Documents filed by an employee with the EEOC should be construed, to the extent consistent with permissible rules of interpretation, to protect the employee\xe2\x80\x99s rights and statutory remedies. Construing ambiguities against the drafter may be the more efficient rule to encourage precise expression in other contexts; here, however, the rule would undermine the remedial scheme Congress adopted. It would encourage individuals to avoid filing errors by retaining counsel, increasing both the cost and likelihood of litigation.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Federal Government interacts with individual citizens through all but countless forms, schedules, manuals, and worksheets. Congress, in most cases, delegates the format and design of these instruments to the agencies that administer the relevant laws and processes. An assumption underlying the congressional decision to delegate rulemaking and enforcement authority to the agency, and the consequent judicial rule of deference to the agency\xe2\x80\x99s determinations, is that the agency will take all efforts to ensure that affected parties will receive the full benefits and protections of the law. Here, because the agency failed to treat respondent\xe2\x80\x99s filing as a charge in the first instance, both sides lost the benefits of the ADEA\xe2\x80\x99s informal dispute resolution process.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The employer\xe2\x80\x99s interests, in particular, were given short shrift, for it was not notified of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s complaint until she filed suit. The court that hears the merits of this litigation can attempt to remedy this deficiency by staying the proceedings to allow an opportunity for conciliation and settlement. True, that remedy would be imperfect. Once the adversary process has begun a dispute may be in a more rigid cast than if conciliation had been attempted at the outset.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This result is unfortunate, but, at least in this case, unavoidable. While courts will use their powers to fashion the best relief possible in situations like this one, the ultimate responsibility for establishing a clearer, more consistent process lies with the agency. The agency already has made some changes to the charge-filing process. See Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 3, n.\xc2\xa02 (noting that the Intake Questionnaire form respondent filed has been replaced with a reworded form). To reduce the risk of further misunderstandings by those who seek its assistance, the agency should determine, in the first instance, what additional revisions in its forms and processes are necessary or appropriate.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment of the Court of Appeals is affirmed.

\n

\nIt is so ordered.

\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 06-1322
\n
\n

FEDERAL EXPRESS CORPORATION, PETITIONER v.\nPAUL HOLOWECKI et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the second circuit

\n

[February 27, 2008]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Thomas, with whom Justice Scalia joins, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Today the Court decides that a \xe2\x80\x9ccharge\xe2\x80\x9d of age discrimination under the Age Discrimination in Employment Act of 1967 (ADEA) is whatever the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) says it is. The filing at issue in this case did not state that it was a charge and did not include a charge form; to the contrary, it included a form that expressly stated it was for the purpose of \xe2\x80\x9cpre-charge\xe2\x80\x9d counseling. What is more, the EEOC did not assign it a charge number, notify the employer of the complainant\xe2\x80\x99s[Footnote 1] allegations, or commence enforcement proceedings. Notwithstanding these facts, the Court concludes, counterintuitively, that respondent\xe2\x80\x99s filing is a charge because it manifests an intent for the EEOC to take \xe2\x80\x9csome action.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9. Because the standard the Court applies is broader than the ordinary meaning of the term \xe2\x80\x9ccharge,\xe2\x80\x9d and because it is so malleable that it effectively absolves the EEOC of its obligation to administer the ADEA according to discernible standards, I respectfully dissent.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the Court notes, the ADEA directs the agency to take certain actions upon receipt of a \xe2\x80\x9ccharge\xe2\x80\x9d but does not define that word. Ante, at 3\xe2\x80\x934. Because there is nothing to suggest that Congress used \xe2\x80\x9ccharge\xe2\x80\x9d as a term of art, we must construe it \xe2\x80\x9cin accordance with its ordinary or natural meaning.\xe2\x80\x9d See FDIC v. Meyer, 510 U. S. 471, 476 (1994). Dictionaries define a \xe2\x80\x9ccharge\xe2\x80\x9d as an accusation or indictment. See, e.g., American Heritage Dictionary 312 (4th ed. 2000); Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third New International Dictionary 377 (1993). In legal parlance, a \xe2\x80\x9ccharge\xe2\x80\x9d is generally a formal allegation of wrongdoing that initiates legal proceedings against an alleged wrongdoer. In criminal law, for example, a charge is defined as \xe2\x80\x9c[a] formal accusation of an offense as a preliminary step to prosecution.\xe2\x80\x9d Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 248 (8th ed. 2004). Similarly, in this context, a \xe2\x80\x9ccharge\xe2\x80\x9d is a formal accusation of discrimination that objectively manifests an intent to initiate enforcement proceedings against the employer. Just as a complaint or police report that describes the commission of a crime is not a \xe2\x80\x9ccharge\xe2\x80\x9d under the criminal law, so too here, a document that merely describes the alleged discrimination and requests the EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s assistance, but does not objectively manifest an intent to initiate enforcement proceedings, is not a \xe2\x80\x9ccharge\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning of the ADEA.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This understanding of a \xe2\x80\x9ccharge\xe2\x80\x9d is common in administrative law. The regulations governing allegations of unlawful employment practices at the Government Accountability Office, for example, define \xe2\x80\x9ccharge\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cany request filed \xe2\x80\xa6 to investigate any matter\xe2\x80\x9d within the jurisdiction of the agency. 4 CFR \xc2\xa728.3 (2006). In actions alleging unfair labor practices, the \xe2\x80\x9cpurpose of the charge is \xe2\x80\xa6 to set in motion the [National Labor Relations] Board\xe2\x80\x99s investigative machinery.\xe2\x80\x9d Flex Plastics, Inc., 262 N.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0B. 651, 652 (1982). In accordance with the charge\xe2\x80\x99s purpose of triggering an investigation that involves notice to the employer, agencies often indicate that the charge will not be kept confidential. For example, the EEOC anticipates that a charge usually will be released to the employer. See, e.g., 1 EEOC Compliance Manual \xc2\xa72.2(b), p. 2:0001 (Aug. 2002) (hereinafter EEOC Manual) (providing that correspondence may be processed as a charge if, inter alia, it \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not express concerns about confidentiality\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa73.6, at 3:0001 (noting that \xe2\x80\x9cit is EEOC policy to \xe2\x80\xa6 serve the [employer] with a copy of ADEA charges unless this will impede EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s law enforcement functions\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The ordinary understanding of the term \xe2\x80\x9ccharge\xe2\x80\x9d applies equally in the employment discrimination context, where a charge is a formal accusation that an employer has violated, or will violate, employment discrimination laws. See 29 CFR \xc2\xa71626.3 (2007) (describing a charge as an allegation that an employer \xe2\x80\x9chas engaged in or is about to engage in actions in violation of the Act\xe2\x80\x9d). The charge is presented to the agency with jurisdiction over such matters\xe2\x80\x94the EEOC\xe2\x80\x94to trigger enforcement proceedings that are intended to eliminate violations of the ADEA. See 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7626(d) (directing the agency, upon receipt of a charge, to notify the employer and take steps to eliminate the allegedly unlawful practice). I therefore agree with the EEOC that the statutory term \xe2\x80\x9ccharge\xe2\x80\x9d must mean, at a minimum,[Footnote 2] a writing that objectively indicates an intent to initiate the agency\xe2\x80\x99s enforcement processes. See Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 15 (noting that a charge must \xe2\x80\x9cobjectively manifest an intent to make a formal accusation\xe2\x80\x9d of an ADEA violation). To the extent the Court\xe2\x80\x99s test is subjective, see, e.g., ante, at 11 (noting that a charge indicates that the complainant \xe2\x80\x9cwants\xe2\x80\x9d to activate the EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s processes), I disagree with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s formulation as inconsistent with the objective test that is the EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cconsidered view.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 3] See Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 15. In any event, respondent\xe2\x80\x99s documents do not objectively indicate an intent to initiate the EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s processes; any test that construes them otherwise is, in my opinion, an unreasonable construction of the statutory term \xe2\x80\x9ccharge,\xe2\x80\x9d and unworthy of deference. See Chevron U. S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., 467 U. S. 837, 843\xe2\x80\x93845 (1984).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The cumulative effect of two aspects of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s documents, the Court holds, illustrates that she filed a charge of discrimination: first, her request in her affidavit that the agency take action, and second, her marking of a box on the questionnaire form consenting to the release of her identity to her employer, Federal Express Corporation (FedEx). Ante, at 14. In my view, neither of these factors, separately or together, objectively indicates that respondent intended to initiate the EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s processes.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The last substantive paragraph of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s affidavit said: \xe2\x80\x9cPlease force Federal Express to end their age discrimination \xe2\x80\xa6 .\xe2\x80\x9d App. 273. But the issue here is not whether respondent wanted the EEOC to cause the company\xe2\x80\x99s compliance by any means; it is whether she wanted the EEOC immediately to employ the particular method of enforcement that consists of filing a charge. Her request to \xe2\x80\x9cforce Federal Express to end their age discrimination\xe2\x80\x9d could have been met by the agency\xe2\x80\x99s beginning the interviewing and counseling process that would ultimately lead to a charge. Or the agency could have proceeded to enforcement without a charge. See infra, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311, n.\xc2\xa05 (discussing the EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s authority to investigate age discrimination in the absence of any charge). Alternately, after receiving indications of repeated violations by a particular company on many intake questionnaires, the agency could have approached the company informally, effectively forcing compliance by the threat of agency litigation. See B. Lindemann & D. Kadue, Age Discrimination in Employment Law 470 (2003) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe EEOC may commence litigation under the ADEA without having to first file a charge, so long as it has attempted conciliation\xe2\x80\x9d). That sort of action would also have satisfied respondent\xe2\x80\x99s request. Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s statement to the EEOC no more constitutes expression of a present intent to file a charge than her request to a lawyer that he put an end to her employer\xe2\x80\x99s discrimination would constitute expression of present intent to file a complaint. The Court is simply wrong to say that a charge must merely request that the agency take \xe2\x80\x9csome action,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 9, or \xe2\x80\x9cwhatever action is necessary to vindicate her rights,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 6, or unspecified \xe2\x80\x9cremedial action to protect the employee\xe2\x80\x99s rights,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 11. To the contrary, a charge must request that the agency take the particular form of remedial action that results from filing a charge.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Aside from revealing the ambiguity in its definition of a \xe2\x80\x9ccharge,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court\xe2\x80\x99s constructions stretch the term far beyond what it can bear. A mere request for help from a complainant\xe2\x80\x94who, the Court acknowledges, may \xe2\x80\x9chave no detailed knowledge of the relevant statutory mechanisms and agency processes,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312\xe2\x80\x94cannot be equated with an intent to file a charge. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s test permits no principled basis for distinguishing a request for the agency to take what might be described as \xe2\x80\x9cpre-charge\xe2\x80\x9d actions, such as interviewing and counseling, from a request for the agency to commence enforcement proceedings. All are properly considered \xe2\x80\x9cagency action,\xe2\x80\x9d all presumably would be part of the agency\xe2\x80\x99s remedial processes, and all are designed to protect the employee\xe2\x80\x99s rights. But a complainant\xe2\x80\x99s intent to trigger actions unrelated to charge-processing plainly cannot form the basis for distinguishing charges from other inquiries because it lacks any grounding in the meaning of the statutory term.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even if respondent\xe2\x80\x99s statement, viewed in isolation, could reasonably be understood as reflecting the requisite intent, it must be viewed in context. It is clear that respondent\xe2\x80\x99s filing, taken as a whole, did not amount to a request for the EEOC to commence enforcement proceedings. In fact, respondent\xe2\x80\x99s affidavit is replete with indications of an intent not to commence formal agency action. The entire first paragraph is an extensive statement that respondent had been assured her affidavit would be kept confidential, App. 266, suggesting that she did not intend the document to initiate enforcement proceedings, which would require the EEOC to notify FedEx of her allegations. See 1 EEOC Manual \xc2\xa72.2(b), at 2:0001 (stating that correspondence expressing concerns about confidentiality should not be treated as a charge). She identified the document as a \xe2\x80\x9ccomplaint.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 266. And although the document was notarized and respondent attested to its truthfulness, nowhere did she state that she authorized the EEOC to attempt to resolve the dispute. Id., at\n266\xe2\x80\x93274. Finally, the affidavit was attached to the\nintake questionnaire, which also gave no objective indication of any intent to activate the EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s enforcement proceedings.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the Court concedes, the agency would not consider respondent\xe2\x80\x99s intake questionnaire a charge. Ante, at 14. Indeed, we are in agreement that the form contains numerous indicators that it will not be considered a charge. Ibid. (stating that the \xe2\x80\x9cdesign of the form \xe2\x80\xa6 does not give rise to the inference that the employee requests action against the employer,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n fact the wording of the questionnaire suggests the opposite\xe2\x80\x9d). The title of the form, \xe2\x80\x9cIntake Questionnaire,\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 4] suggests that its purpose is preliminary information-gathering, not the filing of a formal charge. Likewise, the statement at the top of the form indicates that further steps are anticipated: \xe2\x80\x9cPlease answer the following questions, telling us briefly why you have been discriminated against in employment. An officer of the EEOC will talk with you after you complete this form.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 265. The form gives the complainant the opportunity to keep her identity confidential. Ibid. And it contains a Privacy Act statement on the back, prominently referenced on the front of the form, which states that the information provided on the questionnaire \xe2\x80\x9cwill be used by Commission employees to determine the existence of facts relevant to a decision as to whether the Commission has jurisdiction over potential charges, complaints or allegations of employment discrimination and to provide such pre-charge filing counseling as is appropriate.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court apparently believes that these objective indicators are trumped by the fact that respondent marked the box authorizing the agency to disclose her identity to her employer. That portion of the form states: \xe2\x80\x9cNormally, your identity will be disclosed to the organization which allegedly discriminated against you. Do you consent or not consent to such disclosure?\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Since the form states it is for a narrow purpose and that identities of complainants are normally disclosed, there is no reason to view respondent\xe2\x80\x99s checking of the box as converting the form\xe2\x80\x99s stated narrow purpose to a broader one.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In comparison to the intake questionnaire, the Charge of Discrimination form contains a number of objective indications that it will trigger the agency\xe2\x80\x99s enforcement processes. Indeed, its very title clearly indicates that it is a charge, and it contains a space for a charge number. 1 EEOC Manual Exh. 2\xe2\x80\x93C, at 2:0009. Although both forms require the complainant to sign and attest that the information is correct, only the Charge of Discrimination requests an attestation that the complainant intends to initiate the agency\xe2\x80\x99s procedures. Just above the space for the complainant\xe2\x80\x99s signature, the form states \xe2\x80\x9cI want this charge filed with both the EEOC and the State or local Agency, if any. I will advise the agencies if I change my address or telephone number and I will cooperate fully with them in the processing of my charge in accordance with their procedures.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The form notes \xe2\x80\x9cCharging party\xe2\x80\x9d at the bottom of the space for the signature. Ibid. And it states on the back that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he purpose of the charge, whether recorded initially on this form or in some other way reduced to writing and later recorded on this form, is to invoke the jurisdiction of the Commission.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., Exh. 2\xe2\x80\x93C, at 2:0010. Also on the back, under \xe2\x80\x9cROUTINE USES,\xe2\x80\x9d the Charge of Discrimination states that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]nformation provided on this form will be used by Commission employees to guide the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s investigatory activities.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Although the EEOC prefers to receive a completed charge form, see Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 18, n.\xc2\xa09 (noting that \xe2\x80\x9cEEOC\xe2\x80\x99s preferred practice is indeed to receive a completed Form 5 whenever possible\xe2\x80\x9d), another writing could indicate a complainant\xe2\x80\x99s intent to commence the EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s enforcement processes. But the form chosen by the complainant must be viewed as strong evidence of the complainant\xe2\x80\x99s intent, and that evidence should be deemed overcome only if the document, viewed as a whole, compels that conclusion.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For the reasons I have described, respondent\xe2\x80\x99s intake questionnaire and attached affidavit do not objectively indicate that she intended to initiate the EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s enforcement processes. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that the two factors \xe2\x80\x9cwere enough to bring the entire filing within the definition of charge,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 15, is not supported by the facts and, in my view, reveals that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s standard is sufficiently vacuous to permit the agency\xe2\x80\x99s post hoc interpretation of a document to control. But we cannot, under the guise of deference, sanction an agency\xe2\x80\x99s use of a standard that the agency has not adequately explained. Cf., e.g., Pearson v. Shalala, 164 F. 3d 650, 660\xe2\x80\x93661 (CADC 1999) (equating an agency\xe2\x80\x99s denial of a party\xe2\x80\x99s request based on the application of a vague term with simply saying \xe2\x80\x9cno\xe2\x80\x9d without explanation).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The malleability of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s test is further revealed by its statement that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here might be instances where the indicated discrimination is so clear or pervasive that the agency could infer from the allegations themselves that action is requested and required.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14. The clarity or pervasiveness of alleged discrimination is irrelevant to the employee\xe2\x80\x99s intent to file a charge. Although the Court states that the \xe2\x80\x9cagency is not required to treat every completed Intake Questionnaire as a charge,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., it apparently would permit the EEOC to do so, because under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s test the EEOC can infer intent from circumstances\xe2\x80\x94such as \xe2\x80\x9cclear or pervasive\xe2\x80\x9d discrimination\xe2\x80\x94that have no grounding in the \xe2\x80\x9cintent to act\xe2\x80\x9d\nrequirement.[Footnote 5]

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Yet another indication that respondent\xe2\x80\x99s documents did not objectively manifest an intent to initiate the EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s enforcement processes is that the agency did not treat them as a charge. It did not assign a charge number, and it did not notify FedEx or commence its enforcement proceedings. This is not surprising: The EEOC accepts charges via a thorough intake process[Footnote 6] in which completed intake questionnaires are not typically viewed as charges, but are used to assist the EEOC in developing the charge. A complainant visiting an EEOC office may be asked to complete an intake questionnaire. See EEOC FAQ, Answer to \xe2\x80\x9cHow do I file a charge of employment discrimination?\xe2\x80\x9d, online at https://eeoc.custhelp.com. An EEOC investigator then conducts a precharge interview, 1 EEOC Manual \xc2\xa72.4, at 2:0001; 2 B. Lindemann & P. Grossman, Employment Discrimination Law 1685 (4th ed. 2007), covering a range of topics, including applicable laws, the complainant\xe2\x80\x99s allegations and other possibly discriminatory practices, confidentiality, time limits, notice requirements, and private suit rights. See 1 EEOC Manual \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72.4(a)\xe2\x80\x93(g), at 2:0001\xe2\x80\x932:0003. Using the information contained in the intake questionnaire and gathered during the interview, the investigator drafts the charge on a Form 5 Charge of Discrimination according to specific agency instructions, and also drafts an affidavit containing background data. See id., \xc2\xa72.5, at 2:0003\xe2\x80\x932:0005. The investigator assigns a charge number and begins the process of serving notice on the employer and investigating the allegations. See 2 Lindemann & Grossman, supra, at 1685\xe2\x80\x931690.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Charges are thus typically completed and filed by the agency, not the complainant. See Edelman v. Lynchburg College, 535 U. S. 106, 115, n.\xc2\xa09 (2002) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe general practice of EEOC staff members is to prepare a formal charge of discrimination for the complainant to review and to verify\xe2\x80\x9d (citing Brief for United States et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 24)); EEOC FAQ, Answers to \xe2\x80\x9cWhere can I obtain copies of the forms to file a charge?\xe2\x80\x9d (stating that the agency\xe2\x80\x99s policy is not to provide blank charge forms); \xe2\x80\x9cHow do I file a charge of employment discrimination?\xe2\x80\x9d (\xe2\x80\x9cWhen the field office has all the information it needs, you will be counseled regarding the strengths and weaknesses of a potential charge and/or you will receive a completed charge form (Form 5) for your signature\xe2\x80\x9d), online at https://eeoc.custhelp.com. Once the charge is complete, the EEOC notifies the employer of the charge, usually attaching a copy of the completed charge form. 1 EEOC Manual \xc2\xa73.6, at 3:0001 (\xe2\x80\x9cWhile 29 CFR \xc2\xa71626.11 only requires notice to the [employer] that an ADEA charge has been filed, it is EEOC policy to also serve the [employer] with a copy of ADEA charges unless this will impede EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s law enforcement functions\xe2\x80\x9d); Inzeo Memorandum, online at http://www.eeoc.gov/charge/memo-8-13-07.html.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To be sure, the EEOC is prepared to accept charges by other methods. If the complainant cannot or will not visit an EEOC office, an investigator may conduct the pre-charge interview and take the charge by telephone, see 1 EEOC Manual \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72.3, 2.4, at 2:0001, but the agency must reduce the allegations to writing before they will be considered a charge, see 29 CFR \xc2\xa71626.8(b) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] charge is sufficient when the Commission receives from the person making the charge either a written statement or information reduced to writing by the Commission that conforms to the requirements of \xc2\xa71626.6\xe2\x80\x9d). When the EEOC receives correspondence that is a potential charge, the investigator must contact the complainant and conduct an intake interview. See 1 EEOC Manual \xc2\xa72.2(a), at 2:0001. Alternatively, if the correspondence \xe2\x80\x9ccontains all information necessary to begin investigating, constitutes a clear and timely request for EEOC to act, and does not express concerns about confidentiality or retaliation,\xe2\x80\x9d then the investigator may process it as a charge without conducting an interview. See id., \xc2\xa72.2(b), at 2:0001.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thus, while the EEOC does not typically view an intake questionnaire as a charge, I would not rule out the possibility that, in appropriate circumstances, an intake questionnaire, like other correspondence, could contain the elements necessary to constitute a charge. But an intake questionnaire\xe2\x80\x94even one accompanied by an affidavit\xe2\x80\x94should not be construed as a charge unless it objectively indicates an intent to initiate the EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s enforcement processes. As I have explained, respondent\xe2\x80\x99s intake questionnaire and attached affidavit fall short of that standard. I would hold that the documents respondent filed with the EEOC were not a charge and thus did not preserve her right to sue.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The implications of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision will reach far beyond respondent\xe2\x80\x99s case. Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision does nothing\xe2\x80\x94absolutely nothing\xe2\x80\x94to solve the problem that under the EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s current processes no one can tell, ex ante, whether a particular filing is or is not a charge. Given the Court\xe2\x80\x99s utterly vague criteria, whatever the agency later decides to regard as a charge is a charge\xe2\x80\x94and the statutorily required notice to the employer and conciliation process will be evaded in the future as it has been in this case. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s failure to apply a clear and sensible rule renders its decision of little use in future cases to complainants, employers, or the agency.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For these reasons, I would reverse the judgment below.
