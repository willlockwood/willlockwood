Culbertson v. Berryhill, ACTING Commissioner of Social Security

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

No. 17\xe2\x80\x93773.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 7, 2018\xe2\x80\x94Decided January 8, 2019

\n

The Social Security Act regulates the fees that attorneys may charge claimants seeking Title II benefits for representation both before the Social Security Administration and in federal court. For representation in administrative proceedings, the Act provides two ways to determine fees. If a fee agreement exists, fees are capped at the lesser of 25% of past-due benefits or a set dollar amount\xe2\x80\x94currently $6,000.\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7406(a)(2)(A). Absent an agreement, the agency may set any \xe2\x80\x9creasonable\xe2\x80\x9d fee. \xc2\xa7406(a)(1). In either case, the agency is required to withhold up to 25% of past-due benefits for direct payment of any fee. \xc2\xa7406(a)(4). For representation in court proceedings, fees are capped at 25% of past-due benefits, and the agency has authority to withhold such benefits to pay these fees. \xc2\xa7406(b)(1)(A).

\n

Petitioner Culbertson represented Katrina Wood in Social Security disability benefit proceedings before the agency and in District Court. The agency ultimately awarded Wood past-due benefits, withheld 25% of those benefits to pay any attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees, and awarded Culbertson fees under \xc2\xa7406(a) for representation before the agency. Culbertson then moved for a separate fee award under \xc2\xa7406(b) for the court proceedings, requesting a full 25% of past-due benefits. The District Court granted the request, but only in part, because Culbertson did not subtract the amount he had already received under \xc2\xa7406(a) for his agency-level representation. The Eleventh Circuit affirmed, holding that the 25% limit under \xc2\xa7406(b) applies to the total fees awarded under both \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7406(a) and (b).

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Section 406(b)(1)(A)\xe2\x80\x99s 25% cap applies only to fees for court representation and not to the aggregate fees awarded under \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7406(a) and (b). Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0Section 406(b) provides that a court rendering a favorable judgment to a claimant \xe2\x80\x9crepresented before the court by an attorney\xe2\x80\x9d may award \xe2\x80\x9ca reasonable fee for such representation, not in excess of 25 percent\xe2\x80\x9d of past-due benefits. Here, the adjective \xe2\x80\x9csuch,\xe2\x80\x9d which means \xe2\x80\x9c[o]f the kind or degree already described or implied,\xe2\x80\x9d refers to the only form of representation \xe2\x80\x9calready described\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa7406(b)\xe2\x80\x94i.e., \xe2\x80\x9crepresent[ation] before the court.\xe2\x80\x9d Thus, the 25% cap applies only to fees for representation before the court, not the agency.

\n

Subsections (a) and (b) address different stages of the representation and use different methods for calculating fees. Given this statutory structure, applying \xc2\xa7406(b)\xe2\x80\x99s 25% cap on court-stage fees to \xc2\xa7406(a) agency-stage fees, or the aggregate of \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7406(a) and (b) fees, would make little sense. For example, such a reading would subject \xc2\xa7406(a)(1)\xe2\x80\x99s reasonableness limitation to \xc2\xa7406(b)\xe2\x80\x99s 25% cap\xe2\x80\x94a limitation not included in the relevant provision of the statute. Had Congress wanted agency-stage fees to be capped at 25%, it presumably would have said so directly in subsection (a). Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0The fact that the agency presently withholds a single pool of 25% of past-due benefits for direct payment of agency and court fees does not support an aggregate reading. The statutory text provides for two pools of money for direct payment of fees. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7406(a)(4), (b)(1)(A). The agency\xe2\x80\x99s choice to withhold only one pool of 25% of past-due benefits does not alter this text. More fundamentally, the amount of past-due benefits that the agency can withhold for direct payment does not delimit the amount of fees that can be approved for representation before the agency or the court. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

861 F. 3d 1197, reversed and remanded.

\n

Thomas, J., delivered the opinion for a unanimous Court.

RICHARD ALLEN CULBERTSON, PETITIONER v. NANCY A. BERRYHILL, ACTING COMMISSIONER OF SOCIAL SECURITY

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

[January 8, 2019]

\n

\n

Justice Thomas delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

Federal law regulates the fees that attorneys may charge Social Security claimants for representation before the Social Security Administration and a reviewing court. See 42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7406(a)\xe2\x80\x93(b). The question in this case is whether the statutory scheme limits the aggregate amount of fees for both stages of representation to 25% of the claimant\xe2\x80\x99s past-due benefits. Because \xc2\xa7406(b) by its terms imposes a 25% cap on fees only for representation before a court, and \xc2\xa7406(a) has separate caps on fees for representation before the agency, we hold that the statute does not impose a 25% cap on aggregate fees.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

Title II of the Social Security Act,\n\n49Stat.\n622, as amended,\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7401 et seq., \xe2\x80\x9cis an insurance program\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cprovides old-age, survivor, and disability benefits to insured individuals irrespective of financial need.\xe2\x80\x9d Bowen v. Galbreath,\n\n485 U.\xc2\xa0S. 74, 75 (1988). A claimant\xe2\x80\x99s application for Title II benefits can result in payments of past-due benefits\xe2\x80\x94i.e., benefits that accrued before a favorable decision, 20 CFR \xc2\xa7404.1703 (2018)\xe2\x80\x94as well as ongoing monthly benefits, see\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7423(a). A claimant who has been denied benefits \xe2\x80\x9cin whole or in part\xe2\x80\x9d by the Social Security Administration may seek administrative review of the initial agency determination, \xc2\xa7405(b), and may then seek judicial review of the resulting final agency decision, \xc2\xa7405(g).

\n

As presently written, the Social Security Act \xe2\x80\x9cdiscretely\xe2\x80\x9d addresses attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees for the administrative and judicial-review stages: \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa7406(a) governs fees for representa-tion in administrative proceedings; \xc2\xa7406(b) controls fees for representation in court.\xe2\x80\x9d Gisbrecht v. Barnhart,\n\n535 U.\xc2\xa0S. 789, 794 (2002). The original Social Security Act made no such provision for attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees in either proceeding. Id., at 793, n.\xc2\xa02. But in 1939, \xe2\x80\x9cCongress amended the Act to permit the Social Security Board to prescribe maximum fees attorneys could charge for representation of claimants before the agency.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. In 1965, Congress added a new subsection (b) to \xc2\xa7406 that explicitly prescribed fees for representation before a court and \xe2\x80\x9callow[ed] withholding of past-due benefits to pay\xe2\x80\x9d these fees directly to the attorney. Social Security Amendments of 1965, \xc2\xa7332,\n\n79Stat.\n403; Bowen, 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 76. In 1968, Congress amended subsection (a) to give the agency similar withholding authority to pay attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees incurred in administrative proceedings. Id., at 76.

\n

Section 406(a) is titled \xe2\x80\x9cRecognition of representatives; fees for representation before Commissioner\xe2\x80\x9d of Social Security. It includes two ways to determine fees for representation before the agency, depending on whether a prior fee agreement exists. If the claimant has a fee agreement, subsection (a)(2) caps fees at the lesser of 25% of past-due benefits or a set dollar amount\xe2\x80\x94currently $6,000. \xc2\xa7406(a)(2)(A); Maximum Dollar Limit in the Fee Agreement Process, 74 Fed. Reg. 6080 (2009). Absent a fee agreement, subsection (a)(1) gives the agency authority to \xe2\x80\x9cprescribe the maximum fees which may be charged for services performed in connection with any claim\xe2\x80\x9d before the agency. If the claimant obtains a favorable agency determination, the agency may allot \xe2\x80\x9ca reasonable fee to compensate such attorney for the services performed by him.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Subsection (a)(4) requires the agency to withhold up to 25% of past-due benefits for direct payment of any fee for representation before the agency:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[I]f the claimant is determined to be entitled to past-due benefits under this subchapter and the person representing the claimant is an attorney, the Commissioner of Social Security shall .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. certify for payment out of such past-due benefits .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to such attorney an amount equal to so much of the maximum fee as does not exceed 25 percent of such past-due benefits .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Section 406(b) is titled \xe2\x80\x9cFees for representation before court.\xe2\x80\x9d Subsection (b)(1)(A) both limits these fees to no more than 25% of past-due benefits and allows the agency to withhold past-due benefits to pay these fees:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cWhenever a court renders a judgment favorable to a claimant under this subchapter who was represented before the court by an attorney, the court may determine and allow as part of its judgment a reasonable fee for such representation, not in excess of 25 percent of the total of the past-due benefits to which the claimant is entitled by reason of such judgment, and the Commissioner of Social Security may .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. certify the amount of such fee for payment to such attorney out of, and not in addition to, the amount of such past-due benefits.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

At issue is whether \xc2\xa7406(b)\xe2\x80\x99s 25% cap limits the aggregate fees awarded for representation before both the agency under \xc2\xa7406(a) and the court under \xc2\xa7406(b), or instead limits only the fee awarded for court representation under \xc2\xa7406(b).

\n

B

\n

Petitioner Richard Culbertson represented claimant Katrina Wood in proceedings seeking Social Security disability benefits. After the agency denied Wood benefits, she brought an action in district court. For the court action, Wood signed a contingency-fee agreement \xe2\x80\x9cto pay a fee of 25 percent of the total of the past-due benefits to which [she] is entitled\xe2\x80\x9d in consideration for Culbertson\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9crepresentation of [her] in Federal Court.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 8\xe2\x80\x939. The agreement excludes fees for \xe2\x80\x9cany representation before\xe2\x80\x9d the agency. Id., at 9.

\n

The District Court reversed the agency\xe2\x80\x99s denial of benefits and remanded for further proceedings. The court granted Wood attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees under the Equal Access to Justice Act (EAJA), which authorizes an award against the Government for reasonable fees in \xe2\x80\x9ccivil action[s].\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72412(d)(1)(A) and (2)(A).

\n

On remand, the agency awarded Wood past-due disability benefits and withheld 25% of those benefits to pay any attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees that might ultimately be awarded. The agency also awarded Culbertson \xc2\xa7406(a) fees for representing Wood before the agency.

\n

Culbertson then moved the District Court for a separate fee award under \xc2\xa7406(b) for representing Wood there. After accounting for the EAJA award, see Gisbrecht, supra, at 796; App. 9, this request amounted to a full 25% of past-due benefits. The court granted Culbertson\xe2\x80\x99s request only in part because he did not subtract the amount he had already received under \xc2\xa7406(a) for his agency-level representation. The Eleventh Circuit affirmed, relying on Circuit precedent to hold that \xe2\x80\x9cthe 25% limit from \xc2\xa7406(b) applies to total fees awarded under both \xc2\xa7406(a) and (b), \xe2\x80\x98preclud[ing] the aggregate allowance of attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees greater than twenty-five percent of the past due benefits received by the claimant.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Wood v. Commissioner of Social Security, 861 F.\xc2\xa03d 1197, 1205 (2017) (quoting Dawson v. Finch, 425 F.\xc2\xa02d 1192, 1195 (CA5 1970); emphasis deleted).[1]*

\n

Given a conflict between the Circuits on this question, see 861 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1205\xe2\x80\x931206, we granted certiorari. 584 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2018). Because no party defends the judgment, we appointed Amy Weil to brief and argue this case as amicus curiae in support of the judgment below. 584 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2018). Amicus Weil has ably discharged her assigned responsibilities.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

We \xe2\x80\x9cbegi[n] with the language of the statute itself, and that is also where the inquiry should end, for the statute\xe2\x80\x99s language is plain.\xe2\x80\x9d Puerto Rico v. Franklin Cal. Tax-Free Trust, 579 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2016) (slip op., at 9) (internal quotation marks omitted). Under \xc2\xa7406(b), when a court \xe2\x80\x9crenders a judgment favorable to a claimant .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. who was represented before the court by an attorney,\xe2\x80\x9d the court may award \xe2\x80\x9ca reasonable fee for such representation, not in excess of 25 percent of the total of the past-due benefits to which the claimant is entitled by reason of such judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7406(b)(1)(A) (emphasis added). Both at the time of enactment and today, the adjective \xe2\x80\x9csuch\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9c[o]f the kind or degree already described or implied.\xe2\x80\x9d H.\xc2\xa0Fowler & F. Fowler, Concise Oxford Dictionary of Current English 1289 (5th ed. 1964); Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 1661 (10th ed. 2014) (\xe2\x80\x9c[t]hat or those; having just been mentioned\xe2\x80\x9d). Here, the only form of representation \xe2\x80\x9calready described\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa7406(b) is \xe2\x80\x9crepresent[ation] before the court by an attorney.\xe2\x80\x9d Accordingly, the 25% cap applies only to fees for representation before the court, not the agency.

\n

This interpretation is supported by \xe2\x80\x9cthe structure of the statute and its other provisions.\xe2\x80\x9d Maracich v. Spears,\n\n570 U.\xc2\xa0S. 48, 60 (2013). As an initial matter, subsections (a) and (b) address different stages of the representation. Section 406(a) addresses fees for representation \xe2\x80\x9cbefore the Commissioner,\xe2\x80\x9d whereas \xc2\xa7406(b) addresses fees for representation in court. Because some claimants will prevail before the agency and have no need to bring a court action, it is unsurprising that the statute contemplates separate fees for each stage of representation.

\n

These subsections also calculate fees differently. Section 406(b) applies a flat 25% cap on fees for court representation. By contrast, \xc2\xa7406(a) provides two ways to determine fees for agency proceedings. Subsection (a)(2) caps fees based on a fee agreement at the lesser of 25% of past-due benefits or $6,000. Supra, at 2. If there is no fee agreement, the agency may set any fee, including a fee greater than 25% of past-due benefits, so long as the fee is \xe2\x80\x9creasonable.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7406(a)(1).

\n

Given this statutory structure, applying \xc2\xa7406(b)\xe2\x80\x99s 25% cap on court-stage fees to \xc2\xa7406(a) agency-stage fees, or the aggregate of \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7406(a) and (b) fees, would make little sense. Many claimants will never litigate in court, yet under the aggregate reading, agency fees would be capped at 25% based on a provision related exclusively to representation in court. Absent a fee agreement, \xc2\xa7406(a)(1) subjects agency fees only to a reasonableness limitation, so applying \xc2\xa7406(b)\xe2\x80\x99s cap to such fees would add a limitation that Congress did not include in the relevant provision of the statute. If Congress had wanted these fees to be capped at 25%, it presumably would have said so directly in subsection (a), instead of providing for a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable fee\xe2\x80\x9d in that subsection and adding a 25% cap in \xc2\xa7406(b) without even referencing subsection (a). Thus, the structure of the statute confirms that \xc2\xa7406(b) caps only court representation fees.

\n

B

\n

Amicus Amy Weil agrees that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa7406(a) and \xc2\xa7406(b) provide separate avenues for an award of attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees for representation of a Social Security claimant,\xe2\x80\x9d but emphasizes that \xe2\x80\x9cthese fees are certified for payment out of a single source: the 25% of past-due benefits withheld by the Commissioner.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Court-Appointed Amicus Curiae 10. According to Amicus, \xe2\x80\x9c[b]ecause the Commissioner withholds only one pool of 25% of past-due benefits from which to pay attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees for both agency and court representation, for an attorney to collect a fee that exceeds the 25% pool of withheld disability benefits,\xe2\x80\x9d the attorney may \xe2\x80\x9cneed to file a lawsuit against his disabled client\xe2\x80\x9d to collect the difference. Id., at 23\xe2\x80\x9324. Therefore, Amicus urges, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen the statute is read as a whole,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cit is evident that Congress placed a cumulative 25% cap on attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees payable for successful representation of a Social Security claimant before both the agency and the court.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 10.

\n

Amicus is quite right that presently the agency withholds a single pool of 25% of past-due benefits for direct payment of agency and court fees. See Social Security Administration, Program Operations Manual System (POMS), GN\xc2\xa003920.035(A), online at https://policy.ssa.gov/ poms.nsf/lnx/0203920035 (as last visited Jan. 2, 2019); see also 20 CFR \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7404.1730(a) and (b)(1)(i). And Amicus sensibly argues that if there is only a single 25% pool for direct payment of fees, Congress might not have intended aggregate fees higher than 25%. This argument is plausible, but the statutory text in fact provides for two pools of money for direct payment of fees. Any shortage of withheld benefits for direct payment of fees is thus due to agency policy.

\n

Under \xc2\xa7406(a)(4), the agency \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d certify for direct payment of agency representation fees \xe2\x80\x9can amount equal to so much of the maximum fee as does not exceed 25 percent of\xe2\x80\x9d past-due benefits. In other words, this subsection requires that the agency withhold the approved fees for work performed in agency proceedings, up to 25% of the amount of the claimant\xe2\x80\x99s past-due benefits. But this is not the only subsection that enables the agency to withhold past-due benefits for direct payment of fees. Section 406(b)(1)(A) provides that the agency \xe2\x80\x9cmay\xe2\x80\x9d certify past-due benefits for direct payment of court representation fees. As the Government explains, the agency has nevertheless \xe2\x80\x9cexercised its discretion .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to withhold a total of 25% of past-due benefits for direct payment of the approved agency and court fees.\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief for Respondent 8 (emphasis added). The agency\xe2\x80\x99s choice to withhold only one pool of 25% of past-due benefits does not alter the statutory text, which differentiates between agency representation in \xc2\xa7406(a) and court representation in \xc2\xa7406(b), contains separate caps on fees for each type of representation, and authorizes two pools of withheld benefits.

\n

More fundamentally, the amount of past-due benefits that the agency can withhold for direct payment does not delimit the amount of fees that can be approved for representation before the agency or the court. The attorney might receive a direct payment out of past-due benefits, but that payment could be less than the fees to which the attorney is entitled. Indeed, prior to 1968, the statute allowed fees for agency representation but lacked a provision for direct payment of such fees from past-due benefits. See supra, at 2. And under the current \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7406(a)(1) and (4), the agency can award a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable fee\xe2\x80\x9d that exceeds the 25% of past-due benefits it can withhold for direct payment.

\n

In short, despite the force of Amicus\xe2\x80\x99 arguments, the statute does not bear her reading. Any concerns about a shortage of withheld benefits for direct payment and the consequences of such a shortage are best addressed to the agency, Congress, or the attorney\xe2\x80\x99s good judgment.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

Because the 25% cap in \xc2\xa7406(b)(1)(A) applies only to fees for court representation, and not to the aggregate fees awarded under \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7406(a) and (b), the judgment of the United States Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.
