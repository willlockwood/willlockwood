SHADY GROVE ORTHOPEDIC ASSOCIATES, P.\xc2\xa0A. v. ALLSTATE INSURANCE CO.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the second circuit

\n

No. 08\xe2\x80\x931008.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 2, 2009\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 31, 2010

\n

After respondent Allstate refused to remit the interest due under New York law on petitioner Shady Grove\xe2\x80\x99s insurance claim, Shady Grove filed this class action in diversity to recover interest Allstate owed it and others. Despite the class action provisions set forth in Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23, the District Court held itself deprived of jurisdiction by N.\xc2\xa0Y. Civ. Prac. Law Ann. \xc2\xa7901(b), which precludes a class action to recover a \xe2\x80\x9cpenalty\xe2\x80\x9d such as statutory interest. Affirming, the Second Circuit acknowledged that a Federal Rule adopted in compliance with the Rules Enabling Act, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72072, would control if it conflicted with \xc2\xa7901(b), but held there was no conflict because \xc2\xa7901(b) and Rule 23 address different issues\xe2\x80\x94eligibility of the particular type of claim for class treatment and certifiability of a given class, respectively. Finding no Federal Rule on point, the Court of Appeals held that \xc2\xa7901(b) must be applied by federal courts sitting in diversity because it is \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning of Erie R. Co. v. Tompkins, 304 U. S. 64 (1938).

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The judgment is reversed, and the case is remanded.

\n

549 F.\xc2\xa03d 137, reversed and remanded.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia delivered the opinion of the Court with respect to Parts I and II\xe2\x80\x93A, concluding that \xc2\xa7901(b) does not preclude a federal district court sitting in diversity from entertaining a class action under Rule 23. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0If Rule 23 answers the question in dispute, it governs here unless it exceeds its statutory authorization or Congress\xe2\x80\x99s rulemaking power. Burlington Northern R. Co. v. Woods, 480 U. S. 1, 4\xe2\x80\x935. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x934.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Rule 23(b) answers the question in dispute\xe2\x80\x94whether Shady Grove\xe2\x80\x99s suit may proceed as a class action\xe2\x80\x94when it states that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] class action may be maintained\xe2\x80\x9d if certain conditions are met. Since \xc2\xa7901(b) attempts to answer the same question, stating that Shady Grove\xe2\x80\x99s suit \xe2\x80\x9cmay not be maintained as a class action\xe2\x80\x9d because of the relief it seeks, that provision cannot apply in diversity suits unless Rule 23 is ultra vires. The Second Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s view that \xc2\xa7901(b) and Rule 23 address different issues is rejected. The line between eligibility and certifiability is entirely artificial and, in any event, Rule 23 explicitly empowers a federal court to certify a class in every case meeting its criteria. Allstate\xe2\x80\x99s arguments based on the exclusion of some federal claims from Rule 23\xe2\x80\x99s reach pursuant to federal statutes and on \xc2\xa7901\xe2\x80\x99s structure are unpersuasive. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x936.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0The dissent\xe2\x80\x99s claim that \xc2\xa7901(b) can coexist with Rule 23 because it addresses only the remedy available to class plaintiffs is foreclosed by \xc2\xa7901(b)\xe2\x80\x99s text, notwithstanding its perceived purpose. The principle that courts should read ambiguous Federal Rules to avoid overstepping the authorizing statute, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72072(b), does not apply because Rule 23 is clear. The dissent\xe2\x80\x99s approach does not avoid a conflict between \xc2\xa7901(b) and Rule 23 but instead would render Rule 23 partially invalid. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia, joined by The Chief Justice, Justice Thomas, and Justice Sotomayor, concluded in Parts II\xe2\x80\x93B and II\xe2\x80\x93D:

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The Rules Enabling Act, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72072, not Erie, controls the validity of a Federal Rule of Procedure. Section 2072(b)\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that federal procedural rules \xe2\x80\x9cnot abridge, enlarge or modify any substantive right\xe2\x80\x9d means that a Rule must \xe2\x80\x9creally regulat[e] procedure,\xe2\x80\x94the judicial process for enforcing rights and duties recognized by substantive law and for justly administering remedy and redress for disregard or infraction of them,\xe2\x80\x9d Sibbach v. Wilson & Co., 312 U. S. 1, 14. Though a Rule may incidentally affect a party\xe2\x80\x99s rights, it is valid so long as it regulates only the process for enforcing those rights, and not the rights themselves, the available remedies, or the rules of decision for adjudicating either. Rule 23 satisfies that criterion, at least insofar as it allows willing plaintiffs to join their separate claims against the same defendants. Allstate\xe2\x80\x99s arguments asserting \xc2\xa7901(b)\xe2\x80\x99s substantive impact are unavailing: It is not the substantive or procedural nature of the affected state law that matters, but that of the Federal Rule. See, e.g., id., at 14. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Opening federal courts to class actions that cannot proceed in state court will produce forum shopping, but that is the inevitable result of the uniform system of federal procedure that Congress created. P.\xc2\xa022.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia, joined by The Chief Justice and Justice Thomas, concluded in Part II\xe2\x80\x93C that the concurrence\xe2\x80\x99s analysis\xe2\x80\x94under which a Federal Rule may displace a state procedural rule that is not \xe2\x80\x9cbound up\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9csufficiently intertwined\xe2\x80\x9d with substantive rights and remedies under state law\xe2\x80\x94squarely conflicts with Sibbach\xe2\x80\x99s single criterion that the Federal Rule \xe2\x80\x9creally regulat[e] procedure,\xe2\x80\x9d 312 U. S., at 13\xe2\x80\x9314. Pp.\xc2\xa016\xe2\x80\x9322.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens agreed that Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23 must apply because it governs whether a class must be certified, and it does not violate the Rules Enabling Act in this case. Pp.\xc2\xa01\xe2\x80\x9322.

\n

\n\n(a)\xc2\xa0When the application of a federal rule would \xe2\x80\x9cabridge, enlarge or modify any substantive right,\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72072(b), the federal rule cannot govern. In rare cases, a federal rule that dictates an answer to a traditionally procedural question could, if applied, displace an unusual state law that is procedural in the ordinary use of the term but is so intertwined with a state right or remedy that it functions to define the scope of the state-created right. Examples may include state laws that make it significantly more difficult to bring or to prove a claim or that function as limits on the amount of recovery. An application of a federal rule that directly collides with such a state law violates the Rules Enabling Act. Pp.\xc2\xa01\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\n\n(b)\xc2\xa0N.\xc2\xa0Y. Civ. Prac. Law Ann. \xc2\xa7901(b), however, is not such a state law. It is a procedural rule that is not part of New York\xe2\x80\x99s substantive law. Pp.\xc2\xa017\xe2\x80\x9322.

SHADY GROVE ORTHOPEDIC ASSOCIATES, P. A.,\nPETITIONER v. ALLSTATE INSURANCE\nCOMPANY

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the second circuit

\n

[March 31, 2010]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia announced the judgment of the Court and delivered the opinion of the Court with respect to Parts I and II\xe2\x80\x93A, an opinion with respect to Parts II\xe2\x80\x93B and II\xe2\x80\x93D, in which The Chief Justice, Justice Thomas, and Justice Sotomayor join, and an opinion with respect to Part II\xe2\x80\x93C, in which The Chief Justice and Justice Thomas join.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0New York law prohibits class actions in suits seeking penalties or statutory minimum damages.[Footnote 1] We consider whether this precludes a federal district court sitting in diversity from entertaining a class action under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23.[Footnote 2]

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s complaint alleged the following: Shady Grove Orthopedic Associates, P. A., provided medical care to Sonia E. Galvez for injuries she suffered in an automobile accident. As partial payment for that care, Galvez assigned to Shady Grove her rights to insurance benefits under a policy issued in New York by Allstate Insurance Co. Shady Grove tendered a claim for the assigned benefits to Allstate, which under New York law had 30 days to pay the claim or deny it. See N.\xc2\xa0Y. Ins. Law Ann. \xc2\xa75106(a) (West 2009). Allstate apparently paid, but not on time, and it refused to pay the statutory interest that accrued on the overdue benefits (at two percent per month), see ibid.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Shady Grove filed this diversity suit in the Eastern District of New York to recover the unpaid statutory interest. Alleging that Allstate routinely refuses to pay interest on overdue benefits, Shady Grove sought relief on behalf of itself and a class of all others to whom Allstate owes interest. The District Court dismissed the suit for lack of jurisdiction. 466 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 467 (2006). It reasoned that N.\xc2\xa0Y. Civ. Prac. Law Ann. \xc2\xa7901(b), which precludes a suit to recover a \xe2\x80\x9cpenalty\xe2\x80\x9d from proceeding as a class action, applies in diversity suits in federal court, despite Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23. Concluding that statutory interest is a \xe2\x80\x9cpenalty\xe2\x80\x9d under New York law, it held that \xc2\xa7901(b) prohibited the proposed class action. And, since Shady Grove conceded that its individual claim (worth roughly $500) fell far short of the amount-in-controversy requirement for individual suits under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71332(a), the suit did not belong in federal court.[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Second Circuit affirmed. 549 F.\xc2\xa03d 137 (2008). The court did not dispute that a federal rule adopted in compliance with the Rules Enabling Act, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72072, would control if it conflicted with \xc2\xa7901(b). But there was no conflict because (as we will describe in more detail below) the Second Circuit concluded that Rule 23 and \xc2\xa7901(b) address different issues. Finding no federal rule on point, the Court of Appeals held that \xc2\xa7901(b) is \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning of Erie R. Co. v. Tompkins, 304 U. S. 64 (1938), and thus must be applied by federal courts sitting in diversity.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari, 556 U. S. ___ (2009).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The framework for our decision is familiar. We must first determine whether Rule 23 answers the question in dispute. Burlington Northern R. Co. v. Woods, 480 U. S. 1, 4\xe2\x80\x935 (1987). If it does, it governs\xe2\x80\x94New York\xe2\x80\x99s law notwithstanding\xe2\x80\x94unless it exceeds statutory authorization or Congress\xe2\x80\x99s rulemaking power. Id., at 5; see Hanna v. Plumer, 380 U. S. 460, 463\xe2\x80\x93464 (1965). We do not wade into Erie\xe2\x80\x99s murky waters unless the federal rule is inapplicable or invalid. See 380 U. S., at 469\xe2\x80\x93471.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The question in dispute is whether Shady Grove\xe2\x80\x99s suit may proceed as a class action. Rule 23 provides an answer. It states that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] class action may be maintained\xe2\x80\x9d if two conditions are met: The suit must satisfy the criteria set forth in subdivision (a) (i.e., numerosity, commonality, typicality, and adequacy of representation), and it also must fit into one of the three categories described in subdivision (b). Fed. Rule Civ. Proc.\xc2\xa023(b). By its terms this creates a categorical rule entitling a plaintiff whose suit meets the specified criteria to pursue his claim as a class action. (The Federal Rules regularly use \xe2\x80\x9cmay\xe2\x80\x9d to confer categorical permission, see, e.g., Fed. Rules Civ. Proc. 8(d)(2)\xe2\x80\x93(3), 14(a)(1), 18(a)\xe2\x80\x93(b), 20(a)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(2), 27(a)(1), 30(a)(1), as do federal statutes that establish procedural entitlements, see, e.g., 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7626(c)(1); 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x935(f)(1).) Thus, Rule 23 provides a one-size-fits-all formula for deciding the class-action question. Because \xc2\xa7901(b) attempts to answer the same question\xe2\x80\x94i.e., it states that Shady Grove\xe2\x80\x99s suit \xe2\x80\x9cmay not be maintained as a class action\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added) because of the relief it seeks\xe2\x80\x94it cannot apply in diversity suits unless Rule 23 is ultra vires.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Second Circuit believed that \xc2\xa7901(b) and Rule 23 do not conflict because they address different issues. Rule 23, it said, concerns only the criteria for determining whether a given class can and should be certified; section 901(b), on the other hand, addresses an antecedent question: whether the particular type of claim is eligible for class treatment in the first place\xe2\x80\x94a question on which Rule 23 is silent. See 549 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 143\xe2\x80\x93144. Allstate embraces this analysis. Brief for Respondent 12\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We disagree. To begin with, the line between eligibility and certifiability is entirely artificial. Both are preconditions for maintaining a class action. Allstate suggests that eligibility must depend on the \xe2\x80\x9cparticular cause of action\xe2\x80\x9d asserted, instead of some other attribute of the suit, id., at 12. But that is not so. Congress could, for example, provide that only claims involving more than a certain number of plaintiffs are \xe2\x80\x9celigible\xe2\x80\x9d for class treatment in federal court. In other words, relabeling Rule 23(a)\xe2\x80\x99s prerequisites \xe2\x80\x9celigibility criteria\xe2\x80\x9d would obviate Allstate\xe2\x80\x99s objection\xe2\x80\x94a sure sign that its eligibility-certifiability distinction is made-to-order.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is no reason, in any event, to read Rule 23 as addressing only whether claims made eligible for class treatment by some other law should be certified as class actions. Allstate asserts that Rule 23 neither explicitly nor implicitly empowers a federal court \xe2\x80\x9cto certify a class in each and every case\xe2\x80\x9d where the Rule\xe2\x80\x99s criteria are met. Id., at 13\xe2\x80\x9314. But that is exactly what Rule 23 does: It says that if the prescribed preconditions are satisfied \xe2\x80\x9c[a] class action may be maintained\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)\xe2\x80\x94not \xe2\x80\x9ca class action may be permitted.\xe2\x80\x9d Courts do not maintain actions; litigants do. The discretion suggested by Rule 23\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cmay\xe2\x80\x9d is discretion residing in the plaintiff: He may bring his claim in a class action if he wishes. And like the rest of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, Rule 23 automatically applies \xe2\x80\x9cin all civil actions and proceedings in the United States district courts,\xe2\x80\x9d Fed. Rule Civ. Proc.\xc2\xa01. See Califano v. Yamasaki, 442 U. S. 682, 699\xe2\x80\x93700 (1979).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Allstate points out that Congress has carved out some federal claims from Rule 23\xe2\x80\x99s reach, see, e.g., 8 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71252(e)(1)(B)\xe2\x80\x94which shows, Allstate contends, that Rule 23 does not authorize class actions for all claims, but rather leaves room for laws like \xc2\xa7901(b). But Congress, unlike New York, has ultimate authority over the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure; it can create exceptions to an individual rule as it sees fit\xe2\x80\x94either by directly amending the rule or by enacting a separate statute overriding it in certain instances. Cf. Henderson v. United States, 517 U. S. 654, 668 (1996). The fact that Congress has created specific exceptions to Rule 23 hardly proves that the Rule does not apply generally. In fact, it proves the opposite. If Rule 23 did not authorize class actions across the board, the statutory exceptions would be unnecessary.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Allstate next suggests that the structure of \xc2\xa7901 shows that Rule 23 addresses only certifiability. Section 901(a), it notes, establishes class-certification criteria roughly analogous to those in Rule 23 (wherefore it agrees that subsection is pre-empted). But \xc2\xa7901(b)\xe2\x80\x99s rule barring class actions for certain claims is set off as its own subsection, and where it applies \xc2\xa7901(a) does not. This shows, according to Allstate, that \xc2\xa7901(b) concerns a separate subject. Perhaps it does concern a subject separate from the subject of \xc2\xa7901(a). But the question before us is whether it concerns a subject separate from the subject of Rule 23\xe2\x80\x94and for purposes of answering that question the way New York has structured its statute is immaterial. Rule 23 permits all class actions that meet its requirements, and a State cannot limit that permission by structuring one part of its statute to track Rule 23 and enacting another part that imposes additional requirements. Both of \xc2\xa7901\xe2\x80\x99s subsections undeniably answer the same question as Rule 23: whether a class action may proceed for a given suit. Cf. Burlington, 480 U. S., at 7\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dissent argues that \xc2\xa7901(b) has nothing to do with whether Shady Grove may maintain its suit as a class action, but affects only the remedy it may obtain if it wins. See post, at 8\xe2\x80\x9317 (opinion of Ginsburg, J.). Whereas \xe2\x80\x9cRule 23 governs procedural aspects of class litigation\xe2\x80\x9d by \xe2\x80\x9cprescrib[ing] the considerations relevant to class certification and postcertification proceedings,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7901(b) addresses only \xe2\x80\x9cthe size of a monetary award a class plaintiff may pursue.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312. Accordingly, the dissent says, Rule 23 and New York\xe2\x80\x99s law may coexist in peace.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We need not decide whether a state law that limits the remedies available in an existing class action would conflict with Rule 23; that is not what \xc2\xa7901(b) does. By its terms, the provision precludes a plaintiff from \xe2\x80\x9cmaintain[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d a class action seeking statutory penalties. Unlike a law that sets a ceiling on damages (or puts other remedies out of reach) in properly filed class actions, \xc2\xa7901(b) says nothing about what remedies a court may award; it prevents the class actions it covers from coming into existence at all.[Footnote 4] Consequently, a court bound by \xc2\xa7901(b) could not certify a class action seeking both statutory penalties and other remedies even if it announces in advance that it will refuse to award the penalties in the event the plaintiffs prevail; to do so would violate the statute\xe2\x80\x99s clear prohibition on \xe2\x80\x9cmaintain[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d such suits as class actions.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dissent asserts that a plaintiff can avoid \xc2\xa7901(b)\xe2\x80\x99s barrier by omitting from his complaint (or removing) a request for statutory penalties. See post, at 14\xe2\x80\x9315. Even assuming all statutory penalties are waivable,[Footnote 5] the fact that a complaint omitting them could be brought as a class action would not at all prove that \xc2\xa7901(b) is addressed only to remedies. If the state law instead banned class actions for fraud claims, a would-be class-action plaintiff could drop the fraud counts from his complaint and proceed with the remainder in a class action. Yet that would not mean the law provides no remedy for fraud; the ban would affect only the procedural means by which the remedy may be pursued. In short, although the dissent correctly abandons Allstate\xe2\x80\x99s eligibility-certifiability distinction, the alternative it offers fares no better.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dissent all but admits that the literal terms of \xc2\xa7901(b) address the same subject as Rule 23\xe2\x80\x94i.e., whether a class action may be maintained\xe2\x80\x94but insists the provision\xe2\x80\x99s purpose is to restrict only remedies. See post, at 12\xe2\x80\x9315; post, at 15 (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hile phrased as responsive to the question whether certain class actions may begin, \xc2\xa7901(b) is unmistakably aimed at controlling how those actions must end\xe2\x80\x9d). Unlike Rule 23, designed to further procedural fairness and efficiency, \xc2\xa7901(b) (we are told) \xe2\x80\x9cresponds to an entirely different concern\xe2\x80\x9d: the fear that allowing statutory damages to be awarded on a class-wide basis would \xe2\x80\x9cproduce overkill.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 12, 9 (internal quotation marks omitted). The dissent reaches this conclusion on the basis of (1) constituent concern recorded in the law\xe2\x80\x99s bill jacket; (2) a commentary suggesting that the Legislature \xe2\x80\x9capparently fear[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d that combining class actions and statutory penalties \xe2\x80\x9ccould result in annihilating punishment of the defendant,\xe2\x80\x9d V. Alexander, Practice Commentaries, C901:11, reprinted in 7B McKinney\xe2\x80\x99s Consolidated Laws of New York Ann., p. 104 (2006) (internal quotation marks omitted); (3) a remark by the Governor in his signing statement that \xc2\xa7901(b) \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0provides a controlled remedy,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 9 (quoting Memorandum on Approving L. 1975, Ch. 207, reprinted in 1975 N.\xc2\xa0Y. Laws, at 1748; emphasis deleted), and (4) a state court\xe2\x80\x99s statement that the final text of \xc2\xa7901(b) \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98was the result of a compromise among competing interests,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 9 (quoting Sperry v. Crompton Corp., 8 N.\xc2\xa0Y. 3d 204, 211, 863 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 1012, 1015 (2007)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This evidence of the New York Legislature\xe2\x80\x99s purpose is pretty sparse. But even accepting the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s account of the Legislature\xe2\x80\x99s objective at face value, it cannot override the statute\xe2\x80\x99s clear text. Even if its aim is to restrict the remedy a plaintiff can obtain, \xc2\xa7901(b) achieves that end by limiting a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s power to maintain a class action. The manner in which the law \xe2\x80\x9ccould have been written,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 23, has no bearing; what matters is the law the Legislature did enact. We cannot rewrite that to reflect our perception of legislative purpose, see Oncale v. Sundowner Offshore Services, Inc., 523 U. S. 75, 79\xe2\x80\x9380 (1998).[Footnote 6] The dissent\xe2\x80\x99s concern for state prerogatives is frustrated rather than furthered by revising state laws when a potential conflict with a Federal Rule arises; the state-friendly approach would be to accept the law as written and test the validity of the Federal Rule.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dissent\xe2\x80\x99s approach of determining whether state and federal rules conflict based on the subjective intentions of the state legislature is an enterprise destined to produce \xe2\x80\x9cconfusion worse confounded,\xe2\x80\x9d Sibbach v. Wilson & Co., 312 U. S. 1, 14 (1941). It would mean, to begin with, that one State\xe2\x80\x99s statute could survive pre-emption (and accordingly affect the procedures in federal court) while another State\xe2\x80\x99s identical law would not, merely because its authors had different aspirations. It would also mean that district courts would have to discern, in every diversity case, the purpose behind any putatively pre-empted state procedural rule, even if its text squarely conflicts with federal law. That task will often prove arduous. Many laws further more than one aim, and the aim of others may be impossible to discern. Moreover, to the extent the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s purpose-driven approach depends on its characterization of \xc2\xa7901(b)\xe2\x80\x99s aims as substantive, it would apply to many state rules ostensibly addressed to procedure. Pleading standards, for example, often embody policy preferences about the types of claims that should succeed\xe2\x80\x94as do rules governing summary judgment, pretrial discovery, and the admissibility of certain evidence. Hard cases will abound. It is not even clear that a state supreme court\xe2\x80\x99s pronouncement of the law\xe2\x80\x99s purpose would settle the issue, since existence of the factual predicate for avoiding federal pre-emption is ultimately a federal question. Predictably, federal judges would be condemned to poring through state legislative history\xe2\x80\x94which may be less easily obtained, less thorough, and less familiar than its federal counterpart, see R. Mersky & D. Dunn, Fundamentals of Legal Research 233 (8th ed. 2002); Torres & Windsor, State Legislative Histories: A Select, Annotated Bibliography, 85 L. Lib. J. 545, 547 (1993).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But while the dissent does indeed artificially narrow the scope of \xc2\xa7901(b) by finding that it pursues only substantive policies, that is not the central difficulty of the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s position. The central difficulty is that even artificial narrowing cannot render \xc2\xa7901(b) compatible with Rule 23. Whatever the policies they pursue, they flatly contradict each other. Allstate asserts (and the dissent implies, see post, at 3, 11) that we can (and must) interpret Rule 23 in a manner that avoids overstepping its authorizing statute.[Footnote 7] If the Rule were susceptible of two meanings\xe2\x80\x94one that would violate \xc2\xa72072(b) and another that would not\xe2\x80\x94we would agree. See Ortiz v. Fibreboard Corp., 527 U. S. 815, 842, 845 (1999); cf. Semtek Int\xe2\x80\x99l Inc. v. Lockheed Martin Corp., 531 U. S. 497, 503\xe2\x80\x93504 (2001). But it is not. Rule 23 unambiguously authorizes any plaintiff, in any federal civil proceeding, to maintain a class action if the Rule\xe2\x80\x99s prerequisites are met. We cannot contort its text, even to avert a collision with state law that might render it invalid. See Walker v. Armco Steel Corp., 446 U. S. 740, 750, n. 9 (1980).[Footnote 8] What the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s approach achieves is not the avoiding of a \xe2\x80\x9cconflict between Rule 23 and \xc2\xa7901(b),\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 17, but rather the invalidation of Rule 23 (pursuant to \xc2\xa72072(b) of the Rules Enabling Act) to the extent that it conflicts with the substantive policies of \xc2\xa7901. There is no other way to reach the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s destination. We must therefore confront head-on whether Rule 23 falls within the statutory authorization.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Erie involved the constitutional power of federal courts to supplant state law with judge-made rules. In that context, it made no difference whether the rule was technically one of substance or procedure; the touchstone was whether it \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly affect[s] the result of a litigation.\xe2\x80\x9d Guaranty Trust Co. v. York, 326 U. S. 99, 109 (1945). That is not the test for either the constitutionality or the statutory validity of a Federal Rule of Procedure. Congress has undoubted power to supplant state law, and undoubted power to prescribe rules for the courts it has created, so long as those rules regulate matters \xe2\x80\x9crationally capable of classification\xe2\x80\x9d as procedure. Hanna, 380 U. S., at 472. In the Rules Enabling Act, Congress authorized this Court to promulgate rules of procedure subject to its review, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72072(a), but with the limitation that those rules \xe2\x80\x9cshall not abridge, enlarge or modify any substantive right,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72072(b).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have long held that this limitation means that the Rule must \xe2\x80\x9creally regulat[e] procedure,\xe2\x80\x94the judicial process for enforcing rights and duties recognized by substantive law and for justly administering remedy and redress for disregard or infraction of them,\xe2\x80\x9d Sibbach, 312 U. S., at 14; see Hanna, supra, at 464; Burlington, 480 U. S., at 8. The test is not whether the rule affects a litigant\xe2\x80\x99s substantive rights; most procedural rules do. Mississippi Publishing Corp. v. Murphree, 326 U. S. 438, 445 (1946). What matters is what the rule itself regulates: If it governs only \xe2\x80\x9cthe manner and the means\xe2\x80\x9d by which the litigants\xe2\x80\x99 rights are \xe2\x80\x9cenforced,\xe2\x80\x9d it is valid; if it alters \xe2\x80\x9cthe rules of decision by which [the] court will adjudicate [those] rights,\xe2\x80\x9d it is not. Id., at 446 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Applying that test, we have rejected every statutory challenge to a Federal Rule that has come before us. We have found to be in compliance with \xc2\xa72072(b) rules prescribing methods for serving process, see id., at 445\xe2\x80\x93446 (Fed. Rule Civ. Proc.\xc2\xa04(f)); Hanna, supra, at 463\xe2\x80\x93465 (Fed. Rule Civ. Proc.\xc2\xa04(d)(1)), and requiring litigants whose mental or physical condition is in dispute to submit to examinations, see Sibbach, supra, at 14\xe2\x80\x9316 (Fed. Rule Civ. Proc.\xc2\xa035); Schlagenhauf v. Holder, 379 U. S. 104, 113\xe2\x80\x93114 (1964) (same). Likewise, we have upheld rules authorizing imposition of sanctions upon those who file frivolous appeals, see Burlington, supra, at 8 (Fed. Rule App. Proc.\xc2\xa038), or who sign court papers without a reasonable inquiry into the facts asserted, see Business Guides, Inc. v. Chromatic Communications Enterprises, Inc., 498 U. S. 533, 551\xe2\x80\x93554 (1991) (Fed. Rule Civ. Proc.\xc2\xa011). Each of these rules had some practical effect on the parties\xe2\x80\x99 rights, but each undeniably regulated only the process for enforcing those rights; none altered the rights themselves, the available remedies, or the rules of decision by which the court adjudicated either.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Applying that criterion, we think it obvious that rules allowing multiple claims (and claims by or against multiple parties) to be litigated together are also valid. See, e.g., Fed. Rules Civ. Proc.\xc2\xa018 (joinder of claims), 20 (joinder of parties), 42(a) (consolidation of actions). Such rules neither change plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 separate entitlements to relief nor abridge defendants\xe2\x80\x99 rights; they alter only how the claims are processed. For the same reason, Rule 23\xe2\x80\x94at least insofar as it allows willing plaintiffs to join their separate claims against the same defendants in a class action\xe2\x80\x94falls within \xc2\xa72072(b)\xe2\x80\x99s authorization. A class action, no less than traditional joinder (of which it is a species), merely enables a federal court to adjudicate claims of multiple parties at once, instead of in separate suits. And like traditional joinder, it leaves the parties\xe2\x80\x99 legal rights and duties intact and the rules of decision unchanged.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Allstate contends that the authorization of class actions is not substantively neutral: Allowing Shady Grove to sue on behalf of a class \xe2\x80\x9ctransform[s] [the] dispute over a five hundred dollar penalty into a dispute over a five million dollar penalty.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 1. Allstate\xe2\x80\x99s aggregate liability, however, does not depend on whether the suit proceeds as a class action. Each of the 1,000-plus members of the putative class could (as Allstate acknowledges) bring a freestanding suit asserting his individual claim. It is undoubtedly true that some plaintiffs who would not bring individual suits for the relatively small sums involved will choose to join a class action. That has no bearing, however, on Allstate\xe2\x80\x99s or the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 legal rights. The likelihood that some (even many) plaintiffs will be induced to sue by the availability of a class action is just the sort of \xe2\x80\x9cincidental effec[t]\xe2\x80\x9d we have long held does not violate \xc2\xa72072(b), Mississippi Publishing, supra,\nat 445.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Allstate argues that Rule 23 violates \xc2\xa72072(b) because the state law it displaces, \xc2\xa7901(b), creates a right that\nthe Federal Rule abridges\xe2\x80\x94namely, a \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive right\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0not to be subjected to aggregated class-action liability\xe2\x80\x9d in a single suit. Brief for Respondent 31. To begin with, we doubt that that is so. Nothing in the text of \xc2\xa7901(b) (which is to be found in New York\xe2\x80\x99s procedural code) confines it to claims under New York law; and of course New York has no power to alter substantive rights and duties created by other sovereigns. As we have said, the consequence of excluding certain class actions may be to cap the damages a defendant can face in a single suit, but the law itself alters only procedure. In that respect, \xc2\xa7901(b) is no different from a state law forbidding simple joinder. As a fallback argument, Allstate argues that even if \xc2\xa7901(b) is a procedural provision, it was enacted \xe2\x80\x9cfor substantive reasons,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 24 (emphasis added). Its end was not to improve \xe2\x80\x9cthe conduct of the litigation process itself\xe2\x80\x9d but to alter \xe2\x80\x9cthe outcome of that process.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 26.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The fundamental difficulty with both these arguments is that the substantive nature of New York\xe2\x80\x99s law, or its substantive purpose, makes no difference. A Federal Rule of Procedure is not valid in some jurisdictions and invalid in others\xe2\x80\x94or valid in some cases and invalid in others\xe2\x80\x94depending upon whether its effect is to frustrate a state substantive law (or a state procedural law enacted for substantive purposes). That could not be clearer in\nSibbach:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe petitioner says the phrase [\xe2\x80\x98substantive rights\xe2\x80\x99 in the Rules Enabling Act] connotes more; that by its use Congress intended that in regulating procedure this Court should not deal with important and substantial rights theretofore recognized. Recognized where and by whom? The state courts are divided as to the power in the absence of statute to order a physical examination. In a number such an order is authorized by statute or rule. \xe2\x80\xa6

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cThe asserted right, moreover, is no more important than many others enjoyed by litigants in District Courts sitting in the several states before the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure altered and abolished old rights or privileges and created new ones in connection with the conduct of litigation. \xe2\x80\xa6 If we were to adopt the suggested criterion of the importance of the alleged right we should invite endless litigation and confusion worse confounded. The test must be whether a rule really regulates procedure . \xe2\x80\xa6\xe2\x80\x9d 312 U. S., at 13\xe2\x80\x9314 (footnotes omitted).

\n

\nHanna unmistakably expressed the same understanding that compliance of a Federal Rule with the Enabling Act is to be assessed by consulting the Rule itself, and not its effects in individual applications:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he court has been instructed to apply the Federal Rule, and can refuse to do so only if the Advisory Committee, this Court, and Congress erred in their prima facie judgment that the Rule in question transgresses neither the terms of the Enabling Act nor constitutional restrictions.\xe2\x80\x9d 380 U. S., at 471.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, it is not the substantive or procedural nature or purpose of the affected state law that matters, but the substantive or procedural nature of the Federal Rule. We have held since Sibbach, and reaffirmed repeatedly, that the validity of a Federal Rule depends entirely upon whether it regulates procedure. See Sibbach, supra, at 14; Hanna, supra, at 464; Burlington, 480 U. S., at 8. If it does, it is authorized by \xc2\xa72072 and is valid in all jurisdictions, with respect to all claims, regardless of its incidental effect upon state-created rights.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A few words in response to the concurrence. We understand it to accept the framework we apply\xe2\x80\x94which requires first, determining whether the federal and state rules can be reconciled (because they answer different questions), and second, if they cannot, determining whether the Federal Rule runs afoul of \xc2\xa72072(b). Post, at 5\xe2\x80\x937 (Stevens, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment). The concurrence agrees with us that Rule 23 and \xc2\xa7901(b) conflict, post, at 15\xe2\x80\x9316, and departs from us only with respect to the second part of the test, i.e., whether application of the Federal Rule violates \xc2\xa72072(b), post, at 7\xe2\x80\x9313. Like us, it answers no, but for a reason different from ours. Post, at 17\xe2\x80\x9322.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The concurrence would decide this case on the basis, not that Rule 23 is procedural, but that the state law it displaces is procedural, in the sense that it does not \xe2\x80\x9cfunction as a part of the State\xe2\x80\x99s definition of substantive rights and remedies.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 1. A state procedural rule is not preempted, according to the concurrence, so long\nas it is \xe2\x80\x9cso bound up with,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9csufficiently intertwined with,\xe2\x80\x9d a substantive state-law right or remedy \xe2\x80\x9cthat it defines the scope of that substantive right or remedy,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 4, 13.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This analysis squarely conflicts with Sibbach, which established the rule we apply. The concurrence contends that Sibbach did not rule out its approach, but that is not so. Recognizing the impracticability of a test that turns on the idiosyncrasies of state law, Sibbach adopted and applied a rule with a single criterion: whether the Federal Rule \xe2\x80\x9creally regulates procedure.\xe2\x80\x9d 312 U. S., at 14.[Footnote 9] That the concurrence\xe2\x80\x99s approach would have yielded the same result in Sibbach proves nothing; what matters is the rule we did apply, and that rule leaves no room for special exemptions based on the function or purpose of a particular state rule.[Footnote 10] We have rejected an attempt to read into Sibbach an exception with no basis in the opinion, see Schlagenhauf, 379 U. S., at 113\xe2\x80\x93114, and we see no reason to find such an implied limitation today.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In reality, the concurrence seeks not to apply Sibbach, but to overrule it (or, what is the same, to rewrite it). Its approach, the concurrence insists, gives short shrift to the statutory text forbidding the Federal Rules from \xe2\x80\x9cabridg[ing], enlarg[ing], or modify[ing] any substantive right,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72072(b). See post, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310. There is something to that. It is possible to understand how it can be determined whether a Federal Rule \xe2\x80\x9cenlarges\xe2\x80\x9d substantive rights without consulting State law: If the Rule creates a substantive right, even one that duplicates some state-created rights, it establishes a new federal right. But it is hard to understand how it can be determined whether a Federal Rule \xe2\x80\x9cabridges\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cmodifies\xe2\x80\x9d substantive rights without knowing what state-created rights would obtain if the Federal Rule did not exist. Sibbach\xe2\x80\x99s exclusive focus on the challenged Federal Rule\xe2\x80\x94driven by the very real concern that Federal Rules which vary from State to State would be chaos, see 312 U. S., at 13\xe2\x80\x9314\xe2\x80\x94is hard to square with \xc2\xa72072(b)\xe2\x80\x99s terms.[Footnote 11]

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Sibbach has been settled law, however, for nearly seven decades.[Footnote 12] Setting aside any precedent requires a \xe2\x80\x9cspecial justification\xe2\x80\x9d beyond a bare belief that it was wrong. Patterson v. McLean Credit Union, 491 U. S. 164, 172 (1989) (internal quotation marks omitted). And a party seeking to overturn a statutory precedent bears an even greater burden, since Congress remains free to correct us, ibid., and adhering to our precedent enables it do so, see, e.g., Finley v. United States, 490 U. S. 545, 556 (1989); 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71367; Exxon Mobil Corp. v. Allapattah Services, Inc., 545 U. S. 546, 558 (2005). We do Congress no service by presenting it a moving target. In all events, Allstate has not even asked us to overrule Sibbach, let alone carried its burden of persuading us to do so. Cf. IBP, Inc. v. Alvarez, 546 U. S. 21, 32 (2005). Why we should cast aside our decades-old decision escapes us, especially since (as the concurrence explains) that would not affect the result.[Footnote 13]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The concurrence also contends that applying Sibbach and assessing whether a Federal Rule regulates substance or procedure is not always easy. See post, at 11, n.\xc2\xa010. Undoubtedly some hard cases will arise (though we have managed to muddle through well enough in the 69 years since Sibbach was decided). But as the concurrence acknowledges, post, at 11, the basic difficulty is unavoidable: The statute itself refers to \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive right[s],\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72072(b), so there is no escaping the substance-procedure distinction. What is more, the concurrence\xe2\x80\x99s approach does nothing to diminish the difficulty, but rather magnifies it many times over. Instead of a single hard question of whether a Federal Rule regulates substance or procedure, that approach will present hundreds of hard questions, forcing federal courts to assess the substantive or procedural character of countless state rules that may conflict with a single Federal Rule.[Footnote 14] And it still does not sidestep the problem it seeks to avoid. At the end of the day, one must come face to face with the decision whether or not the state policy (with which a putatively procedural state rule may be \xe2\x80\x9cbound up\xe2\x80\x9d) pertains to a \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive right or remedy,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 19\xe2\x80\x94that is, whether it is substance or procedure.[Footnote 15] The more one explores the alternatives to Sibbach\xe2\x80\x99s rule, the more its wisdom becomes apparent.

\n

D

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We must acknowledge the reality that keeping the federal-court door open to class actions that cannot proceed in state court will produce forum shopping. That is unacceptable when it comes as the consequence of judge-made rules created to fill supposed \xe2\x80\x9cgaps\xe2\x80\x9d in positive federal law. See Hanna, 380 U. S., at 471\xe2\x80\x93472. For where neither the Constitution, a treaty, nor a statute provides the rule of decision or authorizes a federal court to supply one, \xe2\x80\x9cstate law must govern because there can be no other law.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; see Clark, Erie\xe2\x80\x99s Constitutional Source, 95 Cal. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1289, 1302, 1311 (2007). But divergence from state law, with the attendant consequence of forum shopping, is the inevitable (indeed, one might say the intended) result of a uniform system of federal procedure. Congress itself has created the possibility that the same case may follow a different course if filed in federal instead of state court. Cf. Hanna, 380 U. S., at 472\xe2\x80\x93473. The short of the matter is that a Federal Rule governing procedure is valid whether or not it alters the outcome of the case in a way that induces forum shopping. To hold otherwise would be to \xe2\x80\x9cdisembowel either the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s grant of power over federal procedure\xe2\x80\x9d or Congress\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of it. Id., at 473\xe2\x80\x93474.

Rule 3, we explained, \xe2\x80\x9cgoverns the date from which various timing requirements of the Federal Rules begin to run, but does not affect state statutes of limitations\xe2\x80\x9d or tolling rules, which it did not \xe2\x80\x9cpurpor[t] to displace.\xe2\x80\x9d 446 U. S., at 751, 750. The texts were therefore not in conflict. While our opinion observed that the State\xe2\x80\x99s actual-service rule was (in the State\xe2\x80\x99s judgment) an \xe2\x80\x9cintegral part of the several policies served by the statute of limitations,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 751, nothing in our decision suggested that a federal court may resolve an obvious conflict between the texts of state and federal rules by resorting to the state law\xe2\x80\x99s ostensible objectives.

\nFootnote 7

\xc2\xa0The dissent also suggests that we should read the Federal Rules \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98with sensitivity to important state interests\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98to avoid conflict with important state regulatory policies.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 7 (quoting Gasperini v. Center for Humanities, Inc., 518 U. S. 415, 427, n.\xc2\xa07, 438, n. 22 (1996)). The search for state interests and policies that are \xe2\x80\x9cimportant\xe2\x80\x9d is just as standardless as the \xe2\x80\x9cimportant or substantial\xe2\x80\x9d criterion we rejected in Sibbach v. Wilson & Co., 312 U. S. 1, 13\xe2\x80\x9314 (1941), to define the state-created rights a Federal Rule may not abridge.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If all the dissent means is that we should read an ambiguous Federal Rule to avoid \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial variations [in outcomes] between state and federal litigation,\xe2\x80\x9d Semtek Int\xe2\x80\x99l Inc. v. Lockheed Martin Corp., 531 U. S. 497, 504 (2001) (internal quotation marks omitted), we entirely agree. We should do so not to avoid doubt as to the Rule\xe2\x80\x99s validity\xe2\x80\x94since a Federal Rule that fails Erie\xe2\x80\x99s forum-shopping test is not ipso facto invalid, see Hanna v. Plumer, 380 U. S. 460, 469\xe2\x80\x93472 (1965)\xe2\x80\x94but because it is reasonable to assume that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress is just as concerned as we have been to avoid significant differences between state and federal courts in adjudicating claims,\xe2\x80\x9d Stewart Organization, Inc. v. Ricoh Corp., 487 U. S. 22, 37\xe2\x80\x9338 (1988) (Scalia, J., dissenting). The assumption is irrelevant here, however, because there is only one reasonable reading of Rule 23.

\nFootnote 8

\xc2\xa0The cases chronicled by the dissent, see post, at 3\xe2\x80\x938, each involved a Federal Rule that we concluded could fairly be read not to \xe2\x80\x9ccontrol the issue\xe2\x80\x9d addressed by the pertinent state law, thus avoiding a \xe2\x80\x9cdirect collision\xe2\x80\x9d between federal and state law, Walker, 446 U. S., at 749 (internal quotation marks omitted). But here, as in Hanna, supra, at 470, a collision is \xe2\x80\x9cunavoidable.\xe2\x80\x9d

\nFootnote 9

\xc2\xa0The concurrence claims that in Sibbach \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Court\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0had no occasion to consider whether the particular application of the Federal Rules in question would offend the Enabling Act.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 12. Had Sibbach been applying the concurrence\xe2\x80\x99s theory, that is quite true\xe2\x80\x94which demonstrates how inconsistent that theory is with Sibbach. For conformity with the Rules Enabling Act was the very issue Sibbach decided: The petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s position was that Rules 35 and 37 exceeded the Enabling Act\xe2\x80\x99s authorization, 312 U. S., at 9, 13; the Court faced and rejected that argument, id., at 13\xe2\x80\x9316, and proceeded to reverse\nthe lower court for failing to apply Rule 37 correctly, id., at 16. There could not be a clearer rejection of the theory that the concurrence now advocates.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The concurrence responds that the \xe2\x80\x9cthe specific question of \xe2\x80\x98the obligation of federal courts to apply the substantive law of a state\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d was not before the Court, post, at 12 (quoting Sibbach, supra, at 9). It is clear from the context, however, that this passage referred to the Erie prohibition of court-created rules that displace state law. The opinion unquestionably dealt with the Federal Rules\xe2\x80\x99 compliance with \xc2\xa72072(b), and it adopted the standard we apply here to resolve the question, which does not depend on whether individual applications of the Rule abridge or modify state-law rights. See 312 U. S., at 13\xe2\x80\x9314. To the extent Sibbach did not address the Federal Rules\xe2\x80\x99 validity vis-\xc3\x80-vis contrary state law, Hanna surely did, see 380 U. S., at 472, and it made clear that Sibbach\xe2\x80\x99s test still controls, see 380 U. S., at 464\xe2\x80\x93465,\n470\xe2\x80\x93471.

\nFootnote 10

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The concurrence insists that we have misread Sibbach, since surely a Federal Rule that \xe2\x80\x9cin most cases\xe2\x80\x9d regulates procedure does not do so when it displaces one of those \xe2\x80\x9crare\xe2\x80\x9d state substantive laws that are disguised as rules of procedure. Post, at 13, n.\xc2\xa013. This mistakes what the Federal Rule regulates for its incidental effects. As we have explained, supra, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313, most Rules have some effect on litigants\xe2\x80\x99 substantive rights or their ability to obtain a remedy, but that does not mean the Rule itself regulates those rights or remedies.

\nFootnote 11

\xc2\xa0The concurrence\xe2\x80\x99s approach, however, is itself unfaithful to the statute\xe2\x80\x99s terms. Section 2072(b) bans abridgement or modification only of \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive rights,\xe2\x80\x9d but the concurrence would prohibit pre-emption of \xe2\x80\x9cprocedural rules that are intimately bound up in the scope of a substantive right or remedy,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 19. This would allow States to force a wide array of parochial procedures on federal courts so long as they are \xe2\x80\x9csufficiently intertwined with a state right or remedy.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 13.

\nFootnote 12

\xc2\xa0The concurrence implies that Sibbach has slipped into desuetude, apparently for lack of sufficient citations. See post, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314, n.\xc2\xa014. We are unaware of any rule to the effect that a holding of ours expires if the case setting it forth is not periodically revalidated. In any event, the concurrence\xe2\x80\x99s account of our shunning of Sibbach is greatly exaggerated. Hanna did not merely cite the case, but recognized it as establishing the governing rule. 380 U. S., at 464\xe2\x80\x93465, 470\xe2\x80\x93471. Mississippi Publishing Corp. v. Murphree, 326 U. S. 438, 445\xe2\x80\x93446 (1946), likewise cited Sibbach and applied the same test, examining the Federal Rule, not the state law it displaced. True, Burlington Northern R. Co. v. Woods, 480 U. S. 1 (1987), and for that matter Business Guides, Inc. v. Chromatic Communications Enterprises, Inc., 498 U. S. 533 (1991), did not cite Sibbach. But both cited and followed Hanna\xe2\x80\x94which as noted held out Sibbach as setting forth the governing rule. See Burlington Northern, supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x936, 8; Business Guides, supra, at 552\xe2\x80\x93554. Thus, while Sibbach itself may appear infrequently in the U. S. Reports, its rule\xe2\x80\x94and in particular its focus on the Federal Rule as the proper unit of analysis\xe2\x80\x94is alive and well.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In contrast, Hanna\xe2\x80\x99s obscure obiter dictum that a court \xe2\x80\x9cneed not wholly blind itself\xe2\x80\x9d to a Federal Rule\xe2\x80\x99s effect on a case\xe2\x80\x99s outcome, 380 U. S., at 473\xe2\x80\x94which the concurrence invokes twice, post, at 8, 13\xe2\x80\x9314, n.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x94has never resurfaced in our opinions in the 45 years since its first unfortunate utterance. Nor does it cast doubt on Sibbach\xe2\x80\x99s straightforward test: As the concurrence notes, Hanna cited Sibbach for that statement, 380 U. S., at 473, showing it saw no inconsistency between the two.

\nFootnote 13

\xc2\xa0The concurrence is correct, post, at 10, n.\xc2\xa09, that under our disposition any rule that \xe2\x80\x9creally regulates\xc2\xa0procedure,\xe2\x80\x9d Sibbach, supra, at 14, will pre-empt a conflicting state rule, however \xe2\x80\x9cbound up\xe2\x80\x9d the latter is with substantive law. The concurrence is wrong, however, that that result proves our interpretation of \xc2\xa72072(b) implausible, post, at 10, n.\xc2\xa09. The result is troubling only if one stretches the term \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive rights\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa72072(b) to mean not only state-law rights themselves, but also any state-law procedures closely connected to them. Neither the text nor our precedent supports that expansive interpretation. The examples the concurrence offers\xe2\x80\x94statutes of limitations, burdens of proof, and standards for appellate review of damages awards\xe2\x80\x94do not make its broad definition of substantive rights more persuasive. They merely illustrate that in rare cases it may be difficult to determine whether a rule \xe2\x80\x9creally regulates\xe2\x80\x9d procedure or substance. If one concludes the latter, there is no pre-emption of the state rule; the Federal Rule itself is invalid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The concurrence\xe2\x80\x99s concern would make more sense if many Federal Rules that effectively alter state-law rights \xe2\x80\x9cbound up with procedures\xe2\x80\x9d would survive under Sibbach. But as the concurrence concedes, post, at 11, n.\xc2\xa010, very few would do so. The possible existence of a few outlier instances does not prove Sibbach\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation is absurd. Congress may well have accepted such anomalies as the price of a uniform system of federal procedure.

\nFootnote 14

\xc2\xa0The concurrence argues that its approach is no more \xe2\x80\x9ctaxing\xe2\x80\x9d than ours because few if any Federal Rules that are \xe2\x80\x9cfacially valid\xe2\x80\x9d under the Enabling Act will fail the concurrence\xe2\x80\x99s test. Post, at 11, and n.\xc2\xa010. But that conclusion will be reached only after federal courts have considered hundreds of state rules applying the concurrence\xe2\x80\x99s inscrutable standard.

\nFootnote 15

\xc2\xa0The concurrence insists that the task will be easier if courts can \xe2\x80\x9cconside[r] the nature and functions of the state law,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 11, n.\xc2\xa010, regardless of the law\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cform,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 4 (emphasis deleted), i.e., what the law actually says. We think that amorphous inquiry into the \xe2\x80\x9cnature and functions\xe2\x80\x9d of a state law will tend to increase, rather than decrease, the difficulty of classifying Federal Rules as substantive or procedural. Walking through the concurrence\xe2\x80\x99s application of its test to \xc2\xa7901(b), post, at 17\xe2\x80\x9322, gives little reason to hope that its approach will lighten the burden for lower courts.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The New York law at issue, N.\xc2\xa0Y. Civ. Prac. Law Ann. (CPLR) \xc2\xa7901(b) (West 2006), is a procedural rule that is not part of New York\xe2\x80\x99s substantive law. Accordingly, I agree with Justice Scalia that Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23 must apply in this case and join Parts I and II\xe2\x80\x93A of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion. But I also agree with Justice Ginsburg that there are some state procedural rules that federal courts must apply in diversity cases because they function as a part of the State\xe2\x80\x99s definition of substantive rights and remedies.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is a long-recognized principle that federal courts sitting in diversity \xe2\x80\x9capply state substantive law and federal procedural law.\xe2\x80\x9d Hanna v. Plumer, 380 U. S. 460, 465 (1965).[Footnote 1] This principle is governed by a statutory framework, and the way that it is administered varies depending upon whether there is a federal rule addressed to the matter. See id., at 469\xe2\x80\x93472. If no federal rule applies, a federal court must follow the Rules of Decision Act, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71652, and make the \xe2\x80\x9crelatively unguided Erie choice,\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 2] Hanna, 380 U. S., at 471, to determine whether the state law is the \xe2\x80\x9crule of decision.\xe2\x80\x9d But when a situation is covered by a federal rule, the Rules of Decision Act inquiry by its own terms does not apply. See \xc2\xa71652; Hanna, 380 U. S., at 471. Instead, the Rules Enabling Act (Enabling Act) controls. See 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72072.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That does not mean, however, that the federal rule always governs. Congress has provided for a system of uniform federal rules, see ibid., under which federal courts sitting in diversity operate as \xe2\x80\x9can independent system for administering justice to litigants who properly invoke its jurisdiction,\xe2\x80\x9d Byrd v. Blue Ridge Rural Elec. Cooperative, Inc., 356 U. S. 525, 537 (1958), and not as state-court clones that assume all aspects of state tribunals but are managed by Article III judges. See Hanna, 380 U. S., at 473\xe2\x80\x93474. But while Congress may have the constitutional power to prescribe procedural rules that interfere with state substantive law in any number of respects, that is not what Congress has done. Instead, it has provided in the Enabling Act that although \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x9d may \xe2\x80\x9cprescribe general rules of practice and procedure,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72072(a), those rules \xe2\x80\x9cshall not abridge, enlarge or modify any substantive right,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72072(b). Therefore, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen a situation is covered by one of the Federal Rules, \xe2\x80\xa6 the court has been instructed to apply the Federal Rule\xe2\x80\x9d unless doing so would violate the Act or the Constitution. Hanna, 380 U. S., at 471.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although the Enabling Act and the Rules of Decision Act \xe2\x80\x9csay, roughly, that federal courts are to apply state \xe2\x80\x98substantive\xe2\x80\x99 law and federal \xe2\x80\x98procedural\xe2\x80\x99 law,\xe2\x80\x9d the inquiries are not the same. Ibid.; see also id., at 469\xe2\x80\x93470. The Enabling Act does not invite federal courts to engage in the \xe2\x80\x9crelatively unguided Erie choice,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 471, but instead instructs only that federal rules cannot \xe2\x80\x9cabridge, enlarge or modify any substantive right,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72072(b). The Enabling Act\xe2\x80\x99s limitation does not mean that federal rules cannot displace state policy judgments; it means only that federal rules cannot displace a State\xe2\x80\x99s definition of its own rights or remedies. See Sibbach v. Wilson & Co., 312 U. S. 1, 13\xe2\x80\x9314 (1941) (reasoning that \xe2\x80\x9cthe phrase \xe2\x80\x98substantive rights\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d embraces only those state rights that are sought to be enforced in the judicial proceedings).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress has thus struck a balance: \xe2\x80\x9c[H]ousekeeping rules for federal courts\xe2\x80\x9d will generally apply in diversity cases, notwithstanding that some federal rules \xe2\x80\x9cwill inevitably differ\xe2\x80\x9d from state rules. Hanna, 380 U. S., at 473. But not every federal \xe2\x80\x9crul[e] of practice or procedure,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72072(a), will displace state law. To the contrary, federal rules must be interpreted with some degree of \xe2\x80\x9csensitivity to important state interests and regulatory policies,\xe2\x80\x9d Gasperini v. Center for Humanities, Inc., 518 U. S. 415, 427, n.\xc2\xa07 (1996), and applied to diversity cases against the background of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 command that such rules not alter substantive rights and with consideration of \xe2\x80\x9cthe degree to which the Rule makes the character and result of the federal litigation stray from the course it would follow in state courts,\xe2\x80\x9d Hanna, 380 U. S., at 473. This can be a tricky balance to implement.[Footnote 3]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is important to observe that the balance Congress has struck turns, in part, on the nature of the state law that is being displaced by a federal rule. And in my view, the application of that balance does not necessarily turn on whether the state law at issue takes the form of what is traditionally described as substantive or procedural. Rather, it turns on whether the state law actually is part of a State\xe2\x80\x99s framework of substantive rights or remedies. See \xc2\xa72072(b); cf. Hanna, 380 U. S., at 471 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe line between \xe2\x80\x98substance\xe2\x80\x99 and \xe2\x80\x98procedure\xe2\x80\x99 shifts as the legal context changes\xe2\x80\x9d); Guaranty Trust Co. v. York, 326 U. S. 99, 108 (1945) (noting that the words \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98substance\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98procedure\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ach impl[y] different variables depending upon the particular problem for which [they] are used\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\nApplying this balance, therefore, requires careful interpretation of the state and federal provisions at issue. \xe2\x80\x9cThe line between procedural and substantive law is hazy,\xe2\x80\x9d Erie R. Co. v. Tompkins, 304 U. S. 64, 92 (1938) (Reed, J., concurring), and matters of procedure and matters of substance are not \xe2\x80\x9cmutually exclusive categories with easily ascertainable contents,\xe2\x80\x9d Sibbach, 312 U. S., at 17 (Frankfurter, J., dissenting). Rather, \xe2\x80\x9c[r]ules which lawyers call procedural do not always exhaust their effect by regulating procedure,\xe2\x80\x9d Cohen v. Beneficial Industrial Loan Corp., 337 U. S. 541, 555 (1949), and in some situations, \xe2\x80\x9cprocedure and substance are so interwoven that rational separation becomes well-nigh impossible,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 559 (Rutledge, J., dissenting). A \xe2\x80\x9cstate procedural rule, though undeniably \xe2\x80\x98procedural\xe2\x80\x99 in the ordinary sense of the term,\xe2\x80\x9d may exist \xe2\x80\x9cto influence substantive outcomes,\xe2\x80\x9d S. A. Healy Co. v. Milwaukee Metropolitan Sewerage Dist., 60 F. 3d 305, 310 (CA7 1995) (Posner, J.), and may in some instances become so bound up with the state-created right or remedy that it defines the scope of that substantive right or remedy. Such laws, for example, may be seemingly procedural rules that make it significantly more difficult to bring or to prove a claim, thus serving to limit the scope of that claim. See, e.g, Cohen, 337 U. S., at 555 (state \xe2\x80\x9cprocedure\xe2\x80\x9d that required plaintiffs to post bond before suing); Guaranty Trust Co., 326 U. S. 99 (state statute of limitations).[Footnote 4] Such \xe2\x80\x9cprocedural rules\xe2\x80\x9d may also define the amount of recovery. See, e.g., Gasperini, 518 U. S., at 427 (state procedure for examining jury verdicts as means of capping the available remedy); Moore \xc2\xa7124.07[3][a] (listing examples of federal courts\xe2\x80\x99 applying state laws that affect the amount of a judgment).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In our federalist system, Congress has not mandated that federal courts dictate to state legislatures the form that their substantive law must take. And were federal courts to ignore those portions of substantive state law that operate as procedural devices, it could in many instances limit the ways that sovereign States may define their rights and remedies. When a State chooses to use a traditionally procedural vehicle as a means of defining the scope of substantive rights or remedies, federal courts must recognize and respect that choice. Cf. Ragan v. Merchants Transfer & Warehouse Co., 337 U. S. 530, 533 (1949) (\xe2\x80\x9cSince th[e] cause of action is created by local law, the measure of it is to be found only in local law\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. Where local law qualifies or abridges it, the federal court must follow suit\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

II

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When both a federal rule and a state law appear to govern a question before a federal court sitting in diversity, our precedents have set out a two-step framework for federal courts to negotiate this thorny area. At both steps of the inquiry, there is a critical question about what the state law and the federal rule mean.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The court must first determine whether the scope of the federal rule is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98sufficiently broad\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98control the issue\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d before the court, \xe2\x80\x9cthereby leaving no room for the operation\xe2\x80\x9d of seemingly conflicting state law. See Burlington Northern R. Co. v. Woods, 480 U. S. 1, 4\xe2\x80\x935 (1987); Walker v. Armco Steel Corp., 446 U. S. 740, 749\xe2\x80\x93750, and n.\xc2\xa09 (1980). If the federal rule does not apply or can operate alongside the state rule, then there is no \xe2\x80\x9cAc[t] of Congress\xe2\x80\x9d governing that particular question, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71652, and the court must engage in the traditional Rules of Decision Act inquiry under Erie and its progeny. In some instances, the \xe2\x80\x9cplain meaning\xe2\x80\x9d of a federal rule will not come into \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98direct collision\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d with the state law, and both can operate. Walker, 446 U. S., at 750, n.\xc2\xa09, 749. In other instances, the rule \xe2\x80\x9cwhen fairly construed,\xe2\x80\x9d Burlington Northern R. Co., 480 U. S., at 4, with \xe2\x80\x9csensitivity to important state interests and regulatory policies,\xe2\x80\x9d Gasperini, 518 U. S., at 427, n.\xc2\xa07, will not collide with the state law.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If, on the other hand, the federal rule is \xe2\x80\x9csufficiently broad to control the issue before the Court,\xe2\x80\x9d such that there is a \xe2\x80\x9cdirect collision,\xe2\x80\x9d Walker, 446 U. S., at 749\xe2\x80\x93750, the court must decide whether application of the federal rule \xe2\x80\x9crepresents a valid exercise\xe2\x80\x9d of the \xe2\x80\x9crulemaking authority \xe2\x80\xa6 bestowed on this Court by the Rules Enabling Act.\xe2\x80\x9d Burlington Northern R.\xc2\xa0Co., 480 U. S., at 5; see also Gasperini, 518 U. S., at 427, n.\xc2\xa07; Hanna, 380 U. S., at 471\xe2\x80\x93474. That Act requires, inter alia, that federal rules \xe2\x80\x9cnot abridge, enlarge or modify any substantive right.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72072(b) (emphasis added). Unlike Justice Scalia, I believe that an application of a federal rule that effectively abridges, enlarges, or modifies a state-created right or remedy violates this command. Congress may have the constitutional power \xe2\x80\x9cto supplant state law\xe2\x80\x9d with rules that are \xe2\x80\x9crationally capable of classification as procedure,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 12 (internal quotation marks omitted), but we should generally presume that it has not done so. Cf. Wyeth v. Levine, 555 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (slip op., at 8) (observing that \xe2\x80\x9cwe start with the assumption\xe2\x80\x9d that a federal statute does not displace a State\xe2\x80\x99s law \xe2\x80\x9cunless that was the clear and manifest purpose of Congress\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). Indeed, the mandate that federal rules \xe2\x80\x9cshall not abridge, enlarge or modify any substantive right\xe2\x80\x9d evinces the opposite intent, as does Congress\xe2\x80\x99 decision to delegate the creation of rules to this Court rather than to a political branch, see 19 C. Wright, A. Miller, & E. Cooper, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa74509, p.\xc2\xa0265 (2d ed. 1996) (hereinafter Wright).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thus, the second step of the inquiry may well bleed back into the first. When a federal rule appears to abridge, enlarge, or modify a substantive right, federal courts must consider whether the rule can reasonably be interpreted to avoid that impermissible result. See, e.g., Semtek Int\xe2\x80\x99l Inc. v. Lockheed Martin Corp., 531 U. S. 497, 503 (2001) (avoiding an interpretation of Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 41(b) that \xe2\x80\x9cwould arguably violate the jurisdictional limitation of the Rules Enabling Act\xe2\x80\x9d contained in \xc2\xa72072(b)).[Footnote 6] And when such a \xe2\x80\x9csaving\xe2\x80\x9d construction is not possible and the rule would violate the Enabling Act, federal courts cannot apply the rule. See 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72072(b) (mandating that federal rules \xe2\x80\x9cshall not\xe2\x80\x9d alter \xe2\x80\x9cany substantive right\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); Hanna, 380 U. S., at 473 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] court, in measuring a Federal Rule against the standards contained in the Enabling Act \xe2\x80\xa6 need not wholly blind itself to the degree to which the Rule makes the character and result of the federal litigation stray from the course it would follow in state courts\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Semtek Int\xe2\x80\x99l Inc., 531 U. S., at 503\xe2\x80\x93504 (noting that if state law granted a particular right, \xe2\x80\x9cthe federal court\xe2\x80\x99s extinguishment of that right\xe2\x80\xa6 would seem to violate [\xc2\xa72072(b)]\xe2\x80\x9d); cf. Statement of Justices Black and Douglas, 374 U. S. 865, 870 (1963) (observing that federal rules \xe2\x80\x9cas applied in given situations might have to be declared invalid\xe2\x80\x9d). A federal rule, therefore, cannot govern a particular case in which the rule would displace a state law that is procedural in the ordinary use of the term but is so intertwined with a state right or remedy that it functions to define the scope of the state-created right. And absent a governing federal rule, a federal court must engage in the traditional Rules of Decision Act inquiry, under the Erie line of cases. This application of the Enabling Act shows \xe2\x80\x9csensitivity to important state interests,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 7, and \xe2\x80\x9cregulatory policies,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 2, but it does so as Congress authorized, by ensuring that federal rules that ordinarily \xe2\x80\x9cprescribe general rules of practice and procedure,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72072(a), do \xe2\x80\x9cnot abridge, enlarge or modify any substantive right,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72072(b).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia believes that the sole Enabling Act question is whether the federal rule \xe2\x80\x9creally regulates procedure,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 12, 16, 17, 20, n.\xc2\xa013 (plurality opinion) (internal quotation marks omitted), which means, apparently, whether it regulates \xe2\x80\x9cthe manner and the means by which the litigants\xe2\x80\x99 rights are enforced,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 13 (internal quotation marks omitted). I respectfully disagree.[Footnote 7] This interpretation of the Enabling Act is consonant with the Act\xe2\x80\x99s first limitation to \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral rules of practice and procedure,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72072(a). But it ignores the second limitation that such rules also \xe2\x80\x9cnot abridge, enlarge or modify any substantive right,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72072(b) (emphasis added),[Footnote 8] and in so doing ignores the balance that Congress struck between uniform rules of federal procedure and respect for a State\xe2\x80\x99s construction of its own rights and remedies. It also ignores the separation-of-powers presumption, see Wright \xc2\xa74509, at 265, and federalism presumption, see Wyeth, 555 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 8), that counsel against judicially created rules displacing state substantive law.[Footnote 9]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although the plurality appears to agree with much of my interpretation of \xc2\xa72072, see ante, at 18\xe2\x80\x9319, it nonetheless rejects that approach for two reasons, both of which are mistaken. First, Justice Scalia worries that if federal courts inquire into the effect of federal rules on state law, it will enmesh federal courts in difficult determinations about whether application of a given rule would displace a state determination about substantive rights. See ante, at 15, 21\xe2\x80\x9322, and nn.\xc2\xa014, 15. I do not see why an Enabling Act inquiry that looks to state law necessarily is more taxing than Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s.[Footnote 10] But in any event, that inquiry is what the Enabling Act requires: While it may not be easy to decide what is actually a \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive right,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cthe designations substantive and procedural become important, for the Enabling Act has made them so.\xe2\x80\x9d Ely 723; see also Wright \xc2\xa74509, at 266. The question, therefore, is not what rule we think would be easiest on federal courts. The question is what rule Congress established. Although, Justice Scalia may generally prefer easily administrable, bright-line rules, his preference does not give us license to adopt a second-best interpretation of the Rules Enabling Act. Courts cannot ignore text and context in the service of simplicity.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, the plurality argues that its interpretation of the Enabling Act is dictated by this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Sibbach, which applied a Federal Rule about when parties must submit to medical examinations. But the plurality misreads that opinion. As Justice Harlan observed in Hanna, \xe2\x80\x9cshorthand formulations which have appeared in earlier opinions are prone to carry untoward results that frequently arise from oversimplification.\xe2\x80\x9d 380 U. S., at 475 (concurring opinion). To understand Sibbach, it is first necessary to understand the issue that was before the Court. The petitioner raised only the facial question whether \xe2\x80\x9cRules 35 and 37 [of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure] are \xe2\x80\xa6 within the mandate of Congress to this court\xe2\x80\x9d and not the specific question of \xe2\x80\x9cthe obligation of federal courts to apply the substantive law of a state.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 11] 312 U. S., at 9. The Court, therefore, had no occasion to consider whether the particular application of the Federal Rules in question would offend the Enabling Act.[Footnote 12]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor, in Sibbach, was any further analysis necessary to the resolution of the case because the matter at issue, requiring medical exams for litigants, did not pertain to \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive rights\xe2\x80\x9d under the Enabling Act. Although most state rules bearing on the litigation process are adopted for some policy reason, few seemingly \xe2\x80\x9cprocedural\xe2\x80\x9d rules define the scope of a substantive right or remedy. The matter at issue in Sibbach reflected competing federal and state judgments about privacy interests. Those privacy concerns may have been weighty and in some sense substantive; but they did not pertain to the scope of any state right or remedy at issue in the litigation. Thus, in response to the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s argument in Sibbach that \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive rights\xe2\x80\x9d include not only \xe2\x80\x9crights sought to be adjudicated by the litigants\xe2\x80\x9d but also \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral principle[s]\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cquestion[s] of public policy that the legislature is able to pass upon,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 2\xe2\x80\x933, we held that \xe2\x80\x9cthe phrase \xe2\x80\x98substantive rights\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d embraces only state rights, such as the tort law in that case, that are sought to be enforced in the judicial proceedings. Id., at 13\xe2\x80\x9314. If the Federal Rule had in fact displaced a state rule that was sufficiently intertwined with a state right or remedy, then perhaps the Enabling Act analysis would have been different.[Footnote 13] Our subsequent cases are not to the contrary.[Footnote 14]

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Ginsburg views the basic issue in this case as whether and how to apply a federal rule that dictates an answer to a traditionally procedural question (whether to join plaintiffs together as a class), when a state law that \xe2\x80\x9cdefines the dimensions\xe2\x80\x9d of a state-created claim dictates the opposite answer. Post, at 12. As explained above, I readily acknowledge that if a federal rule displaces a state rule that is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98procedural\xe2\x80\x99 in the ordinary sense of the term,\xe2\x80\x9d S. A. Healy Co., 60 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 310, but sufficiently interwoven with the scope of a substantive right or remedy, there would be an Enabling Act problem, and the federal rule would have to give way. In my view, however, this is not such a case.

\n

Rule 23 Controls Class Certification

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When the District Court in the case before us was asked to certify a class action, Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23 squarely governed the determination whether the court should do so. That is the explicit function of Rule 23. Rule 23, therefore, must apply unless its application would abridge, enlarge, or modify New York rights or remedies.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Notwithstanding the plain language of Rule 23, I understand the dissent to find that Rule 23 does not govern the question of class certification in this matter because New York has made a substantive judgment that such a class should not be certified, as a means of proscribing damages. Although, as discussed infra, at 17\xe2\x80\x9320, I do not accept the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s view of \xc2\xa7901(b), I also do not see how the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of Rule 23 follows from that view.[Footnote 15] I agree with Justice Ginsburg that courts should \xe2\x80\x9cavoi[d] immoderate interpretations of the Federal Rules that would trench on state prerogatives,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 3\xe2\x80\x934, and should in some instances \xe2\x80\x9cinterpre[t] the federal rules to avoid conflict with important state regulatory policies,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 5 (internal quotation marks omitted). But that is not what the dissent has done. Simply because a rule should be read in light of federalism concerns, it does not follow that courts may rewrite the rule.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At bottom, the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of Rule 23 seems to be that Rule 23 covers only those cases in which its application would create no Erie problem. The dissent would apply the Rules of Decision Act inquiry under Erie even to cases in which there is a governing federal rule, and thus the Act, by its own terms, does not apply. But \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen a situation is covered by one of the Federal Rules, the question facing the court is a far cry from the typical, relatively unguided Erie choice.\xe2\x80\x9d Hanna, 380 U. S., at 471. The question is only whether the Enabling Act is satisfied. Although it reflects a laudable concern to protect \xe2\x80\x9cstate regulatory policies,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 5 (internal quotation marks omitted), Justice Ginsburg\xe2\x80\x99s approach would, in my view, work an end run around Congress\xe2\x80\x99 system of uniform federal rules, see 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72072, and our decision in Hanna. Federal courts can and should interpret federal rules with sensitivity to \xe2\x80\x9cstate prerogatives,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 4; but even when \xe2\x80\x9cstate interests \xe2\x80\xa6 warrant our respectful consideration,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 8, federal courts cannot rewrite the rules. If my dissenting colleagues feel strongly that \xc2\xa7901(b) is substantive and that class certification should be denied, then they should argue within the Enabling Act\xe2\x80\x99s framework. Otherwise, \xe2\x80\x9cthe Federal Rule applies regardless of contrary state law.\xe2\x80\x9d Gasperini, 518 U. S., at 427, n.\xc2\xa07; accord, Hanna, 380 U. S., at 471.

\n

\nApplying Rule 23 Does Not Violate the Enabling Act

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As I have explained, in considering whether to certify a class action such as this one, a federal court must inquire whether doing so would abridge, enlarge, or modify New York\xe2\x80\x99s rights or remedies, and thereby violate the Enabling Act. This inquiry is not always a simple one because \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t is difficult to conceive of any rule of procedure that cannot have a significant effect on the outcome of a case,\xe2\x80\x9d Wright \xc2\xa74508, at 232\xe2\x80\x93233, and almost \xe2\x80\x9cany rule can be said to have \xe2\x80\xa6 \xe2\x80\x98substantive effects,\xe2\x80\x99 affecting society\xe2\x80\x99s distribution of risks and rewards,\xe2\x80\x9d Ely 724, n. 170. Faced with a federal rule that dictates an answer to a traditionally procedural question and that displaces a state rule, one can often argue that the state rule was really some part of the State\xe2\x80\x99s definition of its rights or remedies.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In my view, however, the bar for finding an Enabling Act problem is a high one. The mere fact that a state law is designed as a procedural rule suggests it reflects a judgment about how state courts ought to operate and not a judgment about the scope of state-created rights and remedies. And for the purposes of operating a federal court system, there are costs involved in attempting to discover the true nature of a state procedural rule and allowing such a rule to operate alongside a federal rule that appears to govern the same question. The mere possibility that a federal rule would alter a state-created right is not sufficient. There must be little doubt.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The text of CPLR \xc2\xa7901(b) expressly and unambiguously applies not only to claims based on New York law but also to claims based on federal law or the law of any other State. And there is no interpretation from New York courts to the contrary. It is therefore hard to see how \xc2\xa7901(b) could be understood as a rule that, though procedural in form, serves the function of defining New York\xe2\x80\x99s rights or remedies. This is all the more apparent because lawsuits under New York law could be joined in federal class actions well before New York passed \xc2\xa7901(b) in 1975, and New York had done nothing to prevent that. It is true, as the dissent points out, that there is a limited amount of legislative history that can be read to suggest that the New York officials who supported \xc2\xa7901(b) wished to create a \xe2\x80\x9climitation\xe2\x80\x9d on New York\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cstatutory damages.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 8. But, as Justice Scalia notes, that is not the law that New York adopted.[Footnote 16] See ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x938 (opinion of the Court).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The legislative history, moreover, does not clearly describe a judgment that \xc2\xa7901(b) would operate as a limitation on New York\xe2\x80\x99s statutory damages. In evaluating that legislative history, it is necessary to distinguish between procedural rules adopted for some policy reason and seemingly procedural rules that are intimately bound up in the scope of a substantive right or remedy. Although almost every rule is adopted for some reason and has some effect on the outcome of litigation, not every state rule \xe2\x80\x9cdefines the dimensions of [a] claim itself,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 12. New York clearly crafted \xc2\xa7901(b) with the intent that only certain lawsuits\xe2\x80\x94those for which there were not statutory penalties\xe2\x80\x94could be joined in class actions in New York courts. That decision reflects a policy judgment about which lawsuits should proceed in New York courts in a class form and which should not. As Justice Ginsburg carefully outlines, see post, at 8\xe2\x80\x9310, \xc2\xa7901(b) was \xe2\x80\x9capparently\xe2\x80\x9d adopted in response to fears that the class-action procedure, applied to statutory penalties, would lead to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0annihilating punishment of the defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d V. Alexander, Practice Commentaries, C901:11, reprinted in 7B McKinney\xe2\x80\x99s Consolidated Laws of New York Ann., p. 104 (2006) (internal quotation marks omitted); see also Sperry v. Crompton Corp., 8 N.\xc2\xa0Y. 3d 204, 211, 863 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 1012, 1015 (2007). But statements such as these are not particularly strong evidence that \xc2\xa7901(b) serves to define who can obtain a statutory penalty or that certifying such a class would enlarge New York\xe2\x80\x99s remedy. Any device that makes litigation easier makes it easier for plaintiffs to recover damages.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In addition to the fear of excessive recoveries, some opponents of a broad class-action device \xe2\x80\x9cargued that there was no need to permit class actions in order to encourage litigation \xe2\x80\xa6 when statutory penalties \xe2\x80\xa6 provided an aggrieved party with a sufficient economic incentive to pursue a claim.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 211, 863 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 1015 (emphasis added). But those opponents may have felt merely that, for any number of reasons, New York courts should not conduct trials in the class format when that format is unnecessary to motivate litigation.[Footnote 17] Justice Ginsburg asserts that this could not be true because \xe2\x80\x9csuits seeking statutory damages are arguably best suited to the class device because individual proof of actual damages is unnecessary.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310. But some people believe that class actions are inefficient or at least unfair, insofar as they join together slightly disparate claims or force courts to adjudicate unwieldy lawsuits. It is not for us to dismiss the possibility that New York legislators shared in those beliefs and thus wanted to exclude the class vehicle when it appeared to be unnecessary.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The legislative history of \xc2\xa7901 thus reveals a classically procedural calibration of making it easier to litigate claims in New York courts (under any source of law) only when it is necessary to do so, and not making it too easy when the class tool is not required. This is the same sort of calculation that might go into setting filing fees or deadlines for briefs. There is of course a difference of degree between those examples and class certification, but not a difference of kind; the class vehicle may have a greater practical effect on who brings lawsuits than do low filing fees, but that does not transform it into a damages \xe2\x80\x9cproscription,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 12, n.\xc2\xa06, 21, or \xe2\x80\x9climitation,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 7, n.\xc2\xa02, 8, 9, 12, 25.[Footnote 18]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The difference of degree is relevant to the forum shopping considerations that are part of the Rules of Decision Act or Erie inquiry. If the applicable federal rule did not govern the particular question at issue (or could be fairly read not to do so), then those considerations would matter, for precisely the reasons given by the dissent. See post, at 17\xe2\x80\x9324. But that is not this case. As the Court explained in Hanna, it is an \xe2\x80\x9cincorrect assumption that the rule of Erie R. Co. v. Thompkins constitutes the appropriate test of \xe2\x80\xa6 the applicability of a Federal Rule of Civil Procedure.\xe2\x80\x9d 380 U. S., at 469\xe2\x80\x93470. \xe2\x80\x9cIt is true that both the Enabling Act and the Erie rule say, roughly, that federal courts are to apply state \xe2\x80\x98substantive\xe2\x80\x99 law and federal \xe2\x80\x98procedural\xe2\x80\x99 law,\xe2\x80\x9d but the tests are different and reflect the fact that \xe2\x80\x9cthey were designed to control very different sorts of decisions.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 471.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because Rule 23 governs class certification, the only decision is whether certifying a class in this diversity case would \xe2\x80\x9cabridge, enlarge or modify\xe2\x80\x9d New York\xe2\x80\x99s substantive rights or remedies. \xc2\xa72072(b). Although one can argue that class certification would enlarge New York\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9climited\xe2\x80\x9d damages remedy, see post, at 7, n.\xc2\xa02, 8, 9, 12, 25, such arguments rest on extensive speculation about what the New York Legislature had in mind when it created \xc2\xa7901(b). But given that there are two plausible competing narratives, it seems obvious to me that we should respect the plain textual reading of \xc2\xa7901(b), a rule in New York\xe2\x80\x99s procedural code about when to certify class actions brought under any source of law, and respect Congress\xe2\x80\x99 decision that Rule 23 governs class certification in federal courts. In order to displace a federal rule, there must be more than just a possibility that the state rule is different than it appears.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, I concur in part and concur in the judgment.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court today approves Shady Grove\xe2\x80\x99s attempt to transform a $500 case into a $5,000,000 award, although the State creating the right to recover has proscribed this alchemy. If Shady Grove had filed suit in New York state court, the 2% interest payment authorized by New York Ins. Law Ann. \xc2\xa75106(a) (West 2009) as a penalty for overdue benefits would, by Shady Grove\xe2\x80\x99s own measure, amount to no more than $500. By instead filing in federal court based on the parties\xe2\x80\x99 diverse citizenship and requesting class certification, Shady Grove hopes to recover, for the class, statutory damages of more than $5,000,000. The New York Legislature has barred this remedy, instructing that, unless specifically permitted, \xe2\x80\x9can action to recover a penalty, or minimum measure of recovery created or imposed by statute may not be maintained as a class action.\xe2\x80\x9d N.\xc2\xa0Y. Civ. Prac. Law Ann. (CPLR) \xc2\xa7901(b) (West 2006). The Court nevertheless holds that Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23, which prescribes procedures for the conduct of class actions in federal courts, preempts the application of \xc2\xa7901(b) in diversity suits.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court reads Rule 23 relentlessly to override New York\xe2\x80\x99s restriction on the availability of statutory damages. Our decisions, however, caution us to ask, before undermining state legislation: Is this conflict really necessary? Cf. Traynor, Is This Conflict Really Necessary? 37 Tex. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 657 (1959). Had the Court engaged in that inquiry, it would not have read Rule 23 to collide with New York\xe2\x80\x99s legitimate interest in keeping certain monetary awards reasonably bounded. I would continue to interpret Federal Rules with awareness of, and sensitivity to, important state regulatory policies. Because today\xe2\x80\x99s judgment radically departs from that course, I dissent.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cUnder the Erie doctrine,\xe2\x80\x9d it is long settled, \xe2\x80\x9cfederal courts sitting in diversity apply state substantive law and federal procedural law.\xe2\x80\x9d Gasperini v. Center for Humanities, Inc., 518 U. S. 415, 427 (1996); see Erie R.\xc2\xa0Co. v. Tompkins, 304 U. S. 64 (1938). Justice Harlan aptly conveyed the importance of the doctrine; he described Erie as \xe2\x80\x9cone of the modern cornerstones of our federalism, expressing policies that profoundly touch the allocation of judicial power between the state and federal systems.\xe2\x80\x9d Hanna v. Plumer, 380 U. S. 460, 474 (1965) (concurring opinion). Although we have found Erie\xe2\x80\x99s application \xe2\x80\x9csometimes [to be] a challenging endeavor,\xe2\x80\x9d Gasperini, 518 U. S., at 427, two federal statutes mark our way.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The first, the Rules of Decision Act,[Footnote 1] prohibits federal courts from generating substantive law in diversity actions. See Erie, 304 U. S., at 78. Originally enacted as part of the Judiciary Act of 1789, this restraint serves a policy of prime importance to our federal system. We have therefore applied the Act \xe2\x80\x9cwith an eye alert to \xe2\x80\xa6 avoiding disregard of State law.\xe2\x80\x9d Guaranty Trust Co. v. York, 326 U. S. 99, 110 (1945).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The second, the Rules Enabling Act, enacted in 1934, authorizes us to \xe2\x80\x9cprescribe general rules of practice and procedure\xe2\x80\x9d for the federal courts, but with a crucial restriction: \xe2\x80\x9cSuch rules shall not abridge, enlarge or modify any substantive right.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72072. Pursuant to this statute, we have adopted the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure. In interpreting the scope of the Rules, including, in particular, Rule 23, we have been mindful of the limits on our authority. See, e.g., Ortiz v. Fibreboard Corp., 527 U. S. 815, 845 (1999) (The Rules Enabling Act counsels against \xe2\x80\x9cadventurous application\xe2\x80\x9d of Rule 23; any tension with the Act \xe2\x80\x9cis best kept within tolerable limits.\xe2\x80\x9d); Amchem Products, Inc. v. Windsor, 521 U. S. 591, 612\xe2\x80\x93613 (1997). See also Semtek Int\xe2\x80\x99l Inc. v. Lockheed Martin Corp., 531 U. S. 497, 503\xe2\x80\x93504 (2001).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If a Federal Rule controls an issue and directly conflicts with state law, the Rule, so long as it is consonant with the Rules Enabling Act, applies in diversity suits. See Hanna, 380 U. S., at 469\xe2\x80\x93474. If, however, no Federal Rule or statute governs the issue, the Rules of Decision Act, as interpreted in Erie, controls. That Act directs federal courts, in diversity cases, to apply state law when failure to do so would invite forum-shopping and yield markedly disparate litigation outcomes. See Gasperini, 518 U. S., at 428; Hanna, 380 U. S., at 468. Recognizing that the Rules of Decision Act and the Rules Enabling Act simultaneously frame and inform the Erie analysis, we have endeavored in diversity suits to remain safely within the bounds of both congressional directives.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In our prior decisions in point, many of them not mentioned in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, we have avoided immoderate interpretations of the Federal Rules that would trench on state prerogatives without serving any countervailing federal interest. \xe2\x80\x9cApplication of the Hanna analysis,\xe2\x80\x9d we have said, \xe2\x80\x9cis premised on a \xe2\x80\x98direct collision\xe2\x80\x99 between the Federal Rule and the state law.\xe2\x80\x9d Walker v. Armco Steel Corp., 446 U. S. 740, 749\xe2\x80\x93750 (1980) (quoting Hanna, 380 U. S., at 472). To displace state law, a Federal Rule, \xe2\x80\x9cwhen fairly construed,\xe2\x80\x9d must be \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98sufficiently broad\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d so as \xe2\x80\x9cto \xe2\x80\x98control the issue\xe2\x80\x99 before the court, thereby leaving no room for the operation of that law.\xe2\x80\x9d Burlington Northern R. Co. v. Woods, 480 U. S. 1, 4\xe2\x80\x935 (1987) (quoting Walker, 446 U. S., at 749\xe2\x80\x93750, and n.\xc2\xa09; emphasis added); cf. Stewart Organization, Inc. v. Ricoh Corp., 487 U. S. 22, 37\xe2\x80\x9338 (1988) (Scalia, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]n deciding whether a federal \xe2\x80\xa6 Rule of Procedure encompasses a particular issue, a broad reading that would create significant disuniformity between state and federal courts should be avoided if the text permits.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In pre-Hanna decisions, the Court vigilantly read the Federal Rules to avoid conflict with state laws. In Palmer v. Hoffman, 318 U. S. 109, 117 (1943), for example, the Court read Federal Rule 8(c), which lists affirmative defenses, to control only the manner of pleading the listed defenses in diversity cases; as to the burden of proof in such cases, Palmer held, state law controls.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Six years later, in Ragan v. Merchants Transfer & Warehouse Co., 337 U. S. 530 (1949), the Court ruled that state law determines when a diversity suit commences for purposes of tolling the state limitations period. Although Federal Rule 3 specified that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] civil action is commenced by filing a complaint with the court,\xe2\x80\x9d we held that the Rule did not displace a state law that tied an action\xe2\x80\x99s commencement to service of the summons. Id., at 531\xe2\x80\x93533. The \xe2\x80\x9ccause of action [wa]s created by local law,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court explained, therefore \xe2\x80\x9cthe measure of it [wa]s to be found only in local law.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 533.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Similarly in Cohen v. Beneficial Industrial Loan Corp., 337 U. S. 541 (1949), the Court held applicable in a diversity action a state statute requiring plaintiffs, as a prerequisite to pursuit of a stockholder\xe2\x80\x99s derivative action, to post a bond as security for costs. At the time of the litigation, Rule 23, now Rule 23.1, addressed a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s institution of a derivative action in federal court. Although the Federal Rule specified prerequisites to a stockholder\xe2\x80\x99s maintenance of a derivative action, the Court found no conflict between the Rule and the state statute in question; the requirements of both could be enforced, the Court observed. See id., at 556. Burdensome as the security-for-costs requirement may be, Cohen made plain, suitors could not escape the upfront outlay by resorting to the federal court\xe2\x80\x99s diversity jurisdiction.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In all of these cases, the Court stated in Hanna, \xe2\x80\x9cthe scope of the Federal Rule was not as broad as the losing party urged, and therefore, there being no Federal Rule which covered the point in dispute, Erie commanded the enforcement of state law.\xe2\x80\x9d 380 U. S., at 470. In Hanna itself, the Court found the clash \xe2\x80\x9cunavoidable,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid.; the petitioner had effected service of process as prescribed by Federal Rule 4(d)(1), but that \xe2\x80\x9chow-to\xe2\x80\x9d method did not satisfy the special Massachusetts law applicable to service on an executor or administrator. Even as it rejected the Massachusetts prescription in favor of the federal procedure, however, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he majority in Hanna recognized \xe2\x80\xa6 that federal rules \xe2\x80\xa6 must be interpreted by the courts applying them, and that the process of interpretation can and should reflect an awareness of legitimate state interests.\xe2\x80\x9d R. Fallon, J. Manning, D. Meltzer, & D. Shapiro, Hart and Wechsler\xe2\x80\x99s The Federal Courts and the Federal System 593 (6th ed. 2009) (hereinafter Hart & Wechsler).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Following Hanna, we continued to \xe2\x80\x9cinterpre[t] the federal rules to avoid conflict with important state regulatory policies.\xe2\x80\x9d Hart & Wechsler 593. In Walker, the Court took up the question whether Ragan should be overruled; we held, once again, that Federal Rule 3 does not directly conflict with state rules governing the time when an action commences for purposes of tolling a limitations period. 446 U. S., at 749\xe2\x80\x93752. Rule 3, we said, addresses only \xe2\x80\x9cthe date from which various timing requirements of the Federal Rules begin to run,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 751, and does not \xe2\x80\x9cpurpor[t] to displace state tolling rules,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 750\xe2\x80\x93751. Significant state policy interests would be frustrated, we observed, were we to read Rule 3 as superseding the state rule, which required actual service on the defendant to stop the clock on the statute of limitations. Id., at 750\xe2\x80\x93752.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We were similarly attentive to a State\xe2\x80\x99s regulatory policy in Gasperini. That diversity case concerned the standard for determining when the large size of a jury verdict warrants a new trial. Federal and state courts alike had generally employed a \xe2\x80\x9cshock the conscience\xe2\x80\x9d test in reviewing jury awards for excessiveness. See 518 U. S., at 422. Federal courts did so pursuant to Federal Rule 59(a) which, as worded at the time of Gasperini, instructed that a trial court could grant a new trial \xe2\x80\x9cfor any of the reasons for which new trials have heretofore been granted in actions at law in the courts of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 59(a) (West 1995). In an effort to provide greater control, New York prescribed procedures under which jury verdicts would be examined to determine whether they \xe2\x80\x9cdeviate[d] materially from what would be reasonable compensation.\xe2\x80\x9d See Gasperini, 518 U. S., at 423\xe2\x80\x93425 (quoting CPLR \xc2\xa75501(c)). This Court held that Rule 59(a) did not inhibit federal-court accommodation of New York\xe2\x80\x99s invigorated test.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Most recently, in Semtek, we addressed the claim-preclusive effect of a federal-court judgment dismissing a diversity action on the basis of a California statute of limitations. The case came to us after the same plaintiff renewed the same fray against the same defendant in a Maryland state court. (Plaintiff chose Maryland because that State\xe2\x80\x99s limitations period had not yet run.) We held that Federal Rule 41(b), which provided that an involuntary dismissal \xe2\x80\x9coperate[d] as an adjudication on the merits,\xe2\x80\x9d did not bar maintenance of the renewed action in Maryland. To hold that Rule 41(b) precluded the Maryland courts from entertaining the case, we said, \xe2\x80\x9cwould arguably violate the jurisdictional limitation of the Rules Enabling Act,\xe2\x80\x9d 531 U. S., at 503, and \xe2\x80\x9cwould in many cases violate [Erie\xe2\x80\x99s] federalism principle,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 504.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, both before and after Hanna, the above-described decisions show, federal courts have been cautioned by this Court to \xe2\x80\x9cinterpre[t] the Federal Rules \xe2\x80\xa6 with sensitivity to important state interests,\xe2\x80\x9d Gasperini, 518 U. S., at 427, n.\xc2\xa07, and a will \xe2\x80\x9cto avoid conflict with important state regulatory policies,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 438, n. 22 (internal quotation marks omitted).[Footnote 2] The Court veers away from that approach\xe2\x80\x94and conspicuously, its most recent reiteration in Gasperini, ante, at 11, n.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x94in favor of a mechanical reading of Federal Rules, insensitive to state interests and productive of discord.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our decisions instruct over and over again that, in the adjudication of diversity cases, state interests\xe2\x80\x94whether advanced in a statute, e.g., Cohen, or a procedural rule, e.g., Gasperini\xe2\x80\x94warrant our respectful consideration. Yet today, the Court gives no quarter to New York\xe2\x80\x99s limitation on statutory damages and requires the lower courts to thwart the regulatory policy at stake: To prevent excessive damages, New York\xe2\x80\x99s law controls the penalty to which a defendant may be exposed in a single suit. The story behind \xc2\xa7901(b)\xe2\x80\x99s enactment deserves telling.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1975, the Judicial Conference of the State of New York proposed a new class-action statute designed \xe2\x80\x9cto set up a flexible, functional scheme\xe2\x80\x9d that would provide \xe2\x80\x9can effective, but controlled group remedy.\xe2\x80\x9d Judicial Conference Report on CPLR, reprinted in 1975 N.\xc2\xa0Y. Laws pp.\xc2\xa01477, 1493 (McKinney). As originally drafted, the legislation addressed only the procedural aspects of class actions; it specified, for example, five prerequisites for certification, eventually codified at \xc2\xa7901(a), that closely tracked those listed in Rule 23. See CPLR \xc2\xa7901(a) (requiring, for class certification, numerosity, predominance, typicality, adequacy of representation, and superiority).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While the Judicial Conference proposal was in the New York Legislature\xe2\x80\x99s hopper, \xe2\x80\x9cvarious groups advocated for the addition of a provision that would prohibit class action plaintiffs from being awarded a statutorily-created penalty \xe2\x80\xa6 except when expressly authorized in the pertinent statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Sperry v. Crompton Corp., 8 N.\xc2\xa0Y. 3d 204, 211, 863 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 1012, 1015 (2007). These constituents \xe2\x80\x9cfeared that recoveries beyond actual damages could lead to excessively harsh results.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. \xe2\x80\x9cThey also argued that there was no need to permit class actions \xe2\x80\xa6 [because] statutory penalties \xe2\x80\xa6 provided an aggrieved party with a sufficient economic incentive to pursue a claim.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Such penalties, constituents observed, often far exceed a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s actual damages. \xe2\x80\x9cWhen lumped together,\xe2\x80\x9d they argued, \xe2\x80\x9cpenalties and class actions produce overkill.\xe2\x80\x9d Attachment to Letter from G. Perkinson, New York State Council of Retail Merchants, Inc., to J. Gribetz, Executive Chamber (June 4, 1975) (Legislative Report), Bill Jacket, L. 1975, Ch. 207.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Aiming to avoid \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0annihilating punishment of the defendant,\xe2\x80\x9d the New York Legislature amended the proposed statute to bar the recovery of statutory damages in class actions. V. Alexander, Practice Commentaries, C901:11, reprinted in 7B McKinney\xe2\x80\x99s Consolidated Laws of New York Ann., p. 104 (2006) (internal quotation marks omitted). In his signing statement, Governor Hugh Carey stated that the new statute \xe2\x80\x9cempowers the court to prevent abuse of the class action device and provides a controlled remedy.\xe2\x80\x9d Memorandum on Approving L. 1975, Ch. 207, reprinted in 1975 N.\xc2\xa0Y. Laws, at 1748 (emphasis added).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he final bill \xe2\x80\xa6 was the result of a compromise among competing interests.\xe2\x80\x9d Sperry, 8 N.\xc2\xa0Y. 3d, at 211, 863 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 1015. Section 901(a) allows courts leeway in deciding whether to certify a class, but \xc2\xa7901(b) rejects the use of the class mechanism to pursue the particular remedy of statutory damages. The limitation was not designed with the fair conduct or efficiency of litigation in mind. Indeed, suits seeking statutory damages are arguably best suited to the class device because individual proof of actual damages is unnecessary. New York\xe2\x80\x99s decision instead to block class-action proceedings for statutory damages therefore makes scant sense, except as a means to a manifestly substantive end: Limiting a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s liability in a single lawsuit in order to prevent the exorbitant inflation of penalties\xe2\x80\x94remedies the New York Legislature created with individual suits in mind.[Footnote 3]

\n

D

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Shady Grove contends\xe2\x80\x94and the Court today agrees\xe2\x80\x94that Rule 23 unavoidably preempts New York\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition on the recovery of statutory damages in class actions. The Federal Rule, the Court emphasizes, states that Shady Grove\xe2\x80\x99s suit \xe2\x80\x9cmay be\xe2\x80\x9d maintained as a class action, which conflicts with \xc2\xa7901(b)\xe2\x80\x99s instruction that it \xe2\x80\x9cmay not\xe2\x80\x9d so proceed. Ante, at 4 (internal quotation marks omitted and emphasis deleted). Accordingly, the Court insists, \xc2\xa7901(b) \xe2\x80\x9ccannot apply in diversity suits unless Rule 23 is ultra vires.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Concluding that Rule 23 does not violate the Rules Enabling Act, the Court holds that the federal provision controls Shady Grove\xe2\x80\x99s ability to seek, on behalf of a class, a statutory penalty of over $5,000,000. Ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9316 (plurality opinion); ante, at 17\xe2\x80\x9322 (Stevens, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court, I am convinced, finds conflict where none is necessary. Mindful of the history behind \xc2\xa7901(b)\xe2\x80\x99s enactment, the thrust of our precedent, and the substantive-rights limitation in the Rules Enabling Act, I conclude, as did the Second Circuit and every District Court to have considered the question in any detail,[Footnote 4] that Rule 23 does not collide with \xc2\xa7901(b). As the Second Circuit well understood, Rule 23 prescribes the considerations relevant to class certification and postcertification proceedings\xe2\x80\x94but it does not command that a particular remedy be available when a party sues in a representative capacity. See 549 F.\xc2\xa03d 137, 143 (2008).[Footnote 5] Section 901(b), in contrast, trains on that latter issue. Sensibly read, Rule 23 governs procedural aspects of class litigation, but allows state law to control the size of a monetary award a class plaintiff may pursue.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In other words, Rule 23 describes a method of enforcing a claim for relief, while \xc2\xa7901(b) defines the dimensions of the claim itself. In this regard, it is immaterial that \xc2\xa7901(b) bars statutory penalties in wholesale, rather than retail, fashion. The New York Legislature could have embedded the limitation in every provision creating a cause of action for which a penalty is authorized; \xc2\xa7901(b) operates as shorthand to the same effect. It is as much a part of the delineation of the claim for relief as it would be were it included claim by claim in the New York Code.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court single-mindedly focuses on whether a suit \xe2\x80\x9cmay\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cmay not\xe2\x80\x9d be maintained as a class action. See ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x936. Putting the question that way, the Court does not home in on the reason why. Rule 23 authorizes class treatment for suits satisfying its prerequisites because the class mechanism generally affords a fair and efficient way to aggregate claims for adjudication. Section 901(b) responds to an entirely different concern; it does not allow class members to recover statutory damages because the New York Legislature considered the result of adjudicating such claims en masse to be exorbitant.[Footnote 6] The fair and efficient conduct of class litigation is the legitimate concern of Rule 23; the remedy for an infraction of state law, however, is the legitimate concern of the State\xe2\x80\x99s lawmakers and not of the federal rulemakers. Cf. Ely, The Irrepressible Myth of Erie, 87 Harv. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 693, 722 (1974) (It is relevant \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the state provision embodies a substantive policy or represents only a procedural disagreement with the federal rulemakers respecting the fairest and most efficient way of conducting litigation.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Suppose, for example, that a State, wishing to cap damages in class actions at $1,000,000, enacted a statute providing that \xe2\x80\x9ca suit to recover more than $1,000,000 may not be maintained as a class action.\xe2\x80\x9d Under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning\xe2\x80\x94which attributes dispositive significance to the words \xe2\x80\x9cmay not be maintained\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94Rule 23 would preempt this provision, nevermind that Congress, by authorizing the promulgation of rules of procedure for federal courts, surely did not intend to displace state-created ceilings on damages.[Footnote 7] The Court suggests that the analysis might differ if the statute \xe2\x80\x9climit[ed] the remedies available in an existing class action,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 7, such that Rule 23 might not conflict with a state statute prescribing that \xe2\x80\x9cno more than $1,000,000 may be recovered in a class action.\xe2\x80\x9d There is no real difference in the purpose and intended effect of these two hypothetical statutes. The notion that one directly impinges on Rule 23\xe2\x80\x99s domain, while the other does not, fundamentally misperceives the office of Rule 23.[Footnote 8]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The absence of an inevitable collision between Rule 23 and \xc2\xa7901(b) becomes evident once it is comprehended that a federal court sitting in diversity can accord due respect to both state and federal prescriptions. Plaintiffs seeking to vindicate claims for which the State has provided a statutory penalty may pursue relief through a class action if they forgo statutory damages and instead seek actual damages or injunctive or declaratory relief; any putative class member who objects can opt out and pursue actual damages, if available, and the statutory penalty in an individual action. See, e.g., Mendez v. The Radec Corp., 260 F.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0D. 38, 55 (WDNY 2009); Brzychnalski v. Unesco, Inc., 35 F. Supp. 2d 351, 353 (SDNY 1999).[Footnote 9] See also Alexander, Practice Commentaries, at 105 (\xe2\x80\x9cEven if a statutory penalty or minimum recovery is involved, most courts hold that it can be waived, thus confining the class recovery to actual damages and eliminating the bar of CPLR 901(b).\xe2\x80\x9d). In this manner, the Second Circuit explained, \xe2\x80\x9cRule 23\xe2\x80\x99s procedural requirements for class actions can be applied along with the substantive requirement of CPLR 901(b).\xe2\x80\x9d 549 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 144. In sum, while phrased as responsive to the question whether certain class actions may begin, \xc2\xa7901(b) is unmistakably aimed at controlling how those actions must end. On that remedial issue, Rule 23 is silent.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Any doubt whether Rule 23 leaves \xc2\xa7901(b) in control of the remedial issue at the core of this case should be dispelled by our Erie jurisprudence, including Hanna, which counsels us to read Federal Rules moderately and cautions against stretching a rule to cover every situation it could conceivably reach.[Footnote 10] The Court states that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here is no reason . . . to read Rule 23 as addressing only whether claims made eligible for class treatment by some other law should be certified as class actions.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 5. To the contrary, Palmer, Ragan, Cohen, Walker, Gasperini, and Semtek provide good reason to look to the law that creates the right to recover. See supra, at 4\xe2\x80\x937. That is plainly so on a more accurate statement of what is at stake: Is there any reason to read Rule 23 as authorizing a claim for relief when the State that created the remedy disallows its pursuit on behalf of a class? None at all is the answer our federal system should give.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Notably, New York is not alone in its effort to contain penalties and minimum recoveries by disallowing class relief; Congress, too, has precluded class treatment for certain claims seeking a statutorily designated minimum recovery. See, e.g., 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71640(a)(2)(B) (Truth in Lending Act) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]n the case of a class action \xe2\x80\xa6 no minimum recovery shall be applicable.\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa71693m(a)(2)(B) (Electronic Fund Transfer Act) (same); 12 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa74010(a)(2)(B)(i) (Expedited Fund Availability Act) (same). Today\xe2\x80\x99s judgment denies to the States the full power Congress has to keep certain monetary awards within reasonable bounds. Cf. Beard v. Kindler, 558 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (slip op., at 8) (\xe2\x80\x9cIn light of \xe2\x80\xa6 federalism and comity concerns \xe2\x80\xa6 it would seem particularly strange to disregard state \xe2\x80\xa6 rules that are substantially similar to those to which we give full force in our own courts.\xe2\x80\x9d). States may hesitate to create determinate statutory penalties in the future if they are impotent to prevent federal-court distortion of the remedy they have shaped.[Footnote 11]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0By finding a conflict without considering whether Rule 23 rationally should be read to avoid any collision, the Court unwisely and unnecessarily retreats from the federalism principles undergirding Erie. Had the Court reflected on the respect for state regulatory interests endorsed in our decisions, it would have found no cause to interpret Rule 23 so woodenly\xe2\x80\x94and every reason not to do so. Cf. Traynor, 37 Tex. L.\xc2\xa0Rev., at 669 (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is bad enough for courts to prattle unintelligibly about choice of law, but unforgiveable when inquiry might have revealed that there was no real conflict.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because I perceive no unavoidable conflict between Rule 23 and \xc2\xa7901(b), I would decide this case by inquiring \xe2\x80\x9cwhether application of the [state] rule would have so important an effect upon the fortunes of one or both of the litigants that failure to [apply] it would be likely to cause a plaintiff to choose the federal court.\xe2\x80\x9d Hanna, 380 U. S., at 468, n.\xc2\xa09. See Gasperini, 518 U. S., at 428.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Seeking to pretermit that inquiry, Shady Grove urges that the class-action bar in \xc2\xa7901(b) must be regarded as \xe2\x80\x9cprocedural\xe2\x80\x9d because it is contained in the CPLR, which \xe2\x80\x9cgovern[s] the procedure in civil judicial proceedings in all courts of the state.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 34 (quoting CPLR \xc2\xa7101; emphasis in original). Placement in the CPLR is hardly dispositive. The provision held \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive\xe2\x80\x9d for Erie purposes in Gasperini is also contained in the CPLR (\xc2\xa75501(c)), as are limitations periods, \xc2\xa7201 et seq., prescriptions plainly \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive\xe2\x80\x9d for Erie purposes however they may be characterized for other purposes, see York, 326 U. S., at 109\xe2\x80\x93112. See also, e.g., 1 Restatement (Second) of Conflict of Laws \xc2\xa7133, Reporter\xe2\x80\x99s Note, p. 369 (1969) (hereinafter Restatement) (\xe2\x80\x9cUnder the rule of Erie \xe2\x80\xa6 the federal courts have classified the burden of persuasion as to contributory negligence as a matter of substantive law that is governed by the rule of the State in which they sit even though the courts of that State have characterized their rule as procedural for choice-of-law purposes.\xe2\x80\x9d); Cook, \xe2\x80\x9cSubstance\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cProcedure\xe2\x80\x9d in the Conflict of Laws, 42 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J. 333 (1933).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Shady Grove also ranks \xc2\xa7901(b) as \xe2\x80\x9cprocedural\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9cnothing in [the statute] suggests that it is limited to rights of action based on New York state law, as opposed to federal law or the law of other states\xe2\x80\x9d; instead it \xe2\x80\x9capplies to actions seeking penalties under any statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 35\xe2\x80\x9336. See also ante, at 17\xe2\x80\x9318 (Stevens, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment) (Section 901(b) cannot \xe2\x80\x9cbe understood as a rule that \xe2\x80\xa6 serves the function of defining New York\xe2\x80\x99s rights or remedies\xe2\x80\x9d because its \xe2\x80\x9ctext \xe2\x80\xa6 expressly and unambiguously applies not only to claims based on New York law but also to claims based on federal law or the law of any other State.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is true that \xc2\xa7901(b) is not specifically limited to claims arising under New York law. But neither is it expressly extended to claims arising under foreign law. The rule prescribes, without elaboration either way, that \xe2\x80\x9can action to recover a penalty \xe2\x80\xa6 may not be maintained as a class action.\xe2\x80\x9d We have often recognized that \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral words\xe2\x80\x9d appearing in a statute may, in fact, have limited application; \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he words \xe2\x80\x98any person or persons,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d for example, \xe2\x80\x9care broad enough to comprehend every human being. But general words must not only be limited to cases within the jurisdiction of the state, but also to those objects to which the legislature intended to apply them.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Palmer, 3 Wheat. 610, 631 (1818) (opinion for the Court by Marshall, C.\xc2\xa0J.). See also Small v. United States, 544 U. S. 385, 388 (2005) (\xe2\x80\x9cIn law, a legislature that uses the statutory phrase \xe2\x80\x98any person\xe2\x80\x99 may or may not mean to include \xe2\x80\x98persons\xe2\x80\x99 outside the jurisdiction of the state.\xe2\x80\x9d (some internal quotation marks omitted)); Flora v. United States, 362 U. S. 145, 149 (1960) (The term \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98any sum\xe2\x80\x99 is a catchall [phrase,] \xe2\x80\xa6 but to say this is not to define what it catches.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, Shady Grove overlooks the most likely explanation for the absence of limiting language: New York legislators make law with New York plaintiffs and defendants in mind, i.e., as if New York were the universe. See Baxter, Choice of Law and the Federal System, 16 Stan. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1, 11 (1963) (\xe2\x80\x9c[L]awmakers often speak in universal terms but must be understood to speak with reference to their constituents.\xe2\x80\x9d); cf. Smith v. United States, 507 U. S. 197, 204, n. 5 (1993) (presumption against extraterritoriality rooted in part in \xe2\x80\x9cthe commonsense notion that Congress generally legislates with domestic concerns in mind\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The point was well put by Brainerd Currie in his seminal article on governmental interest analysis in conflict-of- laws cases. The article centers on a now-archaic Massachusetts law that prevented married women from binding themselves by contract as sureties for their husbands. Discussing whether the Massachusetts prescription applied to transactions involving foreign factors (a foreign forum, foreign place of contracting, or foreign parties), Currie observed:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cWhen the Massachusetts legislature addresses itself to the problem of married women as sureties, the undeveloped image in its mind is that of Massachusetts married women, husbands, creditors, transactions, courts, and judgments. In the history of Anglo-American law the domestic case has been normal, the conflict-of-laws case marginal.\xe2\x80\x9d Married Women\xe2\x80\x99s Contracts: A Study in Conflict-of-Laws Method, 25 U. Chi. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 227, 231 (1958) (emphasis added).

\n

\nShady Grove\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion that States must specifically limit their laws to domestic rights of action if they wish their enactments to apply in federal diversity litigation misses the obvious point: State legislators generally do not focus on an interstate setting when drafting statutes.[Footnote 12]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Shady Grove also observes that a New York court has applied \xc2\xa7901(b) to a federal claim for relief under the Telephone Consumer Protection Act of 1991 (TCPA), 47 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7227, see Rudgayzer & Gratt v. Cape Canaveral Tour & Travel, Inc., 22 App. Div. 3d 148, 799 N.\xc2\xa0Y.\xc2\xa0S. 2d 795 (2005), thus revealing \xc2\xa7901(b)\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cprocedural\xe2\x80\x9d cast. Brief for Petitioner 36. We note first that the TCPA itself calls for the application of state law. See Rudgayzer, 22 App. Div. 3d, at 149\xe2\x80\x93150, 799 N.\xc2\xa0Y.\xc2\xa0S. 2d, at 796\xe2\x80\x93797 (federal action authorized in state court \xe2\x80\x9cif otherwise permitted by the laws or rules of the court of [the] State\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting 47 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7227(b)(3))). See also Gottlieb v. Carnival Corp., 436 F.\xc2\xa03d 335, 342 (CA2 2006) (Sotomayor, J.) (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress sought, via the TCPA, to enact the functional equivalent of a state law.\xe2\x80\x9d). The TCPA, the Supreme Court of Connecticut has recognized, thus \xe2\x80\x9ccarves out an exception to th[e] general rule\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cwhen Erie \xe2\x80\xa6 is reversed \xe2\x80\xa6 , a state court hearing a federal case is normally required to apply federal substantive law\xe2\x80\x9d: \xe2\x80\x9cUnder \xc2\xa7227(b)(3) \xe2\x80\xa6 it is state substantive law that determines, as a preliminary matter, whether a federal action under the act may be brought in state court.\xe2\x80\x9d Weber v. U. S. Sterling Securities, Inc., 282 Conn. 722, 736, 924 A.\xc2\xa02d 816, 826 (2007) (in TCPA action governed by New York substantive law, \xc2\xa7901(b) applied even though the claim was pursued in Connecticut state court).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, statutes qualify as \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive\xe2\x80\x9d for Erie purposes even when they have \xe2\x80\x9cprocedural\xe2\x80\x9d thrusts as well. See, e.g., Cohen, 337 U. S., at 555; cf. Woods v. Interstate Realty Co., 337 U. S. 535, 536\xe2\x80\x93538, and n.\xc2\xa01 (1949) (holding diversity case must be dismissed based on state statute that, by its terms, governed only proceedings in state court). Statutes of limitations are, again, exemplary. They supply \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive\xe2\x80\x9d law in diversity suits, see York, 326 U. S., at 109\xe2\x80\x93112, even though, as Shady Grove acknowledges, state courts often apply the forum\xe2\x80\x99s limitations period as a \xe2\x80\x9cprocedural\xe2\x80\x9d bar to claims arising under the law of another State, see Reply Brief 24, n.\xc2\xa016; Tr. of Oral Arg. 16\xe2\x80\x9317. See also Restatement \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7142\xe2\x80\x93143 (when adjudicating a foreign cause of action, State may use either its own or the foreign jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x99s statute of limitations, whichever is shorter). Similarly, federal courts sitting in diversity give effect to state laws governing the burden of proving contributory negligence, see Palmer v. Hoffman, 318 U. S. 109, 177 (1943), yet state courts adjudicating foreign causes of action often apply their own local law to this issue. See Restatement \xc2\xa7133 and Reporter\xe2\x80\x99s Note.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In short, Shady Grove\xe2\x80\x99s effort to characterize \xc2\xa7901(b) as simply \xe2\x80\x9cprocedural\xe2\x80\x9d cannot successfully elide this fundamental norm: When no federal law or rule is dispositive of an issue, and a state statute is outcome affective in the sense our cases on Erie (pre- and post-Hanna) develop, the Rules of Decision Act commands application of the State\xe2\x80\x99s law in diversity suits. Gasperini, 518 U. S., at 428; Hanna, 380 U. S., at 468, n.\xc2\xa09; York, 326 U. S., at 109. As this case starkly demonstrates, if federal courts exercising diversity jurisdiction are compelled by Rule 23 to award statutory penalties in class actions while New York courts are bound by \xc2\xa7901(b)\xe2\x80\x99s proscription, \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial variations between state and federal [money judgments] may be expected.\xe2\x80\x9d Gasperini, 518 U. S., at 430 (quoting Hanna, 380 U. S., at 467\xe2\x80\x93468 (internal quotation marks omitted)). The \xe2\x80\x9cvariation\xe2\x80\x9d here is indeed \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial.\xe2\x80\x9d Shady Grove seeks class relief that is ten thousand times greater than the individual remedy available to it in state court. As the plurality acknowledges, ante, at 22, forum shopping will undoubtedly result if a plaintiff need only file in federal instead of state court to seek a massive monetary award explicitly barred by state law. See Gasperini, 518 U. S., at 431 (\xe2\x80\x9cErie precludes a recovery in federal court significantly larger than the recovery that would have been tolerated in state court.\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 13] The \xe2\x80\x9caccident of diversity of citizenship,\xe2\x80\x9d Klaxon Co. v. Stentor Elec. Mfg. Co., 313 U. S. 487, 496 (1941), should not subject a defendant to such augmented liability. See Hanna, 380 U. S., at 467 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Erie rule is rooted in part in a realization that it would be unfair for the character or result of a litigation materially to differ because the suit had been brought in a federal court.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is beyond debate that \xe2\x80\x9ca statutory cap on damages would supply substantive law for Erie purposes.\xe2\x80\x9d Gasperini, 518 U. S., at 428. See also id., at 439\xe2\x80\x93440 (Stevens, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cA state-law ceiling on allowable damages \xe2\x80\xa6 is a substantive rule of decision that federal courts must apply in diversity cases governed by New York law.\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 464 (Scalia, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cState substantive law controls what injuries are compensable and in what amount.\xe2\x80\x9d). In Gasperini, we determined that New York\xe2\x80\x99s standard for measuring the alleged excessiveness of a jury verdict was designed to provide a control analogous to a damages cap. Id., at 429. The statute was framed as \xe2\x80\x9ca procedural instruction,\xe2\x80\x9d we noted, \xe2\x80\x9cbut the State\xe2\x80\x99s objective [wa]s manifestly substantive.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Gasperini\xe2\x80\x99s observations apply with full force in this case. By barring the recovery of statutory damages in a class action, \xc2\xa7901(b) controls a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s maximum liability in a suit seeking such a remedy. The remedial provision could have been written as an explicit cap: \xe2\x80\x9cIn any class action seeking statutory damages, relief is limited to the amount the named plaintiff would have recovered in an individual suit.\xe2\x80\x9d That New York\xe2\x80\x99s Legislature used other words to express the very same meaning should be inconsequential.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have long recognized the impropriety of displacing, in a diversity action, state-law limitations on state-created remedies. See Woods, 337 U. S., at 538 (in a diversity case, a plaintiff \xe2\x80\x9cbarred from recovery in the state court \xe2\x80\xa6 should likewise be barred in the federal court\xe2\x80\x9d); York, 326 U. S., at 108\xe2\x80\x93109 (federal court sitting in diversity \xe2\x80\x9ccannot afford recovery if the right to recover is made unavailable by the State nor can it substantively affect the enforcement of the right as given by the State\xe2\x80\x9d). Just as Erie precludes a federal court from entering a deficiency judgment when a State has \xe2\x80\x9cauthoritatively announced that [such] judgments cannot be secured within its borders,\xe2\x80\x9d Angel v. Bullington, 330 U. S. 183, 191 (1947), so too Erie should prevent a federal court from awarding statutory penalties aggregated through a class action when New York prohibits this recovery. See also Ragan, 337 U. S., at 533 (\xe2\x80\x9cWhere local law qualifies or abridges [a claim], the federal court must follow suit. Otherwise there is a different measure of the cause of action in one court than in the other, and the principle of Erie \xe2\x80\xa6 is transgressed.\xe2\x80\x9d). In sum, because \xe2\x80\x9cNew York substantive law governs [this] claim for relief, New York law \xe2\x80\xa6 guide[s] the allowable damages.\xe2\x80\x9d Gasperini, 518 U. S., at 437.[Footnote 14]

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s erosion of Erie\xe2\x80\x99s federalism grounding impels me to point out the large irony in today\xe2\x80\x99s judgment. Shady Grove is able to pursue its claim in federal court only by virtue of the recent enactment of the Class Action Fairness Act of 2005 (CAFA), 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71332(d). In CAFA, Congress opened federal-court doors to state-law-based class actions so long as there is minimal diversity, at least 100 class members, and at least $5,000,000 in controversy. Ibid. By providing a federal forum, Congress sought to check what it considered to be the overreadiness of some state courts to certify class actions. See, e.g., S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 109\xe2\x80\x9314, p. 4 (2005) (CAFA prevents lawyers from \xe2\x80\x9cgam[ing] the procedural rules [to] keep nationwide or multi-state class actions in state courts whose judges have reputations for readily certifying classes.\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)); id., at 22 (disapproving \xe2\x80\x9cthe \xe2\x80\x98I never met a class action I didn\xe2\x80\x99t like\xe2\x80\x99 approach to class certification\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cis prevalent in state courts in some localities\xe2\x80\x9d). In other words, Congress envisioned fewer\xe2\x80\x94not more\xe2\x80\x94class actions overall. Congress surely never anticipated that CAFA would make federal courts a mecca for suits of the kind Shady Grove has launched: class actions seeking state-created penalties for claims arising under state law\xe2\x80\x94claims that would be barred from class treatment in the State\xe2\x80\x99s own courts. Cf. Woods, 337 U. S., at 537 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he policy of Erie \xe2\x80\xa6 preclude[s] maintenance in \xe2\x80\xa6 federal court \xe2\x80\xa6 of suits to which the State ha[s] closed its courts.\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 15]
