MONTGOMERY v. LOUISIANA

\n

certiorari to the supreme court of louisiana

\n

No. 14\xe2\x80\x93280.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 13, 2015\xe2\x80\x94Decided January 25, 2016

\n

Petitioner Montgomery was 17 years old in 1963, when he killed a deputy sheriff in Louisiana. The jury returned a verdict of \xe2\x80\x9cguilty without capital punishment,\xe2\x80\x9d which carried an automatic sentence of life without parole. Nearly 50 years after Montgomery was taken into custody, this Court decided that mandatory life without parole for juvenile homicide offenders violates the\n \nEighth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition on \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98cruel and unusual punishments.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Miller v. Alabama, 567 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. Montgomery sought state collateral relief, arguing that Miller rendered his mandatory life-without-parole sentence illegal. The trial court denied his motion, and his application for a supervisory writ was denied by the Louisiana Supreme Court, which had previously held that Miller does not have retroactive effect in cases on state collateral review.

\n

Held:

\n

1.\xc2\xa0This Court has jurisdiction to decide whether the Louisiana Supreme Court correctly refused to give retroactive effect to Miller. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0Teague v. Lane,\n\n489 U.\xc2\xa0S. 288\n, a federal habeas case, set forth a framework for the retroactive application of a new constitutional rule to convictions that were final when the new rule was announced. While the Court held that new constitutional rules of criminal procedure are generally not retroactive, it recognized that courts must give retroactive effect to new watershed procedural rules and to substantive rules of constitutional law. Substantive constitutional rules include \xe2\x80\x9crules forbidding criminal punishment of certain primary conduct\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9crules prohibiting a certain category of punishment for a class of defendants because of their status or offense,\xe2\x80\x9d Penry v. Lynaugh,\n\n492 U.\xc2\xa0S. 302\n. Court-appointed amicus contends that because Teague was an interpretation of the federal habeas statute, not a constitutional command, its retroactivity holding has no application in state collateral review proceedings. However, neither Teague nor Danforth v. Minnesota,\n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 264\n\xe2\x80\x94which concerned only Teague\xe2\x80\x99s general retroactivity bar for new constitutional rules of criminal procedure\xe2\x80\x94had occasion to address whether States are required as a constitutional matter to give retroactive effect to new substantive rules. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0When a new substantive rule of constitutional law controls the outcome of a case, the Constitution requires state collateral review courts to give retroactive effect to that rule. This conclusion is established by precedents addressing the nature of substantive rules, their differences from procedural rules, and their history of retroactive application. As Teague, supra, at 292, 312, and Penry, supra, at 330, indicate, substantive rules set forth categorical constitutional guarantees that place certain criminal laws and punishments altogether beyond the State\xe2\x80\x99s power to impose. It follows that when a State enforces a proscription or penalty barred by the Constitution, the resulting conviction or sentence is, by definition, unlawful. In contrast, where procedural error has infected a trial, a conviction or sentence may still be accurate and the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s continued confinement may still be lawful, see Schriro v. Summerlin,\n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 348\n\xe2\x80\x93353; for this reason, a trial conducted under a procedure found unconstitutional in a later case does not automatically invalidate a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conviction or sentence. The same possibility of a valid result does not exist where a substantive rule has eliminated a State\xe2\x80\x99s power to proscribe the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conduct or impose a given punishment. See United States v. United States Coin & Currency,\n\n401 U.\xc2\xa0S. 715\n. By holding that new substantive rules are, indeed, retroactive, Teague continued a long tradition of recognizing that substantive rules must have retroactive effect regardless of when the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conviction became final; for a conviction under an unconstitutional law \xe2\x80\x9cis not merely erroneous, but is illegal and void, and cannot be a legal cause of imprisonment,\xe2\x80\x9d Ex parte Siebold,\n\n100 U.\xc2\xa0S. 371\n\xe2\x80\x93377. The same logic governs a challenge to a punishment that the Constitution deprives States of authority to impose, Penry, supra, at 330. It follows that a court has no authority to leave in place a conviction or sentence that violates a substantive rule, regardless of whether the conviction or sentence became final before the rule was announced. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents may not directly control the question here, but they bear on the necessary analysis, for a State that may not constitutionally insist that a prisoner remain in jail on federal habeas review may not constitutionally insist on the same result in its own postconviction proceedings. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

2.\xc2\xa0Miller\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition on mandatory life without parole for juvenile offenders announced a new substantive rule that, under the Constitution, is retroactive in cases on state collateral review. The \xe2\x80\x9cfoundation stone\xe2\x80\x9d for Miller\xe2\x80\x99s analysis was the line of precedent holding certain punishments disproportionate when applied to juveniles, 567 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___, n.\xc2\xa04. Relying on Roper v. Simmons, \n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 551\n, and Graham v. Florida,\n\n560 U.\xc2\xa0S. 48\n, Miller recognized that children differ from adults in their \xe2\x80\x9cdiminished culpability and greater prospects for reform,\xe2\x80\x9d 567 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___, and that these distinctions \xe2\x80\x9cdiminish the penological justifications\xe2\x80\x9d for imposing life without parole on juvenile offenders, id., at ___. Because Miller determined that sentencing a child to life without parole is excessive for all but \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the rare juvenile offender whose crime reflects irreparable corruption,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d id., at ___, it rendered life without parole an unconstitutional penalty for \xe2\x80\x9ca class of defendants because of their status\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94i.e., juvenile offenders whose crimes reflect the transient immaturity of youth, Penry, 492 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 330. Miller therefore announced a substantive rule of constitutional law, which, like other substantive rules, is retroactive because it \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98necessarily carr[ies] a significant risk that a defendant\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94here, the vast majority of juvenile offenders\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98faces a punishment that the law cannot impose upon him.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Schriro, supra, at 352.

\n

A State may remedy a Miller violation by extending parole eligibility to juvenile offenders. This would neither impose an onerous burden on the States nor disturb the finality of state convictions. And it would afford someone like Montgomery, who submits that he has evolved from a troubled, misguided youth to a model member of the prison community, the opportunity to demonstrate the truth of Miller\xe2\x80\x99s central intuition\xe2\x80\x94that children who commit even heinous crimes are capable of change. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9321.

\n

2013\xe2\x80\x931163 (La. 6/20/14), 141 So.\xc2\xa03d 264, reversed and remanded.

HENRY MONTGOMERY, PETITIONER v. LOUISIANA

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of louisiana

\n

[January 25, 2016]

\n

\n

Justice Kennedy delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

This is another case in a series of decisions involving the sentencing of offenders who were juveniles when their crimes were committed. In Miller v. Alabama, 567 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012), the Court held that a juvenile convicted of a homicide offense could not be sentenced to life in prison without parole absent consideration of the juvenile\xe2\x80\x99s special circumstances in light of the principles and purposes of juvenile sentencing. In the wake of Miller, the question has arisen whether its holding is retroactive to juvenile offenders whose convictions and sentences were final when Miller was decided. Courts have reached different conclusions on this point. Compare, e.g., Martin v. Symmes, 782 F.\xc2\xa03d 939, 943 (CA8 2015); Johnson v. Ponton, 780 F.\xc2\xa03d 219, 224\xe2\x80\x93226 (CA4 2015); Chambers v. State, 831 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 311, 331 (Minn. 2013); and State v. Tate, 2012\xe2\x80\x932763, p.\xc2\xa017 (La. 11/5/13), 130 So. 3d 829, 841, with Diatchenko v. District Attorney for Suffolk Dist., 466 Mass. 655, 661\xe2\x80\x93667, 1 N.\xc2\xa0E. 3d 270, 278\xe2\x80\x93282 (2013); Aiken v. Byars, 410 S.\xc2\xa0C. 534, 548, 765 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 572, 578 (2014); State v. Mares, 2014 WY 126, \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb647\xe2\x80\x9363, 335 P.\xc2\xa03d 487, 504\xe2\x80\x93508; and People v. Davis, 2014 IL 115595, \xc2\xb641, 6 N.\xc2\xa0E. 3d 709, 722. Certiorari was granted in this case to resolve the question.

\n

I

\n

Petitioner is Henry Montgomery. In 1963, Montgomery killed Charles Hurt, a deputy sheriff in East Baton Rouge, Louisiana. Montgomery was 17 years old at the time of the crime. He was convicted of murder and sentenced to death, but the Louisiana Supreme Court reversed his conviction after finding that public prejudice had pre-vented a fair trial. State v. Montgomery, 181 So.\xc2\xa02d 756, 762 (La. 1966).

\n

Montgomery was retried. The jury returned a verdict of \xe2\x80\x9cguilty without capital punishment.\xe2\x80\x9d State v. Montgomery, 242 So.\xc2\xa02d 818 (La. 1970). Under Louisiana law, this verdict required the trial court to impose a sentence of life without parole. The sentence was automatic upon the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict, so Montgomery had no opportunity to present mitigation evidence to justify a less severe sentence. That evidence might have included Montgomery\xe2\x80\x99s young age at the time of the crime; expert testimony regarding his limited capacity for foresight, self-discipline, and judgment; and his potential for rehabilitation. Montgomery, now 69 years old, has spent almost his entire life in prison.

\n

Almost 50 years after Montgomery was first taken into custody, this Court decided Miller v. Alabama, 567 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___. Miller held that mandatory life without parole for juvenile homicide offenders violates the\n \nEighth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition on \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98cruel and unusual punishments.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 2). \xe2\x80\x9cBy making youth (and all that accompanies it) irrelevant to imposition of that harshest prison sentence,\xe2\x80\x9d mandatory life without parole \xe2\x80\x9cposes too great a risk of disproportionate punishment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 17). Miller required that sentencing courts consider a child\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cdiminished culpability and heightened capacity for change\xe2\x80\x9d before condemning him or her to die in prison. Ibid. Although Miller did not foreclose a sentencer\xe2\x80\x99s ability to impose life without parole on a juvenile, the Court explained that a lifetime in prison is a disproportionate sentence for all but the rarest of children, those whose crimes reflect \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98irreparable corruption.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Roper v. Simmons,\n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 551,\n573 (2005)\n).

\n

After this Court issued its decision in Miller, Montgomery sought collateral review of his mandatory life-without-parole sentence. In Louisiana there are two principal mechanisms for collateral challenge to the lawfulness of imprisonment. Each begins with a filing in the trial court where the prisoner was convicted and sentenced. La. Code Crim. Proc. Ann., Arts. 882, 926 (West 2008). The first procedure permits a prisoner to file an application for postconviction relief on one or more of seven grounds set forth in the statute. Art. 930.3. The Louisiana Supreme Court has held that none of those grounds provides a basis for collateral review of sentencing errors. See State ex rel. Melinie v. State, 93\xe2\x80\x931380 (La. 1/12/96), 665 So.\xc2\xa02d 1172 (per curiam). Sentencing errors must instead be raised through Louisiana\xe2\x80\x99s second collateral review procedure.

\n

This second mechanism allows a prisoner to bring a collateral attack on his or her sentence by filing a motion to correct an illegal sentence. See Art. 882. Montgomery invoked this procedure in the East Baton Rouge Parish District Court.

\n

The state statute provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n illegal sentence may be corrected at any time by the court that imposed the sentence.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. An illegal sentence \xe2\x80\x9cis primarily restricted to those instances in which the term of the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s sentence is not authorized by the statute or statutes which govern the penalty\xe2\x80\x9d for the crime of conviction. State v. Mead, 2014\xe2\x80\x931051, p.\xc2\xa03 (La. App. 4 Cir. 4/22/15), 165 So. 3d 1044, 1047; see also State v. Alexander, 2014\xe2\x80\x930401 (La. 11/7/14), 152 So. 3d 137 (per curiam). In the ordinary course Louisiana courts will not consider a challenge to a disproportionate sentence on collateral review; rather, as a general matter, it appears that prisoners must raise\n \nEighth Amendment sentencing chal-lenges on direct review. See State v. Gibbs, 620 So.\xc2\xa02d 296, 296\xe2\x80\x93297 (La. App. 1993); Mead, 165 So. 3d, at 1047.

\n

Louisiana\xe2\x80\x99s collateral review courts will, however, consider a motion to correct an illegal sentence based on a decision of this Court holding that the\n \nEighth Amendment to the Federal Constitution prohibits a punishment for a type of crime or a class of offenders. When, for example, this Court held in Graham v. Florida,\n\n560 U.\xc2\xa0S. 48 (2010)\n, that the\n \nEighth Amendment bars life-without-parole sentences for juvenile nonhomicide offenders, Louisiana courts heard Graham claims brought by prisoners whose sentences had long been final. See, e.g., State v. Shaffer, 2011\xe2\x80\x931756, pp. 1\xe2\x80\x934 (La. 11/23/11), 77 So. 3d 939, 940\xe2\x80\x93942 (per curiam) (considering motion to correct an illegal sentence on the ground that Graham rendered illegal a life-without-parole sentence for a juvenile nonhomicide offender). Montgomery\xe2\x80\x99s motion argued that Miller rendered his mandatory life-without-parole sentence illegal.

\n

The trial court denied Montgomery\xe2\x80\x99s motion on the ground that Miller is not retroactive on collateral review. Montgomery then filed an application for a supervisory writ. The Louisiana Supreme Court denied the application. 2013\xe2\x80\x931163 (6/20/14), 141 So. 3d 264. The court relied on its earlier decision in State v. Tate, 2012\xe2\x80\x932763, 130 So. 3d 829, which held that Miller does not have retroactive effect in cases on state collateral review. Chief Justice Johnson and Justice Hughes dissented in Tate, and Chief Justice Johnson again noted his dissent in Montgomery\xe2\x80\x99s case.

\n

This Court granted Montgomery\xe2\x80\x99s petition for certiorari. The petition presented the question \xe2\x80\x9cwhether Miller adopts a new substantive rule that applies retroactively on collateral review to people condemned as juveniles to die in prison.\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Cert. i. In addition, the Court directed the parties to address the following question: \xe2\x80\x9cDo we have jurisdiction to decide whether the Supreme Court of Louisiana correctly refused to give retroactive effect in this case to our decision in Miller?\xe2\x80\x9d 575 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015).

\n

II

\n

The parties agree that the Court has jurisdiction to decide this case. To ensure this conclusion is correct, the Court appointed Richard D. Bernstein as amicus curiae to brief and argue the position that the Court lacks jurisdiction. He has ably discharged his assigned responsibilities.

\n

Amicus argues that a State is under no obligation to give a new rule of constitutional law retroactive effect in its own collateral review proceedings. As those proceedings are created by state law and under the State\xe2\x80\x99s plenary control, amicus contends, it is for state courts to define applicable principles of retroactivity. Under this view, the Louisiana Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision does not implicate a federal right; it only determines the scope of relief avail-able in a particular type of state proceeding\xe2\x80\x94a question of state law beyond this Court\xe2\x80\x99s power to review.

\n

If, however, the Constitution establishes a rule and requires that the rule have retroactive application, then a state court\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to give the rule retroactive effect is reviewable by this Court. Cf. Griffith v. Kentucky,\n\n479 U.\xc2\xa0S. 314,\n328 (1987)\n (holding that on direct review, a new constitutional rule must be applied retroactively \xe2\x80\x9cto all cases, state or federal\xe2\x80\x9d). States may not disregard a controlling, constitutional command in their own courts. See Martin v. Hunter\xe2\x80\x99s Lessee, 1 Wheat. 304, 340\xe2\x80\x93341, 344 (1816); see also Yates v. Aiken,\n\n484 U.\xc2\xa0S. 211,\n218 (1988)\n (when a State has not \xe2\x80\x9cplaced any limit on the issues that it will entertain in collateral proceedings .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. it has a duty to grant the relief that federal law requires\xe2\x80\x9d). Amicus\xe2\x80\x99 argument therefore hinges on the premise that this Court\xe2\x80\x99s retroactivity precedents are not a constitutional mandate.

\n

Justice O\xe2\x80\x99Connor\xe2\x80\x99s plurality opinion in Teague v. Lane,\n\n489 U.\xc2\xa0S. 288 (1989)\n, set forth a framework for retroactiv-ity in cases on federal collateral review. Under Teague, a new constitutional rule of criminal procedure does not apply, as a general matter, to convictions that were final when the new rule was announced. Teague recognized, however, two categories of rules that are not subject to its general retroactivity bar. First, courts must give retroactive effect to new substantive rules of constitutional law. Substantive rules include \xe2\x80\x9crules forbidding criminal punishment of certain primary conduct,\xe2\x80\x9d as well as \xe2\x80\x9crules prohibiting a certain category of punishment for a class of defendants because of their status or offense.\xe2\x80\x9d Penry v. Lynaugh,\n\n492 U.\xc2\xa0S. 302,\n330 (1989)\n; see also Teague, supra, at 307. Although Teague describes new substantive rules as an exception to the bar on retroactive application of procedural rules, this Court has recognized that substantive rules \xe2\x80\x9care more accurately characterized as .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. not subject to the bar.\xe2\x80\x9d Schriro v. Summerlin, 542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 348, 352, n.\xc2\xa04 (2004). Second, courts must give retroactive effect to new \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cwatershed rules of criminal procedure\xe2\x80\x9d implicating the fundamental fairness and accuracy of the criminal proceeding.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 352; see also Teague, 489 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 312\xe2\x80\x93313.

\n

It is undisputed, then, that Teague requires the retroactive application of new substantive and watershed procedural rules in federal habeas proceedings. Amicus, however, contends that Teague was an interpretation of the federal habeas statute, not a constitutional command; and so, the argument proceeds, Teague\xe2\x80\x99s retroactivity holding simply has no application in a State\xe2\x80\x99s own collateral review proceedings.

\n

To support this claim, amicus points to language in Teague that characterized the Court\xe2\x80\x99s task as \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98defin[ing] the scope of the writ.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 308 (quoting Kuhlmann v. Wilson,\n\n477 U.\xc2\xa0S. 436,\n447 (1986)\n (plurality opinion)); see also 489 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 317 (White, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9cIf we are wrong in construing the reach of the habeas corpus statutes, Congress can of course correct us .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. \xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 332 (Brennan, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cNo new facts or arguments have come to light suggesting that our [past] reading of the federal habeas statute .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. was plainly mistaken\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

In addition, amicus directs us to Danforth v. Minnesota,\n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 264 (2008)\n, in which a majority of the Court held that Teague does not preclude state courts from giving retroactive effect to a broader set of new constitutional rules than Teague itself required. 552 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 266. The Danforth majority concluded that Teague\xe2\x80\x99s general rule of nonretroactivity for new constitutional rules of criminal procedure \xe2\x80\x9cwas an exercise of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s power to interpret the federal habeas statute.\xe2\x80\x9d 552 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 278. Since Teague\xe2\x80\x99s retroactivity bar \xe2\x80\x9climit[s] only the scope of federal habeas relief,\xe2\x80\x9d the Danforth majority reasoned, States are free to make new procedural rules retroactive on state collateral review. 552 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 281\xe2\x80\x93282.

\n

Amicus, however, reads too much into these statements. Neither Teague nor Danforth had reason to address whether States are required as a constitutional matter to give retroactive effect to new substantive or watershed procedural rules. Teague originated in a federal, not state, habeas proceeding; so it had no particular reason to discuss whether any part of its holding was required by the Constitution in addition to the federal habeas statute. And Danforth held only that Teague\xe2\x80\x99s general rule of nonretroactivity was an interpretation of the federal habeas statute and does not prevent States from providing greater relief in their own collateral review courts. The Danforth majority limited its analysis to Teague\xe2\x80\x99s general retroactivity bar, leaving open the question whether Teague\xe2\x80\x99s two exceptions are binding on the States as a matter of constitutional law. 552 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 278; see also id., at 277 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he case before us now does not involve either of the \xe2\x80\x98Teague exceptions\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

In this case, the Court must address part of the question left open in Danforth. The Court now holds that when a new substantive rule of constitutional law controls the outcome of a case, the Constitution requires state collateral review courts to give retroactive effect to that rule. Teague\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion establishing the retroactivity of new substantive rules is best understood as resting upon constitutional premises. That constitutional command is, like all federal law, binding on state courts. This holding is limited to Teague\xe2\x80\x99s first exception for substantive rules; the constitutional status of Teague\xe2\x80\x99s exception for watershed rules of procedure need not be addressed here.

\n

This Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents addressing the nature of substantive rules, their differences from procedural rules, and their history of retroactive application establish that the Constitution requires substantive rules to have retroactive effect regardless of when a conviction became final.

\n

The category of substantive rules discussed in Teague originated in Justice Harlan\xe2\x80\x99s approach to retroactivity. Teague adopted that reasoning. See 489 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 292, 312 (discussing Mackey v. United States,\n\n401 U.\xc2\xa0S. 667,\n692 (1971)\n (opinion concurring in judgments in part and dissenting in part); and Desist v. United States,\n\n394 U.\xc2\xa0S. 244\n, n.\xc2\xa02 (1969) (Harlan, J., dissenting)). Justice Harlan defined substantive constitutional rules as \xe2\x80\x9cthose that place, as a matter of constitutional interpretation, certain kinds of primary, private individual conduct beyond the power of the criminal law-making authority to proscribe.\xe2\x80\x9d Mackey, supra, at 692. In Penry v. Lynaugh, decided four months after Teague, the Court recognized that \xe2\x80\x9cthe first exception set forth in Teague should be understood to cover not only rules forbidding criminal punishment of certain primary conduct but also rules prohibiting a certain category of punishment for a class of defendants because of their status or offense.\xe2\x80\x9d 492\xc2\xa0U.\xc2\xa0S., at 330. Penry explained that Justice Harlan\xe2\x80\x99s first exception spoke \xe2\x80\x9cin terms of substantive categorical guarantees accorded by the Constitution, regardless of the procedures followed.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 329. Whether a new rule bars States from proscribing certain conduct or from inflicting a certain punishment, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n both cases, the Constitution itself deprives the State of the power to impose a certain pen-alty.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 330.

\n

Substantive rules, then, set forth categorical constitutional guarantees that place certain criminal laws and punishments altogether beyond the State\xe2\x80\x99s power to impose. It follows that when a State enforces a proscription or penalty barred by the Constitution, the resulting conviction or sentence is, by definition, unlawful. Procedural rules, in contrast, are designed to enhance the accuracy of a conviction or sentence by regulating \xe2\x80\x9cthe manner of determining the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s culpability.\xe2\x80\x9d Schriro, 542 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 353; Teague, supra, at 313. Those rules \xe2\x80\x9cmerely raise the possibility that someone convicted with use of the invalidated procedure might have been acquitted otherwise.\xe2\x80\x9d Schriro, supra, at 352. Even where proce-dural error has infected a trial, the resulting conviction or sentence may still be accurate; and, by extension, the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s continued confinement may still be lawful. For this reason, a trial conducted under a procedure found to be unconstitutional in a later case does not, as a general matter, have the automatic consequence of invalidating a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conviction or sentence.

\n

The same possibility of a valid result does not exist where a substantive rule has eliminated a State\xe2\x80\x99s power to proscribe the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conduct or impose a given punishment. \xe2\x80\x9c[E]ven the use of impeccable factfinding procedures could not legitimate a verdict\xe2\x80\x9d where \xe2\x80\x9cthe conduct being penalized is constitutionally immune from punishment.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. United States Coin & Currency,\n\n401 U.\xc2\xa0S. 715,\n724 (1971)\n. Nor could the use of flawless sentencing procedures legitimate a punishment where the Constitution immunizes the defendant from the sentence imposed. \xe2\x80\x9cNo circumstances call more for the invocation of a rule of complete retroactivity.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

By holding that new substantive rules are, indeed, retroactive, Teague continued a long tradition of giving retroactive effect to constitutional rights that go beyond procedural guarantees. See Mackey, supra, at 692\xe2\x80\x93693 (opinion of Harlan, J.) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he writ has historically been available for attacking convictions on [substantive] grounds\xe2\x80\x9d). Before Brown v. Allen,\n\n344 U.\xc2\xa0S. 443 (1953)\n, \xe2\x80\x9cfederal courts would never consider the merits of a constitutional claim if the habeas petitioner had a fair opportunity to raise his arguments in the original proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d Desist, 394 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 261 (Harlan, J., dissenting). Even in the pre-1953 era of restricted federal habeas, however, an exception was made \xe2\x80\x9cwhen the habeas petitioner attacked the constitutionality of the state statute under which he had been convicted. Since, in this situation, the State had no power to proscribe the conduct for which the petitioner was imprisoned, it could not constitutionally insist that he remain in jail.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 261, n. 2 (Harlan, J., dissenting) (citation omitted).

\n

In Ex parte Siebold,\n\n100 U.\xc2\xa0S. 371 (1880)\n, the Court addressed why substantive rules must have retroactive effect regardless of when the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conviction became final. At the time of that decision, \xe2\x80\x9c[m]ere error in the judgment or proceedings, under and by virtue of which a party is imprisoned, constitute[d] no ground for the issue of the writ.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 375. Before Siebold, the law might have been thought to establish that so long as the conviction and sentence were imposed by a court of competent jurisdiction, no habeas relief could issue. In Siebold, however, the petitioners attacked the judgments on the ground that they had been convicted under unconstitutional statutes. The Court explained that if \xe2\x80\x9cthis position is well taken, it affects the foundation of the whole proceedings.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 376. A conviction under an unconstitutional law

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cis not merely erroneous, but is illegal and void, and cannot be a legal cause of imprisonment. It is true, if no writ of error lies, the judgment may be final, in the sense that there may be no means of reversing it. But .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. if the laws are unconstitutional and void, the Circuit Court acquired no jurisdiction of the causes.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 376\xe2\x80\x93377.

\n

As discussed, the Court has concluded that the same logic governs a challenge to a punishment that the Constitution deprives States of authority to impose. Penry, supra, at 330; see also Friendly, Is Innocence Irrelevant? Collateral Attack on Criminal Judgments, 38 U. Chi. L. Rev. 142, 151 (1970) (\xe2\x80\x9cBroadly speaking, the original sphere for collateral attack on a conviction was where the tribunal lacked jurisdiction either in the usual sense or because the statute under which the defendant had been prosecuted was unconstitutional or because the sentence was one the court could not lawfully impose\xe2\x80\x9d (footnotes omitted)). A conviction or sentence imposed in violation of a substantive rule is not just erroneous but contrary to law and, as a result, void. See Siebold, 100 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 376. It follows, as a general principle, that a court has no authority to leave in place a conviction or sentence that violates a substantive rule, regardless of whether the conviction or sentence became final before the rule was announced.

\n

Siebold and the other cases discussed in this opinion, of course, do not directly control the question the Court now answers for the first time. These precedents did not involve a state court\xe2\x80\x99s postconviction review of a conviction or sentence and so did not address whether the Constitution requires new substantive rules to have retroactive effect in cases on state collateral review. These decisions, however, have important bearing on the analysis necessary in this case.

\n

In support of its holding that a conviction obtained under an unconstitutional law warrants habeas relief, the Siebold Court explained that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n unconstitutional law is void, and is as no law.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. A penalty imposed pursuant to an unconstitutional law is no less void because the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s sentence became final before the law was held unconstitutional. There is no grandfather clause that permits States to enforce punishments the Constitution forbids. To conclude otherwise would undercut the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s substantive guarantees. Writing for the Court in United States Coin & Currency, Justice Harlan made this point when he declared that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o circumstances call more for the invocation of a rule of complete retroactivity\xe2\x80\x9d than when \xe2\x80\x9cthe conduct being penalized is constitutionally immune from punishment.\xe2\x80\x9d 401 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 724. United States Coin & Currency involved a case on direct review; yet, for the reasons explained in this opinion, the same principle should govern the application of substantive rules on collateral review. As Justice Harlan explained, where a State lacked the power to proscribe the habeas petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s conduct, \xe2\x80\x9cit could not constitutionally insist that he remain in jail.\xe2\x80\x9d Desist, supra, at 261, n. 2 (dissenting opinion).

\n

If a State may not constitutionally insist that a prisoner remain in jail on federal habeas review, it may not constitutionally insist on the same result in its own postconviction proceedings. Under the Supremacy Clause of the Constitution, state collateral review courts have no greater power than federal habeas courts to mandate that aprisoner continue to suffer punishment barred by the Constitution. If a state collateral proceeding is open to a claim controlled by federal law, the state court \xe2\x80\x9chas a duty to grant the relief that federal law requires.\xe2\x80\x9d Yates, 484 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 218. Where state collateral review proceedings permit prisoners to challenge the lawfulness of their confinement, States cannot refuse to give retroactive effect to a substantive constitutional right that determines the outcome of that challenge.

\n

As a final point, it must be noted that the retroactive application of substantive rules does not implicate a State\xe2\x80\x99s weighty interests in ensuring the finality of convictions and sentences. Teague warned against the intrusiveness of \xe2\x80\x9ccontinually forc[ing] the States to marshal resources in order to keep in prison defendants whose trials and appeals conformed to then-existing constitutional standards.\xe2\x80\x9d 489 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 310. This concern has no application in the realm of substantive rules, for no resources marshaled by a State could preserve a conviction or sentence that the Constitution deprives the State of power to impose. See Mackey, 401 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 693 (opinion of Harlan, J.) (\xe2\x80\x9cThere is little societal interest in permitting the criminal process to rest at a point where it ought properly never to repose\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

In adjudicating claims under its collateral review procedures a State may not deny a controlling right asserted under the Constitution, assuming the claim is properly presented in the case. Louisiana follows these basic Supremacy Clause principles in its postconviction proceedings for challenging the legality of a sentence. The State\xe2\x80\x99s collateral review procedures are open to claims that a decision of this Court has rendered certain sentences illegal, as a substantive matter, under the\n \nEighth Amendment. See, e.g., State v. Dyer, 2011\xe2\x80\x931758, pp. 1\xe2\x80\x932 (La. 11/23/11), 77 So. 3d 928, 928\xe2\x80\x93929 (per curiam) (considering claim on collateral review that this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Graham v. Florida,\n\n560 U.\xc2\xa0S. 48\n, rendered petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s life-without-parole sentence illegal). Montgomery alleges that Miller announced a substantive constitutional rule and that the Louisiana Supreme Court erred by failing to recognize its retroactive effect. This Court has jurisdiction to review that determination.

\n

III

\n

This leads to the question whether Miller\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition on mandatory life without parole for juvenile offenders indeed did announce a new substantive rule that, under the Constitution, must be retroactive.

\n

As stated above, a procedural rule \xe2\x80\x9cregulate[s] only the manner of determining the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s culpability.\xe2\x80\x9d Schriro, 542 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 353. A substantive rule, in contrast, forbids \xe2\x80\x9ccriminal punishment of certain primary conduct\xe2\x80\x9d or prohibits \xe2\x80\x9ca certain category of punishment for a class of defendants because of their status or offense.\xe2\x80\x9d Penry, 492 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 330; see also Schriro, supra, at 353 (A substantive rule \xe2\x80\x9calters the range of conduct or the class of persons that the law punishes\xe2\x80\x9d). Under this standard, and for the reasons explained below, Miller announced a substantive rule that is retroactive in cases on collateral review.

\n

The \xe2\x80\x9cfoundation stone\xe2\x80\x9d for Miller\xe2\x80\x99s analysis was this Court\xe2\x80\x99s line of precedent holding certain punishments disproportionate when applied to juveniles. 567 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___, n.\xc2\xa04 (slip op., at 8, n. 4). Those cases include Graham v. Florida, supra, which held that the\n \nEighth Amendment bars life without parole for juvenile nonhomicide offenders, and Roper v. Simmons, \n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 551\n, which held that the\n \nEighth Amendment prohibits capital punishment for those under the age of 18 at the time of their crimes. Protection against disproportionate punishment is the central substantive guarantee of the\n \nEighth Amendment and goes far beyond the manner of determining a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s sentence. See Graham, supra, at 59 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe concept of proportionality is central to the\n \nEighth Amendment\xe2\x80\x9d);see also Weems v. United States,\n\n217 U.\xc2\xa0S. 349\n(1910); Harmelin v. Michigan,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 957\n\xe2\x80\x93998 (1991) (Kennedy, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment).

\n

Miller took as its starting premise the principle established in Roper and Graham that \xe2\x80\x9cchildren are constitutionally different from adults for purposes of sentencing.\xe2\x80\x9d 567 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 8) (citing Roper, supra, at 569\xe2\x80\x93570; and Graham, supra, at 68). These differences result from children\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cdiminished culpability and greater prospects for reform,\xe2\x80\x9d and are apparent in three primary ways:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cFirst, children have a \xe2\x80\x98lack of maturity and an underdeveloped sense of responsibility,\xe2\x80\x99 leading to recklessness, impulsivity, and heedless risk-taking. Second, children \xe2\x80\x98are more vulnerable to negative influences and outside pressures,\xe2\x80\x99 including from their family and peers; they have limited \xe2\x80\x98control over their own environment\xe2\x80\x99 and lack the ability to extricate themselves from horrific, crime-producing settings. And third, a child\xe2\x80\x99s character is not as \xe2\x80\x98well formed\xe2\x80\x99 as an adult\xe2\x80\x99s; his traits are \xe2\x80\x98less fixed\xe2\x80\x99 and his actions less likely to be \xe2\x80\x98evidence of irretrievable depravity.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 567 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 8) (quoting Roper, supra, at 569\xe2\x80\x93570; alterations, citations, and some internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

As a corollary to a child\xe2\x80\x99s lesser culpability, Miller recognized that \xe2\x80\x9cthe distinctive attributes of youth diminish the penological justifications\xe2\x80\x9d for imposing life without parole on juvenile offenders. 567 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 9). Because retribution \xe2\x80\x9crelates to an offender\xe2\x80\x99s blameworthiness, the case for retribution is not as strong with a minor as with an adult.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Graham, supra, at 71; internal quotation marks omitted). The deterrence rationale likewise does not suffice, since \xe2\x80\x9cthe same characteristics that render juveniles less culpable than adults\xe2\x80\x94their immaturity, recklessness, and impetuosity\xe2\x80\x94make them less likely to consider potential punishment.\xe2\x80\x9d 567 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 9\xe2\x80\x9310) (internal quotation marks omitted). The need for incapacitation is lessened, too, because ordinary adolescent development diminishes the likelihood that a juvenile offender \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98forever will be a danger to society.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 10) (quoting Graham, 560 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 72). Rehabilitation is not a satisfactory rationale, either. Rehabilitation cannot justify the sentence, as life without parole \xe2\x80\x9cforswears altogether the rehabilitative ideal.\xe2\x80\x9d 567 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 10) (quoting Graham, supra, at 74).

\n

These considerations underlay the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding in Miller that mandatory life-without-parole sentences for children \xe2\x80\x9cpos[e] too great a risk of disproportionate punishment.\xe2\x80\x9d 567 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 17). Miller requires that before sentencing a juvenile to life without parole, the sentencing judge take into account \xe2\x80\x9chow children are different, and how those differences counsel against irrevocably sentencing them to a lifetime in prison.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The Court recognized that a sentencer mightencounter the rare juvenile offender who exhibits such irretrievable depravity that rehabilitation is impossible and life without parole is justified. But in light of \xe2\x80\x9cchildren\xe2\x80\x99s diminished culpability and heightened capacity for change,\xe2\x80\x9d Miller made clear that \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate occasions for sentencing juveniles to this harshest possible penalty will be uncommon.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

Miller, then, did more than require a sentencer to consider a juvenile offender\xe2\x80\x99s youth before imposing life without parole; it established that the penological justifications for life without parole collapse in light of \xe2\x80\x9cthe distinctive attributes of youth.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 9). Even if a court considers a child\xe2\x80\x99s age before sentencing him or her to a lifetime in prison, that sentence still violates the\n \nEighth Amendment for a child whose crime reflects \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98unfortunate yet transient immaturity.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 17) (quoting Roper, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 573). Because Miller determined that sentencing a child to life without parole is excessive for all but \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the rare juvenile offender whose crime reflects irreparable corruption,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 567 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 17) (quoting Roper, supra, at 573), it rendered life without parole an unconstitutional penalty for \xe2\x80\x9ca class of defendants because of their status\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94that is, juvenile offenders whose crimes reflect the transient immaturity of youth. Penry, 492 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 330. As a result, Miller announced a substantive rule of constitutional law. Like other substantive rules, Miller is retroactive because it \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98necessarily carr[ies] a significant risk that a defendant\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94here, the vast majority of juvenile offenders\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98faces a punishment that the law cannot impose upon him.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Schriro, 542 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 352 (quoting Bousley v. United States,\n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 614,\n620 (1998)\n).

\n

Louisiana nonetheless argues that Miller is procedural because it did not place any punishment beyond the State\xe2\x80\x99s power to impose; it instead required sentencing courts to take children\xe2\x80\x99s age into account before condemning them to die in prison. In support of this argument, Louisiana points to Miller\xe2\x80\x99s statement that the decision \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not categorically bar a penalty for a class of offenders or type of crime\xe2\x80\x94as, for example, we did in Roper or Graham. Instead, it mandates only that a sentencer follow a certain process\xe2\x80\x94considering an offender\xe2\x80\x99s youth and attendant characteristics\xe2\x80\x94before imposing a particular penalty.\xe2\x80\x9d Miller, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 20). Miller, it is true, did not bar a punishment for all juvenile offenders, as the Court did in Roper or Graham. Miller did bar life without parole, however, for all but the rarest of juvenile offenders, those whose crimes reflect permanent incorrigibility. For that reason, Miller is no less substantive than are Roper and Graham. Before Miller, every juvenile convicted of a homicide offense could be sentenced to life without parole. After Miller, it will be the rare juvenile offender who can receive that same sentence. The only difference between Roper and Graham, on the one hand, and Miller, on the other hand, is that Miller drew a line between children whose crimes reflect transient immaturity and those rare children whose crimes reflect irreparable corruption. The fact that life without parole could be a proportionate sentence for the latter kind of juvenile offender does not mean that all other children imprisoned under a disproportionate sentence have not suffered the deprivation of a substantive right.

\n

To be sure, Miller\xe2\x80\x99s holding has a procedural component. Miller requires a sentencer to consider a juvenile offender\xe2\x80\x99s youth and attendant characteristics before determining that life without parole is a proportionate sentence. See 567 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 20). Louisiana contends that because Miller requires this process, it must have set forth a procedural rule. This argument, however, conflates a procedural requirement necessary to implement a substantive guarantee with a rule that \xe2\x80\x9cregulate[s] only the manner of determining the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s culpability.\xe2\x80\x9d Schriro, supra, at 353. There are instances in which a substantive change in the law must be attended by a procedure that enables a prisoner to show that he falls within the category of persons whom the law may no longer punish. See Mackey, 401 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 692, n.\xc2\xa07 (opinion of Harlan, J.) (\xe2\x80\x9cSome rules may have both procedural and substantive ramifications, as I have used those terms here\xe2\x80\x9d). For example, when an element of a criminal offense is deemed unconstitutional, a prisoner convicted under that offense receives a new trial where the government must prove the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s conduct still fits within the modified definition of the crime. In a similar vein, when the Constitution prohibits a particular form of punishment for a class of persons, an affected prisoner receives a procedure through which he can show that he belongs to the protected class. See, e.g., Atkins v. Virginia,\n\n536 U.\xc2\xa0S. 304,\n317 (2002)\n (requiring a procedure to determine whether a particular individual with an intellectual disability \xe2\x80\x9cfall[s] within the range of [intellectually disabled] offenders about whom there is a national consensus\xe2\x80\x9d that execution is impermissible). Those procedural requirements do not, of course, transform substantive rules into procedural ones.

\n

The procedure Miller prescribes is no different. A hearing where \xe2\x80\x9cyouth and its attendant characteristics\xe2\x80\x9d are considered as sentencing factors is necessary to separate those juveniles who may be sentenced to life without parole from those who may not. 567 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 1). The hearing does not replace but rather gives effect to Miller\xe2\x80\x99s substantive holding that life without parole is an excessive sentence for children whose crimes reflect transient immaturity.

\n

Louisiana suggests that Miller cannot have made a constitutional distinction between children whose crimes reflect transient immaturity and those whose crimes reflect irreparable corruption because Miller did not require trial courts to make a finding of fact regarding a child\xe2\x80\x99s incorrigibility. That this finding is not required, however, speaks only to the degree of procedure Miller mandated in order to implement its substantive guarantee. When a new substantive rule of constitutional law is established, this Court is careful to limit the scope of any attendant procedural requirement to avoid intruding more than necessary upon the States\xe2\x80\x99 sovereign administration of their criminal justice systems. See Ford v. Wainwright,\n\n477 U.\xc2\xa0S. 399\n\xe2\x80\x93417 (1986) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e leave to the State[s] the task of developing appropriate ways to enforce the constitutional restriction upon [their] execution of sentences\xe2\x80\x9d). Fidelity to this important principle of federalism, however, should not be construed to demean the substantive character of the federal right at issue. That Miller did not impose a formal factfinding requirement does not leave States free to sentence a child whose crime reflects transient immaturity to life without parole. To the contrary, Miller established that this punishment is disproportionate under the\n \nEighth Amendment.

\n

For this reason, the death penalty cases Louisiana cites in support of its position are inapposite. See, e.g., Beard v. Banks,\n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 406,\n408 (2004)\n (holding nonretroactive the rule that forbids instructing a jury to disregard mitigating factors not found by a unanimous vote); O\xe2\x80\x99Dell v. Netherland,\n\n521 U.\xc2\xa0S. 151,\n153 (1997)\n (holding nonretroactive the rule providing that, if the prosecutor cites future dangerousness, the defendant may inform the jury of his ineligibility for parole); Sawyer v. Smith,\n\n497 U.\xc2\xa0S. 227,\n229 (1990)\n (holding nonretroactive the rule that forbids suggesting to a capital jury that it is not responsible for a death sentence). Those decisions altered the processes in which States must engage before sentencing a person to death. The processes may have had some effect on the likelihood that capital punishment would be imposed, but none of those decisions rendered a certain penalty unconstitutionally excessive for a category of offenders.

\n

The Court now holds that Miller announced a substantive rule of constitutional law. The conclusion that Miller states a substantive rule comports with the principles that informed Teague. Teague sought to balance the important goals of finality and comity with the liberty interests of those imprisoned pursuant to rules later deemed unconstitutional. Miller\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that the sentence of life without parole is disproportionate for the vast majority of juvenile offenders raises a grave risk that many are being held in violation of the Constitution.

\n

Giving Miller retroactive effect, moreover, does not require States to relitigate sentences, let alone convictions, in every case where a juvenile offender received mandatory life without parole. A State may remedy a Miller violation by permitting juvenile homicide offenders to be considered for parole, rather than by resentencing them. See, e.g., Wyo. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa76\xe2\x80\x9310\xe2\x80\x93301(c) (2013) (juvenile homicide offenders eligible for parole after 25 years). Allowing those offenders to be considered for parole ensures that juveniles whose crimes reflected only transient immaturity\xe2\x80\x94and who have since matured\xe2\x80\x94will not be forced to serve a disproportionate sentence in violation of the\n \nEighth Amendment.

\n

Extending parole eligibility to juvenile offenders does not impose an onerous burden on the States, nor does it disturb the finality of state convictions. Those prisoners who have shown an inability to reform will continue to serve life sentences. The opportunity for release will be afforded to those who demonstrate the truth of Miller\xe2\x80\x99s central intuition\xe2\x80\x94that children who commit even heinous crimes are capable of change.

\n

Petitioner has discussed in his submissions to this Court his evolution from a troubled, misguided youth to a model member of the prison community. Petitioner states that he helped establish an inmate boxing team, of which he later became a trainer and coach. He alleges that he has contributed his time and labor to the prison\xe2\x80\x99s silkscreen department and that he strives to offer advice and serve as a role model to other inmates. These claims have not been tested or even addressed by the State, so the Court does not confirm their accuracy. The petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s sub-missions are relevant, however, as an example of onekind of evidence that prisoners might use to demonstrate rehabilitation.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

Henry Montgomery has spent each day of the past 46 years knowing he was condemned to die in prison. Perhaps it can be established that, due to exceptional circumstances, this fate was a just and proportionate punishment for the crime he committed as a 17-year-old boy. In light of what this Court has said in Roper, Graham, and Miller about how children are constitutionally different from adults in their level of culpability, however, prisoners like Montgomery must be given the opportunity to show their crime did not reflect irreparable corruption; and, if it did not, their hope for some years of life outside prison walls must be restored.

\n

The judgment of the Supreme Court of Louisiana is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n

The Court has no jurisdiction to decide this case, and the decision it arrives at is wrong. I respectfully dissent.

\n

I. Jurisdiction

\n

Louisiana postconviction courts willingly entertain\n \nEighth Amendment claims but, with limited exceptions, apply the law as it existed when the state prisoner was convicted and sentenced. Shortly after this Court announced Teague v. Lane,\n\n489 U.\xc2\xa0S. 288 (1989)\n, the Louisiana Supreme Court adopted Teague\xe2\x80\x99s framework to govern the provision of postconviction remedies available to state prisoners in its state courts as a matter of state law. Taylor v. Whitley, 606 So.\xc2\xa02d 1292 (1992). In doing so, the court stated that it was \xe2\x80\x9cnot bound\xe2\x80\x9d to adopt that federal framework. Id., at 1296. One would think, then, that it is none of our business that a 69-year-old Louisiana prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s state-law motion to be resentenced according to Miller v. Alabama, 567 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012), a case announced almost half a century after his sentence was final, was met with a firm rejection on state-law grounds by the Louisiana Supreme Court. But a majority of this Court, eager to reach the merits of this case, resolves the question of our jurisdiction by deciding that the Constitution requires state postconviction courts to adopt Teague\xe2\x80\x99s exception for so-called \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive\xe2\x80\x9d new rules and to provide state-law remedies for the violations of those rules to prisoners whose sentences long ago became final. This conscription into federal service of state postconviction courts is nothing short of astonishing.

\n

A

\n

Teague announced that federal courts could not grant habeas corpus to overturn state convictions on the basis of a \xe2\x80\x9cnew rule\xe2\x80\x9d of constitutional law\xe2\x80\x94meaning one announced after the convictions became final\xe2\x80\x94unless that new rule was a \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive rule\xe2\x80\x9d or a \xe2\x80\x9cwatershed rul[e] of criminal procedure.\xe2\x80\x9d 489 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 311. The Teague prescription followed from Justice Harlan\xe2\x80\x99s view of the \xe2\x80\x9cretroactivity problem\xe2\x80\x9d detailed in his separate opinion in Desist v. United States,\n\n394 U.\xc2\xa0S. 244,\n256 (1969)\n (dissenting opinion), and later in Mackey v. United States,\n\n401 U.\xc2\xa0S. 667,\n675 (1971)\n (opinion concurring in judgment in part and dissenting in part). Placing the rule\xe2\x80\x99s first exception in context requires more analysis than the majority has applied.

\n

The Court in the mid-20th century was confounded by what Justice Harlan called the \xe2\x80\x9cswift pace of constitu-tional change,\xe2\x80\x9d Pickelsimer v. Wainwright,\n\n375 U.\xc2\xa0S. 2,\n4 (1963)\n (dissenting opinion), as it vacated and remanded many cases in the wake of Gideon v. Wainwright,\n\n372 U.\xc2\xa0S. 335 (1963)\n. Justice Harlan called upon the Court to engage in \xe2\x80\x9cinformed and deliberate consideration\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the States are constitutionally required to apply [Gideon\xe2\x80\x99s] new rule retrospectively, which may well require the reopening of cases long since finally adjudicated in accordance with then applicable decisions of this Court.\xe2\x80\x9d Pickelsimer, supra, at 3. The Court answered that call in Linkletter v. Walker,\n\n381 U.\xc2\xa0S. 618 (1965)\n. Linkletter began with the premise \xe2\x80\x9cthat we are neither required to apply, nor prohibited from applying, a decision retrospectively\xe2\x80\x9d and went on to adopt an equitable rule-by-rule approach to retroactivity, considering \xe2\x80\x9cthe prior his-tory of the rule in question, its purpose and effect, and whether retrospective operation will further or retard its operation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 629.

\n

The Linkletter framework proved unworkable when the Court began applying the rule-by-rule approach not only to cases on collateral review but also to cases on direct review, rejecting any distinction \xe2\x80\x9cbetween convictions now final\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cconvictions at various stages of trial and direct review.\xe2\x80\x9d Stovall v. Denno,\n\n388 U.\xc2\xa0S. 293,\n300 (1967)\n. It was this rejection that drew Justice Harlan\xe2\x80\x99s reproach in Desist and later in Mackey. He urged that \xe2\x80\x9call \xe2\x80\x98new\xe2\x80\x99 rules of constitutional law must, at a minimum, be applied to all those cases which are still subject to direct review by this Court at the time the \xe2\x80\x98new\xe2\x80\x99 decision is handed down.\xe2\x80\x9d Desist, supra, at 258 (dissenting opinion). \xe2\x80\x9cSimply fishing one case from the stream of appellate review, using it as a vehicle for pronouncing new constitutional standards, and then permitting a stream of similar cases subsequently to flow by unaffected by that new rule constitute an indefensible departure from th[e] model of judicial review.\xe2\x80\x9d Mackey, supra, at 679.

\n

The decision in Griffith v. Kentucky,\n\n479 U.\xc2\xa0S. 314 (1987)\n, heeded this constitutional concern. The Court jettisoned the Linkletter test for cases pending on direct review and adopted for them Justice Harlan\xe2\x80\x99s rule of redressability: \xe2\x80\x9c[F]ailure to apply a newly declared constitutional rule to criminal cases pending on direct review violates basic norms of constitutional adjudication.\xe2\x80\x9d 479 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 322 (emphasis added). We established in Griffith that this Court must play by our own \xe2\x80\x9cold rules\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94rules we have settled before the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conviction and sentence become final, even those that are a \xe2\x80\x9cclear break from existing precedent\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94for cases pending before us on direct appeal. Id., at 323. Since the Griffith rule is constitutionally compelled, we instructed the lower state and federal courts to comply with it as well. Ibid.

\n

When Teague followed on Griffith\xe2\x80\x99s heels two years later, the opinion contained no discussion of \xe2\x80\x9cbasic norms of constitutional adjudication,\xe2\x80\x9d Griffith, supra, at 322, nor any discussion of the obligations of state courts. Doing away with Linkletter for good, the Court adopted Justice Harlan\xe2\x80\x99s solution to \xe2\x80\x9cthe retroactivity problem\xe2\x80\x9d for cases pending on collateral review\xe2\x80\x94which he described not as a constitutional problem but as \xe2\x80\x9ca problem as to the scope of the habeas writ.\xe2\x80\x9d Mackey, supra, at 684 (emphasis added). Teague held that federal habeas courts could no longer upset state-court convictions for violations of so-called \xe2\x80\x9cnew rules,\xe2\x80\x9d not yet announced when the conviction became final. 489 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 310. But it allowed for the previously mentioned exceptions to this rule of nonredressability: substantive rules placing \xe2\x80\x9ccertain kinds of primary, private individual conduct beyond the power of the criminal law-making authority to proscribe\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cwatershed rules of criminal procedure.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 311. Then in Penry v. Lynaugh,\n\n492 U.\xc2\xa0S. 302 (1989)\n, the Court expanded this first exception for substantive rules to embrace new rules \xe2\x80\x9cprohibiting a certain category of punishment for a class of defendants because of their status or offense.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 330.

\n

Neither Teague nor its exceptions are constitutionally compelled. Unlike today\xe2\x80\x99s majority, the Teague-era Court understood that cases on collateral review are fundamentally different from those pending on direct review because of \xe2\x80\x9cconsiderations of finality in the judicial process.\xe2\x80\x9d Shea v. Louisiana, \n\n470 U.\xc2\xa0S. 51\n\xe2\x80\x9360 (1985). That line of finality demarcating the constitutionally required rule in Griffith from the habeas rule in Teague supplies the answer to the not-so-difficult question whether a state postconviction court must remedy the violation of a new substantive rule: No. A state court need only apply the law as it existed at the time a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conviction and sentence became final. See Griffith, supra, at 322. And once final, \xe2\x80\x9ca new rule cannot reopen a door already closed.\xe2\x80\x9d James B. Beam Distilling Co. v. Georgia,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 529,\n541 (1991)\n (opinion of Souter, J.). Any relief a prisoner might receive in a state court after finality is a matter of grace, not constitutional prescription.

\n

B

\n

The majority can marshal no case support for its con-trary position. It creates a constitutional rule where none had been before: \xe2\x80\x9cTeague\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion establishing the retroactivity of new substantive rules is best understood as resting upon constitutional premises\xe2\x80\x9d binding in both federal and state courts. Ante, at 8. \xe2\x80\x9cBest understood.\xe2\x80\x9d Because of what? Surely not because of its history and derivation.

\n

Because of the Supremacy Clause, says the majority. Ante, at 12. But the Supremacy Clause cannot possibly answer the question before us here. It only elicits another question: What federal law is supreme? Old or new? The majority\xe2\x80\x99s champion, Justice Harlan, said the old rules apply for federal habeas review of a state-court conviction: \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he habeas court need only apply the constitutional standards that prevailed at the time the original proceedings took place,\xe2\x80\x9d Desist, 394 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 263 (dissenting opinion), for a state court cannot \xe2\x80\x9ctoe the constitutional mark\xe2\x80\x9d that does not yet exist, Mackey, 401 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 687 (opinion of Harlan, J.). Following his analysis, we have clarified time and again\xe2\x80\x94recently in Greene v. Fisher, 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2011) (slip op., at 4\xe2\x80\x935)\xe2\x80\x94that federal habeas courts are to review state-court decisions against the law and factual record that existed at the time the decisions were made. \xe2\x80\x9cSection 2254(d)(1) [of the federal habeas statute] refers, in the past tense, to a state-court adjudication that \xe2\x80\x98resulted in\xe2\x80\x99 a decision that was contrary to, or \xe2\x80\x98involved\xe2\x80\x99 an unreasonable application of, established law. This backward-looking language requires an examination of the state-court decision at the time it was made.\xe2\x80\x9d Cullen v. Pinholster,\n\n563 U.\xc2\xa0S. 170\n\xe2\x80\x93182 (2011). How can it possibly be, then, that the Constitution requires a state court\xe2\x80\x99s review of its own convictions to be governed by \xe2\x80\x9cnew rules\xe2\x80\x9d rather than (what suffices when federal courts review state courts) \xe2\x80\x9cold rules\xe2\x80\x9d?

\n

The majority relies on the statement in United States v. United States Coin & Currency,\n\n401 U.\xc2\xa0S. 715 (1971)\n, that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[n]o circumstances call more for the invocation of a rule of complete retroactivity\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d than when \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the conduct being penalized is constitutionally immune from punishment.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310 (quoting 401 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 724). The majority neglects to mention that this statement was addressing the \xe2\x80\x9ccircumstances\xe2\x80\x9d of a conviction that \xe2\x80\x9chad not become final,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 724, n.\xc2\xa013 (emphasis added), when the \xe2\x80\x9crule of complete retroactivity\xe2\x80\x9d was invoked. Coin & Currency, an opinion written by (guess whom?) Justice Harlan, merely foreshadowed the rule announced in Griffith, that all cases pending on direct review receive the benefit of newly announced rules\xe2\x80\x94better termed \xe2\x80\x9cold rules\xe2\x80\x9d for such rules were announced before finality.

\n

The majority also misappropriates Yates v. Aiken,\n\n484 U.\xc2\xa0S. 211 (1988)\n, which reviewed a state habeas petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s\n \nFourteenth Amendment claim that the jury instructions at his trial lessened the State\xe2\x80\x99s burden to prove every element of his offense beyond a reasonable doubt. That case at least did involve a conviction that was final. But the majority is oblivious to the critical fact that Yates\xe2\x80\x99s claim depended upon an old rule, settled at the time of his trial. Id., at 217. This Court reversed the state habeas court for its refusal to consider that the jury instructions violated that old rule. Ibid. The majority places great weight upon the dictum in Yates that the South Carolina habeas court \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98ha[d] a duty to grant the relief that federal law requires.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13 (quoting Yates, supra, at 218). It is simply wrong to divorce that dictum from the facts it addressed. In that context, Yates merely reinforces the line drawn by Griffith: when state courts provide a forum for postconviction relief, they need to play by the \xe2\x80\x9cold rules\xe2\x80\x9d announced before the date on which a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conviction and sentence became final.

\n

The other sleight of hand performed by the majority is its emphasis on Ex parte Siebold,\n\n100 U.\xc2\xa0S. 371 (1880)\n. That case considered a petition for a federal writ of habeas corpus following a federal conviction, and the initial issue it confronted was its jurisdiction. A federal court has no inherent habeas corpus power, Ex parte Bollman, 4 Cranch 75, 94 (1807), but only that which is conferred (and limited) by statute, see, e.g., Felker v. Turpin,\n\n518 U.\xc2\xa0S. 651,\n664 (1996)\n. As Siebold stated, it was forbidden to use the federal habeas writ \xe2\x80\x9cas a mere writ of error.\xe2\x80\x9d 100 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 375. \xe2\x80\x9cThe only ground on which this court, or any court, without some special statute authorizing it, [could] give relief on habeas corpus to a prisoner under conviction and sentence of another court is the want of jurisdiction in such court over the person or the cause, or some other matter rendering its proceedings void.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Turning to the facts before it, the Court decided it was within its power to hear Siebold\xe2\x80\x99s claim, which did not merely protest that the conviction and sentence were \xe2\x80\x9cerroneous\xe2\x80\x9d but contended that the statute he was con-victed of violating was unconstitutional and the conviction therefore void: \xe2\x80\x9c[I]f the laws are unconstitutional and void, the Circuit Court acquired no jurisdiction of the causes.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 376\xe2\x80\x93377. Siebold is thus a decision that expands the limits of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s power to issue a federal habeas writ for a federal prisoner.

\n

The majority, however, divines from Siebold \xe2\x80\x9ca general principle\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9ca court has no authority to leave in place a conviction or sentence that violates a substantive rule, regardless of whether the conviction or sentence became final before the rule was announced.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11. That is utterly impossible. No \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral principle\xe2\x80\x9d can rationally be derived from Siebold about constitutionally required remedies in state courts; indeed, the opinion does not even speak to constitutionally required remedies in federal courts. It is a decision about this Court\xe2\x80\x99s statutory power to grant the Original Writ, not about its constitutional obligation to do so. Nowhere in Siebold did this Court intimate that relief was constitutionally required\xe2\x80\x94or as the majority puts it, that a court would have had \xe2\x80\x9cno authority\xe2\x80\x9d to leave in place Siebold\xe2\x80\x99s conviction, ante, at 11.

\n

The majority\xe2\x80\x99s sorry acknowledgment that \xe2\x80\x9cSiebold and the other cases discussed in this opinion, of course, do not directly control the question the Court now answers for the first time,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., is not nearly enough of a disclaimer. It is not just that they \xe2\x80\x9cdo not directly control,\xe2\x80\x9d but that the dicta cherry picked from those cases are irrelevant; they addressed circumstances fundamentally different from those to which the majority now applies them. Indeed, we know for sure that the author of some of those dicta, Justice Harlan, held views that flatly contradict the majority.

\n

The majority\xe2\x80\x99s maxim that \xe2\x80\x9cstate collateral review courts have no greater power than federal habeas courts to mandate that a prisoner continue to suffer punishment barred by the Constitution,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313, begs the question rather than contributes to its solution. Until today, no federal court was constitutionally obliged to grant relief for the past violation of a newly announced substantive rule. Until today, it was Congress\xe2\x80\x99s prerogative to do away with Teague\xe2\x80\x99s exceptions altogether. Indeed, we had left unresolved the question whether Congress had already done that when it amended a section of the habeas corpus statute to add backward-looking language governing the review of state-court decisions. See Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996, \xc2\xa7104,\n\n110Stat.\n1219, codified at\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1); Greene, 565 U.\xc2\xa0S, at ___, n. (slip op., at 5, n.). A maxim shown to be more relevant to this case, by the analysis that the majority omitted, is this: The Supremacy Clause does not impose upon state courts a constitutional obligation it fails to impose upon federal courts.

\n

C

\n

All that remains to support the majority\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion is that all-purpose Latin canon: ipse dixit. The majority opines that because a substantive rule eliminates a State\xe2\x80\x99s power to proscribe certain conduct or impose a certain punishment, it has \xe2\x80\x9cthe automatic consequence of invalidating a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conviction or sentence.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9. What provision of the Constitution could conceivably produce such a result? The Due Process Clause? It surely cannot be a denial of due process for a court to pronounce a final judgment which, though fully in accord with federal constitutional law at the time, fails to anticipate a change to be made by this Court half a century into the future. The Equal Protection Clause? Both statutory and (increasingly) constitutional laws change. If it were a denial of equal protection to hold an earlier defendant to a law more stringent than what exists today, it would also be a denial of equal protection to hold a later defendant to a law more stringent than what existed 50 years ago. No principle of equal protection requires the criminal law of all ages to be the same.

\n

The majority grandly asserts that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here is no grandfather clause that permits States to enforce punishments the Constitution forbids.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12 (emphasis added). Of course the italicized phrase begs the question. There most certainly is a grandfather clause\xe2\x80\x94one we have called finality\xe2\x80\x94which says that the Constitution does not require States to revise punishments that were lawful when they were imposed. Once a conviction has become final, whether new rules or old ones will be applied to revisit the conviction is a matter entirely within the State\xe2\x80\x99s control; the Constitution has nothing to say about that choice. The majority says that there is no \xe2\x80\x9cpossibility of a valid result\xe2\x80\x9d when a new substantive rule is not applied retroactively. Ante, at 9. But the whole controversy here arises because many think there is a valid result when a defendant has been convicted under the law that existed when his conviction became final. And the States are unquestionably entitled to take that view of things.

\n

The majority\xe2\x80\x99s imposition of Teague\xe2\x80\x99s first exception upon the States is all the worse because it does not adhere to that exception as initially conceived by Justice Harlan\xe2\x80\x94an exception for rules that \xe2\x80\x9cplace, as a matter of constitutional interpretation, certain kinds of primary, private individual conduct beyond the power of the criminal lawmaking authority to proscribe.\xe2\x80\x9d Mackey, 401 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 692 (emphasis added). Rather, it endorses the exception as expanded by Penry, to include \xe2\x80\x9crules prohibiting a certain category of punishment for a class of defendants because of their status or offense.\xe2\x80\x9d 492 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 330. That expansion empowered and obligated federal (and after today state) habeas courts to invoke this Court\xe2\x80\x99s\n \nEighth Amendment \xe2\x80\x9cevolving standards of decency\xe2\x80\x9d jurisprudence to upset punishments that were constitutional when imposed but are \xe2\x80\x9ccruel and unusual,\xe2\x80\x9d U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Amdt. 8, in our newly enlightened society. See Trop v. Dulles,\n\n356 U.\xc2\xa0S. 86,\n101 (1958)\n. The \xe2\x80\x9cevolving standards\xe2\x80\x9d test concedes that in 1969 the State had the power to punish Henry Montgomery as it did. Indeed, Montgomery could at that time have been sentenced to death by our yet unevolved society. Even 20 years later, this Court reaffirmed that the Constitution posed no bar to death sentences for juveniles. Stanford v. Kentucky,\n\n492 U.\xc2\xa0S. 361 (1989)\n. Not until our People\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cstandards of decency\xe2\x80\x9d evolved a mere 10 years ago\xe2\x80\x94nearly 40 years after Montgomery\xe2\x80\x99s sentence was imposed\xe2\x80\x94did this Court declare the death penalty unconstitutional for juveniles. Roper v. Simmons,\n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 551 (2005)\n. Even then, the Court reassured States that \xe2\x80\x9cthe punishment of life imprisonment without the possibility of parole is itself a severe sanction,\xe2\x80\x9d implicitly still available for juveniles. Id., at 572. And again five years ago this Court left in place this severe sanction for juvenile homicide offenders. Graham v. Florida,\n\n560 U.\xc2\xa0S. 48,\n69 (2010)\n. So for the five decades Montgomery has spent in prison, not one of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents called into question the legality of his sentence\xe2\x80\x94until the People\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cstandards of decency,\xe2\x80\x9d as perceived by five Justices, \xe2\x80\x9cevolved\xe2\x80\x9d yet again in Miller.

\n

Teague\xe2\x80\x99s central purpose was to do away with the old regime\xe2\x80\x99s tendency to \xe2\x80\x9ccontinually force the States to marshal resources in order to keep in prison defendants whose trials and appeals conformed to then-existing constitutional standards.\xe2\x80\x9d 489 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 310. Today\xe2\x80\x99s holding thwarts that purpose with a vengeance. Our ever-evolving Constitution changes the rules of \xe2\x80\x9ccruel and unusual punishments\xe2\x80\x9d every few years. In the passage from Mackey that the majority\xe2\x80\x99s opinion quotes, ante, at 13, Justice Harlan noted the diminishing force of finality (and hence the equitable propriety\xe2\x80\x94not the constitutional requirement\xe2\x80\x94of disregarding it) when the law punishes nonpunishable conduct, see 401 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 693. But one cannot imagine a clearer frustration of the sensible policy of Teague when the ever-moving target of impermissible punishments is at issue. Today\xe2\x80\x99s holding not only fore-closes Congress from eliminating this expansion of Teague in federal courts, but also foists this distortion upon the States.

\n

II. The Retroactivity of Miller

\n

Having created jurisdiction by ripping Teague\xe2\x80\x99s first exception from its moorings, converting an equitable rule governing federal habeas relief to a constitutional command governing state courts as well, the majority proceeds to the merits. And here it confronts a second obstacle to its desired outcome. Miller, the opinion it wishes to impose upon state postconviction courts, simply does not decree what the first part of the majority\xe2\x80\x99s opinion says Teague\xe2\x80\x99s first exception requires to be given retroactive effect: a rule \xe2\x80\x9cset[ting] forth categorical constitutional guarantees that place certain criminal laws and punishments altogether beyond the State\xe2\x80\x99s power to impose.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9 (emphasis added). No problem. Having distorted Teague, the majority simply proceeds to rewrite Miller.

\n

The majority asserts that Miller \xe2\x80\x9crendered life without parole an unconstitutional penalty for \xe2\x80\x98a class of defendants because of their status\xe2\x80\x99\xe2\x80\x94that is, juvenile offenders whose crimes reflect the transient immaturity of youth.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 17. It insists that Miller barred life-without-parole sentences \xe2\x80\x9cfor all but the rarest of juvenile offenders, those whose crimes reflect permanent incorrigibility. For that reason, Miller is no less substantive than are Roper and Graham.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 17\xe2\x80\x9318. The problem is that Miller stated, quite clearly, precisely the opposite: \xe2\x80\x9cOur decision does not categorically bar a penalty for a class of offenders or type of crime\xe2\x80\x94as, for example, we did in Roper or Graham. Instead, it mandates only that a sentencer follow a certain process\xe2\x80\x94considering an offender\xe2\x80\x99s youth and attendant characteristics\xe2\x80\x94before imposing a particular penalty.\xe2\x80\x9d 567 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 20) (emphasis added).

\n

To contradict that clear statement, the majority opinion quotes passages from Miller that assert such things as \xe2\x80\x9cmandatory life-without-parole sentences for children \xe2\x80\x98pos[e] too great a risk of disproportionate punishment\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98appropriate occasions for sentencing juveniles to this harshest possible penalty will be uncommon.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16 (quoting Miller, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 17)). But to say that a punishment might be inappropriate and disproportionate for certain juvenile offenders is not to say that it is unconstitutionally void. All of the statements relied on by the majority do nothing more than express the reason why the new, youth-protective procedure prescribed by Miller is desirable: to deter life sentences for certain juvenile offenders. On the issue of whether Miller rendered life-without-parole penalties unconstitutional, it is impossible to get past Miller\xe2\x80\x99s unambiguous statement that \xe2\x80\x9c[o]ur decision does not categorically bar a penalty for a class of offenders\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cmandates only that a sentencer follow a certain process .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. before imposing a particular penalty.\xe2\x80\x9d 567 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 20). It is plain as day that the majority is not applying Miller, but rewriting it.[1]

\n

And the rewriting has consequences beyond merely making Miller\xe2\x80\x99s procedural guarantee retroactive. If, indeed, a State is categorically prohibited from imposing life without parole on juvenile offenders whose crimes do not \xe2\x80\x9creflect permanent incorrigibility,\xe2\x80\x9d then even when the procedures that Miller demands are provided the constitutional requirement is not necessarily satisfied. It remains available for the defendant sentenced to life without parole to argue that his crimes did not in fact \xe2\x80\x9creflect permanent incorrigibility.\xe2\x80\x9d Or as the majority\xe2\x80\x99s opinion puts it: \xe2\x80\x9cThat Miller did not impose a formal factfinding requirement does not leave States free to sentence a child[[2]] whose crime reflects transient immaturity to life without parole. To the contrary, Miller established that this punishment is disproportionate under the\n \nEighth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 20.

\n

How wonderful. Federal and (like it or not) state judges are henceforth to resolve the knotty \xe2\x80\x9clegal\xe2\x80\x9d question: whether a 17-year-old who murdered an innocent sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s deputy half a century ago was at the time of his trial \xe2\x80\x9cincorrigible.\xe2\x80\x9d Under Miller, bear in mind, the inquiry is whether the inmate was seen to be incorrigible when he was sentenced\xe2\x80\x94not whether he has proven corrigible and so can safely be paroled today. What silliness. (And how impossible in practice, see Brief for National District Attorneys Assn. et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 9\xe2\x80\x9317.) When in Lockett v. Ohio,\n\n438 U.\xc2\xa0S. 586,\n608 (1978)\n, the Court imposed the thitherto unheard-of requirement that the sentencer in capital cases must consider and weigh all \xe2\x80\x9crelevant mitigating factors,\xe2\x80\x9d it at least did not impose the substantive (and hence judicially reviewable) requirement that the aggravators must outweigh the mitigators; it would suffice that the sentencer thought so. And, fairly read, Miller did the same. Not so with the \xe2\x80\x9cincorrigibility\xe2\x80\x9d requirement that the Court imposes today to make Miller retroactive.

\n

But have no fear. The majority does not seriously expect state and federal collateral-review tribunals to engage in this silliness, probing the evidence of \xe2\x80\x9cincorrigibility\xe2\x80\x9d that existed decades ago when defendants were sentenced. What the majority expects (and intends) to happen is set forth in the following not-so-subtle invitation: \xe2\x80\x9cA State may remedy a Miller violation by permitting juvenile homicide offenders to be considered for parole, rather than by resentencing them.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 21. Of course. This whole exercise, this whole distortion of Miller, is just a devious way of eliminating life without parole for juvenile offenders. The Court might have done that expressly (as we know, the Court can decree anything), but that would have been something of an embarrassment. After all, one of the justifications the Court gave for decreeing an end to the death penalty for murders (no matter how many) committed by a juvenile was that life without parole was a severe enough punishment. See Roper, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 572. How could the majority\xe2\x80\x94in an opinion written by the very author of Roper\xe2\x80\x94now say that punishment is also unconstitutional? The Court expressly refused to say so in Miller. 567 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 17). So the Court refuses again today, but merely makes imposition of that severe sanction a practical impossibility. And then, in Godfather fashion, the majority makes state legislatures an offer they can\xe2\x80\x99t refuse: Avoid all the utterly impossible nonsense we have prescribed by simply \xe2\x80\x9cpermitting juvenile homicide offenders to be considered for parole.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 21. Mission accomplished.

HENRY MONTGOMERY, PETITIONER v. LOUISIANA

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of louisiana

\n

[January 25, 2016]

\n

\n

Justice Thomas, dissenting.

\n

I join Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s dissent. I write separately to explain why the Court\xe2\x80\x99s resolution of the jurisdictional question, ante, at 5\xe2\x80\x9314, lacks any foundation in the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s text or our historical traditions. We have jurisdiction under\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71257 only if the Louisiana Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision implicates a federal right. That condition is satisfied, the Court holds, because the Constitution purportedly requires state and federal postconviction courts to give \xe2\x80\x9cretroactive effect\xe2\x80\x9d to new substantive constitutional rules by applying them to overturn long-final convictions and sentences. Ante, at 8. Because our Constitution and traditions embrace no such right, I respectfully dissent.

\n

I

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[O]ur jurisprudence concerning the \xe2\x80\x98retroactivity\xe2\x80\x99 of \xe2\x80\x98new rules\xe2\x80\x99 of constitutional law is primarily concerned, not with the question whether a constitutional violation occurred, but with the availability or nonavailability of remedies.\xe2\x80\x9d Danforth v. Minnesota,\n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 264\n\xe2\x80\x93291 (2008). Accordingly, the issue in this case is not whether prisoners who received mandatory life-without-parole sentences for crimes they committed decades ago as juveniles had an\n \nEighth Amendment right not to receive such a sentence. Rather, the question is how, when, and in what forum that newfound right can be enforced. See ibid.

\n

The Court answers that question one way: It says that state postconviction and federal habeas courts are constitutionally required to supply a remedy because a sentence or conviction predicated upon an unconstitutional law is a legal nullity. See ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x9314. But nothing in the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s text or in our constitutional tradition provides such a right to a remedy on collateral review.

\n

A

\n

No provision of the Constitution supports the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding. The Court invokes only the Supremacy Clause, asserting that the Clause deprives state and federal postconviction courts alike of power to leave an unconstitutional sentence in place. Ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313. But that leaves the question of what provision of the Constitution supplies that underlying prohibition.

\n

The Supremacy Clause does not do so. That Clause merely supplies a rule of decision: If a federal constitutional right exists, that right supersedes any contrary provisions of state law. See Art.\xc2\xa0VI, cl.\xc2\xa02 (\xe2\x80\x9cThis Constitution, and the Laws of the United States which shall be made in Pursuance thereof .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall be the supreme Law of the Land; and the Judges in every State shall be bound thereby, any Thing in the Constitution or Laws of any State to the Contrary notwithstanding\xe2\x80\x9d). Accordingly, as we reaffirmed just last Term, the Supremacy Clause is no independent font of substantive rights. Armstrong v. Exceptional Child Center, Inc., 575 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2015) (slip op., at 3).

\n

Nor am I aware of any other provision in the Constitution that would support the Court\xe2\x80\x99s new constitutional right to retroactivity. Of the natural places to look\xe2\x80\x94Article III, the Due Process Clauses of the Fifth and\n \nFourteenth Amendments, and the Equal Protection Clause of the\n \nFourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x94none establishes a right to void an unconstitutional sentence that has long been final.

\n

To begin, Article III does not contain the requirement that the Court announces today. Article III vests \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he judicial Power\xe2\x80\x9d in this Court and whatever inferior courts Congress creates, Art. III, \xc2\xa71, and \xe2\x80\x9cextend[s]\xe2\x80\x9d that power to various \xe2\x80\x9cCases .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and Controversies,\xe2\x80\x9d Art. III, \xc2\xa72. Article III thus defines the scope of federal judicial power. It cannot compel state postconviction courts to apply new substantive rules retroactively.

\n

Even if the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding were limited to federal courts, Article III would not justify it. The nature of \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial power\xe2\x80\x9d may constrain the retroactivity rules that Article III courts can apply.[1]* But even our broad modern precedents treat Article III as requiring courts to apply new rules only on direct review. Thus in Griffith v. Kentucky,\n\n479 U.\xc2\xa0S. 314 (1987)\n, the Court suggested\xe2\x80\x94based on Justice Harlan\xe2\x80\x99s views\xe2\x80\x94that \xe2\x80\x9cafter we have decided a new rule in the case selected, the integrity of judicial review requires that we apply that rule to all similar cases pending on direct review.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 322\xe2\x80\x93323. But, as Justice Harlan had explained, that view of Article III has no force on collateral review: \xe2\x80\x9cWhile the entire theoretical underpinnings of judicial review and constitutional supremacy dictate that federal courts having jurisdiction on direct review adjudicate every issue of law .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. fairly implicated by the trial process below and properly presented on appeal, federal courts have never had a similar obligation on habeas corpus.\xe2\x80\x9d Mackey v. United States,\n\n401 U.\xc2\xa0S. 667,\n682 (1971)\n (opinion concurring in judgment in part and dissenting in part).

\n

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding also cannot be grounded in the Due Process Clause\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition on \xe2\x80\x9cdepriv[ations] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law.\xe2\x80\x9d Amdts. V and XIV, \xc2\xa71. Quite possibly, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[d]ue process of law\xe2\x80\x99 was originally used as a shorthand expression for governmental proceedings according to the \xe2\x80\x98law of the land\xe2\x80\x99 as it existed at the time of those proceedings.\xe2\x80\x9d In re Winship,\n\n397 U.\xc2\xa0S. 358,\n378 (1970)\n (Black, J., dissenting) (emphasis added); accord, Johnson v. United States, 576 U.\xc2\xa0S.___, ___ (2015) (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment)(slip op., at 17). Under that understanding, due process excluded any right to have new substantive rules apply retroactively.

\n

Even if due process required courts to anticipate this Court\xe2\x80\x99s new substantive rules, it would not compel courts to revisit settled convictions or sentences on collateral review. We have never understood due process to require further proceedings once a trial ends. The Clause \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not establish any right to an appeal .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and certainly does not establish any right to collaterally attack a final judgment of conviction.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. MacCollom,\n\n426 U.\xc2\xa0S. 317,\n323 (1976)\n (plurality opinion); see Pennsylvania v. Finley,\n\n481 U.\xc2\xa0S. 551,\n557 (1987)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cStates have no obligation to provide [postconviction] relief\xe2\x80\x9d). Because the Constitution does not require postconviction remedies, it certainly does not require postconviction courts to revisit every potential type of error. Cf. Martinez v. Court of Appeal of Cal., Fourth Appellate Dist.,\n\n528 U.\xc2\xa0S. 152\n\xe2\x80\x93166 (2000) (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9cSince a State could .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. subject its trial-court determinations to no review whatever, it could a fortiori subject them to review which consists of a nonadversarial reexamination of convictions by a panel of government experts\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Nor can the Equal Protection Clause justify requiring courts on collateral review to apply new substantive rules retroactively. That Clause prohibits a State from \xe2\x80\x9cdeny[ing] to any person within its jurisdiction the equal protection of the laws.\xe2\x80\x9d Amdt. XIV, \xc2\xa71. But under our precedents \xe2\x80\x9ca classification neither involving fundamental rights nor proceeding along suspect lines .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. cannot run afoul of the Equal Protection Clause if there is a rational relationship between the disparity of treatment and some legitimate governmental purpose.\xe2\x80\x9d Armour v. Indianapolis, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 6) (internal quotation marks omitted; ellipsis in original).

\n

The disparity the Court eliminates today\xe2\x80\x94between prisoners whose cases were on direct review when this Court announced a new substantive constitutional rule, and those whose convictions had already become final\xe2\x80\x94is one we have long considered rational. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he notion that different standards should apply on direct and collateral review runs throughout our recent habeas jurisprudence.\xe2\x80\x9d Wright v. West,\n\n505 U.\xc2\xa0S. 277,\n292 (1992)\n; see Brecht v. Abrahamson,\n\n507 U.\xc2\xa0S. 619\n\xe2\x80\x93635 (1993). Thus, our precedents recognize a right to counsel on direct review, but not in collateral proceedings. Compare Douglas v. California,\n\n372 U.\xc2\xa0S. 353\n\xe2\x80\x93358 (1963) (courts must provide counsel on an initial direct appeal), with Finley, supra, at 555 (no such right on habeas). The\n \nFourth Amendment also applies differently on direct and collateral review. Compare Mapp v. Ohio,\n\n367 U.\xc2\xa0S. 643\n\xe2\x80\x93660 (1961) (courts on direct review must exclude evidence obtained in violation of the\n \nFourth Amendment), with Stone v. Powell,\n\n428 U.\xc2\xa0S. 465\n\xe2\x80\x93496 (1976) (no relitigation of such claims on collateral review).

\n

These distinctions are reasonable. They reflect the \xe2\x80\x9csignificant costs\xe2\x80\x9d of collateral review, including disruption of \xe2\x80\x9cthe State\xe2\x80\x99s significant interest in repose for concluded litigation.\xe2\x80\x9d Wright, supra, at 293 (internal quotation marks omitted). Our equal protection precedents, therefore, do not compel a uniform rule of retroactivity in direct and collateral proceedings for new substantive constitutional rules.

\n

B

\n

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s new constitutional right also finds no basis in the history of state and federal postconviction proceedings. Throughout our history, postconviction relief for alleged constitutional defects in a conviction or sentence was available as a matter of legislative grace, not constitutional command.

\n

The Constitution mentions habeas relief only in the Suspension Clause, which specifies that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Privilege of the Writ of Habeas Corpus shall not be suspended, unless when in Cases of Rebellion or Invasion the public Safety may require it.\xe2\x80\x9d Art. I, \xc2\xa79, cl. 2. But that Clause does not specify the scope of the writ. And the First Congress, in prescribing federal habeas jurisdiction in the 1789 Judiciary Act, understood its scope to reflect \xe2\x80\x9cthe black-letter principle of the common law that the writ was simply not available at all to one convicted of crime by a court of competent jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Bator, Finality in Criminal Law and Federal Habeas Corpus for State Prisoners, 76 Harv. L. Rev. 441, 466 (1963). Early cases echoed that understanding. E.g., Ex parte Watkins, 3 Pet. 193, 202 (1830) (\xe2\x80\x9cAn imprisonment under a judgment cannot be unlawful, unless that judgment be an absolute nullity; and it is not a nullity if the court has general jurisdiction of the subject, although it should be erroneous\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

For nearly a century thereafter, this Court understood the Judiciary Act and successor provisions as limiting habeas relief to instances where the court that rendered the judgment lacked jurisdiction over the general category of offense or the person of the prisoner. See Wright, supra, at 285 (recounting history). Federal habeas courts thus afforded no remedy for a claim that a sentence or conviction was predicated on an unconstitutional law. Nor did States. Indeed, until 1836, Vermont made no provision for any state habeas proceedings. See Oaks, Habeas Corpus in the States 1776\xe2\x80\x931865, 32 U. Chi. L. Rev. 243, 250 (1965). Even when States allowed collateral attacks in state court, review was unavailable if the judgment of conviction was rendered by a court with general jurisdiction over the subject matter and the defendant. Id., at 261\xe2\x80\x93262.

\n

The Court portrays Ex parte Siebold,\n\n100 U.\xc2\xa0S. 371 (1880)\n, as a departure from this history and as the genesis of a constitutional principle that \xe2\x80\x9ca conviction obtained under an unconstitutional law warrants habeas relief.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12. But Siebold\xe2\x80\x94a case construing the scope of federal habeas review under the 1789 Judiciary Act\xe2\x80\x94does not support the Court\xe2\x80\x99s position. Ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x938 (Scalia, J., dissenting). Siebold did not imply that the Constitution requires courts to stop enforcing convictions under an unconstitutional law. Rather, Siebold assumed that prisoners would lack a remedy if the federal habeas statute did not allow challenges to such convictions. 100 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 377 (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is true, if no writ of error lies, the judgment may be final, in the sense that there may be no means of reversing it\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Moreover, when Congress authorized appeals as a matter of right in federal criminal cases, the Court renounced Siebold and stopped entertaining federal habeas chal-lenges to the constitutionality of the statute under which a defendant was sentenced or convicted. See Bator, supra, at 473\xe2\x80\x93474, and n. 77. If the Constitution prevented courts from enforcing a void conviction or sentence even after the conviction is final, this Court would have been incapable of withdrawing relief.

\n

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s purported constitutional right to retroactiv-ity on collateral review has no grounding even in our mod-ern precedents. In the 1950\xe2\x80\x99s, this Court began recognizing many new constitutional rights in criminal proceedings. Even then, however, the Court did not perceive any constitutional right for prisoners to vacate their convictions or sentences on collateral review based on the Court\xe2\x80\x99s new interpretations of the Constitution. To the contrary, the Court derived Miranda warnings and the exclusionary rule from the Constitution, yet drew the line at creating a constitutional right to retroactivity. E.g., Linkletter v. Walker,\n\n381 U.\xc2\xa0S. 618,\n629 (1965)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he Constitution neither prohibits nor requires retrospective effect. As Justice Cardozo said, \xe2\x80\x98We think the Federal Constitution has no voice upon the subject\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Only in 1987, in Griffith v. Kentucky,\n\n479 U.\xc2\xa0S. 314\n, did this Court change course and hold that the Constitution requires courts to give constitutional rights some retroactive effect. Even then, Griffith was a directive only to courts on direct review. It held that \xe2\x80\x9ca new rule for the conduct of criminal prosecutions is to be applied retroactively to all cases, state or federal, pending on direct review or not yet final.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 328. It said nothing about what happens once a case becomes final. That was resolved in Teague v. Lane,\n\n489 U.\xc2\xa0S. 288 (1989)\n\xe2\x80\x94which announced the narrow exceptions to the rule against retroactivity on collateral review\xe2\x80\x94but which did so by interpreting the scope of the federal habeas writ, not the Constitution.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

Not only does the Court\xe2\x80\x99s novel constitutional right lack any constitutional foundation; the reasoning the Court uses to construct this right lacks any logical stopping point. If, as the Court supposes, the Constitution bars courts from insisting that prisoners remain in prison when their convictions or sentences are later deemed unconstitutional, why can courts let stand a judgment that wrongly decided any constitutional question?

\n

The Court confronted this question when Siebold and other cases began expanding the federal habeas statute to encompass claims that a sentence or conviction was constitutionally void. But the Court could not find a satisfactory answer: \xe2\x80\x9cA judgment may be erroneous and not void, and it may be erroneous because it is void. The distinctions .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. are very nice, and they may fall under the one class or the other as they are regarded for different purposes.\xe2\x80\x9d Ex parte Lange, 18 Wall. 163, 175\xe2\x80\x93176 (1874).

\n

The lack of any limiting principle became apparent as the Court construed the federal habeas statute to supply jurisdiction to address prerequisites to a valid sentence or conviction (like an indictment). See Bator, 76 Harv. L.\xc2\xa0Rev., at 467\xe2\x80\x93468, and n. 56, 471. As Justice Bradley, Siebold\xe2\x80\x99s author, later observed for the Court: \xe2\x80\x9cIt is difficult to see why a conviction and punishment under an unconstitutional law is more violative of a person\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional rights, than an unconstitutional conviction and punishment under a valid law.\xe2\x80\x9d In re Nielsen,\n\n131 U.\xc2\xa0S. 176,\n183 (1889)\n.

\n

I doubt that today\xe2\x80\x99s rule will fare any better. By refashioning Siebold as the foundation of a purported constitutional right, the Court transforms an unworkable doctrine into an immutable command. Because Justice Bradley\xe2\x80\x99s dicta in Siebold was a gloss on the 1789 Judiciary Act, Congress could at least supply a fix to it. But the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reinvention of Siebold as a constitutional imperative eliminates any room for legislative adjustment.

\n

B

\n

There is one silver lining to today\xe2\x80\x99s ruling: States still have a way to mitigate its impact on their court systems. As the Court explains, States must enforce a constitutional right to remedies on collateral review only if such pro-ceedings are \xe2\x80\x9copen to a claim controlled by federal law.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13. State courts, on collateral review, thus must provide remedies for claims under Miller v. Alabama, 567 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012), only if those courts are open to \xe2\x80\x9cclaims that a decision of this Court has rendered certain sentences illegal .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. under the\n \nEighth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d See ante,at 13.

\n

Unlike the rule the Court announces today, this limitation at least reflects a constitutional principle. Only when state courts have chosen to entertain a federal claim can the Supremacy Clause conceivably command a state court to apply federal law. As we explained last Term, private parties have no \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutional .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. right to enforce federal laws against the States.\xe2\x80\x9d Armstrong, 575 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 4). Instead, the Constitution leaves the initial choice to entertain federal claims up to state courts, which are \xe2\x80\x9ctribunals over which the government of the Union has no adequate control, and which may be closed to any claim asserted under a law of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Osborn v. Bank of United States, 9 Wheat. 738, 821 (1824).

\n

States therefore have a modest path to lessen the burdens that today\xe2\x80\x99s decision will inflict on their courts. States can stop entertaining claims alleging that this Court\xe2\x80\x99s\n \nEighth Amendment decisions invalidated a sentence, and leave federal habeas courts to shoulder the burden of adjudicating such claims in the first instance. Whatever the desirability of that choice, it is one the Constitution allows States to make.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision repudiates established principles of finality. It finds no support in the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s text, and cannot be reconciled with our Nation\xe2\x80\x99s tradition of considering the availability of postconviction remedies a matter about which the Constitution has nothing to say. I respectfully dissent.
