LEWIS et\xc2\xa0al. v. CITY OF CHICAGO, ILLINOIS

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the seventh circuit

\n

No. 08\xe2\x80\x93974.\xe2\x80\x83Argued February 22, 2010\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 24, 2010

\n

In 1995, respondent the City of Chicago gave a written examination to applicants seeking firefighter positions. In January 1996, the City announced it would draw candidates randomly from a list of applicants who scored at least 89 out of 100 points on the examination, whom it designated as \xe2\x80\x9cwell qualified.\xe2\x80\x9d It informed those who scored below 65 that they had failed and would not be considered further. It informed applicants who scored between 65 and 88, whom it designated as \xe2\x80\x9cqualified,\xe2\x80\x9d that it was unlikely they would be called for further processing but that the City would keep them on the eligibility list for as long as that list was used. That May, the City selected its first class of applicants to advance, and it repeated this process multiple times over the next six years. Beginning in March 1997, several African-American applicants who scored in the \xe2\x80\x9cqualified\xe2\x80\x9d range but had not been hired filed discrimination charges with the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) and received right-to-sue letters. They then filed suit, alleging (as relevant here) that the City\xe2\x80\x99s practice of selecting only applicants who scored 89 or above had a disparate impact on African-Americans in violation of Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, see 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x932(k)(1)(A)(i). The District Court certified a class\xe2\x80\x94petitioners here\xe2\x80\x94of African-Americans who scored in the \xe2\x80\x9cqualified\xe2\x80\x9d range but were not hired. The court denied the City\xe2\x80\x99s summary judgment motion, rejecting its claim that petitioners had failed to file EEOC charges within 300 days \xe2\x80\x9cafter the unlawful employment practice occurred,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x935(e)(1), and finding instead that the City\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9congoing reliance\xe2\x80\x9d on the 1995 test results constituted a continuing Title VII violation. The litigation then proceeded, and petitioners prevailed on the merits. The Seventh Circuit reversed the judgment in their favor, holding that the suit was untimely because the earliest EEOC charge was filed more than 300 days after the only discriminatory act\xe2\x80\x94sorting the scores into the \xe2\x80\x9cwell qualified,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cqualified,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cnot qualified\xe2\x80\x9d categories. The later hiring decisions, the Seventh Circuit held, were an automatic consequence of the test scores, not new discriminatory acts.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0A plaintiff who does not file a timely charge challenging the adoption of a practice may assert a disparate-impact claim in a timely charge challenging the employer\xe2\x80\x99s later application of that practice as long as he alleges each of the elements of a disparate-impact claim. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Determining whether petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 charges were timely requires \xe2\x80\x9cidentify[ing] precisely the \xe2\x80\x98unlawful employment practice\xe2\x80\x99 of which\xe2\x80\x9d they complain. Delaware State College v. Ricks, 449 U. S. 250, 257. With the exception of the first selection round, all agree that the challenged practice here\xe2\x80\x94the City\xe2\x80\x99s selection of firefighter hires on the basis announced in 1996\xe2\x80\x94occurred within the charging period. Thus, the question is not whether a claim predicated on that conduct is timely, but whether the practice thus defined can be the basis for a disparate-impact claim at all. It can. A Title VII plaintiff establishes a prima facie claim by showing that the employer \xe2\x80\x9cuses a particular employment practice that causes a disparate impact\xe2\x80\x9d on one of the prohibited bases. \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x932(k). The term \xe2\x80\x9cemployment practice\xe2\x80\x9d clearly encompasses the conduct at issue: exclusion of passing applicants who scored below 89 when selecting those who would advance. The City \xe2\x80\x9cuse[d]\xe2\x80\x9d that practice each time it filled a new class of firefighters, and petitioners allege that doing so caused a disparate impact. It is irrelevant that subsection (k) does not address \xe2\x80\x9caccrual\xe2\x80\x9d of disparate-impact claims, since the issue here is not when the claims accrued but whether the claims stated a violation. They did. Whether petitioners proved a violation is not before the Court. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The City argues that the only actionable discrimination occurred in 1996 when it used the test results to create the hiring list, which it concedes was unlawful. It may be true that the City\xe2\x80\x99s adoption in 1996 of the cutoff score gave rise to a freestanding disparate-impact claim. If so, because no timely charge was filed, the City is now \xe2\x80\x9centitled to treat that past act as lawful,\xe2\x80\x9d United Air Lines, Inc. v. Evans, 431 U. S. 553, 558. But it does not follow that no new violation occurred\xe2\x80\x94and no new claims could arise\xe2\x80\x94when the City later implemented the 1996 decision. Evans and later cases the City cites establish only that a Title VII plaintiff must show a \xe2\x80\x9cpresent violation\xe2\x80\x9d within the limitations period. For disparate-treatment claims\xe2\x80\x94which require discriminatory intent\xe2\x80\x94the plaintiff must demonstrate deliberate discrimination within the limitations period. But no such demonstration is needed for claims, such as this one, that do not require discriminatory intent. Cf., e.g., Ledbetter v. Goodyear Tire & Rubber Co., 550 U. S. 618, 640. Contrary to the Seventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning, even if both types of claims take aim at prohibited discrimination, it does not follow that their reach is coextensive. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0The City and its amici warn that this reading will result in a host of practical problems for employers and employees alike. The Court, however, must give effect to the law Congress enacted, not assess the consequences of each approach and adopt the one that produces the least mischief. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(d)\xc2\xa0It is left to the Seventh Circuit to determine whether the judgment must be modified to the extent that the District Court awarded relief based on the first round of hiring, which occurred outside the charging period even for the earliest EEOC charge. P.\xc2\xa011.

\n

528 F.\xc2\xa03d 488, reversed and remanded.

\n

ARTHUR L. LEWIS, Jr., et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. CITY OF CHICAGO, ILLINOIS

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the seventh circuit

\n

[May 24, 2010]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 prohibits employers from using employment practices that cause a disparate impact on the basis of race (among other bases). 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x932(k)(1)(A)(i). It also requires plaintiffs, before beginning a federal lawsuit, to file a timely charge of discrimination with the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC). \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x935(e)(1). We consider whether a plaintiff who does not file a timely charge challenging the adoption of a practice\xe2\x80\x94here, an employer\xe2\x80\x99s decision to exclude employment applicants who did not achieve a certain score on an examination\xe2\x80\x94may assert a disparate-impact claim in a timely charge challenging the employer\xe2\x80\x99s later application of that practice.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In July 1995, the City of Chicago administered a written examination to over 26,000 applicants seeking to serve in the Chicago Fire Department. After scoring the examinations, the City reported the results. It announced in a January 26, 1996, press release that it would begin drawing randomly from the top tier of scorers, i.e., those who scored 89 or above (out of 100), whom the City called \xe2\x80\x9cwell qualified.\xe2\x80\x9d Those drawn from this group would proceed to the next phase\xe2\x80\x94a physical-abilities test, background check, medical examination, and drug test\xe2\x80\x94and if they cleared those hurdles would be hired as candidate firefighters. Those who scored below 65, on the other hand, learned by letters sent the same day that they had failed the test. Each was told he had not achieved a passing score, would no longer be considered for a firefighter\nposition, and would not be contacted again about the examination.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The applicants in-between\xe2\x80\x94those who scored between 65 and 88, whom the City called \xe2\x80\x9cqualified\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 1]\xe2\x80\x94were notified that they had passed the examination but that, based on the City\xe2\x80\x99s projected hiring needs and the number of \xe2\x80\x9cwell-qualified\xe2\x80\x9d applicants, it was not likely they would be called for further processing. The individual notices added, however, that because it was not possible to predict how many applicants would be hired in the next few years, each \xe2\x80\x9cqualified\xe2\x80\x9d applicant\xe2\x80\x99s name would be kept on the eligibility list maintained by the Department of Personnel for as long as that list was used. Eleven days later, the City officially adopted an \xe2\x80\x9cEligible List\xe2\x80\x9d reflecting the breakdown described above.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On May 16, 1996, the City selected its first class of applicants to advance to the next stage. It selected a second on October 1, 1996, and repeated the process nine more times over the next six years. As it had announced, in each round the City drew randomly from among those who scored in the \xe2\x80\x9cwell-qualified\xe2\x80\x9d range on the 1995 test. In the last round it exhausted that pool, so it filled the remaining slots with \xe2\x80\x9cqualified\xe2\x80\x9d candidates instead.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On March 31, 1997, Crawford M. Smith, an African-American applicant who scored in the \xe2\x80\x9cqualified\xe2\x80\x9d range and had not been hired as a candidate firefighter, filed a charge of discrimination with the EEOC. Five others followed suit, and on July 28, 1998, the EEOC issued all six of them right-to-sue letters. Two months later, they filed this civil action against the City, alleging (as relevant here) that its practice of selecting for advancement only applicants who scored 89 or above caused a disparate impact on African-Americans in violation of Title VII. The District Court certified a class\xe2\x80\x94petitioners here\xe2\x80\x94consisting of the more than 6,000 African-Americans who scored in the \xe2\x80\x9cqualified\xe2\x80\x9d range on the 1995 examination but had not been hired.[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The City sought summary judgment on the ground that petitioners had failed to file EEOC charges within 300 days after their claims accrued. See \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x935(e)(1). The District Court denied the motion, concluding that the City\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9congoing reliance\xe2\x80\x9d on the 1995 test results constituted a \xe2\x80\x9ccontinuing violation\xe2\x80\x9d of Title VII. App. to Pet. for Cert. 45a. The City stipulated that the 89-point cutoff had a \xe2\x80\x9csevere disparate impact against African Americans,\xe2\x80\x9d Final Pretrial Order, Record, Doc. 223, Schedule A, p. 2, but argued that its cutoff score was justified by business necessity. After an 8-day bench trial, the District Court ruled for petitioners, rejecting the City\xe2\x80\x99s business-necessity defense. It ordered the City to hire 132 randomly selected members of the class (reflecting the number of African-Americans the Court found would have been hired but for the City\xe2\x80\x99s practices) and awarded backpay to be divided among the remaining class members.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Seventh Circuit reversed. 528 F.\xc2\xa03d 488 (2008). It held that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 suit was untimely because the earliest EEOC charge was filed more than 300 days after the only discriminatory act: sorting the scores into the \xe2\x80\x9cwell-qualified,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cqualified,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cnot-qualified\xe2\x80\x9d categories. The hiring decisions down the line were immaterial, it reasoned, because \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he hiring only of applicants classified \xe2\x80\x98well qualified\xe2\x80\x99 was the automatic consequence of the test scores rather than the product of a fresh act of discrimination.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 491. We granted certiorari. 557 U. S. __ (2009).

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Before beginning a Title VII suit, a plaintiff must first file a timely EEOC charge. In this case, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 charges were due within 300 days \xe2\x80\x9cafter the alleged unlawful employment practice occurred.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x935(e)(1).[Footnote 3] Determining whether a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s charge is timely thus requires \xe2\x80\x9cidentify[ing] precisely the \xe2\x80\x98unlawful employment practice\xe2\x80\x99 of which he complains.\xe2\x80\x9d Delaware State College v. Ricks, 449 U. S. 250, 257 (1980). Petitioners here challenge the City\xe2\x80\x99s practice of picking only those who had scored 89 or above on the 1995 examination when it later chose applicants to advance. Setting aside the first round of selection in May 1996, which all agree is beyond the cut-off, no one disputes that the conduct petitioners challenge occurred within the charging period.[Footnote 4] The real question, then, is not whether a claim predicated on that conduct is timely, but whether the practice thus defined can be the basis for a disparate-impact claim at all.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We conclude that it can. As originally enacted, Title VII did not expressly prohibit employment practices that cause a disparate impact. That enactment made it an \xe2\x80\x9cunlawful employment practice\xe2\x80\x9d for an employer \xe2\x80\x9cto fail or refuse to hire or to discharge any individual, or otherwise to discriminate against any individual with respect to his compensation, terms, conditions, or privileges of employment, because of such individual\xe2\x80\x99s race, color, religion, sex, or national origin,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x932(a)(1), or \xe2\x80\x9cto limit, segregate, or classify his employees or applicants for employment in any way which would deprive or tend to deprive any individual of employment opportunities or otherwise adversely affect his status as an employee, because of\xe2\x80\x9d any of the same reasons, \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x932(a)(2). In Griggs v. Duke Power Co., 401 U. S. 424, 431 (1971), we interpreted the latter provision to \xe2\x80\x9cproscrib[e] not only overt discrimination but also practices that are fair in form, but discriminatory in operation.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Two decades later, Congress codified the requirements of the \xe2\x80\x9cdisparate impact\xe2\x80\x9d claims Griggs had recognized. Pub. L. 102\xe2\x80\x93166, \xc2\xa7105, 105 Stat. 1074, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x932(k). That provision states:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(1)(A) An unlawful employment practice based on disparate impact is established under this subchapter only if\xe2\x80\x94

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9c(i) a complaining party demonstrates that a respondent uses a particular employment practice that causes a disparate impact on the basis of race, color, religion, sex, or national origin and the respondent fails to demonstrate that the challenged practice is job related for the position in question and consistent with business necessity\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\nThus, a plaintiff establishes a prima facie disparate-impact claim by showing that the employer \xe2\x80\x9cuses a particular employment practice that causes a disparate impact\xe2\x80\x9d on one of the prohibited bases. Ibid. (emphasis added). See Ricci v. DeStefano, 557 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (slip op., at 18).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claim satisfies that requirement. Title VII does not define \xe2\x80\x9cemployment practice,\xe2\x80\x9d but we think it clear that the term encompasses the conduct of which petitioners complain: the exclusion of passing applicants who scored below 89 (until the supply of scores 89 or above was exhausted) when selecting those who would advance. The City \xe2\x80\x9cuse[d]\xe2\x80\x9d that practice in each round of selection. Although the City had adopted the eligibility list (embodying the score cutoffs) earlier and announced its intention to draw from that list, it made use of the practice of excluding those who scored 88 or below each time it filled a new class of firefighters. Petitioners alleged that this exclusion caused a disparate impact. Whether they adequately proved that is not before us. What matters is that their allegations, based on the City\xe2\x80\x99s actual implementation of its policy, stated a cognizable claim.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The City argues that subsection (k) is inapposite because it does not address \xe2\x80\x9caccrual\xe2\x80\x9d of disparate-impact claims. Section 2000e\xe2\x80\x935(e)(1), it says, specifies when the time to file a charge starts running. That is true but irrelevant. Aside from the first round of selection in May 1996 (which all agree is beyond the 300-day charging period), the acts petitioners challenge\xe2\x80\x94the City\xe2\x80\x99s use of its cutoff score in selecting candidates\xe2\x80\x94occurred within the charging period. Accordingly, no one disputes that if petitioners could bring new claims based on those acts, their claims were timely. The issue, in other words, is not when petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims accrued, but whether they could accrue at all.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The City responds that subsection (k) does not answer that question either; that it speaks, as its title indicates, only to the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9c[b]urden of proof in disparate impact cases,\xe2\x80\x9d not to the elements of disparate-impact claims, which the City says are be found in \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x932(a)(2). That is incorrect. Subsection (k) does indeed address the burden of proof\xe2\x80\x94not just who bears it, however, but also what it consists of. It does set forth the essential ingredients of a disparate-impact claim: It says that a claim \xe2\x80\x9cis established\xe2\x80\x9d if an employer \xe2\x80\x9cuses\xe2\x80\x9d an \xe2\x80\x9cemployment practice\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9ccauses a disparate impact\xe2\x80\x9d on one of the enumerated bases. \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x932(k)(1)(A)(i). That it also sets forth a business-necessity defense employers may raise, \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x932(k)(1)(A)(i), and explains how plaintiffs may prevail despite that defense, \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x932(k)(1)(A)(ii), is irrelevant. Unless and until the defendant pleads and proves a business-necessity defense, the plaintiff wins simply by showing the stated elements.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Notwithstanding the text of \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x932(k)(1)(A)(i) and petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 description of the practice they claim was unlawful, the City argues that the unlawful employment practice here was something else entirely. The only actionable discrimination, it argues, occurred in 1996 when it \xe2\x80\x9cused the examination results to create the hiring eligibility list, limited hiring to the \xe2\x80\x98well qualified\xe2\x80\x99 classification, and notified petitioners.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 23. That initial decision, it concedes, was unlawful. But because no timely charge challenged the decision, that cannot now be the basis for liability. And because, the City claims, the exclusion of petitioners when selecting classes of firefighters followed inevitably from the earlier decision to adopt the cutoff score, no new violations could have occurred. The Seventh Circuit adopted the same analysis. See 528 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 490\xe2\x80\x93491.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The City\xe2\x80\x99s premise is sound, but its conclusion does not follow. It may be true that the City\xe2\x80\x99s January 1996 decision to adopt the cutoff score (and to create a list of the applicants above it) gave rise to a freestanding disparate-impact claim. Cf. Connecticut v. Teal, 457 U. S. 440, 445\xe2\x80\x93451 (1982). If that is so, the City is correct that since no timely charge was filed attacking it, the City is now \xe2\x80\x9centitled to treat that past act as lawful.\xe2\x80\x9d United Air Lines, Inc. v. Evans, 431 U. S. 553, 558 (1977). But it does not follow that no new violation occurred\xe2\x80\x94and no new claims could arise\xe2\x80\x94when the City implemented that decision down the road. If petitioners could prove that the City \xe2\x80\x9cuse[d]\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9cpractice\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9ccauses a disparate impact,\xe2\x80\x9d they could prevail.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The City, like the Seventh Circuit, see 528 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 490\xe2\x80\x93491, insists that Evans and a line of cases following it require a different result. See also Ledbetter v. Goodyear Tire & Rubber Co., 550 U. S. 618 (2007); Lorance v. AT&T Technologies, Inc., 490 U. S. 900 (1989); Ricks, 449 U. S. 250. Those cases, we are told, stand for the proposition that present effects of prior actions cannot lead to Title VII liability.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We disagree. As relevant here, those cases establish only that a Title VII plaintiff must show a \xe2\x80\x9cpresent violation\xe2\x80\x9d within the limitations period. Evans, supra, at 558 (emphasis deleted). What that requires depends on the claim asserted. For disparate-treatment claims\xe2\x80\x94and others for which discriminatory intent is required\xe2\x80\x94that means the plaintiff must demonstrate deliberate discrimination within the limitations period. See Ledbetter, supra, at 624\xe2\x80\x93629; Lorance, supra, at 904\xe2\x80\x93905; Ricks, supra, at 256\xe2\x80\x93258; Evans, supra, at 557\xe2\x80\x93560; see also Chardon v. Fernandez, 454 U. S. 6, 8 (1981) (per curiam). But for claims that do not require discriminatory intent, no such demonstration is needed. Cf. Ledbetter, supra, at 640; Lorance, supra, at 904, 908\xe2\x80\x93909. Our opinions, it is true, described the harms of which the unsuccessful plaintiffs in those cases complained as \xe2\x80\x9cpresent effect[s]\xe2\x80\x9d of past discrimination. Ledbetter, supra, at 628; see also Lorance, supra, at 907; Chardon, supra, at 8; Ricks, supra, at 258; Evans, supra, at 558. But the reason they could not be the present effects of present discrimination was that the charged discrimination required proof of discriminatory intent, which had not even been alleged. That reasoning has no application when, as here, the charge is disparate impact, which does not require discriminatory intent.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Seventh Circuit resisted this conclusion, reasoning that the difference between disparate-treatment and disparate-impact claims is only superficial. Both take aim at the same evil\xe2\x80\x94discrimination on a prohibited basis\xe2\x80\x94but simply seek to establish it by different means. 528 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 491\xe2\x80\x93492. Disparate-impact liability, the Court of Appeals explained, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98is primarily intended to lighten the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s heavy burden of proving intentional discrimination after employers learned to cover their tracks.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 492 (quoting Finnegan v. Trans World Airlines, Inc., 967 F.\xc2\xa02d 1161, 1164 (CA7 1992)). But even if the two theories were directed at the same evil, it would not follow that their reach is therefore coextensive. If the effect of applying Title VII\xe2\x80\x99s text is that some claims that would be doomed under one theory will survive under the other, that is the product of the law Congress has written. It is not for us to rewrite the statute so that it covers only what we think is necessary to achieve what we think Congress really intended. See Oncale v. Sundowner Offshore Services, Inc., 523 U. S. 75, 79\xe2\x80\x9380 (1998).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The City also argues that, even if petitioners could have proved a present disparate-impact violation, they never did so under the proper test. The parties litigated the merits\xe2\x80\x94and the City stipulated that the cutoff score caused disparate impact\xe2\x80\x94after the District Court adopted petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9ccontinuing violation\xe2\x80\x9d theory. App. to Pet. for Cert. 45a. That theory, which petitioners have since abandoned, treated the adoption and application of the cutoff score as a single, ongoing wrong. As a result, the City says, \xe2\x80\x9cpetitioners never proved, or even attempted to prove, that use of the [eligibility] list had disparate impact,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 32 (emphasis added), since the theory they advanced did not require them to do so. If the Court of Appeals determines that the argument has been preserved it may be available on remand. But it has no bearing here. The only question presented to us is whether the claim petitioners brought is cognizable. Because we conclude that it is, our inquiry is at an end.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The City and its amici warn that our reading will result in a host of practical problems for employers and employees alike. Employers may face new disparate-impact suits for practices they have used regularly for years. Evidence essential to their business-necessity defenses might be unavailable (or in the case of witnesses\xe2\x80\x99 memories, unreliable) by the time the later suits are brought. And affected employees and prospective employees may not even know they have claims if they are unaware the employer is still applying the disputed practice.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Truth to tell, however, both readings of the statute produce puzzling results. Under the City\xe2\x80\x99s reading, if an employer adopts an unlawful practice and no timely charge is brought, it can continue using the practice indefinitely, with impunity, despite ongoing disparate impact. Equitable tolling or estoppel may allow some affected employees or applicants to sue, but many others will be left out in the cold. Moreover, the City\xe2\x80\x99s reading may induce plaintiffs aware of the danger of delay to file charges upon the announcement of a hiring practice, before they have any basis for believing it will produce a disparate impact.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In all events, it is not our task to assess the consequences of each approach and adopt the one that produces the least mischief. Our charge is to give effect to the law Congress enacted. By enacting \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x932(k)(1)(A)(i), Congress allowed claims to be brought against an employer who uses a practice that causes disparate impact, whatever the employer\xe2\x80\x99s motives and whether or not he has employed the same practice in the past. If that effect was unintended, it is a problem for Congress, not one that federal courts can fix.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The City asserts that one aspect of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment still must be changed. The first round of hiring firefighters occurred outside the charging period even for the earliest EEOC charge. Yet the District Court, applying the continuing-violation theory, awarded relief based on those acts. Petitioners do not disagree, and they do not oppose the City\xe2\x80\x99s request for a remand to resolve this issue. We therefore leave it to the Seventh Circuit to determine, to the extent that point was properly preserved, whether the judgment must be modified in light of our decision.

\n

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment of the Court of Appeals is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.
