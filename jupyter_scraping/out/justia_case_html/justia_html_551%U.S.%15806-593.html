\nLONG ISLAND CARE AT HOME, LTD. V. COKE

\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

LONG ISLAND CARE AT HOME, LTD., et\xc2\xa0al. v. COKE

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the second circuit

\n

No. 06\xe2\x80\x93593.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 16, 2007\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 11, 2007

\n

The Fair Labor Standards Amendments of 1974 exempted from the minimum wage and maximum hours rules of the Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938 (FLSA) persons \xe2\x80\x9cemployed in domestic service employment to provide companionship services for individuals \xe2\x80\xa6 unable to care for themselves.\xe2\x80\x9d 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7213(a)(15). Under a Labor Department (DOL) regulation labeled an \xe2\x80\x9cInterpretatio[n]\xe2\x80\x9d (hereinafter third-party regulation), the exemption includes those \xe2\x80\x9ccompanionship\xe2\x80\x9d workers \xe2\x80\x9cemployed by an \xe2\x80\xa6 agency other than the family or household using their services.\xe2\x80\x9d 29 CFR \xc2\xa7552.109(a). However, DOL\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cGeneral Regulations\xe2\x80\x9d also define the statutory term \xe2\x80\x9cdomestic service employment\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cservices of a household nature performed by an employee in or about a private home \xe2\x80\xa6 of the person by whom he or she is employed.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7552.3 (emphasis added). Respondent, a \xe2\x80\x9ccompanionship services\xe2\x80\x9d provider to the elderly and infirm, sued petitioners, her former employer Long Island Care and its owner, seeking minimum and overtime wages they allegedly owed her. The parties assume the FLSA requires the payments only if its \xe2\x80\x9ccompanionship services\xe2\x80\x9d exemption does not apply to workers paid by third-party agencies such as Long Island Care. The District Court dismissed the suit, finding the third-party regulation valid and controlling. The Second Circuit found the regulation unenforceable and set the judgment aside.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The third-party regulation is valid and binding. Pp. 4\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0An agency\xe2\x80\x99s power to administer a congressionally created program necessarily requires the making of rules to fill any \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98gap\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d left, implicitly or explicitly, by Congress. Chevron U. S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., 467 U. S. 837, 843. When an agency fills such a gap reasonably, and in accordance with other applicable (e.g., procedural) requirements, that result is legally binding. Id., at 843\xe2\x80\x93844. On its face, the third-party regulation seems to fill a statutory gap. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x935. \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The regulation does not exceed DOL\xe2\x80\x99s delegated rulemaking authority. The FLSA explicitly leaves gaps as to the scope and definition of its \xe2\x80\x9cdomestic service employment\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ccompanionship services\xe2\x80\x9d terms, 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7213(a)(15), and empowers the DOL to fill these gaps through regulations, 1974 Amendments, \xc2\xa729(b). Whether to include workers paid by third parties is one of the details left to the DOL to work out. Although the pre-1974 FLSA already covered some third-party-paid companionship workers, e.g., those employed by large private enterprises, it did not then cover others, e.g., those employed directly by the aged person\xe2\x80\x99s family or by many smaller private agencies. Thus, whether, or how, the statutory definition should apply to such workers raises a set of complex questions, e.g., should the FLSA cover all of them, some of them, or none of them? How should the need for a simple, uniform application of the exemption be weighed against the fact that some (but not all) of the workers were previously covered? Given the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s expertise, satisfactory answers to the foregoing questions may well turn upon its thorough knowledge of the area and ability to consult at length with affected parties. It is therefore reasonable to infer that Congress intended its broad grant of definitional authority to the DOL to include the authority to answer such questions. Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on the Social Security statute, whose text expressly answers a \xe2\x80\x9cthird party\xe2\x80\x9d coverage question, and on conflicting statements in the 1974 Amendments\xe2\x80\x99 legislative history, is unavailing. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Although the literal language of the third-party regulation and the \xe2\x80\x9cGeneral Regulation,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7552.3, conflicts as to whether third-party-paid workers are included within the statutory exemption, several reasons compel the Court to agree with the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s position, set forth in an \xe2\x80\x9cAdvisory Memorandum\xe2\x80\x9d explaining (and defending) the third-party regulation, that that regulation governs here. First, a decision that \xc2\xa7552.3 controls would create serious problems as to the coverage of particular domestic service employees by the statutory exemption or by the FLSA as a whole. Second, given that the third-party regulation\xe2\x80\x99s sole purpose is to explain how the companionship services exemption applies to persons employed by third-party entities, whereas \xc2\xa7552.3\xe2\x80\x99s primary purpose is to describe the kind of work that must be performed to qualify someone as a \xe2\x80\x9cdomestic service\xe2\x80\x9d employee, the third-party regulation is the more specific with respect to the question at issue and therefore governs, see, e.g., Morales v. Trans World Airlines, Inc., 504 U. S. 374, 384\xe2\x80\x93385. Third, that the DOL may have interpreted the two regulations differently at different times in their history is not a ground for disregarding the present interpretation, which the DOL reached after proposing a different interpretation through notice-and-comment rulemaking, making any unfair surprise unlikely, cf. Bowen v. Georgetown Univ. Hospital, 488 U. S. 204, 212. Fourth, while the Advisory Memorandum was issued only to DOL personnel and written in response to this litigation, this Court has accepted such an interpretation where, as here, an agency\xe2\x80\x99s course of action indicates that its interpretation of its own regulation reflects its considered views on the matter in question and there is no reason to suspect that its interpretation is merely a post hoc rationalization. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(d)\xc2\xa0Several factors compel the Court to reject respondent\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the third-party regulation is an \xe2\x80\x9cinterpretation\xe2\x80\x9d not meant to fill a statutory \xe2\x80\x9cgap,\xe2\x80\x9d but simply to describe the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s view of what the FLSA means, and thus is not entitled to Chevron deference, cf. United States v. Mead Corp., 533 U. S. 218, 232. For one thing, the regulation directly governs the conduct of members of the public, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98affecting individual rights and obligations.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Chrysler Corp. v. Brown, 441 U. S. 281, 302. When promulgating the regulation and when considering amending it, the DOL has always employed full public notice-and-comment procedures, which under the Administrative Procedure Act (APA) need not be used when producing an \xe2\x80\x9cinterpretive\xe2\x80\x9d rule, 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7553(b)(A). And for the past 30 years, according to the Advisory Memorandum (and not disputed by respondent), the DOL has treated the regulation as a legally binding exercise of its rulemaking authority. For another thing, the DOL may have placed the third-party regulation in Subpart B of Part 552, entitled \xe2\x80\x9cInterpretations,\xe2\x80\x9d rather than in Subpart A, \xe2\x80\x9cGeneral Regulations,\xe2\x80\x9d because Subpart B contains matters of detail, interpreting and applying Subpart A\xe2\x80\x99s more general definitions. Indeed, Subpart B\xe2\x80\x99s other regulations\xe2\x80\x94involving, e.g., employer \xe2\x80\x9ccredit[s]\xe2\x80\x9d against minimum wages for provision of \xe2\x80\x9cfood,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9clodging,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cdrycleaning\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94strongly indicate that such details, not a direct interpretation of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s language, are at issue. Finally, the Court assumes Congress meant and expected courts to treat a regulation as within a delegation of \xe2\x80\x9cgap-filling\xe2\x80\x9d authority where, as here, the rule sets forth important individual rights and duties, the agency focuses fully and directly upon the issue and uses full notice-and-comment procedures, and the resulting rule falls within the statutory grant of authority and is reasonable. Mead, supra, at 229\xe2\x80\x93233. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(e)\xc2\xa0The Court disagrees with respondent\xe2\x80\x99s claim that the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s 1974 notice-and-comment proceedings were legally \xe2\x80\x9cdefective\xe2\x80\x9d because the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s notice and explanation were inadequate. Fair notice is the object of the APA requirement that a notice of proposed rulemaking contain \xe2\x80\x9ceither the terms or substance of the proposed rule or a description of the subjects and issues involved,\xe2\x80\x9d 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7553(b)(3). The Circuits have generally interpreted this to mean that the final rule must be a logical outgrowth of the rule proposed. Initially, the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s proposed regulation would have placed outside the \xc2\xa7213(a)(15) exemption (and hence left subject to FLSA wage and hour rules) individuals employed by the large enterprise third-party employers covered before 1974. Since that was simply a proposal, however, its presence meant that the DOL was considering the matter and might later choose to keep the proposal or to withdraw it. The DOL finally withdrew it, resulting in a determination exempting all third-party-employed companionship workers from the FLSA, and that possibility was reasonably foreseeable. There is also no significant legal problem with the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s explanation that its final interpretation is more consistent with FLSA language. No one seems to have objected to this explanation at the time, and it still remains a reasonable, albeit brief, explanation. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

462 F.\xc2\xa03d 48, reversed and remanded.

LONG ISLAND CARE AT HOME, LTD., et\xc2\xa0al.,\nPETITIONERS v. EVELYN COKE

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the second circuit

\n

[June 11, 2007]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Breyer delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A provision of the Fair Labor Standards Act exempts from the statute\xe2\x80\x99s minimum wage and maximum hours rules

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cany employee employed in domestic service employment to provide companionship services for individuals who (because of age or infirmity) are unable to care for themselves (as such terms are defined and delimited by regulations of the Secretary [of Labor]).\xe2\x80\x9d 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7213(a)(15).

\n

\nA Department of Labor regulation (labeled an \xe2\x80\x9cinterpretation\xe2\x80\x9d) says that this statutory exemption includes those \xe2\x80\x9ccompanionship\xe2\x80\x9d workers who \xe2\x80\x9care employed by an employer or agency other than the family or household using their services.\xe2\x80\x9d 29 CFR \xc2\xa7552.109(a) (2006). The question before us is whether, in light of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text and history, and a different (apparently conflicting) regulation, the Department\xe2\x80\x99s regulation is valid and binding. See Chevron U. S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., 467 U. S. 837, 843\xe2\x80\x93844 (1984). We conclude that it is.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1974, Congress amended the Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938 (FLSA or Act), 52 Stat. 1060, to include many \xe2\x80\x9cdomestic service\xe2\x80\x9d employees not previously subject to its minimum wage and maximum hour requirements. See Fair Labor Standards Amendments of 1974 (1974 Amendments), \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77(b)(1), (2), 88 Stat. 62 (adding 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7206(f), which provides for a minimum wage for domestic service employees, and \xc2\xa7207(l), which extends overtime restrictions to domestic service employees). When doing so, Congress simultaneously created an exemption that excluded from FLSA coverage certain subsets of employees \xe2\x80\x9cemployed in domestic service employment,\xe2\x80\x9d including babysitters \xe2\x80\x9cemployed on a casual basis\xe2\x80\x9d and the companionship workers described above. \xc2\xa77(b)(3), 88 Stat. 62, (codified at 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7213(a)(15)).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Department of Labor (Department or DOL) then promulgated a set of regulations that included two regulations at issue here. The first, set forth in a subpart of the proposed regulations entitled \xe2\x80\x9cGeneral Regulations,\xe2\x80\x9d defines the statutory term \xe2\x80\x9cdomestic service employment\xe2\x80\x9d as

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cservices of a household nature performed by an employee in or about a private home \xe2\x80\xa6 of the person by whom he or she is employed \xe2\x80\xa6 such as cooks, waiters, butlers, valets, maids, housekeepers, governesses, nurses, janitors, laundresses, caretakers, handymen, gardeners, footmen, grooms, and chauffeurs of automobiles for family use [as well as] babysitters employed on other than a casual basis.\xe2\x80\x9d 40 Fed. Reg. 7405 (1975) (emphasis added) (codified at 29 CFR \xc2\xa7552.3).

\n

\nThe second, set forth in a later subsection entitled \xe2\x80\x9cInterpretations,\xe2\x80\x9d says that exempt companionship workers include those

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cwho are employed by an employer or agency other than the family or household using their services \xe2\x80\xa6 [whether or not] such an employee [is assigned] to more than one household or family in the same workweek\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d 40 Fed. Reg. 7407 (codified at 29 CFR \xc2\xa7552.109(a)).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This latter regulation (which we shall call the \xe2\x80\x9cthird-party regulation\xe2\x80\x9d) has proved controversial in recent years. On at least three separate occasions during the past 15 years, the Department considered changing the regulation and narrowing the exemption in order to bring within the scope of the FLSA\xe2\x80\x99s wage and hour coverage companionship workers paid by third parties (other than family members of persons receiving the services, who under the proposals were to remain exempt). 58 Fed. Reg. 69310\xe2\x80\x9369312 (1993); 60 Fed. Reg. 46798 (1995); 66 Fed. Reg. 5481, 5485 (2001). But the Department ultimately decided not to make any change. 67 Fed. Reg. 16668 (2002).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In April 2002, Evelyn Coke (respondent), a domestic worker who provides \xe2\x80\x9ccompanionship services\xe2\x80\x9d to elderly and infirm men and women, brought this lawsuit against her former employer, Long Island Care at Home, Ltd., and its owner, Maryann Osborne (petitioners). App. 1, 19; 267 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 332, 333\xe2\x80\x93334 (EDNY 2003). She alleged that the petitioners failed to pay her the minimum wages and overtime wages to which she was entitled under the FLSA and a New York statute, and she sought a judgment for those unpaid wages. App. 21\xe2\x80\x9322. All parties assume for present purposes that the FLSA entitles Coke to the payments if, but only if, the statutory exemption for \xe2\x80\x9ccompanionship services\xe2\x80\x9d does not apply to companionship workers paid by third-party agencies such as Long Island Care. The District Court found the Department\xe2\x80\x99s third-party regulation valid and controlling, and it consequently dismissed Coke\xe2\x80\x99s lawsuit. 267 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 341.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On appeal, the Second Circuit found the Department\xe2\x80\x99s third-party regulation \xe2\x80\x9cunenforceable\xe2\x80\x9d and set aside the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment. 376 F.\xc2\xa03d 118, 133, 135 (2004). Long Island Care and Osborne sought certiorari. At the Solicitor General\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion, we vacated the Second Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision and remanded the case so that the Circuit could consider a recent DOL \xe2\x80\x9cAdvisory Memorandum\xe2\x80\x9d explaining (and defending) the regulation. 546 U. S. 1147 (2006); App. E to Pet. for Cert. 50a (Wage and Hour Advisory Memorandum No. 2005\xe2\x80\x931 (Dec. 1, 2005) (hereinafter Advisory Memorandum)). The memorandum failed to convince the Second Circuit, which again held the regulation unenforceable. 462 F.\xc2\xa03d 48, 50\xe2\x80\x9352 (2006) (per curiam). Long Island Care and Osborne again sought certiorari. And this time, we granted their petition and set the case for argument.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have previously pointed out that the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98power of an administrative agency to administer a congressionally created \xe2\x80\xa6 program necessarily requires the formulation of policy and the making of rules to fill any gap left, implicitly or explicitly, by Congress.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Chevron, 467 U. S., at 843 (quoting Morton v. Ruiz, 415 U. S. 199, 231 (1974); omission in original). When an agency fills such a \xe2\x80\x9cgap\xe2\x80\x9d reasonably, and in accordance with other applicable (e.g., procedural) requirements, the courts accept the result as legally binding. 467 U. S., at 843\xe2\x80\x93844; United States v. Mead Corp., 533 U. S. 218, 227 (2001).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this case, the FLSA explicitly leaves gaps, for example as to the scope and definition of statutory terms such as \xe2\x80\x9cdomestic service employment\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ccompanionship services.\xe2\x80\x9d 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7213(a)(15). It provides the Department of Labor with the power to fill these gaps through rules and regulations. Ibid.; 1974 Amendments, \xc2\xa729(b), 88 Stat. 76 (authorizing the Secretary of Labor \xe2\x80\x9cto prescribe necessary rules, regulations, and orders with regard to the amendments made by this Act\xe2\x80\x9d). The subject matter of the regulation in question concerns a matter in respect to which the agency is expert, and it concerns an interstitial matter, i.e., a portion of a broader definition, the details of which, as we said, Congress entrusted the agency to work out.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Department focused fully upon the matter in question. It gave notice, it proposed regulations, it received public comment, and it issued final regulations in light of that comment. 39 Fed. Reg. 35383 (1974); 40 Fed. Reg. 7404. See Mead, supra, at 230. The resulting regulation says that employees who provide \xe2\x80\x9ccompanionship services\xe2\x80\x9d fall within the terms of the statutory exemption irrespective of who pays them. Since on its face the regulation seems to fill a statutory gap, one might ask what precisely is it about the regulation that might make it unreasonable or otherwise unlawful?

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent argues, and the Second Circuit concluded, that a thorough examination of the regulation\xe2\x80\x99s content, its method of promulgation, and its context reveals serious legal problems\xe2\x80\x94problems that led the Second Circuit to conclude that the regulation was unenforceable. In particular, respondent claims that the regulation falls outside the scope of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 delegation; that it is inconsistent with another, legally governing regulation; that it is an \xe2\x80\x9cinterpretive\xe2\x80\x9d regulation not warranting judicial deference; and that it was improperly promulgated. We shall examine each of these claims in turn.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent refers to the statute\xe2\x80\x99s language exempting from FLSA coverage those \xe2\x80\x9cemployed in domestic service employment to provide companionship services for individuals who (because of age or infirmity) are unable to care for themselves.\xe2\x80\x9d 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7213(a)(15). She claims that the words \xe2\x80\x9cdomestic service employment\xe2\x80\x9d limit the provision\xe2\x80\x99s scope to those workers employed by persons who themselves receive the services (or are part of that person\xe2\x80\x99s household) and exclude those who are employed by \xe2\x80\x9cthird parties.\xe2\x80\x9d And she advances several arguments in favor of this position.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent points to the overall purpose of the 1974 Amendments, namely to extend FLSA coverage, see, e.g., H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 93\xe2\x80\x93232, pp. 2, 8 (1973); she notes that prior to the amendments the FLSA already covered companionship workers employed by certain third parties (e.g., private agencies that were large enough, in terms of annual sales, to qualify for the FLSA\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9centerprise coverage\xe2\x80\x9d provisions, 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7206(a), 207(a)(1) (1970 ed.), see \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7203(r), (s)(1) (defining \xe2\x80\x9centerprise\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9centerprise engaged in commerce or the production of goods for commerce\xe2\x80\x9d)); and she concludes that Congress must therefore have meant its \xe2\x80\x9cdomestic service employment\xe2\x80\x9d language in the exemption to apply only to persons not employed by third parties such as Long Island Care. Respondent tries to bolster this argument by pointing to statements made by some Members of Congress during floor debates over the 1974 Amendments. See, e.g., 119 Cong. Rec. 24801 (1973) (statement of Sen. Burdick) (\xe2\x80\x9cI am not concerned about the professional domestic who does this as a daily living,\xe2\x80\x9d but rather about \xe2\x80\x9cpeople who might have an aged father, an aged mother, an infirm father, an infirm mother, and a neighbor comes in and sits with them\xe2\x80\x9d). And she also points to a different statute, the Social Security statute, which defines \xe2\x80\x9cdomestic service employment\xe2\x80\x9d as domestic work performed in \xe2\x80\x9ca private home of the employer.\xe2\x80\x9d 26 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73510(c)(1) (2000 ed.) (emphasis added; internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We do not find these arguments convincing. The statutory language refers broadly to \xe2\x80\x9cdomestic service employment\xe2\x80\x9d and to \xe2\x80\x9ccompanionship services.\xe2\x80\x9d It expressly instructs the agency to work out the details of those broad definitions. And whether to include workers paid by third parties within the scope of the definitions is one of those details.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although the FLSA in 1974 already covered some of the third-party-paid workers, it did not at that point cover others. It did not cover, for example, companionship workers employed directly by the aged person\xe2\x80\x99s family; nor did it cover workers employed by many smaller private agencies. The result is that whether, or how, the definition should apply to workers paid by third parties raises a set of complex questions. Should the FLSA cover all companionship workers paid by third parties? Or should the FLSA cover some such companionship workers, perhaps those working for some (say, large but not small) private agencies, or those hired by a son or daughter to help an aged or infirm mother living in a distant city? Should it cover none? How should one weigh the need for a simple, uniform application of the exemption against the fact that some (but not all) third-party employees were previously covered? Satisfactory answers to such questions may well turn upon the kind of thorough knowledge of the subject matter and ability to consult at length with affected parties that an agency, such as the Department of Labor, possesses. And it is consequently reasonable to infer (and we do infer) that Congress intended its broad grant of definitional authority to the Department to include the authority to answer these kinds of questions.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because respondent refers to the Social Security statute and the legislative history, we add that unlike the text of the Social Security statute, the text of the FLSA does not expressly answer the third-party-employment question. Compare 26 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73510(c)(1) with 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7213(a)(15). Nor can one find any clear answer in the statute\xe2\x80\x99s legislative history. Compare 119 Cong. Rec. 24801 (statement of Sen. Burdick, quoted above), with, e.g., id., at 24798 (statement of Sen. Johnston) (expressing concern that requiring payment of minimum wage to companionship workers might make such services so expensive that some people would be forced to leave the work force in order to take care of aged or infirm parents).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent says that the third-party regulation conflicts with the Department\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cGeneral Regulation\xe2\x80\x9d that defines the statutory term \xe2\x80\x9cdomestic service employment.\xe2\x80\x9d Title 29 CFR \xc2\xa7552.3 says that the term covers services \xe2\x80\x9cof a household nature performed by \xe2\x80\xa6 employee[s]\xe2\x80\x9d ranging from \xe2\x80\x9cmaids\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9ccooks\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9chousekeepers\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9ccaretakers\xe2\x80\x9d and others, \xe2\x80\x9cin or about a private home \xe2\x80\xa6 of the person by whom he or she is employed\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added). See also \xc2\xa7552.101(a). A companionship worker employed by a third party to work at the home of an aged or infirm man or woman is not working at the \xe2\x80\x9chome \xe2\x80\xa6 of the person by whom he or she is employed\xe2\x80\x9d (i.e., she is not working at the home of the third-party employer). Hence, the two regulations are inconsistent, for the one limits the definition of \xe2\x80\x9cdomestic service employee\xe2\x80\x9d for purposes of the 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7213(a)(15) exemption to workers employed by the household, but the other includes in the subclass of exempt companionship workers persons who are not employed by the household. Respondent adds that, given the conflict, the former \xe2\x80\x9cGeneral Regulation\xe2\x80\x9d must govern (primarily because, in her view, only the former regulation is entitled to Chevron deference, an issue we address in Part II\xe2\x80\x93C, infra).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent is correct when she says that the literal language of the two regulations conflicts as to whether workers paid by third parties are included within the statutory exemption. The question remains, however, which regulation governs in light of this conflict. The Department, in its Advisory Memorandum, suggests that the third-party regulation governs, and we agree, for several reasons.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, if we were to decide the contrary, i.e., that the text of the General Regulation, 29 CFR \xc2\xa7552.3, controls on the issue of third-party employment, our interpretation would create serious problems. Although \xc2\xa7552.3 states that it is supplying a definition of \xe2\x80\x9cdomestic service employment\xe2\x80\x9d only \xe2\x80\x9c[a]s [that term is] used\xe2\x80\x9d in the statutory exemption, 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7213(a)(15), the rule appears in other ways to have been meant to supply a definition of \xe2\x80\x9cdomestic service employment\xe2\x80\x9d for the FLSA as a whole (a prospect the Department endorses in its Advisory Memorandum). Why else would the Department have included the extensive list of qualifying professions, virtually none of which have anything to do with the subjects of \xc2\xa7213(a)(15), babysitting and companionship services? But if we were to apply \xc2\xa7552.3\xe2\x80\x99s literal definition of \xe2\x80\x9cdomestic service employment\xe2\x80\x9d (including the \xe2\x80\x9chome \xe2\x80\xa6 of the [employer]\xe2\x80\x9d language) across the FLSA, that would place outside the scope of FLSA\xe2\x80\x99s wage and hour rules any butlers, chauffeurs, and so forth who are employed by any third party. That result seems clearly contrary to Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent in enacting the 1974 Amendments, particularly if it would withdraw from FLSA coverage all domestic service employees previously covered by the \xe2\x80\x9centerprise coverage\xe2\x80\x9d provisions of the Act.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If, on the other hand, \xc2\xa7552.3\xe2\x80\x99s definition of \xe2\x80\x9cdomestic service employment\xe2\x80\x9d were limited to the statute\xe2\x80\x99s exemption provision, applying this definition literally (by removing all third-party employees from the exemption) would extend the Act\xe2\x80\x99s coverage not simply to third-party-employed companionship workers paid by large institutions, but also to those paid directly by a family member of an elderly or infirm person receiving such services whenever the family member lived in a different household than the invalid. Nothing in the statute suggests that Congress intended to make the exemption contingent on whether a family member chose to reside in the same household as the invalid, and it is a result that respondent herself seems to wish to avoid. See Brief for Respondent 34, n. 31.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, normally the specific governs the general. E.g., Morales v. Trans World Airlines, Inc., 504 U. S. 374, 384\xe2\x80\x93385 (1992); Simpson v. United States, 435 U. S. 6, 15 (1978). The sole purpose of the third-party regulation, \xc2\xa7552.109(a), is to explain how the companionship services exemption applies to persons employed by third-party entities, whereas the primary (if not sole) purpose of the conflicting general definitional regulation, \xc2\xa7552.3, is to describe the kind of work that must be performed by someone to qualify as a \xe2\x80\x9cdomestic service\xe2\x80\x9d employee. Given that context, \xc2\xa7552.109(a) is the more specific regulation with respect to the third-party-employment question.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Third, we concede that the Department may have interpreted these regulations differently at different times in their history. See, e.g., 58 Fed. Reg. 69311 (employees of a third-party employer qualify for the exemption only if they are also jointly employed \xe2\x80\x9cby the family or household using their services\xe2\x80\x9d); D. Sweeney, DOL Opinion Letter, Home Health Aides/Companionship Exemption, 6A LRR, Wages and Hours Manual 99:8205 (Jan. 6, 1999) (similar). But as long as interpretive changes create no unfair surprise\xe2\x80\x94and the Department\xe2\x80\x99s recourse to notice-and-comment rulemaking in an attempt to codify its new interpretation, see 58 Fed. Reg. 69311, makes any such surprise unlikely here\xe2\x80\x94the change in interpretation alone presents no separate ground for disregarding the Department\xe2\x80\x99s present interpretation. Cf. Bowen v. Georgetown Univ. Hospital, 488 U. S. 204, 212 (1988).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Fourth, we must also concede, as respondent points out, that the Department set forth its most recent interpretation of these regulations in an \xe2\x80\x9cAdvisory Memorandum\xe2\x80\x9d issued only to internal Department personnel and which the Department appears to have written in response to this litigation. We have \xe2\x80\x9cno reason,\xe2\x80\x9d however, \xe2\x80\x9cto suspect that [this] interpretation\xe2\x80\x9d is merely a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98post hoc rationalizatio[n]\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of past agency action, or that it \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not reflect the agency\xe2\x80\x99s fair and considered judgment on the matter in question.\xe2\x80\x9d Auer v. Robbins, 519 U. S. 452, 462 (1997) (quoting Bowen, supra). Where, as here, an agency\xe2\x80\x99s course of action indicates that the interpretation of its own regulation reflects its considered views\xe2\x80\x94the Department has clearly struggled with the third-party-employment question since at least 1993\xe2\x80\x94we have accepted that interpretation as the agency\xe2\x80\x99s own, even if the agency set those views forth in a legal brief. See 519 U. S., at 462.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For all these reasons, we conclude that the Department\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the two regulations falls well within the principle that an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of its own regulations is \xe2\x80\x9ccontrolling\xe2\x80\x9d unless \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cplainly erroneous or inconsistent with\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the regulations being interpreted. Id., at 461 (quoting Robertson v. Methow Valley Citizens Council, 490 U. S. 332, 359 (1989), in turn quoting Bowles v. Seminole Rock & Sand Co., 325 U. S. 410, 414 (1945)). See also Udall v. Tallman, 380 U. S. 1, 16\xe2\x80\x9317 (1965).

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent also argues that, even if the third-party regulation is within the scope of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s delegation, is perfectly reasonable, and otherwise complies with the law, courts still should not treat the regulation as legally binding. Her reason is a special one. She says that the regulation is an \xe2\x80\x9cinterpretive\xe2\x80\x9d regulation, a kind of regulation that may be used, not to fill a statutory \xe2\x80\x9cgap,\xe2\x80\x9d but simply to describe an agency\xe2\x80\x99s view of what a statute means. That kind of regulation may \xe2\x80\x9cpersuade\xe2\x80\x9d a reviewing court, Skidmore v. Swift & Co., 323 U. S. 134, 140 (1944), but will not necessarily \xe2\x80\x9cbind\xe2\x80\x9d a reviewing court. Cf. Mead, 533 U. S., at 232 (\xe2\x80\x9cinterpretive rules \xe2\x80\xa6 enjoy no Chevron status as a class\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Like respondent, the Court of Appeals concluded that the third-party regulation did not fill a statutory gap and hence was not legally binding. 376 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 131\xe2\x80\x93133; 462 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 50\xe2\x80\x9351. It based its conclusion upon three considerations: First, when the Department promulgated a series of regulations to implement the \xc2\xa7213(a)(15) exemptions, 29 CFR pt. 552, it placed the third-party regulation in Subpart B, entitled \xe2\x80\x9cInterpretations,\xe2\x80\x9d not in Subpart A, entitled \xe2\x80\x9cGeneral Regulations.\xe2\x80\x9d Second, the Department said that regulations 552.3, .4, .5, and .6, all in Subpart A, contained the \xe2\x80\x9cdefinitions\xe2\x80\x9d that the statute \xe2\x80\x9crequire[s].\xe2\x80\x9d Third, the Department initially said in 1974 that Subpart A would \xe2\x80\x9cdefin[e] and delimi[t] \xe2\x80\xa6the ter[m] \xe2\x80\x98domestic service employee,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d while Subpart B would \xe2\x80\x9cse[t] forth \xe2\x80\xa6 a statement of general policy and interpretation concerning the application of the [FLSA] to domestic service employees.\xe2\x80\x9d 376 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 131\xe2\x80\x93132; 462 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 50\xe2\x80\x9351 (quoting 39 Fed. Reg. 35382).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These reasons do not convince us that the Department intended its third-party regulation to carry no special legal weight. For one thing, other considerations strongly suggest the contrary, namely that the Department intended the third-party regulation as a binding application of its rulemaking authority. The regulation directly governs the conduct of members of the public, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98affecting individual rights and obligations.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Chrysler Corp. v. Brown, 441 U. S. 281, 302 (1979) (quoting Morton, 415 U. S., at 232). When promulgating the rule, the agency used full public notice-and-comment procedures, which under the Administrative Procedure Act an agency need not use when producing an \xe2\x80\x9cinterpretive\xe2\x80\x9d rule. 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7553(b)(A) (exempting \xe2\x80\x9cinterpretative rules, general statements of policy, or rules of agency organization, procedure, or practice\xe2\x80\x9d from notice-and-comment procedures). Each time the Department has considered amending the rule, it has similarly used full notice-and-comment rulemaking procedures. 58 Fed. Reg. 69310 (1993); 60 Fed. Reg. 46797 (1995); 66 Fed. Reg. 5485 (2001). And for the past 30 years, according to the Department\xe2\x80\x99s Advisory Memorandum (and not disputed by respondent), the Department has treated the third-party regulation like the others, i.e., as a legally binding exercise of its rulemaking authority. App. E to Pet. for Cert. 63a\xe2\x80\x9364a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For another thing, the Subpart B heading \xe2\x80\x9cInterpretations\xe2\x80\x9d (and the other indicia upon which the Court of Appeals relied) could well refer to the fact that Subpart B contains matters of detail, interpreting and applying the more general definitions of Subpart A. Indeed, Subpart B\xe2\x80\x99s other regulations\xe2\x80\x94involving such matters as employer \xe2\x80\x9ccredit[s]\xe2\x80\x9d against minimum wage payments for provision of \xe2\x80\x9cfood,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9clodging,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cdrycleaning,\xe2\x80\x9d 29 CFR \xc2\xa7552.100(b), and so forth\xe2\x80\x94strongly indicate that such details, not a direct interpretation of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s language, are at issue.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, the ultimate question is whether Congress would have intended, and expected, courts to treat an agency\xe2\x80\x99s rule, regulation, application of a statute, or other agency action as within, or outside, its delegation to the agency of \xe2\x80\x9cgap-filling\xe2\x80\x9d authority. Where an agency rule sets forth important individual rights and duties, where the agency focuses fully and directly upon the issue, where the agency uses full notice-and-comment procedures to promulgate a rule, where the resulting rule falls within the statutory grant of authority, and where the rule itself is reasonable, then a court ordinarily assumes that Congress intended it to defer to the agency\xe2\x80\x99s determination. See Mead, supra, at 229\xe2\x80\x93233.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The three contrary considerations to which the Court of Appeals points are insufficient, in our view, to overcome the other factors we have mentioned, all of which suggest that courts should defer to the Department\xe2\x80\x99s rule. And that, in our view, is what the law requires.

\n

D

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s final claim is that the 1974 agency notice-and-comment procedure, leading to the promulgation of the third-party regulation, was legally \xe2\x80\x9cdefective\xe2\x80\x9d because notice was inadequate and the Department\xe2\x80\x99s explanation also inadequate. We do not agree.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Administrative Procedure Act requires an agency conducting notice-and-comment rulemaking to publish in its notice of proposed rulemaking \xe2\x80\x9ceither the terms or substance of the proposed rule or a description of the subjects and issues involved.\xe2\x80\x9d 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7553(b)(3). The Courts of Appeals have generally interpreted this to mean that the final rule the agency adopts must be \xe2\x80\x9ca \xe2\x80\x98logical outgrowth\xe2\x80\x99 of the rule proposed.\xe2\x80\x9d National Black Media Coalition v. FCC, 791 F.\xc2\xa02d 1016, 1022 (CA2 1986). See also, e.g., United Steelworkers of America, AFL\xe2\x80\x93CIO\xe2\x80\x93CLC v. Marshall, 647 F.\xc2\xa02d 1189, 1221 (CADC 1980), cert. denied sub nom. Lead Industries Assn., Inc. v. Donovan, 453 U. S. 913 (1981); South Terminal Corp. v. EPA, 504 F.\xc2\xa02d 646, 659 (CA1 1974). The object, in short, is one of fair notice.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Initially the Department proposed a rule of the kind that respondent seeks, namely a rule that would have placed outside the exemption (and hence left subject to FLSA wage and hour rules) individuals employed by third-party employers whom the Act had covered prior to 1974. 39 Fed. Reg. 35385 (companionship workers \xe2\x80\x9cnot exempt\xe2\x80\x9d if employed by a third party that already was a \xe2\x80\x9ccovered enterprise\xe2\x80\x9d under the FLSA). The clear implication of the proposed rule was that companionship workers employed by third-party enterprises that were not covered by the FLSA prior to the 1974 Amendments (e.g., most smaller private agencies) would be included within the \xc2\xa7213(a)(15) exemption.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Since the proposed rule was simply a proposal, its presence meant that the Department was considering the matter; after that consideration the Department might choose to adopt the proposal or to withdraw it. As it turned out, the Department did withdraw the proposal for special treatment of employees of \xe2\x80\x9ccovered enterprises.\xe2\x80\x9d The result was a determination that exempted all third-party-employed companionship workers from the Act. We do not understand why such a possibility was not reasonably foreseeable. See, e.g., Arizona Public Serv. Co. v. EPA, 211 F.\xc2\xa03d 1280, 1299\xe2\x80\x931300 (CADC 2000) (notice sufficient where agency first proposed that Indian tribes be required to meet the \xe2\x80\x9csame requirements\xe2\x80\x9d as States with respect to judicial review of Clean Air Act permitting actions, but then adopted a final rule that exempted tribes from certain, though not all, requirements), cert. denied sub nom. Michigan v. EPA, 532 U. S. 970 (2001).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Neither can we find any significant legal problem with the Department\xe2\x80\x99s explanation for the change. The agency said that it had \xe2\x80\x9cconcluded that these exemptions can be available to such third party employers\xe2\x80\x9d because that interpretation is \xe2\x80\x9cmore consistent\xe2\x80\x9d with statutory language that refers to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98any employee\xe2\x80\x99 engaged \xe2\x80\x98in\xe2\x80\x99 the enumerated services\xe2\x80\x9d and with \xe2\x80\x9cprior practices concerning other similarly worded exemptions.\xe2\x80\x9d 40 Fed. Reg. 7405. There is no indication that anyone objected to this explanation at the time. And more than 30 years later it remains a reasonable, albeit brief, explanation. See Global Crossing Telecommunications, Inc. v. Metrophones Telecommunications, Inc., 550 U. S. ___, ___ (2007) (slip op., at 18).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s only contrary argument apparently consists of her claim that the explanation does not take proper account of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s reference to \xe2\x80\x9cdomestic service employees,\xe2\x80\x9d which term (given the Social Security statute and legislative history) must refer only to those who are paid by the household for whom they provide services. If so, she simply repeats in different form arguments that we have already considered and rejected. See Part II\xe2\x80\x93A, supra.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For these reasons the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 judgment is reversed, and we remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.
