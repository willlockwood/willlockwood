\nSYLLABUS
\nOCTOBER TERM, 2009
\nUNITED STATES V. O\'BRIEN
\n
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\n
\n

UNITED STATES v. O\xe2\x80\x99BRIEN et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the first circuit

\n

No. 08\xe2\x80\x931569.\xe2\x80\x83Argued February 23, 2010\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 24, 2010

\n

Respondents O\xe2\x80\x99Brien and Burgess each carried a firearm during an attempted robbery. Count three of their indictment charged them with using a firearm in furtherance of a crime of violence, which carries a mandatory minimum 5-year prison term. 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(A)(i). Count four alleged use of a machinegun (here, a pistol that authorities believed operated as a fully automatic firearm) in furtherance of that crime, which carries a 30-year mandatory minimum term. \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(B)(ii). The Government moved to dismiss the fourth count on the basis that it could not establish the count beyond a reasonable doubt, but it maintained that \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(B)(ii)\xe2\x80\x99s machinegun provision was a sentencing enhancement to be determined by the District Court upon a conviction on count three. The court dismissed count four and rejected the Government\xe2\x80\x99s sentencing-enhancement position. Respondents then pleaded guilty to the remaining counts. The court sentenced O\xe2\x80\x99Brien to a 102-month term and Burgess to an 84-month term for their \xc2\xa7924(c) convictions. In affirming the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(B)(ii) ruling, the First Circuit looked primarily to Castillo v. United States, 530 U. S. 120, which held that the machinegun provision in an earlier version of \xc2\xa7924(c) constituted an element of an offense, not a sentencing factor. The court found that Castillo was \xe2\x80\x9cclose to binding,\xe2\x80\x9d absent clearer or more dramatic changes than those made by Congress\xe2\x80\x99 1998 amendment of \xc2\xa7924(c) or a clearer legislative history.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The fact that a firearm was a machinegun is an element to be proved to the jury beyond a reasonable doubt, not a sentencing factor to be proved to the judge at sentencing. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Generally, a fact that \xe2\x80\x9cincrease[s] the prescribed range of penalties to which a criminal defendant is exposed\xe2\x80\x9d is an element of a crime, Apprendi v. New Jersey, 530 U. S. 466, 490, to be charged in an indictment and proved to a jury beyond a reasonable doubt, Hamling v. United States, 418 U. S. 87, 117, rather than proved to a judge at sentencing by a preponderance of the evidence, McMillan v. Pennsylvania, 477 U. S. 79, 9192. Subject to this constitutional constraint, Congress determines whether a factor is an element or a sentencing factor. When Congress is not explicit, courts look to a statute\xe2\x80\x99s provisions and framework for guidance. Analysis of the current machinegun provision begins with Castillo, where the Court found the bare language of \xc2\xa7924\xe2\x80\x99s prior version \xe2\x80\x9cneutral,\xe2\x80\x9d 530 U. S., at 124, but ruled that four factors\xe2\x80\x94(1) language and structure, (2) tradition, (3) risk of unfairness, and (4) severity of the sentence\xe2\x80\x94favored treating the machinegun provision as an element of an offense, id., at 124131; while a fifth factor\xe2\x80\x94legislative history\xe2\x80\x94did not favor either side, ibid. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0As relevant here, the 1998 amendment divided what was once a lengthy principal sentence into separate subparagraphs. Thus, with regard to the first Castillo factor, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s observation that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress placed the element \xe2\x80\x98uses or carries a firearm\xe2\x80\x99 and the word \xe2\x80\x98machinegun\xe2\x80\x99 in a single sentence, not broken up with dashes or separated into subsections,\xe2\x80\x9d 530 U. S., at 124125, no longer holds true. However, the amendment did not affect the second through fifth Castillo factors. Each of them, except for legislative history (which remains relatively silent), continues to favor treating the machinegun provision as an element. The amendment\xe2\x80\x99s effect on the language and structure factor requires closer examination. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Given the Court\xe2\x80\x99s determination in Castillo that the machinegun provision in \xc2\xa7924\xe2\x80\x99s prior version is an element, a substantive change in the statute should not be inferred \xe2\x80\x9c[a]bsent a clear indication from Congress of a change in policy,\xe2\x80\x9d Grogan v. Garner, 498 U. S. 279, 290. Nothing in the 1998 amendment indicates such a change. There are three principal differences between the previous and current \xc2\xa7924(c). The first, a substantive change, shifts what were once mandatory 5-year and 30-year sentences to mandatory minimum sentences. The second, also substantive\xe2\x80\x94made in direct response to the holding in Bailey v. United States, 516 U. S. 137, that \xe2\x80\x9cuses or carries\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa7924\xe2\x80\x99s preamendment version connotes \xe2\x80\x9cmore than mere possession,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 143\xe2\x80\x94adds \xe2\x80\x9cpossesses\xe2\x80\x9d to the \xe2\x80\x9cuses or carries\xe2\x80\x9d language in \xc2\xa7924(c)\xe2\x80\x99s principal paragraph and provides sentencing enhancements for brandishing or discharging the firearm, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(A)(ii) and (iii), which do state sentencing factors, Harris v. United States, 536 U. S. 545, 552556. Neither of these substantive changes suggests that Congress meant to transform the machinegun provision from an element into a sentencing factor. The third difference is the machinegun provision\xe2\x80\x99s relocation from the principal paragraph that unmistakably lists offense elements to a separate subsection, \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(B), but this structural or stylistic change provides no \xe2\x80\x9cclear indication\xe2\x80\x9d that Congress meant to alter its treatment of machineguns as an offense element. A more logical explanation is that the restructuring was intended to break up a lengthy principal paragraph, which exceeded 250 words, into a more readable statute, which is in step with current legislative drafting guidelines. While this Court has recognized that placing factors in separate subsections is one way Congress might signal that it is treating them as sentencing factors rather than elements, Castillo, supra, at 124125, it has rejected the view that such a structural consideration predominates even when other factors point in the other direction, Harris, supra, at 553. The current structure of \xc2\xa7924(c) is more favorable to treating the machinegun provision as a sentencing factor than was true in Castillo, particularly because the machinegun provision is now positioned between the sentencing factors provided in (A)(ii) and (iii) and those in (C)(i) and (ii). This structural point is overcome by the substantial weight of the other Castillo factors and the principle that Congress would not enact so significant a change without a clear indication of its purpose to do so. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

542 F. 3d 921, affirmed.

\nOPINION OF THE COURT
\nUNITED STATES V. O\'BRIEN
\n560 U. S. ____ (2010)
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 08-1569
\n
\n

UNITED STATES, PETITIONER v. MARTIN O\xe2\x80\x99BRIEN and ARTHUR BURGESS

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the first circuit

\n

[May 24, 2010]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Kennedy delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court must interpret, once again, \xc2\xa7924(c) of Title 18 of the United States Code. This provision prohibits the use or carrying of a firearm in relation to a crime of violence or drug trafficking crime, or the possession of a firearm in furtherance of such crimes. \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(A). A violation of the statute carries a mandatory minimum term of five years\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment, \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(A)(i); but if the firearm is a machinegun, the statute requires a 30-year mandatory minimum sentence, \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(B)(ii). Whether a firearm was used, carried, or possessed is, as all concede, an element of the offense. At issue here is whether the fact that the firearm was a machinegun is an element to be proved to the jury beyond a reasonable doubt or a sentencing factor to be proved to the judge at sentencing.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In an earlier case the Court determined that an analogous machinegun provision in a previous version of \xc2\xa7924 constituted an element of an offense to be proved to the jury. Castillo v. United States, 530 U. S. 120 (2000). The Castillo decision, however, addressed the statute as it existed before congressional amendments made in 1998. And in a case after Castillo, the brandishing provision in the post-1998 version of \xc2\xa7924 was held to provide a sentencing factor, not an element of the offense. Harris v. United States, 536 U. S. 545 (2002). In light of the 1998 amendments and the Harris decision, the question of how to interpret \xc2\xa7924\xe2\x80\x99s machinegun provision is considered once more in the instant case.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On June 16, 2005, respondents Martin O\xe2\x80\x99Brien and Arthur Burgess attempted to rob an armored car making a scheduled delivery of cash to a bank. Along with a third collaborator, respondents hid in a minivan and waited for the armored car to make its stop. Each of the men carried a firearm. Containing nearly $2 million and attended by two guards, the armored car arrived. A guard began to unload boxes of coins. The three men came out of the van and, while one of them brandished his weapon, they ordered the guards to get on the ground. One guard did so, but the other ran to a nearby restaurant. The respondents abandoned the robbery and fled without taking any money. No shots were fired, and no one was injured.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Authorities apprehended respondents and recovered the three firearms used during the attempted robbery. The firearms were a semiautomatic Sig-Sauer pistol, an AK47 semiautomatic rifle, and a Cobray pistol. The Cobray pistol had been manufactured as, and had the external appearance of, a semiautomatic firearm. According to the Federal Bureau of Investigation, though, it operated as a fully automatic weapon, apparently due to some alteration of its original firing mechanism. Respondents dispute whether the Cobray in fact did operate as a fully automatic weapon.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents were indicted on multiple counts. Relevant here are counts three and four, both of which charged offenses under \xc2\xa7924(c). Count three charged respondents with using a firearm in furtherance of a crime of violence, which carries a statutory minimum of five years\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment. Count four charged respondents in more specific terms, alleging use of a machinegun (the Cobray) in furtherance of a crime of violence, as proscribed by \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(A) and (B)(ii). The latter provision mandates a minimum sentence of 30 years\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government moved to dismiss count four on the basis that it would be unable to establish the count beyond a reasonable doubt. (The issues in the present case do not require the Court to consider any contention that a defendant who uses, carries, or possesses a firearm must be aware of the weapon\xe2\x80\x99s characteristics. This opinion expresses no views on the point.)

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government then maintained that the machinegun provision in \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(B)(ii) was a sentencing factor, so that, if respondents were convicted of carrying a firearm under count three, the court could determine at sentencing that the particular firearm was a machinegun, thus activating the 30-year mandatory minimum. The District Court dismissed count four, as the Government requested, but rejected the Government\xe2\x80\x99s position that the machinegun provision was a sentencing enhancement to be determined by the court at sentencing once there was a conviction on count three. It ruled that the machinegun provision states an element of a crime. Thus, to invoke the 30-year minimum sentence, the Government was required to charge in the indictment, and then prove to the jury, that the Cobray was a machinegun.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At this point, after the District Court foreclosed the possibility of respondents\xe2\x80\x99 facing a 30-year minimum, respondents pleaded guilty to the remaining counts, including count three. \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court sentenced O\xe2\x80\x99Brien to a 102-month term for his \xc2\xa7924(c) conviction, to run consecutively with his sentence on two other counts. It sentenced Burgess to an 84-month term for his \xc2\xa7924(c) conviction, also to run consecutively to his sentence on the other charges. The Government appealed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling that the \xc2\xa7924 machinegun provision constitutes an element of an offense instead of a sentencing factor.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The United States Court of Appeals for the First Circuit affirmed. It looked primarily to Castillo, 530 U. S. 120, which held that the machinegun provision in an earlier version of \xc2\xa7924(c) constituted an element of an offense, not a sentencing factor. The court noted that the statute under consideration in Castillo had been revised by Congress, \xe2\x80\x9cbreak[ing] what was a single run-on sentence into subparagraphs,\xe2\x80\x9d and it acknowledged that the earlier repealed version of the statute was \xe2\x80\x9cslightly more favorable to the [respondents] than the current version[,] but not markedly so.\xe2\x80\x9d 542 F.\xc2\xa03d 921, 925 (2008). It found \xe2\x80\x9cno evidence that the breaking up of the sentence into the present subdivisions or recasting of language was anything more than a current trend\xe2\x80\x94probably for ease of reading\xe2\x80\x94to convert lengthy sentences in criminal statutes into subsections in the fashion of the tax code.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 926. The court concluded: \xe2\x80\x9cAbsent a clearer or more dramatic change in language or legislative history expressing a specific intent to assign judge or jury functions, we think that Castillo is close to binding,\xe2\x80\x9d and any reconsideration of the issue should be left to this Court. Ibid.; see also Rodriguez de Quijas v. Shearson/American Express, Inc., 490 U. S. 477, 484 (1989) (\xe2\x80\x9cIf a precedent of this Court has direct application in a case, yet appears to rest on reasons rejected in some other line of decisions, the Court of Appeals should follow the case which directly controls, leaving to this Court the prerogative of overruling its own decisions\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari. 557 U. S. ___ (2009).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Elements of a crime must be charged in an indictment and proved to a jury beyond a reasonable doubt. Hamling v. United States, 418 U. S. 87, 117 (1974); Jones v. United States, 526 U. S. 227, 232 (1999). Sentencing factors, on the other hand, can be proved to a judge at sentencing by a preponderance of the evidence. See McMillan v. Pennsylvania, 477 U. S. 79, 9192 (1986). Though one exception has been established, see Almendarez-Torres v. United States, 523 U. S. 224, 228 (1998), \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[i]t is unconstitutional for a legislature to remove from the jury the assessment of facts that increase the prescribed range of penalties to which a criminal defendant is exposed.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Apprendi v. New Jersey, 530 U. S. 466, 490 (2000) (quoting Jones, supra, at 252253 (Stevens, J., concurring)). In other words, while sentencing factors may guide or confine a judge\xe2\x80\x99s discretion in sentencing an offender \xe2\x80\x9cwithin the range prescribed by statute,\xe2\x80\x9d Apprendi, supra, at 481, judge-found sentencing factors cannot increase the maximum sentence a defendant might otherwise receive based purely on the facts found by the jury.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Subject to this constitutional constraint, whether a given fact is an element of the crime itself or a sentencing factor is a question for Congress. When Congress is not explicit, as is often the case because it seldom directly addresses the distinction between sentencing factors and elements, courts look to the provisions and the framework of the statute to determine whether a fact is an element or a sentencing factor. Almendarez-Torres, supra, at 228. In examining whether the machinegun provision in \xc2\xa7924 is an element or a sentencing factor, the analysis must begin with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s previous examination of the question in Castillo.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Castillo, the Court considered a prior version of \xc2\xa7924, which provided:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(c)(1) Whoever, during and in relation to any crime of violence or drug trafficking crime \xe2\x80\xa6 , uses or carries a firearm, shall, in addition to the punishment provided for such crime of violence or drug trafficking crime, be sentenced to imprisonment for five years, and if the firearm is a short-barreled rifle [or a] short-barreled shotgun to imprisonment for ten years, and if the firearm is a machinegun, or a destructive device, or is equipped with a firearm silencer or firearm muffler, to imprisonment for thirty years\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7924(c)(1) (1988 ed., Supp. V).

\n

\nIn determining whether the machinegun provision in the just-quoted version of \xc2\xa7924 constituted an element or a sentencing factor, the Court in Castillo observed that the bare statutory language was \xe2\x80\x9cneutral.\xe2\x80\x9d 530 U. S., at 124. It examined five factors directed at determining congressional intent: (1) language and structure, (2) tradition, (3) risk of unfairness, (4) severity of the sentence, and (5) legislative history. Id., at 124131. The Court unanimously concluded that the machinegun provision provided an element of an offense, noting that the first four factors favored treating it as such while legislative history did not significantly favor either side. Ibid.

\n

III

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 924(c) was amended to its current form in 1998.

\n

The amendment had been enacted when the Court considered Castillo, supra, at 125, but the pre-1998 version of the statute was at issue there. The instant case concerns the post-1998 (and current) version of the statute, which provides:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(A) Except to the extent that a greater minimum sentence is otherwise provided by this subsection or by any other provision of law, any person who, during and in relation to any crime of violence or drug trafficking crime \xe2\x80\xa6 uses or carries a firearm, or who, in furtherance of any such crime, possesses a firearm, shall, in addition to the punishment provided for such crime of violence or drug trafficking crime\xe2\x80\x94

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(i) be sentenced to a term of imprisonment of not less than 5 years;

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(ii) if the firearm is brandished, be sentenced to a term of imprisonment of not less than 7 years; and

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(iii) if the firearm is discharged, be sentenced to a term of imprisonment of not less than 10 years.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(B) If the firearm possessed by a person convicted of a violation of this subsection\xe2\x80\x94

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(i) is a short-barreled rifle, short-barreled shotgun, or semiautomatic assault weapon, the person shall be sentenced to a term of imprisonment of not less than 10 years; or

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(ii) is a machinegun or a destructive device, or is equipped with a firearm silencer or firearm muffler, the person shall be sentenced to a term of imprisonment of not less than 30 years.\xe2\x80\x9d 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7924(c)(1) (2006 ed.).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The 1998 amendment did make substantive changes to the statute, to be discussed below; but for purposes of the present case the most apparent effect of the amendment was to divide what was once a lengthy principal sentence into separate subparagraphs. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s observation in considering the first Castillo factor, that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress placed the element \xe2\x80\x98uses or carries a firearm\xe2\x80\x99 and the word \xe2\x80\x98machinegun\xe2\x80\x99 in a single sentence, not broken up with dashes or separated into subsections,\xe2\x80\x9d 530 U. S., at 124125, no longer holds true. Aside from this new structure, however, the 1998 amendment of \xc2\xa7924 did nothing to affect the second through fifth Castillo factors. Each of the factors, except for legislative history (which, assuming its relevance, remains relatively silent), continues to favor the conclusion that the machinegun provision is an element of an offense.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Legal tradition and past congressional practice are the second Castillo factor. The factor is to be consulted when, as here, a statute\xe2\x80\x99s text is unclear as to whether certain facts constitute elements or sentencing factors. Sentencing factors traditionally involve characteristics of the offender\xe2\x80\x94such as recidivism, cooperation with law enforcement, or acceptance of responsibility. Id., at 126. Characteristics of the offense itself are traditionally treated as elements, and the use of a machinegun under \xc2\xa7924(c) lies \xe2\x80\x9cclosest to the heart of the crime at issue.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 127. This is no less true today than it was 10 years ago in Castillo. Unsurprisingly, firearm type is treated as an element in a number of statutes, as \xe2\x80\x9cnumerous gun crimes make substantive distinctions between weapons such as pistols and machineguns.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; see, e.g., 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7922(a)(4), 922(b)(4), and 922(o)(1).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government counters that this tradition or pattern has evolved since the version of \xc2\xa7924(c) under review in Castillo was enacted. The Government contends that the Federal Sentencing Guidelines altered the tradition by treating the possession of a firearm as a sentencing factor. Brief for United States 23 (citing United States Sentencing Commission, Guidelines Manual \xc2\xa72K2.1(a)(5) (Nov. 1998) (raising base offense level \xe2\x80\x9cif the offense involved a firearm\xe2\x80\x9d)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The argument is not persuasive. The Sentencing Reform Act of 1984, 98 Stat. 1987, establishing the Federal Sentencing Guidelines, was enacted four years before the version of \xc2\xa7924 under review in Castillo, see Anti-Drug Abuse Act of 1988, \xc2\xa76460, 102 Stat. 4373. While the resulting Guidelines were not effective until 1987, this was still before the 1988 enactment of the statute at issue in Castillo, and 13 years before this Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion in Castillo that firearm type is traditionally treated as an offense element. The Government cannot claim the benefit of any shift in how the law traditionally treats firearm type from the Guidelines, for that supposed shift would have occurred before the 1988 version of \xc2\xa7924 was enacted. The Guidelines were explicitly taken into account when this Court analyzed the traditions in Castillo. 530 U. S., at 126 (discussing Federal Sentencing Guidelines in determining what traditionally qualifies as a sentencing factor).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The third Castillo factor, potential unfairness, was unchanged by the restructuring of \xc2\xa7924. The Court explained in Castillo that treating the machinegun provision as a sentencing factor \xe2\x80\x9cmight unnecessarily produce a conflict between the judge and the jury\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9ca jury may well have to decide which of several weapons\xe2\x80\x9d a defendant used. Id., at 128. The concern was that the judge may not know which weapon the jurors determined a defendant used, and \xe2\x80\x9ca judge\xe2\x80\x99s later, sentencing-related decision that the defendant used the machinegun, rather than, say, the pistol, might conflict with the jury\xe2\x80\x99s belief that he actively used the pistol.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. This same concern arises under the current version of \xc2\xa7924, where jurors might have to determine which among several weapons a defendant used, carried, or possessed in furtherance of a crime.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government\xe2\x80\x99s response, that permitting a judge to make this finding would \xe2\x80\x9cstreamlin[e] guilt-stage proceedings, without interfering with the accuracy of fact-finding,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 33, is unconvincing. It does not address the particular unfairness concern expressed in Castillo, which was not alleviated by the restructuring of \xc2\xa7924. And the Government does not suggest that it would be subjected to any unfairness if the machinegun provision continues to be treated as an element.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The fourth Castillo factor, the severity of the sentence accompanying a finding that a defendant carried a machinegun under \xc2\xa7924, was also unaffected by the statute\xe2\x80\x99s restructuring. A finding that a defendant carried a machinegun under \xc2\xa7924, in contrast to some less dangerous firearm, vaults a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s mandatory minimum sentence from 5 to 30 years, Castillo, supra, at 131, or from 7 to 30 years if, as in this case, the firearm was brandished, \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(A)(ii). This is not akin to the \xe2\x80\x9cincremental changes in the minimum\xe2\x80\x9d that one would \xe2\x80\x9cexpect to see in provisions meant to identify matters for the sentencing judge\xe2\x80\x99s consideration,\xe2\x80\x9d Harris, 536 U. S., at 554 (from 5 years to 7 years); it is a drastic, sixfold increase that strongly suggests a separate substantive crime.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is one substantive difference between the old and new versions of \xc2\xa7924 that might bear on this fourth factor. The previous version of \xc2\xa7924 provided mandatory sentences: 5 years for using or carrying a firearm and 30 years if the firearm is a machinegun, for example. See \xc2\xa7924(c)(1) (1988 ed., Supp. V). The current statute provides only mandatory minimums: not less than 5 years for using or possessing a firearm; not less than 7 for brandishing it; and not less than 30 if the firearm is a machinegun. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(A)(i), (A)(ii), (B)(ii). The Government argues that this difference is critical because a 30-year sentence is conceivable under the statute even without a finding that the particular weapon is a machinegun. Brief for United States 25.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This is a distinction in theory, perhaps, but not in practice. Neither the Government nor any party or amicus has identified a single defendant whose conviction under \xc2\xa7924 for possessing or brandishing a nonspecific firearm led to a sentence approaching the 30-year sentence that is required when the firearm is a machinegun. Respondents advise, without refutation, that most courts impose the mandatory minimum of 7 years\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment for brandishing a nonspecific weapon and the longest sentence that has come to the litigants\xe2\x80\x99 or the Court\xe2\x80\x99s attention is 14 years. Brief for Respondent O\xe2\x80\x99Brien 46, 48 (citing United States v. Batts, 317 Fed. Appx. 329 (CA4 2009) (per curiam)); see also Harris, supra, at 578 (Thomas, J., dissenting). Indeed, in the instant case, Burgess received the statutory minimum 7-year sentence, and O\xe2\x80\x99Brien received only 18 months more than that. Once the machinegun enhancement was off the table, the Government itself did not seek anything approaching 30-year terms, instead requesting 12-year terms for each respondent.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The immense danger posed by machineguns, the moral depravity in choosing the weapon, and the substantial increase in the minimum sentence provided by the statute support the conclusion that this prohibition is an element of the crime, not a sentencing factor. It is not likely that Congress intended to remove the indictment and jury trial protections when it provided for such an extreme sentencing increase. See Jones, 526 U. S., at 233 (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is at best questionable whether the specification of facts sufficient to increase a penalty range by two-thirds, let alone from 15 years to life, was meant to carry none of the process safeguards that elements of an offense bring with them for a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s benefit\xe2\x80\x9d). Perhaps Congress was not concerned with parsing the distinction between elements and sentencing factors, a matter more often discussed by the courts when discussing the proper allocation of functions between judge and jury. Instead, it likely was more focused on deterring the crime by creating the mandatory minimum sentences. But the severity of the increase in this case counsels in favor of finding that the prohibition is an element, at least absent some clear congressional indication to the contrary.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The fifth factor considered in Castillo was legislative history, and the Court there found it to be of little help. 530 U. S., at 130 (\xe2\x80\x9cInsofar as this history may be relevant, however, it does not significantly help the Government\xe2\x80\x9d). The 1998 amendment has its own legislative record, discussed below, but the parties accurately observe that it is silent as to congressional consideration of the distinction between elements and sentencing factors. Brief for United States 29; Brief for Respondent O\xe2\x80\x99Brien 2829. This silence is not neutral, however, because as explained below, it tends to counsel against finding that Congress made a substantive change to this statutory provision.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Four of the five factors the Court relied upon in Castillo point in the same direction they did 10 years ago. How the 1998 amendment affects the remaining factor\xe2\x80\x94the provision\xe2\x80\x99s language and structure\xe2\x80\x94requires closer\nexamination.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Castillo, the Court interpreted \xc2\xa7924(c) in its original version, though Congress had at that point already amended the provision. Here, the applicable principle is that Congress does not enact substantive changes sub silentio. See Director of Revenue of Mo. v. CoBank ACB, 531 U. S. 316, 323 (2001). In light of Castillo\xe2\x80\x99s determination that the machinegun provision in the previous version of \xc2\xa7924 is an element, a change should not be inferred \xe2\x80\x9c[a]bsent a clear indication from Congress of a change in policy.\xe2\x80\x9d Grogan v. Garner, 498 U. S. 279, 290 (1991); see also Rivers v. Roadway Express, Inc., 511 U. S. 298, 313, n. 12 (1994) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hen this Court construes a statute, it is explaining its understanding of what the statute has meant continuously since the date when it became law\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government argues that the 1998 amendment restructuring \xc2\xa7924(c) demonstrates the congressional judgment to reclassify the machinegun provision as a sentencing factor, rather than as an offense element. But the better understanding, as the Government acknowledged in its submission in Castillo, is that \xe2\x80\x9cthere is nothing to suggest that the 1998 amendments were intended to change, rather than simply reorganize and clarify, [\xc2\xa7924]\xe2\x80\x99s treatment of firearm type.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States, O. T. 1999, No. 99\xe2\x80\x93658, p. 41. A closer review of the 1998 amendment confirms this.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There are three principal differences between the previous and current versions of \xc2\xa7924(c): two substantive changes and a third regarding the stylistic structure of the statute. The first difference, as discussed above, supra, at 10, is that the amendment changed what were once mandatory sentences into mandatory minimum sentences. A person convicted of the primary offense of using or carrying a firearm during a crime of violence was once to \xe2\x80\x9cbe sentenced to imprisonment for five years,\xe2\x80\x9d but under the current version he or she is to \xe2\x80\x9cbe sentenced to a term of imprisonment of not less than 5 years.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The second difference is that the amended version includes the word \xe2\x80\x9cpossesses\xe2\x80\x9d in addition to \xe2\x80\x9cuses or carries\xe2\x80\x9d in its principal paragraph, and then adds the substantive provisions in \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(A)(ii) and (iii), which provide mandatory minimums for brandishing (7 years) and discharging (10 years) the firearm. These provisions are new substantive additions to the text of the previous version, which provided a bare 5-year mandatory minimum for any offender who \xe2\x80\x9cuse[d] or carrie[d] a firearm,\xe2\x80\x9d without concern for how the firearm was used.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The changes were a direct response to this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Bailey v. United States, 516 U. S. 137 (1995), which held that the word \xe2\x80\x9cuse\xe2\x80\x9d in the pre&nbhyph;amendment version of \xc2\xa7924 \xe2\x80\x9cmust connote more than mere possession of a firearm by a person who commits a drug offense.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 143. The Court in Bailey went on to observe that, \xe2\x80\x9c[h]ad Congress intended possession alone to trigger liability under \xc2\xa7924(c)(1), it easily could have so provided\xe2\x80\x9d by using the word \xe2\x80\x9cpossess,\xe2\x80\x9d as it had so frequently done in other statutory provisions. Ibid. Three years later, Congress made the change and added the word \xe2\x80\x9cpossesses\xe2\x80\x9d to the principal paragraph. Congress additionally provided mandatory sentences above the 5-year minimum depending on whether and how the firearm was used. Sections 924(c)(1)(A)(ii) and (iii) provide sentencing enhancements for brandishing or discharging the firearm, and the Court has held that these enhancements are sentencing factors to be found by a judge. See Harris, 536 U. S., at 552556; see also Dean v. United States, 556 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (slip op., at 5) (referring to the brandishing and discharge provisions as \xe2\x80\x9csentencing factors\xe2\x80\x9d). The 1998 amendment was colloquially known as the \xe2\x80\x9cBailey Fix Act.\xe2\x80\x9d 144 Cong. Rec. 26608 (1998) (remarks of Sen. DeWine); see also Dean, supra, at ___ (Stevens, J., dissenting) (slip op.,\nat 3).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Aside from shifting the mandatory sentences to mandatory minimums, and this so-called Bailey fix, Congress left the substance of the statute unchanged. Neither of these substantive changes suggests that Congress meant to transform the machinegun provision from an element into a sentencing factor.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government stresses a third, structural, difference in the statute, pointing out that the machinegun provision now resides in a separate subsection, \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(B), whereas it once resided in the principal paragraph that unmistakably lists offense elements. This structural or stylistic change, though, does not provide a \xe2\x80\x9cclear indication\xe2\x80\x9d that Congress meant to alter its treatment of machineguns as an offense element. See Grogan, 498 U. S., at 290. A more logical explanation for the restructuring is that it broke up a lengthy principal paragraph, which exceeded 250 words even before adding more to it for the Bailey fix, into a more readable statute. This is in step with current legislative drafting guidelines, which advise drafters to break lengthy statutory provisions into separate subsections that can be read more easily. See House Legislative Counsel\xe2\x80\x99s Manual on Drafting Style, HLC No. 104.1, \xc2\xa7312, pp.\xc2\xa02325 (1995); Senate Office of the Legislative Counsel, Legislative Drafting Manual \xc2\xa7112, pp. 1011 (1997).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While the Court has indicated that placing factors in separate subsections is one way Congress might signal that it is treating them as sentencing factors as opposed to elements, Castillo, 530 U. S., at 124125, Harris, 536 U. S., at 552553, it has rejected the view that this structural consideration predominates even when other factors point in the other direction, id., at 553 (\xe2\x80\x9c[E]ven if a statute \xe2\x80\x98has a look to it suggesting that the numbered subsections are only sentencing provisions,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the Court will not ignore \xe2\x80\x9ccompelling evidence to the contrary\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Jones, 526 U. S., at 232)). For instance, in Jones the Court found that the federal carjacking statute set forth elements of multiple offenses despite a structure similar to the statute at issue here. Id., at 232239. And in Harris, the Court was careful to point out that, unlike the case at bar, the other Castillo factors \xe2\x80\x9creinforce[d] the single-offense interpretation implied by the statute\xe2\x80\x99s structure.\xe2\x80\x9d 536 U. S., at 553.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In examining the amended version of \xc2\xa7924(c)\xe2\x80\x99s structure, there is an additional consideration that supports interpreting the machinegun provision to be an offense element. As explained above, the brandishing and discharge provisions codified in \xc2\xa7924(A)(ii) and (iii) do state sentencing factors. See Harris, supra, at 552556; Dean, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 5). Had Congress intended to treat firearm type as a sentencing factor, it likely would have listed firearm types as clauses (iv) and (v) of subparagraph (A), instead of as clauses (i) and (ii) of subparagraph (B). By listing firearm type in stand-alone subparagraph (B), Congress set it apart from the sentencing factors in (A)(ii) and (iii); this is consistent with preserving firearm type as an element of a separate offense.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To be sure, there are some arguments in favor of treating the machinegun provision as a sentencing factor. The current structure of \xc2\xa7924(c) is more favorable to that interpretation than was true in Castillo, particularly because the machinegun provision is now positioned between the sentencing factors provided in (A)(ii) and (iii), see Harris, supra, at 552556, and the recidivist provisions in (C)(i) and (ii), which are typically sentencing factors as well. See Almendarez-Torres, 523 U. S., at 230. These points are overcome, however, by the substantial weight of the other Castillo factors and the principle that Congress would not enact so significant a change without a clear indication of its purpose to do so. The evident congressional purpose was to amend the statute to counteract Bailey and to make the statute more readable but not otherwise to alter the substance of the statute. The analysis and holding of Castillo control this case. The machinegun provision in \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(B)(ii) is an element of an offense.

\n

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment of the Court of Appeals is affirmed.

UNITED STATES, PETITIONER v. MARTIN O\xe2\x80\x99BRIEN and ARTHUR BURGESS

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the first circuit

\n

[May 24, 2010]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens, concurring.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A \xe2\x80\x9csentencing factor\xe2\x80\x9d may serve two very different functions. As a historical matter, the term has described a fact that a trial judge might rely upon when choosing a specific sentence within the range authorized by the legislature. In that setting, the judge has broad discretion in determining both the significance of the factor and whether it has been established by reliable evidence.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the 1970\xe2\x80\x99s and 1980\xe2\x80\x99s, as part of a national effort to enact tougher sentences,[Footnote 1] a new type of \xe2\x80\x9csentencing factor\xe2\x80\x9d emerged. Since then the term has been used to describe facts, found by the judge by a preponderance of the evidence, that have the effect of imposing mandatory limits on a sentencing judge\xe2\x80\x99s discretion. When used as an element of a mandatory sentencing scheme, a sentencing factor is the functional equivalent of an element of the criminal offense itself. In these circumstances, I continue to believe the Constitution requires proof beyond a reasonable doubt of this \xe2\x80\x9cfactor.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We first encountered the use of a \xe2\x80\x9csentencing factor\xe2\x80\x9d in the mandatory minimum context in McMillan v. Pennsylvania, 477 U. S. 79 (1986), when we examined the constitutionality of Pennsylvania\xe2\x80\x99s 1982 Mandatory Minimum Sentencing Act (Act).[Footnote 2] The Pennsylvania statute subjected anyone convicted of a specified felony to a mandatory minimum 5-year sentence if the trial judge found, by a preponderance of the evidence, that the defendant \xe2\x80\x9cvisibly possessed a firearm\xe2\x80\x9d during the commission of the offense. See id., at 81\xe2\x80\x9382. In four prosecutions under the Act, the trial judges had each held that the statute was unconstitutional and imposed sentences lower than the 5-year mandatory minimum, presumably because they recognized that the statute treated the visible possession of a firearm as the functional equivalent of an offense element. Id., at 82. On appeal, the Pennsylvania Supreme Court consolidated the four cases and reversed.[Footnote 3] Id., at 83. It reasoned that because visible possession of a firearm was a mere \xe2\x80\x9csentencing factor,\xe2\x80\x9d rather than an element of any of the specified offenses defined by the legislature, the protections afforded by cases like In re Winship, 397 U. S. 358 (1970),[Footnote 4] did not apply.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A bare majority of the McMillan Court endorsed this novel use of the sentencing factor concept. Five Justices concluded that the prerequisite for a mandatory sentence is just a \xe2\x80\x9csentencing factor,\xe2\x80\x9d rather than an \xe2\x80\x9celement of the offense,\xe2\x80\x9d because the factor does not \xe2\x80\x9calte[r] the maximum penalty for the crime\xe2\x80\x9d and merely \xe2\x80\x9climit[s] the sentencing court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion in selecting a penalty within the range already available to it.\xe2\x80\x9d 477 U. S., at 87\xe2\x80\x9388. Yet, although the Pennsylvania Act\xe2\x80\x99s 5-year mandatory sentence for visible possession of a firearm during the commission of an offense did not exceed the statutory maximum that otherwise applied for the crimes of conviction, a positive finding on the so-called sentencing factor mandated the imposition of a sentence that exceeded the punishment the defendant would have otherwise received. See id., at 103\xe2\x80\x93104 (Stevens, J., dissenting).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority opinion in McMillan can fairly be described as pathmarking, but unlike one of its predecessors, Winship, it pointed in the wrong direction. For reasons set forth in the opinions joined by the four dissenting Justices in McMillan, I continue to believe that McMillan was incorrectly decided. See id., at 93\xe2\x80\x9394 (Marshall, J., dissenting); id., at 95\xe2\x80\x93104 (Stevens, J., dissenting).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Not only was McMillan wrong the day it was decided, but its reasoning has been substantially undermined\xe2\x80\x94if not eviscerated\xe2\x80\x94by the development of our Sixth Amendment jurisprudence in more recent years. We now understand that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[i]t is unconstitutional [under the Sixth Amendment] for a legislature to remove from the jury the assessment of facts that increase the prescribed range of penalties to which a criminal defendant is exposed.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Apprendi v. New Jersey, 530 U. S. 466, 490 (2000) (quoting Jones v. United States, 526 U. S. 227, 252\xe2\x80\x93253 (1999) (Stevens, J., concurring)). Harmonizing Apprendi with our existing Sixth Amendment jurisprudence, we explained that \xe2\x80\x9cany fact that increases the penalty for a crime beyond the prescribed statutory maximum must be submitted to a jury, and proved beyond a reasonable doubt.\xe2\x80\x9d 530 U. S., at 490 (emphasis added). In other words, we narrowed our holding to those facts that effectively raised the ceiling on the offense, but did not then consider whether the logic of our holding applied also\nto those facts necessary to set the floor of a particular sentence.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As Justice Thomas eloquently explained in his dissent in Harris v. United States, 536 U. S. 545, 572 (2002), the reasoning in our decision in Apprendi applies with equal force in the context of mandatory minimums. There is, quite simply, no reason to distinguish between facts that trigger punishment in excess of the statutory maximum and facts that trigger a mandatory minimum. This case vividly illustrates the point. It is quite plain that there is a world of difference between the 812-year sentence and the 7-year sentence the judge imposed on the defendants in this case and the 30-year sentence mandated by the machinegun finding under 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(B).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Mandatory minimums may have a particularly acute practical effect in this type of statutory scheme which contains an implied statutory maximum of life, see ante, at 10. There is, in this type of case, no ceiling; there is only a floor below which a sentence cannot fall. Furthermore, absent a positive finding on one of \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)\xe2\x80\x99s enumerated factors, it is quite clear that no judge would impose a sentence as great as the sentences commanded by the provision at issue in this case. Indeed, it appears that, but for those subject to the 30-year mandatory minimum, no defendant has ever been sentenced to a sentence anywhere near 30 years for a \xc2\xa7924(c) offense. See Brief for Respondent O\xe2\x80\x99Brien 46\xe2\x80\x9347, and n.\xc2\xa015.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Apprendi should have signaled the end of McMillan, just as it signaled the unconstitutionality of state and federal determinate sentencing schemes in Blakely v. Washington, 542 U. S. 296 (2004), and United States v. Booker, 543 U. S. 220 (2005). But thanks to an unpersuasive attempt to distinguish Apprendi,[Footnote 5] and a reluctant Apprendi dissenter, McMillan survived over the protest of four Members of the Court. See Harris, 536 U. S., at 569\xe2\x80\x93570 (Breyer, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9cI cannot easily distinguish Apprendi \xe2\x80\xa6 from this case in terms of logic. For that reason, I cannot agree with the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s opinion insofar as it finds such a distinction. At the same time \xe2\x80\xa6 I cannot yet accept [Apprendi\xe2\x80\x99s] rule\xe2\x80\x9d). It appears, however, that the reluctant Apprendi dissenter may no longer be reluctant.[Footnote 6]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I am therefore in full agreement with Justice Thomas\xe2\x80\x99 separate writing today, post, at 1\xe2\x80\x932, as I was with his Harris dissent. McMillan and Harris should be overruled, at least to the extent that they authorize judicial factfinding on a preponderance of the evidence standard of facts that \xe2\x80\x9cexpos[e] a defendant to [a] greater punishment than what is otherwise legally prescribed \xe2\x80\xa6\xe2\x80\x9d Harris, 536 U. S., at 579 (Thomas, J., dissenting). Any such fact is the functional equivalent of an element of the offense.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In my view, the simplest, and most correct, solution to the case before us would be to recognize that any fact mandating the imposition of a sentence more severe than a judge would otherwise have discretion to impose should be treated as an element of the offense. The unanimity of our decision today does not imply that McMillan is safe from a direct challenge to its foundation.

L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 61, 64\xe2\x80\x9365 (1993) (footnote omitted); see also id., at 69 (\xe2\x80\x9c[M]ost of the current mandatory enhancement laws did not appear until the 1970s\xe2\x80\x9d); Schulhofer, Rethinking Mandatory Minimums, 28 Wake Forest L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 199, 200\xe2\x80\x93201 (1993) (discussing history of federal mandatory minimum sentencing regime).

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Thomas, concurring in the judgment.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Harris v. United States, 536 U. S. 545 (2002), this Court held that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[o]ther than the fact of a prior conviction, any fact that increases the penalty for a crime beyond the prescribed statutory maximum,\xe2\x80\x99 whether the statute calls it an element or a sentencing factor, \xe2\x80\x98must be submitted to a jury, and proved beyond a reasonable doubt,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 550 (quoting Apprendi v. New Jersey, 530 U. S. 466, 490 (2000)). I continue to believe that this constitutional requirement applies to sentencing facts that, like the machinegun enhancement at issue here, 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(B)(ii), \xe2\x80\x9calte[r] the [defendant\xe2\x80\x99s] statutorily mandated sentencing range, by increasing the mandatory minimum sentence,\xe2\x80\x9d regardless of whether they alter the statutory maximum penalty, Harris, 536 U. S., at 577 (Thomas, J., dissenting); see id., at 577\xe2\x80\x93578 (\xe2\x80\x9cAs a matter of common sense, an increased mandatory minimum heightens the loss of liberty and represents the increased stigma society attaches to the offense. Consequently, facts that trigger an increased mandatory minimum sentence warrant constitutional safeguards\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In my view, it makes no difference whether the sentencing fact \xe2\x80\x9cvaults a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s mandatory minimum sentence\xe2\x80\x9d by many years, ante, at 10, or only \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98incremental[ly] changes\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d it by a few, ibid. (quoting Harris, supra, at 554). Nor does it make a difference whether the sentencing fact \xe2\x80\x9cinvolve[s] characteristics of the offender\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9c[c]haracteristics of the offense,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 8, or which direction the other factors in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s five-factor test may tilt. One question decides the matter: If a sentencing fact either \xe2\x80\x9craises the floor or raises the ceiling\xe2\x80\x9d of the range of punishments to which a defendant is exposed, it is, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98by definition [an] \xe2\x80\x9celemen[t].\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Harris, supra, at 579 (Thomas, J., dissenting) (quoting Apprendi, supra, at 483, n.\xc2\xa010).

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Without a finding that a defendant used a machinegun, the penalty range for a conviction under \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(A)(i) is five years to life imprisonment. But once that finding is added, the penalty range becomes harsher\xe2\x80\x9430 years to life imprisonment, \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(B)(ii)\xe2\x80\x94thus \xe2\x80\x9cexpos[ing] a defendant to greater punishment than what is otherwise legally prescribed,\xe2\x80\x9d Harris, 536 U. S., at 579 (Thomas, J., dissenting). As a consequence, \xe2\x80\x9cit is ultimately beside the point whether as a matter of statutory interpretation [the machinegun enhancement] is a sentencing factor.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 576. \xe2\x80\x9c[A]s a constitutional matter,\xe2\x80\x9d because it establishes a harsher range of punishments, it must be treated as an element of a separate, aggravated offense that is submitted to a jury and proved beyond a reasonable doubt. Ibid.
