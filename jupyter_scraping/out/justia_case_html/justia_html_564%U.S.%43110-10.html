\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

TURNER v. ROGERS et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the supreme court of south carolina

\n

No. 10\xe2\x80\x9310.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 23, 2011\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 20, 2011

\n

After a South Carolina family court ordered petitioner Turner to pay $51.73 per week to respondent Rogers to help support their child, Turner repeatedly failed to pay the amount due and was held in contempt five times. For the first four, he was sentenced to 90 days\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment, but he ultimately paid what he owed (twice without being jailed, twice after spending a few days in custody). The fifth time he did not pay but completed a 6-month sentence. After his release, the family court clerk issued a new \xe2\x80\x9cshow cause\xe2\x80\x9d order against Turner because he was $5728.76 in arrears. Both he and Rogers were unrepresented by counsel at his brief civil contempt hearing. The judge found Turner in willful contempt and sentenced him to 12 months in prison without making any finding as to his ability to pay or indicating on the contempt order form whether he was able to make support payments. After Turner completed his sentence, the South Carolina Supreme Court rejected his claim that the Federal Constitution entitled him to counsel at his contempt hearing, declaring that civil contempt does not require all the constitutional safeguards applicable in criminal contempt proceedings.

\n

Held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0Even though Turner has completed his 12-month sentence, and there are not alleged to be collateral consequences of the contempt determination that might keep the dispute alive, this case is not moot, because it is \xe2\x80\x9ccapable of repetition\xe2\x80\x9d while \xe2\x80\x9cevading review,\xe2\x80\x9d Southern Pac. Terminal Co. v. Interstate Commerce Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 219 U. S. 498, 515. A case remains live if \xe2\x80\x9c(1) the challenged action [is] in its duration too short to be fully litigated prior to its cessation or expiration, and (2) there [is] a reasonable expectation that the same complaining party [will] be subjected to the same action again.\xe2\x80\x9d Weinstein v. Bradford, 423 U. S. 147, 149. Here, the \xe2\x80\x9cchallenged action,\xe2\x80\x9d Turner\xe2\x80\x99s imprisonment for up to 12 months, is \xe2\x80\x9cin its duration too short to be fully litigated\xe2\x80\x9d through the state courts (and arrive here) prior to its \xe2\x80\x9cexpiration.\xe2\x80\x9d First Nat\xe2\x80\x99l Bank of Boston v. Bellotti, 435 U. S. 765, 774. And there is a more than \xe2\x80\x9creasonable\xe2\x80\x9d likelihood that Turner will again be \xe2\x80\x9csubjected to the same action\xe2\x80\x9d because he has frequently failed to make his support payments, has been the subject of several civil contempt proceedings, has been imprisoned several times, and is, once again, the subject of civil contempt proceedings for failure to pay. DeFunis v. Odegaard, 416 U. S. 312, and St. Pierre v. United States, 319 U. S. 41, distinguished. Pp. 5\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0The Fourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Due Process Clause does not automatically require the State to provide counsel at civil contempt proceedings to an indigent noncustodial parent who is subject to a child support order, even if that individual faces incarceration. In particular, that Clause does not require that counsel be provided where the opposing parent or other custodian is not represented by counsel and the State provides alternative procedural safeguards equivalent to adequate notice of the importance of the ability to pay, a fair opportunity to present, and to dispute, relevant information, and express court findings as to the supporting parent\xe2\x80\x99s ability to comply with the support order. Pp. 7\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0This Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents provide no definitive answer to the question whether counsel must be provided. The Sixth Amendment grants an indigent criminal defendant the right to counsel, see, e.g., United States v. Dixon, 509 U. S. 688, 696, but does not govern civil cases. Civil and criminal contempt differ. A court may not impose punishment \xe2\x80\x9cin a civil contempt proceeding when it is clearly established that the alleged contemnor is unable to comply with the terms of the order.\xe2\x80\x9d Hicks v. Feiock, 485 U. S. 624, 638, n.\xc2\xa09. And once a civil contemnor complies with the underlying order, he is purged of the contempt and is free. Id., at 633. The Due Process Clause allows a State to provide fewer procedural protections in civil contempt proceedings than in a criminal case. Id., at 637\xe2\x80\x93641. Cases directly concerning a right to counsel in civil cases have found a presumption of such a right \xe2\x80\x9conly\xe2\x80\x9d in cases involving incarceration, but have not held that a right to counsel exists in all such cases. See In\xc2\xa0re Gault, 387 U. S. 1; Vitek v. Jones, 445 U. S. 480; and Lassiter v. Department of Social Servs. of Durham Cty., 452 U. S. 18. Pp. 7\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Because a contempt proceeding to compel support payments is civil, the question whether the \xe2\x80\x9cspecific dictates of due process\xe2\x80\x9d require appointed counsel is determined by examining the \xe2\x80\x9cdistinct factors\xe2\x80\x9d this Court has used to decide what specific safeguards are needed to make a civil proceeding fundamentally fair. Mathews v. Eldridge, 424 U. S. 319, 335. As relevant here those factors include (1) the nature of \xe2\x80\x9cthe private interest that will be affected,\xe2\x80\x9d (2) the comparative \xe2\x80\x9crisk\xe2\x80\x9d of an \xe2\x80\x9cerroneous deprivation\xe2\x80\x9d of that interest with and without \xe2\x80\x9cadditional or substitute procedural safeguards,\xe2\x80\x9d and (3) the nature and magnitude of any countervailing interest in not providing \xe2\x80\x9cadditional or substitute procedural requirement[s].\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The \xe2\x80\x9cprivate interest that will be affected\xe2\x80\x9d argues strongly for the right to counsel here. That interest consists of an indigent defendant\xe2\x80\x99s loss of personal liberty through imprisonment. Freedom \xe2\x80\x9cfrom bodily restraint\xe2\x80\x9d lies \xe2\x80\x9cat the core of the liberty protected by the Due Process Clause.\xe2\x80\x9d Foucha v. Louisiana, 504 U. S. 71, 80. Thus, accurate decisionmaking as to the \xe2\x80\x9cability to pay\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94which marks a dividing line between civil and criminal contempt, Hicks, supra, at 635, n. 7\xe2\x80\x94must be assured because an incorrect decision can result in a wrongful incarceration. And because ability to comply divides civil and criminal contempt proceedings, an erroneous determination would also deprive a defendant of the procedural protections a criminal proceeding would demand. Questions about ability to pay are likely to arise frequently in child custody cases. On the other hand, due process does not always require the provision of counsel in civil proceedings where incarceration is threatened. See Gagnon v. Scarpelli, 411 U. S. 778. To determine whether a right to counsel is required here, opposing interests and the probable value of \xe2\x80\x9cadditional or substitute procedural safeguards\xe2\x80\x9d must be taken into account. Mathews, supra, at 335.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Doing so reveals three related considerations that, taken together, argue strongly against requiring counsel in every proceeding of the present kind. First, the likely critical question in these cases is the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s ability to pay, which is often closely related to his indigence and relatively straightforward. Second, sometimes, as here, the person opposing the defendant at the hearing is not the government represented by counsel but the custodial parent unrepresented by counsel. A requirement that the State provide counsel to the noncustodial parent in these cases could create an asymmetry of representation that would \xe2\x80\x9calter significantly the nature of the proceeding,\xe2\x80\x9d Gagnon, supra, at 787, creating a degree of formality or delay that would unduly slow payment to those immediately in need and make the proceedings less fair overall. Third, as the Federal Government points out, an available set of \xe2\x80\x9csubstitute procedural safeguards,\xe2\x80\x9d Mathews, supra, at 335, if employed together, can significantly reduce the risk of an erroneous deprivation of liberty. These include (1) notice to the defendant that his \xe2\x80\x9cability to pay\xe2\x80\x9d is a critical issue in the contempt proceeding; (2) the use of a form (or the equivalent) to elicit relevant financial information from him; (3) an opportunity at the hearing for him to respond to statements and questions about his financial status; and (4) an express finding by the court that the defendant has the ability to pay.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This decision does not address civil contempt proceedings where the underlying support payment is owed to the State, e.g., for reimbursement of welfare funds paid to the custodial parent, or the question what due process requires in an unusually complex case where a defendant \xe2\x80\x9ccan fairly be represented only by a trained advocate,\xe2\x80\x9d Gagnon, supra, at 788. Pp. 10\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa03.\xc2\xa0Under the circumstances, Turner\xe2\x80\x99s incarceration violated due process because he received neither counsel nor the benefit of alternative procedures like those the Court describes. He did not have clear notice that his ability to pay would constitute the critical question in his civil contempt proceeding. No one provided him with a form (or the equivalent) designed to elicit information about his financial circumstances. And the trial court did not find that he was able to pay his arrearage, but nonetheless found him in civil contempt and ordered him incarcerated. P. 16.

MICHAEL D. TURNER, PETITIONER v. REBECCA L.\nROGERS et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

[June 20, 2011]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Breyer delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0South Carolina\xe2\x80\x99s Family Court enforces its child support orders by threatening with incarceration for civil contempt those who are (1) subject to a child support order, (2) able to comply with that order, but (3) fail to do so. We must decide whether the Fourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Due Process Clause requires the State to provide counsel (at a civil contempt hearing) to an indigent person potentially faced with such incarceration. We conclude that where as here the custodial parent (entitled to receive the support) is unrepresented by counsel, the State need not provide counsel to the noncustodial parent (required to provide the support). But we attach an important caveat, namely, that the State must nonetheless have in place alternative procedures that assure a fundamentally fair determination of the critical incarceration-related question, whether the supporting parent is able to comply with the support order.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0South Carolina family courts enforce their child support orders in part through civil contempt proceedings. Each month the family court clerk reviews outstanding child support orders, identifies those in which the supporting parent has fallen more than five days behind, and sends that parent an order to \xe2\x80\x9cshow cause\xe2\x80\x9d why he should not be held in contempt. S.\xc2\xa0C. Rule Family Ct. 24 (2011). The \xe2\x80\x9cshow cause\xe2\x80\x9d order and attached affidavit refer to the relevant child support order, identify the amount of\nthe arrearage, and set a date for a court hearing. At the hearing that parent may demonstrate that he is not in contempt, say, by showing that he is not able to make the required payments. See Moseley v. Mosier, 279 S.\xc2\xa0C. 348, 351, 306 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 624, 626 (1983) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhen the parent is unable to make the required payments, he is not in contempt\xe2\x80\x9d). If he fails to make the required showing, the court may hold him in civil contempt. And it may require that he be imprisoned unless and until he purges himself of contempt by making the required child support payments (but not for more than one year regardless). See S.\xc2\xa0C. Code Ann. \xc2\xa763\xe2\x80\x933\xe2\x80\x93620 (Supp. 2010) (imprisonment for up to one year of \xe2\x80\x9cadult who wilfully violates\xe2\x80\x9d a court order); Price v. Turner, 387 S.\xc2\xa0C. 142, 145, 691 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 470, 472 (2010) (civil contempt order must permit purging of contempt through compliance).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In June 2003 a South Carolina family court entered an order, which (as amended) required petitioner, Michael Turner, to pay $51.73 per week to respondent, Rebecca Rogers, to help support their child. (Rogers\xe2\x80\x99 father, Larry Price, currently has custody of the child and is also a respondent before this Court.) Over the next three years, Turner repeatedly failed to pay the amount due and was held in contempt on five occasions. The first four times he was sentenced to 90 days\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment, but he ultimately paid the amount due (twice without being jailed, twice after spending two or three days in custody). The fifth time he did not pay but completed a 6-month sentence.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After his release in 2006 Turner remained in arrears. On March 27, 2006, the clerk issued a new \xe2\x80\x9cshow cause\xe2\x80\x9d order. And after an initial postponement due to Turner\xe2\x80\x99s failure to appear, Turner\xe2\x80\x99s civil contempt hearing took place on January 3, 2008. Turner and Rogers were present, each without representation by counsel.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The hearing was brief. The court clerk said that Turner was $5,728.76 behind in his payments. The judge asked Turner if there was \xe2\x80\x9canything you want to say.\xe2\x80\x9d Turner replied,

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cWell, when I first got out, I got back on dope. I done meth, smoked pot and everything else, and I paid a little bit here and there. And, when I finally did get to working, I broke my back, back in September. I filed for disability and SSI. And, I didn\xe2\x80\x99t get straightened out off the dope until I broke my back and laid up for two months. And, now I\xe2\x80\x99m off the dope and everything. I just hope that you give me a chance. I don\xe2\x80\x99t know what else to say. I mean, I know I done wrong, and I should have been paying and helping her, and I\xe2\x80\x99m sorry. I mean, dope had a hold to me.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 17a.

\n

\nThe judge then said, \xe2\x80\x9c[o]kay,\xe2\x80\x9d and asked Rogers if she had anything to say. Ibid. After a brief discussion of federal benefits, the judge stated,

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIf there\xe2\x80\x99s nothing else, this will be the Order of\nthe Court. I find the Defendant in willful contempt. I\xe2\x80\x99m [going to] sentence him to twelve months in the Oconee County Detention Center. He may purge himself of the contempt and avoid the sentence by having a zero balance on or before his release. I\xe2\x80\x99ve also placed a lien on any SSI or other benefits.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 18a.

\n

\nThe judge added that Turner would not receive good-time or work credits, but \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f you\xe2\x80\x99ve got a job, I\xe2\x80\x99ll make you eligible for work release.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. When Turner asked why he could not receive good-time or work credits, the judge said, \xe2\x80\x9c[b]ecause that\xe2\x80\x99s my ruling.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The court made no express finding concerning Turner\xe2\x80\x99s ability to pay his arrearage (though Turner\xe2\x80\x99s wife had voluntarily submitted a copy of Turner\xe2\x80\x99s application for disability benefits, cf. post, at 7, n.\xc2\xa03 (Thomas, J., dissenting); App. 135a\xe2\x80\x93136a). Nor did the judge ask any followup questions or otherwise address the ability-to-pay issue. After the hearing, the judge filled out a prewritten form titled \xe2\x80\x9cOrder for Contempt of Court,\xe2\x80\x9d which included the statement:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cDefendant (was) (was not) gainfully employed and/or (had) (did not have) the ability to make these support payments when due.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 60a, 61a.

\n

\nBut the judge left this statement as is without indicating whether Turner was able to make support payments.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While serving his 12-month sentence, Turner, with the help of pro bono counsel, appealed. He claimed that\nthe Federal Constitution entitled him to counsel at his con- tempt hearing. The South Carolina Supreme Court decided Turner\xe2\x80\x99s appeal after he had completed his sentence. And it rejected his \xe2\x80\x9cright to counsel\xe2\x80\x9d claim. The court pointed out that civil contempt differs significantly from criminal contempt. The former does not require all the \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutional safeguards\xe2\x80\x9d applicable in criminal proceedings. 387 S.\xc2\xa0C., at 145, 691 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 472. And the right to government-paid counsel, the Supreme Court held, was one of the \xe2\x80\x9csafeguards\xe2\x80\x9d not required. Ibid.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Turner sought certiorari. In light of differences among state courts (and some federal courts) on the applicability of a \xe2\x80\x9cright to counsel\xe2\x80\x9d in civil contempt proceedings enforcing child support orders, we granted the writ. Compare, e.g., Pasqua v. Council, 186 N.\xc2\xa0J. 127, 141\xe2\x80\x93146, 892 A.\xc2\xa02d 663, 671\xe2\x80\x93674 (2006); Black v. Division of Child Support Enforcement, 686 A.\xc2\xa02d 164, 167\xe2\x80\x93168 (Del. 1996); Mead v. Batchlor, 435 Mich. 480, 488\xe2\x80\x93505, 460 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 493, 496\xe2\x80\x93504 (1990); Ridgway v. Baker, 720 F.\xc2\xa02d 1409, 1413\xe2\x80\x931415 (CA5 1983) (all finding a federal constitutional right to counsel for indigents facing imprisonment in a child support civil contempt proceeding), with Rodriguez v. Eighth Judicial Dist. Ct., County of Clark, 120 Nev. 798, 808\xe2\x80\x93813, 102 P.\xc2\xa03d 41, 48\xe2\x80\x9351 (2004) (no right to counsel in civil contempt hearing for nonsupport, except in \xe2\x80\x9crarest of cases\xe2\x80\x9d); Andrews v. Walton, 428 So.\xc2\xa02d 663, 666 (Fla. 1983) (\xe2\x80\x9cno circumstances in which a parent is entitled to court-appointed counsel in a civil contempt proceeding for failure to pay child support\xe2\x80\x9d). Compare also In\xc2\xa0re Grand Jury Proceedings, 468 F.\xc2\xa02d 1368, 1369 (CA9 1972) (per curiam) (general right to counsel in civil contempt proceedings), with Duval v. Duval, 114 N.\xc2\xa0H. 422, 425\xe2\x80\x93427, 322 A.\xc2\xa02d 1, 3\xe2\x80\x934 (1974) (no general right, but counsel may be required on case-by-case basis).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents argue that this case is moot. See Massachusetts v. Mellon, 262 U. S. 447, 480 (1923) (Article III judicial power extends only to actual \xe2\x80\x9ccases\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ccontroversies\xe2\x80\x9d); Alvarez v. Smith, 558 U. S. __, __ (2009) (slip op., at 4) (\xe2\x80\x9cAn actual controversy must be extant at all stages of review\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). They point out that Turner completed his 12-month prison sentence in 2009. And they add that there are no \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral consequences\xe2\x80\x9d of that particular contempt determination that might keep the dispute alive. Compare Sibron v. New York, 392 U. S. 40, 55\xe2\x80\x9356 (1968) (release from prison does not moot a criminal case because \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral consequences\xe2\x80\x9d are presumed to continue), with Spencer v. Kemna, 523 U. S. 1, 14 (1998) (declining to extend the presumption to parole revocation).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The short, conclusive answer to respondents\xe2\x80\x99 mootness claim, however, is that this case is not moot because it falls within a special category of disputes that are \xe2\x80\x9ccapable of repetition\xe2\x80\x9d while \xe2\x80\x9cevading review.\xe2\x80\x9d Southern Pacific Terminal Co. v. ICC, 219 U. S. 498, 515 (1911). A dispute falls into that category, and a case based on that dispute remains live, if \xe2\x80\x9c(1) the challenged action [is] in its duration too short to be fully litigated prior to its cessation or expiration, and (2) there [is] a reasonable expectation that the same complaining party [will] be subjected to the same action again.\xe2\x80\x9d Weinstein v. Bradford, 423 U. S. 147, 149 (1975) (per curiam).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our precedent makes clear that the \xe2\x80\x9cchallenged action,\xe2\x80\x9d Turner\xe2\x80\x99s imprisonment for up to 12 months, is \xe2\x80\x9cin its duration too short to be fully litigated\xe2\x80\x9d through the state courts (and arrive here) prior to its \xe2\x80\x9cexpiration.\xe2\x80\x9d See, e.g., First Nat. Bank of Boston v. Bellotti, 435 U. S. 765,\n774 (1978) (internal quotation marks omitted) (18-month period too short); Southern Pacific Terminal Co., supra, at 514\xe2\x80\x93516 (2-year period too short). At the same time, there is a more than \xe2\x80\x9creasonable\xe2\x80\x9d likelihood that Turner will again be \xe2\x80\x9csubjected to the same action.\xe2\x80\x9d As we have pointed out, supra, at 2\xe2\x80\x933, Turner has frequently failed to make his child support payments. He has been the subject of several civil contempt proceedings. He has been imprisoned on several of those occasions. Within months of his release from the imprisonment here at issue he was again the subject of civil contempt proceedings. And he was again imprisoned, this time for six months. As of December 9, 2010, Turner was $13,814.72 in arrears, and another contempt hearing was scheduled for May 4, 2011. App. 104a; Reply Brief for Petitioner 3, n.\xc2\xa01. These facts bring this case squarely within the special category of cases that are not moot because the underlying dispute\nis \xe2\x80\x9ccapable of repetition, yet evading review.\xe2\x80\x9d See, e.g., Nebraska Press Assn. v. Stuart, 427 U. S. 539, 546\xe2\x80\x93547 (1976) (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, the underlying facts make this case unlike DeFunis v. Odegaard, 416 U. S. 312 (1974) (per curiam), and St. Pierre v. United States, 319 U. S. 41 (1943) (per curiam), two cases that respondents believe require us to find this case moot regardless. DeFunis was moot, but that is because the plaintiff himself was unlikely to again suffer the conduct of which he complained (and others likely to suffer from that conduct could bring their own lawsuits). Here petitioner himself is likely to suffer future imprisonment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0St. Pierre was moot because the petitioner (a witness held in contempt and sentenced to five months\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment) had failed to \xe2\x80\x9capply to this Court for a stay\xe2\x80\x9d of the federal-court order imposing imprisonment. 319 U. S., at 42\xe2\x80\x9343. And, like the witness in St. Pierre, Turner did not seek a stay of the contempt order requiring his imprisonment. But this case, unlike St. Pierre, arises out of a state-court proceeding. And respondents give us no reason to believe that we would have (or that we could have) granted a timely request for a stay had one been made.\nCf. 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71257 (granting this Court jurisdiction to review final state-court judgments). In Sibron, we rejected a similar \xe2\x80\x9cmootness\xe2\x80\x9d argument for just that reason. 392 U. S., at 53, n.\xc2\xa013. And we find this case similar in this respect to Sibron, not to St. Pierre.

\n

III

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We must decide whether the Due Process Clause grants an indigent defendant, such as Turner, a right to state-appointed counsel at a civil contempt proceeding, which may lead to his incarceration. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents provide no definitive answer to that question. This Court has long held that the Sixth Amendment grants an indigent defendant the right to state-appointed counsel in\na criminal case. Gideon v. Wainwright, 372 U. S. 335 (1963). And we have held that this same rule applies to criminal contempt proceedings (other than summary proceedings). United States v. Dixon, 509 U. S. 688, 696 (1993); Cooke v. United States, 267 U. S. 517, 537 (1925).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But the Sixth Amendment does not govern civil cases. Civil contempt differs from criminal contempt in that it seeks only to \xe2\x80\x9ccoerc[e] the defendant to do\xe2\x80\x9d what a court had previously ordered him to do. Gompers v. Bucks Stove & Range Co., 221 U. S. 418, 442 (1911). A court may not impose punishment \xe2\x80\x9cin a civil contempt proceeding when it is clearly established that the alleged contemnor is unable to comply with the terms of the order.\xe2\x80\x9d Hicks v. Feiock, 485 U. S. 624, 638, n.\xc2\xa09 (1988). And once a civil contemnor complies with the underlying order, he is purged of the contempt and is free. Id., at 633 (he \xe2\x80\x9ccarr[ies] the keys of [his] prison in [his] own pockets\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Consequently, the Court has made clear (in a case not involving the right to counsel) that, where civil contempt is at issue, the Fourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Due Process Clause allows a State to provide fewer procedural protections than in a criminal case. Id., at 637\xe2\x80\x93641 (State may place the burden of proving inability to pay on the defendant).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court has decided only a handful of cases that more directly concern a right to counsel in civil matters. And the application of those decisions to the present case is not clear. On the one hand, the Court has held that the Fourteenth Amendment requires the State to pay for representation by counsel in a civil \xe2\x80\x9cjuvenile delinquency\xe2\x80\x9d proceeding (which could lead to incarceration). In\xc2\xa0re Gault, 387 U. S. 1, 35\xe2\x80\x9342 (1967). Moreover, in Vitek v. Jones, 445 U. S. 480, 496\xe2\x80\x93497 (1980), a plurality of four Members of this Court would have held that the Fourteenth Amendment requires representation by counsel in a proceeding to transfer a prison inmate to a state hospital for the mentally ill. Further, in Lassiter v. Department of Social Servs. of Durham Cty., 452 U. S. 18 (1981), a case that focused upon civil proceedings leading to loss of parental rights, the Court wrote that the

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cpre-eminent generalization that emerges from this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents on an indigent\xe2\x80\x99s right to appointed counsel is that such a right has been recognized to exist only where the litigant may lose his physical liberty if he loses the litigation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 25.

\n

\nAnd the Court then drew from these precedents \xe2\x80\x9cthe presumption that an indigent litigant has a right to appointed counsel only when, if he loses, he may be deprived of his physical liberty.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 26\xe2\x80\x9327.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On the other hand, the Court has held that a criminal offender facing revocation of probation and imprisonment does not ordinarily have a right to counsel at a probation revocation hearing. Gagnon v. Scarpelli, 411 U. S. 778 (1973); see also Middendorf v. Henry, 425 U. S. 25 (1976) (no due process right to counsel in summary court-martial proceedings). And, at the same time, Gault, Vitek, and Lassiter are readily distinguishable. The civil juvenile delinquency proceeding at issue in Gault was \xe2\x80\x9clittle different\xe2\x80\x9d from, and \xe2\x80\x9ccomparable in seriousness\xe2\x80\x9d to, a criminal prosecution. 387 U. S., at 28, 36. In Vitek, the controlling opinion found no right to counsel. 445 U. S., at 499\xe2\x80\x93500 (Powell, J., concurring in part) (assistance of mental health professionals sufficient). And the Court\xe2\x80\x99s statements in Lassiter constitute part of its rationale for denying a right to counsel in that case. We believe those statements are best read as pointing out that the Court previously had found a right to counsel \xe2\x80\x9conly\xe2\x80\x9d in cases involving incarceration, not that a right to counsel exists in all such cases (a position that would have been difficult to reconcile with Gagnon).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Civil contempt proceedings in child support cases con-\nstitute one part of a highly complex system designed to assure a noncustodial parent\xe2\x80\x99s regular payment of funds typically necessary for the support of his children. Often the family receives welfare support from a state-administered federal program, and the State then seeks reimbursement from the noncustodial parent. See 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7608(a)(3) (2006 ed., Supp. III), 656(a)(1) (2006 ed.); S.\xc2\xa0C. Code Ann. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa743\xe2\x80\x935\xe2\x80\x9365(a)(1), (2) (2010 Cum. Supp.). Other times the custodial parent (often the mother, but sometimes the father, a grandparent, or another person with custody) does not receive government benefits and is entitled to receive the support payments herself.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Federal Government has created an elaborate procedural mechanism designed to help both the government and custodial parents to secure the payments to which they are entitled. See generally Blessing v. Freestone, 520 U. S. 329, 333 (1997) (describing the \xe2\x80\x9cinterlocking set of cooperative federal-state welfare programs\xe2\x80\x9d as they relate to child support enforcement); 45 CFR pt. 303 (2010) (prescribing standards for state child support agencies). These systems often rely upon wage withholding, expedited procedures for modifying and enforcing child support orders, and automated data processing. 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7666(a), (b), 654(24). But sometimes States will use contempt orders to ensure that the custodial parent receives support payments or the government receives reimbursement. Although some experts have criticized this last-mentioned procedure, and the Federal Government believes that \xe2\x80\x9cthe routine use of contempt for non-payment of child support is likely to be an ineffective strategy,\xe2\x80\x9d the Government also tells us that \xe2\x80\x9ccoercive enforcement remedies, such as contempt, have a role to play.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 21\xe2\x80\x9322, and n.\xc2\xa08 (citing Dept. of Health and Human Services, National Child Support Enforcement, Strategic Plan: FY 2005\xe2\x80\x932009, pp. 2, 10). South Carolina, which relies heavily on contempt proceedings, agrees that they are an important tool.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We here consider an indigent\xe2\x80\x99s right to paid counsel at such a contempt proceeding. It is a civil proceeding. And we consequently determine the \xe2\x80\x9cspecific dictates of due process\xe2\x80\x9d by examining the \xe2\x80\x9cdistinct factors\xe2\x80\x9d that this Court has previously found useful in deciding what specific safeguards the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s Due Process Clause re-quires in order to make a civil proceeding fundamentally fair. Mathews v. Eldridge, 424 U. S. 319, 335 (1976) (considering fairness of an administrative proceeding). As relevant here those factors include (1) the nature of \xe2\x80\x9cthe private interest that will be affected,\xe2\x80\x9d (2) the comparative \xe2\x80\x9crisk\xe2\x80\x9d of an \xe2\x80\x9cerroneous deprivation\xe2\x80\x9d of that interest with and without \xe2\x80\x9cadditional or substitute procedural safeguards,\xe2\x80\x9d and (3) the nature and magnitude of any countervailing interest in not providing \xe2\x80\x9cadditional or substitute procedural requirement[s].\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. See also Lassiter, 452 U. S., at 27\xe2\x80\x9331 (applying the Mathews framework).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The \xe2\x80\x9cprivate interest that will be affected\xe2\x80\x9d argues strongly for the right to counsel that Turner advocates. That interest consists of an indigent defendant\xe2\x80\x99s loss of personal liberty through imprisonment. The interest in securing that freedom, the freedom \xe2\x80\x9cfrom bodily restraint,\xe2\x80\x9d lies \xe2\x80\x9cat the core of the liberty protected by the Due Process Clause.\xe2\x80\x9d Foucha v. Louisiana, 504 U. S. 71, 80 (1992). And we have made clear that its threatened loss through legal proceedings demands \xe2\x80\x9cdue process protection.\xe2\x80\x9d Addington v. Texas, 441 U. S. 418, 425 (1979).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Given the importance of the interest at stake, it is ob-viously important to assure accurate decisionmaking in respect to the key \xe2\x80\x9cability to pay\xe2\x80\x9d question. Moreover, the fact that ability to comply marks a dividing line between civil and criminal contempt, Hicks, 485 U. S., at 635, n. 7, reinforces the need for accuracy. That is because an incorrect decision (wrongly classifying the contempt proceeding as civil) can increase the risk of wrongful incarceration by depriving the defendant of the procedural protections (including counsel) that the Constitution would demand in a criminal proceeding. See, e.g., Dixon, 509 U. S., at 696 (proof beyond a reasonable doubt, protection from double jeopardy); Codispoti v. Pennsylvania, 418 U. S. 506, 512\xe2\x80\x93513, 517 (1974) (jury trial where the result is more than six months\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment). And since 70% of child support arrears nationwide are owed by parents with either no reported income or income of $10,000 per year or less, the issue of ability to pay may arise fairly often. See\nE. Sorensen, L. Sousa, & S. Schaner, Assessing Child Support Arrears in Nine Large States and the Nation\n22 (2007) (prepared by The Urban Institute), online at http://aspe.hhs.gov/hsp/07/assessing-CS-debt/report.pdf (as visited June 16, 2011, and available in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file); id., at 23 (\xe2\x80\x9cresearch suggests that many obligors who do not have reported quarterly wages have relatively limited resources\xe2\x80\x9d); Patterson, Civil Contempt and the Indigent Child Support Obligor: The Silent Return of Debtor\xe2\x80\x99s Prison, 18 Cornell J.\xc2\xa0L. & Pub. Pol\xe2\x80\x99y 95, 117 (2008). See also, e.g., McBride v. McBride, 334 N.\xc2\xa0C. 124, 131, n.\xc2\xa04, 431 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 14, 19, n. 4 (1993) (surveying North Carolina contempt orders and finding that the \xe2\x80\x9cfailure of trial courts to make a determination of a contemnor\xe2\x80\x99s ability to comply is not altogether infrequent\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On the other hand, the Due Process Clause does not always require the provision of counsel in civil proceedings where incarceration is threatened. See Gagnon, 411 U. S. 778. And in determining whether the Clause requires a right to counsel here, we must take account of opposing interests, as well as consider the probable value of \xe2\x80\x9cadditional or substitute procedural safeguards.\xe2\x80\x9d Mathews, supra, at 335.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Doing so, we find three related considerations that, when taken together, argue strongly against the Due Process Clause requiring the State to provide indigents with counsel in every proceeding of the kind before us.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, the critical question likely at issue in these cases concerns, as we have said, the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s ability to pay. That question is often closely related to the question of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s indigence. But when the right procedures are in place, indigence can be a question that in many\xe2\x80\x94but not all\xe2\x80\x94cases is sufficiently straightforward to warrant determination prior to providing a defendant with counsel, even in a criminal case. Federal law, for example, requires a criminal defendant to provide information showing that he is indigent, and therefore entitled to state-funded counsel, before he can receive that assistance. See 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73006A(b).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, sometimes, as here, the person opposing the defendant at the hearing is not the government represented by counsel but the custodial parent unrepresented by counsel. See Dept. of Health and Human Services, Office of Child Support Enforcement, Understanding Child Support Debt: A Guide to Exploring Child Support Debt in Your State 5, 6 (2004) (51% of nationwide arrears, and 58% in South Carolina, are not owed to the government). The custodial parent, perhaps a woman with custody of one or more children, may be relatively poor, unemployed, and unable to afford counsel. Yet she may have encouraged the court to enforce its order through contempt. Cf. Tr. Contempt Proceedings (Sept. 14, 2005), App. 44a\xe2\x80\x9345a (Rogers asks court, in light of pattern of nonpayment, to confine Turner). She may be able to provide the court with significant information. Cf. id., at 41a\xe2\x80\x9343a (Rogers describes where Turner lived and worked). And the proceeding is ultimately for her benefit.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A requirement that the State provide counsel to the noncustodial parent in these cases could create an asymmetry of representation that would \xe2\x80\x9calter significantly the nature of the proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d Gagnon, supra, at 787. Doing so could mean a degree of formality or delay that would unduly slow payment to those immediately in need. And, perhaps more important for present purposes, doing so could make the proceedings less fair overall, increasing the risk of a decision that would erroneously deprive a family of the support it is entitled to receive. The needs of such families play an important role in our analysis. Cf. post, at 10\xe2\x80\x9312 (opinion of Thomas, J.).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Third, as the Solicitor General points out, there is available a set of \xe2\x80\x9csubstitute procedural safeguards,\xe2\x80\x9d Mathews, 424 U. S., at 335, which, if employed together, can significantly reduce the risk of an erroneous deprivation of liberty. They can do so, moreover, without incurring some of the drawbacks inherent in recognizing an automatic right to counsel. Those safeguards include (1) notice to the de-fendant that his \xe2\x80\x9cability to pay\xe2\x80\x9d is a critical issue in the contempt proceeding; (2) the use of a form (or the equivalent) to elicit relevant financial information; (3) an opportunity at the hearing for the defendant to respond to statements and questions about his financial status, (e.g., those triggered by his responses on the form); and (4) an express finding by the court that the defendant has the ability to pay. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 26\xe2\x80\x9327; Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 23\xe2\x80\x9325. In presenting these alternatives, the Government draws upon considerable experience in helping to manage statutorily mandated federal-state efforts to enforce child support orders. See supra, at 10. It does not claim that they are the only possible alternatives, and this Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases suggest, for example, that sometimes assistance other than purely legal assistance (here, say, that of a neutral social worker) can prove constitutionally sufficient. Cf. Vitek, 445 U. S., at 499\xe2\x80\x93500 (Powell, J., concurring in part) (provision of mental health professional). But the Government does claim that these alternatives can assure the \xe2\x80\x9cfundamental fairness\xe2\x80\x9d of the proceeding even where the State does not pay for counsel for an indigent defendant.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While recognizing the strength of Turner\xe2\x80\x99s arguments, we ultimately believe that the three considerations we have just discussed must carry the day. In our view, a categorical right to counsel in proceedings of the kind before us would carry with it disadvantages (in the form of unfairness and delay) that, in terms of ultimate fairness, would deprive it of significant superiority over the alternatives that we have mentioned. We consequently hold that the Due Process Clause does not automatically require the provision of counsel at civil contempt proceedings to an indigent individual who is subject to a child support order, even if that individual faces incarceration (for up to a year). In particular, that Clause does not require the provision of counsel where the opposing parent or other custodian (to whom support funds are owed) is not represented by counsel and the State provides alternative procedural safeguards equivalent to those we have mentioned (adequate notice of the importance of ability to pay, fair opportunity to present, and to dispute, relevant information, and court findings).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We do not address civil contempt proceedings where the underlying child support payment is owed to the State, for example, for reimbursement of welfare funds paid to the parent with custody. See supra, at 10. Those proceedings more closely resemble debt-collection proceedings. The government is likely to have counsel or some other competent representative. Cf. Johnson v. Zerbst, 304 U. S. 458, 462\xe2\x80\x93463 (1938) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he average defendant does not have the professional legal skill to protect himself when brought before a tribunal with power to take his life or liberty, wherein the prosecution is presented by experienced and learned counsel\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). And this kind of proceeding is not before us. Neither do we address what due process requires in an unusually complex case where a defendant \xe2\x80\x9ccan fairly be represented only by a trained advocate.\xe2\x80\x9d Gagnon, 411 U. S., at 788; see also Reply Brief for Petitioner 18\xe2\x80\x9320 (not claiming that Turner\xe2\x80\x99s case is especially complex).

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The record indicates that Turner received neither counsel nor the benefit of alternative procedures like those we have described. He did not receive clear notice that his ability to pay would constitute the critical question in his civil contempt proceeding. No one provided him with a form (or the equivalent) designed to elicit information about his financial circumstances. The court did not find that Turner was able to pay his arrearage, but instead left the relevant \xe2\x80\x9cfinding\xe2\x80\x9d section of the contempt order blank. The court nonetheless found Turner in contempt and ordered him incarcerated. Under these circumstances Turner\xe2\x80\x99s incarceration violated the Due Process Clause.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We vacate the judgment of the South Carolina Supreme Court and remand the case for further proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment does not provide a right to appointed counsel for indi-\ngent defendants facing incarceration in civil contempt pro- ceedings. Therefore, I would affirm. Although the Court agrees that appointed counsel was not required in this case, it nevertheless vacates the judgment of the South Carolina Supreme Court on a different ground, which the parties have never raised. Solely at the invitation of\nthe United States as amicus curiae, the majority decides that Turner\xe2\x80\x99s contempt proceeding violated due process be- cause it did not include \xe2\x80\x9calternative procedural safeguards.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 15. Consistent with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s longstanding practice, I would not reach that question.[Footnote 1]

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The only question raised in this case is whether the\nDue Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment creates a right to appointed counsel for all indigent defendants facing incarceration in civil contempt proceedings. It does not.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under an original understanding of the Constitution, there is no basis for concluding that the guarantee of due process secures a right to appointed counsel in civil contempt proceedings. It certainly does not do so to the extent that the Due Process Clause requires \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98that our\nGovernment must proceed according to the \xe2\x80\x9claw of the land\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94that is, according to written constitutional and statutory provisions.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Hamdi v. Rumsfeld, 542 U. S. 507, 589 (2004) (Thomas, J., dissenting) (quoting In\xc2\xa0re Winship, 397 U. S. 358, 382 (1970) (Black, J., dissenting)). No one contends that South Carolina law entitles Turner to appointed counsel. Nor does any federal statute or constitutional provision so provide. Although the Sixth Amendment secures a right to \xe2\x80\x9cthe Assistance of Counsel,\xe2\x80\x9d it does\nnot apply here because civil contempt proceedings are not \xe2\x80\x9ccriminal prosecutions.\xe2\x80\x9d U. S. Const., Amdt. 6; see ante, at 8. Moreover, as originally understood, the Sixth Amendment guaranteed only the \xe2\x80\x9cright to employ counsel, or to use volunteered services of counsel\xe2\x80\x9d; it did not require the court to appoint counsel in any circumstance. Padilla v. Kentucky, 559 U. S. ___, ___ (2010) (Scalia, J., dissenting) (slip op., at 2); see also United States v. Van Duzee, 140 U. S. 169, 173 (1891); W. Beaney, The Right to Counsel in American Courts 21\xe2\x80\x9322, 28\xe2\x80\x9329 (1955); F. Heller, The Sixth Amendment to the Constitution of the United States 110 (1951).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Appointed counsel is also not required in civil contempt proceedings under a somewhat broader reading of the Due Process Clause, which takes it to approve \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[a] process of law, which is not otherwise forbidden, \xe2\x80\xa6 [that] can show the sanction of settled usage.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Weiss v. United States, 510 U. S. 163, 197 (1994) (Scalia, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment) (quoting Hurtado v. California, 110 U. S. 516, 528 (1884)). Despite a long history of courts exercising contempt authority, Turner has not identified any evidence that courts appointed counsel in those proceedings. See Mine Workers v. Bagwell, 512 U. S. 821, 831 (1994) (describing courts\xe2\x80\x99 traditional assumption of \xe2\x80\x9cinherent contempt authority\xe2\x80\x9d); see also 4 W. Blackstone, Commentaries on the Laws of England 280\xe2\x80\x93285 (1769) (describing the \xe2\x80\x9csummary proceedings\xe2\x80\x9d used to adjudicate contempt). Indeed, Turner concedes that contempt proceedings without appointed counsel have the blessing of history. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 15\xe2\x80\x9316 (admitting that there is no historical support for Turner\xe2\x80\x99s rule); see also Brief for Respondents 47\xe2\x80\x9348.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s modern interpretation of the Constitution, the Due Process Clause does not provide a right to appointed counsel for all indigent defendants facing incarceration in civil contempt proceedings. Such\na reading would render the Sixth Amendment right to counsel\xe2\x80\x94as it is currently understood\xe2\x80\x94superfluous. Moreover, it appears that even cases applying the Court\xe2\x80\x99s modern interpretation of due process have not understood it to categorically require appointed counsel in circumstances outside those otherwise covered by the Sixth Amendment.

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s current jurisprudence, the Sixth Amendment entitles indigent defendants to appointed coun- sel in felony cases and other criminal cases resulting\nin a sentence of imprisonment. See Gideon v. Wainwright, 372 U. S. 335, 344\xe2\x80\x93345 (1963); Argersinger v. Hamlin, 407 U. S. 25, 37 (1972); Scott v. Illinois, 440 U. S. 367, 373\xe2\x80\x93374 (1979); Alabama v. Shelton, 535 U. S. 654, 662 (2002). Turner concedes that, even under these cases, the Sixth Amendment does not entitle him to appointed counsel. See Reply Brief for Petitioner 12 (acknowledging that \xe2\x80\x9ccivil contempt is not a \xe2\x80\x98criminal prosecution\xe2\x80\x99 within the meaning of the Sixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x9d). He argues instead that \xe2\x80\x9cthe right to the assistance of counsel for persons facing incarceration arises not only from the Sixth Amendment, but also from the requirement of fundamental fairness under the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 28. In his view, this Court has relied on due process to \xe2\x80\x9crejec[t] formalistic distinctions between criminal and civil proceedings, instead concluding that incarceration or other confinement triggers the right to counsel.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 33.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But if the Due Process Clause created a right to appointed counsel in all proceedings with the potential for detention, then the Sixth Amendment right to appointed counsel would be unnecessary. Under Turner\xe2\x80\x99s theory, every instance in which the Sixth Amendment guarantees a right to appointed counsel is covered also by the Due Process Clause. The Sixth Amendment, however, is the only constitutional provision that even mentions the assistance of counsel; the Due Process Clause says nothing about counsel. Ordinarily, we do not read a general provision to render a specific one superfluous. Cf. Morales v. Trans World Airlines, Inc., 504 U. S. 374, 384 (1992)\n(\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is a commonplace of statutory construction that the specific governs the general\xe2\x80\x9d). The fact that one constitutional provision expressly provides a right to appointed counsel in specific circumstances indicates that the Constitution does not also sub silentio provide that right far more broadly in another, more general, provision. Cf. Albright v. Oliver, 510 U. S. 266, 273 (1994) (plurality opinion) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhere a particular Amendment provides an explicit textual source of constitutional protection against a particular sort of government behavior, that Amendment, not the more generalized notion of \xe2\x80\x98substantive due process,\xe2\x80\x99 must be the guide for analyzing these claims\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)); id., at 281 (Kennedy, J., concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9cI agree with the plurality that an allegation of arrest without probable cause must be analyzed under the Fourth Amendment without reference to more general considerations of due process\xe2\x80\x9d); Stop the Beach Renourishment, Inc. v. Florida Dept. of Environmental Protection, 560 U. S. ___, ___ (2010) (opinion of Scalia, J.) (slip op., at 16) (applying Albright to the Takings Clause).

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, contrary to Turner\xe2\x80\x99s assertions, the holdings in this Court\xe2\x80\x99s due process decisions regarding the right to counsel are actually quite narrow. The Court has never found in the Due Process Clause a categorical right to appointed counsel outside of criminal prosecutions or proceedings \xe2\x80\x9cfunctionally akin to a criminal trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Gag-non v. Scarpelli, 411 U. S. 778, 789, n. 12 (1973) (dis-cussing In\xc2\xa0re Gault, 387 U. S. 1 (1967)). This is consistent with the conclusion that the Due Process Clause does not expand the right to counsel beyond the boundaries set by the Sixth Amendment.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After countless factors weighed, mores evaluated, and practices surveyed, the Court has not determined that due process principles of fundamental fairness categorically require counsel in any context outside criminal proceedings. See, e.g., Lassiter v. Department of Social Servs. of Durham Cty., 452 U. S. 18, 31\xe2\x80\x9332 (1981); Wolff v. McDonnell, 418 U. S. 539, 569\xe2\x80\x93570 (1974); see also Walters v. National Assn. of Radiation Survivors, 473 U. S. 305, 307\xe2\x80\x93308, 320\xe2\x80\x93326 (1985); Goss v. Lopez, 419 U. S. 565, 583 (1975). Even when the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s liberty is at stake, the Court has not concluded that fundamental fairness requires that counsel always be appointed if the proceeding is not criminal.[Footnote 2] See, e.g., Scarpelli, supra, at 790 (probation revocation); Middendorf v. Henry, 425 U. S. 25, 48 (1976) (summary court-martial); Parham v. J.\xc2\xa0R., 442 U. S. 584, 599\xe2\x80\x93600, 606\xe2\x80\x93607, 610, n. 18 (1979) (commitment of minor to mental hospital); Vitek v. Jones, 445 U. S. 480, 497\xe2\x80\x93500 (1980) (Powell, J., controlling opinion concurring in part) (transfer of prisoner to mental hospital). Indeed, the only circumstance in which the Court has found that due process categorically requires appointed counsel is juvenile delinquency proceedings, which the Court has described as \xe2\x80\x9cfunctionally akin to a criminal trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Scarpelli, supra, at 789, n. 12 (discussing In\xc2\xa0re Gault, supra); see ante, at 9.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Despite language in its opinions that suggests it could find otherwise, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s consistent judgment has been that fundamental fairness does not categorically require appointed counsel in any context outside of criminal\nproceedings. The majority is correct, therefore, that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent does not require appointed counsel in the absence of a deprivation of liberty. Id., at 9\xe2\x80\x9310. But a more complete description of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases is that even when liberty is at stake, the Court has required appointed counsel in a category of cases only where it would have found the Sixth Amendment required it\xe2\x80\x94in criminal prosecutions.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority agrees that the Constitution does not entitle Turner to appointed counsel. But at the invitation of the Federal Government as amicus curiae, the majority holds that his contempt hearing violated the Due Process Clause for an entirely different reason, which the parties have never raised: The family court\xe2\x80\x99s procedures \xe2\x80\x9cwere\nin adequate to ensure an accurate determination of [Turner\xe2\x80\x99s] present ability to pay.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 19 (capitalization and boldface type deleted); see ante, at 14\xe2\x80\x9316. I would not reach this issue.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There are good reasons not to consider new issues raised for the first and only time in an amicus brief. As here,\nthe new issue may be outside the question presented.[Footnote 3] See Pet. for Cert. i (\xe2\x80\x9cWhether \xe2\x80\xa6 an indigent defendant has no constitutional right to appointed counsel at a civil contempt proceeding that results in his incarceration\xe2\x80\x9d); see also ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x935 (identifying the conflict among lower courts as regarding \xe2\x80\x9cthe right to counsel\xe2\x80\x9d). As here, the new issue may not have been addressed by, or even presented to, the state court. See 387 S.\xc2\xa0C. 142, 144, 691 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 470, 472 (2010) (describing the only question as whether \xe2\x80\x9cthe Sixth and Fourteenth Amendments of the United States Constitution guarantee [Turner], as an indigent defendant in family court, the right to appointed counsel\xe2\x80\x9d). As here, the parties may not have preserved the issue, leaving the record undeveloped. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 49, 43 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe record is insufficient\xe2\x80\x9d regarding alternative procedures because \xe2\x80\x9c[t]hey were raised for the very first\ntime at the merits stage here; so, there\xe2\x80\x99s been no development\xe2\x80\x9d); Brief for Respondents 63. As here, the parties may not address the new issue in this Court, leaving its boundaries untested. See Brief for Petitioner 27, n.\xc2\xa015 (reiterating that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he particular constitutional violation that Turner challenges in this case is the failure of the family court to appoint counsel\xe2\x80\x9d); Brief for Respondents 62 (declining to address the Government\xe2\x80\x99s argument because it is not \xe2\x80\x9cproperly before this Court\xe2\x80\x9d (capitalization and boldface type deleted). Finally, as here, a party may even oppose the position taken by its allegedly supportive amicus. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 7\xe2\x80\x9312, 14\xe2\x80\x9315 (Turner\xe2\x80\x99s counsel rejecting the Government\xe2\x80\x99s argument that any procedures short of a categorical right to appointed counsel could satisfy due process); Reply Brief for Petitioner 14\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, it is the wise and settled general practice of this Court not to consider an issue in the first instance, much less one raised only by an amicus. See this Court\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 14.1(a) (\xe2\x80\x9cOnly the questions set out in the petition, or fairly included therein, will be considered by the Court\xe2\x80\x9d); Adarand Constructors, Inc. v. Mineta, 534 U. S. 103, 110 (2001) (per curiam) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]his is a court of final review and not first view\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)); United Parcel Service, Inc. v. Mitchell, 451 U. S. 56, 60, n.\xc2\xa02 (1981) (declining to consider an amicus\xe2\x80\x99 argument \xe2\x80\x9csince it was not raised by either of the parties here or below\xe2\x80\x9d and was outside the grant of certiorari). This is doubly true when we review the decision of a state court and triply so when the new issue is a constitutional matter. See McGoldrick v. Compagnie Generale Transatlantique, 309 U. S. 430, 434 (1940) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is only in exceptional cases, and then only in cases coming from the federal courts, that [this Court] considers questions urged by a petitioner or appellant not pressed or passed upon in the courts below\xe2\x80\x9d); Cardinale v. Louisiana, 394 U. S. 437, 438 (1969) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he Court will not decide federal constitutional issues raised here for the first time on review of state court decisions\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority errs in moving beyond the question that was litigated below, decided by the state courts, petitioned to this Court, and argued by the parties here, to resolve a question raised exclusively in the Federal Government\xe2\x80\x99s amicus brief. In some cases, the Court properly affirms a lower court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment on an alternative ground or accepts the persuasive argument of an amicus on a question that the parties have raised. See, e.g., United States v. Tinklenberg, 563 U. S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 13). But it transforms a case entirely to vacate a state court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment based on an alternative constitutional ground advanced only by an amicus and outside the question on which the petitioner sought (and this Court granted) review.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It should come as no surprise that the majority confines its analysis of the Federal Government\xe2\x80\x99s new issue to ac- knowledging the Government\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cconsiderable experience\xe2\x80\x9d in the field of child support enforcement and then adopting the Government\xe2\x80\x99s suggestions in toto. See ante,\nat 14\xe2\x80\x9315. Perhaps if the issue had been preserved and briefed by the parties, the majority would have had alternative solutions or procedures to consider. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 43 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]here\xe2\x80\x99s been no development. We don\xe2\x80\x99t know what other States are doing, the range of options out there\xe2\x80\x9d). The Federal Government\xe2\x80\x99s interest in States\xe2\x80\x99 child support enforcement efforts may give the Government a valuable perspective,[Footnote 4] but it does not overcome the strong reasons behind the Court\xe2\x80\x99s practice of not considering new issues, raised and addressed only by an amicus, for the first time in this Court.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For the reasons explained in the previous two sections,\nI would not engage in the majority\xe2\x80\x99s balancing analysis. But there is yet another reason not to undertake the Mathews v. Eldridge balancing test here. 424 U. S. 319 (1976). That test weighs an individual\xe2\x80\x99s interest against that of the Government. Id., at 335 (identifying the opposing interest as \xe2\x80\x9cthe Government\xe2\x80\x99s interest\xe2\x80\x9d); Lassiter, 452 U. S., at 27 (same). It does not account for the interests of the child and custodial parent, who is usually the child\xe2\x80\x99s mother. But their interests are the very reason for the child support obligation and the civil contempt proceedings that enforce it.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When fathers fail in their duty to pay child support, children suffer. See Cancian, Meyer, & Han, Child Support: Responsible Fatherhood and the Quid Pro Quo, 635 Annals Am. Acad. Pol. & Soc. Sci. 140, 153 (2011) (finding that child support plays an important role in reducing child poverty in single-parent homes); cf. Sorensen & Zibman, Getting to Know Poor Fathers Who Do Not Pay Child Support, 75 Soc. Serv. Rev. 420, 423 (2001) (finding that children whose fathers reside apart from them are 54 percent more likely to live in poverty than their fathers). Nonpayment or inadequate payment can press children and mothers into poverty. M. Garrison, The Goals and Limits of Child Support Policy, in Child Support: The Next Frontier 16 (J. Oldham & M. Melli eds. 2000); see also Dept. of Commerce, Census Bureau, T. Grall, Custodial Mothers and Fathers and Their Child Support: 2007, pp. 4\xe2\x80\x935 (2009) (hereinafter Custodial Mothers and Fathers) (reporting that 27 percent of custodial mothers lived in poverty in 2007).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The interests of children and mothers who depend on child support are notoriously difficult to protect. See, e.g., Hicks v. Feiock, 485 U. S. 624, 644 (1988) (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe failure of enforcement efforts in this area has become a national scandal\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). Less than half of all custodial parents receive the full amount of child support ordered; 24 percent\nof those owed support receive nothing at all. Custodial Mothers and Fathers 7; see also Dept. of Health and\nHuman Services, Office of Child Support Enforcement, FY 2008 Annual Report to Congress, App. III, Table 71 (showing national child support arrears of $105.5 billion in 2008). In South Carolina alone, more than 139,000 noncustodial parents defaulted on their child support obligations during 2008, and at year end parents owed $1.17 billion in total arrears. Id., App. III, Tables 73 and 71.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That some fathers subject to a child support agreement report little or no income \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not mean they do not have the ability to pay any child support.\xe2\x80\x9d Dept. of Health and Human Services, H. Sorensen, L. Sousa, & S. Schaner, Assessing Child Support Arrears in Nine Large States and\nthe Nation 22 (2007) (prepared by The Urban Institute) (hereinafter Assessing Arrears). Rather, many \xe2\x80\x9cdeadbeat dads\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 5] \xe2\x80\x9copt to work in the underground economy\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9cshield their earnings from child support enforcement efforts.\xe2\x80\x9d Mich. Sup.\xc2\xa0Ct., Task Force Report: The Underground Economy 10 (2010) (hereinafter Underground Economy). To avoid attempts to garnish their wages or otherwise enforce the support obligation, \xe2\x80\x9cdeadbeats\xe2\x80\x9d quit their jobs, jump from job to job, become self-employed, work under the table, or engage in illegal activity.[Footnote 6] See Waller & Plotnick, Effective Child Support Policy for Low-Income Families: Evidence from Street Level Research, 20 J. Pol\xe2\x80\x99y Analysis & Mgmt. 89, 104 (2001); Assessing Arrears 22\xe2\x80\x9323.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because of the difficulties in collecting payment through traditional enforcement mechanisms, many States also use civil contempt proceedings to coerce \xe2\x80\x9cdeadbeats\xe2\x80\x9d into paying what they owe. The States that use civil contempt with the threat of detention find it a \xe2\x80\x9chighly effective\xe2\x80\x9d tool for collecting child support when nothing else works. Compendium of Responses Collected by the U. S. Dept. of Health and Human Services Office of Child Support Enforcement (Dec. 28, 2010), reprinted in App. to Brief\nfor Sen. DeMint et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 7a; see id., at 3a, 9a. For example, Virginia, which uses civil contempt as\n\xe2\x80\x9ca last resort,\xe2\x80\x9d reports that in 2010 \xe2\x80\x9cdeadbeats\xe2\x80\x9d paid approximately $13 million \xe2\x80\x9ceither before a court hearing\nto avoid a contempt finding or after a court hearing to purge the contempt finding.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 13a\xe2\x80\x9314a. Other States confirm that the mere threat of imprisonment is often quite effective because most contemners \xe2\x80\x9cwill pay \xe2\x80\xa6 rather than go to jail.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 4a; see also Underground Economy C\xe2\x80\x932 (\xe2\x80\x9cMany judges \xe2\x80\xa6 report that the prospect of [detention] often causes obligors to discover previously undisclosed resources that they can use to make child support payments\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case illustrates the point. After the family court imposed Turner\xe2\x80\x99s weekly support obligation in June 2003, he made no payments until the court held him in contempt three months later, whereupon he paid over $1,000 to avoid confinement. App. 17a\xe2\x80\x9318a, 131a. Three more times, Turner refused to pay until the family court held him in contempt\xe2\x80\x94then paid in short order. Id., at 23a\xe2\x80\x9325a, 31a\xe2\x80\x9334a, 125a\xe2\x80\x93126a, 129a\xe2\x80\x93130a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although I think that the majority\xe2\x80\x99s analytical framework does not account for the interests that children and mothers have in effective and flexible methods to secure payment, I do not pass on the wisdom of the majority\xe2\x80\x99s preferred procedures. Nor do I address the wisdom of\nthe State\xe2\x80\x99s decision to use certain methods of enforcement. Whether \xe2\x80\x9cdeadbeat dads\xe2\x80\x9d should be threatened with incarceration is a policy judgment for state and federal lawmakers, as is the entire question of government involvement in the area of child support. See Elrod & Dale, Paradigm Shifts and Pendulum Swings in Child Custody, 42 Fam. L.\xc2\xa0Q. 381, 382 (2008) (observing the \xe2\x80\x9cfederalization of many areas of family law\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). This and other repercussions of the shift away from the nuclear family are ultimately the business of the policymaking branches. See, e.g., D. Popenoe, Family in Decline in America, reprinted in War Over the Family 3, 4 (2005) (discussing \xe2\x80\x9cfour major social trends\xe2\x80\x9d that emerged in the 1960\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cto signal a widespread \xe2\x80\x98flight\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d from the \xe2\x80\x9cnuclear family\xe2\x80\x9d); Krause, Child Support Reassessed, 24 Fam. L.\xc2\xa0Q. 1, 16 (1990) (\xe2\x80\x9cEasy-come, easy-go marriage and casual cohabitation and procreation are on a collision course with the economic and social needs of children\xe2\x80\x9d); M. Boumil & J. Friedman, Deadbeat Dads 23\xe2\x80\x9324 (1996) (\xe2\x80\x9cMany [children of deadbeat dads] are born out of wedlock \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. Others have lost a parent to divorce at such a young age that they have little conscious memory of it\xe2\x80\x9d).

As that is the only issue properly before the Court, I respectfully dissent.
