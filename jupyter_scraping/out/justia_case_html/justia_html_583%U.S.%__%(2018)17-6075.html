SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

\n

KEITH THARPE v. ERIC SELLERS, WARDEN

\n

on petition for writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

No. 17\xe2\x80\x936075.\xe2\x80\x83Decided January 8, 2018

\n

\n

Per Curiam.

\n

Petitioner Keith Tharpe moved to reopen his federal habeas corpus proceedings regarding his claim that the Georgia jury that convicted him of murder included a white juror, Barney Gattie, who was biased against Tharpe because he is black. See Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 60(b)(6). The District Court denied the motion on the ground that, among other things, Tharpe\xe2\x80\x99s claim was procedurally defaulted in state court. The District Court also noted that Tharpe could not overcome that procedural default because he had failed to produce any clear and convincing evidence contradicting the state court\xe2\x80\x99s determination that Gattie\xe2\x80\x99s presence on the jury did not prejudice him. See Tharpe v. Warden, No. 5:10\xe2\x80\x93cv\xe2\x80\x93433 (MD Ga., Sept. 5, 2017), App. B to Pet. for Cert. 19.

\n

Tharpe sought a certificate of appealability (COA). The Eleventh Circuit denied his COA application after deciding that jurists of reason could not dispute that the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s procedural ruling was correct. See Tharpe v. Warden, 2017 WL 4250413, *3 (Sept. 21, 2017). The Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision, as we read it, was based solely on its conclusion, rooted in the state court\xe2\x80\x99s factfinding, that Tharpe had failed to show prejudice in connection with his procedurally defaulted claim, i.e., that Tharpe had \xe2\x80\x9cfailed to demonstrate that Barney Gattie\xe2\x80\x99s behavior \xe2\x80\x98had substantial and injurious effect or influence in determining the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Brecht v. Abrahamson,\n\n507 U.\xc2\xa0S. 619,\n637 (1993)\n).

\n

Our review of the record compels a different conclusion. The state court\xe2\x80\x99s prejudice determination rested on its finding that Gattie\xe2\x80\x99s vote to impose the death penalty was not based on Tharpe\xe2\x80\x99s race. See Tharpe v. Warden, No. 93\xe2\x80\x93cv\xe2\x80\x93144 (Super. Ct. Butts Cty., Ga., Dec. 1, 2008), App. F to Pet. for Cert. 102. And that factual determination is binding on federal courts, including this Court, in the absence of clear and convincing evidence to the contrary. See\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(e)(1). Here, however, Tharpe produced a sworn affidavit, signed by Gattie, indicating Gattie\xe2\x80\x99s view that \xe2\x80\x9cthere are two types of black people: 1. Black folks and 2. Niggers\xe2\x80\x9d; that Tharpe, \xe2\x80\x9cwho wasn\xe2\x80\x99t in the \xe2\x80\x98good\xe2\x80\x99 black folks category in my book, should get the electric chair for what he did\xe2\x80\x9d; that \xe2\x80\x9c[s]ome of the jurors voted for death because they felt Tharpe should be an example to other blacks who kill blacks, but that wasn\xe2\x80\x99t my reason\xe2\x80\x9d; and that, \xe2\x80\x9c[a]fter studying the Bible, I have wondered if black people even have souls.\xe2\x80\x9d App. B to Pet. for Cert. 15\xe2\x80\x9316 (internal quotation marks omitted). Gattie\xe2\x80\x99s remarkable affidavit\xe2\x80\x94which he never retracted\xe2\x80\x94presents a strong factual basis for the argument that Tharpe\xe2\x80\x99s race affected Gattie\xe2\x80\x99s vote for a death verdict. At the very least, jurists of reason could debate whether Tharpe has shown by clear and convincing evidence that the state court\xe2\x80\x99s factual determination was wrong. The Eleventh Circuit erred when it concluded otherwise.

\n

The question of prejudice\xe2\x80\x94the ground on which the Eleventh Circuit chose to dispose of Tharpe\xe2\x80\x99s application\xe2\x80\x94is not the only question relevant to the broader inquiry whether Tharpe should receive a COA. The District Court denied Tharpe\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 60(b) motion on several grounds not addressed by the Eleventh Circuit. We express no view of those issues here. In light of the standard for relief from judgment under Rule 60(b)(6), which is available only in \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98extraordinary circumstances,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Gonzalez v. Crosby,\n\n545 U.\xc2\xa0S. 524,\n536 (2005)\n, Tharpe faces a high bar in showing that jurists of reason could disagree whether the District Court abused its discretion in denying his motion. It may be that, at the end of the day, Tharpe should not receive a COA. And review of the denial of a COA is certainly not limited to grounds expressly addressed by the court whose decision is under review. But on the unusual facts of this case, the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 review should not have rested on the ground that it was indisputable among reasonable jurists that Gattie\xe2\x80\x99s service on the jury did not prejudice Tharpe.

\n

We therefore grant Tharpe\xe2\x80\x99s motion to proceed in forma pauperis, grant the petition for certiorari, vacate the judgment of the Court of Appeals, and remand the case for further consideration of the question whether Tharpe is entitled to a COA.

\n

It is so ordered.

SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

\n

KEITH THARPE v. ERIC SELLERS, WARDEN

\n

on petition for writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

No. 17\xe2\x80\x936075.\xe2\x80\x83Decided January 8, 2018

\n

\n

Justice Thomas, with whom Justice Alito and Justice Gorsuch join, dissenting.

\n

If bad facts make bad law, then \xe2\x80\x9cunusual facts\xe2\x80\x9d inspire unusual decisions. Ante, at 3. In its brief per curiam opinion, the Court misreads a lower court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion to find an error that is not there, and then refuses to entertain alternative grounds for affirmance. The Court does this to accomplish little more than a do-over in the Court of Appeals: As it concedes, petitioner Keith Tharpe faces a \xe2\x80\x9chigh bar\xe2\x80\x9d on remand to obtain even a certificate of appealability (COA). Ante, at 2.

\n

One might wonder why the Court engages in this pointless exercise. The only possible explanation is its concern with the \xe2\x80\x9cunusual facts\xe2\x80\x9d of this case, specifically a juror affidavit that expresses racist opinions about blacks. The opinions in the affidavit are certainly odious. But their odiousness does not excuse us from doing our job correctly, or allow us to pretend that the lower courts have not done theirs.

\n

The responsibility of courts is to decide cases, both usual and unusual, by neutrally applying the law. The law reflects society\xe2\x80\x99s considered judgments about the balance of competing interests, and we must respect those judgments. In bending the rules here to show its concern for a black capital inmate, the Court must think it is showing its concern for racial justice. It is not. Its summary vacatur will not stop Tharpe\xe2\x80\x99s execution or erase the \xe2\x80\x9cunusual fac[t]\xe2\x80\x9d of the affidavit. It will only delay justice for Ja- quelin Freeman, who was also black, who is ignored by the majority, and who was murdered by Tharpe 27 years ago. I respectfully dissent.

\n

I

\n

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s terse opinion tells the reader that this case involves a petitioner, a juror, an affidavit, and a prejudice determination. But it involves much more than that. This case also has a victim, a second affidavit, numerous depositions, factfinding by a state court, and several decisions from federal judges that provide multiple grounds for denying a COA. I will briefly provide this omitted context.

\n

A

\n

Keith Tharpe\xe2\x80\x99s wife, Migrisus, left him in 1990. Despite a no-contact order, Tharpe called her and told her that if she wanted to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98play dirty\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d he would show her \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98what dirty was.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Tharpe v. Warden, 834 F.\xc2\xa03d 1323, 1325 (CA11 2016). The next morning, Tharpe ambushed his wife and her sister, Jaquelin Freeman, as they drove to work, pulling his truck in front of their car and forcing them to stop. Tharpe aimed a shotgun at the car and ordered his wife to get into his truck. He then told Freeman that he was going to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98f\xe2\x80\x94 [her] up\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and took her to the rear of his truck. Ibid. Tharpe shot Freeman, rolled her body into a ditch, reloaded, and shot her again, killing her. After murdering Freeman, Tharpe kidnaped and raped his wife, leaving Freeman\xe2\x80\x99s body lying in the ditch. Freeman\xe2\x80\x99s husband found her a short time later, while driving their children to school.

\n

A jury convicted Tharpe of malice murder and two counts of aggravated kidnaping. After hearing the evidence, the jury needed less than two hours to return a unanimous sentence of death. As aggravating factors, the jury found that Tharpe murdered Freeman while committing two other capital felonies\xe2\x80\x94the aggravated kidnapings of his wife and Freeman\xe2\x80\x94and that the murder was outrageously or wantonly vile, horrible, or inhuman.

\n

B

\n

More than seven years after his trial, Tharpe\xe2\x80\x99s lawyers interviewed one of his jurors, Barney Gattie. The resulting affidavit stated that Gattie knew Freeman, and that her family was \xe2\x80\x9cwhat [he] would call a nice [b]lack family.\xe2\x80\x9d Tharpe v. Warden, No. 5:10\xe2\x80\x93cv\xe2\x80\x93433 (MD Ga., Sept. 5, 2017), App. B to Pet. for Cert. 15. The affidavit continued that, in Gattie\xe2\x80\x99s view, \xe2\x80\x9cthere are two types of black people: 1. Black folks and 2. Niggers.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Tharpe \xe2\x80\x9cwasn\xe2\x80\x99t in the \xe2\x80\x98good\xe2\x80\x99 black folks category,\xe2\x80\x9d according to the affidavit, and if Freeman had been \xe2\x80\x9cthe type Tharpe is, then picking between life and death for Tharpe wouldn\xe2\x80\x99t have mattered so much.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 16. But because Freeman and her family were \xe2\x80\x9cgood black folks,\xe2\x80\x9d the affidavit continued, Gattie thought Tharpe \xe2\x80\x9cshould get the electric chair for what he did.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Gattie\xe2\x80\x99s affidavit went on to explain that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]fter studying the Bible,\xe2\x80\x9d he had \xe2\x80\x9cwondered if black people even have souls.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The affidavit also noted that some of the other jurors \xe2\x80\x9cwanted blacks to know they weren\xe2\x80\x99t going to get away with killing each other.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

A couple of days later, the State obtained another affidavit from Gattie. In that second affidavit, Gattie stated that he \xe2\x80\x9cdid not vote to impose the death penalty because [Tharpe] was a black man,\xe2\x80\x9d but instead because the evidence presented at trial justified it and because Tharpe showed no remorse. Record in No. 5:10\xe2\x80\x93cv\xe2\x80\x93433 (MD Ga., June 21, 2017) (Record), Doc. 77\xe2\x80\x933, p.\xc2\xa02. The affidavit explained that Gattie had consumed \xe2\x80\x9cseven or more beers\xe2\x80\x9d on the afternoon he signed the first affidavit. Ibid. Al- though he had signed it, he \xe2\x80\x9cnever swore to [it] nor was [he] ever asked if [the] statement was true and accurate.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 3. He also attested that many of the statements in the first affidavit \xe2\x80\x9cwere taken out of context and simply not accurate.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. And he felt that the lawyers who took it \xe2\x80\x9cwere deceiving and misrepresented what they stood for.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 5.

\n

A state postconviction court presided over Gattie\xe2\x80\x99s deposition. Gattie again testified that, although he signed the affidavit, he did not swear to its contents. Gattie also testified that when he signed the affidavit he had consumed \xe2\x80\x9c[m]aybe a 12 pack, [and] a few drinks of whiskey, over the period of the day.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., Doc. 15\xe2\x80\x938, p. 80. Tharpe\xe2\x80\x99s lawyers did not question Gattie about the contents of his first affidavit at the deposition. They instead spent much of the deposition asking Gattie unrelated questions about race, which the state court ruled irrelevant\xe2\x80\x94like whether he was familiar with Uncle Tom\xe2\x80\x99s Cabin or whether his granddaughter would play with a black doll. The lawyers\xe2\x80\x99 failure to address the contents of Gattie\xe2\x80\x99s first affidavit troubled the state court. Just before it permitted Gattie to leave, the court advised Tharpe\xe2\x80\x99s lawyers that it might \xe2\x80\x9ctotally discoun[t]\xe2\x80\x9d Gattie\xe2\x80\x99s first affidavit, and it again invited them to ask Gattie questions about its contents. Id., at 105. Tharpe\xe2\x80\x99s lawyers declined the opportunity.

\n

The state court also heard deposition testimony from ten of Tharpe\xe2\x80\x99s other jurors and received an affidavit from the eleventh. None of the jurors, two of whom were black, corroborated the statements in Gattie\xe2\x80\x99s first affidavit about how some of the jurors had considered race. The ten jurors who testified all said that race played no role in the jury\xe2\x80\x99s deliberations. The eleventh juror did not mention any consideration of race either.

\n

C

\n

Tharpe sought state postconviction relief. One of his claims was that \xe2\x80\x9cimproper racial animus .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. infected the deliberations of the jury.\xe2\x80\x9d Tharpe v. Warden, 2017 WL 4250413, *1 (CA11, Sept. 21, 2017).

\n

The state court rejected this claim for two reasons. First, Tharpe could not prove juror misconduct because Georgia law did not allow parties to impeach a jury verdict with post-trial testimony from jurors. Tharpe v. Warden, No. 93\xe2\x80\x93cv\xe2\x80\x93144 (Super. Ct. Butts Cty., Ga., Dec. 1, 2008), App. F to Pet. for Cert. 99\xe2\x80\x93101. Second, Tharpe had procedurally defaulted his claim because he had failed to raise it on direct appeal, and he could not establish cause and prejudice to overcome that default. Id., at 102. Tharpe\xe2\x80\x99s allegation of ineffective assistance of counsel was insufficient to establish cause because he had \xe2\x80\x9cfailed to establish the requisite deficiency or prejudice.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. And Tharpe failed to establish prejudice because the state court credited Gattie\xe2\x80\x99s testimony that he had not relied on race when voting to sentence Tharpe. Id., at 102\xe2\x80\x93103.

\n

D

\n

Tharpe then raised his juror-bias claim in a federal petition for a writ of habeas corpus. The United States District Court for the Middle District of Georgia denied his claim as procedurally defaulted. The District Court acknowledged that ineffective assistance of counsel can provide cause to overcome a procedural default, but it explained that Tharpe \xe2\x80\x9cfail[ed] to provide any details regarding this allegation.\xe2\x80\x9d 2017 WL 4250413, *2. The District Court concluded that Tharpe \xe2\x80\x9cha[d] not established that his counsels\xe2\x80\x99 ineffectiveness constituted cause to overcome the procedural defaul[t]\xe2\x80\x9d and that he \xe2\x80\x9cfailed to show actual prejudice.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

Tharpe did not seek a COA on his juror-bias claim. The United States Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit affirmed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision, Tharpe, 834 F.\xc2\xa03d 1323, and this Court denied certiorari, Tharpe v. Sellers, 582 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2017).

\n

In June 2017, Tharpe moved to reopen his federal ha- beas proceedings under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 60(b). He pointed to this Court\xe2\x80\x99s recent decisions in Buck v. Davis, 580 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2017), and Pena-Rodriguez v. Colorado, 580 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2017), as extraordinary circumstances that entitled him to relief. According to Tharpe, Buck established that extraordinary circumstances are present when a defendant was sentenced due to his race and new law provides an opportunity to consider the merits of his previously defaulted, race-based sentencing claim. Pena-Rodriguez supplied that new law, Tharpe argued, because it held that a state no-impeachment rule must yield when there is a \xe2\x80\x9cclear statement that indicates [a juror] relied on racial stereotypes or animus to convict a criminal defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d 580 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 17).

\n

The District Court denied Tharpe\xe2\x80\x99s motion. It first explained that Pena-Rodriguez announced a new procedural rule that does not apply retroactively on federal collateral review. App. B to Pet. for Cert. 6\xe2\x80\x9314. It alternatively deferred to the state court\xe2\x80\x99s finding that Tharpe could not prove cause or prejudice to overcome his procedural default. Id., at 18\xe2\x80\x9321. After the depositions of Gattie and ten other jurors, the state court credited Gattie\xe2\x80\x99s testimony that he did not vote for death based on race. Id., at 21. The District Court deferred to that credibility determination, and nothing in Pena-Rodriguez undermined that determination. App. B to Pet. for Cert. 19\xe2\x80\x9321.

\n

The Eleventh Circuit denied a COA. It explained that the District Court had concluded in its first decision that Tharpe failed to prove cause and prejudice. 2017 WL 4250413, *2. The District Court had later rejected Tharpe\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 60(b) motion both because Pena-Rodriguez was not retroactively applicable on federal collateral review and because it \xe2\x80\x9cpresumed the correctness\xe2\x80\x9d of the state court\xe2\x80\x99s finding that Tharpe failed to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98establish cause and prejudice.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 2017 WL 4250413, *2. The Eleventh Circuit then offered two reasons why Tharpe was not entitled to a COA. First, Tharpe had not \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98made a substantial showing of the denial of a constitutional right.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at *3 (quoting\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72253(c)(2)). \xe2\x80\x9cAs the [state court] and the District Court found, Tharpe failed to demonstrate that Barney Gattie\xe2\x80\x99s behavior \xe2\x80\x98had substantial and injurious effect or influence in determining the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 2017 WL 4250413, *3 (quoting Brecht v. Abrahamson,\n\n507 U.\xc2\xa0S. 619,\n637 (1993)\n). \xe2\x80\x9cNor,\xe2\x80\x9d the Eleventh Circuit continued, \xe2\x80\x9chas Tharpe shown that \xe2\x80\x98jurists of reason would find it debatable whether the district court was correct in its procedural ruling.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 2017 WL 4250413, *3 (quoting Slack v. McDaniel,\n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 473,\n484 (2000)\n).[1]

\n

Shortly before his execution, Tharpe filed a petition for a writ of certiorari and a stay application with this Court. We issued a stay.

\n

II

\n

To obtain a COA, Tharpe must show \xe2\x80\x9cthat jurists of reason would find it debatable whether the petition states a valid claim of the denial of a constitutional right\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cthat jurists of reason would find it debatable whether the district court was correct in its procedural ruling.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 484. The Court is not willing to say that Tharpe can satisfy this standard. See ante, at 3 (\xe2\x80\x9cIt may be that, at the end of the day, Tharpe should not receive a COA\xe2\x80\x9d). Instead, its opinion makes two moves. First, it \xe2\x80\x9cread[s]\xe2\x80\x9d the decision below as resting \xe2\x80\x9csolely\xe2\x80\x9d on Tharpe\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cfail[ure] to show prejudice\xe2\x80\x9d to overcome his procedural default. Ante, at 1. It does not read the decision as reaching cause, and it declines to consider that or any other alternative reason to affirm the Eleventh Circuit. See ante, at 1\xe2\x80\x932. Second, the Court holds, contrary to the Eleventh Circuit, that jurists of reason could debate whether Tharpe has proven prejudice. See ante, at 2. Neither of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s moves is justified.

\n

A

\n

1

\n

The majority misreads the decision below as resting \xe2\x80\x9csolely\xe2\x80\x9d on prejudice. See ante, at 1. The Eleventh Circuit addressed cause as well.

\n

The Eleventh Circuit first held that Tharpe had failed to make a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98substantial showing of the denial of a constitutional right,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d explaining that he had \xe2\x80\x9cfailed to demonstrate that .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. Gattie\xe2\x80\x99s behavior \xe2\x80\x98had substantial and injurious effect or influence in determining the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 2017 WL 4250413, *3 (quoting Brecht, supra, at 637). Then the Eleventh Circuit alternatively held that Tharpe had not \xe2\x80\x9cshown that \xe2\x80\x98jurists of reason would find it debatable whether the district court was correct in its procedural ruling.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 2017 WL 4250413, *3 (quoting Slack, supra, at 484). The \xe2\x80\x9cprocedural ruling\xe2\x80\x9d of the District Court rested on both cause and prejudice\xe2\x80\x94as the Eleventh Circuit explained earlier in its opinion, quoting the District Court at length. See 2017 WL 4250413, *2. Indeed, neither party suggests that the Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision did not reach cause, and both parties briefed the issue to this Court. See Brief in Opposition 16\xe2\x80\x9317; Reply Brief 7\xe2\x80\x938. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s reading of the decision below is untenable.

\n

Even if its reading were tenable, the Court does not explain why the strong medicine of a summary disposition is warranted here. Summary decisions are \xe2\x80\x9crare\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cusually reserved by this Court for situations in which .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the decision below is clearly in error.\xe2\x80\x9d Schweiker v. Hansen,\n\n450 U.\xc2\xa0S. 785,\n791 (1981)\n (Marshall, J., dissenting). The majority\xe2\x80\x99s reading of the decision below is not the better one, much less the clearly correct one. By adopting the least charitable reading of the Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision, the majority \xe2\x80\x9cdisrespects the judges of the courts of appeals, who are appointed and confirmed as we are.\xe2\x80\x9d Wellons v. Hall,\n\n558 U.\xc2\xa0S. 220,\n228 (2010)\n (Scalia, J., dissenting). This Court should not \xe2\x80\x9cvacate and send back their authorized judgments for inconsequential imperfection of opinion\xe2\x80\x94as though we were schoolmasters grading their homework.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. In fact, \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n appropriately self-respecting response to today\xe2\x80\x99s summary vacatur would be summary reissuance of the same opinion,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., with a sentence clarifying that the Eleventh Circuit agrees with the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision on cause.

\n

2

\n

Putting aside its misreading of the decision below, the Court inexplicably declines to consider alternative grounds for affirmance. The Court acknowledges that our review \xe2\x80\x9cis certainly not limited to grounds expressly addressed by the court whose decision is under review.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 3. But the Court does not explain why it nonetheless limits itself to the question of prejudice. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s self-imposed limitation is inexcusable given that Tharpe\xe2\x80\x99s collateral challenges to his sentence have lasted 24 years, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s failure to consider alternative grounds has halted an imminent execution, the alternative grounds were reached below, several of them were briefed here, and many of them are obviously correct. In fact, the District Court identified two grounds for denying Tharpe relief that no reasonable jurist could debate.

\n

First, no reasonable jurist could argue that Pena-Rodriguez applies retroactively on collateral review. Pena-Rodriguez established a new rule: The opinion states that it is answering a question \xe2\x80\x9cleft open\xe2\x80\x9d by this Court\xe2\x80\x99s ear- lier precedents. 580 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 13). A new rule does not apply retroactively unless it is substantive or a \xe2\x80\x9cwatershed rul[e] of criminal procedure.\xe2\x80\x9d Teague v. Lane,\n\n489 U.\xc2\xa0S. 288,\n311 (1989)\n (plurality opinion). Since Pena-Rodriguez permits a trial court \xe2\x80\x9cto consider [certain] evidence,\xe2\x80\x9d 580 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 17), and does not \xe2\x80\x9calte[r] the range of conduct or the class of persons that the law punishes,\xe2\x80\x9d Schriro v. Summerlin,\n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 348\n, 353 (2004), it cannot be a substantive rule.[2] And Tharpe does not even attempt to argue that Pena-Rodriguez established a watershed rule of criminal procedure\xe2\x80\x94a class of rules that is so \xe2\x80\x9cnarrow\xe2\x80\x9d that it is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98unlikely that any has yet to emerge.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Schriro, supra, at 352 (quoting Tyler v. Cain,\n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 656,\n667, n.\n7 (2001)\n; alterations omitted). Nor could he. Not even the right to have a jury decide a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s eligibility for death counts as a watershed rule of criminal procedure. Schriro, supra, at 355\xe2\x80\x93358.[3]

\n

Second, no reasonable jurist could argue that Tharpe demonstrated cause for his procedural default. The only cause that Tharpe raised in state court was ineffective assistance of counsel. The state court rejected this claim because Tharpe presented only a conclusory allegation to support it. No reasonable jurist could debate that decision. Nor could a reasonable jurist debate the cause argument that Tharpe raises here. In his reply brief in support of certiorari in this Court, Tharpe argues that he did not have to raise his claim of juror bias on direct appeal. Reply Brief 7\xe2\x80\x938. But Tharpe never raised this argument in state court, so the state court did not err in failing to accept it. Nor did the District Court abuse its discretion in failing to address it, since Tharpe merely mentioned it in a footnote in his reply brief where he was explaining the state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision. And even if Tharpe\xe2\x80\x99s description of Georgia law is correct and relevant in a federal habeas proceeding, he offers no explanation for why he waited seven years after his trial to obtain Gattie\xe2\x80\x99s affidavit. See Fults v. GDCP Warden, 764 F.\xc2\xa03d 1311, 1317 (CA11 2014). In short, Tharpe has not offered a viable argument on cause in any court.

\n

B

\n

On the one issue it does address\xe2\x80\x94prejudice\xe2\x80\x94the Court falters again. Its conclusion that reasonable jurists could debate prejudice plows through three levels of deference. First, it ignores the deference that appellate courts must give to trial courts\xe2\x80\x99 findings on questions of juror bias. See Skilling v. United States,\n\n561 U.\xc2\xa0S. 358,\n396 (2010)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cIn reviewing claims [of juror bias], the deference due to district courts is at its pinnacle: \xe2\x80\x98A trial court\xe2\x80\x99s findings of juror impartiality may be overturned only for manifest error\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Mu\xe2\x80\x99Min v. Virginia,\n\n500 U.\xc2\xa0S. 415,\n428 (1991)\n)). Then, it ignores the deference that federal ha- beas courts must give to state courts\xe2\x80\x99 factual findings. See 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa72254(e)(1). Finally, it ignores the deference that federal appellate courts must give to federal district courts\xe2\x80\x99 discretionary decisions under Rule 60(b). See Browder v. Director, Dept. of Corrections of Ill.,\n\n434 U.\xc2\xa0S. 257\n, n.\xc2\xa07 (1978).

\n

With all this deference, no reasonable jurist could debate the question of prejudice. The state court\xe2\x80\x99s finding that Tharpe \xe2\x80\x9cfailed to show that any alleged racial bias of Mr. Gattie\xe2\x80\x99s was the basis for sentencing\xe2\x80\x9d him, App. F to Pet. for Cert. 102, was supported by ample evidence. Gattie testified in his second affidavit that he did not impose a death sentence because of Tharpe\xe2\x80\x99s race. He also denied having sworn to the first affidavit and explained that he had consumed a substantial amount of alcohol on the day he signed it. Gattie\xe2\x80\x99s testimony was consistent with the testimony of the other ten jurors deposed in front of the trial court, each of whom testified that they did not consider race and that race was not discussed during their deliberations. To be sure, there was some evidence cutting the other way\xe2\x80\x94most notably, Gattie\xe2\x80\x99s first affidavit. But the state court heard all of the evidence, saw the witnesses\xe2\x80\x99 demeanor, and decided to credit Gattie\xe2\x80\x99s testimony that he did not vote for the death penalty because of Tharpe\xe2\x80\x99s race. Even if we were reviewing the state court directly, its finding would be entitled to substantial deference. See Skilling, supra, at 396.

\n

But we are not reviewing the state court directly. Instead, the relevant question is whether a reasonable jurist could argue that the District Court abused its discretion by concluding that the state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to credit Gattie\xe2\x80\x99s testimony has not been rebutted by clear and convincing evidence. Even if \xe2\x80\x9c[r]easonable minds reviewing the record might disagree about\xe2\x80\x9d the evidence, \xe2\x80\x9con habeas review that does not suffice to supersede the [state] court\xe2\x80\x99s credibility determination.\xe2\x80\x9d Rice v. Collins,\n\n546 U.\xc2\xa0S. 333\n\xe2\x80\x93342 (2006). And even if we might have made a different call, abuse-of-discretion review means we cannot \xe2\x80\x9csubstitute [our] judgment for that of the district court.\xe2\x80\x9d Horne v. Flores,\n\n557 U.\xc2\xa0S. 433,\n493 (2009)\n (Breyer, J., dissenting). Under these standards, no reasonable jurist could argue that Tharpe rebutted the state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision by clear and convincing evidence, much less that the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s deference to the state court\xe2\x80\x99s credibility determination was an abuse of discretion.

\n

III

\n

The Court is cognizant of the weakness of Tharpe\xe2\x80\x99s claims. It openly anticipates that he will not be able to obtain a COA, which makes sense given the insurmount- able barriers he faces on remand. Moreover, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s preliminary decision that reasonable jurists could debate prejudice says little about how a court of appeals could ever rule in Tharpe\xe2\x80\x99s favor on the merits of that question, given the multiple levels of deference that apply. At most, then, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision merely delays Tharpe\xe2\x80\x99s inevit- able execution.

\n

The Court tries to justify its decision \xe2\x80\x9con the unusual facts of this case.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 3. But there is nothing un- usual about deferring to a district court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to defer to a state court\xe2\x80\x99s credibility findings. This case involves a mine-run denial of a COA by a lower court on the eve of an execution, one that this Court routinely denies certiorari to address.

\n

Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision can be explained only by the \xe2\x80\x9cunusual fac[t]\xe2\x80\x9d of Gattie\xe2\x80\x99s first affidavit. Ibid. The Court must be disturbed by the racist rhetoric in that affidavit, and must want to do something about it. But the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision is no profile in moral courage. By remanding this case to the Court of Appeals for a useless do-over, the Court is not doing Tharpe any favors. And its unusual disposition of his case callously delays justice for Jaquelin Freeman, the black woman who was brutally murdered by Tharpe 27 years ago. Because this Court should not be in the business of ceremonial handwringing, I respectfully dissent.
