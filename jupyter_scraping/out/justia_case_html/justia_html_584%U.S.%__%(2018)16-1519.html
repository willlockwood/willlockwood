Lagos v. United States

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit

\n

No. 16\xe2\x80\x931519.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 18, 2018\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 29, 2018

\n

Petitioner Sergio Fernando Lagos was convicted of using a company he controlled to defraud a lender of tens of millions of dollars. After the fraudulent scheme came to light and Lagos\xe2\x80\x99 company went bankrupt, the lender conducted a private investigation of Lagos\xe2\x80\x99 fraud and participated as a party in the company\xe2\x80\x99s bankruptcy proceedings. Between the private investigation and the bankruptcy proceedings, the lender spent nearly $5 million in legal, accounting, and consulting fees related to the fraud. After Lagos pleaded guilty to federal wire fraud charges, the District Court ordered him to pay restitution to the lender for those fees. The Fifth Circuit affirmed, holding that such restitution was required by the Mandatory Victims Restitution Act of 1996, which requires defendants convicted of certain federal offenses, including wire fraud, to, among other things, \xe2\x80\x9creimburse the victim for lost income and necessary child care, transportation, and other expenses incurred during participation in the investigation or prosecution of the offense or attendance at proceedings related to the offense,\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73663A(b)(4).

\n

Held:

\n

1.\xc2\xa0The words \xe2\x80\x9cinvestigation\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cproceedings\xe2\x80\x9d in subsection (b)(4) of the Mandatory Victims Restitution Act are limited to government investigations and criminal proceedings and do not include private investigations and civil or bankruptcy proceedings. The word \xe2\x80\x9cinvestigation\xe2\x80\x9d appears in the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cthe investigation or prosecution.\xe2\x80\x9d Because the word \xe2\x80\x9cprosecution\xe2\x80\x9d must refer to a government\xe2\x80\x99s criminal prosecution, this suggests that the word \xe2\x80\x9cinvestigation\xe2\x80\x9d refers to a government\xe2\x80\x99s criminal investigation. Similar reasoning suggests that the immediately following reference to \xe2\x80\x9cproceedings\xe2\x80\x9d refers to criminal proceedings. Furthermore, the statute refers to the victim\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cparticipation\xe2\x80\x9d in the \xe2\x80\x9cinvestigation,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cattendance\xe2\x80\x9d at \xe2\x80\x9cproceedings,\xe2\x80\x9d which would be odd ways to describe a victim\xe2\x80\x99s role in its own private investigation and as a party in noncriminal court proceedings, but which are natural ways to describe a victim\xe2\x80\x99s role in a government\xe2\x80\x99s investigation and in the criminal proceedings that a government conducts.

\n

Moreover, the statute lists three specific items that must be reimbursed: lost income, child care expenses, and transportation expenses. These are precisely the kind of expenses that a victim is likely to incur when missing work and traveling to participate in a government investigation or to attend criminal proceedings. In contrast, the statute says nothing about the kinds of expenses a victim would often incur during private investigations or noncriminal proceedings, namely, the costs of hiring private investigators, attorneys, or accountants. This supports the Court\xe2\x80\x99s more limited reading of the statute.

\n

A broad reading would also require district courts to resolve difficult, fact-intensive disputes about whether particular expenses \xe2\x80\x9cincurred during\xe2\x80\x9d participation in a private investigation were in fact \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary,\xe2\x80\x9d and about whether proceedings such as a licensing proceeding or a Consumer Products Safety Commission hearing were sufficiently \xe2\x80\x9crelated to the offense.\xe2\x80\x9d The Court\xe2\x80\x99s narrower interpretation avoids such controversies, which are often irrelevant to the victim because over 90% of criminal restitution is never collected.

\n

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation means that some victims will not receive restitution for all of their losses from a crime, but that is consistent with the Mandatory Victims Restitution Act\xe2\x80\x99s enumeration of limited categories of covered expenses, in contrast with the broader language that other federal restitution statutes use, see, e.g., 18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72248(b), 2259(b), 2264(b), 2327(b). Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

2.\xc2\xa0That the victim shared the results of its private investigation with the Government does not make the costs of conducting the private investigation \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. other expenses incurred during participation in the investigation .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of the offense.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73663A(b)(4). That language does not cover the costs of a private investigation that the victim chooses on its own to conduct, which are not \xe2\x80\x9cincurred during\xe2\x80\x9d participation in a government\xe2\x80\x99s investigation. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

864 F.\xc2\xa03d 320, reversed and remanded.

\n

Breyer, J., delivered the opinion for a unanimous Court.

SERGIO FERNANDO LAGOS, PETITIONER v. UNITED STATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit

\n

[May 29, 2018]

\n

\n

Justice Breyer delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

The Mandatory Victims Restitution Act of 1996 requires defendants convicted of a listed range of offenses to

\n

\xe2\x80\x9creimburse the victim for lost income and necessary child care, transportation, and other expenses incurred during participation in the investigation or prosecution of the offense or attendance at proceedings related to the offense.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73663A(b)(4) (emphasis added).

\n

We must decide whether the words \xe2\x80\x9cinvestigation\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cproceedings\xe2\x80\x9d are limited to government investigations and criminal proceedings, or whether they include private investigations and civil proceedings. In our view, they are limited to government investigations and criminal proceedings.

\n

I

\n

The petitioner, Sergio Fernando Lagos, was convicted of using a company that he controlled (Dry Van Logistics) to defraud a lender (General Electric Capital Corporation, or GE) of tens of millions of dollars. The fraud involved generating false invoices for services that Dry Van Logistics had not actually performed and then borrowing money from GE using the false invoices as collateral. Eventually, the scheme came to light. Dry Van Logistics went bankrupt. GE investigated. The Government indicted Lagos. Lagos pleaded guilty to wire fraud. And the judge, among other things, ordered him to pay GE restitution.

\n

The issue here concerns the part of the restitution order that requires Lagos to reimburse GE for expenses GE incurred during its own investigation of the fraud and during its participation in Dry Van Logistics\xe2\x80\x99 bankruptcy proceedings. The amounts are substantial (about $5 million), and primarily consist of professional fees for attorneys, accountants, and consultants. The Government argued that the District Court must order restitution of these amounts under the Mandatory Victims Restitution Act because these sums were \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. other expenses incurred during participation in the investigation .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of the offense or attendance at proceedings related to the offense.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73663A(b)(4). The District Court agreed, as did the U.\xc2\xa0S. Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit. 864 F.\xc2\xa03d 320, 323 (2017).

\n

Lagos filed a petition for certiorari. And in light of a division of opinion on the matter, we granted the petition. Compare United States v. Papagno, 639 F.\xc2\xa03d 1093, 1100 (CADC 2011) (subsection (b)(4) of the Mandatory Victims Restitution Act does not cover private investigation costs), with United States v. Elson, 577 F.\xc2\xa03d 713, 726\xe2\x80\x93729 (CA6 2009) (statute not so limited); United States v. Hosking, 567 F.\xc2\xa03d 329, 331\xe2\x80\x93332 (CA7 2009) (same); United States v. Stennis-Williams, 557 F.\xc2\xa03d 927, 930 (CA8 2009) (same); United States v. Amato, 540 F.\xc2\xa03d 153, 159\xe2\x80\x93163 (CA2 2008) (same); United States v. Gordon, 393 F.\xc2\xa03d 1044, 1056\xe2\x80\x931057 (CA9 2004) (same).

\n

II

\n

The Mandatory Victims Restitution Act is one of several federal statutes that govern federal court orders requiring defendants convicted of certain crimes to pay their victims restitution. It concerns \xe2\x80\x9ccrime[s] of violence,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9coffense[s] against property . \xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0, including any offense committed by fraud or deceit,\xe2\x80\x9d and two specific offenses, one concern- ing tampering with a consumer product and the other concerning theft of medical products.\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73663A(c)(1)(A). It requires, in the case of property offenses, return of the property taken or its value, \xc2\xa73663A(b)(1); in the case of bodily injury, the payment of medical expenses and lost income, \xc2\xa73663A(b)(2); in the case of death, the payment of funeral expenses, \xc2\xa73663A(b)(3); and, as we have said, supra, at 1, in all cases, \xe2\x80\x9creimburse[ment]\xe2\x80\x9d to

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cthe victim for lost income and necessary child care, transportation, and other expenses incurred during participation in the investigation or prosecution of the offense or attendance at proceedings related to the offense.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73663A(b)(4) (emphasis added).

\n

We here consider the meaning of that italicized phrase. Specifically, we ask whether the scope of the words \xe2\x80\x9cinvestigation\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cproceedings\xe2\x80\x9d is limited to government investigations and criminal proceedings, or whether it includes private investigations and civil or bankruptcy litigation. We conclude that those words are limited to government investigations and criminal proceedings.

\n

Our conclusion rests in large part upon the statute\xe2\x80\x99s wording, both its individual words and the text taken as a whole. The individual words suggest (though they do not demand) our limited interpretation. The word \xe2\x80\x9cinvestigation\xe2\x80\x9d is directly linked by the word \xe2\x80\x9cor\xe2\x80\x9d to the word \xe2\x80\x9cprosecution,\xe2\x80\x9d with which it shares the article \xe2\x80\x9cthe.\xe2\x80\x9d This suggests that the \xe2\x80\x9cinvestigation[s]\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cprosecution[s]\xe2\x80\x9d that the statute refers to are of the same general type. And the word \xe2\x80\x9cprosecution\xe2\x80\x9d must refer to a government\xe2\x80\x99s criminal prosecution, which suggests that the word \xe2\x80\x9cinvestigation\xe2\x80\x9d may refer to a government\xe2\x80\x99s criminal investigation. A similar line of reasoning suggests that the immediately following reference to \xe2\x80\x9cproceedings\xe2\x80\x9d also refers to criminal proceedings in particular, rather than to \xe2\x80\x9cproceedings\xe2\x80\x9d of any sort.

\n

Furthermore, there would be an awkwardness about the statute\xe2\x80\x99s use of the word \xe2\x80\x9cparticipation\xe2\x80\x9d to refer to a victim\xe2\x80\x99s role in its own private investigation, and the word \xe2\x80\x9cattendance\xe2\x80\x9d to refer to a victim\xe2\x80\x99s role as a party in noncriminal court proceedings. A victim opting to pursue a private investigation of an offense would be more naturally said to \xe2\x80\x9cprovide for\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cconduct\xe2\x80\x9d the private investigation (in which he may, or may not, actively \xe2\x80\x9cparticipate\xe2\x80\x9d). And a victim who pursues civil or bankruptcy litigation does not merely \xe2\x80\x9catten[d]\xe2\x80\x9d such other \xe2\x80\x9cproceedings related to the offense\xe2\x80\x9d but instead \xe2\x80\x9cparticipates\xe2\x80\x9d in them as a party. In contrast, there is no awkwardness, indeed it seems perfectly natural, to say that a victim \xe2\x80\x9cparticipat[es] in the investigation\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cattend[s] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. proceedings related to the offense\xe2\x80\x9d if the investigation at issue is a government\xe2\x80\x99s criminal investigation, and if the proceedings at issue are criminal proceedings conducted by a government.

\n

Moreover, to consider the statutory phrase as a whole strengthens these linguistic points considerably. The phrase lists three specific items that must be reimbursed, namely, lost income, child care, and transportation; and it then adds the words, \xe2\x80\x9cand other expenses.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73663A(b)(4). Lost income, child care expenses, and transportation expenses are precisely the kind of expenses that a victim would be likely to incur when he or she (or, for a corporate victim like GE, its employees) misses work and travels to talk to government investigators, to participate in a government criminal investigation, or to testify before a grand jury or attend a criminal trial. At the same time, the statute says nothing about the kinds of expenses a victim would often incur when private investigations, or, say, bankruptcy proceedings are at issue, namely, the costs of hiring private investigators, attorneys, or accountants. Thus, if we look to noscitur a sociis, the well-worn Latin phrase that tells us that statutory words are often known by the company they keep, we find here both the presence of company that suggests limitation and the absence of company that suggests breadth. See, e.g., Yates v. United States, 574 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2015) (slip op., at 14).

\n

We add a practical fact: A broad reading would create significant administrative burdens. The statute provides for mandatory restitution, and the portion we construe is limited to \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. other expenses.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73663A(b)(4) (emphasis added). The word \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary\xe2\x80\x9d would, if the statute is broadly interpreted, invite disputes as to whether particular expenses \xe2\x80\x9cincurred during\xe2\x80\x9d participation in a private investigation or attendance at, say, a bankruptcy proceeding, were in fact \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary.\xe2\x80\x9d Such disputes may become burdensome in cases involving multimillion dollar investigation expenses for teams of lawyers and accountants. A district court might, for example, need to decide whether each witness interview and each set of documents reviewed was really \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary\xe2\x80\x9d to the investigation. Similarly, the statute also limits restitution to expenses incurred only during \xe2\x80\x9cattendance at proceedings related to the offense,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. (emphasis added), inviting disputes as to whether, say, a licensing proceeding, a human resources review, an in-house disciplinary proceeding, a job interview, a Consumer Product Safety Commission hearing, or a neighborhood watch meeting qualified as \xe2\x80\x9cproceedings\xe2\x80\x9d sufficiently \xe2\x80\x9crelated to the offense\xe2\x80\x9d so as to be eligible for restitution.

\n

To interpret the statute broadly is to invite controversy on those and other matters; our narrower construction avoids it. And one begins to doubt whether Congress intended, in making this restitution mandatory, to require courts to resolve these potentially time-consuming controversies as part of criminal sentencing\xe2\x80\x94particularly once one realizes that few victims are likely to benefit because more than 90% of criminal restitution is never collected. See GAO, Federal Criminal Restitution: Most Debt Is Outstanding and Oversight of Collections Could Be Improved 25 (GAO\xe2\x80\x9318\xe2\x80\x93203, 2018) (explaining that the Justice Department considers 91% of outstanding criminal restitution to be \xe2\x80\x9cuncollectible\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

There are, of course, contrary arguments\xe2\x80\x94arguments favoring a broad interpretation. The Government points out, in particular, that our narrow interpretation will sometimes leave a victim without a restitution remedy sufficient to cover some expenses (say, those related to his private investigation) which he undoubtedly incurred as a result of the offense. Leaving the victim without that restitution remedy, the Government adds, runs contrary to the broad purpose of the Mandatory Victims Restitution Act, namely, \xe2\x80\x9cto ensure that victims of a crime receive full restitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Dolan v. United States,\n\n560 U.\xc2\xa0S. 605, 612 (2010).

\n

But a broad general purpose of this kind does not always require us to interpret a restitution statute in a way that favors an award. After all, Congress has enacted many different restitution statutes with differing language, governing different circumstances. Some of those statutes specifically require restitution for the \xe2\x80\x9cfull amount of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses,\xe2\x80\x9d defined to include \xe2\x80\x9cany .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. losses suffered by the victim as a proximate result of the offense.\xe2\x80\x9d See 18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72248(b), 2259(b), 2264(b), 2327(b). The Mandatory Victims Restitution Act, however, contains no such language; it specifically lists the kinds of losses and expenses that it covers. Moreover, in at least one other statute Congress has expressly provided for restitution of \xe2\x80\x9cthe value of the time reasonably spent by the victim in an attempt to remediate the intended or actual harm incurred by the victim from the offense.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73663(b)(6). Again the Mandatory Victims Restitution Act has no similar provision. And given those differences between the Mandatory Victims Restitution Act and other restitution statutes, we conclude that the considerations we have mentioned, particularly those based on a reading of the statute as a whole, tip the balance in favor of our more limited interpretation.

\n

We add that this interpretation does not leave a victim such as GE totally without a remedy for additional losses not covered by the Mandatory Victims Restitution Act. GE also brought a civil lawsuit against Lagos for the full extent of its losses, and obtained an over-$30 million judgment against him. The Government says that GE has largely been unable to collect on that judgment, but there is no reason to think that collection efforts related to a criminal restitution award would prove any more successful.

\n

The Government makes one additional argument. It points out that GE shared with the Government the information that its private investigation uncovered. And that fact, the Government says, should bring the expenses of that investigation within the terms of the statute even if the \xe2\x80\x9cinvestigation\xe2\x80\x9d referred to by the statute is a government\xe2\x80\x99s criminal investigation. The short, conclusive answer to that claim, however, lies in the fact that the statute refers to \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary child care, transportation, and other expenses incurred during participation in the investigation or prosecution of the offense.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73663A(b)(4) (emphasis added). It does not refer to expenses incurred before the victim\xe2\x80\x99s participation in a government\xe2\x80\x99s investigation began. And the Government does not deny that it is those preparticipation expenses\xe2\x80\x94the expenses of conducting GE\xe2\x80\x99s investigation, not those of sharing the results from it\xe2\x80\x94that are at issue here. We therefore need not address in this case whether this part of the Mandatory Victims Restitution Act would cover similar expenses incurred during a private investigation that was pursued at a government\xe2\x80\x99s invitation or request. It is enough to hold that it does not cover the costs of a private investigation that the victim chooses on its own to conduct.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

For the reasons stated, we conclude that the words \xe2\x80\x9cinvestigation\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cproceedings\xe2\x80\x9d in the Mandatory Victims Restitution Act refer to government investigations and criminal proceedings. Consequently Lagos is not obliged to pay the portion of the restitution award that he here challenges. We reverse the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 judgment to the contrary, and we remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

\n

It is so ordered.
