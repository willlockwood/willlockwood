PEREZ, SECRETARY OF LABOR, et\xc2\xa0al. v. MORTGAGE BANKERS ASSOCIATION et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the district of columbia circuit

\n

No. 13\xe2\x80\x931041.\xe2\x80\x83Argued December 1, 2014\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 9, 2015[1]

\n

The Administrative Procedure Act (APA) establishes the procedures federal administrative agencies use for \xe2\x80\x9crule making,\xe2\x80\x9d defined as the process of \xe2\x80\x9cformulating, amending, or repealing a rule.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7551(5). The APA distinguishes between two types of rules: So-called \xe2\x80\x9clegislative rules\xe2\x80\x9d are issued through notice-and-comment rulemaking, see \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7553(b), (c), and have the \xe2\x80\x9cforce and effect of law,\xe2\x80\x9d Chrysler Corp. v. Brown,\n\n441 U.\xc2\xa0S. 281\n\xe2\x80\x93303. \xe2\x80\x9cInterpretive rules,\xe2\x80\x9d by contrast, are \xe2\x80\x9cissued . . . to advise the public of the agency\xe2\x80\x99s construction of the statutes and rules which it administers,\xe2\x80\x9d Shalala v. Guernsey Memorial Hospital,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 87\n, do not require notice-and-comment rulemaking, and \xe2\x80\x9cdo not have the force and effect of law,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid.

\n

\n \n \n \n In 1999 and 2001, the Department of Labor\xe2\x80\x99s Wage and Hour Division issued letters opining that mortgage-loan officers do not qualify for the administrative exemption to overtime pay requirements under the Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938. In 2004, the Department issued new regulations regarding the exemption. Respondent Mortgage Bankers Association (MBA) requested a new interpretation of the revised regulations as they applied to mortgage-loan officers, and in 2006, the Wage and Hour Division issued an opinion letter finding that mortgage-loan officers fell within the administrative exemption under the 2004 regulations. In 2010, the Department again altered its interpretation of the administrative exemption. Without notice or an opportunity for comment, the Department withdrew the 2006 opinion letter and issued an Administrator\xe2\x80\x99s Interpretation concluding that mortgage-loan officers do not qualify for the administrative exemption.

\n

\n \n \n \n MBA filed suit contending, as relevant here, that the Administrator\xe2\x80\x99s Interpretation was procedurally invalid under the D. C. Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Paralyzed Veterans of Am. v. D.\xc2\xa0C. Arena L.\xc2\xa0P., 117 F. 3d 579. The Paralyzed Veterans doctrine holds that an agency must use the APA\xe2\x80\x99s notice-and-comment procedures when it wishes to issue a new interpretation of a regulation that deviates significantly from a previously adopted interpretation. The District Court granted summary judgment to the Department, but the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit applied Paralyzed Veterans and reversed.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The Paralyzed Veterans doctrine is contrary to the clear text of the APA\xe2\x80\x99s rulemaking provisions and improperly imposes on agencies an obligation beyond the APA\xe2\x80\x99s maximum procedural requirements. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n \n \n \n (a)\xc2\xa0The APA\xe2\x80\x99s categorical exemption of interpretive rules from the notice-and-comment process is fatal to the Paralyzed Veterans doctrine. The D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s reading of the APA conflates the differing purposes of \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71 and 4 of the Act. Section 1 requires agencies to use the same procedures when they amend or repeal a rule as they used to issue the rule, see\n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7551(5), but it does not say what procedures an agency must use when it engages in rulemaking. That is the purpose of \xc2\xa74. And \xc2\xa74 specifically exempts interpretive rules from notice-and-comment requirements. Because an agency is not required to use notice-and-comment procedures to issue an initial interpretive rule, it is also not required to use those procedures to amend or repeal that rule. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\n \n \n \n (b)\xc2\xa0This straightforward reading of the APA harmonizes with longstanding principles of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s administrative law jurisprudence, which has consistently held that the APA \xe2\x80\x9csets forth the full extent of judicial authority to review executive agency action for procedural correctness,\xe2\x80\x9d FCC v. Fox Television Stations, Inc.,\n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 502\n. The APA\xe2\x80\x99s rulemaking provisions are no exception: \xc2\xa74 establishes \xe2\x80\x9cthe maximum procedural requirements\xe2\x80\x9d that courts may impose upon agencies engaged in rulemaking. Vermont Yankee Nuclear Power Corp. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc.,\n\n435 U.\xc2\xa0S. 519\n. By mandating notice-and-comment procedures when an agency changes its interpretation of one of the regulations it enforces, Paralyzed Veterans creates a judge-made procedural right that is inconsistent with Congress\xe2\x80\x99 standards. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n \n \n \n (c)\xc2\xa0MBA\xe2\x80\x99s reasons for upholding the Paralyzed Veterans doctrine are unpersuasive. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n \n \n \n \n \n (1)\xc2\xa0MBA asserts that an agency interpretation of a regulation that significantly alters the agency\xe2\x80\x99s prior interpretation effectively amends the underlying regulation. That assertion conflicts with the ordinary meaning of the words \xe2\x80\x9camend\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cinterpret,\xe2\x80\x9d and it is impossible to reconcile with the longstanding recognition that interpretive rules do not have the force and effect of law. MBA\xe2\x80\x99s theory is particularly odd in light of the limitations of the Paralyzed Veterans doctrine, which applies only when an agency has previously adopted an interpretation of its regulation. MBA fails to explain why its argument regarding revised interpretations should not also extend to the agency\xe2\x80\x99s first interpretation. Christensen v. Harris County,\n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 576\n, and Shalala v. Guernsey Memorial Hospital,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 87\n, distinguished. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n \n \n \n \n \n (2)\xc2\xa0MBA also contends that the Paralyzed Veterans doctrine reinforces the APA\xe2\x80\x99s goal of procedural fairness. But the APA already provides recourse to regulated entities from agency decisions that skirt notice-and-comment provisions by placing a variety of constraints on agency decisionmaking, e.g., the arbitrary and capricious standard. In addition, Congress may include safe-harbor provisions in legislation to shelter regulated entities from liability when they rely on previous agency interpretations. See, e.g., 29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7259(a), (b)(1). Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\n \n \n \n \n \n (3)\xc2\xa0MBA has waived its argument that the 2010 Administrator\xe2\x80\x99s Interpretation should be classified as a legislative rule. From the beginning, this suit has been litigated on the understanding that the Administrator\xe2\x80\x99s Interpretation is an interpretive rule. Neither the District Court nor the Court of Appeals addressed this argument below, and MBA did not raise it here in opposing certiorari. P. 14.

\n

720 F. 3d 966, reversed.

Justice Sotomayor delivered the opinion of the Court.

When a federal administrative agency first issues a rule interpreting one of its regulations, it is generally not required to follow the notice-and-comment rulemaking procedures of the Administrative Procedure Act (APA or Act). See5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7553(b)(A). The United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit has nevertheless held, in a line of cases beginning with Paralyzed Veterans of Am. v. D.\xc2\xa0C. Arena L. P., 117 F.\xc2\xa03d 579 (1997), that an agency must use the APA\xe2\x80\x99s notice-and-comment procedures when it wishes to issue a new interpretation of a regulation that deviates significantly from one the agency has previously adopted. The question in these cases is whether the rule announced in Paralyzed Veterans is consistent with the APA. We hold that it is not.

I

A

The APA establishes the procedures federal administrative agencies use for \xe2\x80\x9crule making,\xe2\x80\x9d defined as the process of \xe2\x80\x9cformulating, amending, or repealing a rule.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7551(5). \xe2\x80\x9cRule,\xe2\x80\x9d in turn, is defined broadly to include \xe2\x80\x9cstatement[s] of general or particular applicability and future effect\xe2\x80\x9d that are designed to \xe2\x80\x9cimplement, interpret, or prescribe law or policy.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7551(4).

Section 4 of the APA,5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7553, prescribes a three-step procedure for so-called \xe2\x80\x9cnotice-and-comment rulemaking.\xe2\x80\x9d First, the agency must issue a \xe2\x80\x9c[g]eneral notice of proposed rule making,\xe2\x80\x9d ordinarily by publication in the Federal Register. \xc2\xa7553(b). Second, if \xe2\x80\x9cnotice [is] required,\xe2\x80\x9d the agency must \xe2\x80\x9cgive interested persons an opportunity to participate in the rule making through submission of written data, views, or arguments.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7553(c). An agency must consider and respond to significant comments received during the period for public comment. See Citizens to Preserve Overton Park, Inc. v. Volpe,401 U.\xc2\xa0S. 402,416 (1971); Thompson v. Clark, 741 F.\xc2\xa02d 401, 408 (CADC 1984). Third, when the agency promulgates the final rule, it must include in the rule\xe2\x80\x99s text \xe2\x80\x9ca concise general statement of [its] basis and purpose.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7553(c). Rules issued through the notice-and-comment process are often referred to as \xe2\x80\x9clegislative rules\xe2\x80\x9d because they have the \xe2\x80\x9cforce and effect of law.\xe2\x80\x9d Chrysler Corp. v. Brown,441 U.\xc2\xa0S. 281\xe2\x80\x93303 (1979) (internal quotation marks omitted).

Not all \xe2\x80\x9crules\xe2\x80\x9d must be issued through the notice-and-comment process. Section 4(b)(A) of the APA provides that, unless another statute states otherwise, the notice-and-comment requirement \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not apply\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9cinterpretative rules, general statements of policy, or rules of agency organization, procedure, or practice.\xe2\x80\x9d5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7553(b)(A). The term \xe2\x80\x9cinterpretative rule,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cinterpretive rule,\xe2\x80\x9d[1] is not further defined by the APA, and its precise meaning is the source of much scholarly and judicial debate. See generally Pierce, Distinguishing Legislative Rules From Interpretative Rules, 52 Admin. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 547 (2000); Manning, Nonlegislative Rules, 72 Geo. Wash. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 893 (2004). We need not, and do not, wade into that debate here. For our purposes, it suffices to say that the critical feature of interpretive rules is that they are \xe2\x80\x9cissued by an agency to advise the public of the agency\xe2\x80\x99s construction of the statutes and rules which it administers.\xe2\x80\x9d Shalala v. Guernsey Memorial Hospital,514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 87,99 (1995) (internal quotation marks omitted). The absence of a notice-and-comment obligation makes the process of issuing interpretive rules comparatively easier for agencies than issuing legislative rules. But that convenience comes at a price: Interpretive rules \xe2\x80\x9cdo not have the force and effect of law and are not accorded that weight in the adjudicatory process.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

B

These cases began as a dispute over efforts by the Department of Labor to determine whether mortgage-loan officers are covered by the Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938 (FLSA),52Stat.1060, as amended,29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7201 et\xc2\xa0seq. The FLSA \xe2\x80\x9cestablishe[s] a minimum wage and overtime compensation for each hour worked in excess of 40 hours in each workweek\xe2\x80\x9d for many employees. Integ-rity Staffing Solutions, Inc. v. Busk, 574 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 3). Certain classes of employees, however, are exempt from these provisions. Among these exempt individuals are those \xe2\x80\x9cemployed in a bona fide executive, administrative, or professional capacity .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. or in the capacity of outside salesman .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. .\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7213(a)(1). The exemption for such employees is known as the \xe2\x80\x9cadministrative\xe2\x80\x9d exemption.

The FLSA grants the Secretary of Labor authority to \xe2\x80\x9cdefin[e]\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cdelimi[t]\xe2\x80\x9d the categories of exempt admin-istrative employees. Ibid. The Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s current regu-lations regarding the administrative exemption were promulgated in 2004 through a notice-and-commentrulemaking. As relevant here, the 2004 regulations differed from the previous regulations in that they contained a new section providing several examples of exempt administrative employees. See 29 CFR \xc2\xa7541.203. One of the examples is \xe2\x80\x9c[e]mployees in the financial services industry,\xe2\x80\x9d who, depending on the nature of their day-to-day work, \xe2\x80\x9cgenerally meet the duties requirements for the administrative exception.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7541.203(b). The financial services example ends with a caveat, noting that \xe2\x80\x9can employee whose primary duty is selling financial products does not qualify for the administrative exemption.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

In 1999 and again in 2001, the Department\xe2\x80\x99s Wage and Hour Division issued letters opining that mortgage-loan officers do not qualify for the administrative exemption. See Opinion Letter, Loan Officers/Exempt Status, 6A LRR, Wages and Hours Manual 99:8351 (Feb. 16, 2001); Opinion Letter, Mortgage Loan Officers/Exempt Status, id., at 99:8249. (May 17, 1999). In other words, the Department concluded that the FLSA\xe2\x80\x99s minimum wage and maximum hour requirements applied to mortgage-loan officers. When the Department promulgated its current FLSA regulations in 2004, respondent Mortgage Bankers Association (MBA), a national trade association representing real estate finance companies, requested a new opinion interpreting the revised regulations. In 2006, the Department issued an opinion letter finding that mortgage-loan officers fell within the administrative exemption under the 2004 regulations. See App. to Pet. for Cert. in No. 13\xe2\x80\x931041, pp. 70a\xe2\x80\x9384a. Four years later, however, the Wage and Hour Division again altered its interpretation of the FLSA\xe2\x80\x99s administrative exemption as it applied to mortgage-loan officers. Id., at 49a\xe2\x80\x9369a. Reviewing the provisions of the 2004 regulations and judicial decisions addressing the administrative exemption, the Department\xe2\x80\x99s 2010 Administrator\xe2\x80\x99s Interpretation concluded that mortgage-loan officers \xe2\x80\x9chave a primary duty of making sales for their employers, and, therefore, do not qualify\xe2\x80\x9d for the administrative exemption. Id., at 49a, 69a. The Department accordingly withdrew its 2006 opinion letter, which it now viewed as relying on \xe2\x80\x9cmisleading assumption[s] and selective and narrow analysis\xe2\x80\x9d of the exemption example in \xc2\xa7541.203(b). Id., at 68a. Like the 1999, 2001, and 2006 opinion letters, the 2010 Administrator\xe2\x80\x99s Interpretation was issued without notice or an opportunity for comment.

C

MBA filed a complaint in Federal District Court challenging the Administrator\xe2\x80\x99s Interpretation. MBA contended that the document was inconsistent with the 2004 regulation it purported to interpret, and thus arbitrary and capricious in violation of \xc2\xa710 of the APA,5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7706. More pertinent to this case, MBA also argued that the Administrator\xe2\x80\x99s Interpretation was procedurally in-valid in light of the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Paralyzed Veterans, 117 F. 3d 579. Under the Paralyzed Veterans doctrine, if \xe2\x80\x9can agency has given its regulation a definitive interpretation, and later significantly revises that interpretation, the agency has in effect amended its rule, something it may not accomplish\xe2\x80\x9d under the APA \xe2\x80\x9cwithout notice and comment.\xe2\x80\x9d Alaska Professional Hunters Assn., Inc. v. FAA, 177 F. 3d 1030, 1034 (CADC 1999). Three former mortgage-loan officers\xe2\x80\x94Beverly Buck, Ryan Henry, and Jerome Nickols\xe2\x80\x94subsequently intervened in thecase to defend the Administrator\xe2\x80\x99s Interpretation.[2]

The District Court granted summary judgment to the Department. Mortgage Bankers Assn. v. Solis, 864 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 193 (DC 2012). Though it accepted the parties\xe2\x80\x99 characterization of the Administrator\xe2\x80\x99s Interpretation as an interpretive rule, id., at 203, n.\xc2\xa07, the District Court determined that the Paralyzed Veterans doctrine was inapplicable because MBA had failed to establish its reliance on the contrary interpretation expressed in the Department\xe2\x80\x99s 2006 opinion letter. The Administrator\xe2\x80\x99s Interpretation, the District Court further determined, was fully supported by the text of the 2004 FLSA regulations. The court accordingly held that the 2010 interpretation was not arbitrary or capricious.[3]

The D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit reversed. Mortgage Bankers Assn. v. Harris, 720 F.\xc2\xa03d 966 (2013). Bound to the rule of Paralyzed Veterans by precedent, the Court of Appeals rejected the Government\xe2\x80\x99s call to abandon the doctrine. 720 F.\xc2\xa03d., at 967, n.\xc2\xa01. In the court\xe2\x80\x99s view, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he only question\xe2\x80\x9d properly before it was whether the District Court had erred in requiring MBA to prove that it relied on the Department\xe2\x80\x99s prior interpretation. Id., at 967. Explaining that reliance was not a required element of the Paralyzed Veterans doctrine, and noting the Department\xe2\x80\x99s concession that a prior, conflicting interpretation of the 2004 regulations existed, the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit concluded that the 2010 Administrator\xe2\x80\x99s Interpretation had to be vacated.

We granted certiorari, 573 U.\xc2\xa0S. __ (2014), and now reverse.

II

The Paralyzed Veterans doctrine is contrary to the clear text of the APA\xe2\x80\x99s rulemaking provisions, and it improperly imposes on agencies an obligation beyond the \xe2\x80\x9cmaximum procedural requirements\xe2\x80\x9d specified in the APA, Vermont Yankee Nuclear Power Corp. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc.,435 U.\xc2\xa0S. 519,524 (1978).

A

The text of the APA answers the question presented. Section 4 of the APA provides that \xe2\x80\x9cnotice of proposed rule making shall be published in the Federal Register.\xe2\x80\x9d5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7553(b). When such notice is required by the APA, \xe2\x80\x9cthe agency shall give interested persons an opportunity to participate in the rule making.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7553(c). But \xc2\xa74 further states that unless \xe2\x80\x9cnotice or hearing is required by statute,\xe2\x80\x9d the Act\xe2\x80\x99s notice-and-comment requirement \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not apply .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to interpretative rules.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7553(b)(A). This exemption of interpretive rules from the notice-and-comment process is categorical, and it is fatal to the rule announced in Paralyzed Veterans.

Rather than examining the exemption for interpretive rules contained in \xc2\xa74(b)(A) of the APA, the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit in Paralyzed Veterans focused its attention on \xc2\xa71 of the Act. That section defines \xe2\x80\x9crule making\xe2\x80\x9d to include not only the initial issuance of new rules, but also \xe2\x80\x9crepeal[s]\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9camend[ments]\xe2\x80\x9d of existing rules. See \xc2\xa7551(5). Because notice-and-comment requirements may apply even to these later agency actions, the court reasoned, \xe2\x80\x9callow[ing] an agency to make a fundamental change in its interpretation of a substantive regulation without notice and comment\xe2\x80\x9d would undermine the APA\xe2\x80\x99s procedural framework. 117 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 586.

This reading of the APA conflates the differing purposes of \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71 and 4 of the Act. Section 1 defines what a rule-making is. It does not, however, say what procedures an agency must use when it engages in rulemaking. That is the purpose of \xc2\xa74. And \xc2\xa74 specifically exempts interpretive rules from the notice-and-comment requirements that apply to legislative rules. So, the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit correctly read \xc2\xa71 of the APA to mandate that agencies use the same procedures when they amend or repeal a rule as they used to issue the rule in the first instance. See FCC v. Fox Television Stations, Inc.,556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 502,515 (2009) (the APA \xe2\x80\x9cmake[s] no distinction .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. between initial agency action and subsequent agency action undoing or revising that action\xe2\x80\x9d). Where the court went wrong was in failing to apply that accurate understanding of \xc2\xa71 to the exemption for interpretive rules contained in \xc2\xa74: Because an agency is not required to use notice-and-comment procedures to issue an initial interpretive rule, it is also not required to use those procedures when it amends or repeals that interpretive rule.

B

The straightforward reading of the APA we now adopt harmonizes with longstanding principles of our administrative law jurisprudence. Time and again, we have reiterated that the APA \xe2\x80\x9csets forth the full extent of judicial authority to review executive agency action for procedural correctness.\xe2\x80\x9d Fox Television Stations, Inc., 556 U. S., at 513. Beyond the APA\xe2\x80\x99s minimum requirements, courts lack authority \xe2\x80\x9cto impose upon [an] agency its own notion of which procedures are \xe2\x80\x98best\xe2\x80\x99 or most likely to further some vague, undefined public good.\xe2\x80\x9d Vermont Yankee, 435 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 549. To do otherwise would violate \xe2\x80\x9cthe very basic tenet of administrative law that agencies should be free to fashion their own rules of procedure.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 544.

These foundational principles apply with equal force to the APA\xe2\x80\x99s procedures for rulemaking. We explained in Vermont Yankee that \xc2\xa74 of the Act \xe2\x80\x9cestablished the maximum procedural requirements which Congress was willing to have the courts impose upon agencies in conducting rulemaking procedures.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 524. \xe2\x80\x9cAgencies are free to grant additional procedural rights in the exercise of their discretion, but reviewing courts are generally not free to impose them if the agencies have not chosen to grant them.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

The Paralyzed Veterans doctrine creates just such a judge-made procedural right: the right to notice and an opportunity to comment when an agency changes its interpretation of one of the regulations it enforces. That requirement may be wise policy. Or it may not. Regardless, imposing such an obligation is the responsibility of Congress or the administrative agencies, not the courts. We trust that Congress weighed the costs and benefits of placing more rigorous procedural restrictions on the issuance of interpretive rules. See id., at 523 (when Congress enacted the APA, it \xe2\x80\x9csettled long-continued and hard-fought contentions, and enact[ed] a formula upon which opposing social and political forces have come to rest\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). In the end, Congress decided to adopt standards that permit agencies to promulgate freely such rules\xe2\x80\x94whether or not they are consistent with earlier interpretations. That the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit would have struck the balance differently does not permit that court or this one to overturn Congress\xe2\x80\x99 contrary judgment. Cf. Law v. Siegel, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 11).

III

MBA offers several reasons why the Paralyzed Veterans doctrine should be upheld. They are not persuasive.

A

MBA begins its defense of the Paralyzed Veterans doctrine by attempting to bolster the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s reading of the APA. \xe2\x80\x9cParalyzed Veterans,\xe2\x80\x9d MBA contends, \xe2\x80\x9csimply acknowledges the reality that where an agency significantly alters a prior, definitive interpretation of a regulation, it has effectively amended the regulation itself,\xe2\x80\x9d something that under the APA requires use of notice-and-comment procedures. Brief for Respondent 20\xe2\x80\x9321.

The act of \xe2\x80\x9camending,\xe2\x80\x9d however, in both ordinary parlance and legal usage, has its own meaning separate and apart from the act of \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreting.\xe2\x80\x9d Compare Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 98 (10th ed. 2014) (defining \xe2\x80\x9camend\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o change the wording of\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cformally alter .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. by striking out, inserting, or substituting words\xe2\x80\x9d), with id., at 943 (defining \xe2\x80\x9cinterpret\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o ascertain the meaning and significance of thoughts expressed in words\xe2\x80\x9d). One would not normally say that a court \xe2\x80\x9camends\xe2\x80\x9d a statute when it interprets its text. So too can an agency \xe2\x80\x9cinterpret\xe2\x80\x9d a regulation without \xe2\x80\x9ceffectively amend[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d the underlying source of law. MBA does not explain how, precisely, an interpretive rule changes the regulation it interprets, and its assertion is impossible to reconcile with the longstanding recognition that interpretive rules do not have the force and effect of law. See Chrysler Corp., 441 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 302, n. 31 (citing Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s Manual on the Administrative Procedure Act 30, n.\xc2\xa03 (1947)); Skidmore v. Swift & Co.,323 U.\xc2\xa0S. 134,140 (1944).

MBA\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cinterpretation-as-amendment\xe2\x80\x9d theory is particularly odd in light of the limitations of the Paralyzed Veterans doctrine. Recall that the rule of Paralyzed Veterans applies only when an agency has previously adopted an interpretation of its regulation. Yet in that initial interpretation as much as all that come after, the agency is giving a definite meaning to an ambiguous text\xe2\x80\x94the very act MBA insists requires notice and comment. MBA is unable to say why its arguments regarding revised interpretations should not also extend to the agency\xe2\x80\x99s first interpretation.[4]

Next, MBA argues that the Paralyzed Veterans doctrine is more consistent with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cfunctional\xe2\x80\x9d approach to interpreting the APA. Relying on Christensen v. Harris County,529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 576 (2000), and Shalala v. Guernsey Memorial Hospital,514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 87, MBA contends that we have already recognized that an agency may not \xe2\x80\x9cavoid notice-and-comment procedures by cloaking its actions in the mantle of mere \xe2\x80\x98interpretation.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 23\xe2\x80\x9324.

Neither of the cases MBA cites supports its argument. Our decision in Christensen did not address a change in agency interpretation. Instead, we there refused to give deference to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of an unambiguous regulation, observing that to defer in such a case would allow the agency \xe2\x80\x9cto create de\xc2\xa0facto a new regulation.\xe2\x80\x9d 529 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 588. Put differently, Christensen held that the agency interpretation at issue was substantively invalid because it conflicted with the text of the regulation the agency purported to interpret. That holding is irrelevant to this suit and to the Paralyzed Veterans rule, which assesses whether an agency interpretation is procedurally invalid.

As for Guernsey, that case is fully consistent with\xe2\x80\x94indeed, confirms\xe2\x80\x94what the text of the APA makes plain: \xe2\x80\x9cInterpretive rules do not require notice and comment.\xe2\x80\x9d 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 99. Sidestepping this inconvenient language, MBA instead quotes a portion of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion stating that \xe2\x80\x9cAPA rulemaking would still be required if [an agency] adopted a new position inconsistent with .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. existing regulations.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 100. But the statement on which MBA relies is dictum. Worse, it is dictum taken out of context. The \xe2\x80\x9cregulations\xe2\x80\x9d to which the Court referred were two provisions of the Medicare reimbursement scheme. And it is apparent from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s description of these regulations in Part II of the opinion that they were legislative rules, issued through the notice-and-comment process. See id., at 91\xe2\x80\x9392 (noting that the disputed regulations were codified in the Code of Federal Regulations). Read properly, then, the cited passage from Guernsey merely means that \xe2\x80\x9can agency may only change its interpretation if the revised interpretation is consistent with the underlying regulations.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners in No. 13\xe2\x80\x931052, p. 44.

B

In the main, MBA attempts to justify the Paralyzed Veterans doctrine on practical and policy grounds. MBA contends that the doctrine reinforces the APA\xe2\x80\x99s goal of \xe2\x80\x9cprocedural fairness\xe2\x80\x9d by preventing agencies from unilaterally and unexpectedly altering their interpretation of important regulations. Brief for Respondent 16.

There may be times when an agency\xe2\x80\x99s decision to issue an interpretive rule, rather than a legislative rule, is driven primarily by a desire to skirt notice-and-comment provisions. But regulated entities are not without recourse in such situations. Quite the opposite. The APA contains a variety of constraints on agency decisionmaking\xe2\x80\x94the arbitrary and capricious standard being among the most notable. As we held in Fox Television Stations, and underscore again today, the APA requires an agency to provide more substantial justification when \xe2\x80\x9cits new policy rests upon factual findings that contradict those which underlay its prior policy; or when its prior policy has engendered serious reliance interests that must be taken into account. It would be arbitrary and capricious to ignore such matters.\xe2\x80\x9d 556 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 515 (citation omitted); see also id., at 535 (Kennedy, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment).

In addition, Congress is aware that agencies sometimes alter their views in ways that upset settled reliance interests. For that reason, Congress sometimes includes in the statutes it drafts safe-harbor provisions that shelter regulated entities from liability when they act in conformance with previous agency interpretations. The FLSA includes one such provision: As amended by the Portal-to-Portal Act of 1947,29 U. S. C. \xc2\xa7251 et\xc2\xa0seq., the FLSA provides that \xe2\x80\x9cno employer shall be subject to any liability\xe2\x80\x9d for failing \xe2\x80\x9cto pay minimum wages or overtime compensation\xe2\x80\x9d if it demonstrates that the \xe2\x80\x9cact or omission complained of was in good faith in conformity with and in reliance on any written administrative regulation, order, ruling, approval, or interpretation\xe2\x80\x9d of the Administrator of the Department\xe2\x80\x99s Wage and Hour Division, even when the guidance is later \xe2\x80\x9cmodified or rescinded.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7259(a), (b)(1). These safe harbors will often protect parties from liability when an agency adopts an interpretation that conflicts with its previous position.[5]

C

MBA changes direction in the second half of its brief, contending that if the Court overturns the Paralyzed Veterans rule, the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment should nonetheless be affirmed. That is so, MBA says, because the agency interpretation at issue\xe2\x80\x94the 2010 Administrator\xe2\x80\x99s Interpretation\xe2\x80\x94should in fact be classified as a legislative rule.

We will not address this argument. From the beginning, the parties litigated this suit on the understanding that the Administrator\xe2\x80\x99s Interpretation was\xe2\x80\x94as its name suggests\xe2\x80\x94an interpretive rule. Indeed, if MBA did not think the Administrator\xe2\x80\x99s Interpretation was an interpretive rule, then its decision to invoke the Paralyzed Veterans doctrine in attacking the rule is passing strange. After all, Paralyzed Veterans applied only to interpretive rules. Consequently, neither the District Court nor the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit considered MBA\xe2\x80\x99s current claim that the Administrator\xe2\x80\x99s Interpretation is actually a legislative rule. Beyond that, and more important still, MBA\xe2\x80\x99s brief in opposition to certiorari did not dispute petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 assertions\xe2\x80\x94in their framing of the question presented and in the substance of their petitions\xe2\x80\x94that the Administrator\xe2\x80\x99s Interpretation is an interpretive rule. Thus, even assuming MBA did not waive the argument below, it has done so in this Court. See this Court\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 15.2; Carcieri v. Salazar,555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 379\xe2\x80\x93396 (2009).

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

For the foregoing reasons, the judgment of the United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit is reversed.

It is so ordered.

Justice Thomas, concurring in the judgment.

I concur in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding that the doctrine first announced in Paralyzed Veterans of America v. D.\xc2\xa0C. Arena L. P., 117 F.\xc2\xa03d 579 (CADC 1997), is inconsistent with the Administrative Procedure Act (APA),5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7551 et\xc2\xa0seq., and must be rejected. An agency\xe2\x80\x99s substantial revision of its interpretation of a regulation does not amount to an \xe2\x80\x9camendment\xe2\x80\x9d of the regulation as that word is used in the statute.

I write separately because these cases call into question the legitimacy of our precedents requiring deference to administrative interpretations of regulations. That line of precedents, beginning with Bowles v. Seminole Rock & Sand Co.,325 U.\xc2\xa0S. 410 (1945), requires judges to defer to agency interpretations of regulations, thus, as happened in these cases, giving legal effect to the interpretations rather than the regulations themselves. Because this doctrine effects a transfer of the judicial power to an executive agency, it raises constitutional concerns. This line of precedents undermines our obligation to provide a judicial check on the other branches, and it subjects regulated parties to precisely the abuses that the Framers sought to prevent.

I

The doctrine of deference to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of regulations is usually traced back to this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Seminole Rock, supra, which involved the interpretation of a war-time price control regulation, id., at 411. Along with a general price freeze, the Administrator of the Office of Price Administration had promulgated specialized regulations governing the maximum price for different commodities. Id., at 413. When the Administrator brought an enforcement action against a manufacturer of crushed stone, the manufacturer challenged the Administrator\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of his regulations.

The lower courts agreed with the manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation, id., at 412\xe2\x80\x93413, but this Court reversed. In setting out the approach it would apply to the case, the Court announced\xe2\x80\x94without citation or explanation\xe2\x80\x94that an administrative interpretation of an ambiguous regulation was entitled to \xe2\x80\x9ccontrolling weight\xe2\x80\x9d:

\xe2\x80\x9cSince this involves an interpretation of an administrative regulation a court must necessarily look to the administrative construction of the regulation if the meaning of the words used is in doubt. The intention of Congress or the principles of the Constitution in some situations may be relevant in the first instance in choosing between various constructions. But the ultimate criterion is the administrative interpretation, which becomes of controlling weight unless it is plainly erroneous or inconsistent with the regulation.\xe2\x80\x9dId., at 413\xe2\x80\x93414.

The Court then concluded that the rule \xe2\x80\x9cclearly\xe2\x80\x9d favored the Administrator\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation, rendering this discussion dictum. Id., at 415\xe2\x80\x93417.

From this unsupported rule developed a doctrine of deference that has taken on a life of its own.[1] It has been broadly applied to regulations issued by agencies across a broad spectrum of subjects. See, e.g., Robertson v. Methow Valley Citizens Council,490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 332\xe2\x80\x93359 (1989) (forests); Ehlert v. United States,402 U.\xc2\xa0S. 99\xe2\x80\x93105 (1971) (Selective Service); INS v. Stanisic,395 U.\xc2\xa0S. 62,72 (1969) (deportation); Udall v. Tallman,380 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\xe2\x80\x9317 (1965) (oil and gas leases). It has even been applied to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of another agency\xe2\x80\x99s regulations. See Pauley v. BethEnergy Mines, Inc.,501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 680\xe2\x80\x93699 (1991). And, it has been applied to an agency interpretation that was inconsistent with a previous interpretation of the same regulation. See Long Island Care at Home, Ltd. v. Coke,551 U.\xc2\xa0S. 158\xe2\x80\x93171 (2007). It has been applied to formal and informal interpretations alike, including those taken during litigation. See Auer v. Robbins,519 U.\xc2\xa0S. 452,462 (1997). Its reasoning has also been extended outside the context of traditional agency regulations into the realm of criminal sentencing. See Stinson v. United States,508 U.\xc2\xa0S. 36\xe2\x80\x9345 (1993) (concluding that the Sentencing Commission\xe2\x80\x99s commentary on its Guidelines is analogous to an agency interpretation of its own regulations, entitled to Seminole Rock deference).

The Court has even applied the doctrine to an agency interpretation of a regulation cast in such vague aspirational terms as to have no substantive content. See Thomas Jefferson Univ. v. Shalala,512 U.\xc2\xa0S. 504\xe2\x80\x93513 (1994); see also id., at 518 (Thomas, J., dissenting).

On this steady march toward deference, the Court only once expressly declined to apply Seminole Rock deference on the ground that the agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation was plainly erroneous.[2] In that case, we were faced with the predict-able consequence of this line of precedents: An agency sought deference to an opinion letter that interpreted a permissive regulation as mandatory. See Christensen v. Harris County,529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 576,588 (2000). We rejected that request for deference as an effort, \xe2\x80\x9cunder the guise of interpreting a regulation, to create de\xc2\xa0facto a new regulation.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. This narrow limit on the broad deference given the agency interpretations, though sound, could not save a doctrine that was constitutionally infirm from the start. Seminole Rock was constitutionally suspect from the start, and this Court\xe2\x80\x99s repeated extensions of it have only magnified the effects and the attendant concerns.

II

We have not always been vigilant about protecting the structure of our Constitution. Although this Court has repeatedly invoked the \xe2\x80\x9cseparation of powers\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cthe constitutional system of checks and balances\xe2\x80\x9d as core principles of our constitutional design, essential to the protection of individual liberty, see, e.g., Stern v. Marshall, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2011) (slip op., at 16\xe2\x80\x9317) (internal quotation marks omitted), it has also endorsed a \xe2\x80\x9cmore pragmatic, flexible approach\xe2\x80\x9d to that design when it has seemed more convenient to permit the powers to be mixed, see, e.g., Nixon v. Administrator of General Services,433 U.\xc2\xa0S. 425,442 (1977). As the history shows, that approach runs the risk of compromising our constitutionalstructure.

A

The Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s particular blend of separated powers and checks and balances was informed by centuries of political thought and experiences. See M. Vile, Constitutionalism and the Separation of Powers 38, 168\xe2\x80\x93169 (2d ed. 1998) (Vile). Though the theories of the separation of powers and checks and balances have roots in the ancient world, events of the 17th and 18th centuries played a crucial role in their development and informed the men who crafted and ratified the Constitution.

Over a century before our War of Independence, the English Civil War catapulted the theory of the separation of powers to prominence. As political theorists of the day witnessed the conflict between the King and Parliament, and the dangers of tyrannical government posed by each, they began to call for a clear division of authority between the two. Id., at 44\xe2\x80\x9345, 48\xe2\x80\x9349. A 1648 work titled The Royalist\xe2\x80\x99s Defence offered perhaps the first extended account of the theory of the separation of powers: \xe2\x80\x9c[W]hilst the Supreamacy, the Power to Judge the Law, and Authority to make new Lawes, are kept in severall hands, the known Law is preserved, but united, it is vanished, instantly thereupon, and Arbytrary and Tyrannicall power is introduced.\xe2\x80\x9d The Royalist\xe2\x80\x99s Defence 80 (1648) (italics in original).

John Locke and Baron de Montesquieu endorsed and expanded on this concept. See Vile 63\xe2\x80\x9364. They agreed with the general theory set forth in The Royalist\xe2\x80\x99s Defence, emphasizing the need for a separation of powers to protect individual liberty. J. Locke, Second Treatise of Civil Government \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7143\xe2\x80\x93144, p. 72 (J. Gough ed. 1947); Montesquieu, Spirit of the Laws bk. XI, ch. 6, pp. 151\xe2\x80\x93152 (O. Piest ed., T. Nugent transl. 1949). But they also advocated a system of checks and balances to reinforce that separation. Vile 72\xe2\x80\x9373, 102. For instance, they agreed that the executive should have the power to assemble and dismiss the legislature and to consent to laws passed by it. See Locke, supra, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7151, 156, at 75, 77\xe2\x80\x9378; Montesquieu, Spirit of the Laws, at 157, 159. Montesquieu warned that \xe2\x80\x9cpower should be a check to power\xe2\x80\x9d lest the legislature \xe2\x80\x9carrogate to itself what authority it pleased .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [and] soon destroy all the other powers.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 150, 157.

The experience of the States during the period between the War of Independence and the ratification of the Constitution confirmed the wisdom of combining these theories. Although many State Constitutions of the time included language unequivocally endorsing the separation of powers, they did not secure that separation with checks and balances, Vile 147, and actively placed traditional executive and judicial functions in the legislature, G.\xc2\xa0Wood, The Creation of the American Republic 1776\xe2\x80\x931787, pp. 155\xe2\x80\x93156 (1969). Under these arrangements, state legislatures arrogated power to themselves and began to confiscate property, approve the printing of paper money, and suspend the ordinary means for the recovery of debts. Id., at 403\xe2\x80\x93409.[3]

When the Framers met for the Constitutional Convention, they understood the need for greater checks and balances to reinforce the separation of powers. As Madison remarked, \xe2\x80\x9cexperience has taught us a distrust\xe2\x80\x9d of the separation of powers alone as \xe2\x80\x9ca sufficient security to each [branch] [against] encroachments of the others.\xe2\x80\x9d 2 Re-cords of the Federal Convention of 1787, p. 77 (M. Farrand rev. 1966). \xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is necessary to introduce such a balance of powers and interests, as will guarantee the provisions on paper.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The Framers thus separated the three main powers of Government\xe2\x80\x94legislative, executive, and judicial\xe2\x80\x94into the three branches created by Articles I, II, and III. But they also created checks and balances to reinforce that separation. For example, they gave Congress specific enumerated powers to enact legislation, Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa78, but gave the President the power to veto that legislation, subject to congressional override by a supermajority vote, Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa77, cls. 2, 3. They gave the President the power to appoint principal officers of the United States, but gave the Senate the power to give advice and consent to those appointments. Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa72, cl.\xc2\xa02. They gave the House and Senate the power to agree to adjourn for more than three days, Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa75, cl.\xc2\xa04, but gave the President the power, \xe2\x80\x9cin Case of Disagreement between them,\xe2\x80\x9d to adjourn the Congress \xe2\x80\x9cto such Time as he shall think proper.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa73, cl.\xc2\xa03. During the ratification debates, Madison argued that this structure represented \xe2\x80\x9cthe great security\xe2\x80\x9d for liberty in the Constitution. The Federalist No. 51, p. 321 (C. Rossiter ed. 1961) (J. Madison).

To the Framers, the separation of powers and checks and balances were more than just theories. They were practical and real protections for individual liberty in the new Constitution. See Mistretta v. United States,488 U.\xc2\xa0S. 361,426 (1989) (Scalia, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9c[The Constitution] is a prescribed structure, a framework, for the conduct of government. In designing that structure, the Framers themselves considered how much commingling [of governmental powers] was, in the generality of things, acceptable, and set forth their conclusions in the document\xe2\x80\x9d). The Judiciary\xe2\x80\x94no less than the other two branches\xe2\x80\x94has an obligation to guard against deviations from those principles. The Seminole Rock line of precedent is one such deviation.

B

Seminole Rock raises two related constitutional concerns. It represents a transfer of judicial power to the Executive Branch, and it amounts to an erosion of the judicial obligation to serve as a \xe2\x80\x9ccheck\xe2\x80\x9d on the political branches.

1

When a party properly brings a case or controversy to an Article III court, that court is called upon to exercise the \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial Power of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0III, \xc2\xa71. For the reasons I explain in this section, the judicial power, as originally understood, requires a court to exercise its independent judgment in interpreting and expounding upon the laws.

Those who ratified the Constitution knew that legal texts would often contain ambiguities. See generally Molot, The Judicial Perspective in the Administrative State: Reconciling Modern Doctrines of Deference with the Judiciary\xe2\x80\x99s Structural Role, 53 Stan. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1, 20\xe2\x80\x9321, and n. 66 (2000); Nelson, Originalism and Interpretive Conventions, 70 U. Chi. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 519, 525\xe2\x80\x93526 (2003). As James Madison explained, \xe2\x80\x9cAll new laws, though penned with the greatest technical skill and passed on the fullest and most mature deliberation, are considered as more or less obscure and equivocal .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 37,at 229.

The judicial power was understood to include the power to resolve these ambiguities over time. See ibid. Alexander Hamilton lauded this power, arguing that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he interpretation of the laws is the proper and peculiar province of the courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., No. 78, at 467. It is undoubtedly true that the other branches of Government have the authority and obligation to interpret the law, but only the judicial interpretation would be considered authoritative in a judicial proceeding. Vile 360.

Although the Federalists and Anti-Federalists engaged in a public debate about this interpretive power, that debate centered on the dangers inherent in the power, not on its allocation under the Constitution. See, e.g., Letters from The Federal Farmer XV (Jan. 18, 1788), in 2 The Complete Anti-Federalist 315\xe2\x80\x93316 (H. Storing ed. 1981) (arguing that the interpretive power made the Judiciary the most dangerous branch). Writing as \xe2\x80\x9cBrutus,\xe2\x80\x9d one leading anti-Federalist argued that judges \xe2\x80\x9cw[ould] not confine themselves to any fixed or established rules, but w[ould] determine, according to what appears to them, the reason and spirit of the constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Essays of Brutus (Jan. 31, 1788), in 2 id., at 420. The Federalists rejected these arguments, assuring the public that judges would be guided \xe2\x80\x9cby strict rules and precedents which serve to define and point out their duty in every particular case that comes before them.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 78, at 471 (A. Hamilton). Those rules included principles of interpretation that had been set out by jurists for centuries. See, e.g., 2 S. von Pufendorf, De Officio Hominis Et Civis Juxta Legem Naturalem Libri Duo 83\xe2\x80\x9386 (1682) (F. Moore transl. 1927); see also 1 W. Blackstone, Commentaries on the Laws of England 59\xe2\x80\x9361 (1765).

One of the key elements of the Federalists\xe2\x80\x99 arguments in support of the allocation of power to make binding interpretations of the law was that Article III judges would exercise independent judgment. Although \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial independence\xe2\x80\x9d is often discussed in terms of independence from external threats, the Framers understood the concept to also require independence from the \xe2\x80\x9cinternal threat\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9chuman will.\xe2\x80\x9d P. Hamburger, Law and Judicial Duty 507, 508 (2008); see also The Federalist No. 78, at 465 (A. Hamilton) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe judiciary . . . may truly be said to have neither FORCE nor WILL but merely judgment .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. \xe2\x80\x9d). Independent judgment required judges to decide cases in accordance with the law of the land, not in accordance with pressures placed upon them through either internal or external sources. Internal sources might include personal biases, while external sources might include pressure from the political branches, the public, or otherinterested parties. See Hamburger, supra, at 508\xe2\x80\x93521.

The Framers made several key decisions at the Convention with these pressures in mind. For example, they rejected proposals to include a federal council of revision after several participants at the Convention expressed concern that judicial involvement in such a council would foster internal biases. Rufus King of Maryland, for example, asserted that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Judges ought to be able to expound the law as it should come before them, free from the bias of having participated in its formation.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 Records of the Federal Convention of 1787, at 98. Alexander Hamilton repeated these concerns in The Federalist, arguing that \xe2\x80\x9cthe judges, who are to be interpreters of the law, might receive an improper bias from having given a previous opinion in their revisionary capacities\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cbe induced to embark too far in the political views of [the Executive]\xe2\x80\x9d from too much association with him. The Federalist No. 73, at 446; see also Hamburger, supra, at 508\xe2\x80\x93512.

The Framers also created structural protections in the Constitution to free judges from external influences. They provided, for example, that judges should \xe2\x80\x9chold their Offices during good Behaviour\xe2\x80\x9d and receive \xe2\x80\x9ca Compensation, which shall not be diminished during their Continuance in Office.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0III, \xc2\xa71. Hamilton noted that such unequivocal language had been shown necessary by the experience of the States, where similar state constitutional protections for judges had not been \xe2\x80\x9csufficiently definiteto preclude legislative evasions\xe2\x80\x9d of the separation of the judicial power. The Federalist No. 79, at 472. Because \xe2\x80\x9cpower over a man\xe2\x80\x99s subsistence amounts to a power over his will,\xe2\x80\x9d he argued that Article III\xe2\x80\x99s structural protections would help ensure that judges fulfilled their constitutional role. Ibid. (emphasis deleted).

The Framers made the opposite choice for legislators and the Executive. Instead of insulating them from external pressures, the Constitution tied them to those pressures. It provided for election of Members of the House of Representatives every two years, Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa72, cl. 1; and selection of Members of the Senate every six years, Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa73, cl.\xc2\xa01. It also provided for the President to be subject to election every four years. Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa71, cl.\xc2\xa01. \xe2\x80\x9cThe President is [thus] directly dependent on the people, and since there is only one President, he is responsible. The people know whom to blame .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d See Morrison v. Olson,487 U.\xc2\xa0S. 654,729 (1988) (Scalia, J., dissenting). To preserve that accountability, we have held that executive officers must be subject to removal by the President to ensure account-ability within the Executive Branch. See Free Enterprise Fund v. Public Company Accounting Oversight Bd.,561 U.\xc2\xa0S. 477,495 (2010); see also Morrison, supra, at 709 (opinion of Scalia, J.) (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is not for us to determine, and we have never presumed to determine, how much of the purely executive powers of government must be within the full control of the President. The Constitution prescribes that they all are\xe2\x80\x9d).

Given these structural distinctions between the branches, it is no surprise that judicial interpretations are defini-tive in cases and controversies before the courts. Courts act as \xe2\x80\x9can intermediate body between the people and the legislature, in order, among other things, to keep the latter within the limits assigned to their authority.\xe2\x80\x9d Federalist No. 78, at 467 (A. Hamilton). The Legislature and Executive may be swayed by popular sentiment to abandon the strictures of the Constitution or other rules of law. But the Judiciary, insulated from both internal and external sources of bias, is duty bound to exercise independent judgment in applying the law.

Interpreting agency regulations calls for that exercise of independent judgment. Substantive regulations have the force and effect of law. See, e.g., United States v. Mead Corp.,533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 218\xe2\x80\x93232 (2001).[4] Agencies and private parties alike can use these regulations in proceedings against regulated parties. See, e.g., Christopher v. SmithKline Beecham Corp., 567 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2012) (slip op., at 6\xe2\x80\x937) (private party relying on Department of Labor regulations); FCC v. Fox Television Stations, Inc., 567 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 6) (agency issuing notices of liability under regulations). Just as it is critical for judges to exercise independent judgment in applying statutes, it is critical for judges to exercise independent judgment in determining that a regulation properly covers the conduct of regulated parties. Defining the legal meaning of the regulation is one aspect of that determination.

Seminole Rock deference, however, precludes judges from independently determining that meaning. Rather than judges\xe2\x80\x99 applying recognized tools of interpretation to determine the best meaning of a regulation, this doctrine demands that courts accord \xe2\x80\x9ccontrolling weight\xe2\x80\x9d to the agency interpretation of a regulation, subject only to the narrow exception for interpretations that are plainly erroneous or inconsistent with the regulation. That deference amounts to a transfer of the judge\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of interpretive judgment to the agency. See 1 S. Johnson, Dictionary of the English Language 499 (4th ed. 1773) (defining \xe2\x80\x9c[d]efer\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cto leave to another\xe2\x80\x99s judgment\xe2\x80\x9d). But the agency, as part of the Executive Branch, lacks the structural protections for independent judgment adopted by the Framers, including the life tenure and salary protections of Article III. Because the agency is thus not properly constituted to exercise the judicial power under the Constitution, the transfer of interpretive judgment raises serious separation-of-powers concerns.

2

Seminole Rock is constitutionally questionable for an additional reason: It undermines the judicial \xe2\x80\x9ccheck\xe2\x80\x9d on the political branches. Unlike the Legislative and Executive Branches, each of which possesses several political checks on the other, the Judiciary has one primary check on the excesses of political branches. That check is the enforcement of the rule of law through the exercise of judicial power.

Judges have long recognized their responsibility to apply the law, even if they did not conceive of it as a \xe2\x80\x9ccheck\xe2\x80\x9d on political power. During the 17th century, for example, King James I sought to pressure Chief Justice Coke to affirm the lawfulness of his efforts to raise revenue without the participation of Parliament. Hamburger, Law and Judicial Duty, at 200\xe2\x80\x93201. Coke sought time to confer with his fellow jurists to \xe2\x80\x9cmake an advised answer according to law and reason.\xe2\x80\x9d Case of Proclamations, 12 Co. Rep. 74, 75, 77 Eng. Rep. 1352, 1353 (K. B. 1611). But the King\xe2\x80\x99s representative, Lord Chancellor Ellesmere, responded that \xe2\x80\x9che would advise the Judges to maintain the power and prerogative of the King\xe2\x80\x9d and suggested that, \xe2\x80\x9cin cases in which there is no authority and precedent,\xe2\x80\x9d the judiciary should \xe2\x80\x9cleave it to the King to order in it according to his wisdom.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Coke famously responded, \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he King cannot change any part of the common law, nor create any offence by his proclamation, which was not an offence before, without Parliament.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. When James I later attempted to do just that, Coke declared the proclamations \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98utterly against Law and reason, and for that void.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Hamburger, supra, at 202.

The Framers expected Article III judges to engage in similar efforts, by applying the law as a \xe2\x80\x9ccheck\xe2\x80\x9d on the excesses of both the Legislative and Executive Branches. See, e.g., 3 J. Elliot, Debates in the Several Conventions on the Adoption of the Federal Constitution 553 (1863) (J. Marshall) (\xe2\x80\x9cIf [the Government of the United States] make a law not warranted by any of the powers enumerated, it would be considered by the judges as an infringement of the Constitution which they are to guard. .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. They would declare it void\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Vile 174. The Framers \xe2\x80\x9ccontemplated [the Constitution], as a rule for the government of courts, as well as of the legislature.\xe2\x80\x9d Marbury v. Madison, 1 Cranch 137, 179\xe2\x80\x93180 (1803). Thus, if a case involved a conflict between a law and the Constitution, judges would have a duty \xe2\x80\x9cto adhere to the latter and disregard the former.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 78, at 468 (A. Hamilton); see also Marbury, 1 Cranch, at 178. Similarly, if a case involved an executive effort to extend a law beyond its meaning, judges would have a duty to adhere to the law that had been properly promulgated under the Constitution. Cf. id., at 157\xe2\x80\x93158 (considering the scope of the President\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional power of appointment). As this Court said long ago, \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he particular phraseology of the constitution of the United States confirms and strengthens the principle, supposed to be essential to all written constitutions, that a law repugnant to the constitution is void; and that courts, as well as other departments, are bound by that instrument.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 180.

Article III judges cannot opt out of exercising their check. As we have long recognized, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Judiciary has a responsibility to decide cases properly before it, even those it \xe2\x80\x98would gladly avoid.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Zivotofsky v. Clinton, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 5) (quoting Cohens v. Virginia, 6 Wheat. 264, 404 (1821)). This responsibility applies not only to constitutional challenges to particular statutes, see, e.g., Shelby County v. Holder, 570 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2013) (slip op., at 2), including those based on the separation of powers, Free Enterprise Fund, 561 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 501\xe2\x80\x93502, but also to more routine questions about the best interpretation of statutes, see, e.g., Whitfield v. United States, 574 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2015) (slip op., at 2\xe2\x80\x933), or the compatibility of agency actions with enabling statutes, Utility Air Regulatory Group v. EPA, 573 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 10). In each case, the Judiciary is called upon to exercise its independent judgment and apply the law.

But we have not consistently exercised the judicial check with respect to administrative agencies. Even though regulated parties have repeatedly challenged agency interpretations as inconsistent with existing regulations, we have just as repeatedly declined to exercise independent judgment as to those claims. Instead, we have deferred to the executive agency that both promulgated the regulations and enforced them. Although an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of a regulation might be the best interpretation, it also might not. When courts refuse even to decide what the best interpretation is under the law, they abandon the judicial check. That abandonment permits precisely the accumulation of governmental powers that the Framers warned against. See The Federalist No. 47, at 302 (J. Madison).

C

This accumulation of governmental powers allows agencies to change the meaning of regulations at their discretion and without any advance notice to the parties. It is precisely this problem that the United States Court of Appeals for the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit attempted to address by requiring agencies to undertake notice and comment procedures before substantially revising definitive interpretations of regulations. Paralyzed Veterans, supra. Though legally erroneous, the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 reasoning was practically sound. When courts give \xe2\x80\x9ccontrolling weight\xe2\x80\x9d to an administrative interpretation of a regulation\xe2\x80\x94instead of to the best interpretation of it\xe2\x80\x94they effectively give the interpretation\xe2\x80\x94and not the regulation\xe2\x80\x94the force and effect of law. To regulated parties, the new interpretation might as well be a new regulation.

These cases provide a classic example of the problem. The Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938 establishes federal minimum wage and overtime requirements, but exempts from these requirements \xe2\x80\x9cany employee engaged in a bona fide executive, administrative, or professional capac-ity\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0, or in the capacity of outside salesman (as such terms are defined and delimited from time to time by regulations of the Secretary).\xe2\x80\x9d29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7213(a)(1). The Department of Labor has accordingly promulgated regulations providing that \xe2\x80\x9can employee whose primary duty is selling financial products does not qualify for the administrative exemption.\xe2\x80\x9d 29 CFR \xc2\xa7541.203(b) (2015).

Unsure whether certain mortgage-loan officers qualified as employees whose primary duty is selling financial products, the Mortgage Bankers Association asked the Department of Labor for advice. In 2006, the Department concluded that the officers are not employees whose primary duty is selling financial products. But in 2010, the Department reversed course, concluding exactly the opposite. If courts accord \xe2\x80\x9ccontrolling weight\xe2\x80\x9d to both the 2006 and 2010 interpretations, the regulated entities are subject to two opposite legal rules imposed under the same regulation.

This practice turns on its head the principle that the United States is \xe2\x80\x9ca government of laws, and not of men.\xe2\x80\x9d Marbury, supra, at 163. Regulations provide notice to regulated parties in only a limited sense because their meaning will ultimately be determined by agencies rather than by the \xe2\x80\x9cstrict rules and precedents\xe2\x80\x9d to which Alexander Hamilton once referred.[5]

III

Although this Court offered no theoretical justifica-tion for Seminole Rock deference when announcing it, sev-eral justifications have been proposed since. None is persuasive.

A

Probably the most oft-recited justification for Seminole Rock deference is that of agency expertise in administering technical statutory schemes. Under this justification, deference to administrative agencies is necessary when a \xe2\x80\x9cregulation concerns \xe2\x80\x98a complex and highly technical regulatory program\xe2\x80\x99 in which the identification and classification of relevant \xe2\x80\x98criteria necessarily require significant expertise and entail the exercise of judgment grounded in policy concerns.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Thomas Jefferson Univ., 512 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 512.

This defense of Seminole Rock deference misidentifies the relevant inquiry. The proper question faced by courts in interpreting a regulation is not what the best policy choice might be, but what the regulation means. Because this Court has concluded that \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive agency regulations have the \xe2\x80\x98force and effect of law,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Chrysler Corp. v. Brown,441 U.\xc2\xa0S. 281,295 (1979), such regulations should be interpreted like any other law. Thus, we should \xe2\x80\x9cassum[e] that the ordinary meaning of the regulation\xe2\x80\x99s language expresses\xe2\x80\x9d its purpose and enforce it \xe2\x80\x9caccording to its terms.\xe2\x80\x9d See Hardt v. Reliance Standard Life Ins. Co.,560 U.\xc2\xa0S. 242,251 (2010) (internal quotation marks omitted). Judges are at least as well suited as administrative agencies to engage in this task. Cf. Marbury, 1 Cranch, at 177 (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is emphatically the province and duty of the judicial department to say what the law is\xe2\x80\x9d). Indeed, judges are frequently called upon to interpret the meaning of legal texts and are able to do so even when those texts involve technical language. See, e.g., Barber v. Gonzales,347 U.\xc2\xa0S. 637\xe2\x80\x93643 (1954) (interpreting deportation statute according to technical meaning).

Fundamentally, the argument about agency expertise is less about the expertise of agencies in interpreting language than it is about the wisdom of according agencies broad flexibility to administer statutory schemes.[6] \xe2\x80\x9cBut policy arguments supporting even useful \xe2\x80\x98political inventions\xe2\x80\x99 are subject to the demands of the Constitution which defines powers and . . . sets out .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. how those powers are to be exercised.\xe2\x80\x9d INS v. Chadha,462 U.\xc2\xa0S. 919,945 (1983). Even in the face of a perceived necessity, the Constitution protects us from ourselves. New York v. United States,505 U.\xc2\xa0S. 144\xe2\x80\x93188 (1992).

B

Another oft-recited justification for Seminole Rock deference is that agencies are better situated to define the original intent behind their regulations. See Martin v. Occupational Safety and Health Review Comm\xe2\x80\x99n,499 U.\xc2\xa0S. 144\xe2\x80\x93153 (1991). Under this justification, \xe2\x80\x9c[b]ecause the Secretary [of Labor] promulgates th[e] standards, the Secretary is in a better position .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to reconstruct the purpose of the regulations in question.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 152.

This justification rings hollow. This Court has afforded Seminole Rock deference to agency interpretations even when the agency was not the original drafter. See Pauley, 501 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 696\xe2\x80\x93698 (applying Seminole Rock deference to one agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of another agency\xe2\x80\x99s regulations because Congress had delegated authority to both to administer the program). It has likewise granted Seminole Rock deference to agency interpretations that are inconsistent with interpretations adopted closer in time to the promulgation of the regulations. See, e.g., Long Island Care at Home, 551 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 170\xe2\x80\x93171.

Even if the scope of Seminole Rock deference more closely matched the original-drafter justification, it would still fail. It is the text of the regulations that have the force and effect of law, not the agency\xe2\x80\x99s intent. \xe2\x80\x9cCitizens arrange their affairs not on the basis of their legislators\xe2\x80\x99 unexpressed intent, but on the basis of the law as it is written and promulgated.\xe2\x80\x9d Zuni Public School Dist. No. 89 v. Department of Education,550 U.\xc2\xa0S. 81,119 (2007) (Scalia, J., dissenting). Cf. Wyeth v. Levine,555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 555\xe2\x80\x93587 (2009) (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment) (noting that only \xe2\x80\x9cfederal standards .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that are set forth in, or necessarily follow from, the statutory text that was produced through the constitutionally required bicameral and presentment procedures\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94not Congress\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cpurposes and objectives\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94can become the \xe2\x80\x9claw of the land\xe2\x80\x9d). \xe2\x80\x9cTo be governed by legislated text rather than legislators\xe2\x80\x99 intentions is what it means to be \xe2\x80\x98a Government of laws, not of men.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Zuni Public School Dist. No. 89, supra, at 119 (Scalia, J., dissenting). Only the text of a regulation goes through the procedures established by Congress for agency rulemaking. And it is that text on which the public is entitled to rely. For the same reasons that we should not accord controlling weight to postenactment expressions of intent by individual Members of Congress, see Sullivan v. Finkelstein,496 U.\xc2\xa0S. 617\xe2\x80\x93632 (1990) (Scalia, J., concurring in part), we should not accord controlling weight to expressions of intent by administrators ofagencies.

C

A third asserted justification for Seminole Rock deference is that Congress has delegated to agencies the authority to interpret their own regulations. See, e.g., Martin, 499 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 151. The theory is that, \xe2\x80\x9c[b]ecause applying an agency\xe2\x80\x99s regulation to complex or changing circumstances calls upon the agency\xe2\x80\x99s unique expertise and policymaking prerogatives, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the power authoritatively to interpret its own regulations is a component of the agency\xe2\x80\x99s delegated lawmaking powers.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

This justification fails because Congress lacks authority to delegate the power. As we have explained in an analogous context, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he structure of the Constitution does not permit Congress to execute the laws; it follows that Congress cannot grant to an officer under its control what it does not possess.\xe2\x80\x9d Bowsher v. Synar,478 U.\xc2\xa0S. 714,726 (1986). Similarly, the Constitution does not empower Congress to issue a judicially binding interpretation of the Constitution or its laws. Lacking the power itself, it cannot delegate that power to an agency.

To hold otherwise would be to vitiate the separation of powers and ignore the \xe2\x80\x9csense of a sharp necessity to separate the legislative from the judicial power .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [that] triumphed among the Framers of the new Federal Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Plaut v. Spendthrift Farm, Inc.,514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 211,221 (1995). As this Court has explained, the \xe2\x80\x9cessential balance\xe2\x80\x9d of the Constitution is that the Legislature is \xe2\x80\x9cpossessed of power to \xe2\x80\x98prescrib[e] the rules by which the duties and rights of every citizen are to be regulated,\xe2\x80\x99 but the power of \xe2\x80\x98[t]he interpretation of the laws\xe2\x80\x99 [is] \xe2\x80\x98the proper and peculiar province of the courts.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 222 (citation omitted; third brackets added). Although the Constitution imposes a duty on all three branches to interpret the laws within their own spheres, the power to create legally binding interpretations rests with the Judiciary. See Marbury, 1 Cranch, at 177, 179\xe2\x80\x93180.

D

A final proposed justification for Seminole Rock deference is that too much oversight of administrative matters would imperil the \xe2\x80\x9cindependence and esteem\xe2\x80\x9d of judges. See, e.g., Charles Evans Hughes, Speech before the Elmira Chamber of Commerce, May 3, 1907, in Addresses of Charles Evans Hughes, 1906\xe2\x80\x931916, p. 185 (2d ed. 1916). The argument goes that questions of administration are those which \xe2\x80\x9clie close to the public impatience,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 186, and thus the courts\xe2\x80\x99 resolution of such questions could \xe2\x80\x9cexpose them to the fire of public criticism,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 187.

But this argument, which boils down to a policy judgment of questionable validity, cannot vitiate the constitutional allocation of powers. The Judicial Branch is separate from the political branches for a reason: It has the obligation to apply the law to cases and controversies that come before it, and concerns about the popular esteem of individual judges\xe2\x80\x94or even the Judiciary as a whole\xe2\x80\x94have no place in that analysis. Our system of Government could not long survive absent adherence to the written Constitution that formed it.

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

Although on the surface these cases require only a straightforward application of the APA, closer scrutiny reveals serious constitutional questions lurking beneath. I have \xe2\x80\x9cacknowledge[d] the importance of stare decisis to the stability of our Nation\xe2\x80\x99s legal system.\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cBut stare decisis is only an \xe2\x80\x98adjunct\xe2\x80\x99 of our duty as judges to decide by our best lights what the Constitution means.\xe2\x80\x9d McDonald v. Chicago,561 U.\xc2\xa0S. 742,812 (2010) (Thomas, J., concurring inpart and concurring in judgment) (citation omitted). By my best lights, the entire line of precedent beginning with Seminole Rock raises serious constitutional questions and should be reconsidered in an appropriate case.

Justice Scalia, concurring in the judgment.

I agree with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision, and all of its reasoning demonstrating the incompatibility of the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s Paralyzed Veterans holding with the Administrative Procedure Act. Paralyzed Veterans of Am. v. D.\xc2\xa0C. Arena L.P., 117 F.\xc2\xa03d 579 (CADC 1997). I do not agree, however, with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s portrayal of the result it produces as a vindication of the balance Congress struck when it \xe2\x80\x9cweighed the costs and benefits of placing more rigorous .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. restrictions on the issuance of interpretive rules.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9. That depiction is accurate enough if one looks at this case in isolation. Considered alongside our law of deference to administrative determinations, however, today\xe2\x80\x99s decision produces a balance between power and procedure quite different from the one Congress chose when it enacted the APA.

\xe2\x80\x9cThe [APA] was framed against a background of rapid expansion of the administrative process as a check upon administrators whose zeal might otherwise have carried them to excesses not contemplated in legislation creating their offices.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Morton Salt Co.,338 U.\xc2\xa0S. 632,644 (1950). The Act guards against excesses in rulemaking by requiring notice and comment. Before an agency makes a rule, it normally must notify the public of the proposal, invite them to comment on its shortcomings, consider and respond to their arguments, and explain its final decision in a statement of the rule\xe2\x80\x99s basis and purpose.5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7553(b)\xe2\x80\x93(c); ante, at 2.

The APA exempts interpretive rules from these requirements. \xc2\xa7553(b)(A). But this concession to agencies was meant to be more modest in its effects than it is today. For despite exempting interpretive rules from notice and comment, the Act provides that \xe2\x80\x9cthe reviewing courtshall .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. interpret constitutional and statutory provisions, and determine the meaning or applicability of the terms of an agency action.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7706 (emphasis added). The Act thus contemplates that courts, not agencies, will authoritatively resolve ambiguities in statutes and regulations. In sucha regime, the exemption for interpretive rules does not add much to agency power. An agency may use interpretive rules to advise the public by explaining its interpretation of the law. But an agency may not use interpretive rules to bind the public by making law, because it remains the responsibility of the court to decide whether the law means what the agency says it means.

Heedless of the original design of the APA, we have developed an elaborate law of deference to agencies\xe2\x80\x99 interpretations of statutes and regulations. Never mentioning \xc2\xa7706\xe2\x80\x99s directive that the \xe2\x80\x9creviewing court .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. interpret .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. statutory provisions,\xe2\x80\x9d we have held that agencies may authoritatively resolve ambiguities in statutes. Chevron U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc.,467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 837\xe2\x80\x93843 (1984). And never mentioning \xc2\xa7706\xe2\x80\x99s directive that the \xe2\x80\x9creviewing court .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. determine the meaning or applicability of the terms of an agency action,\xe2\x80\x9d we have\xe2\x80\x94relying on a case decided before the APA, Bowles v. Seminole Rock & Sand Co.,325 U.\xc2\xa0S. 410 (1945)\xe2\x80\x94held that agencies may authoritatively resolve ambiguities in regulations. Auer v. Robbins,519 U.\xc2\xa0S. 452,461 (1997).

By supplementing the APA with judge-made doctrines of deference, we have revolutionized the import of interpretive rules\xe2\x80\x99 exemption from notice-and-comment rulemaking. Agencies may now use these rules not just to advise the public, but also to bind them. After all, if an interpretive rule gets deference, the people are bound to obey it on pain of sanction, no less surely than they are bound to obey substantive rules, which are accorded similar deference. Interpretive rules that command deference do have the force of law.

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasons for resisting this obvious point would not withstand a gentle breeze. Even when an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation gets deference, the Court argues, \xe2\x80\x9cit is the court that ultimately decides whether [the text] means what the agency says.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311, n.\xc2\xa04. That is not quite so. So long as the agency does not stray beyond the ambiguity in the text being interpreted, deference compels the reviewing court to \xe2\x80\x9cdecide\xe2\x80\x9d that the text means what the agency says. The Court continues that \xe2\x80\x9cdeference is not an inexorable command in all cases,\xe2\x80\x9d because (for example) it does not apply to plainly erroneous interpretations. Ibid. True, but beside the point. Saying all interpretive rules lack force of law because plainly erroneous interpretations do not bind courts is like saying all substantive rules lack force of law because arbitrary and capricious rules do not bind courts. Of course an interpretive rule must meet certain conditions before it gets deference\xe2\x80\x94the interpretation must, for instance, be reason-able\xe2\x80\x94but once it does so it is every bit as binding as a substantive rule. So the point stands: By deferring to interpretive rules, we have allowed agencies to make binding rules unhampered by notice-and-commentprocedures.

The problem is bad enough, and perhaps insoluble if Chevron is not to be uprooted, with respect to interpretive rules setting forth agency interpretation of statutes. But an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of its own regulations is an-other matter. By giving that category of interpretive rules Auer deference, we do more than allow the agency to make binding regulations without notice and comment. Because the agency (not Congress) drafts the substantive rules that are the object of those interpretations, giving them deference allows the agency to control the extent of its notice-and-comment-free domain. To expand this domain, the agency need only write substantive rules morebroadly and vaguely, leaving plenty of gaps to be filled in later, using interpretive rules unchecked by notice and comment. The APA does not remotely contemplate this regime.

Still and all, what are we to do about the problem? The Paralyzed Veterans doctrine is a courageous (indeed, brazen) attempt to limit the mischief by requiring an interpretive rule to go through notice and comment if it revises an earlier definitive interpretation of a regulation. That solution is unlawful for the reasons set forth in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion: It contradicts the APA\xe2\x80\x99s unqualified exemption of interpretive rules from notice-and-comment rulemaking.

But I think there is another solution\xe2\x80\x94one unavailable to the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit since it involves the overruling of one this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions (that being even a greater fault than merely ignoring the APA). As I have described elsewhere, the rule of Chevron, if it did not comport with the APA, at least was in conformity with the long history of judicial review of executive action, where \xe2\x80\x9c[s]tatutory ambiguities .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. were left to reasonable resolution by the Executive.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Mead Corp.,533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 218,243 (2001) (Scalia, J., dissenting). I am unaware of any such history justifying deference to agency interpretations of its own regulations. And there are weighty reasons to deny a lawgiver the power to write ambiguous laws and then be the judge of what the ambiguity means. See Decker v. Northwest Environmental Defense Center, 568 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2013) (Scalia, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part) (slip op., at 1\xe2\x80\x937). I would therefore restore the balance originally struck by the APA with respect to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of its own regulations, not by rewriting the Act in order to make up for Auer, but by abandoning Auer and applying the Act as written. The agency is free to interpret its own regulations with or without notice and comment; but courts will decide\xe2\x80\x94with no deference to the agency\xe2\x80\x94whether that interpretation is correct.
