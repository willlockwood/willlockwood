\nOCTOBER TERM, 2006
\nROCKWELL INT\'L CORP. V. UNITED STATES
\n

\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

ROCKWELL INTERNATIONAL CORP. et\xc2\xa0al. v. UNITED STATES et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the tenth circuit

\n

No. 05\xe2\x80\x931272.\xe2\x80\x83Argued December 5, 2006\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 27, 2007

\n

While employed as an engineer at a nuclear weapons plant run by petitioner Rockwell under a Government contract, respondent Stone predicted that Rockwell\xe2\x80\x99s system for creating solid \xe2\x80\x9cpondcrete\xe2\x80\x9d blocks from toxic pond sludge and cement would not work because of problems in piping the sludge. However, Rockwell successfully made such blocks and discovered \xe2\x80\x9cinsolid\xe2\x80\x9d ones only after Stone was laid off in 1986. In 1989, Stone filed a qui tam suit under the False Claims Act, which prohibits submitting false or fraudulent payment claims to the United States, 31 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73729(a); permits remedial civil actions to be brought by the Attorney General, \xc2\xa73730(a), or by private individuals in the Government\xe2\x80\x99s name, \xc2\xa73730(b)(1); but eliminates federal-court jurisdiction over actions \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon the public disclosure of allegations or transactions \xe2\x80\xa6 , unless the action is brought by the Attorney General or the person bringing the action is an original source of the information,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73730(e)(4)(A). An \xe2\x80\x9coriginal source\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9chas direct and independent knowledge of the information on which the allegations are based and has voluntarily provided the information to the Government before filing an action \xe2\x80\xa6 based on the information.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73730(e)(4)(B). In 1996, the Government intervened, and, with Stone, filed an amended complaint, which did not allege that Stone\xe2\x80\x99s predicted piping-system defect caused the insolid blocks. Nor was such defect mentioned in a statement of claims included in the final pretrial order, which instead alleged that the pondcrete failed because a new foreman used an insufficient cement-to-sludge ratio. The jury found for respondents with respect to claims covering the pondcrete allegations, but found for Rockwell with respect to all other claims. The District Court denied Rockwell\xe2\x80\x99s postverdict motion to dismiss Stone\xe2\x80\x99s claims, finding that Stone was an original source. The Tenth Circuit affirmed in part, but remanded for the District Court to determine whether Stone had disclosed his information to the Government before filing the action. The District Court found Stone\xe2\x80\x99s disclosure inadequate, but the Tenth Circuit disagreed and held that Stone was an original source.

\n

Held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0Section 3730(e)(4)\xe2\x80\x99s original-source requirement is jurisdictional. Thus, regardless of whether Rockwell conceded Stone\xe2\x80\x99s original-source status, this Court must decide whether Stone meets this jurisdictional requirement. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0Because Stone does not meet \xc2\xa73730(e)(4)(B)\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that a relator have \xe2\x80\x9cdirect and independent knowledge of the information on which the allegations are based,\xe2\x80\x9d he is not an original source. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The \xe2\x80\x9cinformation\xe2\x80\x9d to which subparagraph (B) speaks is the information on which the relator\xe2\x80\x99s allegations are based rather than the information on which the publicly disclosed allegations that triggered the public-disclosure bar are based. The subparagraph standing on its own suggests that disposition. And those \xe2\x80\x9callegations\xe2\x80\x9d are not the same as the allegations referred to in subparagraph (A), which bars actions based on the \xe2\x80\x9cpublic disclosure of allegations or transactions\xe2\x80\x9d with an exception for cases brought by \xe2\x80\x9can original source of the information.\xe2\x80\x9d Had Congress wanted to link original-source status to information underlying public disclosure it would have used the identical phrase, \xe2\x80\x9callegations or transactions.\xe2\x80\x9d Furthermore, it is difficult to understand why Congress would care whether a relator knows about the information underlying a publicly disclosed allegation when the relator has direct and independent knowledge of different information supporting the same allegation. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0In determining which \xe2\x80\x9callegations\xe2\x80\x9d are relevant, that term is not limited to \xe2\x80\x9callegations\xe2\x80\x9d in the original complaint, but includes the allegations as amended. The statute speaks of the relator\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9callegations,\xe2\x80\x9d simpliciter. Absent some limitation of \xc2\xa73730(e)(4)\xe2\x80\x99s requirement to the initial complaint, this Court will not infer one. Here, where the final pretrial order superseded prior pleadings, this Court looks to the final pretrial order to determine original-source status. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Judged according to these principles, Stone\xe2\x80\x99s knowledge falls short. The only false claims found by the jury involved insolid pondcrete discovered after Stone left his employment. Thus, he did not know that the pondcrete had failed; he predicted it. And his prediction was a failed one, for Stone believed the piping system was defective when, in fact, the pondcrete problem would be caused by a foreman\xe2\x80\x99s actions after Stone had left the plant. Stone\xe2\x80\x99s original-source status with respect to a separate, spray-irrigation claim did not provide jurisdiction over all of his claims. Section 3730(e)(4) does not permit jurisdiction in gross just because a relator is an original source with respect to some claim. Pp.\xc2\xa017\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa03.\xc2\xa0The Government\xe2\x80\x99s intervention in this case did not provide an independent basis of jurisdiction with respect to Stone. The statute draws a sharp distinction between actions brought by a private person under \xc2\xa73730(b) and actions brought by the Attorney General under \xc2\xa73730(b). An action originally brought by a private person, which the Attorney General has joined, becomes an action brought by the Attorney General only after the private person has been ousted. Pp.\xc2\xa018\xe2\x80\x9320.

\n

92 Fed. Appx. 708, reversed.

ROCKWELL INTERNATIONAL CORP., et\xc2\xa0al.,\nPETITIONERS v. UNITED STATES et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the tenth circuit

\n

[March 27, 2007]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The False Claims Act, 31 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73729\xe2\x80\x933733, eliminates federal-court jurisdiction over actions under \xc2\xa73730 of the Act that are based upon the public disclosure of allegations or transactions \xe2\x80\x9cunless the action is brought by the Attorney General or the person bringing the action is an original source of the information.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73730(e)(4)(A). We decide whether respondent James Stone was an original source.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The mixture of concrete and pond sludge that is the subject of this case has taken nearly two decades to seep, so to speak, into this Court. Given the long history and the complexity of this litigation, it is well to describe the facts in some detail.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0From 1975 through 1989, petitioner Rockwell International Corp. was under a management and operating contract with the Department of Energy (DOE) to run the Rocky Flats nuclear weapons plant in Colorado. The most significant portion of Rockwell\xe2\x80\x99s compensation came in the form of a semiannual \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98award fee,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the amount of which depended on DOE\xe2\x80\x99s evaluation of Rockwell\xe2\x80\x99s performance in a number of areas, including environmental, safety, and health concerns. United States ex rel. Stone v. Rockwell Int\xe2\x80\x99l, Corp., 92 Fed. Appx. 708, 714 (CA10 2004).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0From November 1980 through March 1986, James Stone worked as an engineer at the Rocky Flats plant. In the early 1980\xe2\x80\x99s, Rockwell explored the possibility of disposing of the toxic pond sludge that accumulated in solar evaporation ponds at the facility, by mixing it with cement. The idea was to pour the mixture into large rectangular boxes, where it would solidify into \xe2\x80\x9cpondcrete\xe2\x80\x9d blocks that could be stored onsite or transported to other sites for disposal.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Stone reviewed a proposed manufacturing process for pondcrete in 1982. He concluded that the proposal \xe2\x80\x9cwould not work,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 175, and communicated that conclusion to Rockwell management in a written \xe2\x80\x9cEngineering Order.\xe2\x80\x9d As Stone would later explain, he believed \xe2\x80\x9cthe suggested process would result in an unstable mixture that would later deteriorate and cause unwanted release of toxic wastes to the environment.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. He believed this because he \xe2\x80\x9cforesaw that the piping system\xe2\x80\x9d that extracted sludge from the solar ponds \xe2\x80\x9cwould not properly remove the sludge and would lead to an inadequate mixture of sludge/waste and cement such that the \xe2\x80\x98pond crete\xe2\x80\x99 blocks would rapidly disintegrate thus creating additional contamination problems.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 290.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Notwithstanding Stone\xe2\x80\x99s prediction, Rockwell proceeded with its pondcrete project and successfully manufactured \xe2\x80\x9cconcrete hard\xe2\x80\x9d pondcrete during the period of Stone\xe2\x80\x99s employment at Rocky Flats. It was only after Stone was laid off in March 1986 that what the parties have called \xe2\x80\x9cinsolid\xe2\x80\x9d pondcrete blocks were discovered. According to respondents, Rockwell knew by October 1986 that a substantial number of pondcrete blocks were insolid, but DOE did not become aware of the problem until May 1988, when several pondcrete blocks began to leak, leading to the discovery of thousands of other insolid blocks. The media reported these discoveries, 3 Appellants\xe2\x80\x99 App. in Nos. 99\xe2\x80\x931351, 99\xe2\x80\x931352, 99\xe2\x80\x931353 (CA10), pp. 889\xe2\x80\x9338 to 889\xe2\x80\x9339; and attributed the malfunction to Rockwell\xe2\x80\x99s reduction of the ratio of concrete to sludge in the mixture.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In June 1987, more than a year after he had left Rockwell\xe2\x80\x99s employ, Stone went to the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) with allegations of environmental crimes at Rocky Flats during the time of his employment. According to the court below, Stone alleged that

\n

\xe2\x80\x9ccontrary to public knowledge, Rocky Flats accepted hazardous and nuclear waste from other DOE facilities; that Rockwell employees were \xe2\x80\x98forbidden from discussing any controversies in front of a DOE employee\xe2\x80\x99; that although Rocky Flats\xe2\x80\x99 fluid bed incinerators failed testing in 1981, the pilot incinerator remained on line and was used to incinerate wastes daily since 1981, including plutonium wastes which were then sent out for burial; that Rockwell distilled and fractionated various oils and solvents although the wastes were geared for incineration; that Stone believed that the ground water was contaminated from previous waste burial and land application, and that hazardous waste lagoons tended to overflow during and after \xe2\x80\x98a good rain,\xe2\x80\x99 causing hazardous wastes to be discharged without first being treated.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 4a.

\n

\nStone provided the FBI with 2,300 pages of documents, buried among which was his 1982 engineering report predicting that the pondcrete-system design would not work. Stone did not discuss his pondcrete allegations with the FBI in their conversations.[Footnote 1]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Based in part on information allegedly learned from Stone, the Government obtained a search warrant for Rocky Flats, and on June 6, 1989, 75 FBI and Environmental Protection Agency agents raided the facility. The affidavit in support of the warrant included allegations (1) that pondcrete blocks were insolid \xe2\x80\x9cdue to an inadequate waste-concrete mixture,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 429, (2) that Rockwell obtained award fees based on its alleged \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98excellent\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d management of Rocky Flats, id., at 98, and (3) that Rockwell made false statements and concealed material facts in violation of the Resource Conservation and Recovery Act of 1976 (RCRA), 90 Stat. 2811, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa76928, and 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71001. Newspapers published these allegations. In March 1992, Rockwell pleaded guilty to 10 environmental violations, including the knowing storage of insolid pondcrete blocks in violation of RCRA. Rockwell agreed to pay $18.5 million in fines.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In July 1989, Stone filed a qui tam suit under the False Claims Act.[Footnote 2] That Act prohibits false or fraudulent claims for payment to the United States, 31 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73729(a), and authorizes civil actions to remedy such fraud to be brought by the Attorney General, \xc2\xa73730(a), or by private individuals in the Government\xe2\x80\x99s name, \xc2\xa73730(b)(1). The Act provides, however, that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o court shall have jurisdiction over an action under this section based upon the public disclosure of allegations or transactions \xe2\x80\xa6 from the news media, unless the action is brought by the Attorney General or the person bringing the action is an original source of the information.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73730(e)(4)(A). An \xe2\x80\x9coriginal source\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9can individual who has direct and independent knowledge of the information on which the allegations are based and has voluntarily provided the information to the Government before filing an action under this section which is based on the information.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73730(e)(4)(B).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Stone\xe2\x80\x99s complaint alleged that Rockwell was required to comply with certain federal and state environmental laws and regulations, including RCRA; that Rockwell committed numerous violations of these laws and regulations throughout the 1980\xe2\x80\x99s[Footnote 3]; and that, in order to induce the Government to make payments or approvals under Rockwell\xe2\x80\x99s contract, Rockwell knowingly presented false and fraudulent claims to the Government in violation of the False Claims Act, 31 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73729(a). As required under the Act, Stone filed his complaint under seal and simultaneously delivered to the Government a confidential disclosure statement describing \xe2\x80\x9csubstantially all material evidence and information\xe2\x80\x9d in his possession, \xc2\xa73730(b)(2). The statement identified 26 environmental and safety issues, only one of which involved pondcrete. With respect to that issue, Stone explained in his statement that he had reviewed the design for the pondcrete system and had foreseen that the piping mechanism would not properly remove the sludge, which in turn would lead to an inadequate mixture of sludge and cement.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In December 1992, Rockwell moved to dismiss Stone\xe2\x80\x99s action for lack of subject-matter jurisdiction, arguing that the action was based on publicly disclosed allegations and that Stone was not an original source. The District Court denied the motion because, in its view, \xe2\x80\x9cStone had direct and independent knowledge that Rockwell\xe2\x80\x99s compensation was linked to its compliance with environmental, health and safety regulations and that it allegedly concealed its deficient performance so that it would continue to receive payments.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 61a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government initially declined to intervene in Stone\xe2\x80\x99s action, but later reversed course, and in November 1996, the District Court granted the Government\xe2\x80\x99s intervention. Several weeks later, at the suggestion of the District Court, the Government and Stone filed a joint amended complaint. As relevant here, the amended complaint alleged that Rockwell violated RCRA by storing leaky pondcrete blocks, but did not allege that any defect in the piping system (as predicted by Stone) caused insolid pondcrete.[Footnote 4] Respondents clarified their allegations even further in a statement of claims which became part of the final pretrial order and which superseded their earlier pleadings. This said that the pondcrete\xe2\x80\x99s insolidity was due to \xe2\x80\x9can incorrect cement/sludge ratio used in pondcrete operations, as well as due to inadequate process controls and inadequate inspection procedures.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 470. It continued:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cDuring the winter of 1986, Rockwell replaced its then pondcrete foreman, Norman Fryback, with Ron Teel. Teel increased pondcrete production rates in part by, among other things, reducing the amount of cement added to the blocks. Following the May 23, 1988 spill, Rockwell acknowledged that this reduced cement-to-sludge ratio was a major contributor to the existence of insufficiently solid pondcrete blocks on the storage pads.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 476\xe2\x80\x93477.

\n

\nThe statement of claims again did not mention the piping problem asserted by Stone years earlier.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 False Claims Act claims went to trial in 1999. None of the witnesses Stone had identified during discovery as having relevant knowledge testified at trial. And none of the documents Stone provided to the Government with his confidential disclosure statement was introduced in evidence at trial. Nor did respondents allege at trial that the defect in the piping system predicted by Stone caused insolid pondcrete. To the contrary, during closing arguments both Stone\xe2\x80\x99s counsel and the Government\xe2\x80\x99s counsel repeatedly explained to the jury that the pondcrete failed because Rockwell\xe2\x80\x99s new foreman used an insufficient cement-to-sludge ratio in an effort to increase pondcrete production.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The verdict form divided the False Claims Act count into several different claims corresponding to different award-fee periods. The jury found in favor of respondents for the three periods covering the pondcrete allegations (April 1, 1987, to September 30, 1988), and found for Rockwell as to the remaining periods. The jury awarded damages of $1,390,775.80, which the District Court trebled pursuant to 31 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73729(a).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Rockwell filed a postverdict motion to dismiss Stone\xe2\x80\x99s claims under \xc2\xa73730(e)(4), arguing that the claims were based on publicly disclosed allegations and that Stone was not an original source. In response, Stone acknowledged that his successful claims were based on publicly disclosed allegations, but asserted original-source status. The District Court agreed with Stone. The United States Court of Appeals for the Tenth Circuit affirmed in relevant part, but remanded the case for the District Court to determine whether Stone had disclosed his information to the Government before filing his qui tam action, as \xc2\xa73730(e)(4)(B) required. On remand, the District Court found that Stone had produced the 1982 engineering order to the Government, but that the order was insufficient to communicate Stone\xe2\x80\x99s allegations. The District Court also found that Stone had not carried his burden of proving that he orally informed the FBI about his allegations before filing suit. On appeal, the Tenth Circuit disagreed with the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion and held (over the dissent of Judge Briscoe) that the 1982 engineering order sufficed to carry Stone\xe2\x80\x99s burden of persuasion. 92 Fed. Appx. 708. We granted certiorari, 548 U. S. ____ (2006), to decide whether Stone was an original source.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 3730(e)(4)(A) provides that

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[n]o court shall have jurisdiction over an action under this section based upon the public disclosure of allegations or transactions in a criminal, civil, or administrative hearing, in a congressional, administrative, or Government Accounting Office report, hearing, audit, or investigation, or from the news media, unless the action is brought by the Attorney General or the person bringing the action is an original source of the information.\xe2\x80\x9d (Footnote omitted.)

\n

\nAs discussed above, \xc2\xa73730(e)(4)(B) defines \xe2\x80\x9coriginal source\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9can individual who [1] has direct and independent knowledge of the information on which the allegations are based and [2] has voluntarily provided the information to the Government before filing an action under this section which is based on the information.\xe2\x80\x9d As this case comes to the Court, it is conceded that the claims on which Stone prevailed were based upon publicly disclosed allegations within the meaning of \xc2\xa73730(e)(4)(A). The question is whether Stone qualified under the original-source exception to the public-disclosure bar.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We begin with the possibility that little analysis is required in this case, for Stone asserts that Rockwell conceded his original-source status. Rockwell responds that it conceded no such thing and that, even had it done so, the concession would have been irrelevant because \xc2\xa73730(e)(4) is jurisdictional. We agree with the latter proposition. It is true enough that the word \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d does not in every context connote subject-matter jurisdiction. Noting that \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a word of many, too many, meanings,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d we concluded in Steel Co. v. Citizens for Better Environment, 523 U. S. 83 (1998), that establishing the elements of an offense was not made a jurisdictional matter merely because the statute creating the cause of action was phrased as providing for \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d over such suits. Id., at 90 (quoting United States v. Vanness, 85 F.\xc2\xa03d 661, 663, n.\xc2\xa02 (CADC 1996)). Here, however, the issue is not whether casting the creation of a cause of action in jurisdictional terms somehow limits the general grant of jurisdiction under which that cause of action would normally be brought, but rather whether a clear and explicit withdrawal of jurisdiction withdraws jurisdiction. It undoubtedly does so. Just last Term we stated that, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f the Legislature clearly states that a threshold limitation on a statute\xe2\x80\x99s scope shall count as jurisdictional, the courts and litigants will be duly instructed and will not be left to wrestle with the issue.\xe2\x80\x9d Arbaugh v. Y & H Corp., 546 U. S. 500, 515\xe2\x80\x93516 (2006) (footnote omitted). Here the jurisdictional nature of the original-source requirement is clear ex visceribus verborum. Indeed, we have already stated that \xc2\xa73730(e)(4) speaks to \xe2\x80\x9cthe power of a particular court\xe2\x80\x9d as well as \xe2\x80\x9cthe substantive rights of the parties.\xe2\x80\x9d Hughes Aircraft Co. v. United States ex rel. Schumer, 520 U. S. 939, 951 (1997).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Stone\xe2\x80\x99s contrary position rests entirely on dicta from a single Court of Appeals decision, see United States ex rel. Fallon v. Accudyne Corp., 97 F.\xc2\xa03d 937, 940\xe2\x80\x93941 (CA7 1996). Accudyne thought it significant that jurisdiction over False Claims Act cases is conferred by 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71331 and 1345 (the federal-question and United-States-as-plaintiff provisions of the Judicial Code) and 31 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73732(a) (the provision of the False Claims Act establishing federal-court venue and conferring federal-court jurisdiction over related state-law claims), rather than \xc2\xa73730, which is the \xe2\x80\x9csection\xe2\x80\x9d referenced in \xc2\xa73730(e)(4). To eliminate jurisdiction, the court believed, it is those jurisdiction-conferring sections that would have to be referenced. We know of nothing in logic or authority to support this. The jurisdiction-removing provision here does not say \xe2\x80\x9cno court shall have jurisdiction under this section,\xe2\x80\x9d but rather \xe2\x80\x9cno court shall have jurisdiction over an action under this section.\xe2\x80\x9d That is surely the most natural way to achieve the desired result of eliminating jurisdiction over a category of False Claims Act actions\xe2\x80\x94rather than listing all the conceivable provisions of the United States Code whose conferral of jurisdiction is being eliminated. (In addition to the provisions cited by the Accudyne court, one might also have to mention the diversity-jurisdiction provision, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71332, and the supplemental-jurisdiction provision, \xc2\xa71367.) Accudyne next observed that the public-disclosure bar limits only who may speak for the United States on a subject and who if anyone gets a financial reward, not the \xe2\x80\x9ccategories of disputes that may be resolved (a real \xe2\x80\x98jurisdictional\xe2\x80\x99 limit).\xe2\x80\x9d 97 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 941. But this is a classic begging of the question, which is precisely whether there has been removed from the courts\xe2\x80\x99 jurisdiction that category of disputes consisting of False Claims Act qui tam suits based on publicly disclosed allegations as to which the relator is not an original source of the information. Nothing prevents Congress from defining the \xe2\x80\x9ccategory\xe2\x80\x9d of excluded suits in any manner it wishes. See, e.g., 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71500 (no jurisdiction over \xe2\x80\x9cany claim for or in respect to which the plaintiff . . . has pending in any other court any suit \xe2\x80\xa6 against the United States\xe2\x80\x9d). Lastly, Accudyne asserted that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Supreme Court had held that a similar reference to jurisdiction in the Norris-LaGuardia Act, 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7101, 104, limits remedies rather than subject-matter jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d 97 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 941 (citing Burlington Northern R. Co. v. Maintenance of Way Employes, 481 U. S. 429, 444\xe2\x80\x93446 (1987)). But the language of the Norris-LaGuardia Act is in fact not similar. It provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o court of the United States shall have jurisdiction to issue any restraining order or temporary or permanent injunction in any case involving or growing out of any labor dispute \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7104 (emphasis added). It is facially a limitation upon the relief that can be accorded, not a removal of jurisdiction over \xe2\x80\x9cany case involving or growing out of a labor dispute.\xe2\x80\x9d Here, by contrast, the text says \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o court shall have jurisdiction over an action under this section.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Whether the point was conceded or not, therefore, we may, and indeed must, decide whether Stone met the jurisdictional requirement of being an original source.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We turn to the first requirement of original-source status, that the relator have \xe2\x80\x9cdirect and independent knowledge of the information on which the allegations are based.\xe2\x80\x9d 31 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73730(e)(4)(B). Because we have not previously addressed this provision, several preliminary questions require our attention.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, does the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cinformation on which the allegations are based\xe2\x80\x9d refer to the information on which the relator\xe2\x80\x99s allegations are based or the information on which the publicly disclosed allegations that triggered the public-disclosure bar are based? The parties agree it is the former. See Brief for Petitioners 26, n.\xc2\xa013; Brief for United States 24, and n.\xc2\xa08; Brief for Respondent Stone 15, 21. But in view of our conclusion that \xc2\xa73730(e)(4) is jurisdictional, we must satisfy ourselves that the parties\xe2\x80\x99 position is correct.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Though the question is hardly free from doubt,[Footnote 5] we agree that the \xe2\x80\x9cinformation\xe2\x80\x9d to which subparagraph (B) speaks is the information upon which the relators\xe2\x80\x99 allegations are based. To begin with, subparagraph (B) standing on its own suggests that disposition. The relator must have \xe2\x80\x9cdirect and independent knowledge of the information on which the allegations are based,\xe2\x80\x9d and he must \xe2\x80\x9cprovid[e] the information to the Government before filing an action under this section which is based on the information.\xe2\x80\x9d Surely the information one would expect a relator to \xe2\x80\x9cprovide to the Government before filing an action \xe2\x80\xa6 based on the information\xe2\x80\x9d is the information underlying the relator\xe2\x80\x99s claims.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Subparagraph (A) complicates matters. As described earlier, it bars actions based on the \xe2\x80\x9cpublic disclosure of allegations or transactions\xe2\x80\x9d and provides an exception for cases brought by \xe2\x80\x9can original source of the information.\xe2\x80\x9d If the allegations referred to in subparagraph (B)\xe2\x80\x99s phrase requiring \xe2\x80\x9cdirect and independent knowledge of the information on which the allegations are based,\xe2\x80\x9d are the same \xe2\x80\x9callegations\xe2\x80\x9d referred to in subparagraph (A), then original-source status would depend on knowledge of information underlying the publicly disclosed allegations. The principal textual difficulty with that interpretation is that subparagraph (A) does not speak simply of \xe2\x80\x9callegations,\xe2\x80\x9d but of \xe2\x80\x9callegations or transactions.\xe2\x80\x9d Had Congress wanted to link original-source status to information underlying the public disclosure, it would surely have used the identical phrase, \xe2\x80\x9callegations or transactions\xe2\x80\x9d; there is no conceivable reason to require direct and independent knowledge of publicly disclosed allegations but not of publicly disclosed transactions.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The sense of the matter offers strong additional support for this interpretation. Section 3730(e)(4)(A) bars actions based on publicly disclosed allegations whether or not the information on which those allegations are based has been made public. It is difficult to understand why Congress would care whether a relator knows about the information underlying a publicly disclosed allegation (e.g., what a confidential source told a newspaper reporter about insolid pondcrete) when the relator has direct and independent knowledge of different information supporting the same allegation (e.g., that a defective process would inevitably lead to insolid pondcrete). Not only would that make little sense, it would raise nettlesome procedural problems, placing courts in the position of comparing the relator\xe2\x80\x99s information with the often unknowable information on which the public disclosure was based. Where that latter information has not been disclosed (by reason, for example, of a reporter\xe2\x80\x99s desire to protect his source), the relator would presumably be out of court. To bar a relator with direct and independent knowledge of information underlying his allegations just because no one can know what information underlies the similar allegations of some other person simply makes no sense.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The contrary conclusion of some lower courts rests on the following logic: The term \xe2\x80\x9cinformation\xe2\x80\x9d in subparagraph (B) must be read in tandem with the term \xe2\x80\x9cinformation\xe2\x80\x9d in subparagraph (A), and the term \xe2\x80\x9cinformation\xe2\x80\x9d in subparagraph (A) refers to the information on which the publicly disclosed allegations are based. See, e.g., United States ex rel. Laird v. Lockheed Martin Eng. & Science Servs. Co., 336 F.\xc2\xa03d 346, 354 (CA5 2003). The major premise of this reasoning seems true enough: \xe2\x80\x9cinformation\xe2\x80\x9d in (A) and (B) means the same thing. The minor premise, however\xe2\x80\x94that \xe2\x80\x9cinformation\xe2\x80\x9d in (A) refers to the information underlying the publicly disclosed allegations or transactions\xe2\x80\x94is highly questionable. The complete phrase at issue is \xe2\x80\x9cunless \xe2\x80\xa6 the person bringing the action is an original source of the information.\xe2\x80\x9d It seems to us more likely (in light of the analysis set forth above) that the information in question is the information underlying the action referred to a few words earlier, to-wit, the action \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon the public disclosure of allegations or transactions\xe2\x80\x9d referred to at the beginning of the provision. On this interpretation, \xe2\x80\x9cinformation\xe2\x80\x9d in subparagraph (A) and \xe2\x80\x9cinformation on which the allegations are based\xe2\x80\x9d in subparagraph (B) are one and the same, viz., information underlying the allegations of the relator\xe2\x80\x99s action.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Having determined that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cinformation on which the allegations are based\xe2\x80\x9d refers to the relator\xe2\x80\x99s allegations and not the publicly disclosed allegations, we confront more textual ambiguity: Which of the relator\xe2\x80\x99s allegations are the relevant ones? Stone\xe2\x80\x99s allegations changed during the course of the litigation, yet he asks that we look only to his original complaint. Rockwell argues that Stone must satisfy the original-source exception through all stages of the litigation.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In our view, the term \xe2\x80\x9callegations\xe2\x80\x9d is not limited to the allegations of the original complaint. It includes (at a minimum) the allegations in the original complaint as amended. The statute speaks not of the allegations in the \xe2\x80\x9coriginal complaint\xe2\x80\x9d (or even the allegations in the \xe2\x80\x9ccomplaint\xe2\x80\x9d), but of the relator\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9callegations\xe2\x80\x9d simpliciter. Absent some limitation of \xc2\xa73730(e)(4)\xe2\x80\x99s requirement to the relator\xe2\x80\x99s initial complaint, we will not infer one. Such a limitation would leave the relator free to plead a trivial theory of fraud for which he had some direct and independent knowledge and later amend the complaint to include theories copied from the public domain or from materials in the Government\xe2\x80\x99s possession. Even the Government concedes that new allegations regarding a fundamentally different fraudulent scheme require reevaluation of the court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction. See Brief for United States 40; Tr. of Oral Arg. 40.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The rule that subject-matter jurisdiction \xe2\x80\x9cdepends on the state of things at the time of the action brought,\xe2\x80\x9d Mollan v. Torrance, 9 Wheat. 537, 539 (1824), does not suggest a different interpretation. The state of things and the originally alleged state of things are not synonymous; demonstration that the original allegations were false will defeat jurisdiction. Anderson v. Watt, 138 U. S. 694, 701 (1891); Morris v. Gilmer, 129 U. S. 315, 326 (1889). So also will the withdrawal of those allegations, unless they are replaced by others that establish jurisdiction. Thus, when a plaintiff files a complaint in federal court and then voluntarily amends the complaint, courts look to the amended complaint to determine jurisdiction. See Wellness Community-Nat. v. Wellness House, 70 F.\xc2\xa03d 46, 49 (CA7 1995); Boelens v. Redman Homes, Inc., 759 F.\xc2\xa02d 504, 508 (CA5 1984).[Footnote 6]

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Here, we have not only an amended complaint, but a final pretrial order that superseded all prior pleadings and \xe2\x80\x9ccontroll[ed] the subsequent course of the action,\xe2\x80\x9d Fed. Rule Civ. Proc.\xc2\xa016(e). See Curtis v. Loether, 415 U. S. 189, 190, n. 1 (1974) (where a claim was not included in the complaint, but was included in the pretrial order, \xe2\x80\x9cit is irrelevant that the pleadings were never formally amended\xe2\x80\x9d (citing Fed. Rules Civ. Proc.\xc2\xa015(b), 16)); Wilson v. Muckala, 303 F.\xc2\xa03d 1207, 1215 (CA10 2002) (\xe2\x80\x9c[C]laims, issues, defenses, or theories of damages not included in the pretrial order are waived even if they appeared in the complaint and, conversely, the inclusion of a claim in the pretrial order is deemed to amend any previous pleadings which did not include that claim\xe2\x80\x9d); Syrie v. Knoll Int\xe2\x80\x99l, 748 F.\xc2\xa02d 304, 308 (CA5 1984) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]ncorporation of a [new] claim into the pre-trial order \xe2\x80\xa6 amends the previous pleadings to state [the new] claim\xe2\x80\x9d). In these circumstances, we look to the allegations as amended\xe2\x80\x94here, the statement of claims in the final pretrial order\xe2\x80\x94to determine original-source status.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government objects that this approach risks driving a wedge between the Government and relators. It worries that future relators might decline to \xe2\x80\x9cacquiesc[e]\xe2\x80\x9d in the Government\xe2\x80\x99s tactical decision to narrow the claims in a case if that would eliminate jurisdiction with respect to the relator. Brief for United States 44. Even if this policy concern were valid, it would not induce us to determine jurisdiction on the basis of whether the relator is an original source of information underlying allegations that he no longer makes.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Judged according to the principles set forth above, Stone\xe2\x80\x99s knowledge falls short. The only false claims ultimately found by the jury (and hence the only ones to which our jurisdictional inquiry is pertinent to the outcome) involved false statements with respect to environmental, safety, and health compliance over a one-and-a-half-year period between April 1, 1987, and September 30, 1988. As described by Stone and the Government in the final pretrial order, the only pertinent problem with respect to this period of time for which Stone claimed to have direct and independent knowledge was insolid pondcrete. Because Stone was no longer employed by Rockwell at the time, he did not know that the pondcrete was insolid; he did not know that pondcrete storage was even subject to RCRA; he did not know that Rockwell would fail to remedy the defect; he did not know that the insolid pondcrete leaked while being stored onsite; and, of course, he did not know that Rockwell made false statements to the Government regarding pondcrete storage.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Stone\xe2\x80\x99s prediction that the pondcrete would be insolid because of a flaw in the piping system does not qualify as \xe2\x80\x9cdirect and independent knowledge\xe2\x80\x9d of the pondcrete defect. Of course a qui tam relator\xe2\x80\x99s misunderstanding of why a concealed defect occurred would normally be immaterial as long as he knew the defect actually existed. But here Stone did not know that the pondcrete failed; he predicted it. Even if a prediction can qualify as direct and independent knowledge in some cases (a point we need not address), it assuredly does not do so when its premise of cause and effect is wrong. Stone\xe2\x80\x99s prediction was a failed prediction, disproved by Stone\xe2\x80\x99s own allegations. As Stone acknowledged, Rockwell was able to produce \xe2\x80\x9cconcrete hard\xe2\x80\x9d pondcrete using the machinery Stone said was defective. According to respondents\xe2\x80\x99 allegations in the final pretrial order, the insolidity problem was caused by a new foreman\xe2\x80\x99s reduction of the cement-to-sludge ratio in the winter of 1986, long after Stone had left Rocky Flats.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Stone counters that his original-source status with respect to his spray-irrigation claim (which related to a time period different from that for his pondcrete claim, App. 492) provided jurisdiction with respect to all of his claims. We disagree. Section 3730(e)(4) does not permit jurisdiction in gross just because a relator is an original source with respect to some claim. We, along with every court to have addressed the question, conclude that \xc2\xa73730(e)(4) does not permit such claim smuggling. See United States ex rel. Merena v. SmithKline Beecham Corp., 205 F.\xc2\xa03d 97, 102 (CA3 2000); Hays v. Hoffman, 325 F.\xc2\xa03d 982, 990 (CA8 2003); Wang ex rel. United States v. FMC Corp., 975 F. 2d 1412, 1415\xe2\x80\x931416, 1420 (CA9 1992). As then-Judge Alito explained, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s decision to join all of his or her claims in a single lawsuit should not rescue claims that would have been doomed by section (e)(4) if they had been asserted in a separate action. And likewise, this joinder should not result in the dismissal of claims that would have otherwise survived.\xe2\x80\x9d SmithKline Beecham, supra, at 102.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because Stone did not have direct and independent knowledge of the information upon which his allegations were based, we need not decide whether Stone met the second requirement of original-source status, that he have voluntarily provided the information to the Government before filing his action.

\n

V

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents contend that even if Stone failed the original-source test as to his pondcrete allegations, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s intervention in his case provided an independent basis of jurisdiction. Section 3730(e)(4)(A) permits jurisdiction over an action based on publicly disclosed allegations or transactions if the action is \xe2\x80\x9cbrought by the Attorney General.\xe2\x80\x9d Respondents say that any inquiry into Stone\xe2\x80\x99s original-source status with respect to amendments to the complaint was unnecessary because the Government had intervened, making this an \xe2\x80\x9caction brought by the Attorney General.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 7] Even assuming that Stone was an original source of allegations in his initial complaint, we reject respondents\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cintervention\xe2\x80\x9d argument.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The False Claims Act contemplates two types of actions. First, under \xc2\xa73730(a), \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f the Attorney General finds that a person has violated or is violating section 3729, the Attorney General may bring a civil action under this section against the person.\xe2\x80\x9d Second, under \xc2\xa73730(b), \xe2\x80\x9c[a] person may bring an action for a violation of section 3729 for the person and for the United States Government.\xe2\x80\x9d When a private person brings an action under \xc2\xa73730(b), the Government may elect to \xe2\x80\x9cproceed with the action,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73730(b)(4)(A), or it may \xe2\x80\x9cdeclin[e] to take over the action, in which case the person bringing the action shall have the right to conduct the action,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73730(b)(4)(B). The statute thus draws a sharp distinction between actions brought by the Attorney General under \xc2\xa73730(a) and actions brought by a private person under \xc2\xa73730(b). An action brought by a private person does not become one brought by the Government just because the Government intervenes and elects to \xe2\x80\x9cproceed with the action.\xe2\x80\x9d Section 3730 elsewhere refers to the Government\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cproceed[ing] with an action brought by a person under subsection (b)\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94which makes crystal clear the distinction between actions brought by the Government and actions brought by a relator where the Government intervenes but does not oust the relator.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Does this conclusion cast into doubt the courts\xe2\x80\x99 jurisdiction with respect to the Government as well? After all, \xc2\xa73730(e)(4)(A) bars jurisdiction over any action brought under \xc2\xa73730, as this one was, unless the action is brought (1) by the Attorney General or (2) by an original source; and we have concluded that this is brought by neither. Not even petitioners have suggested the bizarre result that the Government\xe2\x80\x99s judgment must be set aside. It is readily enough avoided, as common sense suggests it must be, by holding that an action originally brought by a private person, which the Attorney General has joined, becomes an action brought by the Attorney General once the private person has been determined to lack the jurisdictional prerequisites for suit. The outcome would be similar to that frequently produced in diversity-jurisdiction cases, where the \xe2\x80\x9ccourts of appeals \xe2\x80\xa6 have the authority to cure a jurisdictional defect by dismissing a dispensable nondiverse party.\xe2\x80\x9d Grupo Dataflux v. Atlas Global Group, L.\xc2\xa0P., 541 U. S. 567, 573 (2004) (citing Newman-Green, Inc. v. Alfonzo-Larrain, 490 U. S. 826, 837 (1989)); see United States Steel Corp. v. EPA, 614 F.\xc2\xa02d 843, 845 (CA3 1979) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]here are instances when an intervenor\xe2\x80\x99s claim does not rise and fall with the claim of the original party\xe2\x80\x9d); 7C C. Wright, A. Miller, & M. Kane, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa71920, p. 491 (2d ed. 1986) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]n intervenor can proceed to decision after a dismissal of the original action \xe2\x80\xa6 if there are independent grounds for jurisdiction of the intervenor\xe2\x80\x99s claim\xe2\x80\x9d). What is cured here, by the jurisdictional ruling regarding Stone\xe2\x80\x99s claim, is the characterization of the action as one brought by an original source. The elimination of Stone leaves in place an action pursued only by the Attorney General, that can reasonably be regarded as being \xe2\x80\x9cbrought\xe2\x80\x9d by him for purposes of \xc2\xa73730(e)(4)(A).

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We hold that the District Court lacked jurisdiction to enter judgment in favor of Stone. We reverse the Tenth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment to the contrary.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Any private citizen may bring an action to enforce the False Claims Act, 31 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73729\xe2\x80\x933733, unless the information on which his allegations are based is already in the public domain. Even if the information is publicly available, however, the citizen may still sue if he was an \xe2\x80\x9coriginal source\xe2\x80\x9d of that information. \xc2\xa73730(e)(4)(A) (\xe2\x80\x9cNo court shall have jurisdiction over an action under this section based upon the public disclosure of allegations or transactions \xe2\x80\xa6 unless the action is brought by the Attorney General or the person bringing the action is an original source of the information\xe2\x80\x9d). Because I believe the Court has misinterpreted these provisions to require that an \xe2\x80\x9coriginal source\xe2\x80\x9d in a qui tam action have knowledge of the actual facts underlying the allegations on which he may ultimately prevail, I respectfully dissent.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In my view, a plain reading of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s provisions\xe2\x80\x94specifically, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73730(e)(4)(A) and (B)\xe2\x80\x94makes clear that it is the information underlying the publicly disclosed allegations, not the information underlying the allegations in the relator\xe2\x80\x99s complaint (original or amended), of which the relator must be an original source.[Footnote 1] Moreover, the statute\xe2\x80\x99s use of the article \xe2\x80\x9can,\xe2\x80\x9d rather than \xe2\x80\x9cthe,\xe2\x80\x9d in describing the original source indicates that the relator need not be the sole source of the information.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0By contrast, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s approach suggests that the relator must have knowledge of actual facts supporting the theory ultimately proven at trial\xe2\x80\x94in other words, knowledge of the information underlying the prevailing claims. See ante, at 17 (limiting the relevant jurisdictional inquiry to those \xe2\x80\x9cfalse claims ultimately found by the jury\xe2\x80\x9d). I disagree. Such a view is not supported by the statute, which requires only that the relator have \xe2\x80\x9cdirect and independent knowledge\xe2\x80\x9d of the information on which the publicly disclosed allegations are based and that the relator provide such information to the Government in a timely manner. As I read the statute, the jurisdictional inquiry focuses on the facts in the public domain at the time the action is commenced. If the process of discovery leads to amended theories of recovery, amendments to the original complaint would not affect jurisdiction that was proper at the time of the original filing.[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this case, as the Court points out, the fact that Rockwell was storing thousands of insolid pondcrete blocks at the Rocky Flats facility had been publicly disclosed by the news media before Stone filed this lawsuit. Ante, at 3, 4. In my view, the record establishes that Stone was an original source of the allegations publicly disclosed by the media in June 1989, even though he thought that the deterioration of the pondcrete blocks would be caused by poor engineering rather than a poor formula for the mixture. The search warrant that was executed on June 6, 1989, and the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) affidavit that was released to the news media on June 9, 1989, were both based, in part, on interviews with Stone and on information Stone had provided to the Government, including the 1982 Engineering Order.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0With respect to earlier media coverage of the pondcrete leakage discovery in May 1988, however, Stone\xe2\x80\x99s status as an original source is less obvious. Stone first went to the FBI with allegations of Rockwell\xe2\x80\x99s environmental violations in March 1986. App. 180. He subsequently met with several FBI agents over the course of several years. Id., at 180\xe2\x80\x93182. During those meetings he provided the FBI with thousands of pages of documents, including the Engineering Order, in which he predicted that the pondcrete system design would not work. On the basis of that record, it seems likely that Stone (1) had \xe2\x80\x9cdirect and independent knowledge of the information on which the [publicly disclosed] allegations [we]re based\xe2\x80\x9d and (2) voluntarily provided such information to the Government before filing suit. It is, however, his burden to establish that he did so. Because there has been no finding as to whether Stone was an original source as to those public disclosures, I would vacate and remand for a determination whether Stone was in fact an original source of the allegations publicly disclosed by the media in 1988 and 1989.
