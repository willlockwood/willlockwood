\n\t\t\t\tFEDERAL TRADE COMMISSION v. ACTAVIS, INC., et\xc2\xa0al.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 12\xe2\x80\x93416.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 25, 2013\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 17, 2013\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe Drug Price Competition and Patent Term Restoration Act of 1984 (Hatch-Waxman Act or Act) creates special procedures for identifying and resolving patent disputes between brand-name and generic drug manufacturers, one of which requires a prospective generic manufacturer to assure the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) that it will not infringe the brand-name\xe2\x80\x99s patents. One way to provide such assurance (the \xe2\x80\x9cparagraph IV\xe2\x80\x9d route) is by certifying that any listed, relevant patent \xe2\x80\x9cis invalid or will not be infringed by the manufacture, use, or sale\xe2\x80\x9d of the generic drug. \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7355(j)(2)(A)(vii)(IV). \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondent Solvay Pharmaceuticals obtained a patent for its approved brand-name drug AndroGel. Subsequently, respondents Actavis and Paddock filed applications for generic drugs modeled after AndroGel and certified under paragraph IV that Solvay\xe2\x80\x99s patent was invalid and that their drugs did not infringe it. Solvay sued Actavis and Paddock, claiming patent infringement. See \n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7271(e)(2)(A). The FDA eventually approved Actavis\xe2\x80\x99 generic product, but instead of bringing its drug to market, Actavis entered into a \xe2\x80\x9creverse payment\xe2\x80\x9d settlement agreement with Solvay, agreeing not to bring its generic to market for a specified number of years and agreeing to promote AndroGel to doctors in exchange for millions of dollars. Paddock made a similar agreement with Solvay, as did respondent Par, another manufacturer aligned in the patent litigation with Paddock.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Federal Trade Commission (FTC) filed suit, alleging that respondents violated \xc2\xa75 of the Federal Trade Commission Act by unlawfully agreeing to abandon their patent challenges, to refrain from launching their low-cost generic drugs, and to share in Solvay\xe2\x80\x99s monopoly profits. The District Court dismissed the complaint. The Eleventh Circuit concluded that as long as the anticompetitive effects of a settlement fall within the scope of the patent\xe2\x80\x99s exclusionary potential, the settlement is immune from antitrust attack. Noting that the FTC had not alleged that the challenged agreements excluded competition to a greater extent than would the patent, if valid, it affirmed the complaint\xe2\x80\x99s dismissal. It further recognized that if parties to this sort of case do not settle, a court might declare a patent invalid. But since public policy favors the settlement of disputes, it held that courts could not require parties to continue to litigate in order to avoid antitrust liability.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0The Eleventh Circuit erred in affirming the dismissal of the FTC\xe2\x80\x99s complaint. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9321.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0Although the anticompetitive effects of the reverse settlement agreement might fall within the scope of the exclusionary potential of Solvay\xe2\x80\x99s patent, this does not immunize the agreement from antitrust attack. For one thing, to refer simply to what the holder of a valid patent could do does not by itself answer the antitrust question. Here, the paragraph IV litigation put the patent\xe2\x80\x99s validity and preclusive scope at issue, and the parties\xe2\x80\x99 settlement\xe2\x80\x94in which, the FTC alleges, the plaintiff agreed to pay the defendants millions to stay out of its market, even though the defendants had no monetary claim against the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x94ended that litigation. That form of settlement is unusual, and there is reason for concern that such settlements tend to have significant adverse effects on competition. It would be incongruous to determine antitrust legality by measuring the settlement\xe2\x80\x99s anticompetitive effects solely against patent law policy, and not against procompetitive antitrust policies as well. Both are relevant in determining the scope of monopoly and antitrust immunity conferred by a patent, see, e.g., United States v. Line Material Co., \n\n333 U.\xc2\xa0S. 287\n, and the antitrust question should be answered by considering traditional antitrust factors. For another thing, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents make clear that patent-related settlement agreements can sometimes violate the antitrust laws. See, e.g., United States v. Singer Mfg. Co., \n\n374 U.\xc2\xa0S. 174\n; United States v. New Wrinkle, Inc., \n\n342 U.\xc2\xa0S. 371\n; Standard Oil Co. (Indiana) v. United States, \n\n283 U.\xc2\xa0S. 163\n. Finally, the Hatch-Waxman Act\xe2\x80\x99s general procompetitive thrust\xe2\x80\x94facilitating challenges to a patent\xe2\x80\x99s validity and requiring parties to a paragraph IV dispute to report settlement terms to federal antitrust regulators\xe2\x80\x94suggests a view contrary to the Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9314.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0While the Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion finds some support in a general legal policy favoring the settlement of disputes, its related underlying practical concern consists of its fear that antitrust scrutiny of a reverse payment agreement would require the parties to engage in time-consuming, complex, and expensive litigation to demonstrate what would have happened to competition absent the settlement. However, five sets of considerations lead to the conclusion that this concern should not determine the result here and that the FTC should have been given the opportunity to prove its antitrust claim. First, the specific restraint at issue has the \xe2\x80\x9cpotential for genuine adverse effects on competition.\xe2\x80\x9d FTC v. Indiana Federation of Dentists, \n\n476 U.\xc2\xa0S. 447\n\xe2\x80\x93461. Payment for staying out of the market keeps prices at patentee-set levels and divides the benefit between the patentee and the challenger, while the consumer loses. And two Hatch-Waxman Act features\xe2\x80\x94the 180-day exclusive-right-to-sell advantage given to the first paragraph IV challenger to win FDA approval, \xc2\xa7355(j)(5)(B)(iv), and the roughly 30-month period that the subsequent manufacturers would be required to wait out before winning FDA approval, \xc2\xa7355(j)(5)(B)(iii)\xe2\x80\x94mean that a reverse settlement agreement with the first filer removes from consideration the manufacturer most likely to introduce competition quickly. Second, these anticompetitive consequences will at least sometimes prove unjustified. There may be justifications for reverse payment that are not the result of having sought or brought about anticompetitive consequences, but that does not justify dismissing the FTC\xe2\x80\x99s complaint without examining the potential justifications. Third, where a reverse payment threatens to work unjustified anticompetitive harm, the patentee likely has the power to bring about that harm in practice. The size of the payment from a branded drug manufacturer to a generic challenger is a strong indicator of such power. Fourth, an antitrust action is likely to prove more feasible administratively than the Eleventh Circuit believed. It is normally not necessary to litigate patent validity to answer the antitrust question. A large, unexplained reverse payment can provide a workable surrogate for a patent\xe2\x80\x99s weakness, all without forcing a court to conduct a detailed exploration of the patent\xe2\x80\x99s validity. Fifth, the fact that a large, unjustified reverse payment risks antitrust liability does not prevent litigating parties from settling their lawsuits. As in other industries, they may settle in other ways, e.g., by allowing the generic manufacturer to enter the patentee\xe2\x80\x99s market before the patent expires without the patentee\xe2\x80\x99s paying the challenger to stay out prior to that point. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9320.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0This Court declines to hold that reverse payment settlement agreements are presumptively unlawful. Courts reviewing such agreements should proceed by applying the \xe2\x80\x9crule of reason,\xe2\x80\x9d rather than under a \xe2\x80\x9cquick look\xe2\x80\x9d approach. See California Dental Assn. v. FTC, \n\n526 U.\xc2\xa0S. 756\n, n.\xc2\xa012. Pp.\xc2\xa020\xe2\x80\x9321.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Breyer delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCompany A sues Company B for patent infringement. The two companies settle under terms that require (1) Company B, the claimed infringer, not to produce the pat-ented product until the patent\xe2\x80\x99s term expires, and (2) Company A, the patentee, to pay B many millions of dollars. Because the settlement requires the patentee to pay the alleged infringer, rather than the other way around, this kind of settlement agreement is often called a \xe2\x80\x9creverse payment\xe2\x80\x9d settlement agreement. And the basic question here is whether such an agreement can sometimes unreasonably diminish competition in violation of the antitrust laws. See, e.g., \n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71 (Sherman Act prohibition of \xe2\x80\x9crestraint[s] of trade or commerce\xe2\x80\x9d). Cf. Palmer v. BRG of Ga., Inc., \n\n498 U.\xc2\xa0S. 46 (1990)\n (per\xc2\xa0curiam) (invalidating agreement not to compete).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn this case, the Eleventh Circuit dismissed a Federal Trade Commission (FTC) complaint claiming that a particular reverse payment settlement agreement violated the antitrust laws. In doing so, the Circuit stated that a reverse payment settlement agreement generally is \xe2\x80\x9cimmune from antitrust attack so long as its anticompetitive effects fall within the scope of the exclusionary potential of the patent.\xe2\x80\x9d FTC v. Watson Pharmaceuticals, Inc., 677 F.\xc2\xa03d 1298, 1312 (2012). And since the alleged infringer\xe2\x80\x99s promise not to enter the patentee\xe2\x80\x99s market expired before the patent\xe2\x80\x99s term ended, the Circuit found the agreement legal and dismissed the FTC complaint. Id., at 1315. In our view, however, reverse payment settlements such as the agreement alleged in the complaint before us can some-times violate the antitrust laws. We consequently hold that the Eleventh Circuit should have allowed the FTC\xe2\x80\x99s lawsuit to proceed.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tApparently most if not all reverse payment settlement agreements arise in the context of pharmaceutical drug regulation, and specifically in the context of suits brought under statutory provisions allowing a generic drug manufacturer (seeking speedy marketing approval) to challenge the validity of a patent owned by an already-approved brand-name drug owner. See Brief for Petitioner 29; 12 P. Areeda & H. Hovenkamp, Antitrust Law \xc2\xb62046, p. 338 (3d ed. 2012) (hereinafter Areeda); Hovenkamp, Sensible Antitrust Rules for Pharmaceutical Competition, 39 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0F. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 11, 24 (2004). We consequently describe four key features of the relevant drug-regulatory framework established by the Drug Price Competition and Patent Term Restoration Act of 1984, \n\n98Stat.\n1585, as amended. That Act is commonly known as the Hatch-Waxman Act.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFirst, a drug manufacturer, wishing to market a new prescription drug, must submit a New Drug Application to the federal Food and Drug Administration (FDA) and undergo a long, comprehensive, and costly testing process, after which, if successful, the manufacturer will receive marketing approval from the FDA. See \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7355(b)(1) (requiring, among other things, \xe2\x80\x9cfull reports of investigations\xe2\x80\x9d into safety and effectiveness; \xe2\x80\x9ca full list of the articles used as components\xe2\x80\x9d; and a \xe2\x80\x9cfull description\xe2\x80\x9d of how the drug is manufactured, processed, and packed).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSecond, once the FDA has approved a brand-name drug for marketing, a manufacturer of a generic drug can obtain similar marketing approval through use of abbrevi-ated procedures. The Hatch-Waxman Act permits a generic manufacturer to file an Abbreviated New Drug Appli-cation specifying that the generic has the \xe2\x80\x9csame active ingredients as,\xe2\x80\x9d and is \xe2\x80\x9cbiologically equivalent\xe2\x80\x9d to, the al-ready-approved brand-name drug. Caraco Pharmaceutical Laboratories, Ltd. v. Novo Nordisk A/S, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 2) (citing 21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7355(j)(2)(A)(ii), (iv)). In this way the generic manufacturer can obtain approval while avoiding the \xe2\x80\x9ccostly and time-consuming studies\xe2\x80\x9d needed to obtain approval \xe2\x80\x9cfor a pioneer drug.\xe2\x80\x9d See Eli Lilly & Co. v. Medtronic, Inc., \n\n496 U.\xc2\xa0S. 661, \n676 (1990)\n. The Hatch-Waxman process, by allowing the generic to piggy-back on the pioneer\xe2\x80\x99s approval efforts, \xe2\x80\x9cspeed[s] the introduction of low-cost generic drugs to market,\xe2\x80\x9d Caraco, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 2), thereby furthering drug competition.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThird, the Hatch-Waxman Act sets forth special pro-cedures for identifying, and resolving, related patent dis-putes. It requires the pioneer brand-name manufacturer to list in its New Drug Application the \xe2\x80\x9cnumber and the expiration date\xe2\x80\x9d of any relevant patent. See \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7355(b)(1). And it requires the generic manufacturer in its Abbreviated New Drug Application to \xe2\x80\x9cassure the FDA\xe2\x80\x9d that the generic \xe2\x80\x9cwill not infringe\xe2\x80\x9d the brand-name\xe2\x80\x99s patents. See Caraco, supra, at___ (slip op., at 3).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe generic can provide this assurance in one of several ways. See \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7355(j)(2)(A)(vii). It can certify that the brand-name manufacturer has not listed any rele- vant patents. It can certify that any relevant patents have expired. It can request approval to market beginning when any still-in-force patents expire. Or, it can certify that any listed, relevant patent \xe2\x80\x9cis invalid or will not be infringed by the manufacture, use, or sale\xe2\x80\x9d of the drug described in the Abbreviated New Drug Application. See \xc2\xa7355(j)(2)(A)(vii)(IV). Taking this last-mentioned route (called the \xe2\x80\x9cparagraph IV\xe2\x80\x9d route), automatically counts as patent infringement, see \n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7271(e)(2)(A) (2006 ed., Supp. V), and often \xe2\x80\x9cmeans provoking litigation.\xe2\x80\x9d Caraco, supra, at___ (slip op., at 5). If the brand-name patentee brings an infringement suit within 45 days, the FDA then must withhold approving the generic, usually for a 30-month period, while the parties litigate patent validity (or infringement) in court. If the courts decide the matter within that period, the FDA follows that determination; if they do not, the FDA may go forward and give approval to market the generic product. See \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7355(j)(5)(B)(iii).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFourth, Hatch-Waxman provides a special incentive for a generic to be the first to file an Abbreviated New Drug Application taking the paragraph IV route. That ap- plicant will enjoy a period of 180 days of exclusivity (from the first commercial marketing of its drug). See \xc2\xa7355(j)(5)(B)(iv) (establishing exclusivity period). During that period of exclusivity no other generic can compete with the brand-name drug. If the first-to-file generic manufacturer can overcome any patent obstacle and bring the generic to market, this 180-day period of exclusivity can prove valuable, possibly \xe2\x80\x9cworth several hundred million dollars.\xe2\x80\x9d Hemphill, Paying for Delay: Pharmaceutical Patent Settlement as a Regulatory Design Problem, 81 N.\xc2\xa0Y.\xc2\xa0U. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1553, 1579 (2006). Indeed, the Generic Pharmaceutical Association said in 2006 that the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98vast majority of potential profits for a generic drug manufacturer materialize during the 180-day exclusivity period.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 6 (quoting statement). The 180-day ex- clusivity period, however, can belong only to the first generic to file. Should that first-to-file generic forfeit the exclusivity right in one of the ways specified by statute, no other generic can obtain it. See \xc2\xa7355(j)(5)(D).\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 1999, Solvay Pharmaceuticals, a respondent here, filed a New Drug Application for a brand-name drug called AndroGel. The FDA approved the application in 2000. In 2003, Solvay obtained a relevant patent and disclosed that fact to the FDA, 677 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1308, as Hatch-Waxman requires. See \xc2\xa7355(c)(2) (requiring, in addition, that FDA must publish new patent information upon submission).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tLater the same year another respondent, Actavis, Inc. (then known as Watson Pharmaceuticals), filed an Abbreviated New Drug Application for a generic drug modeled after AndroGel. Subsequently, Paddock Laboratories, also a respondent, separately filed an Abbreviated New Drug Application for its own generic product. Both Actavis and Paddock certified under paragraph IV that Solvay\xe2\x80\x99s listed patent was invalid and their drugs did not infringe it. A fourth manufacturer, Par Pharmaceutical, likewise a re- spondent, did not file an application of its own but joined forces with Paddock, agreeing to share the patent litigation costs in return for a share of profits if Paddock obtained approval for its generic drug.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSolvay initiated paragraph IV patent litigation against Actavis and Paddock. Thirty months later the FDA approved Actavis\xe2\x80\x99 first-to-file generic product, but, in 2006, the patent-litigation parties all settled. Under the terms of the settlement Actavis agreed that it would not bring its generic to market until August 31, 2015, 65 months before Solvay\xe2\x80\x99s patent expired (unless someone else marketed a generic sooner). Actavis also agreed to promote AndroGel to urologists. The other generic manufacturers made roughly similar promises. And Solvay agreed to pay millions of dollars to each generic\xe2\x80\x94$12 million in total to Paddock; $60 million in total to Par; and an estimated $19\xe2\x80\x93$30 million annually, for nine years, to Actavis. See App. 46, 49\xe2\x80\x9350, Complaint \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb666, 77. The companies de- scribed these payments as compensation for other services the generics promised to perform, but the FTC contends the other services had little value. According to the FTC the true point of the payments was to compensate the generics for agreeing not to compete against AndroGel until 2015. See id., at 50\xe2\x80\x9353, Complaint \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb681\xe2\x80\x9385.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOn January 29, 2009, the FTC filed this lawsuit against all the settling parties, namely, Solvay, Actavis, Paddock, and Par. The FTC\xe2\x80\x99s complaint (as since amended) alleged that respondents violated \xc2\xa75 of the Federal Trade Commission Act, \n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa745, by unlawfully agreeing \xe2\x80\x9cto share in Solvay\xe2\x80\x99s monopoly profits, abandon their patent challenges, and refrain from launching their low-cost generic products to compete with AndroGel for nine years.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 29, Complaint \xc2\xb65. See generally FTC v. Indiana Federation of Dentists, \n\n476 U.\xc2\xa0S. 447, \n454 (1986)\n (Section 5 \xe2\x80\x9cencompass[es] . . . practices that violate the Sherman Act and the other antitrust laws\xe2\x80\x9d). The District Court held that these allegations did not set forth an antitrust law violation. In\xc2\xa0re Androgel Antitrust Litigation (No. II), 687 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1371, 1379 (ND Ga. 2010). It accordingly dismissed the FTC\xe2\x80\x99s complaint. The FTC appealed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit affirmed the District Court. It wrote that \xe2\x80\x9cabsent sham litigation or fraud in obtaining the patent, a reverse payment settlement is immune from antitrust attack so long as its anticompetitive effects fall within the scope of the exclusionary potential of the patent.\xe2\x80\x9d 677 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1312. The court recognized that \xe2\x80\x9cantitrust laws typically prohibit agreements where one company pays a potential competitor not to enter the market.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1307 (citing Valley Drug Co. v. Geneva Pharmaceuticals, Inc., 344 F.\xc2\xa03d 1294, 1304 (CA11 2003)). See also Palmer, 498 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 50 (agreement to divide territorial markets held \xe2\x80\x9cunlawful on its face\xe2\x80\x9d). But, the court found that \xe2\x80\x9creverse payment settlements of patent litigation presen[t] atypical cases because one of the parties owns a patent.\xe2\x80\x9d 677 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1307 (internal quotation marks and second alteration omitted). Patent holders have a \xe2\x80\x9clawful right to exclude others from the market,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted); thus a patent \xe2\x80\x9cconveys the right to cripple competition.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1310 (internal quotation marks omitted). The court recognized that, if the parties to this sort of case do not settle, a court might declare the patent invalid. Id., at 1305. But, in light of the public policy favoring settlement of disputes (among other considerations) it held that the courts could not require the parties to continue to litigate in order to avoid antitrust liability. Id., at 1313\xe2\x80\x931314.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe FTC sought certiorari. Because different courts have reached different conclusions about the application of the antitrust laws to Hatch-Waxman-related patent settlements, we granted the FTC\xe2\x80\x99s petition. Compare, e.g., id., at 1312 (case below) (settlements generally \xe2\x80\x9cimmune from antitrust attack\xe2\x80\x9d); In\xc2\xa0re Ciprofloxacin Hydrochloride Antitrust Litigation, 544 F.\xc2\xa03d 1323, 1332\xe2\x80\x931337 (CA Fed. 2008) (similar); In\xc2\xa0re Tamoxifen Citrate Antitrust Litigation, 466 F.\xc2\xa03d 187, 212\xe2\x80\x93213 (CA2 2006) (similar), with In\xc2\xa0re K-Dur Antitrust Litigation, 686 F.\xc2\xa03d 197, 214\xe2\x80\x93218 (CA3 2012) (settlements presumptively unlawful).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSolvay\xe2\x80\x99s patent, if valid and infringed, might have permitted it to charge drug prices sufficient to recoup the reverse settlement payments it agreed to make to its po- tential generic competitors. And we are willing to take this fact as evidence that the agreement\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9canticompetitive effects fall within the scope of the exclusionary potential of the patent.\xe2\x80\x9d 677 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1312. But we do not agree that that fact, or characterization, can immunize the agreement from antitrust attack.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor one thing, to refer, as the Circuit referred, simply to what the holder of a valid patent could do does not by itself answer the antitrust question. The patent here may or may not be valid, and may or may not be infringed. \xe2\x80\x9c[A] valid patent excludes all except its owner from the use of the protected process or product,\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Line Material Co., \n\n333 U.\xc2\xa0S. 287, \n308 (1948)\n (emphasis added). And that exclusion may permit the patent owner to charge a higher-than-competitive price for the patented product. But an invalidated patent carries with it no such right. And even a valid patent confers no right to exclude products or processes that do not actually infringe. The paragraph IV litigation in this case put the patent\xe2\x80\x99s validity at issue, as well as its actual preclusive scope. The parties\xe2\x80\x99 settlement ended that litigation. The FTC alleges that in substance, the plaintiff agreed to pay the defendants many millions of dollars to stay out of its market, even though the defendants did not have any claim that the plaintiff was liable to them for damages. That form of settlement is unusual. And, for reasons discussed in Part II\xe2\x80\x93B, infra, there is reason for concern that settlements tak- ing this form tend to have significant adverse effects on competition.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tGiven these factors, it would be incongruous to determine antitrust legality by measuring the settlement\xe2\x80\x99s anticompetitive effects solely against patent law policy, rather than by measuring them against procompetitive antitrust policies as well. And indeed, contrary to the Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s view that the only pertinent question is whether \xe2\x80\x9cthe settlement agreement . . . fall[s] within\xe2\x80\x9d the legitimate \xe2\x80\x9cscope\xe2\x80\x9d of the patent\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cexclusionary potential,\xe2\x80\x9d 677 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1309, 1312, this Court has indicated that patent and antitrust policies are both relevant in determining the \xe2\x80\x9cscope of the patent monopoly\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94and consequently antitrust law immunity\xe2\x80\x94that is conferred by a patent.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThus, the Court in Line Material explained that \xe2\x80\x9cthe improper use of [a patent] monopoly,\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cinvalid\xe2\x80\x9d under the antitrust laws and resolved the antitrust question in that case by seeking an accommodation \xe2\x80\x9cbetween the law- ful restraint on trade of the patent monopoly and the illegal restraint prohibited broadly by the Sherman Act.\xe2\x80\x9d 333 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 310. To strike that balance, the Court asked questions such as whether \xe2\x80\x9cthe patent statute specifically gives a right\xe2\x80\x9d to restrain competition in the manner challenged; and whether \xe2\x80\x9ccompetition is impeded to a greater degree\xe2\x80\x9d by the restraint at issue than other restraints previously approved as reasonable. Id., at 311. See also United States v. United States Gypsum Co., \n\n333 U.\xc2\xa0S. 364\n\xe2\x80\x93391 (1948) (courts must \xe2\x80\x9cbalance the privileges of [the patent holder] and its licensees under the patent grants with the prohibitions of the Sherman Act against combi- nations and attempts to monopolize\xe2\x80\x9d); Walker Process Equipment, Inc. v. Food Machinery & Chemical Corp., \n\n382 U.\xc2\xa0S. 172, \n174 (1965)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[E]nforcement of a patent procured by fraud\xe2\x80\x9d may violate the Sherman Act). In short, rather than measure the length or amount of a restriction solely against the length of the patent\xe2\x80\x99s term or its earning potential, as the Court of Appeals apparently did here, this Court answered the antitrust question by considering traditional antitrust factors such as likely anticompetitive effects, redeeming virtues, market power, and potentially offsetting legal considerations present in the circumstances, such as here those related to patents. See Part II\xe2\x80\x93B, infra. Whether a particular restraint lies \xe2\x80\x9cbeyond the limits of the patent monopoly\xe2\x80\x9d is a conclusion that flows from that analysis and not, as The Chief Justice suggests, its starting point. Post, at 3, 8 (dissenting opinion).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor another thing, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents make clear that patent-related settlement agreements can sometimes violate the antitrust laws. In United States v. Singer Mfg. Co., \n\n374 U.\xc2\xa0S. 174 (1963)\n, for example, two sewing machine companies possessed competing patent claims; a third company sought a patent under circumstances where doing so might lead to the disclosure of information that would invalidate the other two firms\xe2\x80\x99 patents. All three firms settled their patent-related disagreements while assigning the broadest claims to the firm best able to enforce the patent against yet other potential competitors. Id., at 190\xe2\x80\x93192. The Court did not examine whether, on the assumption that all three patents were valid, patent law would have allowed the patents\xe2\x80\x99 holders to do the same. Rather, emphasizing that the Sherman Act \xe2\x80\x9cim- poses strict limitations on the concerted activities in which patent owners may lawfully engage,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 197, it held that the agreements, although settling patent disputes, violated the antitrust laws. Id., at 195, 197. And that, in important part, was because \xe2\x80\x9cthe public interest in granting patent monopolies\xe2\x80\x9d exists only to the extent that \xe2\x80\x9cthe public is given a novel and useful invention\xe2\x80\x9d in \xe2\x80\x9cconsideration for its grant.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 199 (White, J., concurring). See also United States v. New Wrinkle, Inc., \n\n342 U.\xc2\xa0S. 371, \n378 (1952)\n (applying antitrust scrutiny to patent settlement); Standard Oil Co. (Indiana) v. United States, \n\n283 U.\xc2\xa0S. 163 (1931)\n (same).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSimilarly, both within the settlement context and without, the Court has struck down overly restrictive patent licensing agreements\xe2\x80\x94irrespective of whether those agreements produced supra-patent-permitted revenues. We concede that in United States v. General Elec. Co., \n\n272 U.\xc2\xa0S. 476, \n489 (1926)\n, the Court permitted a single patentee to grant to a single licensee a license containing a minimum resale price requirement. But in Line Material, supra, at 308, 310\xe2\x80\x93311, the Court held that the antitrust laws forbid a group of patentees, each owning one or more patents, to cross-license each other, and, in doing so, to insist that each licensee maintain retail prices set collectively by the patent holders. The Court was willing to presume that the single-patentee practice approved in General Electric was a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable restraint\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9caccords with the patent monopoly granted by the patent law,\xe2\x80\x9d 333 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 312, but declined to extend that conclusion to multiple-patentee agreements: \xe2\x80\x9cAs the Sherman Act prohibits agreements to fix prices, any arrangement between patentees runs afoul of that prohibition and is outside the patent monopoly.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. In New Wrinkle, 342 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 378, the Court held roughly the same, this time in respect to a similar arrangement in settlement of a litigation between two patentees, each of which contended that its own patent gave it the exclusive right to control production. That one or the other company (we may presume) was right about its patent did not lead the Court to confer antitrust immunity. Far from it, the agreement was found to violate the Sherman Act. Id., at 380.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally in Standard Oil Co. (Indiana), the Court upheld cross-licensing agreements among patentees that settled actual and impending patent litigation, 283 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 168, which agreements set royalty rates to be charged third parties for a license to practice all the patents at issue (and which divided resulting revenues). But, in doing so, Justice Brandeis, writing for the Court, warned that such an arrangement would have violated the Sherman Act had the patent holders thereby \xe2\x80\x9cdominate[d]\xe2\x80\x9d the industry and \xe2\x80\x9ccurtail[ed] the manufacture and supply of an unpatented product.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 174. These cases do not simply ask whether a hypothetically valid patent\xe2\x80\x99s holder would be able to charge, e.g., the high prices that the challenged patent-related term allowed. Rather, they seek to ac-commodate patent and antitrust policies, finding challenged terms and conditions unlawful unless patent law policy offsets the antitrust law policy strongly favoring competition.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThus, contrary to the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion, post, at 4\xe2\x80\x936, there is nothing novel about our approach. What does appear novel are the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s suggestions that a patent holder may simply \xe2\x80\x9cpa[y] a competitor to respect its patent\xe2\x80\x9d and quit its patent invalidity or noninfringement claim without any antitrust scrutiny whatever, post, at 3, and that \xe2\x80\x9csuch settlements . . . are a well-known feature of intellectual property litigation,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 10. Closer examination casts doubt on these claims. The dissent does not identify any patent statute that it understands to grant such a right to a patentee, whether expressly or by fair implication. It would be difficult to reconcile the proposed right with the patent-related policy of eliminating unwarranted patent grants so the public will not \xe2\x80\x9ccontinually be required to pay tribute to would-be monopolists without need or justification.\xe2\x80\x9d Lear, Inc. v. Adkins, \n\n395 U.\xc2\xa0S. 653, \n670 (1969)\n. And the authorities cited for this proposition (none from this Court, and none an antitrust case) are not on point. Some of them say that when Company A sues Company B for patent infringement and demands, say, $100 million in damages, it is not uncommon for B (the defendant) to pay A (the plaintiff) some amount less than the full demand as part of the settlement\xe2\x80\x94$40 million, for example. See Schildkraut, Patent-Splitting Settlements and the Reverse Payment Fallacy, 71 Antitrust L.\xc2\xa0J. 1033, 1046 (2004) (suggesting that this hypothetical settlement includes \xe2\x80\x9can implicit net payment\xe2\x80\x9d from A to B of $60 million\xe2\x80\x94i.e., the amount of the settlement discount). The cited authorities also indicate that if B has a counterclaim for damages against A, the original infringement plaintiff, A might end up paying B to settle B\xe2\x80\x99s counterclaim. Cf. Metro-Goldwyn Mayer, Inc. v. 007 Safety Prods., Inc., 183 F.\xc2\xa03d 10, 13 (CA1 1999) (describing trademark dispute and settlement). Insofar as the dissent urges that settlements taking these commonplace forms have not been thought for that reason alone subject to antitrust liability, we agree, and do not intend to alter that understanding. But the dissent appears also to suggest that reverse payment settlements\xe2\x80\x94e.g., in which A, the plaintiff, pays money to defendant B purely so B will give up the patent fight\xe2\x80\x94should be viewed for antitrust purposes in the same light as these familiar settlement forms. See post, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310. We cannot agree. In the traditional examples cited above, a party with a claim (or counterclaim) for damages receives a sum equal to or less than the value of its claim. In reverse payment settlements, in contrast, a party with no claim for damages (something that is usually true of a paragraph IV litigation defendant) walks away with money simply so it will stay away from the patentee\xe2\x80\x99s market. That, we think, is something quite different. Cf. Verizon Communications, Inc. v. Law Offices of Curtis V. Trinko, LLP, \n\n540 U.\xc2\xa0S. 398, \n408 (2004)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[C]ollusion\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cthe supreme evil of antitrust\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, the Hatch-Waxman Act itself does not embody a statutory policy that supports the Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s view. Rather, the general procompetitive thrust of the statute, its specific provisions facilitating challenges to a patent\xe2\x80\x99s validity, see Part I\xe2\x80\x93A, supra, and its later-added provisions requiring parties to a patent dispute triggered by a paragraph IV filing to report settlement terms to the FTC and the Antitrust Division of the Department of Justice, all suggest the contrary. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71112\xe2\x80\x931113, \n\n117Stat.\n2461\xe2\x80\x932462. Those interested in legislative history may also wish to examine the statements of individual Members of Congress condemning reverse payment settlements in advance of the 2003 amendments. See, e.g., 148 Cong. Rec. 14437 (2002) (remarks of Sen. Hatch) (\xe2\x80\x9cIt was and is very clear that the [Hatch-Waxman Act] was not designed to allow deals between brand and generic companies to delay competition\xe2\x80\x9d); 146 Cong. Rec. 18774 (2000) (remarks of Rep. Waxman) (introducing bill to deter companies from \xe2\x80\x9cstrik[ing] collusive agreements to trade multimillion dol- lar payoffs by the brand company for delays in the introduction of lower cost, generic alternatives\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion finds some degree of support in a general legal policy favoring the settlement of disputes. 677 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1313\xe2\x80\x931314. See also Schering-Plough Corp. v. FTC, 402 F.\xc2\xa03d 1056, 1074\xe2\x80\x931075 (2005) (same); In\xc2\xa0re Tamoxifen Citrate, 466 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 202 (noting public\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98strong interest in settlement\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of complex and expensive cases). The Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s related underlying practical concern consists of its fear that antitrust scrutiny of a reverse payment agreement would require the parties to litigate the validity of the patent in order to demonstrate what would have happened to competition in the absence of the settlement. Any such litigation will prove time consuming, complex, and expensive. The antitrust game, the Circuit may believe, would not be worth that litigation candle.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe recognize the value of settlements and the patent litigation problem. But we nonetheless conclude that this patent-related factor should not determine the result here. Rather, five sets of considerations lead us to conclude that the FTC should have been given the opportunity to prove its antitrust claim.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFirst, the specific restraint at issue has the \xe2\x80\x9cpotential for genuine adverse effects on competition.\xe2\x80\x9d Indiana Federation of Dentists, 476 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 460\xe2\x80\x93461 (citing 7 Areeda \xc2\xb61511, at 429 (1986)). The payment in effect amounts to a purchase by the patentee of the exclusive right to sell its product, a right it already claims but would lose if the patent litigation were to continue and the patent were held invalid or not infringed by the generic product. Suppose, for example, that the exclusive right to sell produces $50 million in supracompetitive profits per year for the patentee. And suppose further that the patent has 10 more years to run. Continued litigation, if it results in patent invalidation or a finding of noninfringement, could cost the patentee $500 million in lost revenues, a sum that then would flow in large part to consumers in the form of lower prices.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe concede that settlement on terms permitting the patent challenger to enter the market before the patent expires would also bring about competition, again to the consumer\xe2\x80\x99s benefit. But settlement on the terms said by the FTC to be at issue here\xe2\x80\x94payment in return for staying out of the market\xe2\x80\x94simply keeps prices at patentee-set levels, potentially producing the full patent-related $500 million monopoly return while dividing that return between the challenged patentee and the patent challenger. The patentee and the challenger gain; the consumer loses. Indeed, there are indications that patentees sometimes pay a generic challenger a sum even larger than what the generic would gain in profits if it won the paragraph IV litigation and entered the market. See Hemphill, 81 N.\xc2\xa0Y. U. L.\xc2\xa0Rev., at 1581. See also Brief for 118 Law, Economics, and Business Professors et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 25 (estimating that this is true of the settlement challenged here). The rationale behind a payment of this size cannot in every case be supported by traditional settlement considerations. The payment may instead provide strong evidence that the patentee seeks to induce the generic challenger to abandon its claim with a share of its monopoly profits that would otherwise be lost in the competitive market.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut, one might ask, as a practical matter would the parties be able to enter into such an anticompetitive agreement? Would not a high reverse payment signal to other potential challengers that the patentee lacks confidence in its patent, thereby provoking additional challenges, perhaps too many for the patentee to \xe2\x80\x9cbuy off?\xe2\x80\x9d Two special features of Hatch-Waxman mean that the an- swer to this question is \xe2\x80\x9cnot necessarily so.\xe2\x80\x9d First, under Hatch-Waxman only the first challenger gains the special advantage of 180 days of an exclusive right to sell a generic version of the brand-name product. See Part I\xe2\x80\x93A, supra. And as noted, that right has proved valuable\xe2\x80\x94indeed, it can be worth several hundred million dollars. See Hemphill, supra, at 1579; Brief for Petitioner 6. Subsequent challengers cannot secure that exclusivity period, and thus stand to win significantly less than the first if they bring a successful paragraph IV challenge. That is, if subsequent litigation results in invalidation of the patent, or a ruling that the patent is not infringed, that litigation victory will free not just the challenger to compete, but all other potential competitors too (once they obtain FDA approval). The potential reward available to a subsequent challenger being significantly less, the patentee\xe2\x80\x99s payment to the initial challenger (in return for not pressing the patent challenge) will not necessarily provoke subsequent challenges. Second, a generic that files a paragraph IV after learning that the first filer has settled will (if sued by the brand-name) have to wait out a stay period of (roughly) 30 months before the FDA may approve its application, just as the first filer did. See \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7355(j)(5)(B)(iii). These features together mean that a reverse payment settlement with the first filer (or, as in this case, all of the initial filers) \xe2\x80\x9cremoves from consideration the most motivated challenger, and the one closest to introducing competition.\xe2\x80\x9d Hemphill, supra, at 1586. The dissent may doubt these provisions matter, post, at 15\xe2\x80\x9317, but scholars in the field tell us that \xe2\x80\x9cwhere only one party owns a patent, it is virtually unheard of outside of pharmaceuticals for that party to pay an accused infringer to settle the lawsuit.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 H. Hovenkamp, M. Janis, M. Lemley, & C. Leslie, IP and Antitrust \xc2\xa715.3, p. 15\xe2\x80\x9345, n. 161 (2d ed. Supp. 2011). It may well be that Hatch-Waxman\xe2\x80\x99s unique regulatory framework, including the special advantage that the 180-day exclusivity period gives to first filers, does much to explain why in this context, but not others, the patentee\xe2\x80\x99s ordinary incentives to resist paying off challengers (i.e., the fear of provoking myriad other challengers) appear to be more frequently overcome. See 12 Areeda \xc2\xb62046, at 341 (3d ed. 2010) (noting that these provisions, no doubt unintentionally, have created special incentives for collusion).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSecond, these anticompetitive consequences will at least sometimes prove unjustified. See 7 id., \xc2\xb61504, at 410\xe2\x80\x93415 (3d ed. 2010); California Dental Assn. v. FTC, 526 U.\xc2\xa0S., 756, 786\xe2\x80\x93787 (1999) (Breyer, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part). As the FTC admits, offsetting or re- deeming virtues are sometimes present. Brief for Petitioner 37\xe2\x80\x9339. The reverse payment, for example, may amount to no more than a rough approximation of the litigation expenses saved through the settlement. That payment may reflect compensation for other services that the generic has promised to perform\xe2\x80\x94such as distributing the patented item or helping to develop a market for that item. There may be other justifications. Where a reverse payment reflects traditional settlement considerations, such as avoided litigation costs or fair value for services, there is not the same concern that a patentee is using its monopoly profits to avoid the risk of patent invalidation or a finding of noninfringement. In such cases, the parties may have provided for a reverse payment without having sought or brought about the anticompetitive consequences we mentioned above. But that possibility does not justify dismissing the FTC\xe2\x80\x99s complaint. An antitrust defendant may show in the antitrust proceeding that legitimate justifications are present, thereby explaining the presence of the challenged term and showing the lawfulness of that term under the rule of reason. See, e.g., Indiana Federation of Dentists, supra, at 459; 7 Areeda \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb61504a\xe2\x80\x931504b, at 401\xe2\x80\x93404 (3d ed. 2010).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThird, where a reverse payment threatens to work unjustified anticompetitive harm, the patentee likely pos- sesses the power to bring that harm about in practice. See id., \xc2\xb61503, at 392\xe2\x80\x93393. At least, the \xe2\x80\x9csize of the payment from a branded drug manufacturer to a prospective generic is itself a strong indicator of power\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94namely, the power to charge prices higher than the competitive level. 12 id., \xc2\xb62046, at 351. An important patent itself helps to assure such power. Neither is a firm without that power likely to pay \xe2\x80\x9clarge sums\xe2\x80\x9d to induce \xe2\x80\x9cothers to stay out of its market.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. In any event, the Commission has referred to studies showing that reverse payment agreements are associated with the presence of higher-than-competitive profits\xe2\x80\x94a strong indication of market power. See Brief for Petitioner 45.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFourth, an antitrust action is likely to prove more fea- sible administratively than the Eleventh Circuit believed. The Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s holding does avoid the need to litigate the patent\xe2\x80\x99s validity (and also, any question of infringement). But to do so, it throws the baby out with the bath water, and there is no need to take that drastic step. That is because it is normally not necessary to litigate patent validity to answer the antitrust question (unless, perhaps, to determine whether the patent litigation is a sham, see 677 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1312). An unexplained large reverse payment itself would normally suggest that the patentee has serious doubts about the patent\xe2\x80\x99s survival. And that fact, in turn, suggests that the payment\xe2\x80\x99s objective is to maintain supracompetitive prices to be shared among the patentee and the challenger rather than face what might have been a competitive market\xe2\x80\x94the very anticompetitive consequence that underlies the claim of antitrust unlawfulness. The owner of a particularly valuable patent might contend, of course, that even a small risk of invalidity justifies a large payment. But, be that as it may, the payment (if otherwise unexplained) likely seeks to prevent the risk of competition. And, as we have said, that consequence constitutes the relevant anticompetitive harm. In a word, the size of the unexplained reverse payment can provide a workable surrogate for a patent\xe2\x80\x99s weakness, all without forcing a court to conduct a detailed exploration of the validity of the patent itself. 12 Areeda \xc2\xb62046, at 350\xe2\x80\x93352.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFifth, the fact that a large, unjustified reverse payment risks antitrust liability does not prevent litigating parties from settling their lawsuit. They may, as in other industries, settle in other ways, for example, by allowing the generic manufacturer to enter the patentee\xe2\x80\x99s market prior to the patent\xe2\x80\x99s expiration, without the patentee paying the challenger to stay out prior to that point. Although the parties may have reasons to prefer settlements that include reverse payments, the relevant antitrust question is: What are those reasons? If the basic reason is a desire to maintain and to share patent-generated monopoly profits, then, in the absence of some other justification, the antitrust laws are likely to forbid the arrangement.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn sum, a reverse payment, where large and unjustified, can bring with it the risk of significant anticompetitive effects; one who makes such a payment may be unable to explain and to justify it; such a firm or individual may well possess market power derived from the patent; a court, by examining the size of the payment, may well be able to assess its likely anticompetitive effects along with its potential justifications without litigating the validity of the patent; and parties may well find ways to settle pa- tent disputes without the use of reverse payments. In our view, these considerations, taken together, outweigh the single strong consideration\xe2\x80\x94the desirability of settlements\xe2\x80\x94that led the Eleventh Circuit to provide near-automatic antitrust immunity to reverse payment settlements.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe FTC urges us to hold that reverse payment settlement agreements are presumptively unlawful and that courts reviewing such agreements should proceed via a \xe2\x80\x9cquick look\xe2\x80\x9d approach, rather than applying a \xe2\x80\x9crule of reason.\xe2\x80\x9d See California Dental, 526 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 775, n.\xc2\xa012 (\xe2\x80\x9cQuick-look analysis in effect\xe2\x80\x9d shifts to \xe2\x80\x9ca defendant the burden to show empirical evidence of procompetitive effects\xe2\x80\x9d); 7 Areeda \xc2\xb61508, at 435\xe2\x80\x93440 (3d ed. 2010). We decline to do so. In California Dental, we held (unanimously) that abandonment of the \xe2\x80\x9crule of reason\xe2\x80\x9d in favor of presumptive rules (or a \xe2\x80\x9cquick-look\xe2\x80\x9d approach) is appropriate only where \xe2\x80\x9can observer with even a rudimentary understanding of economics could conclude that the arrangements in question would have an anticompetitive effect on customers and markets.\xe2\x80\x9d 526 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 770; id., at 781 (Breyer, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part). We do not believe that reverse payment settlements, in the context we here discuss, meet this criterion.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThat is because the likelihood of a reverse payment bringing about anticompetitive effects depends upon its size, its scale in relation to the payor\xe2\x80\x99s anticipated future litigation costs, its independence from other services for which it might represent payment, and the lack of any other convincing justification. The existence and degree of any anticompetitive consequence may also vary as among industries. These complexities lead us to conclude that the FTC must prove its case as in other rule-of-reason cases.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo say this is not to require the courts to insist, contrary to what we have said, that the Commission need litigate the patent\xe2\x80\x99s validity, empirically demonstrate the virtues or vices of the patent system, present every possible supporting fact or refute every possible pro-defense theory. As a leading antitrust scholar has pointed out, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[t]here is always something of a sliding scale in appraising reason-ableness,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and as such \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the quality of proof required should vary with the circumstances.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d California Dental, supra, at 780 (quoting with approval 7 Areeda \xc2\xb61507, at 402 (1986)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs in other areas of law, trial courts can structure antitrust litigation so as to avoid, on the one hand, the use of antitrust theories too abbreviated to permit proper analysis, and, on the other, consideration of every possible fact or theory irrespective of the minimal light it may shed on the basic question\xe2\x80\x94that of the presence of sig-nificant unjustified anticompetitive consequences. See 7 id., \xc2\xb61508c, at 438\xe2\x80\x93440. We therefore leave to the lower courts the structuring of the present rule-of-reason antitrust litigation. We reverse the judgment of the Eleventh Circuit. And we remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIt is so ordered.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tFEDERAL TRADE COMMISSION, PETITIONER v. ACTAVIS, INC., et\xc2\xa0al.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tChief Justice Roberts, with whom Justice Scalia and Justice Thomas join, dissenting.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSolvay Pharmaceuticals holds a patent. It sued two generic drug manufacturers that it alleged were infringing that patent. Those companies counterclaimed, contending the patent was invalid and that, in any event, their products did not infringe. The parties litigated for three years before settling on these terms: Solvay agreed to pay the generics millions of dollars and to allow them into the market five years before the patent was set to expire; in exchange, the generics agreed to provide certain services (help with marketing and manufacturing) and to honor Solvay\xe2\x80\x99s patent. The Federal Trade Commission alleges that such a settlement violates the antitrust laws. The question is how to assess that claim.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA patent carves out an exception to the applicability of antitrust laws. The correct approach should therefore be to ask whether the settlement gives Solvay monopoly power beyond what the patent already gave it. The Court, however, departs from this approach, and would instead use antitrust law\xe2\x80\x99s amorphous rule of reason to inquire into the anticompetitive effects of such settlements. This novel approach is without support in any statute, and will discourage the settlement of patent litigation. I respectfully dissent.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe point of antitrust law is to encourage competitive markets to promote consumer welfare. The point of patent law is to grant limited monopolies as a way of encouraging innovation. Thus, a patent grants \xe2\x80\x9cthe right to exclude others from profiting by the patented invention.\xe2\x80\x9d Dawson Chemical Co. v. Rohm & Haas Co., \n\n448 U.\xc2\xa0S. 176, \n215 (1980)\n. In doing so it provides an exception to antitrust law, and the scope of the patent\xe2\x80\x94i.e., the rights conferred by the patent\xe2\x80\x94forms the zone within which the patent holder may operate without facing antitrust liability.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis should go without saying, in part because we\xe2\x80\x99ve said it so many times. Walker Process Equipment, Inc. v. Food Machinery & Chemical Corp., \n\n382 U.\xc2\xa0S. 172, \n177 (1965)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98A patent .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is an exception to the general rule against monopolies\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d); United States v. Line Material Co., \n\n333 U.\xc2\xa0S. 287, \n300 (1948)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he precise terms of the grant define the limits of a patentee\xe2\x80\x99s monopoly and the area in which the patentee is freed from competition\xe2\x80\x9d); United States v. General Elec. Co., \n\n272 U.\xc2\xa0S. 476, \n485 (1926)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is only when .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [the patentee] steps out of the scope of his patent rights\xe2\x80\x9d that he comes within the operation of the Sherman Act); Simpson v. Union Oil Co. of Cal., \n\n377 U.\xc2\xa0S. 13, \n24 (1964)\n (similar). Thus, although it is per se unlawful to fix prices under antitrust law, we have long recognized that a patent holder is entitled to license a competitor to sell its product on the condition that the competitor charge a certain, fixed price. See, e.g., General Elec. Co., supra, at 488\xe2\x80\x93490.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe have never held that it violates antitrust law for a competitor to refrain from challenging a patent. And by extension, we have long recognized that the settlement of patent litigation does not by itself violate the antitrust laws. Standard Oil Co. (Indiana) v. United States, \n\n283 U.\xc2\xa0S. 163, \n171 (1931)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cWhere there are legitimately conflicting claims or threatened interferences, a settlement by agreement, rather than litigation, is not precluded by the [Sherman] Act\xe2\x80\x9d). Like most litigation, patent litigation is settled all the time, and such settlements\xe2\x80\x94which can include agreements that clearly violate antitrust law, such as licenses that fix prices, or agreements among competitors to divide territory\xe2\x80\x94do not ordinarily subject the litigants to antitrust liability. See 1 H. Hovenkamp, M. Janis, M. Lemley, & C. Leslie, IP and Antitrust \xc2\xa77.3, pp. 7\xe2\x80\x9313 to 7\xe2\x80\x9315 (2d ed. 2003) (hereinafter Hovenkamp).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe key, of course, is that the patent holder\xe2\x80\x94when doing anything, including settling\xe2\x80\x94must act within the scope of the patent. If its actions go beyond the monopoly powers conferred by the patent, we have held that such actions are subject to antitrust scrutiny. See, e.g., United States v. Singer Mfg. Co., \n\n374 U.\xc2\xa0S. 174\n\xe2\x80\x93197 (1963). If its actions are within the scope of the patent, they are not subject to antitrust scrutiny, with two exceptions concededly not applicable here: (1) when the parties settle sham litigation, cf. Professional Real Estate Investors, Inc. v. Columbia Pictures Industries, Inc., \n\n508 U.\xc2\xa0S. 49\n\xe2\x80\x9361 (1993); and (2) when the litigation involves a patent obtained through fraud on the Patent and Trademark Office. Walker Process Equipment, supra, at 177.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThus, under our precedent, this is a fairly straight-forward case. Solvay paid a competitor to respect its patent\xe2\x80\x94conduct which did not exceed the scope of its patent. No one alleges that there was sham litigation, or that Solvay\xe2\x80\x99s patent was obtained through fraud on the PTO. As in any settlement, Solvay gave its competitors something of value (money) and, in exchange, its competitors gave it something of value (dropping their legal claims). In doing so, they put an end to litigation that had been dragging on for three years. Ordinarily, we would think this a good thing.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tToday, however, the Court announces a new rule. It is willing to accept that Solvay\xe2\x80\x99s actions did not exceed the scope of its patent. Ante, at 8. But it does not agree that this is enough to \xe2\x80\x9cimmunize the agreement from antitrust attack.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. According to the majority, if a patent holder settles litigation by paying an alleged infringer a \xe2\x80\x9clarge and unjustified\xe2\x80\x9d payment, in exchange for having the alleged infringer honor the patent, a court should employ the antitrust rule of reason to determine whether the settlement violates antitrust law. Ante, at 19.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court\xe2\x80\x99s justifications for this holding are unpersuasive. First, the majority explains that \xe2\x80\x9cthe patent here may or may not be valid, and may or may not be infringed.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8. Because there is \xe2\x80\x9cuncertainty\xe2\x80\x9d about whether the patent is actually valid, the Court says that any questions regarding the legality of the settlement should be \xe2\x80\x9cmeasur[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d by \xe2\x80\x9cprocompetitive antitrust policies,\xe2\x80\x9d rather than \xe2\x80\x9cpatent law policy.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9. This simply states the conclusion. The difficulty with such an approach is that a patent holder acting within the scope of its patent has an obvious defense to any antitrust suit: that its patent allows it to engage in conduct that would otherwise violate the antitrust laws. But again, that\xe2\x80\x99s the whole point of a patent: to confer a limited monopoly. The problem, as the Court correctly recognizes, is that we\xe2\x80\x99re not quite certain if the patent is actually valid, or if the competitor is infringing it. But that is always the case, and is plainly a question of patent law.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority, however, would assess those patent law issues according to \xe2\x80\x9cantitrust policies.\xe2\x80\x9d According to the majority, this is what the Court did in Line Material\xe2\x80\x94i.e., it \xe2\x80\x9caccommodat[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d antitrust principles and struck a \xe2\x80\x9cbalance\xe2\x80\x9d between patent and antitrust law. Ante, at 9. But the Court in Line Material did no such thing. Rather, it explained that it is \xe2\x80\x9cwell settled that the possession of a valid patent or patents does not give the patentee any exemption from the provisions of the Sherman Act beyond the limits of the patent monopoly.\xe2\x80\x9d 333 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 308 (emphasis added). It then, in the very next sentence, stated that \xe2\x80\x9c[b]y aggregating patents in one control, the holder of the patents cannot escape the prohibitions of the Sherman Act.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. That second sentence follows only if such conduct\xe2\x80\x94the aggregation of multiple patents\xe2\x80\x94goes \xe2\x80\x9cbeyond the limits of the patent monopoly,\xe2\x80\x9d which is precisely what the Court concluded. See id., at 312 (\xe2\x80\x9cThere is no suggestion in the patent statutes of authority to combine with other patent owners to fix prices on articles covered by the respective patents\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). The Court stressed, over and over, that a patent holder does not violate the antitrust laws when it acts within the scope of its patent. See id., at 305 (\xe2\x80\x9cWithin the limits of the patentee\xe2\x80\x99s rights under his patent, monopoly of the process or product by him is authorized by the patent statutes\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 310 (\xe2\x80\x9cprice limitations on patented devices beyond the limits of a patent monopoly violate the Sherman Act\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority suggests that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hether a particular restraint lies \xe2\x80\x98beyond the limits of the patent monopoly\xe2\x80\x99 is a conclusion that flows from\xe2\x80\x9d applying traditional antitrust principles. Ante, at 10. It seems to have in mind a regime where courts ignore the patent, and simply conduct an antitrust analysis of the settlement without regard to the validity of the patent. But a patent holder acting within the scope of its patent does not engage in any unlawful anticompetitive behavior; it is simply exercising the monopoly rights granted to it by the Government. Its behavior would be unlawful only if its patent were invalid or not infringed. And the scope of the patent\xe2\x80\x94i.e., what rights are conferred by the patent\xe2\x80\x94should be determined by reference to patent law. While it is conceivable to set up a legal system where you assess the validity of patents or questions of infringement by bringing an antitrust suit, neither the majority nor the Government suggests that Congress has done so.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSecond, the majority contends that \xe2\x80\x9cthis Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents make clear that patent-related settlement agreements can sometimes violate the antitrust laws.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10. For this carefully worded proposition, it cites Singer Manufacturing Co., United States v. New Wrinkle, Inc., \n\n342 U.\xc2\xa0S. 371 (1952)\n, and Standard Oil Co. (Indiana). But each of those cases stands for the same, uncontroversial point: that when a patent holder acts outside the scope of its patent, it is no longer protected from antitrust scrutiny by the patent.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo begin, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s description of Singer is inaccurate. In Singer, several patent holders with competing claims entered into a settlement agreement in which they cross-licensed their patents to each other, and did so in order to disadvantage Japanese competition. See 374 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 194\xe2\x80\x93195 (finding that the agreement had \xe2\x80\x9ca common purpose to suppress the Japanese machine competition in the United States\xe2\x80\x9d (footnote omitted)). According to the majority, the Court in Singer \xe2\x80\x9cdid not examine whether, on the assumption that all three patents were valid, patent law would have allowed the patents\xe2\x80\x99 hold- ers to do the same.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10. Rather, the majority contends, Singer held that this agreement violated the anti-trust laws because \xe2\x80\x9cin important part .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. \xe2\x80\x98the public interest in granting patent monopolies\xe2\x80\x99 exists only to the extent that \xe2\x80\x98the public is given a novel and useful invention\xe2\x80\x99 in \xe2\x80\x98consideration for its grant.\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Singer, 374 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 199 (White, J., concurring)). But the majority in Singer certainly did ask whether patent law permitted such an arrangement, concluding that it did not. See id., at 196\xe2\x80\x93197 (reiterating that it \xe2\x80\x9cis equally well settled that the possession of a valid patent or patents does not give the patentee any exemption from the provisions of the Sherman Act beyond the limits of the patent monopoly\xe2\x80\x9d and holding that \xe2\x80\x9cthose limitations have been exceeded in this case\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added; internal quotation marks omitted)); see also Hovenkamp \xc2\xa77.2b, at 7\xe2\x80\x938, n. 15 (citing Singer as a quintessential case in which patent holders were subject to antitrust liability because their settlement agreement went beyond the scope of their patents and thus conferred monopoly power beyond what the patent lawfully authorized). Even Justice White\xe2\x80\x99s concurrence, on which the majority relies, emphasized that the conduct at issue in Singer\xe2\x80\x94collusion between patent holders to exclude Japanese competition and to prevent disclosure of prior art\xe2\x80\x94was not authorized by the patent laws. 374 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 197, 200.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNew Wrinkle is to the same effect. There, the Court explained that because \xe2\x80\x9c[p]rice control through cross-licensing [is] barred as beyond the patent monopoly,\xe2\x80\x9d an \xe2\x80\x9carrangement . . . made between patent holders to pool their patents and fix prices on the products for themselves and their licensees\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. . plainly violate[s] the Sherman Act.\xe2\x80\x9d 342 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 379, 380 (emphasis added). As the Court further explained, a patent holder may not, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98acting in concert with all members of an industry .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. issue substantially identical licenses to all members of the industry under the terms of which the industry is completely regimented, the production of competitive unpatented products suppressed, a class of distributors squeezed out, and prices on unpatented products stabilized.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 379\xe2\x80\x93380 (quoting United States v. United States Gypsum Co., \n\n333 U.\xc2\xa0S. 364, \n400 (1948)\n). The majority here, however, ignores this discussion, and instead categorizes the case as \xe2\x80\x9capplying antitrust scrutiny to [a] patent settlement.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAgain, in Standard Oil Co. (Indiana), the parties settled claims regarding \xe2\x80\x9ccompeting patented processes for manufacturing an unpatented product,\xe2\x80\x9d which threatened to create a monopoly over the unpatented product. 283 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 175. The Court explained that \xe2\x80\x9can exchange of licenses for the purpose of curtailing the . . . supply of an unpatented product, is beyond the privileges conferred by the patents.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 174.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority is therefore right to suggest that these \xe2\x80\x9cprecedents make clear that patent-related settlement agreements can sometimes violate the antitrust laws.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10 (emphasis added). The key word is sometimes. And those some times are spelled out in our prece- dents. Those cases have made very clear that patent settlements\xe2\x80\x94and for that matter, any agreements relating to patents\xe2\x80\x94are subject to antitrust scrutiny if they confer benefits beyond the scope of the patent. This makes sense. A patent exempts its holder from the antitrust laws only insofar as the holder operates within the scope of the patent. When the holder steps outside the scope of the patent, he can no longer use the patent as his defense. The majority points to no case where a patent settlement was subject to antitrust scrutiny merely because the validity of the patent was uncertain. Not one. It is remarkable, and surely worth something, that in the 123 years since the Sherman Act was passed, we have never let antitrust law cross that Rubicon.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNext, the majority points to the \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral procompetitive thrust\xe2\x80\x9d of the Hatch-Waxman Act, the fact that Hatch-Waxman \xe2\x80\x9cfacilitat[es] challenges to a patent\xe2\x80\x99s validity,\xe2\x80\x9d and its \xe2\x80\x9cprovisions requiring parties to [such] patent dispute[s] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to report settlement terms to the FTC and the Antitrust Division of the Department of Justice.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13. The Hatch-Waxman Act surely seeks to encourage competition in the drug market. And, like every law, it accomplishes its ends through specific provisions. These provisions, for example, allow generic manufacturers to enter the market without undergoing a duplicative application process; they also grant a 180-day monopoly to the first qualifying generic to commercially market a competing product. See 21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7355(j)(2)(A)(ii), (iv), 355(j)(5)(B)(iv). So yes, the point of these provisions is to encourage competition. But it should by now be trite\xe2\x80\x94and unnecessary\xe2\x80\x94to say that \xe2\x80\x9cno legislation pursues its purposes at all costs\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9cit frustrates rather than effectuates legislative intent simplistically to assume that whatever furthers the statute\xe2\x80\x99s primary objective must be the law.\xe2\x80\x9d Rodriguez v. United States, \n\n480 U.\xc2\xa0S. 522\n\xe2\x80\x93526 (1987) (per curiam). It is especially disturbing here, where the Court discerns from specific provisions a very broad policy\xe2\x80\x94a \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral procompetitive thrust,\xe2\x80\x9d in its words\xe2\x80\x94and uses that policy to unsettle the established relationship between patent and antitrust law. Ante, at 13. Indeed, for whatever it may be worth, Congress has repeatedly declined to enact legislation addressing the issue the Court takes on today. See Brief for Actavis, Inc. 57 (citing 11 such bills introduced in the House or Senate since 2006).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn addition, it is of no consequence that settlement terms must be reported to the FTC and the Department of Justice. Such a requirement does not increase the role of antitrust law in scrutinizing patent settlements. Rather, it ensures that such terms are scrutinized consistent with existing antitrust law. In other words, it ensures that the FTC and Antitrust Division can review the settlements to make sure that they do not confer monopoly power beyond the scope of the patent.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority suggests that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]pparently most if not all reverse payment settlement agreements arise in the context of pharmaceutical drug regulation.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 2. This claim is not supported empirically by anything the majority cites, and seems unlikely. The term \xe2\x80\x9creverse payment agreement\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94coined to create the impression that such settlements are unique\xe2\x80\x94simply highlights the fact that the party suing ends up paying. But this is no anomaly, nor is it evidence of a nefarious plot; it simply results from the fact that the patent holder plaintiff is a defendant against an invalidity counterclaim\xe2\x80\x94not a rare situation in intellectual property litigation. Whatever one might call them, such settlements\xe2\x80\x94paying an alleged infringer to drop its invalidity claim\xe2\x80\x94are a well-known feature of intellectual property litigation, and reflect an intuitive way to settle such disputes. See Metro-Goldwyn Mayer, Inc. v. 007 Safety Prods., Inc., 183 F.\xc2\xa03d 10, 13 (CA1 1999); see also Schildkraut, Patent-Splitting Settlements and the Reverse Payment Fallacy, 71 Antitrust L.\xc2\xa0J. 1033, 1033, 1046\xe2\x80\x931049 (2004); Brief for Actavis 54, n.\xc2\xa020 (citing examples). To the extent there are not scores and scores of these settlements to point to, this is because such settlements\xe2\x80\x94outside the context of Hatch-Waxman\xe2\x80\x94are private agreements that for obvious reasons are generally not appealed, nor publicly available.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority suggests that reverse-payment agreements are distinct because \xe2\x80\x9ca party with no claim for damages .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. walks away with money simply so it will stay away from the patentee\xe2\x80\x99s market.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13. Again a distinction without a difference. While the alleged infringer may not be suing for the patent holder\xe2\x80\x99s money, it is suing for the right to use and market the (intellectual) property, which is worth money.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, the majority complains that nothing in \xe2\x80\x9cany patent statute\xe2\x80\x9d gives patent-holders the right to settle when faced with allegations of invalidity. Ante, at 12. But the right to settle generally accompanies the right to litigate in the first place; no one contends that drivers in an automobile accident may not settle their competing claims merely because no statute grants them that authority. The majority suggests that such a right makes it harder to \xe2\x80\x9celiminat[e] unwarranted patent grants.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. That may be so, but such a result\xe2\x80\x94true of all patent settlements\xe2\x80\x94is no reason to adjudicate questions of patent law under antitrust principles. Our cases establish that antitrust law has no business prying into a patent settlement so long as that settlement confers to the patent holder no monopoly power beyond what the patent itself conferred\xe2\x80\x94unless, of course, the patent was invalid, but that again is a question of patent law, not antitrust law.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn sum, none of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasons supports its conclusion that a patent holder, when settling a claim that its patent is invalid, is not immunized by the fact that it is acting within the scope of its patent. And I fear the Court\xe2\x80\x99s attempt to limit its holding to the context of patent settlements under Hatch-Waxman will not long hold.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority\xe2\x80\x99s rule will discourage settlement of patent litigation. Simply put, there would be no incentive to settle if, immediately after settling, the parties would have to litigate the same issue\xe2\x80\x94the question of patent validity\xe2\x80\x94as part of a defense against an antitrust suit. In that suit, the alleged infringer would be in the especially awkward position of being for the patent after being against it.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis is unfortunate because patent litigation is particularly complex, and particularly costly. As one treatise noted, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he median patent case that goes to trial costs each side $1.5 million in legal fees\xe2\x80\x9d alone. Hovenkamp \xc2\xa77.1c, at 7\xe2\x80\x935, n. 6. One study found that the cost of litigation in this specific context\xe2\x80\x94a generic challenging a brand name pharmaceutical patent\xe2\x80\x94was about $10 million per suit. See Herman, Note, The Stay Dilemma: Examining Brand and Generic Incentives for Delaying the Resolution of Pharmaceutical Patent Litigation, 111 Colum. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1788, 1795, n. 41 (2011) (citing M. Goodman, G. Nachman, & L. Chen, Morgan Stanley Equity Research, Quantifying the Impact from Authorized Generics 9 (2004)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court acknowledges these problems but nonetheless offers \xe2\x80\x9cfive sets of considerations\xe2\x80\x9d that it tells us overcome these concerns: (1) sometimes patent settlements will have \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98genuine adverse effects on competition\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d; (2) \xe2\x80\x9cthese anticompetitive consequences will at least sometimes prove unjustified\xe2\x80\x9d; (3) \xe2\x80\x9cwhere a reverse payment threatens to work unjustified anticompetitive harm, the patentee likely possesses the power to bring that harm about in practice\xe2\x80\x9d; (4) \xe2\x80\x9cit is normally not necessary to litigate patent validity to answer the antitrust question\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n unexplained large reverse payment itself would normally suggest that the patentee has serious doubts about the patent\xe2\x80\x99s survival,\xe2\x80\x9d and using a \xe2\x80\x9cpayment .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to prevent the risk of competition .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. constitutes the relevant anticompetitive harm\xe2\x80\x9d; and (5) parties may still \xe2\x80\x9csettle in other ways\xe2\x80\x9d such as \xe2\x80\x9cby allowing the generic manufacturer to enter the patentee\xe2\x80\x99s market prior to the patent\xe2\x80\x99s expiration, without the patentee paying the challenger to stay out prior to that point.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14\xe2\x80\x9319 (emphasis added).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAlmost all of these are unresponsive to the basic problem that settling a patent claim cannot possibly impose unlawful anticompetitive harm if the patent holder is acting within the scope of a valid patent and therefore permitted to do precisely what the antitrust suit claims is unlawful. This means that in any such antitrust suit, the defendant (patent holder) will want to use the validity of his patent as a defense\xe2\x80\x94in other words, he\xe2\x80\x99ll want to say \xe2\x80\x9cI can do this because I have a valid patent that lets me do this.\xe2\x80\x9d I therefore don\xe2\x80\x99t see how the majority can conclude that it won\xe2\x80\x99t normally be \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary to litigate patent validity to answer the antitrust question,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 18, unless it means to suggest that the defendant (patent holder) cannot raise his patent as a defense in an antitrust suit. But depriving him of such a defense\xe2\x80\x94if that\xe2\x80\x99s what the majority means to do\xe2\x80\x94defeats the point of the patent, which is to confer a lawful monopoly on its holder.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority seems to think that even if the patent is valid, a patent holder violates the antitrust laws merely because the settlement took away some chance that his patent would be declared invalid by a court. See ante, at 18\xe2\x80\x9319 (\xe2\x80\x9cpayment .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to prevent the risk of competition .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. constitutes the relevant anticompetitive harm\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). This is flawed for several reasons.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFirst, a patent is either valid or invalid. The parties of course don\xe2\x80\x99t know the answer with certainty at the outset of litigation; hence the litigation. But the same is true of any hard legal question that is yet to be adjudicated. Just because people don\xe2\x80\x99t know the answer doesn\xe2\x80\x99t mean there is no answer until a court declares one. Yet the majority would impose antitrust liability based on the parties\xe2\x80\x99 subjective uncertainty about that legal conclusion.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court does so on the assumption that offering a \xe2\x80\x9clarge\xe2\x80\x9d sum is reliable evidence that the patent holder has serious doubts about the patent. Not true. A patent holder may be 95% sure about the validity of its patent, but particularly risk averse or litigation averse, and willing to pay a good deal of money to rid itself of the 5% chance of a finding of invalidity. What is actually motivating a patent holder is apparently a question district courts will have to resolve on a case-by-case basis. The task of trying to discern whether a patent holder is motivated by uncertainty about its patent, or other legitimate factors like risk aversion, will be made all the more difficult by the fact that much of the evidence about the party\xe2\x80\x99s motivation may be embedded in legal advice from its attorney, which would presumably be shielded from discovery.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSecond, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s position leads to absurd results. Let\xe2\x80\x99s say in 2005, a patent holder sues a competitor for infringement and faces a counterclaim that its patent is invalid. The patent holder determines that the risk of losing on the question of validity is low, but after a year of litigating, grows increasingly risk averse, tired of litigation, and concerned about the company\xe2\x80\x99s image, so it pays the competitor a \xe2\x80\x9clarge\xe2\x80\x9d payment, ante, at 18, in exchange for having the competitor honor its patent. Then let\xe2\x80\x99s say in 2006, a different competitor, inspired by the first competitor\xe2\x80\x99s success, sues the patent holder and seeks a similar payment. The patent holder, recognizing that this dynamic is unsustainable, litigates this suit to conclusion, all the way to the Supreme Court, which unanimously decides the patent was valid. According to the majority, the first settlement would violate the antitrust laws even though the patent was ultimately declared valid, because that first settlement took away some chance that the patent would be invalidated in the first go around. Under this approach, a patent holder may be found liable under antitrust law for doing what its perfectly valid patent allowed it to do in the first place; its sin was to settle, rather than prove the correctness of its position by litigating until the bitter end.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThird, this logic\xe2\x80\x94that taking away any chance that a patent will be invalidated is itself an antitrust problem\xe2\x80\x94cannot possibly be limited to reverse-payment agreements, or those that are \xe2\x80\x9clarge.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The Government\xe2\x80\x99s brief acknowledges as much, suggesting that if antitrust scru-tiny is invited for such cash payments, it may also be required for \xe2\x80\x9cother consideration\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9calternative arrange-ments.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 36, n.\xc2\xa07. For example, when a patent holder licenses its product to a licensee at a fixed monopoly price, surely it takes away some chance that its patent will be challenged by that licensee. According to the majority\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning, that\xe2\x80\x99s an antitrust problem that must be analyzed under the rule of reason. But see General Elec. Co., 272 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 488 (holding that a patent holder may license its invention at a fixed price). Indeed, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s own solution\xe2\x80\x94that patent holders should negotiate to allow generics into the market sooner, rather than paying them money\xe2\x80\x94also takes away some chance that the generic would have litigated until the patent was invalidated.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThus, although the question posed by this case is fundamentally a question of patent law\xe2\x80\x94i.e., whether Solvay\xe2\x80\x99s patent was valid and therefore permitted Solvay to pay competitors to honor the scope of its patent\xe2\x80\x94the majority declares that such questions should henceforth be scrutinized by antitrust law\xe2\x80\x99s unruly rule of reason. Good luck to the district courts that must, when faced with a patent settlement, weigh the \xe2\x80\x9clikely anticompetitive effects, redeeming virtues, market power, and potentially offsetting legal considerations present in the circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310; but see Pacific Bell Telephone Co. v. linkLine Communications, Inc., \n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 438, \n452 (2009)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cWe have repeatedly emphasized the importance of clear rules in antitrust law\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIV\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority invokes \xe2\x80\x9cprocompetitive antitrust policies,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 9, but misses the basic point that patent laws promote consumer interests in a different way, by pro-viding protection against competition. As one treatise explains:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cThe purpose of the rule of reason is to determine whether, on balance, a practice is reasonably likely to be anticompetitive or competitively harmless\xe2\x80\x94that is, whether it yields lower or higher marketwide output. By contrast, patent policy encompasses a set of judgments about the proper tradeoff between competition and the incentive to innovate over the long run. Antitrust\xe2\x80\x99s rule of reason was not designed for such judgments and is not adept at making them.\xe2\x80\x9d Hovenkamp \xc2\xa77.3, at 7\xe2\x80\x9313 (footnote omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority recognizes that \xe2\x80\x9ca high reverse payment\xe2\x80\x9d may \xe2\x80\x9csignal to other potential challengers that the patentee lacks confidence in its patent, thereby provoking addi-tional challenges.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16. It brushes this off, how-ever, because of two features of Hatch-Waxman that make it \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98not necessarily so.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. First, it points out that the first challenger gets a 180-day exclusive period to market a generic version of the brand name drug, and that subsequent challengers cannot secure that exclusivity period\xe2\x80\x94meaning when the patent holder buys off the first challenger, it has bought off its most motivated competitor. There are two problems with this argument. First, according to the Food and Drug Administration, all manufacturers who file on the first day are considered \xe2\x80\x9cfirst applicants\xe2\x80\x9d who share the exclusivity period. Thus, if ten gener- ics file an application to market a generic drug on the first day, all will be considered \xe2\x80\x9cfirst applicants.\xe2\x80\x9d See \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7355(j)(5)(B)(iv)(II)(bb); see also FDA, Guid- ance for Industry: 180-Day Exclusivity When Multiple ANDAs Are Submitted on the Same Day 4 (July 2003). This is not an unusual occurrence. See Brief for Generic Pharmaceutical Association as Amicus Curiae 23\xe2\x80\x9324 (citing FTC data indicating that some drugs \xe2\x80\x9chave been subject to as many as sixteen first-day\xe2\x80\x9d generic applications; that in 2005, the average number of first-day applications per drug was 11; and that between 2002 and 2008, the yearly average never dropped below three first-day applications per drug).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSecond, and more fundamentally, the 180 days of exclusivity simply provides more incentive for generic challenges. Even if a subsequent generic would not be entitled to this additional incentive, it will have as much or nearly as much incentive to challenge the patent as a potential challenger would in any other context outside of Hatch-Waxman, where there is no 180-day exclusivity period. And a patent holder who gives away notably large sums of money because it is, as the majority surmises, concerned about the strength of its patent, would be putting blood in water where sharks are always near.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority also points to the fact that, under Hatch-Waxman, the FDA is enjoined from approving a generic\xe2\x80\x99s application to market a drug for 30 months if the brand name sues the generic for patent infringement within 45 days of that application being filed. Ante, at 16 (citing \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7355(j)(5)(B)(iii)). According to the majority, this provision will chill subsequent generics from challenging the patent (because they will have to wait 30 months before receiving FDA approval to market their drug). But this overlooks an important feature of the law: the FDA may approve the application before the 30 months are up \xe2\x80\x9cif before the expiration of [the 30 months,] the district court decides that the patent is invalid or not infringed.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7355(j)(5)(B)(iii)(I). And even if the FDA did not have to wait 30 months, it is far from clear that a generic would want to market a drug prior to obtaining a judgment of invalidity or noninfringement. Doing so may expose it to ruinous liability for infringement.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe irony of all this is that the majority\xe2\x80\x99s decision may very well discourage generics from challenging pharmaceutical patents in the first place. Patent litigation is costly, time consuming, and uncertain. See Cybor Corp. v. FAS Techs., Inc., 138 F.\xc2\xa03d 1448, 1476, n.\xc2\xa04 (CA Fed. 1998) (opinion of Rader, J.) (en banc) (discussing study showing that the Federal Circuit wholly or partially reversed in almost 40 percent of claim construction appeals in a 30-month period); Brief for Generic Pharmaceutical Association as Amicus Curiae 16 (citing a 2010 study analyzing the prior decade\xe2\x80\x99s cases and showing that generics prevailed in 82 cases and lost in 89 cases). Generics \xe2\x80\x9center this risky terrain only after careful analysis of the potential gains if they prevail and the potential exposure if they lose.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 19. Taking the prospect of settlements off the table\xe2\x80\x94or limiting settlements to an earlier entry date for the generic, which may still be many years in the future\xe2\x80\x94puts a damper on the generic\xe2\x80\x99s expected value going into litigation, and decreases its incentive to sue in the first place. The majority assures us, with no support, that everything will be okay because the parties can settle by simply negotiating an earlier entry date for the generic drug manufacturer, rather than settling with money. Ante, at 17. But it\xe2\x80\x99s a matter of common sense, confirmed by experience, that parties are more likely to settle when they have a broader set of valuable things to trade. See Brief for Mediation and Negotiation Professionals as Amici Curiae 6\xe2\x80\x938.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tV\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority today departs from the settled approach separating patent and antitrust law, weakens the protections afforded to innovators by patents, frustrates the public policy in favor of settling, and likely undermines the very policy it seeks to promote by forcing generics who step into the litigation ring to do so without the prospect of cash settlements. I would keep things as they were and not subject basic questions of patent law to an unbounded inquiry under antitrust law, with its treble damages and famously burdensome discovery. See \n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa715; Bell Atlantic Corp. v. Twombly, \n\n550 U.\xc2\xa0S. 544\n\xe2\x80\x93559 (2007). I respectfully dissent.\n\t\t\t
