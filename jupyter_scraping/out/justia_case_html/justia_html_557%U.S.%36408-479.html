SAFFORD UNIFIED SCHOOL DISTRICT #1 et\xc2\xa0al. v. REDDING

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 08\xe2\x80\x93479.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 21, 2009\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 25, 2009

\n

After escorting 13-year-old Savana Redding from her middle school classroom to his office, Assistant Principal Wilson showed her a day planner containing knives and other contraband. She admitted owning the planner, but said that she had lent it to her friend Marissa and that the contraband was not hers. He then produced four prescription-strength, and one over-the-counter, pain relief pills, all of which are banned under school rules without advance permission. She denied knowledge of them, but Wilson said that he had a report that she was giving pills to fellow students. She denied it and agreed to let him search her belongings. He and Helen Romero, an administrative assistant, searched Savana\xe2\x80\x99s backpack, finding nothing. Wilson then had Romero take Savana to the school nurse\xe2\x80\x99s office to search her clothes for pills. After Romero and the nurse, Peggy Schwallier, had Savana remove her outer clothing, they told her to pull her bra out and shake it, and to pull out the elastic on her underpants, thus exposing her breasts and pelvic area to some degree. No pills were found. Savana\xe2\x80\x99s mother filed suit against petitioner school district (Safford), Wilson, Romero, and Schwallier, alleging that the strip search violated Savana\xe2\x80\x99s Fourth Amendment rights. Claiming qualified immunity, the individuals (hereinafter petitioners) moved for summary judgment. The District Court granted the motion, finding that there was no Fourth Amendment violation, and the en banc Ninth Circuit reversed. Following the protocol for evaluating qualified immunity claims, see Saucier v. Katz, 533 U. S. 194, 200, the court held that the strip search was unjustified under the Fourth Amendment test for searches of children by school officials set out in New Jersey v. T. L. O., 469 U. S. 325. It then applied the test for qualified immunity. Finding that Savana\xe2\x80\x99s right was clearly established at the time of the search, it reversed the summary judgment as to Wilson, but affirmed as to Schwallier and Romero because they were not independent decisionmakers.

\n

Held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0The search of Savana\xe2\x80\x99s underwear violated the Fourth Amendment. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0For school searches, \xe2\x80\x9cthe public interest is best served by a Fourth Amendment standard of reasonableness that stops short of probable cause.\xe2\x80\x9d T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O., 469 U. S., at 341. Under the resulting reasonable suspicion standard, a school search \xe2\x80\x9cwill be permissible \xe2\x80\xa6 when the measures adopted are reasonably related to the objectives of the search and not excessively intrusive in light of the age and sex of the student and the nature of the infraction.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 342. The required knowledge component of reasonable suspicion for a school administrator\xe2\x80\x99s evidence search is that it raise a moderate chance of finding evidence of wrongdoing. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Wilson had sufficient suspicion to justify searching Savana\xe2\x80\x99s backpack and outer clothing. A week earlier, a student, Jordan, had told the principal and Wilson that students were bringing drugs and weapons to school and that he had gotten sick from some pills. On the day of the search, Jordan gave Wilson a pill that he said came from Marissa. Learning that the pill was prescription strength, Wilson called Marissa out of class and was handed the day planner. Once in his office, Wilson, with Romero present, had Marissa turn out her pockets and open her wallet, producing, inter alia, an over-the-counter pill that Marissa claimed was Savana\xe2\x80\x99s. She also denied knowing about the day planner\xe2\x80\x99s contents. Wilson did not ask her when she received the pills from Savana or where Savana might be hiding them. After a search of Marissa\xe2\x80\x99s underwear by Romero and Schwallier revealed no additional pills, Wilson called Savana into his office. He showed her the day planner and confirmed her relationship with Marissa. He knew that the girls had been identified as part of an unusually rowdy group at a school dance, during which alcohol and cigarettes were found in the girls\xe2\x80\x99 bathroom. He had other reasons to connect them with this contraband, for Jordan had told the principal that before the dance, he had attended a party at Savana\xe2\x80\x99s house where alcohol was served. Thus, Marissa\xe2\x80\x99s statement that the pills came from Savana was sufficiently plausible to warrant suspicion that Savana was involved in pill distribution. A student who is reasonably suspected of giving out contraband pills is reasonably suspected of carrying them on her person and in her backpack. Looking into Savana\xe2\x80\x99s bag, in her presence and in the relative privacy of Wilson\xe2\x80\x99s office, was not excessively intrusive, any more than Romero\xe2\x80\x99s subsequent search of her outer clothing. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Because the suspected facts pointing to Savana did not indicate that the drugs presented a danger to students or were concealed in her underwear, Wilson did not have sufficient suspicion to warrant extending the search to the point of making Savana pull out her underwear. Romero and Schwallier said that they did not see anything when Savana pulled out her underwear, but a strip search and its Fourth Amendment consequences are not defined by who was looking and how much was seen. Savana\xe2\x80\x99s actions in their presence necessarily exposed her breasts and pelvic area to some degree, and both subjective and reasonable societal expectations of personal privacy support the treatment of such a search as categorically distinct, requiring distinct elements of justification on the part of school authorities for going beyond a search of outer clothing and belongings. Savana\xe2\x80\x99s subjective expectation of privacy is inherent in her account of it as embarrassing, frightening, and humiliating. The reasonableness of her expectation is indicated by the common reaction of other young people similarly searched, whose adolescent vulnerability intensifies the exposure\xe2\x80\x99s patent intrusiveness. Its indignity does not outlaw the search, but it does implicate the rule that \xe2\x80\x9cthe search [be] \xe2\x80\x98reasonably related in scope to the circumstances which justified the interference in the first place.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O., supra, at 341. Here, the content of the suspicion failed to match the degree of intrusion. Because Wilson knew that the pills were common pain relievers, he must have known of their nature and limited threat and had no reason to suspect that large amounts were being passed around or that individual students had great quantities. Nor could he have suspected that Savana was hiding common painkillers in her underwear. When suspected facts must support the categorically extreme intrusiveness of a search down to an adolescent\xe2\x80\x99s body, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 general belief that students hide contraband in their clothing falls short; a reasonable search that extensive calls for suspicion that it will succeed. Nondangerous school contraband does not conjure up the specter of stashes in intimate places, and there is no evidence of such behavior at the school; neither Jordan nor Marissa suggested that Savana was doing that, and the search of Marissa yielded nothing. Wilson also never determined when Marissa had received the pills from Savana; had it been a few days before, that would weigh heavily against any reasonable conclusion that Savana presently had the pills on her person, much less in her underwear. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0Although the strip search violated Savana\xe2\x80\x99s Fourth Amendment rights, petitioners Wilson, Romero, and Schwallier are protected from liability by qualified immunity because \xe2\x80\x9cclearly established law [did] not show that the search violated the Fourth Amendment,\xe2\x80\x9d Pearson v. Callahan, 555 U. S. ___, ___. The intrusiveness of the strip search here cannot, under T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O., be seen as justifiably related to the circumstances, but lower court cases viewing school strip searches differently are numerous enough, with well-reasoned majority and dissenting opinions, to counsel doubt about the clarity with which the right was previously stated. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa03.\xc2\xa0The issue of petitioner Safford\xe2\x80\x99s liability under Monell v. New York City Dept. of Social Servs., 436 U. S. 658, 694, should be addressed on remand. P.\xc2\xa013.

SAFFORD UNIFIED SCHOOL DISTRICT #1, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. APRIL REDDING

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[June 25, 2009]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Souter delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The issue here is whether a 13-year-old student\xe2\x80\x99s Fourth Amendment right was violated when she was subjected to a search of her bra and underpants by school officials acting on reasonable suspicion that she had brought forbidden prescription and over-the-counter drugs to school. Because there were no reasons to suspect the drugs presented a danger or were concealed in her underwear, we hold that the search did violate the Constitution, but because there is reason to question the clarity with which the right was established, the official who ordered the unconstitutional search is entitled to qualified immunity from liability.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The events immediately prior to the search in question began in 13-year-old Savana Redding\xe2\x80\x99s math class at Safford Middle School one October day in 2003. The assistant principal of the school, Kerry Wilson, came into the room and asked Savana to go to his office. There, he showed her a day planner, unzipped and open flat on his desk, in which there were several knives, lighters, a permanent marker, and a cigarette. Wilson asked Savana whether the planner was hers; she said it was, but that a few days before she had lent it to her friend, Marissa Glines. Savana stated that none of the items in the planner belonged to her.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Wilson then showed Savana four white prescription-strength ibuprofen 400-mg pills, and one over-the-counter blue naproxen 200-mg pill, all used for pain and inflammation but banned under school rules without advance permission. He asked Savana if she knew anything about the pills. Savana answered that she did not. Wilson then told Savana that he had received a report that she was giving these pills to fellow students; Savana denied it and agreed to let Wilson search her belongings. Helen Romero, an administrative assistant, came into the office, and together with Wilson they searched Savana\xe2\x80\x99s backpack, finding nothing.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At that point, Wilson instructed Romero to take Savana to the school nurse\xe2\x80\x99s office to search her clothes for pills. Romero and the nurse, Peggy Schwallier, asked Savana to remove her jacket, socks, and shoes, leaving her in stretch pants and a T-shirt (both without pockets), which she was then asked to remove. Finally, Savana was told to pull her bra out and to the side and shake it, and to pull out the elastic on her underpants, thus exposing her breasts and pelvic area to some degree. No pills were found.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Savana\xe2\x80\x99s mother filed suit against Safford Unified School District #1, Wilson, Romero, and Schwallier for conducting a strip search in violation of Savana\xe2\x80\x99s Fourth Amendment rights. The individuals (hereinafter petitioners) moved for summary judgment, raising a defense of qualified immunity. The District Court for the District of Arizona granted the motion on the ground that there was no Fourth Amendment violation, and a panel of the Ninth Circuit affirmed. 504 F.\xc2\xa03d 828 (2007).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A closely divided Circuit sitting en banc, however, reversed. Following the two-step protocol for evaluating claims of qualified immunity, see Saucier v. Katz, 533 U. S. 194, 200 (2001), the Ninth Circuit held that the strip search was unjustified under the Fourth Amendment test for searches of children by school officials set out in New Jersey v. T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O., 469 U. S. 325 (1985). 531 F.\xc2\xa03d 1071, 1081\xe2\x80\x931087 (2008). The Circuit then applied the test for qualified immunity, and found that Savana\xe2\x80\x99s right was clearly established at the time of the search: \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[t]hese notions of personal privacy are \xe2\x80\x9cclearly established\xe2\x80\x9d in that they inhere in all of us, particularly middle school teenagers, and are inherent in the privacy component of the Fourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s proscription against unreasonable searches.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1088\xe2\x80\x931089 (quoting Brannum v. Overton Cty. School Bd., 516 F.\xc2\xa03d 489, 499 (CA6 2008)). The upshot was reversal of summary judgment as to Wilson, while affirming the judgments in favor of Schwallier, the school nurse, and Romero, the administrative assistant, since they had not acted as independent decisionmakers. 531 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1089.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari, 555 U. S. ___ (2009), and now affirm in part, reverse in part, and remand.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Fourth Amendment \xe2\x80\x9cright of the people to be secure in their persons \xe2\x80\xa6 against unreasonable searches and seizures\xe2\x80\x9d generally requires a law enforcement officer to have probable cause for conducting a search. \xe2\x80\x9cProbable cause exists where \xe2\x80\x98the facts and circumstances within [an officer\xe2\x80\x99s] knowledge and of which [he] had reasonably trustworthy information [are] sufficient in themselves to warrant a man of reasonable caution in the belief that\xe2\x80\x99 an offense has been or is being committed,\xe2\x80\x9d Brinegar v. United States, 338 U. S. 160, 175\xe2\x80\x93176 (1949) (quoting Carroll v. United States, 267 U. S. 132, 162 (1925)), and that evidence bearing on that offense will be found in the place to be searched.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O., we recognized that the school setting \xe2\x80\x9crequires some modification of the level of suspicion of illicit activity needed to justify a search,\xe2\x80\x9d 469 U. S., at 340, and held that for searches by school officials \xe2\x80\x9ca careful balancing of governmental and private interests suggests that the public interest is best served by a Fourth Amendment standard of reasonableness that stops short of probable cause,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 341. We have thus applied a standard of reasonable suspicion to determine the legality of a school administrator\xe2\x80\x99s search of a student, id., at 342, 345, and have held that a school search \xe2\x80\x9cwill be permissible in its scope when the measures adopted are reasonably related to the objectives of the search and not excessively intrusive in light of the age and sex of the student and the nature of the infraction,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 342.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A number of our cases on probable cause have an implicit bearing on the reliable knowledge element of reasonable suspicion, as we have attempted to flesh out the knowledge component by looking to the degree to which known facts imply prohibited conduct, see, e.g., Adams v. Williams, 407 U. S. 143, 148 (1972); id., at 160, n.\xc2\xa09 (Marshall, J., dissenting), the specificity of the information received, see, e.g., Spinelli v. United States, 393 U. S. 410, 416\xe2\x80\x93417 (1969), and the reliability of its source, see, e.g., Aguilar v. Texas, 378 U. S. 108, 114 (1964). At the end of the day, however, we have realized that these factors cannot rigidly control, Illinois v. Gates, 462 U. S. 213, 230 (1983), and we have come back to saying that the standards are \xe2\x80\x9cfluid concepts that take their substantive content from the particular contexts\xe2\x80\x9d in which they are being assessed. Ornelas v. United States, 517 U. S. 690, 696 (1996).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Perhaps the best that can be said generally about the required knowledge component of probable cause for a law enforcement officer\xe2\x80\x99s evidence search is that it raise a \xe2\x80\x9cfair probability,\xe2\x80\x9d Gates, 462 U. S., at 238, or a \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial chance,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 244, n.\xc2\xa013, of discovering evidence of criminal activity. The lesser standard for school searches could as readily be described as a moderate chance of finding evidence of wrongdoing.

\n

III

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this case, the school\xe2\x80\x99s policies strictly prohibit the nonmedical use, possession, or sale of any drug on school grounds, including \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[a]ny prescription or over-the-counter drug, except those for which permission to use in school has been granted pursuant to Board policy.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 128a.[Footnote 1] A week before Savana was searched, another student, Jordan Romero (no relation of the school\xe2\x80\x99s administrative assistant), told the principal and Assistant Principal Wilson that \xe2\x80\x9ccertain students were bringing drugs and weapons on campus,\xe2\x80\x9d and that he had been sick after taking some pills that \xe2\x80\x9che got from a classmate.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 8a. On the morning of October 8, the same boy handed Wilson a white pill that he said Marissa Glines had given him. He told Wilson that students were planning to take the pills at lunch.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Wilson learned from Peggy Schwallier, the school nurse, that the pill was Ibuprofen 400 mg, available only by prescription. Wilson then called Marissa out of class. Outside the classroom, Marissa\xe2\x80\x99s teacher handed Wilson the day planner, found within Marissa\xe2\x80\x99s reach, containing various contraband items. Wilson escorted Marissa back to his office.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the presence of Helen Romero, Wilson requested Marissa to turn out her pockets and open her wallet. Marissa produced a blue pill, several white ones, and a razor blade. Wilson asked where the blue pill came from, and Marissa answered, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98I guess it slipped in when she gave me the IBU 400s.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 13a. When Wilson asked whom she meant, Marissa replied, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Savana Redding.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Wilson then enquired about the day planner and its contents; Marissa denied knowing anything about them. Wilson did not ask Marissa any followup questions to determine whether there was any likelihood that Savana presently had pills: neither asking when Marissa received the pills from Savana nor where Savana might be hiding them.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Schwallier did not immediately recognize the blue pill, but information provided through a poison control hotline[Footnote 2] indicated that the pill was a 200-mg dose of an antiinflammatory drug, generically called naproxen, available over the counter. At Wilson\xe2\x80\x99s direction, Marissa was then subjected to a search of her bra and underpants by Romero and Schwallier, as Savana was later on. The search revealed no additional pills.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It was at this juncture that Wilson called Savana into his office and showed her the day planner. Their conversation established that Savana and Marissa were on friendly terms: while she denied knowledge of the contraband, Savana admitted that the day planner was hers and that she had lent it to Marissa. Wilson had other reports of their friendship from staff members, who had identified Savana and Marissa as part of an unusually rowdy group at the school\xe2\x80\x99s opening dance in August, during which alcohol and cigarettes were found in the girls\xe2\x80\x99 bathroom. Wilson had reason to connect the girls with this contraband, for Wilson knew that Jordan Romero had told the principal that before the dance, he had been at a party at Savana\xe2\x80\x99s house where alcohol was served. Marissa\xe2\x80\x99s statement that the pills came from Savana was thus sufficiently plausible to warrant suspicion that Savana was involved in pill distribution.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This suspicion of Wilson\xe2\x80\x99s was enough to justify a search of Savana\xe2\x80\x99s backpack and outer clothing.[Footnote 3] If a student is reasonably suspected of giving out contraband pills, she is reasonably suspected of carrying them on her person and in the carryall that has become an item of student uniform in most places today. If Wilson\xe2\x80\x99s reasonable suspicion of pill distribution were not understood to support searches of outer clothes and backpack, it would not justify any search worth making. And the look into Savana\xe2\x80\x99s bag, in her presence and in the relative privacy of Wilson\xe2\x80\x99s office, was not excessively intrusive, any more than Romero\xe2\x80\x99s subsequent search of her outer clothing.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Here it is that the parties part company, with Savana\xe2\x80\x99s claim that extending the search at Wilson\xe2\x80\x99s behest to the point of making her pull out her underwear was constitutionally unreasonable. The exact label for this final step in the intrusion is not important, though strip search is a fair way to speak of it. Romero and Schwallier directed Savana to remove her clothes down to her underwear, and then \xe2\x80\x9cpull out\xe2\x80\x9d her bra and the elastic band on her underpants. Id., at 23a. Although Romero and Schwallier stated that they did not see anything when Savana followed their instructions, App. to Pet. for Cert. 135a, we would not define strip search and its Fourth Amendment consequences in a way that would guarantee litigation about who was looking and how much was seen. The very fact of Savana\xe2\x80\x99s pulling her underwear away from her body in the presence of the two officials who were able to see her necessarily exposed her breasts and pelvic area to some degree, and both subjective and reasonable societal expectations of personal privacy support the treatment of such a search as categorically distinct, requiring distinct elements of justification on the part of school authorities for going beyond a search of outer clothing and belongings.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Savana\xe2\x80\x99s subjective expectation of privacy against such a search is inherent in her account of it as embarrassing, frightening, and humiliating. The reasonableness of her expectation (required by the Fourth Amendment standard) is indicated by the consistent experiences of other young people similarly searched, whose adolescent vulnerability intensifies the patent intrusiveness of the exposure. See Brief for National Association of Social Workers et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 6\xe2\x80\x9314; Hyman & Perone, The Other Side of School Violence: Educator Policies and Practices that may Contribute to Student Misbehavior, 36 J. School Psychology 7, 13 (1998) (strip search can \xe2\x80\x9cresult in serious emotional damage\xe2\x80\x9d). The common reaction of these adolescents simply registers the obviously different meaning of a search exposing the body from the experience of nakedness or near undress in other school circumstances. Changing for gym is getting ready for play; exposing for a search is responding to an accusation reserved for suspected wrongdoers and fairly understood as so degrading that a number of communities have decided that strip searches in schools are never reasonable and have\nbanned them no matter what the facts may\nbe, see, e.g., New York City Dept. of Education, Reg. No. A\xe2\x80\x93432, p. 2 (2005), online at http://docs.nycenet.edu/\ndocushare/dsweb/Get/Document-21/A-432.pdf (\xe2\x80\x9cUnder no circumstances shall a strip-search of a student be\nconducted\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The indignity of the search does not, of course, outlaw it, but it does implicate the rule of reasonableness as stated in T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O., that \xe2\x80\x9cthe search as actually conducted [be] reasonably related in scope to the circumstances which justified the interference in the first place.\xe2\x80\x9d 469 U. S., at 341 (internal quotation marks omitted). The scope will be permissible, that is, when it is \xe2\x80\x9cnot excessively intrusive in light of the age and sex of the student and the nature of the infraction.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 342.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Here, the content of the suspicion failed to match the degree of intrusion. Wilson knew beforehand that the pills were prescription-strength ibuprofen and over-the-counter naproxen, common pain relievers equivalent to two Advil, or one Aleve.[Footnote 4] He must have been aware of the nature and limited threat of the specific drugs he was searching for, and while just about anything can be taken in quantities that will do real harm, Wilson had no reason to suspect that large amounts of the drugs were being passed around, or that individual students were receiving great numbers of pills.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor could Wilson have suspected that Savana was hiding common painkillers in her underwear. Petitioners suggest, as a truth universally acknowledged, that \xe2\x80\x9cstudents \xe2\x80\xa6 hid[e] contraband in or under their clothing,\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief for Petitioners 8, and cite a smattering of cases of students with contraband in their underwear, id., at 8\xe2\x80\x939. But when the categorically extreme intrusiveness of a search down to the body of an adolescent requires some justification in suspected facts, general background possibilities fall short; a reasonable search that extensive calls for suspicion that it will pay off. But nondangerous school contraband does not raise the specter of stashes in intimate places, and there is no evidence in the record of any general practice among Safford Middle School students of hiding that sort of thing in underwear; neither Jordan nor Marissa suggested to Wilson that Savana was doing that, and the preceding search of Marissa that Wilson ordered yielded nothing. Wilson never even determined when Marissa had received the pills from Savana; if it had been a few days before, that would weigh heavily against any reasonable conclusion that Savana presently had the pills on her person, much less in her underwear.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, what was missing from the suspected facts that pointed to Savana was any indication of danger to the students from the power of the drugs or their quantity, and any reason to suppose that Savana was carrying pills in her underwear. We think that the combination of these deficiencies was fatal to finding the search reasonable.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In so holding, we mean to cast no ill reflection on the assistant principal, for the record raises no doubt that his motive throughout was to eliminate drugs from his school and protect students from what Jordan Romero had gone through. Parents are known to overreact to protect their children from danger, and a school official with responsibility for safety may tend to do the same. The difference is that the Fourth Amendment places limits on the official, even with the high degree of deference that courts must pay to the educator\xe2\x80\x99s professional judgment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We do mean, though, to make it clear that the T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O. concern to limit a school search to reasonable scope requires the support of reasonable suspicion of danger or of resort to underwear for hiding evidence of wrongdoing before a search can reasonably make the quantum leap from outer clothes and backpacks to exposure of intimate parts. The meaning of such a search, and the degradation its subject may reasonably feel, place a search that intrusive in a category of its own demanding its own specific suspicions.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A school official searching a student is \xe2\x80\x9centitled to qualified immunity where clearly established law does not show that the search violated the Fourth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d Pearson v. Callahan, 555 U. S. __, __ (2009) (slip op., at 18). To be established clearly, however, there is no need that \xe2\x80\x9cthe very action in question [have] previously been held unlawful.\xe2\x80\x9d Wilson v. Layne, 526 U. S. 603, 615 (1999). The unconstitutionality of outrageous conduct obviously will be unconstitutional, this being the reason, as Judge Posner has said, that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he easiest cases don\xe2\x80\x99t even arise.\xe2\x80\x9d K.\xc2\xa0H. v. Morgan, 914 F.\xc2\xa02d 846, 851 (CA7 1990). But even as to action less than an outrage, \xe2\x80\x9cofficials can still be on notice that their conduct violates established law \xe2\x80\xa6 in novel factual circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Hope v. Pelzer, 536 U. S. 730, 741 (2002).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O. directed school officials to limit the intrusiveness of a search, \xe2\x80\x9cin light of the age and sex of the student and the nature of the infraction,\xe2\x80\x9d 469 U. S., at 342, and as we have just said at some length, the intrusiveness of the strip search here cannot be seen as justifiably related to the circumstances. But we realize that the lower courts have reached divergent conclusions regarding how the T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O. standard applies to such searches.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A number of judges have read T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O. as the en banc minority of the Ninth Circuit did here. The Sixth Circuit upheld a strip search of a high school student for a drug, without any suspicion that drugs were hidden next to her body. Williams v. Ellington, 936 F.\xc2\xa02d 881, 882\xe2\x80\x93883, 887 (1991). And other courts considering qualified immunity for strip searches have read T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O. as \xe2\x80\x9ca series of abstractions, on the one hand, and a declaration of seeming deference to the judgments of school officials, on the other,\xe2\x80\x9d Jenkins v. Talladega City Bd. of Ed., 115 F.\xc2\xa03d 821, 828 (CA11 1997) (en banc), which made it impossible \xe2\x80\x9cto establish clearly the contours of a Fourth Amendment right \xe2\x80\xa6 [in] the wide variety of possible school settings different from those involved in T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O.\xe2\x80\x9d itself. Ibid. See also Thomas v. Roberts, 323 F.\xc2\xa03d 950 (CA11 2003) (granting qualified immunity to a teacher and police officer who conducted a group strip search of a fifth grade class when looking for a missing $26).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We think these differences of opinion from our own are substantial enough to require immunity for the school officials in this case. We would not suggest that entitlement to qualified immunity is the guaranteed product of disuniform views of the law in the other federal, or state, courts, and the fact that a single judge, or even a group of judges, disagrees about the contours of a right does not automatically render the law unclear if we have been clear. That said, however, the cases viewing school strip searches differently from the way we see them are numerous enough, with well-reasoned majority and dissenting opinions, to counsel doubt that we were sufficiently clear in the prior statement of law. We conclude that qualified immunity is warranted.

\n

V

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The strip search of Savana Redding was unreasonable and a violation of the Fourth Amendment, but petitioners Wilson, Romero, and Schwallier are nevertheless protected from liability through qualified immunity. Our conclusions here do not resolve, however, the question of the liability of petitioner Safford Unified School District #1 under Monell v. New York City Dept. of Social Servs., 436 U. S. 658, 694 (1978), a claim the Ninth Circuit did not address. The judgment of the Ninth Circuit is therefore affirmed in part and reversed in part, and this case is remanded for consideration of the Monell claim.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In New Jersey v. T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O., 469 U. S. 325 (1985), the Court established a two-step inquiry for determining the reasonableness of a school official\xe2\x80\x99s decision to search a student. First, the Court explained, the search must be \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98justified at its inception\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d by the presence of \xe2\x80\x9creasonable grounds for suspecting that the search will turn up evidence that the student has violated or is violating either the law or the rules of the school.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 342. Second, the search must be \xe2\x80\x9cpermissible in its scope,\xe2\x80\x9d which is achieved \xe2\x80\x9cwhen the measures adopted are reasonably related to the objectives of the search and not excessively intrusive in light of the age and sex of the student and the nature of the infraction.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nothing the Court decides today alters this basic framework. It simply applies T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O. to declare unconstitutional a strip search of a 13-year-old honors student that was based on a groundless suspicion that she might be hiding medicine in her underwear. This is, in essence, a case in which clearly established law meets clearly outrageous conduct. I have long believed that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[i]t does not require a constitutional scholar to conclude that a nude search of a 13-year-old child is an invasion of constitutional rights of some magnitude.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 382, n. 25 (Stevens, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part) (quoting Doe v. Renfrow, 631 F.\xc2\xa02d 91, 92\xe2\x80\x9393 (CA7 1980)). The strip search of Savana Redding in this case was both more intrusive and less justified than the search of the student\xe2\x80\x99s purse in T. L. O. Therefore, while I join Parts I\xe2\x80\x93III of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, I disagree with its decision to extend qualified immunity to the school official who authorized this unconstitutional search.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court reaches a contrary conclusion about qualified immunity based on the fact that various Courts of Appeals have adopted seemingly divergent views about T. L. O.\xe2\x80\x99s application to strip searches. Ante, at 12. But the clarity of a well-established right should not depend on whether jurists have misread our precedents. And while our cases have previously noted the \xe2\x80\x9cdivergence of views\xe2\x80\x9d among courts in deciding whether to extend qualified immunity, e.g., Pearson v. Callahan, (2009) 555 U. S., ___, ___ (slip op., at 20) (noting the unsettled constitutionality of the so-called \xe2\x80\x9cconsent-once-removed\xe2\x80\x9d doctrine); Wilson v. Layne, 526 U. S. 603, 618 (1999) (considering conflicting views on the constitutionality of law enforcement\xe2\x80\x99s practice of allowing the media to enter a private home to observe and film attempted arrests), we have relied on that consideration only to spare officials from having \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98to predict the future course of constitutional law,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 617 (quoting Procunier v. Navarette, 434 U. S. 555, 562 (1978); emphasis added). In this case, by contrast, we chart no new constitutional path. We merely decide whether the decision to strip search Savana Redding, on these facts, was prohibited under T. L. O. Our conclusion leaves the boundaries of the law undisturbed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals properly rejected the school official\xe2\x80\x99s qualified immunity defense, and I would affirm that court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment in its entirety.

Brown, 738 F.\xc2\xa02d 1462 (1984), which held that a strip search performed under similar circumstances violated the Constitution. New Jersey v. T. L. O., 469 U. \nOPINION OF THOMAS, J.

\n557 U. S. ____ (2009)
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 08-479
\n
\n

SAFFORD UNIFIED SCHOOL DISTRICT #1, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. APRIL REDDING

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[June 25, 2009]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Thomas, concurring in the judgment in part and dissenting in part.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I agree with the Court that the judgment against the school officials with respect to qualified immunity should be reversed. See ante, at 11\xe2\x80\x9313. Unlike the majority, however, I would hold that the search of Savana Redding did not violate the Fourth Amendment. The majority imposes a vague and amorphous standard on school administrators. It also grants judges sweeping authority to second-guess the measures that these officials take to maintain discipline in their schools and ensure the health and safety of the students in their charge. This deep intrusion into the administration of public schools exemplifies why the Court should return to the common-law doctrine of in loco parentis under which \xe2\x80\x9cthe judiciary was reluctant to interfere in the routine business of school administration, allowing schools and teachers to set and enforce rules and to maintain order.\xe2\x80\x9d Morse v. Frederick, 551 U. S. 393, 414 (2007) (Thomas, J., concurring). But even under the prevailing Fourth Amendment test established by New Jersey v. T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O., 469 U. S. 325 (1985), all petitioners, including the school district, are entitled to judgment as a matter of law in their favor.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cAlthough the underlying command of the Fourth Amendment is always that searches and seizures be reasonable, what is reasonable depends on the context within which a search takes place.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 337. Thus, although public school students retain Fourth Amendment rights under this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent, see id., at 333\xe2\x80\x93337, those rights \xe2\x80\x9care different \xe2\x80\xa6 than elsewhere; the \xe2\x80\x98reasonableness\xe2\x80\x99 inquiry cannot disregard the schools\xe2\x80\x99 custodial and tutelary responsibility for children,\xe2\x80\x9d Vernonia School Dist. 47J v. Acton, 515 U. S. 646, 656 (1995); see also T. L. O., 469 U. S., at 339 (identifying \xe2\x80\x9cthe substantial interest of teachers and administrators in maintaining discipline in the classroom and on school grounds\xe2\x80\x9d). For nearly 25 years this Court has understood that \xe2\x80\x9c[m]aintaining order in the classroom has never been easy, but in more recent years, school disorder has often taken particularly ugly forms: drug use and violent crime in the schools have become major social problems.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. In schools, \xe2\x80\x9c[e]vents calling for discipline are frequent occurrences and sometimes require immediate, effective action.\xe2\x80\x9d Goss v. Lopez, 419 U. S. 565, 580 (1975); see also T. L. O., 469 U. S., at 340 (explaining that schools have a \xe2\x80\x9clegitimate need to maintain an environment in which learning can take place\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For this reason, school officials retain broad authority to protect students and preserve \xe2\x80\x9corder and a proper educational environment\xe2\x80\x9d under the Fourth Amendment. Id., at 339. This authority requires that school officials be able to engage in the \xe2\x80\x9cclose supervision of schoolchildren, as well as \xe2\x80\xa6 enforc[e] rules against conduct that would be perfectly permissible if undertaken by an adult.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Seeking to reconcile the Fourth Amendment with this unique public school setting, the Court in T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O. held that a school search is \xe2\x80\x9creasonable\xe2\x80\x9d if it is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98justified at its inception\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98reasonably related in scope to the circumstances which justified the interference in the first place.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 341\xe2\x80\x93342 (quoting Terry v. Ohio, 392 U. S. 1, 20 (1968)). The search under review easily meets this standard.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A \xe2\x80\x9csearch of a student by a teacher or other school official will be \xe2\x80\x98justified at its inception\xe2\x80\x99 when there are reasonable grounds for suspecting that the search will turn up evidence that the student has violated or is violating either the law or the rules of the school.\xe2\x80\x9d T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O., supra, at 341\xe2\x80\x93342 (footnote omitted). As the majority rightly concedes, this search was justified at its inception because there were reasonable grounds to suspect that Redding possessed medication that violated school rules. See ante, at 7. A finding of reasonable suspicion \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not deal with hard certainties, but with probabilities.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Cortez, 449 U. S. 411, 418 (1981); see also T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O., supra, at 346 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he requirement of reasonable suspicion is not a requirement of absolute certainty\xe2\x80\x9d). To satisfy this standard, more than a mere \xe2\x80\x9chunch\xe2\x80\x9d of wrongdoing is required, but \xe2\x80\x9cconsiderably\xe2\x80\x9d less suspicion is needed than would be required to \xe2\x80\x9csatisf[y] a preponderance of the evidence standard.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Arvizu, 534 U. S. 266, 274 (2002) (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Furthermore, in evaluating whether there is a reasonable \xe2\x80\x9cparticularized and objective\xe2\x80\x9d basis for conducting a search based on suspected wrongdoing, government officials must consider the \xe2\x80\x9ctotality of the circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 273 (internal quotation marks omitted). School officials have a specialized understanding of the school environment, the habits of the students, and the concerns of the community, which enables them to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98formulat[e] certain common-sense conclusions about human behavior.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Sokolow, 490 U. S. 1, 8 (1989) (quoting Cortez, supra, at 418). And like police officers, school officials are \xe2\x80\x9centitled to make an assessment of the situation in light of [this] specialized training and familiarity with the customs of the [school].\xe2\x80\x9d See Arvizu, supra, at 276.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Here, petitioners had reasonable grounds to suspect that Redding was in possession of prescription and nonprescription drugs in violation of the school\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition of the \xe2\x80\x9cnon-medical use, possession, or sale of a drug\xe2\x80\x9d on school property or at school events. 531 F.\xc2\xa03d 1071, 1076 (CA9 2008) (en banc); see also id., at 1107 (Hawkins, J., dissenting) (explaining that the school policy defined \xe2\x80\x9cdrugs\xe2\x80\x9d to include \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[a]ny prescription or over-the-counter drug, except those for which permission to use in school has been granted\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). As an initial matter, school officials were aware that a few years earlier, a student had become \xe2\x80\x9cseriously ill\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cspent several days in intensive care\xe2\x80\x9d after ingesting prescription medication obtained from a classmate. App. 10a. Fourth Amendment searches do not occur in a vacuum; rather, context must inform the judicial inquiry. See Cortez, supra, at 417\xe2\x80\x93418. In this instance, the suspicion of drug possession arose at a middle school that had \xe2\x80\x9ca history of problems with students using and distributing prohibited and illegal substances on campus.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 7a, 10a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The school\xe2\x80\x99s substance-abuse problems had not abated by the 2003\xe2\x80\x932004 school year, which is when the challenged search of Redding took place. School officials had found alcohol and cigarettes in the girls\xe2\x80\x99 bathroom during the first school dance of the year and noticed that a group of students including Redding and Marissa Glines smelled of alcohol. Ibid. Several weeks later, another student, Jordan Romero, reported that Redding had hosted a party before the dance where she served whiskey, vodka, and tequila. Id., at 8a, 11a. Romero had provided this report to school officials as a result of a meeting his mother scheduled with the officials after Romero \xe2\x80\x9cbec[a]me violent\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9csick to his stomach\xe2\x80\x9d one night and admitted that \xe2\x80\x9che had taken some pills that he had got[ten] from a classmate.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 7a\xe2\x80\x938a, 10a\xe2\x80\x9311a. At that meeting, Romero admitted that \xe2\x80\x9ccertain students were bringing drugs and weapons on campus.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 8a, 11a. One week later, Romero handed the assistant principal a white pill that he said he had received from Glines. Id., at 11a. He reported \xe2\x80\x9cthat a group of students [were] planning on taking the pills at lunch.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0School officials justifiably took quick action in light of the lunchtime deadline. The assistant principal took the pill to the school nurse who identified it as prescription-strength 400-mg Ibuprofen. Id., at 12a. A subsequent search of Glines and her belongings produced a razor blade, a Naproxen 200-mg pill, and several Ibuprofen 400-mg pills. Id., at 13a. When asked, Glines claimed that she had received the pills from Redding. Ibid. A search of Redding\xe2\x80\x99s planner, which Glines had borrowed, then uncovered \xe2\x80\x9cseveral knives, several lighters, a cigarette, and a permanent marker.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 12a, 14a, 22a. Thus, as the majority acknowledges, ante, at 7, the totality of relevant circumstances justified a search of Redding for pills.[Footnote 1]

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The remaining question is whether the search was reasonable in scope. Under T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O., \xe2\x80\x9ca search will be permissible in its scope when the measures adopted are reasonably related to the objectives of the search and not excessively intrusive in light of the age and sex of the student and the nature of the infraction.\xe2\x80\x9d 469 U. S., at 342. The majority concludes that the school officials\xe2\x80\x99 search of Redding\xe2\x80\x99s underwear was not \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98reasonably related in scope to the circumstances which justified the interference in the first place,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d see ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x9311, notwithstanding the officials\xe2\x80\x99 reasonable suspicion that Redding \xe2\x80\x9cwas involved in pill distribution,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 7. According to the majority, to be reasonable, this school search required a showing of \xe2\x80\x9cdanger to the students from the power of the drugs or their quantity\xe2\x80\x9d or a \xe2\x80\x9creason to suppose that [Redding] was carrying pills in her underwear.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10. Each of these additional requirements is an unjustifiable departure from bedrock Fourth Amendment law in the school setting, where this Court has heretofore read the Fourth Amendment to grant considerable leeway to school officials. Because the school officials searched in a location where the pills could have been hidden, the search was reasonable in scope under T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O.

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority finds that \xe2\x80\x9csubjective and reasonable societal expectations of personal privacy support \xe2\x80\xa6 treat[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d this type of search, which it labels a \xe2\x80\x9cstrip search,\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9ccategorically distinct, requiring distinct elements of justification on the part of school authorities for going beyond a search of clothing and belongings.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8.[Footnote 2] Thus, in the majority\xe2\x80\x99s view, although the school officials had reasonable suspicion to believe that Redding had the pills on her person, see ante, at 7, they needed some greater level of particularized suspicion to conduct this \xe2\x80\x9cstrip search.\xe2\x80\x9d There is no support for this contortion of the Fourth Amendment.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court has generally held that the reasonableness of a search\xe2\x80\x99s scope depends only on whether it is limited to the area that is capable of concealing the object of the search. See, e.g., Wyoming v. Houghton, 526 U. S. 295, 307 (1999) (Police officers \xe2\x80\x9cmay inspect passengers\xe2\x80\x99 belongings found in the car that are capable of concealing the object of the search\xe2\x80\x9d); Florida v. Jimeno, 500 U. S. 248, 251 (1991) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe scope of a search is generally defined by its expressed object\xe2\x80\x9d); United States v. Johns, 469 U. S. 478, 487 (1985) (search reasonable because \xe2\x80\x9cthere is no plausible argument that the object of the search could not have been concealed in the packages\xe2\x80\x9d); United States v. Ross, 456 U. S. 798, 820 (1982) (\xe2\x80\x9cA lawful search \xe2\x80\xa6 generally extends to the entire area in which the object of the search may be found\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In keeping with this longstanding rule, the \xe2\x80\x9cnature of the infraction\xe2\x80\x9d referenced in T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O. delineates the proper scope of a search of students in a way that is identical to that permitted for searches outside the school\xe2\x80\x94i.e., the search must be limited to the areas where the object of that infraction could be concealed. See Horton v. California, 496 U. S. 128, 141 (1990) (\xe2\x80\x9cPolice with a warrant for a rifle may search only places where rifles might be\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)); Ross, supra, at 824 (\xe2\x80\x9c[P]robable cause to believe that undocumented aliens are being transported in a van will not justify a warrantless search of a suitcase\xe2\x80\x9d). A search of a student therefore is permissible in scope under T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O. so long as it is objectively reasonable to believe that the area searched could conceal the contraband. The dissenting opinion below correctly captured this Fourth Amendment standard, noting that \xe2\x80\x9cif a student brought a baseball bat on campus in violation of school policy, a search of that student\xe2\x80\x99s shirt pocket would be patently unjustified.\xe2\x80\x9d 531 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1104 (opinion of Hawkins, J.).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The analysis of whether the scope of the search here was permissible under that standard is straightforward. Indeed, the majority does not dispute that \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral background possibilities\xe2\x80\x9d establish that students conceal \xe2\x80\x9ccontraband in their underwear.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10. It acknowledges that school officials had reasonable suspicion to look in Redding\xe2\x80\x99s backpack and outer clothing because if \xe2\x80\x9cWilson\xe2\x80\x99s reasonable suspicion of pill distribution were not understood to support searches of outer clothes and backpack, it would not justify any search worth making.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 7. The majority nevertheless concludes that proceeding any further with the search was unreasonable. See ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x9310; see also ante, at 1 (Ginsburg, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part) (\xe2\x80\x9cAny reasonable search for the pills would have ended when inspection of Redding\xe2\x80\x99s backpack and jacket pockets yielded nothing\xe2\x80\x9d). But there is no support for this conclusion. The reasonable suspicion that Redding possessed the pills for distribution purposes did not dissipate simply because the search of her backpack turned up nothing. It was eminently reasonable to conclude that the backpack was empty because Redding was secreting the pills in a place she thought no one would look. See Ross, supra, at 820 (\xe2\x80\x9cContraband goods rarely are strewn\xe2\x80\x9d about in plain view; \xe2\x80\x9cby their very nature such goods must be withheld from public view\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Redding would not have been the first person to conceal pills in her undergarments. See Hicks, Man Gets 17-Year Drug Sentence, [Corbin, KY] Times-Tribune, Oct. 7, 2008, p.\xc2\xa01 (Drug courier \xe2\x80\x9ctold officials she had the [Oxycontin] pills concealed in her crotch\xe2\x80\x9d); Conley, Whitehaven: Traffic Stop Yields Hydrocodone Pills, [Memphis] Commercial Appeal, Aug. 3, 2007, p. B3 (\xe2\x80\x9cAn additional 40 hydrocodone pills were found in her pants\xe2\x80\x9d); Caywood, Police Vehicle Chase Leads to Drug Arrests, [Worcester] Telegram & Gazette, June 7, 2008, p. A7 (25-year-old \xe2\x80\x9callegedly had a cigar tube stuffed with pills tucked into the waistband of his pants\xe2\x80\x9d); Hubartt, 23-Year-Old Charged With Dealing Ecstasy, The [Fort Wayne] Journal Gazette, Aug. 8, 2007, p. C2 (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hile he was being put into a squad car, his pants fell down and a plastic bag containing pink and orange pills fell on the ground\xe2\x80\x9d); Sebastian Residents Arrested in Drug Sting, Vero Beach Press Journal, Sept. 16, 2006, p. B2 (Arrestee \xe2\x80\x9ctold them he had more pills \xe2\x80\x98down my pants\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). Nor will she be the last after today\xe2\x80\x99s decision, which announces the safest place to secrete contraband in school.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority compounds its error by reading the \xe2\x80\x9cnature of the infraction\xe2\x80\x9d aspect of the T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O. test as a license to limit searches based on a judge\xe2\x80\x99s assessment of a particular school policy. According to the majority, the scope of the search was impermissible because the school official \xe2\x80\x9cmust have been aware of the nature and limited threat of the specific drugs he was searching for\xe2\x80\x9d and because he \xe2\x80\x9chad no reason to suspect that large amounts of the drugs were being passed around, or that individual students were receiving great numbers of pills.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310. Thus, in order to locate a rationale for finding a Fourth Amendment violation in this case, the majority retreats from its observation that the school\xe2\x80\x99s firm no-drug policy \xe2\x80\x9cmakes sense, and there is no basis to claim that the search was unreasonable owing to some defect or shortcoming of the rule it was aimed at enforcing.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 5, n.\xc2\xa01.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even accepting the majority\xe2\x80\x99s assurances that it is not attacking the rule\xe2\x80\x99s reasonableness, it certainly is attacking the rule\xe2\x80\x99s importance. This approach directly conflicts with T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O. in which the Court was \xe2\x80\x9cunwilling to adopt a standard under which the legality of a search is dependent upon a judge\xe2\x80\x99s evaluation of the relative importance of school rules.\xe2\x80\x9d 469 U. S., at 342, n.\xc2\xa09. Indeed, the Court in T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O. expressly rejected the proposition that the majority seemingly endorses\xe2\x80\x94that \xe2\x80\x9csome rules regarding student conduct are by nature too \xe2\x80\x98trivial\xe2\x80\x99 to justify a search based upon reasonable suspicion.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; see also id., at 343, n.\xc2\xa09 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe promulgation of a rule forbidding specified conduct presumably reflects a judgment on the part of school officials that such conduct is destructive of school order or of a proper educational environment. Absent any suggestion that the rule violates some substantive constitutional guarantee, the courts should as a general matter, defer to that judgment\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority\xe2\x80\x99s decision in this regard also departs from another basic principle of the Fourth Amendment: that law enforcement officials can enforce with the same vigor all rules and regulations irrespective of the perceived importance of any of those rules. \xe2\x80\x9cIn a long line of cases, we have said that when an officer has probable cause to believe a person committed even a minor crime in his presence, the balancing of private and public interests is not in doubt. The arrest is constitutionally reasonable.\xe2\x80\x9d Virginia v. Moore, 553 U. S. ___, ___ (2008) (slip op., at 6). The Fourth Amendment rule for searches is the same: Police officers are entitled to search regardless of the perceived triviality of the underlying law. As we have explained, requiring police to make \xe2\x80\x9csensitive, case-by-case determinations of government need,\xe2\x80\x9d Atwater v. Lago Vista, 532 U. S. 318, 347 (2001), for a particular prohibition before conducting a search would \xe2\x80\x9cplace police in an almost impossible spot,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 350.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority has placed school officials in this \xe2\x80\x9cimpossible spot\xe2\x80\x9d by questioning whether possession of Ibuprofen and Naproxen causes a severe enough threat to warrant investigation. Had the suspected infraction involved a street drug, the majority implies that it would have approved the scope of the search. See ante, at 9 (relying on the \xe2\x80\x9climited threat of the specific drugs he was searching for\xe2\x80\x9d); ante, at 10 (relying on the limited \xe2\x80\x9cpower of the drugs\xe2\x80\x9d involved). In effect, then, the majority has replaced a school rule that draws no distinction among drugs with a new one that does. As a result, a full search of a student\xe2\x80\x99s person for prohibited drugs will be permitted only if the Court agrees that the drug in question was sufficiently dangerous. Such a test is unworkable and unsound. School officials cannot be expected to halt searches based on the possibility that a court might later find that the particular infraction at issue is not severe enough to warrant an intrusive investigation.[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A rule promulgated by a school board represents the judgment of school officials that the rule is needed to maintain \xe2\x80\x9cschool order\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ca proper educational environment.\xe2\x80\x9d T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O., 469 U. S., at 343, n.\xc2\xa09. Teachers, administrators, and the local school board are called upon both to \xe2\x80\x9cprotect the \xe2\x80\xa6 safety of students and school personnel\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cmaintain an environment conducive to learning.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 353 (Blackmun, J., concurring in judgment). They are tasked with \xe2\x80\x9cwatch[ing] over a large number of students\xe2\x80\x9d who \xe2\x80\x9care inclined to test the outer boundaries of acceptable conduct and to imitate the misbehavior of a peer if that misbehavior is not dealt with quickly.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 352. In such an environment, something as simple as a \xe2\x80\x9cwater pistol or peashooter can wreak [havoc] until it is taken away.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The danger posed by unchecked distribution and consumption of prescription pills by students certainly needs no elaboration.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Judges are not qualified to second-guess the best manner for maintaining quiet and order in the school environment. Such institutional judgments, like those concerning the selection of the best methods for \xe2\x80\x9crestrain[ing students] from assaulting one another, abusing drugs and alcohol, and committing other crimes,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 342, n.\xc2\xa09, \xe2\x80\x9cinvolve a host of policy choices that must be made by locally elected representatives, rather than by federal judges interpreting the basic charter of Government for the entire country.\xe2\x80\x9d Collins v. Harker Heights, 503 U. S. 115, 129 (1992); cf. Regents of Univ. of Mich. v. Ewing, 474 U. S. 214, 226 (1985) (observing that federal courts are not \xe2\x80\x9csuited to evaluat[ing] the substance of the multitude of academic decisions\xe2\x80\x9d or disciplinary decisions \xe2\x80\x9cthat are made daily by faculty members of public educational institutions\xe2\x80\x9d). It is a mistake for judges to assume the responsibility for deciding which school rules are important enough to allow for invasive searches and which rules are not.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even if this Court were authorized to second-guess the importance of school rules, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s assessment of the importance of this district\xe2\x80\x99s policy is flawed. It is a crime to possess or use prescription-strength Ibuprofen without a prescription. See Ariz. Rev. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa713\xe2\x80\x933406(A)(1) (West Supp. 2008) (\xe2\x80\x9cA person shall not knowingly \xe2\x80\xa6 [p]ossess or use a prescription-only drug unless the person obtains the prescription-only drug pursuant to a valid prescription of a prescriber who is licensed pursuant to [state law]\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 5] By prohibiting unauthorized prescription drugs on school grounds\xe2\x80\x94and conducting a search to ensure students abide by that prohibition\xe2\x80\x94the school rule here was consistent with a routine provision of the state criminal code. It hardly seems unreasonable for school officials to enforce a rule that, in effect, proscribes conduct that amounts to a crime.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, school districts have valid reasons for punishing the unauthorized possession of prescription drugs on school property as severely as the possession of street drugs; \xe2\x80\x9c[t]eenage abuse of over-the-counter and prescription drugs poses an increasingly alarming national crisis.\xe2\x80\x9d Get Teens Off Drugs, The Education Digest 75 (Dec. 2006). As one study noted, \xe2\x80\x9cmore young people ages 12\xe2\x80\x9317 abuse prescription drugs than any illicit drug except marijuana\xe2\x80\x94more than cocaine, heroin, and methamphetamine combined.\xe2\x80\x9d Executive Office of the President, Office of National Drug Control Policy (ONDCP), Prescription for Danger 1 (Jan. 2008) (hereinafter Prescription for Danger). And according to a 2005 survey of teens, \xe2\x80\x9cnearly one in five (19 percent or 4.5 million) admit abusing prescription drugs in their lifetime.\xe2\x80\x9d Columbia University, The National Center on Addiction and Substance Abuse (CASA), \xe2\x80\x9cYou\xe2\x80\x99ve Got Drugs!\xe2\x80\x9d V: Prescription Drug Pushers on the Internet 2 (July 2008); see also Dept. of Health and Human Services, National Institute on Drug Abuse, High School and Youth Trends 2 (Dec. 2008) (\xe2\x80\x9cIn 2008, 15.4 percent of 12th-graders reported using a prescription drug nonmedically within the past year\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0School administrators can reasonably conclude that this high rate of drug abuse is being fueled, at least in part, by the increasing presence of prescription drugs on school campuses. See, e.g., Gibson, Grand Forks Schools See Rise In Prescription Drug Abuse, Grand Forks Herald, Nov. 16, 2008, p.\xc2\xa01 (explaining that \xe2\x80\x9cprescription drug abuse is growing into a larger problem\xe2\x80\x9d as students \xe2\x80\x9cbring them to school and sell them or just give them to their friends\xe2\x80\x9d). The risks posed by the abuse of these drugs are every bit as serious as the dangers of using a typical street drug.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Teenagers are nevertheless apt to \xe2\x80\x9cbelieve the myth that these drugs provide a medically safe high.\xe2\x80\x9d ONDCP, Teens and Prescription Drugs: An Analysis of Recent Trends on the Emerging Drug Threat 3 (Feb. 2007) (hereinafter Teens and Prescription Drugs). In light of the risks involved, a school\xe2\x80\x99s decision to establish and enforce a school prohibition on the possession of any unauthorized drug is thus a reasonable judgment.[Footnote 6]

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0By declaring the search unreasonable in this case, the majority has \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98surrender[ed] control of the American public school system to public school students\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d by invalidating school policies that treat all drugs equally and by second-guessing swift disciplinary decisions made by school officials. See Morse, 551 U. S., at 421 (Thomas, J., concurring) (quoting Tinker v. Des Moines Independent Community School Dist., 393 U. S. 503, 526 (1969) (Black, J., dissenting)). The Court\xe2\x80\x99s interference in these matters of great concern to teachers, parents, and students illustrates why the most constitutionally sound approach to the question of applying the Fourth Amendment in local public schools would in fact be the complete restoration of the common-law doctrine of in loco parentis.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c[I]n the early years of public schooling,\xe2\x80\x9d courts applied the doctrine of in loco parentis to transfer to teachers the authority of a parent to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98command obedience, to control stubbornness, to quicken diligence, and to reform bad habits.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Morse, supra, at 413\xe2\x80\x93414 (Thomas, J., concurring) (quoting State v. Pendergrass, 19 N.\xc2\xa0C. 365, 365\xe2\x80\x93366 (1837)). So empowered, schoolteachers and administrators had almost complete discretion to establish and enforce the rules they believed were necessary to maintain control over their classrooms. See 2 J. Kent, Commentaries on American Law 205 (1873) (\xe2\x80\x9cSo the power allowed by law to the parent over the person of the child may be delegated to a tutor or instructor, the better to accomplish the purpose of education\xe2\x80\x9d); 1 W. Blackstone, Commentaries on the Laws of England 441 (1765) (\xe2\x80\x9cHe may also delegate part of his parental authority, during his life, to the tutor or schoolmaster of his child; who is then in loco parentis, and has such a portion of the parent committed to his charge, viz. that of restraint and correction, as may be necessary to answer the purposes for which he is employed\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 7] The perils of judicial policymaking inherent in applying Fourth Amendment protections to public schools counsel in favor of a return to the understanding that existed in this Nation\xe2\x80\x99s first public schools, which gave teachers discretion to craft the rules needed to carry out the disciplinary responsibilities delegated to them by parents.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If the common-law view that parents delegate to teachers their authority to discipline and maintain order were to be applied in this case, the search of Redding would stand. There can be no doubt that a parent would have had the authority to conduct the search at issue in this case. Parents have \xe2\x80\x9cimmunity from the strictures of the Fourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x9d when it comes to searches of a child or that child\xe2\x80\x99s belongings. T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O., 469 U. S., at 337; see also id., at 336 (A parent\xe2\x80\x99s authority is \xe2\x80\x9cnot subject to the limits of the Fourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x9d); Griffin v. Wisconsin, 483 U. S. 868, 876 (1987) (\xe2\x80\x9c[P]arental custodial authority\xe2\x80\x9d does not require \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial approval for [a] search of a minor child\xe2\x80\x99s room\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As acknowledged by this Court, this principle is based on the \xe2\x80\x9csocietal understanding of superior and inferior\xe2\x80\x9d with respect to the \xe2\x80\x9cparent and child\xe2\x80\x9d relationship. Georgia v. Randolph, 547 U. S. 103, 114 (2006). In light of this relationship, the Court has indicated that a parent can authorize a third-party search of a child by consenting to such a search, even if the child denies his consent. See ibid.; see also 4 W. LaFave, Search and Seizure \xc2\xa78.3(d), p. 160 (4th ed. 2004) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] father, as the head of the household with the responsibility and the authority for the discipline, training and control of his children, has a superior interest in the family residence to that of his minor son, so that the father\xe2\x80\x99s consent to search would be effective notwithstanding the son\xe2\x80\x99s contemporaneous on-the-scene objection\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). Certainly, a search by the parent himself is no different, regardless of whether or not a child would prefer to be left alone. See id., \xc2\xa78.4(b), at 202 (\xe2\x80\x9c[E]ven [if] a minor child \xe2\x80\xa6 may think of a room as \xe2\x80\x98his,\xe2\x80\x99 the overall dominance will be in his parents\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Restoring the common-law doctrine of in loco parentis would not, however, leave public schools entirely free to impose any rule they choose. \xe2\x80\x9cIf parents do not like the rules imposed by those schools, they can seek redress in school boards or legislatures; they can send their children to private schools or home school them; or they can simply move.\xe2\x80\x9d See Morse, 551 U. S., at 419 (Thomas, J., concurring). Indeed, parents and local government officials have proved themselves quite capable of challenging overly harsh school rules or the enforcement of sensible rules in insensible ways.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For example, one community questioned a school policy that resulted in \xe2\x80\x9can 11-year-old [being] arrested, handcuffed, and taken to jail for bringing a plastic butter knife to school.\xe2\x80\x9d Downey, Zero Tolerance Doesn\xe2\x80\x99t Always Add Up, The Atlanta Journal-Constitution, Apr. 6, 2009, p.\xc2\xa0A11. In another, \xe2\x80\x9c[a]t least one school board member was outraged\xe2\x80\x9d when 14 elementary-school students were suspended for \xe2\x80\x9cimitating drug activity\xe2\x80\x9d after they combined Kool-Aid and sugar in plastic bags. Grant, Pupils Trading Sweet Mix Get Sour Shot of Discipline, Pittsburgh Post-Gazette, May 18, 2006, p. B1. Individuals within yet another school district protested a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98zero-tolerance\xe2\x80\x99 policy toward weapons\xe2\x80\x9d that had become \xe2\x80\x9cso rigid that it force[d] schools to expel any student who belongs to a military organization, a drum-and-bugle corps or any other legitimate extracurricular group and is simply transporting what amounts to harmless props.\xe2\x80\x9d Richardson, School Gun Case Sparks Cries For \xe2\x80\x9cCommon Sense,\xe2\x80\x9d Washington Times, Feb. 13, 2009, p. A1.[Footnote 8]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These local efforts to change controversial school policies through democratic processes have proven successful in many cases. See, e.g., Postal, Schools\xe2\x80\x99 Zero Tolerance Could Lose Some Punch, Orlando Sentinel, Apr. 24, 2009, p. B3 (\xe2\x80\x9cState lawmakers want schools to dial back strict zero-tolerance policies so students do not end up in juvenile detention for some \xe2\x80\x98goofy thing\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d); Richardson, Tolerance Waning for Zero-tolerance Rules, Washington Times, Apr. 21, 2009, p. Utah now allows students to bring asthma inhalers to school without violating the zero-tolerance policy on drugs\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Nussbaum, Becoming Fed Up With Zero Tolerance, New York Times, Sept. 3, 2000, Section 14, p.\xc2\xa01 (discussing a report that found that \xe2\x80\x9cwidespread use of zero-tolerance discipline policies was creating as many problems as it was solving and that there were many cases around the country in which students were harshly disciplined for infractions where there was no harm intended or done\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the end, the task of implementing and amending public school policies is beyond this Court\xe2\x80\x99s function. Parents, teachers, school administrators, local politicians, and state officials are all better suited than judges to determine the appropriate limits on searches conducted by school officials. Preservation of order, discipline, and safety in public schools is simply not the domain of the Constitution. And, common sense is not a judicial monopoly or a Constitutional imperative.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he nationwide drug epidemic makes the war against drugs a pressing concern in every school.\xe2\x80\x9d Board of Ed. of Independent School Dist. No. 92 of Pottawatomie Cty. v. Earls, 536 U. S. 822, 834 (2002). And yet the Court has limited the authority of school officials to conduct searches for the drugs that the officials believe pose a serious safety risk to their students. By doing so, the majority has confirmed that a return to the doctrine of in loco parentis is required to keep the judiciary from essentially seizing control of public schools. Only then will teachers again be able to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98govern the[ir] pupils, quicken the slothful, spur the indolent, restrain the impetuous, and control the stubborn\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d by making \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98rules, giv[ing] commands, and punish[ing] disobedience\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d without interference from judges. See Morse, supra, at 414. By deciding that it is better equipped to decide what behavior should be permitted in schools, the Court has undercut student safety and undermined the authority of school administrators and local officials. Even more troubling, it has done so in a case in which the underlying response by school administrators was reasonable and justified. I cannot join this regrettable decision. I, therefore, respectfully dissent from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s determination that this search violated the Fourth Amendment.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Ginsburg, concurring in part and dissenting in part.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I agree with the Court that Assistant Principal Wilson\xe2\x80\x99s subjection of 13-year-old Savana Redding to a humiliating stripdown search violated the Fourth Amendment. But I also agree with Justice Stevens, ante, at 1\xe2\x80\x932, that our opinion in New Jersey v. T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O., 469 U. S. 325 (1985), \xe2\x80\x9cclearly established\xe2\x80\x9d the law governing this case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Fellow student Marissa Glines, caught with pills in her pocket, accused Redding of supplying them. App. 13a. Asked where the blue pill among several white pills in Glines\xe2\x80\x99s pocket came from, Glines answered: \xe2\x80\x9cI guess it slipped in when she gave me the IBU 400s.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Asked next \xe2\x80\x9cwho is she?\xe2\x80\x9d, Glines responded: \xe2\x80\x9cSavana Redding.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. As the Court observes, ante, at 6, 10, no followup questions were asked. Wilson did not test Glines\xe2\x80\x99s accusation for veracity by asking Glines when did Redding give her the pills, where, for what purpose. Any reasonable search for the pills would have ended when inspection of Redding\xe2\x80\x99s backpack and jacket pockets yielded nothing. Wilson had no cause to suspect, based on prior experience at the school or clues in this case, that Redding had hidden pills\xe2\x80\x94containing the equivalent of two Advils or one Aleve\xe2\x80\x94in her underwear or body. To make matters worse, Wilson did not release Redding, to return to class or to go home, after the search. Instead, he made her sit on a chair outside his office for over two hours. At no point did he attempt to call her parent. Abuse of authority of that order should not be shielded by official immunity.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In contrast to T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O., where a teacher discovered a student smoking in the lavatory, and where the search was confined to the student\xe2\x80\x99s purse, the search of Redding involved her body and rested on the bare accusation of another student whose reliability the Assistant Principal had no reason to trust. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O. plainly stated the controlling Fourth Amendment law: A search ordered by a school official, even if \xe2\x80\x9cjustified at its inception,\xe2\x80\x9d crosses the constitutional boundary if it becomes \xe2\x80\x9cexcessively intrusive in light of the age and sex of the student and the nature of the infraction.\xe2\x80\x9d 469 U. S., at 342 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Here, \xe2\x80\x9cthe nature of the [supposed] infraction,\xe2\x80\x9d the slim basis for suspecting Savana Redding, and her \xe2\x80\x9cage and sex,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., establish beyond doubt that Assistant Principal Wilson\xe2\x80\x99s order cannot be reconciled with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O. Wilson\xe2\x80\x99s treatment of Redding was abusive and it was not reasonable for him to believe that the law permitted it. I join Justice Stevens in dissenting from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s acceptance of Wilson\xe2\x80\x99s qualified immunity plea, and would affirm the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 judgment in all respects.
