\n\t\t\t\tCOMPUCREDIT CORP. et\xc2\xa0al. v. GREENWOOD et\xc2\xa0al.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 10\xe2\x80\x93948.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 11, 2011\xe2\x80\x94Decided January 10, 2012\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tAlthough respondents\xe2\x80\x99 credit card agreement required their claims to be resolved by binding arbitration, they filed a lawsuit against petitioner CompuCredit Corporation and a division of petitioner bank, alleging, inter alia, violations of the Credit Repair Organizations Act (CROA). The Federal District Court denied the defendants\xe2\x80\x99 motion to compel arbitration, concluding that Congress intended CROA claims to be nonarbitrable. The Ninth Circuit affirmed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0Because the CROA is silent on whether claims under the Act can proceed in an arbitrable forum, the Federal Arbitration Act (FAA) requires the arbitration agreement to be enforced according to its terms. Pp.\xc2\xa02\xe2\x80\x9310.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0Section 2 of the FAA establishes \xe2\x80\x9ca liberal federal policy favoring arbitration.\xe2\x80\x9d Moses H. Cone Memorial Hospital v. Mercury Constr. Corp., \n\n460 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n. It requires that courts enforce arbitration agreements according to their terms. See Dean Witter Reynolds Inc. v. Byrd, \n\n470 U.\xc2\xa0S. 213\n. That is the case even when federal statutory claims are at issue, unless the FAA\xe2\x80\x99s mandate has been \xe2\x80\x9coverridden by a contrary congressional command.\xe2\x80\x9d Shearson/American Express Inc. v. McMahon, \n\n482 U.\xc2\xa0S. 220\n. Pp.\xc2\xa02\xe2\x80\x933.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0The CROA provides no such command. Respondents contend that the CROA\xe2\x80\x99s disclosure provision\xe2\x80\x94which requires credit repair organizations to provide consumers with a statement that includes the sentence \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98You have a right to sue a credit repair organization that violates the [Act],\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d \n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71679c(a)\xe2\x80\x94gives consumers the right to bring an action in a court of law; and that, because the CROA prohibits the waiver of \xe2\x80\x9cany right of the consumer under this subchapter,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71679f(a), the arbitration agreement\xe2\x80\x99s waiver of the \xe2\x80\x9cright\xe2\x80\x9d to bring a court action cannot be enforced. Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 premise is flawed. The disclosure provision creates only a right for consumers to receive a specific statement describing the consumer protections that the law elsewhere provides, one of which is the right to enforce a credit repair organization\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cliab[ility]\xe2\x80\x9d for \xe2\x80\x9cfail[ure] to comply with [the Act].\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71679g(a). That provision does not override the FAA\xe2\x80\x99s mandate. Its mere contemplation of judicial enforcement does not demonstrate that the Act provides consumers with a \xe2\x80\x9cright\xe2\x80\x9d to initial judicial enforcement. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x938.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0At the time of the CROA\xe2\x80\x99s enactment in 1996, arbitration clauses such as the one at issue were no rarity in consumer contracts generally, or in financial services contracts in particular. Had Congress meant to prohibit these very common provisions in the CROA, it would have done so in a manner less obtuse than what respondents suggest. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x939.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t615 F.\xc2\xa03d 1204, reversed and remanded.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Scalia delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe consider whether the Credit Repair Organizations Act (CROA), \n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71679 et seq., precludes enforcement of an arbitration agreement in a lawsuit alleging violations of that Act.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondents are individuals who applied for and received an Aspire Visa credit card marketed by petitioner CompuCredit Corporation and issued by Columbus Bank and Trust, now a division of petitioner Synovus Bank. In their applications they agreed to be bound by a provision which read: \xe2\x80\x9cAny claim, dispute or controversy (whether in contract, tort, or otherwise) at any time arising from or relating to your Account, any transferred balances or this Agreement (collectively, \xe2\x80\x98Claims\xe2\x80\x99), upon the election of you or us, will be resolved by binding arbitration .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 62.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 2008, respondents filed a class-action complaint against CompuCredit and Columbus in the United States District Court for the Northern District of California, alleging, as relevant here, violations of the CROA. The claims largely involved the defendants\xe2\x80\x99 allegedly misleading representation that the credit card could be used to rebuild poor credit and their assessment of multiple fees upon opening of the accounts, which greatly reduced the advertised credit limit.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe District Court denied the defendants\xe2\x80\x99 motion to compel arbitration of the claims, concluding that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress intended claims under the CROA to be non-arbitrable.\xe2\x80\x9d 617 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 980, 988 (2009). A panel of the United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit affirmed, Judge Tashima dissenting. 615 F.\xc2\xa03d 1204 (2010). We granted certiorari, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2011).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe background law governing the issue before us is the Federal Arbitration Act (FAA), \n9 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71 et seq., enacted in 1925 as a response to judicial hostility to arbitration. AT&T Mobility LLC v. Concepcion, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 4). As relevant here, the FAA provides:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cA written provision in any maritime transaction or a contract evidencing a transaction involving commerce to settle by arbitration a controversy thereafter arising out of such contract or transaction .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall be valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, save upon such grounds as exist at law or in equity for the revocation of any contract.\xe2\x80\x9d \n9 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThis provision establishes \xe2\x80\x9ca liberal federal policy favoring arbitration agreements.\xe2\x80\x9d Moses H. Cone Memorial Hospital v. Mercury Constr. Corp., \n\n460 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1, \n24 (1983)\n. See also, e.g., Concepcion, supra, at __ (slip op., at 4); Gilmer v. Interstate/Johnson Lane Corp., \n\n500 U.\xc2\xa0S. 20, \n25 (1991)\n. It requires courts to enforce agreements to arbitrate according to their terms. See Dean Witter Reynolds Inc. v. Byrd, \n\n470 U.\xc2\xa0S. 213, \n221 (1985)\n. That is the case even when the claims at issue are federal statutory claims, unless the FAA\xe2\x80\x99s mandate has been \xe2\x80\x9coverridden by a contrary congressional command.\xe2\x80\x9d Shearson/American Express Inc. v. McMahon, \n\n482 U.\xc2\xa0S. 220, \n226 (1987)\n. See also Mitsubishi Motors Corp. v. Soler Chrysler-Plymouth, Inc., \n\n473 U.\xc2\xa0S. 614, \n628 (1985)\n. Respondents contend that the CROA contains such a command.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThat statute regulates the practices of credit repair organizations, defined as certain entities that offer services for the purpose of \xe2\x80\x9c(i) improving any consumer\xe2\x80\x99s credit record, credit history, or credit rating; or (ii) providing advice or assistance to any consumer with regard to any activity or service described in clause (i).\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t \n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71679a(3). In its principal substantive provisions, the CROA prohibits certain practices, \xc2\xa71679b, establishes certain requirements for contracts with consumers, \xc2\xa71679d, and gives consumers a right to cancel, \xc2\xa71679e. Enforcement is achieved through the Act\xe2\x80\x99s provision of a private cause of action for violation, \xc2\xa71679g, as well as through federal and state administrative enforcement, \xc2\xa71679h.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tLike the District Court and the Ninth Circuit, respondents focus on the CROA\xe2\x80\x99s disclosure and nonwaiver provisions. The former, which is reproduced in full in the Appendix, infra, sets forth a statement that the credit re-pair organization must provide to the consumer before any contract is executed. \xc2\xa71679c(a). One sentence of that required statement reads, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98You have a right to sue a credit repair organization that violates the Credit Repair Organization Act.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d The Act\xe2\x80\x99s nonwaiver provision states, \xe2\x80\x9cAny waiver by any consumer of any protection provided by or any right of the consumer under this subchapter\xe2\x80\x94 (1) shall be treated as void; and (2) may not be enforced by any Federal or State court or any other person.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71679f(a).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Ninth Circuit adopted the following line of reasoning, urged upon us by respondents here: The disclosure provision gives consumers the \xe2\x80\x9cright to sue,\xe2\x80\x9d which \xe2\x80\x9cclearly involves the right to bring an action in a court of law.\xe2\x80\x9d 615 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1208. Because the nonwaiver provision prohibits the waiver of \xe2\x80\x9cany right of the consumer under this subchapter,\xe2\x80\x9d the arbitration agreement\xe2\x80\x94which waived the right to bring an action in a court of law\xe2\x80\x94cannot be enforced. Id., at 1214.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe flaw in this argument is its premise: that the disclosure provision provides consumers with a right to bring an action in a court of law. It does not. Rather, it imposes an obligation on credit repair organizations to supply consumers with a specific statement set forth (in quotation marks) in the statute. The only consumer right it creates is the right to receive the statement, which is meant to describe the consumer protections that the law elsewhere provides. The statement informs consumers, for instance, that they can dispute the accuracy of information in their credit file and that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[t]he credit bureau must then reinvestigate and modify or remove inaccurate or incomplete information.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d \n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71679c(a). That description is derived from \xc2\xa71681i(a), which sets out in great detail the procedures to be followed by a credit bureau in the event of challenges to the accuracy of its information. Similarly, the required statement informs consumers that they may \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98cancel your contract with any credit repair organization for any reason within 3 business days from the date you signed it\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94the right created and set forth in more detail in \xc2\xa71679e. And the \xe2\x80\x9cright to sue\xe2\x80\x9d language describes the consumer\xe2\x80\x99s right to enforce the credit repair organization\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cliab[ility]\xe2\x80\x9d for \xe2\x80\x9cfail[ure] to comply with any provision of this subchapter\xe2\x80\x9d provided for in \xc2\xa71679g(a).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Thus, con-trary to the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s assertion, our interpretation does not \xe2\x80\x9c[r]educ[e] the required disclosure to insignificance,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 6. The disclosure provision informs consumers of their right to enforce liability for any failure to conform to the statute\xe2\x80\x94information they might otherwise not possess. It is the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation that effectively reduces a portion of the CROA to a nullity. Interpreting the \xe2\x80\x9cright to sue\xe2\x80\x9d language in \xc2\xa71679c(a) to \xe2\x80\x9ccreate\xe2\x80\x9d a right to sue in court not only renders it strikingly out of place in a section that is otherwise devoted to giving the consumer notice of rights created elsewhere; it also renders the creation of the \xe2\x80\x9cright to sue\xe2\x80\x9d elsewhere superfluous.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondents suggest that the CROA\xe2\x80\x99s civil-liability pro-vision, \xc2\xa71679g (set forth in full in the Appendix, infra), demonstrates that the Act provides consumers with a \xe2\x80\x9cright\xe2\x80\x9d to bring an action in court. They cite the provision\xe2\x80\x99s repeated use of the terms \xe2\x80\x9caction,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cclass action,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ccourt\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94terms that they say call to mind a judicial proceeding. These references cannot do the heavy lifting that respondents assign them. It is utterly commonplace for statutes that create civil causes of action to describe the details of those causes of action, including the relief available, in the context of a court suit. If the mere formulation of the cause of action in this standard fashion were sufficient to establish the \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary congressional com- mand\xe2\x80\x9d overriding the FAA, McMahon, supra, at 226, valid arbitration agreements covering federal causes of action would be rare indeed. But that is not the law. In Gilmer we enforced an arbitration agreement with respect to a cause of action created by the Age Discrimination in Employment Act of 1967 (ADEA) which read, in part: \xe2\x80\x9cAny person aggrieved may bring a civil action in any court of competent jurisdiction for such legal or equitable relief as will effectuate the purposes of this chapter.\xe2\x80\x9d \n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7626(c)(1). In McMahon we enforced an arbitration agreement with respect to a cause of action created by a provision of the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act (RICO) which read, in part: \xe2\x80\x9cAny person injured in his business or property by reason of a violation of section 1962 of this chapter may sue therefor in any appropriate United States district court and shall recover threefold the damages he sustains and the cost of the suit .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d \n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71964(c). And in Mitsubishi Motors we enforced an arbitration agreement with respect to a cause of action created by a provision of the Clayton Act which read, in part: \xe2\x80\x9c[A]ny person who shall be injured in his business or property by reason of anything forbidden in the antitrust laws may sue therefor in any district court of the United States .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and shall recover threefold the damages by him sustained, and the cost of suit, including a reasonable attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fee.\xe2\x80\x9d \n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa715(a). Thus, we have repeatedly recognized that contractually required arbitration of claims satisfies the statutory prescription of civil liability in court. See Gilmer, 500 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 28; McMahon, 482 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 240; Mitsubishi Motors, 473 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 637. To be sure, none of the statutes described above contained a nonwaiver provision, as the statute before us does. But if a cause-of-action provision mentioning judicial enforcement does not create a right to initial judicial enforcement, the waiver of initial judicial enforcement is not the waiver of a \xe2\x80\x9cright of the consumer,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71679f(a). It takes a considerable stretch to regard the nonwaiver provision as a \xe2\x80\x9ccongressional command\xe2\x80\x9d that the FAA shall not apply.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMoreover, if one believes that \xc2\xa71679g\xe2\x80\x99s contemplation of court suit (combined with \xc2\xa71679f(a)) establishes a nonwaiv-able right to initial judicial enforcement, one must also believe that it establishes a nonwaivable right to initial judicial enforcement in any competent judicial tribunal, since it contains no limitation. We think it clear, however, that this mere \xe2\x80\x9ccontemplation\xe2\x80\x9d of suit in any competent court does not guarantee suit in all competent courts, disabling the parties from adopting a reasonable forum-selection clause. And just as the contemplated availability of all judicial forums may be reduced to a single forum by contractual specification, so also can the contemplated availability of judicial action be limited to judicial action compelling or reviewing initial arbitral adjudication. The parties remain free to specify such matters, so long as the guarantee of \xc2\xa71679g\xe2\x80\x94the guarantee of the legal power to impose liability\xe2\x80\x94is preserved.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondents and the dissent maintain that if the CROA does not create a right to a judicial forum, then the disclosure provision effectively requires that credit repair organizations mislead consumers. We think not. The disclosure provision is meant to describe the law to consumers in a manner that is concise and comprehensible to the layman\xe2\x80\x94which necessarily means that it will be imprecise. The required statement says, for example, that the CROA \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98prohibits deceptive practices by credit repair organiza- tions,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d \n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71679c(a). This is in some respects an overstatement, and in some respects an understatement, of the \xe2\x80\x9cProhibited practices\xe2\x80\x9d set forth in \xc2\xa71679b. It would include, for example, deception apart from \xe2\x80\x9cthe offer or sale of the services of the credit repair organization,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71679b(a)(4). Yet we would not hold, in order to prevent the required statement from being \xe2\x80\x9cmisleading,\xe2\x80\x9d that a consumer has a right to be protected from deceptive practices beyond those actually covered by \xc2\xa71679b. So also with respect to the statement\xe2\x80\x99s description of a \xe2\x80\x9cright to sue.\xe2\x80\x9d This is a colloquial method of communicating to consumers that they have the legal right, enforceable in court, to recover damages from credit repair organizations that violate the CROA. We think most consumers would understand it this way, without regard to whether the suit in court has to be preceded by an arbitration proceeding. Leaving that possibility out may be imprecise, but it is not misleading\xe2\x80\x94and certainly not so misleading as to demand, in order to avoid that result, reading the statute to contain a guaranteed right it does not in fact contain.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIV\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAt the time of the CROA\xe2\x80\x99s enactment in 1996, arbitration clauses in contracts of the type at issue here were no rarity. Quite the contrary, the early 1990\xe2\x80\x99s saw the increased use of arbitration clauses in consumer contracts generally, and in financial services contracts in particular. See Ware, Arbitration and Unconscionability After Doctor\xe2\x80\x99s Associates, Inc. v. Casarotto, 31 Wake Forest L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1001, 1001, and n.\xc2\xa03 (1996); J. Shimabukuro, Congressional Research Service Report for Congress, The Federal Arbitration Act: Background and Recent Developments 1 (2002).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tHad Congress meant to prohibit these very common provisions in the CROA, it would have done so in a manner less obtuse than what respondents suggest. When it has restricted the use of arbitration in other contexts, it has done so with a clarity that far exceeds the claimed indications in the CROA. See, e.g., \n7 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa726(n)(2) (2006 ed., Supp.\xc2\xa0IV) (\xe2\x80\x9cNo predispute arbitration agreement shall be valid or enforceable, if the agreement requires arbitration of a dispute arising under this section\xe2\x80\x9d); \n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71226(a)(2) (2006 ed.) \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tClaims alleging the violation of a statute, such as the Credit Repair Organizations Act (Act), \n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71679 et\xc2\xa0seq., are generally subject to valid arbitration agreements unless Congress evinces a contrary intent in the text, history, or purpose of the statute. See Gilmer v. Interstate/Johnson Lane Corp., \n\n500 U.\xc2\xa0S. 20, \n26 (1991)\n. I agree with the Court that Congress has not shown that intent here. But for the reasons stated by the dissent, I find this to be a much closer case than the majority opinion suggests.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Act creates a cause of action in its liability provision, see \xc2\xa71679g(a),\xc2\xa0denominates the cause of action a \xe2\x80\x9cright to sue\xe2\x80\x9d in the mandatory disclosure statement, \xc2\xa71679c(a), and then provides that \xe2\x80\x9cright[s]\xe2\x80\x9d may not be waived, \xc2\xa71679f(a). Those for whom Congress wrote the Act\xe2\x80\x94lay readers \xe2\x80\x9cof limited economic means and .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. inexperienced in credit matters,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71679(a)(2)\xe2\x80\x94reasonably may interpret the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cright to sue\xe2\x80\x9d as promising a right to sue in court. And it is plausible to think that Congress, aware of the impact of its words, intended such a construction of the liability provision.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut while this interpretation of the Act is plausible, it is in my view no more compelling than the contrary construction that petitioners urge. As the majority opinion notes, the disclosure provision does not itself confer a cause of action, and the liability provision that does is materially indistinguishable from other statutes that we have held not to preclude arbitration. In my mind this leaves the parties\xe2\x80\x99 arguments in equipoise, and our precedents require that petitioners prevail in this circumstance. This is because respondents, as the opponents of arbitration, bear the burden of showing that Congress disallowed arbitration of their claims, and because we resolve doubts in favor of arbitration. See id., at 26. Of course, if we have misread Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent, then Congress can correct our error by amending the statute.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI add one more point. The majority opinion contrasts the liability provision of the Act with other, more recently enacted statutes that expressly disallow arbitration. I do not understand the majority opinion to hold that Congress must speak so explicitly in order to convey its intent to preclude arbitration of statutory claims. We have never said as much, and on numerous occasions have held that proof of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent may also be discovered in the history or purpose of the statute in question. See ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9cIf such an intention exists, it will be discoverable in the text of the [statute], its legislative history, or an \xe2\x80\x98inherent conflict\xe2\x80\x99 between arbitration and the [statute\xe2\x80\x99s] underlying purposes\xe2\x80\x9d); Shearson/American Express Inc. v. McMahon, \n\n482 U.\xc2\xa0S. 220, \n227 (1987)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cIf Congress did intend to limit or prohibit waiver of a judicial forum for a particular claim, such an intent \xe2\x80\x98will be deducible from [the statute\xe2\x80\x99s] text or legislative history,\xe2\x80\x99 or from an inherent conflict between arbitration and the statute\xe2\x80\x99s underlying pur-poses\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Mitsubishi Motors Corp. v. Soler Chrysler-Plymouth, Inc., \n\n473 U.\xc2\xa0S. 614, \n628 (1985)\n; citation omitted)). I agree with the dissent that the statutes the majority opinion cites shed little light on the thoughts of the Congress that passed the Act. But the Act\xe2\x80\x99s text is not dispositive, and respondents identify nothing in the legislative history or purpose of the Act that would tip the balance of the scale in favor of their interpretation.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress enacted the Credit Repair Organizations Act (CROA) to protect consumers \xe2\x80\x9cwho have experienced credit problems\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cparticularly those of limited economic means\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94against the unfair and deceptive practices of credit repair organizations. \n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71679(a). Central to the legislation, Congress sought to arm consumers with information needed to make intelligent decisions about purchasing a repair organization\xe2\x80\x99s services. To that end, Congress directed that, \xe2\x80\x9cbefore [execution of] any contract .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. between [a] consumer and [a] credit repair organization,\xe2\x80\x9d the organization must make certain disclosures. One of the required disclosures reads:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cYou have a right to sue a credit repair organization that violates the [CROA]. This law prohibits deceptive practices by [such] organizations.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71679c(a).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe Act\xe2\x80\x99s civil-liability provision describes suits consumers may bring in court: individual and class actions for damages (actual and punitive) and attorneys\xe2\x80\x99 fees. A further provision renders void any purported waiver of any protection or right the Act grants to consumers.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court today holds that credit repair organizations can escape suit by providing in their take-it-or-leave-it contracts that arbitration will serve as the parties\xe2\x80\x99 sole dispute-resolution mechanism. The \xe2\x80\x9cright to sue,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court explains, merely connotes the vindication of legal rights, whether in court or before an arbitrator. That reading may be comprehensible to one trained to \xe2\x80\x9cthink like a lawyer.\xe2\x80\x9d But Congress enacted the CROA with vulnerable consumers in mind\xe2\x80\x94consumers likely to read the words \xe2\x80\x9cright to sue\xe2\x80\x9d to mean the right to litigate in court, not the obligation to submit disputes to binding arbitration.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn accord with the Ninth Circuit, I would hold that Congress, in an Act meant to curb deceptive practices, did not authorize credit repair organizations to make a false or misleading disclosure\xe2\x80\x94telling consumers of a right they do not, in fact, possess. If the Act affords consumers a nonwaivable right to sue in court, as I believe it does, a credit repair organization cannot retract that right by making arbitration the consumer\xe2\x80\x99s sole recourse.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCompuCredit marketed a credit card to consumers with weak credit ratings. It did so through massive direct-mail and Internet solicitations, urging recipients to acquire a card under the brand name Aspire Visa, and thereby \xe2\x80\x9crebuild poor credit\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cimprove [their] credit rating.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 40, Complaint \xc2\xb611 (internal quotation marks omitted). Plaintiffs, individuals who applied for and received CompuCredit\xe2\x80\x99s card, sought redress for multiple violations of the CROA.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTheir complaint alleged that CompuCredit\xe2\x80\x99s promotional materials told potential customers that no deposit would be required, and that cardholders would receive, upfront, a credit line of $300. In fact, plaintiffs asserted, they were charged an initial finance fee of $29, a monthly fee of $6.50, and an annual fee of $150, assessed immediately against the $300 limit. In the aggregate, plaintiffs calculated, fees charged the first year amounted to $257. CompuCredit\xe2\x80\x99s fee exactions did appear in the promotional materials: in small print, buried amidst other information, and removed from the clearer representation that no de- posit would be required. Id., at 40\xe2\x80\x9341, Complaint \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb612\xe2\x80\x9313. Far from improving their credit rating, plaintiffs complained, CompuCredit knew that its card, saddled with these fees, \xe2\x80\x9cwould not provide any meaningful assistance whatsoever with regard to rebuilding credit and improving a credit rating.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 48, Complaint \xc2\xb641(b). Furthermore, plaintiffs stated, CompuCredit did not provide them with the written disclosures of their rights required by the CROA. Id., at 42, Complaint \xc2\xb623.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSeeking damages for the alleged violations, along with attorneys\xe2\x80\x99 fees, plaintiffs requested class certification. In the District Court and Court of Appeals, they successfully resisted CompuCredit\xe2\x80\x99s motion to compel arbitration pursuant to a form contract that barred class proceedings.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t This Court, however, interprets the CROA to permit CompuCredit\xe2\x80\x99s demand that plaintiffs proceed, if at all, before an arbitrator.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t I read the governing statute differently.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThree sections of the CROA, considered together, indicate Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intention to preclude mandatory, creditor-imposed, arbitration of CROA claims. See 15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71679c(a), 1679g, and 1679f. Before entering into any consumer contract, credit repair organizations must give potential customers a written statement of rights they possess under that Act and related consumer-protection laws. \xc2\xa71679c(a). Congress dictated every word of the required notification. Credit repair organizations must tell consumers, in plain terms, how they may enforce their rights: \xe2\x80\x9cYou have a right to sue a credit repair organization that violates the Credit Repair Organization Act.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe \xe2\x80\x9cright to sue\xe2\x80\x9d refers to the claim for relief Congress afforded consumers in \xc2\xa71679g. \xe2\x80\x9cAny person\xe2\x80\x9d who violates another\xe2\x80\x99s rights under the CROA \xe2\x80\x9cshall be liable\xe2\x80\x9d for actual damages and attorneys\xe2\x80\x99 fees, and may be liable for punitive damages as well. \xc2\xa71679g(a)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(3). The Act sets out the factors \xe2\x80\x9cthe court shall consider\xe2\x80\x9d in determining the amount of punitive damages \xe2\x80\x9cthe court may allow\xe2\x80\x9d aggrieved consumers to recover, either individually or as a class. \xc2\xa71679g(a)(2) and (b). The liability created here, in \xc2\xa71679g, is precisely what the consumer, in light of \xc2\xa71679c, may sue to enforce.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Act renders void and unenforceable \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny waiver by any consumer of any protection provided by or any right of the consumer under this subchapter.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71679f (emphasis added).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t The rights listed in \xc2\xa71679c(a) rendered nonwaiv- able by \xc2\xa71679f are the \xe2\x80\x9cright to sue\xe2\x80\x9d and the \xe2\x80\x9cright to cancel [a] contract .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. for any reason within 3 business days from the date [the consumer] signed it.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe question on which this case turns is what Congress meant when it created a nonwaivable \xe2\x80\x9cright to sue.\xe2\x80\x9d Recall that Congress\xe2\x80\x99 target audience in the CROA is not composed of lawyers and judges accustomed to nuanced reading of statutory texts, but laypersons who receive a disclosure statement in the mail. Recall, as well, Congress\xe2\x80\x99 findings that these individuals are often \xe2\x80\x9cof limited economic means and .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. inexperienced in credit matters.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71679(a)(2). Attributing little importance to this context, the Court construes the right to sue as \xe2\x80\x9cthe legal right, enforceable in court, to recover damages .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. without regard to whether the suit in court has to be preceded by an arbitration proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8. I read Congress\xe2\x80\x99 words without that sophisticated gloss: The right to sue, I would hold, means the right to litigate in court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court is quite right in recognizing that consumers \xe2\x80\x9chave the legal right, enforceable in court, to recover damages from credit repair organizations that violate the CROA.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. But the Court is quite wrong, as I see it, to characterize as merely \xe2\x80\x9cimprecise,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., Congress\xe2\x80\x99 failure to include the caveat that access to court may be conditioned upon an anterior arbitration. The \xe2\x80\x9cright to sue\xe2\x80\x9d may well be \xe2\x80\x9ca colloquial method of communicating to consumers.\xe2\x80\x9d See ibid. But it surely is not colloquially understood by recipients of the required disclosures as the right, not to adjudicate in court, but only to seek, or defend against, court enforcement of an award rendered by the arbitrator chosen by the credit repair organization. Few, if any, credit repair customers would equate the \xe2\x80\x9cright to sue,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71679c(a), with the extremely limited judicial review given to an arbitrator\xe2\x80\x99s award, see, e.g., Hall Street Associates, L. L. C. v. Mattel, Inc., \n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 576\n\xe2\x80\x93589 (2008).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court discounts the references to \xe2\x80\x9caction,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cclass action,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ccourt\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa71679g, the provision that \xe2\x80\x9ccreate[s]\xe2\x80\x9d the consumers\xe2\x80\x99 claim for relief. See ante, at 5. Despite similar statutory language, the Court observes, we have enforced arbitration agreements to settle disputes arising under other Acts of Congress. The CROA, how- ever, is distinguished by its disclosure requirements, prime among them, the obligation imposed on the credit repair organization to inform potential customers they \xe2\x80\x9chave a right to sue\xe2\x80\x9d an organization that violates the Act. \xc2\xa71679c(a). Yet the Court refuses to read this language in concert with \xc2\xa71679g, notwithstanding our frequent acknowledgment that \xe2\x80\x9ca statute is to be read as a whole, since the meaning of statutory language .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. depends on context.\xe2\x80\x9d King v. St. Vincent\xe2\x80\x99s Hospital, \n\n502 U.\xc2\xa0S. 215, \n221 (1991)\n (citation omitted). As just explained, I believe Congress meant what an ordinary reader of the disclosure requirement would likely comprehend: A credit repair organization that engages in deceptive practices may be sued in court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tReducing the required disclosure to insignificance, see ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x935, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s construction of the CROA scarcely advances the Act\xe2\x80\x99s goals. Congress aimed to ensure prospective customers \xe2\x80\x9care provided with the information necessary to make an informed decision,\xe2\x80\x9d and also to \xe2\x80\x9cprotect the public from unfair or deceptive advertising and business practices.\xe2\x80\x9d \n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71679(b). The Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation, however, enables the very deception Congress sought to suppress. Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision permits credit repair organizations to deny consumers, through fine print in a contract, an important right whose disclosure is decreed in the U.\xc2\xa0S. Code.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis unfortunate result is not compelled by our precedents. The Court cites three decisions for the proposition, by now uncontroversial, that the mere existence of a stat- utory right of action does not preclude agreements to arbitrate disputes. See ante, at 5\xe2\x80\x936 (citing Gilmer v. Interstate/Johnson Lane Corp., \n\n500 U.\xc2\xa0S. 20, \n28 (1991)\n; Shearson/American Express Inc. v. McMahon, \n\n482 U.\xc2\xa0S. 220, \n240 (1987)\n; and Mitsubishi Motors Corp. v. Soler Chrysler-Plymouth, Inc., \n\n473 U.\xc2\xa0S. 614, \n637 (1985)\n). As the Court acknowledges, ante, at 6, none of the statutes at issue in those cases contained a nonwaiver clause analogous to \xc2\xa71679f. Yet the presence of such a clause would not have affected the outcome, the Court maintains; a nonwaiver provision would not have precluded arbitration because the statutes conferred no underlying right to proceed in court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPrecisely the point: The CROA differs from the statutes we have construed in the past in just that respect. The Act does not merely create a claim for relief. It designates that claim as an action entailing a \xe2\x80\x9cright to sue\xe2\x80\x9d; mandates that consumers be informed, prior to entering any contract, of that right; and precludes the waiver of any \xe2\x80\x9cright\xe2\x80\x9d conferred by the Act. Neither Gilmer, McMahon, nor Mitsubishi construed a statute of a similar order.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court\xe2\x80\x99s final point is that, elsewhere, Congress has spoken with particular clarity in guaranteeing a judicial forum and proscribing arbitration, but here, it did not do so. The two statutes the Court cites as exemplary postdate the CROA\xe2\x80\x99s enactment by 14 and 6 years, respectively. (A third merely delegates regulatory authority over certain arbitration agreements.) See ante, at 9. Notably, these recent statutes were framed following a string of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions compelling arbitration pursuant to contractual stipulations.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Our decisions have increasingly alerted Congress to the utility of drafting antiwaiver prescriptions with meticulous care. But the Congress that drafted the CROA was not similarly stimulated, and we cannot fairly assess that enactment in the light of subsequent legislative responses to developments unknown to the CROA\xe2\x80\x99s drafters. Cf. United States v. Price, \n\n361 U.\xc2\xa0S. 304, \n313 (1960)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he views of a subsequent Congress form a hazardous basis for inferring the intent of an earlier one.\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBeyond question, the Federal Arbitration Act, \xe2\x80\x9cstanding alone,\xe2\x80\x9d favors the enforcement of arbitration agreements. McMahon, 482 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 226. To depart from that rule, however, Congress need not employ \xe2\x80\x9cmagic words.\xe2\x80\x9d See Tr. of Oral Arg. 6. In determining whether the Arbitration Act\xe2\x80\x99s general rule has been displaced by another statutory prescription, it remains our responsibility to examine carefully \xe2\x80\x9cthe text of the [statute], its legislative history,\xe2\x80\x9d and Congress\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cunderlying purposes.\xe2\x80\x9d Gilmer, 500 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 26 (citing McMahon, 482 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 227). See also 14 Penn Plaza LLC v. Pyett, \n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 247, \n258 (2009)\n (arbitration agreements will be enforced \xe2\x80\x9cunless Congress itself has evinced an intention to preclude a waiver of judicial remedies for the statutory rights at issue\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Gilmer, 500 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 26, in turn quoting Mitsubishi, 473 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 628)). No \xe2\x80\x9cunmistakably clear\xe2\x80\x9d statement is necessary to proscribe the arbitration clause CompuCredit seeks to enforce.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe CROA mandates that potential customers shall be told of their \xe2\x80\x9cright to sue a credit repair organization\xe2\x80\x9d for damages arising from deceptive practices. \n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71679c(a). But CompuCredit\xe2\x80\x99s adhesion contract provided that consumers would \xe2\x80\x9cnot have the right to go to court.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 61 (capitalization omitted). Congress\xe2\x80\x99 direction must prevail over CompuCredit\xe2\x80\x99s opposing declaration. Accordingly, I would affirm the judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit.\n\t\t\t
