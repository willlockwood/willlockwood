Syllabus

ZIGLAR v. ABBASI et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the second circuit

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x931358.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 18, 2017\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 19, 2017[1]

\n

In the immediate aftermath of the September 11 terrorist attacks, the Federal Government ordered hundreds of illegal aliens to be taken into custody and held pending a determination whether a particular detainee had connections to terrorism. Respondents, six men of Arab or South Asian descent, were detained for periods of three to six months in a federal facility in Brooklyn. After their release, they were removed from the United States. They then filed this putative class action against petitioners, two groups of federal officials. The first group consisted of former Attorney General John Ashcroft, former Federal Bureau of Investigation Director Robert Mueller, and former Immigration and Naturalization Service Commissioner James Ziglar (Executive Officials). The second group consisted of the facility\xe2\x80\x99s warden and assistant warden Dennis Hasty and James Sherman (Wardens). Respondents sought damages for constitutional violations under the implied cause of action theory adopted in Bivens v. Six Unknown Fed. Narcotics Agents,\n\n403 U.\xc2\xa0S. 388\n, alleging that petitioners detained them in harsh pretrial conditions for a punitive purpose, in violation of the\n \nFifth Amendment; that petitioners did so because of their actual or apparent race, religion, or national origin, in violation of the\n \nFifth Amendment; that the Wardens subjected them to punitive strip searches, in violation of the Fourth and\n \nFifth Amendments; and that the Wardens knowingly allowed the guards to abuse them, in violation of the\n \nFifth Amendment. Respondents also brought a claim under\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71985(3), which forbids certain conspiracies to violate equal protection rights. The District Court dismissed the claims against the Executive Officials but allowed the claims against the Wardens to go forward. The Second Circuit affirmed in most respects as to the Wardens but reversed as to the Executive Officials, reinstating respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The judgment is reversed in part and vacated and remanded in part.

\n

789 F.\xc2\xa03d 218, reversed in part and vacated and remanded in part.

\n

Justice Kennedy delivered the opinion of the Court, except as to Part IV\xe2\x80\x93B, concluding:

\n

1.\xc2\xa0The limited reach of the Bivens action informs the decision whether an implied damages remedy should be recognized here. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0In\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983, Congress provided a specific damages remedy for plaintiffs whose constitutional rights were violated by state officials, but Congress provided no corresponding remedy for constitutional violations by agents of the Federal Government. In 1971, and against this background, this Court recognized in Bivens an implied damages action to compensate persons injured by federal officers who violated the\n \nFourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition against unreasonable searches and seizures. In the following decade, the Court allowed Bivens-type remedies twice more, in a\n \nFifth Amendment gender-discrimination case, Davis v. Passman,\n\n442 U.\xc2\xa0S. 228\n, and in an\n \nEighth Amendment Cruel and Unusual Punishments Clause case, Carlson v. Green,\n\n446 U.\xc2\xa0S. 14\n. These are the only cases in which the Court has approved of an implied damages remedy under the Constitution itself. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0Bivens, Davis, and Carlson were decided at a time when the prevailing law assumed that a proper judicial function was to \xe2\x80\x9cprovide such remedies as are necessary to make effective\xe2\x80\x9d a statute\xe2\x80\x99s purpose. J.\xc2\xa0I. Case Co. v. Borak,\n\n377 U.\xc2\xa0S. 426\n. The Court has since adopted a far more cautious course, clarifying that, when deciding whether to recognize an implied cause of action, the \xe2\x80\x9cdeterminative\xe2\x80\x9d question is one of statutory intent. Alexander v. Sandoval,\n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 275\n. If a statute does not evince Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent \xe2\x80\x9cto create the private right of action asserted,\xe2\x80\x9d Touche Ross & Co. v. Redington,\n\n442 U.\xc2\xa0S. 560\n, no such action will be created through judicial mandate. Similar caution must be exercised with respect to damages actions implied to enforce the Constitution itself. Bivens is well-settled law in its own context, but expanding the Bivens remedy is now considered a \xe2\x80\x9cdisfavored\xe2\x80\x9d judicial activity. Ashcroft v. Iqbal,\n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 662\n.

\n

When a party seeks to assert an implied cause of action under the Constitution, separation-of-powers principles should be central to the analysis. The question is whether Congress or the courts should decide to authorize a damages suit. Bush v. Lucas,\n\n462 U.\xc2\xa0S. 367\n. Most often it will be Congress, for Bivens will not be extended to a new context if there are \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98special factors counselling hesitation in the absence of affirmative action by Congress.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Carlson, supra, at 18. If there are sound reasons to think Congress might doubt the efficacy or necessity of a damages remedy as part of the system for enforcing the law and correcting a wrong, courts must refrain from creating that kind of remedy. An alternative remedial structure may also limit the Judiciary\xe2\x80\x99s power to infer a new Bivens cause of action. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

2.\xc2\xa0Considering the relevant special factors here, a Bivens-type remedy should not be extended to the claims challenging the confinement conditions imposed on respondents pursuant to the formal policy adopted by the Executive Officials in the wake of the September 11 attacks. These \xe2\x80\x9cdetention policy claims\xe2\x80\x9d include the allegations that petitioners violated respondents\xe2\x80\x99 due process and equal protection rights by holding them in restrictive conditions of confinement, and the allegations that the Wardens violated the Fourth and\n \nFifth Amendments by subjecting respondents to frequent strip searches. The detention policy claims do not include the guard-abuse claim against Warden Hasty. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9323.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0The proper test for determining whether a claim arises in a new Bivens context is as follows. If the case is different in a meaningful way from previous Bivens cases decided by this Court, then the context is new. Meaningful differences may include, e.g., the rank of the officers involved; the constitutional right at issue; the extent of judicial guidance for the official conduct; the risk of disruptive intrusion by the Judiciary into the functioning of other branches; or the presence of potential special factors not considered in previous Bivens cases. Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 detention policy claims bear little resemblance to the three Bivens claims the Court has approved in previous cases. The Second Circuit thus should have held that this was a new Bivens context and then performed a special factors analysis before allowing this damages suit to proceed. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0The special factors here indicate that Congress, not the courts, should decide whether a damages action should be allowed.

\n

With regard to the Executive Officials, a Bivens action is not \xe2\x80\x9ca proper vehicle for altering an entity\xe2\x80\x99s policy,\xe2\x80\x9d Correctional Services Corp. v. Malesko,\n\n534 U.\xc2\xa0S. 61\n, and is not designed to hold officers responsible for acts of their subordinates, see Iqbal, supra, at 676. Even an action confined to the Executive Officers\xe2\x80\x99 own discrete conduct would call into question the formulation and implementation of a high-level executive policy, and the burdens of that litigation could prevent officials from properly discharging their duties, see Cheney v. United States Dist. Court for D. C.,\n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 367\n. The litigation process might also implicate the discussion and deliberations that led to the formation of the particular policy, requiring courts to interfere with sensitive Executive Branch functions. See Clinton v. Jones,\n\n520 U.\xc2\xa0S. 681\n.

\n

Other special factors counsel against extending Bivens to cover the detention policy claims against any of the petitioners. Because those claims challenge major elements of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s response to the September 11 attacks, they necessarily require an inquiry into national-security issues. National-security policy, however, is the prerogative of Congress and the President, and courts are \xe2\x80\x9creluctant to intrude upon\xe2\x80\x9d that authority absent congressional authorization. Department of Navy v. Egan,\n\n484 U.\xc2\xa0S. 518\n. Thus, Congress\xe2\x80\x99 failure to provide a damages remedy might be more than mere oversight, and its silence might be more than \xe2\x80\x9cinadvertent.\xe2\x80\x9d Schweiker v. Chilicky,\n\n487 U.\xc2\xa0S. 412\n. That silence is also relevant and telling here, where Congress has had nearly 16 years to extend \xe2\x80\x9cthe kind of remedies [sought by] respondents,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 426, but has not done so. Respondents also may have had available \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98other alternative forms of judicial relief,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Minneci v. Pollard,\n\n565 U.\xc2\xa0S. 118\n, including injunctions and habeas petitions.

\n

The proper balance in situations like this, between deterring constitutional violations and freeing high officials to make the lawful decisions necessary to protect the Nation in times of great peril, is one for the Congress to undertake, not the Judiciary. The Second Circuit thus erred in allowing respondents\xe2\x80\x99 detention policy claims to proceed under Bivens. Pp.\xc2\xa017\xe2\x80\x9323.

\n

3.\xc2\xa0The Second Circuit also erred in allowing the prisoner abuse claim against Warden Hasty to go forward without conducting the required special factors analysis. Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 prisoner abuse allegations against Warden Hasty state a plausible ground to find a constitutional violation should a Bivens remedy be implied. But the first question is whether the claim arises in a new Bivens context. This claim has significant parallels to Carlson, which extended Bivens to cover a failure to provide medical care to a prisoner, but this claim nevertheless seeks to extend Carlson to a new context. The constitutional right is different here: Carlson was predicated on the\n \nEighth Amendment while this claim was predicated on the Fifth. The judicial guidance available to this warden with respect to his supervisory duties was less developed. There might have been alternative remedies available. And Congress did not provide a standalone damages remedy against federal jailers when it enacted the Prison Litigation Reform Act some 15 years after Carlson. Given this Court\xe2\x80\x99s expressed caution about extending the Bivens remedy, this context must be regarded as a new one. Pp.\xc2\xa023\xe2\x80\x9326.

\n

4.\xc2\xa0Petitioners are entitled to qualified immunity with respect to respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims under\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71985(3). Pp.\xc2\xa026\xe2\x80\x9332.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0Assuming that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 allegations are true and well pleaded, the question is whether a reasonable officer in petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 position would have known the alleged conduct was an unlawful conspiracy. The qualified-immunity inquiry turns on the \xe2\x80\x9cobjective legal reasonableness\xe2\x80\x9d of the official\xe2\x80\x99s acts, Harlow v. Fitzgerald,\n\n457 U.\xc2\xa0S. 800\n, \xe2\x80\x9cassessed in light of the legal rules that were \xe2\x80\x98clearly established\xe2\x80\x99 at the time [the action] was taken,\xe2\x80\x9d Anderson v. Creighton,\n\n483 U.\xc2\xa0S. 635\n. If it would have been clear to a reasonable officer that the alleged conduct \xe2\x80\x9cwas unlawful in the situation he confronted,\xe2\x80\x9d Saucier v. Katz,\n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 194\n, the defendant officer is not entitled to qualified immunity. But if a reasonable officer might not have known that the conduct was unlawful, then the officer is entitled to qualified immunity. Pp.\xc2\xa027\xe2\x80\x9329.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0Here, reasonable officials in petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 positions would not have known with sufficient certainty that \xc2\xa71985(3) prohibited their joint consultations and the resulting policies. There are two reasons. First, the conspiracy is alleged to have been among officers in the same Department of the Federal Government. And there is no clearly established law on the issue whether agents of the same executive department are distinct enough to \xe2\x80\x9cconspire\xe2\x80\x9d with one another within the meaning of\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71985(3). Second, open discussion among federal officers should be encouraged to help those officials reach consensus on department policies, so there is a reasonable argument that \xc2\xa71985(3) liability should not extend to cases like this one. As these considerations indicate, the question whether federal officials can be said to \xe2\x80\x9cconspire\xe2\x80\x9d in these kinds of situations is sufficiently open that the officials in this suit would not have known that \xc2\xa71985(3) applied to their discussions and actions. It follows that reasonable officers in petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 positions would not have known with any certainty that the alleged agreements were forbidden by that statute. Pp. 29\xe2\x80\x9332.

Justice Kennedy delivered the opinion of the Court, except as to Part IV\xe2\x80\x93B.

\n

After the September 11 terrorist attacks in this country, and in response to the deaths, destruction, and dangers they caused, the United States Government ordered hundreds of illegal aliens to be taken into custody and held. Pending a determination whether a particular detainee had connections to terrorism, the custody, under harsh conditions to be described, continued. In many instances custody lasted for days and weeks, then stretching into months. Later, some of the aliens who had been detained filed suit, leading to the cases now before the Court.

\n

The complaint named as defendants three high executive officers in the Department of Justice and two of the wardens at the facility where the detainees had been held. Most of the claims, alleging various constitutional violations, sought damages under the implied cause of action theory adopted by this Court in Bivens v. Six Unknown Fed. Narcotics Agents,\n\n403 U.\xc2\xa0S. 388 (1971)\n. Another claim in the complaint was based upon the statutory cause of action authorized and created by Congress under Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa71980,\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71985(3). This statutory cause of action allows damages to persons injured by conspiracies to deprive them of the equal protection of the laws.

\n

The suit was commenced in the United States District Court for the Eastern District of New York. After this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Ashcroft v. Iqbal,\n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 662 (2009)\n, a fourth amended complaint was filed; and that is the complaint to be considered here. Motions to dismiss the fourth amended complaint were denied as to some defendants and granted as to others. These rulings were the subject of interlocutory appeals to the United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit. Over a dissenting opinion by Judge Raggi with respect to the decision of the three-judge panel\xe2\x80\x94and a second unsigned dissent from the court\xe2\x80\x99s declining to rehear the suit en banc, joined by Judge Raggi and five other judges\xe2\x80\x94the Court of Appeals ruled that the complaint was sufficient for the action to proceed against the named officials who are now before us. See Turkmen v. Hasty, 789 F.\xc2\xa03d 218 (2015) (panel decision); Turkmen v. Hasty, 808 F.\xc2\xa03d 197 (2015) (en banc decision).

\n

The Court granted certiorari to consider these rulings. 580 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2016). The officials who must defend the suit on the merits, under the ruling of the Court of Appeals, are the petitioners here. The former detainees who seek relief under the fourth amended complaint are the respondents. The various claims and theories advanced for recovery, and the grounds asserted for their dismissal as insufficient as a matter of law, will be addressed in turn.

\n

I

\n

Given the present procedural posture of the suit, the Court accepts as true the facts alleged in the complaint. See Iqbal, 556 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 678.

\n

A

\n

In the weeks following the September 11, 2001, terrorist attacks\xe2\x80\x94the worst in American history\xe2\x80\x94the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) received more than 96,000 tips from members of the public. See id., at 667. Some tips were based on well-grounded suspicion of terrorist activity, but many others may have been based on fear of Arabs and Muslims. FBI agents \xe2\x80\x9cquestioned more than 1,000 people with suspected links to the [September 11] attacks in particular or to terrorism in general.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

While investigating the tips\xe2\x80\x94including the less substantiated ones\xe2\x80\x94the FBI encountered many aliens who were present in this country without legal authorization. As a result, more than 700 individuals were arrested and detained on immigration charges. Ibid. If the FBI designated an alien as not being \xe2\x80\x9cof interest\xe2\x80\x9d to the investigation, then he or she was processed according to normal procedures. In other words the alien was treated just as if, for example, he or she had been arrested at the border after an illegal entry. If, however, the FBI designated an alien as \xe2\x80\x9cof interest\xe2\x80\x9d to the investigation, or if it had doubts about the proper designation in a particular case, the alien was detained subject to a \xe2\x80\x9chold-until-cleared policy.\xe2\x80\x9d The aliens were held without bail.

\n

Respondents were among some 84 aliens who were subject to the hold-until-cleared policy and detained at the Metropolitan Detention Center (MDC) in Brooklyn, New York. They were held in the Administrative Maximum Special Housing Unit (or Unit) of the MDC. The complaint includes these allegations: Conditions in the Unit were harsh. Pursuant to official Bureau of Prisons policy, detainees were held in \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98tiny cells for over 23 hours a day.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 789 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 228. Lights in the cells were left on 24 hours. Detainees had little opportunity for exercise or recreation. They were forbidden to keep anything in their cells, even basic hygiene products such as soap or a toothbrush. When removed from the cells for any reason, they were shackled and escorted by four guards. They were denied access to most forms of communication with the outside world. And they were strip searched often\xe2\x80\x94any time they were moved, as well as at random in their cells.

\n

Some of the harsh conditions in the Unit were not imposed pursuant to official policy. According to the complaint, prison guards engaged in a pattern of \xe2\x80\x9cphysical and verbal abuse.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Guards allegedly slammed detainees into walls; twisted their arms, wrists, and fingers; broke their bones; referred to them as terrorists; threatened them with violence; subjected them to humiliating sexual comments; and insulted their religion.

\n

B

\n

Respondents are six men of Arab or South Asian descent. Five are Muslims. Each was illegally in this country, arrested during the course of the September 11 investigation, and detained in the Administrative Maximum Special Housing Unit for periods ranging from three to eight months. After being released respondents were removed from the United States.

\n

Respondents then sued on their own behalf, and on behalf of a putative class, seeking compensatory and punitive damages, attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees, and costs. Respondents, it seems fair to conclude from the arguments presented, acknowledge that in the ordinary course aliens who are present in the United States without legal authorization can be detained for some period of time. But here the challenge is to the conditions of their confinement and the reasons or motives for imposing those conditions. The gravamen of their claims was that the Government had no reason to suspect them of any connection to terrorism, and thus had no legitimate reason to hold them for so long in these harsh conditions.

\n

As relevant here, respondents sued two groups of federal officials in their official capacities. The first group consisted of former Attorney General John Ashcroft, former FBI Director Robert Mueller, and former Immigration and Naturalization Service Commissioner James Ziglar. This opinion refers to these three petitioners as the \xe2\x80\x9cExecutive Officials.\xe2\x80\x9d The other petitioners named in the complaint were the MDC\xe2\x80\x99s warden, Dennis Hasty, and associate warden, James Sherman. This opinion refers to these two petitioners as the \xe2\x80\x9cWardens.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Seeking to invoke the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Bivens, respondents brought four claims under the Constitution itself. First, respondents alleged that petitioners detained them in harsh pretrial conditions for a punitive purpose, in violation of the substantive due process component of the\n \nFifth Amendment. Second, respondents alleged that petitioners detained them in harsh conditions because of their actual or apparent race, religion, or national origin, in violation of the equal protection component of the\n \nFifth Amendment. Third, respondents alleged that the Wardens subjected them to punitive strip searches unrelated to any legitimate penological interest, in violation of the\n \nFourth Amendment and the substantive due process component of the\n \nFifth Amendment. Fourth, respondents alleged that the Wardens knowingly allowed the guards to abuse respondents, in violation of the substantive due process component of the\n \nFifth Amendment.

\n

Respondents also brought a claim under\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71985(3), which forbids certain conspiracies to violate equal protection rights. Respondents alleged that petitioners conspired with one another to hold respondents in harsh conditions because of their actual or apparent race, religion, or national origin.

\n

C

\n

The District Court dismissed the claims against the Executive Officials but allowed the claims against the Wardens to go forward. The Court of Appeals affirmed in most respects as to the Wardens, though it held that the prisoner abuse claim against Sherman (the associate warden) should have been dismissed. 789 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 264\xe2\x80\x93265. As to the Executive Officials, however, the Court of Appeals reversed, reinstating respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims. Ibid. As noted above, Judge Raggi dissented. She would have held that only the prisoner abuse claim against Hasty should go forward. Id., at 295, n. 41, 302 (opinion concurring in part in judgment and dissenting in part). The Court of Appeals declined to rehear the suit en banc, 808 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 197; and, again as noted above, Judge Raggi joined a second dissent along with five other judges, id., at 198. This Court granted certiorari. 580 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2016).

\n

II

\n

The first question to be discussed is whether petitioners can be sued for damages under Bivens and the ensuing cases in this Court defining the reach and the limits of that precedent.

\n

A

\n

In 1871, Congress passed a statute that was later codified at Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa71979,\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983. It entitles an injured person to money damages if a state official violates his or her constitutional rights. Congress did not create an analogous statute for federal officials. Indeed, in the 100 years leading up to Bivens, Congress did not pro-vide a specific damages remedy for plaintiffs whose con-stitutional rights were violated by agents of the Federal Government.

\n

In 1971, and against this background, this Court decided Bivens. The Court held that, even absent statutoryauthorization, it would enforce a damages remedy to compensate persons injured by federal officers who vio-lated the prohibition against unreasonable search and sei-zures. See 403 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 397. The Court acknowledged that the\n \nFourth Amendment does not provide for money damages \xe2\x80\x9cin so many words.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 396. The Court noted, however, that Congress had not foreclosed a damages remedy in \xe2\x80\x9cexplicit\xe2\x80\x9d terms and that no \xe2\x80\x9cspecial factors\xe2\x80\x9d suggested that the Judiciary should \xe2\x80\x9chesitat[e]\xe2\x80\x9d in the face of congressional silence. Id., at 396\xe2\x80\x93397. The Court, accordingly, held that it could authorize a remedy under general principles of federal jurisdiction. See id., at 392 (citing Bell v. Hood,\n\n327 U.\xc2\xa0S. 678,\n684 (1946)\n).

\n

In the decade that followed, the Court recognized what has come to be called an implied cause of action in two cases involving other constitutional violations. In Davis v. Passman,\n\n442 U.\xc2\xa0S. 228 (1979)\n, an administrative assistant sued a Congressman for firing her because she was a woman. The Court held that the\n \nFifth Amendment Due Process Clause gave her a damages remedy for gender discrimination. Id., at 248\xe2\x80\x93249. And in Carlson v. Green,\n\n446 U.\xc2\xa0S. 14 (1980)\n, a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s estate sued federal jailers for failing to treat the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s asthma. The Court held that the\n \nEighth Amendment Cruel and Unusual Punishments Clause gave him a damages remedy for failure to provide adequate medical treatment. See id., at 19. These three cases\xe2\x80\x94Bivens, Davis, and Carlson\xe2\x80\x94represent the only instances in which the Court has approved of an implied damages remedy under the Constitution itself.

\n

B

\n

To understand Bivens and the two other cases implying a damages remedy under the Constitution, it is necessary to understand the prevailing law when they were decided. In the mid-20th century, the Court followed a different approach to recognizing implied causes of action than it follows now. During this \xe2\x80\x9cancien regime,\xe2\x80\x9d Alexander v. Sandoval,\n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 275,\n287 (2001)\n, the Court assumed it to be a proper judicial function to \xe2\x80\x9cprovide such remedies as are necessary to make effective\xe2\x80\x9d a statute\xe2\x80\x99s purpose, J.\xc2\xa0I. Case Co. v. Borak,\n\n377 U.\xc2\xa0S. 426,\n433 (1964)\n. Thus, as a routine matter with respect to statutes, the Court would imply causes of action not explicit in the statutory text itself. See, e.g., id., at 430\xe2\x80\x93432; Allen v. State Bd. of Elections,\n\n393 U.\xc2\xa0S. 544,\n557 (1969)\n; Sullivan v. Little Hunting Park, Inc.,\n\n396 U.\xc2\xa0S. 229,\n239 (1969)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cThe existence of a statutory right implies the existence of all necessary and appropriate remedies\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

These statutory decisions were in place when Bivens recognized an implied cause of action to remedy a constitutional violation. Against that background, the Bivens decision held that courts must \xe2\x80\x9cadjust their remedies so as to grant the necessary relief\xe2\x80\x9d when \xe2\x80\x9cfederally protected rights have been invaded.\xe2\x80\x9d 403 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 392 (quoting Bell, supra, at 678); see also 403 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 402 (Harlan, J., concurring) (discussing cases recognizing implied causes of action under federal statutes). In light of this interpretive framework, there was a possibility that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Court would keep expanding Bivens until it became the substantial equivalent of\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983.\xe2\x80\x9d Kent, Are Damages Different?: Bivens and National Security, 87 S.\xc2\xa0Cal. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1123, 1139\xe2\x80\x931140 (2014).

\n

C

\n

Later, the arguments for recognizing implied causes of action for damages began to lose their force. In cases decided after Bivens, and after the statutory implied cause-of-action cases that Bivens itself relied upon, the Court adopted a far more cautious course before finding implied causes of action. In two principal cases under other statutes, it declined to find an implied cause of action. See Piper v. Chris-Craft Industries, Inc.,\n\n430 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n\xe2\x80\x9346 (1977); Cort v. Ash,\n\n422 U.\xc2\xa0S. 66\n\xe2\x80\x9369 (1975). Later, in Cannon v. University of Chicago,\n\n441 U.\xc2\xa0S. 677 (1979)\n, the Court did allow an implied cause of action; but it cautioned that, where Congress \xe2\x80\x9cintends private litigants to have a cause of action,\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9cfar better course\xe2\x80\x9d is for Congress to confer that remedy in explicit terms. Id., at 717.

\n

Following this expressed caution, the Court clarified in a series of cases that, when deciding whether to recognize an implied cause of action, the \xe2\x80\x9cdeterminative\xe2\x80\x9d question is one of statutory intent. Sandoval, 532 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 286. If the statute itself does not \xe2\x80\x9cdispla[y] an intent\xe2\x80\x9d to create \xe2\x80\x9ca private remedy,\xe2\x80\x9d then \xe2\x80\x9ca cause of action does not exist and courts may not create one, no matter how desirable that might be as a policy matter, or how compatible with the statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 286\xe2\x80\x93287; see also Transamerica Mortgage Advisors, Inc. v. Lewis,\n\n444 U.\xc2\xa0S. 11\n\xe2\x80\x9316, 23\xe2\x80\x9324 (1979); Karahalios v. Federal Employees,\n\n489 U.\xc2\xa0S. 527\n\xe2\x80\x93537 (1989). The Court held that the judicial task was instead \xe2\x80\x9climited solely to determining whether Congress intended to create the private right of action asserted.\xe2\x80\x9d Touche Ross & Co. v. Redington,\n\n442 U.\xc2\xa0S. 560,\n568 (1979)\n. If the statute does not itself so provide, a private cause of action will not be created through judicial mandate. See Transamerica, supra, at 24.

\n

The decision to recognize an implied cause of action under a statute involves somewhat different considerations than when the question is whether to recognize an implied cause of action to enforce a provision of the Constitution itself. When Congress enacts a statute, there are specific procedures and times for considering its terms and the proper means for its enforcement. It is logical, then, to assume that Congress will be explicit if it intends to create a private cause of action. With respect to the Constitution, however, there is no single, specific congressional action to consider and interpret.

\n

Even so, it is a significant step under separation-of-powers principles for a court to determine that it has the authority, under the judicial power, to create and enforce a cause of action for damages against federal officials in order to remedy a constitutional violation. When determining whether traditional equitable powers suffice to give necessary constitutional protection\xe2\x80\x94or whether, in addition, a damages remedy is necessary\xe2\x80\x94there are a number of economic and governmental concerns to con-sider. Claims against federal officials often create sub-stantial costs, in the form of defense and indemnification. Congress, then, has a substantial responsibility to determine whether, and the extent to which, monetary and other liabilities should be imposed upon individual officers and employees of the Federal Government. In addition, the time and administrative costs attendant upon intrusions resulting from the discovery and trial process are significant factors to be considered. In an analogous context, Congress, it is fair to assume, weighed those concerns in deciding not to substitute the Government as defendant in suits seeking damages for constitutional violations. See\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72679(b)(2)(A) (providing that certain provisions of the Federal Tort Claims Act do not apply to any claim against a federal employee \xe2\x80\x9cwhich is brought for a violation of the Constitution\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

For these and other reasons, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s expressed caution as to implied causes of actions under congressional statutes led to similar caution with respect to actions in the Bivens context, where the action is implied to enforce the Constitution itself. Indeed, in light of the changes to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s general approach to recognizing implied damages remedies, it is possible that the analysis in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s three Bivens cases might have been different if they were decided today. To be sure, no congressional enactment has disapproved of these decisions. And it must be understood that this opinion is not intended to cast doubt on the continued force, or even the necessity, of Bivens in the search-and-seizure context in which it arose. Bivens does vindicate the Constitution by allowing some redress for injuries, and it provides instruction and guidance to federal law enforcement officers going forward. The settled law of Bivens in this common and recurrent sphere of law enforcement, and the undoubted reliance upon it as a fixed principle in the law, are powerful reasons to retain it in that sphere.

\n

Given the notable change in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s approach to recognizing implied causes of action, however, the Court has made clear that expanding the Bivens remedy is now a \xe2\x80\x9cdisfavored\xe2\x80\x9d judicial activity. Iqbal, 556 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 675. This is in accord with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s observation that it has \xe2\x80\x9cconsistently refused to extend Bivens to any new context or new category of defendants.\xe2\x80\x9d Correctional Services Corp. v. Malesko,\n\n534 U.\xc2\xa0S. 61,\n68 (2001)\n. Indeed, the Court has refused to do so for the past 30 years.

\n

For example, the Court declined to create an implied damages remedy in the following cases: a\n \nFirst Amendment suit against a federal employer, Bush v. Lucas,\n\n462 U.\xc2\xa0S. 367,\n390 (1983)\n; a race-discrimination suit against military officers, Chappell v. Wallace,\n\n462 U.\xc2\xa0S. 296\n\xe2\x80\x93305 (1983); a substantive due process suit against military officers, United States v. Stanley,\n\n483 U.\xc2\xa0S. 669\n\xe2\x80\x93672, 683\xe2\x80\x93684 (1987); a procedural due process suit against Social Security officials, Schweiker v. Chilicky,\n\n487 U.\xc2\xa0S. 412,\n414 (1988)\n; a procedural due process suit against a federal agency for wrongful termination, FDIC v. Meyer,\n\n510 U.\xc2\xa0S. 471\n\xe2\x80\x93474 (1994); an\n \nEighth Amendment suit against a private prison operator, Malesko, supra, at 63; a due process suit against officials from the Bureau of Land Management, Wilkie v. Robbins,\n\n551 U.\xc2\xa0S. 537\n\xe2\x80\x93548, 562 (2007); and an\n \nEighth Amendment suit against prison guards at a private prison, Minneci v. Pollard,\n\n565 U.\xc2\xa0S. 118,\n120 (2012)\n.

\n

When a party seeks to assert an implied cause of action under the Constitution itself, just as when a party seeks to assert an implied cause of action under a federal statute, separation-of-powers principles are or should be central to the analysis. The question is \xe2\x80\x9cwho should decide\xe2\x80\x9d whether to provide for a damages remedy, Congress or the courts? Bush, 462 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 380.

\n

The answer most often will be Congress. When an issue \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98involves a host of considerations that must be weighed and appraised,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d it should be committed to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98those who write the laws\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d rather than \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98those who interpret them.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting United States v. Gilman,\n\n347 U.\xc2\xa0S. 507\n\xe2\x80\x93513 (1954)). In most instances, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents now instruct, the Legislature is in the better position to consider if \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the public interest would be served\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d by imposing a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98new substantive legal liability.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Schweiker, supra, at 426\xe2\x80\x93427 (quoting Bush, supra, at 390). As a result, the Court has urged \xe2\x80\x9ccaution\xe2\x80\x9d before \xe2\x80\x9cextending Bivens remedies into any new context.\xe2\x80\x9d Malesko, supra, at 74. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents now make clear that a Bivens remedy will not be available if there are \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98special factors counselling hesitation in the absence of affirmative action by Congress.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Carlson, 446 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 18 (quoting Bivens, 403 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 396).

\n

This Court has not defined the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cspecial factors counselling hesitation.\xe2\x80\x9d The necessary inference, though, is that the inquiry must concentrate on whether the Judiciary is well suited, absent congressional action or instruction, to consider and weigh the costs and benefits of allowing a damages action to proceed. Thus, to be a \xe2\x80\x9cspecial factor counselling hesitation,\xe2\x80\x9d a factor must cause acourt to hesitate before answering that question in the affirmative.

\n

It is not necessarily a judicial function to establish whole categories of cases in which federal officers must defend against personal liability claims in the complex sphere of litigation, with all of its burdens on some and benefits to others. It is true that, if equitable remedies prove insufficient, a damages remedy might be necessary to redress past harm and deter future violations. Yet the decision to recognize a damages remedy requires an assessment of its impact on governmental operations systemwide. Those matters include the burdens on Government employees who are sued personally, as well as the projected costs and consequences to the Government itself when the tort and monetary liability mechanisms of the legal system are used to bring about the proper formulation and implementation of public policies. These and other considerations may make it less probable that Congress would want the Judiciary to entertain a damages suit in a given case.

\n

Sometimes there will be doubt because the case arises in a context in which Congress has designed its regulatory authority in a guarded way, making it less likely that Congress would want the Judiciary to interfere. See Chappell, supra, at 302 (military); Stanley, supra, at 679 (same); Meyer, supra, at 486 (public purse); Wilkie, supra, at 561\xe2\x80\x93562 (federal land). And sometimes there will be doubt because some other feature of a case\xe2\x80\x94difficult to predict in advance\xe2\x80\x94causes a court to pause before acting without express congressional authorization. In sum, if there are sound reasons to think Congress might doubt the efficacy or necessity of a damages remedy as part of the system for enforcing the law and correcting a wrong, the courts must refrain from creating the remedy in order to respect the role of Congress in determining the nature and extent of federal-court jurisdiction under Article III.

\n

In a related way, if there is an alternative remedial structure present in a certain case, that alone may limit the power of the Judiciary to infer a new Bivens cause of action. For if Congress has created \xe2\x80\x9cany alternative, existing process for protecting the [injured party\xe2\x80\x99s] interest\xe2\x80\x9d that itself may \xe2\x80\x9camoun[t] to a convincing reason for the Judicial Branch to refrain from providing a new and freestanding remedy in damages.\xe2\x80\x9d Wilkie, supra, at 550; see also Bush, supra, at 385\xe2\x80\x93388 (recognizing that civil-service regulations provided alternative means for relief); Malesko, 534 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 73\xe2\x80\x9374 (recognizing that state tort law provided alternative means for relief); Minneci, supra, at 127\xe2\x80\x93130 (same).

\n

III

\n

It is appropriate now to turn first to the Bivens claims challenging the conditions of confinement imposed on respondents pursuant to the formal policy adopted by the Executive Officials in the wake of the September 11 attacks. The Court will refer to these claims as the \xe2\x80\x9cdetention policy claims.\xe2\x80\x9d The detention policy claims allege that petitioners violated respondents\xe2\x80\x99 due process and equal protection rights by holding them in restrictive conditions of confinement; the claims further allege that the Wardens violated the Fourth and\n \nFifth Amendments by subjecting respondents to frequent strip searches. The term \xe2\x80\x9cdetention policy claims\xe2\x80\x9d does not include respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claim alleging that Warden Hasty allowed guards to abuse the detainees. That claim will be considered separately, and further, below. At this point, the question is whether, having considered the relevant special factors in the whole context of the detention policy claims, the Court should extend a Bivens-type remedy to those claims.

\n

A

\n

Before allowing respondents\xe2\x80\x99 detention policy claims to proceed under Bivens, the Court of Appeals did not perform any special factors analysis at all. 789 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 237. The reason, it said, was that the special factors analysis is necessary only if a plaintiff asks for a Bivens remedy in a new context. 789 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 234. And in the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 view, the context here was not new. Id., at 235.

\n

To determine whether the Bivens context was novel, the Court of Appeals employed a two-part test. First, it asked whether the asserted constitutional right was at issue in a previous Bivens case. 789 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 234. Second, it asked whether the mechanism of injury was the same mechanism of injury in a previous Bivens case. 789 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 234. Under the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 approach, if the answer to both questions is \xe2\x80\x9cyes,\xe2\x80\x9d then the context is not new and no special factors analysis is required. Ibid.

\n

That approach is inconsistent with the analysis in Malesko. Before the Court decided that case, it had approved a Bivens action under the\n \nEighth Amendment against federal prison officials for failure to provide medical treatment. See Carlson, 446 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 16, n. 1, 18\xe2\x80\x9319. In Malesko, the plaintiff sought relief against a private prison operator in almost parallel circumstances. 534 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 64. In both cases, the right at issue was the same: the\n \nEighth Amendment right to be free from cruel and unusual punishment. And in both cases, the mechanism of injury was the same: failure to provide adequate medical treatment. Thus, if the approach followed by the Court of Appeals is the correct one, this Court should have held that the cases arose in the same context, obviating any need for a special factors inquiry.

\n

That, however, was not the controlling analytic framework in Malesko. Even though the right and the mechanism of injury were the same as they were in Carlson, the Court held that the contexts were different. 534 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 70, and n.\xc2\xa04. The Court explained that special factors counseled hesitation and that the Bivens remedy was therefore unavailable. 534 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 74.

\n

For similar reasons, the holding of the Court of Appeals in the instant suit is inconsistent with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s ana-lytic framework in Chappell. In Davis, decided before the Court\xe2\x80\x99s cautionary instructions with respect to Bivens suits, see supra, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312, the Court had held that an employment-discrimination claim against a Congressman could proceed as a Bivens-type action. Davis, 442 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 230\xe2\x80\x93231. In Chappell, however, the cautionary rules were applicable; and, as a result, a similar discrimination suit against military officers was not allowed to proceed. It is the Chappell framework that now controls; and, under it, the Court of Appeals erred by holding that this suit did not present a new Bivens context.

\n

The proper test for determining whether a case presents a new Bivens context is as follows. If the case is different in a meaningful way from previous Bivens cases decided by this Court, then the context is new. Without endeavoring to create an exhaustive list of differences that are meaningful enough to make a given context a new one, some examples might prove instructive. A case might differ in a meaningful way because of the rank of the officers involved; the constitutional right at issue; the generality or specificity of the official action; the extent of judicial guidance as to how an officer should respond to the problem or emergency to be confronted; the statutory or other legal mandate under which the officer was operating; the risk of disruptive intrusion by the Judiciary into the functioning of other branches; or the presence of potential special factors that previous Bivens cases did not consider.

\n

In the present suit, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 detention policy claims challenge the confinement conditions imposed on illegal aliens pursuant to a high-level executive policy created in the wake of a major terrorist attack on American soil. Those claims bear little resemblance to the three Bivens claims the Court has approved in the past: a claim against FBI agents for handcuffing a man in his own home without a warrant; a claim against a Congressman for firing his female secretary; and a claim against prison officials for failure to treat an inmate\xe2\x80\x99s asthma. See Bivens,\n\n403 U.\xc2\xa0S. 388\n; Davis,\n\n442 U.\xc2\xa0S. 228\n; Chappell,\n\n462 U.\xc2\xa0S. 296\n. The Court of Appeals therefore should have held that this was a new Bivens context. Had it done so, it would have recognized that a special factors analysis was required before allowing this damages suit to proceed.

\n

B

\n

After considering the special factors necessarily implicated by the detention policy claims, the Court now holds that those factors show that whether a damages action should be allowed is a decision for the Congress to make, not the courts.

\n

With respect to the claims against the Executive Officials, it must be noted that a Bivens action is not \xe2\x80\x9ca proper vehicle for altering an entity\xe2\x80\x99s policy.\xe2\x80\x9d Malesko, supra, at 74. Furthermore, a Bivens claim is brought against the individual official for his or her own acts, not the acts of others. \xe2\x80\x9cThe purpose of Bivens is to deter the officer.\xe2\x80\x9d Meyer, 510 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 485. Bivens is not designed to hold officers responsible for acts of their subordinates. See Iqbal, 556 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 676 (\xe2\x80\x9cGovernment officials may not be held liable for the unconstitutional conduct of their subordinates under a theory of respondeat superior\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Even if the action is confined to the conduct of a particular Executive Officer in a discrete instance, these claims would call into question the formulation and implementation of a general policy. This, in turn, would necessarily require inquiry and discovery into the whole course of the discussions and deliberations that led to the policies and governmental acts being challenged. These consequences counsel against allowing a Bivens action against the Executive Officials, for the burden and demand of litigation might well prevent them\xe2\x80\x94or, to be more precise, future officials like them\xe2\x80\x94from devoting the time and effort required for the proper discharge of their duties. See Cheney v. United States Dist. Court for D. C.,\n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 367,\n382 (2004)\n (noting \xe2\x80\x9cthe paramount necessity of protecting the Executive Branch from vexatious litigation that might distract it from the energetic performance of its constitutional duties\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

A closely related problem, as just noted, is that the discovery and litigation process would either border upon or directly implicate the discussion and deliberations that led to the formation of the policy in question. See Federal Open Market Comm. v. Merrill,\n\n443 U.\xc2\xa0S. 340,\n360 (1979)\n (noting that disclosure of Executive Branch documents \xe2\x80\x9ccould inhibit the free flow of advice, including analysis, reports, and expression of opinion within an agency\xe2\x80\x9d). Allowing a damages suit in this context, or in a like context in other circumstances, would require courts to interfere in an intrusive way with sensitive functions of the Executive Branch. See Clinton v. Jones,\n\n520 U.\xc2\xa0S. 681,\n701 (1997)\n (recognizing that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[e]ven when a branch does not arrogate power to itself .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the separation-of-powers doctrine requires that a branch not impair another in the performance of its constitutional duties\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Loving v. United States,\n\n517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 748,\n757 (1996)\n)). These considerations also counsel against allowing a damages claim to proceed against the Executive Officials. See Cheney, supra, at 385 (noting that \xe2\x80\x9cspecial considerations control\xe2\x80\x9d when a case implicates \xe2\x80\x9cthe Executive Branch\xe2\x80\x99s interests in maintaining the autonomy of its office and safeguarding the confidentiality of its communications\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

In addition to this special factor, which applies to the claims against the Executive Officials, there are three other special factors that apply as well to the detention policy claims against all of the petitioners. First, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 detention policy claims challenge more than standard \xe2\x80\x9claw enforcement operations.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Verdugo-Urquidez,\n\n494 U.\xc2\xa0S. 259,\n273 (1990)\n. They challenge as well major elements of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s whole response to the September 11 attacks, thus of necessity requiring an inquiry into sensitive issues of national security. Were this inquiry to be allowed in a private suit for damages, the Bivens action would assume dimensions far greater than those present in Bivens itself, or in either of its two follow-on cases, or indeed in any putative Bivens case yet to come before the Court.

\n

National-security policy is the prerogative of the Congress and President. See U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa78; Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa71, \xc2\xa72. Judicial inquiry into the national-security realm raises \xe2\x80\x9cconcerns for the separation of powers in trenching on matters committed to the other branches.\xe2\x80\x9d Christopher v. Harbury,\n\n536 U.\xc2\xa0S. 403,\n417 (2002)\n. These concerns are even more pronounced when the judicial inquiry comes in the context of a claim seeking money damages rather than a claim seeking injunctive or other equitable relief. The risk of personal damages liability is more likely to cause an official to second-guess difficult but necessary decisions concerning national-security policy.

\n

For these and other reasons, courts have shown deference to what the Executive Branch \xe2\x80\x9chas determined .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is \xe2\x80\x98essential to national security.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Winter v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc.,\n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 7,\n24,\n26 (2008)\n. Indeed, \xe2\x80\x9ccourts traditionally have been reluctant to intrude upon the authority of the Executive in military and national security affairs\xe2\x80\x9d unless \xe2\x80\x9cCongress specifically has provided otherwise.\xe2\x80\x9d Department of Navy v. Egan,\n\n484 U.\xc2\xa0S. 518,\n530 (1988)\n. Congress has not provided otherwise here.

\n

There are limitations, of course, on the power of the Executive under Article II of the Constitution and in the powers authorized by congressional enactments, even with respect to matters of national security. See, e.g., Hamdi v. Rumsfeld,\n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 507\n\xe2\x80\x93537 (2004) (plurality opinion) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhatever power the United States Constitution envisions for the Executive . . . in times of conflict, it most assuredly envisions a role for all three branches when individual liberties are at stake\xe2\x80\x9d); Boumediene v. Bush,\n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 723,\n798 (2008)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cLiberty and security can be reconciled; and in our system they are reconciled within the framework of the law\xe2\x80\x9d). And national-security concerns must not become a talisman used to ward off inconvenient claims\xe2\x80\x94a \xe2\x80\x9clabel\xe2\x80\x9d used to \xe2\x80\x9ccover a multitude of sins.\xe2\x80\x9d Mitchell v. Forsyth,\n\n472 U.\xc2\xa0S. 511,\n523 (1985)\n. This \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98danger of abuse\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d is even more heightened given \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the difficulty of defining\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98security interest\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in domestic cases. Ibid. (quoting United States v. United States Dist. Court for Eastern Dist. of Mich.,\n\n407 U.\xc2\xa0S. 297\n\xe2\x80\x93314 (1972)).

\n

Even so, the question is only whether \xe2\x80\x9ccongressionally uninvited intrusion\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cinappropriate\xe2\x80\x9d action for the Judiciary to take. Stanley, 483 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 683. The factors discussed above all suggest that Congress\xe2\x80\x99 failure to provide a damages remedy might be more than mere oversight, and that congressional silence might be more than \xe2\x80\x9cinadvertent.\xe2\x80\x9d Schweiker, 487 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 423. This possibility counsels hesitation \xe2\x80\x9cin the absence of affirmative action by Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Bivens, 403 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 396.

\n

Furthermore, in any inquiry respecting the likely or probable intent of Congress, the silence of Congress is relevant; and here that silence is telling. In the almost 16 years since September 11, the Federal Government\xe2\x80\x99s responses to that terrorist attack have been well documented. Congressional interest has been \xe2\x80\x9cfrequent and intense,\xe2\x80\x9d Schweiker, supra, at 425, and some of that interest has been directed to the conditions of confinement at issue here. Indeed, at Congress\xe2\x80\x99 behest, the Department of Justice\xe2\x80\x99s Office of the Inspector General compiled a 300-page report documenting the conditions in the MDC in great detail. See 789 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 279 (opinion of Raggi, J.) (noting that the USA PATRIOT Act required \xe2\x80\x9cthe Department\xe2\x80\x99s Inspector General to review and report semi-annually to Congress on any identified abuses of civil rights and civil liberties in fighting terrorism\xe2\x80\x9d). Nevertheless, \xe2\x80\x9c[a]t no point did Congress choose to extend to any person the kind of remedies that respondents seek in this lawsuit.\xe2\x80\x9d Schweiker, 487 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 426.

\n

This silence is notable because it is likely that high-level policies will attract the attention of Congress. Thus, when Congress fails to provide a damages remedy in circumstances like these, it is much more difficult to believe that \xe2\x80\x9ccongressional inaction\xe2\x80\x9d was \xe2\x80\x9cinadvertent.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 423.

\n

It is of central importance, too, that this is not a case like Bivens or Davis in which \xe2\x80\x9cit is damages or nothing.\xe2\x80\x9d Bivens, supra, at 410 (Harlan, J., concurring in judgment); Davis, 442 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 245. Unlike the plaintiffs in those cases, respondents do not challenge individual instances of discrimination or law enforcement overreach, which due to their very nature are difficult to address except by way of damages actions after the fact. Respondents instead challenge large-scale policy decisions concerning the conditions of confinement imposed on hundreds of prisoners. To address those kinds of decisions, detainees may seek injunctive relief. And in addition to that, we have left open the question whether they might be able to challenge their confinement conditions via a petition for a writ of habeas corpus. See Bell v. Wolfish,\n\n441 U.\xc2\xa0S. 520,\n526, n.\n6 (1979)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e leave to another day the question of the propriety of using a writ of habeas corpus to obtain review of the conditions of confinement\xe2\x80\x9d); Preiser v. Rodriguez,\n\n411 U.\xc2\xa0S. 475,\n499 (1973)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cWhen a prisoner is put under additional and unconstitutional restraints during his lawful custody, it is arguable that habeas corpus will lie to remove the restraints making custody illegal\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Indeed, the habeas remedy, if necessity required its use, would have provided a faster and more direct route to relief than a suit for money damages. A successful habeas petition would have required officials to place respondents in less-restrictive conditions immediately; yet this dam-ages suit remains unresolved some 15 years later. (As in Bell and Preiser, the Court need not determine the scope or availability of the habeas corpus remedy, a question that is not before the Court and has not been briefed or argued.) In sum, respondents had available to them \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98other alternative forms of judicial relief.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Minneci, 565 U.\xc2\xa0S.,at 124. And when alternative methods of relief are available, a Bivens remedy usually is not. See Bush, 462 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 386\xe2\x80\x93388; Schweiker, supra, at 425\xe2\x80\x93426; Malesko, 534 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 73\xe2\x80\x9374; Minneci, supra, at 125\xe2\x80\x93126.

\n

There is a persisting concern, of course, that absent a Bivens remedy there will be insufficient deterrence to prevent officers from violating the Constitution. In circumstances like those presented here, however, the stakes on both sides of the argument are far higher than in past cases the Court has considered. If Bivens liability were to be imposed, high officers who face personal liability for damages might refrain from taking urgent and lawful action in a time of crisis. And, as already noted, the costs and difficulties of later litigation might intrude upon and interfere with the proper exercise of their office.

\n

On the other side of the balance, the very fact that some executive actions have the sweeping potential to affect the liberty of so many is a reason to consider proper means to impose restraint and to provide some redress from injury. There is therefore a balance to be struck, in situations like this one, between deterring constitutional violations and freeing high officials to make the lawful decisions necessary to protect the Nation in times of great peril. Cf. Stanley, supra, at 681 (noting that the special-factors analysis in that case turned on \xe2\x80\x9chow much occasional, unintended impairment of military discipline one is willing to tolerate\xe2\x80\x9d). The proper balance is one for the Congress, not the Judiciary, to undertake. For all of these reasons, the Court of Appeals erred by allowing respondents\xe2\x80\x99 detention policy claims to proceed under Bivens.

\n

IV

\n

A

\n

One of respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims under Bivens requires a different analysis: the prisoner abuse claim against the MDC\xe2\x80\x99s warden, Dennis Hasty. The allegation is that Warden Hasty violated the\n \nFifth Amendment by allowing prison guards to abuse respondents.

\n

The warden argues, as an initial matter, that the complaint does not \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98state a claim to relief that is plausible on its face.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Iqbal, 556 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 678 (quoting Bell Atlantic Corp. v. Twombly,\n\n550 U.\xc2\xa0S. 544,\n570 (2007)\n). Applying its precedents, the Court of Appeals held that the substantive standard for the sufficiency of the claim is whether the warden showed \xe2\x80\x9cdeliberate indifference\xe2\x80\x9d to prisoner abuse. 789 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 249\xe2\x80\x93250. The parties appear to agree on this standard, and, for purposes of this case, the Court assumes it to be correct.

\n

The complaint alleges that guards routinely abused respondents; that the warden encouraged the abuse by referring to respondents as \xe2\x80\x9cterrorists\xe2\x80\x9d; that he prevented respondents from using normal grievance procedures; that he stayed away from the Unit to avoid seeing the abuse; that he was made aware of the abuse via \xe2\x80\x9cinmate complaints, staff complaints, hunger strikes, and suicide attempts\xe2\x80\x9d; that he ignored other \xe2\x80\x9cdirect evidence of [the] abuse, including logs and other official [records]\xe2\x80\x9d; that he took no action \xe2\x80\x9cto rectify or address the situation\xe2\x80\x9d; and that the abuse resulted in the injuries described above, see supra, at 4. These allegations\xe2\x80\x94assumed here to be true, subject to proof at a later stage\xe2\x80\x94plausibly show the warden\xe2\x80\x99s deliberate indifference to the abuse. Consistent with the opinion of every judge in this case to have considered the question, including the dissenters in the Court of Appeals, the Court concludes that the prisoner abuse allegations against Warden Hasty state a plausible ground to find a constitutional violation if a Bivens remedy is to be implied.

\n

Warden Hasty argues, however, that Bivens ought not to be extended to this instance of alleged prisoner abuse. As noted above, the first question a court must ask in a case like this one is whether the claim arises in a new Bivens context, i.e., whether \xe2\x80\x9cthe case is different in a meaningful way from previous Bivens cases decided by this Court.\xe2\x80\x9d Supra, at 16.

\n

It is true that this case has significant parallels to one of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s previous Bivens cases, Carlson v. Green,\n\n446 U.\xc2\xa0S. 14\n. There, the Court did allow a Bivens claim for prisoner mistreatment\xe2\x80\x94specifically, for failure to provide medical care. And the allegations of injury here are just as compelling as those at issue in Carlson. This is especially true given that the complaint alleges serious violations of Bureau of Prisons policy. See 28 CFR \xc2\xa7552.20 (2016) (providing that prison staff may use force \xe2\x80\x9conly as a last alternative after all other reasonable efforts to resolve a situation have failed\xe2\x80\x9d and that staff may \xe2\x80\x9cuse only that amount of force necessary to [ensure prison safety and security]\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa7552.22(j) (\xe2\x80\x9cAll incidents involving the use of force .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. must be carefully documented\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa7542.11 (requiring the warden to investigate certain complaints of inmate abuse).

\n

Yet even a modest extension is still an extension. And this case does seek to extend Carlson to a new context. As noted above, a case can present a new context for Bivens purposes if it implicates a different constitutional right; if judicial precedents provide a less meaningful guide for official conduct; or if there are potential special factors that were not considered in previous Bivens cases. See supra, at 13.

\n

The constitutional right is different here, since Carlson was predicated on the\n \nEighth Amendment and this claim is predicated on the Fifth. See 446 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 16. And the judicial guidance available to this warden, with respect to his supervisory duties, was less developed. The Court has long made clear the standard for claims alleging failure to provide medical treatment to a prisoner\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cdeliberate indifference to serious medical needs.\xe2\x80\x9d Estelle v. Gamble,\n\n429 U.\xc2\xa0S. 97,\n104 (1976)\n. The standard for a claim alleging that a warden allowed guards to abuse pre-trial detainees is less clear under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents.

\n

This case also has certain features that were not considered in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s previous Bivens cases and that might discourage a court from authorizing a Bivens remedy. As noted above, the existence of alternative remedies usually precludes a court from authorizing a Bivens action. Supra, at 14. And there might have been alternative remedies available here, for example, a writ of habeas corpus, Wolfish, 441 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 526, n.\xc2\xa06; an injunction requiring the warden to bring his prison into compliance with the regulations discussed above; or some other form of equitable relief.

\n

Furthermore, legislative action suggesting that Congress does not want a damages remedy is itself a factor counseling hesitation. See supra, at 14. Some 15 years after Carlson was decided, Congress passed the Prison Litigation Reform Act of 1995, which made comprehensive changes to the way prisoner abuse claims must be brought in federal court. See\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71997e. So it seems clear that Congress had specific occasion to consider the matter of prisoner abuse and to consider the proper way to rem-edy those wrongs. This Court has said in dicta that the Act\xe2\x80\x99s exhaustion provisions would apply to Bivens suits. See Porter v. Nussle,\n\n534 U.\xc2\xa0S. 516,\n524 (2002)\n. But the Act itself does not provide for a standalone damages remedy against federal jailers. It could be argued that this suggests Congress chose not to extend the Carlson dam-ages remedy to cases involving other types of prisoner mistreatment.

\n

The differences between this claim and the one in Carlson are perhaps small, at least in practical terms. Given this Court\xe2\x80\x99s expressed caution about extending the Bivens remedy, however, the new-context inquiry is easily satisfied. Some differences, of course, will be so trivial that they will not suffice to create a new Bivens context. But here the differences identified above are at the very least meaningful ones. Thus, before allowing this claim to proceed under Bivens, the Court of Appeals should have performed a special factors analysis. It should have analyzed whether there were alternative remedies available or other \xe2\x80\x9csound reasons to think Congress might doubt the efficacy or necessity of a damages remedy\xe2\x80\x9d in a suit like this one. Supra, at 15.

\n

B

\n

Although the Court could perform that analysis in the first instance, the briefs have concentrated almost all of their efforts elsewhere. Given the absence of a comprehensive presentation by the parties, and the fact that the Court of Appeals did not conduct the analysis, the Court declines to perform the special factors analysis itself. The better course is to vacate the judgment below, allowing the Court of Appeals or the District Court to do so on remand.

\n

V

\n

One issue remains to be addressed: the claim that petitioners are subject to liability for civil conspiracy under\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71985(3). Unlike the prisoner abuse claim just discussed, this claim implicates the activities of all the petitioners\xe2\x80\x94the Executive Officials as well as the Wardens\xe2\x80\x94in creating the conditions of confinement at issue here.

\n

The civil-conspiracy prohibition contained in \xc2\xa71985(3) was enacted as a significant part of the civil rights legislation passed in the aftermath of the Civil War. See Carpenters v. Scott,\n\n463 U.\xc2\xa0S. 825\n\xe2\x80\x93837 (1983) (detailing the legislative history of \xc2\xa71985(3)); Griffin v. Breckenridge,\n\n403 U.\xc2\xa0S. 88\n\xe2\x80\x93101 (1971) (same); Great American Fed. Sav. & Loan Assn. v. Novotny,\n\n442 U.\xc2\xa0S. 366,\n379 (1979)\n (Powell, J., concurring) (describing \xc2\xa71985(3) as a \xe2\x80\x9cCivil War Era remedial statute\xe2\x80\x9d). The statute imposes liability on two or more persons who \xe2\x80\x9cconspire .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. for the purpose of depriving .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any person or class of persons of the equal protection of the laws.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71985(3). In the instant suit, respondents allege that petitioners violated the statute by \xe2\x80\x9cagreeing to implement a policy\xe2\x80\x9d under which respondents would be detained in harsh conditions \xe2\x80\x9cbecause of their race, religion, ethnicity, and national origin.\xe2\x80\x9d Assuming these allegations to be true and well pleaded, the question is whether petitioners are entitled to qualified immunity.

\n

A

\n

The qualified immunity rule seeks a proper balance between two competing interests. On one hand, damages suits \xe2\x80\x9cmay offer the only realistic avenue for vindication of constitutional guarantees.\xe2\x80\x9d Harlow v. Fitzgerald,\n\n457 U.\xc2\xa0S. 800,\n814 (1982)\n. \xe2\x80\x9cOn the other hand, permitting damages suits against government officials can entail substantial social costs, including the risk that fear of personal monetary liability and harassing litigation will unduly inhibit officials in the discharge of their duties.\xe2\x80\x9d Anderson v. Creighton,\n\n483 U.\xc2\xa0S. 635,\n638 (1987)\n. As one means to accommodate these two objectives, the Court has held that Government officials are entitled to qualified immunity with respect to \xe2\x80\x9cdiscretionary functions\xe2\x80\x9d performed in their official capacities. Ibid. The doctrine of qualified immunity gives officials \xe2\x80\x9cbreathing room to make reasonable but mistaken judgments about open legal questions.\xe2\x80\x9d Ashcroft v. al-Kidd,\n\n563 U.\xc2\xa0S. 731,\n743 (2011)\n.

\n

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases provide additional instruction to define and implement that immunity. Whether qualified immunity can be invoked turns on the \xe2\x80\x9cobjective legal reasonableness\xe2\x80\x9d of the official\xe2\x80\x99s acts. Harlow, supra, at 819. And reasonableness of official action, in turn, must be \xe2\x80\x9cassessed in light of the legal rules that were clearly established at the time [the action] was taken.\xe2\x80\x9d Anderson, supra, at 639 (internal quotation marks omitted); see also Mitchell, 472 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 528. This requirement\xe2\x80\x94that an official loses qualified immunity only for violating clearly established law\xe2\x80\x94protects officials accused of violating \xe2\x80\x9cextremely abstract rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Anderson, supra, at 639.

\n

The\n \nFourth Amendment provides an example of how qualified immunity functions with respect to abstract rights. By its plain terms, the Amendment forbids unreasonable searches and seizures, yet it may be difficult for an officer to know whether a search or seizure will be deemed reasonable given the precise situation encountered. See Saucier v. Katz,\n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 194,\n205 (2001)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is sometimes difficult for an officer to determine how the relevant legal doctrine, here excessive force, will apply to the factual situation the officer confronts\xe2\x80\x9d). For this reason, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he dispositive question is \xe2\x80\x98whether the violative nature of particular conduct is clearly established.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Mullenix v. Luna, 577 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2015) (per curiam) (slip op., at 5) (quoting Ashcroft, supra, at 742).

\n

It is not necessary, of course, that \xe2\x80\x9cthe very action in question has previously been held unlawful.\xe2\x80\x9d Anderson, supra, at 640. That is, an officer might lose qualified immunity even if there is no reported case \xe2\x80\x9cdirectly on point.\xe2\x80\x9d Ashcroft, supra, at 741. But \xe2\x80\x9cin the light of pre-existing law,\xe2\x80\x9d the unlawfulness of the officer\xe2\x80\x99s conduct \xe2\x80\x9cmust be apparent.\xe2\x80\x9d Anderson, supra, at 640. To subject officers to any broader liability would be to \xe2\x80\x9cdisrupt the balance that our cases strike between the interests in vindication of citizens\xe2\x80\x99 constitutional rights and in public officials\xe2\x80\x99 effective performance of their duties.\xe2\x80\x9d Davis v. Scherer,\n\n468 U.\xc2\xa0S. 183,\n195 (1984)\n. For then, both as a practical and legal matter, it would be difficult for officials \xe2\x80\x9creasonably [to] anticipate when their conduct may give rise to liability for damages.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

In light of these concerns, the Court has held that qualified immunity protects \xe2\x80\x9call but the plainly incompetent or those who knowingly violate the law.\xe2\x80\x9d Malley v. Briggs,\n\n475 U.\xc2\xa0S. 335,\n341 (1986)\n. To determine whether a given officer falls into either of those two categories, a court must ask whether it would have been clear to a reasonable officer that the alleged conduct \xe2\x80\x9cwas unlawful in the situation he confronted.\xe2\x80\x9d Saucier, supra, at 202. If so, then the defendant officer must have been either incompetent or else a knowing violator of the law, and thus not entitled to qualified immunity. If not, however\xe2\x80\x94i.e., if a reasonable officer might not have known for certain that the conduct was unlawful\xe2\x80\x94then the officer is immune from liability.

\n

B

\n

Under these principles, it must be concluded that reasonable officials in petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 positions would not have known, and could not have predicted, that \xc2\xa71985(3) prohibited their joint consultations and the resulting policies that caused the injuries alleged.

\n

At least two aspects of the complaint indicate that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 potential liability for this statutory offense would not have been known or anticipated by reasonable officials in their position. First, the conspiracy recited in the complaint is alleged to have been between or among officers in the same branch of the Government (the Executive Branch) and in the same Department (the Department of Justice). Second, the discussions were the preface to, and the outline of, a general and far-reaching policy.

\n

As to the fact that these officers were in the same Department, an analogous principle discussed in the context of antitrust law is instructive. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent indicates that there is no unlawful conspiracy when officers within a single corporate entity consult among themselves and then adopt a policy for the entity. See Copperweld Corp v. Independence Tube Corp.,\n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 752\n,769\xe2\x80\x93771 (1984). Under this principle\xe2\x80\x94sometimes called the intracorporate-conspiracy doctrine\xe2\x80\x94an agreement between or among agents of the same legal entity, when the agents act in their official capacities, is not an unlawful conspiracy. Ibid. The rule is derived from the nature of the conspiracy prohibition. Conspiracy requires an agreement\xe2\x80\x94and in particular an agreement to do an unlawful act\xe2\x80\x94between or among two or more separate persons. When two agents of the same legal entity make an agreement in the course of their official duties, how-ever, as a practical and legal matter their acts are attributed to their principal. And it then follows that there has not been an agreement between two or more separate people. See id., at 771 (analogizing to \xe2\x80\x9ca multiple team of horses drawing a vehicle under the control of a single driver\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

To be sure, this Court has not given its approval to this doctrine in the specific context of \xc2\xa71985(3). See Great American, 442 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 372, n. 11. There is a division in the courts of appeals, moreover, respecting the validity or correctness of the intracorporate-conspiracy doctrine with reference to \xc2\xa71985 conspiracies. See Hull v. Shuck,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1261\n\xe2\x80\x931262 (1991) (White, J., dissenting from denial of certiorari) (discussing the Circuit split); Bowie v. Maddox, 642 F. 3d 1122, 1130\xe2\x80\x931131 (CADC 2011) (detailing a longstanding split about whether the intracorporate-conspiracy doctrine applies to civil rights conspiracies). Nothing in this opinion should be interpreted as either approving or disapproving the intracorporate-conspiracy doctrine\xe2\x80\x99s application in the context of an alleged \xc2\xa71985(3) violation. The Court might determine, in some later case, that different considerations apply to a conspiracy respecting equal protection guarantees, as distinct from a conspiracy in the antitrust context. Yet the fact that the courts are divided as to whether or not a \xc2\xa71985(3) conspiracy can arise from official discussions between or among agents of the same entity demonstrates that the law on the point is not well established. When the courts are divided on an issue so central to the cause of action alleged, a reasonable official lacks the notice required before imposing liability. See Wilson v. Layne,\n\n526 U.\xc2\xa0S. 603,\n618 (1999)\n (noting that it would be \xe2\x80\x9cunfair\xe2\x80\x9d to subject officers to damages liability when even \xe2\x80\x9cjudges .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. disagree\xe2\x80\x9d); Reichle v. Howards,\n\n566 U.\xc2\xa0S. 658\n\xe2\x80\x93670 (2012) (same).

\n

In addition to the concern that agents of the same legal entity are not distinct enough to conspire with one another, there are other sound reasons to conclude that conver-sations and agreements between and among federal officials in the same Department should not be the subject of a private cause of action for damages under \xc2\xa71985(3). To state a claim under \xc2\xa71985(3), a plaintiff must first show that the defendants conspired\xe2\x80\x94that is, reached an agreement\xe2\x80\x94with one another. See Carpenters, 463 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 828 (stating that the elements of a \xc2\xa71985(3) claim include \xe2\x80\x9ca conspiracy\xe2\x80\x9d). Thus, a \xc2\xa71985(3) claim against federal officials by necessity implicates the substance of their official discussions.

\n

As indicated above with respect to other claims in this suit, open discussion among federal officers is to be encouraged, so that they can reach consensus on the policies a department of the Federal Government should pursue. See supra, at 17\xe2\x80\x9318. Close and frequent consultations to facilitate the adoption and implementation of policies are essential to the orderly conduct of governmental affairs. Were those discussions, and the resulting policies, to be the basis for private suits seeking damages against the officials as individuals, the result would be to chill the interchange and discourse that is necessary for the adoption and implementation of governmental policies. See Cheney, 542 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 383 (discussing the need for confidential communications among Executive Branch officials); Merrill, 443 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 360 (same).

\n

These considerations suggest that officials employed by the same governmental department do not conspire when they speak to one another and work together in their official capacities. Whether that contention should prevail need not be decided here. It suffices to say that the question is sufficiently open so that the officials in this suit could not be certain that \xc2\xa71985(3) was applicable to their discussions and actions. Thus, the law respondents seek to invoke cannot be clearly established. It follows that reasonable officers in petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 positions would not have known with any certainty that the alleged agreements were forbidden by law. See Saucier, 533 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 202. Petitioners are entitled to qualified immunity with respect to the claims under\n\n42 U. S. C. \xc2\xa71985(3).

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

If the facts alleged in the complaint are true, then what happened to respondents in the days following September 11 was tragic. Nothing in this opinion should be read to condone the treatment to which they contend they were subjected. The question before the Court, however, is not whether petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 alleged conduct was proper, nor whether it gave decent respect to respondents\xe2\x80\x99 dignity and well-being, nor whether it was in keeping with the idea of the rule of law that must inspire us even in times of crisis.

\n

Instead, the question with respect to the Bivens claims is whether to allow an action for money damages in the absence of congressional authorization. For the reasons given above, the Court answers that question in the negative as to the detention policy claims. As to the prisoner abuse claim, because the briefs have not concentrated on that issue, the Court remands to allow the Court of Appeals to consider the claim in light of the Bivens analysis set forth above.

\n

The question with respect to the \xc2\xa71985(3) claim is whether a reasonable officer in petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 position would have known the alleged conduct was an unlawful conspiracy. For the reasons given above, the Court answers that question, too, in the negative.

\n

The judgment of the Court of Appeals is reversed as to all of the claims except the prisoner abuse claim against Warden Hasty. The judgment of the Court of Appeals with respect to that claim is vacated, and that case is remanded for further proceedings.

\n

It is so ordered.

I join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion except for Part IV\xe2\x80\x93B. \n

I

\n

With respect to respondents\xe2\x80\x99 Bivens claims, I join the opinion of the Court to the extent it reverses the Second Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s ruling. The Court correctly applies our precedents to hold that Bivens does not supply a cause of action against petitioners for most of the alleged Fourth and \n \nFifth Amendment violations. It also correctly recognizes that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims against petitioner Dennis Hasty seek to extend Bivens to a new context. See ante, at 24.

\n

I concur in the judgment of the Court vacating the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 judgment with regard to claims against Hasty. Ante, at 29. I have previously noted that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Bivens is a relic of the heady days in which this Court assumed common-law powers to create causes of action.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Wilkie v. Robbins,\n\n551 U.\xc2\xa0S. 537,\n568 (2007)\n (concurring opinion) (quoting Correctional Services Corp. v. Malesko,\n\n534 U.\xc2\xa0S. 61,\n75 (2001)\n (Scalia, J., concurring)). I have thus declined to \xe2\x80\x9cextend Bivens even [where] its reasoning logically applied,\xe2\x80\x9d thereby limiting \xe2\x80\x9cBivens and its progeny .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to the precise circumstances that they involved.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted). This would, in most cases, mean a reversal of the judgment of the Court of Appeals is in order. However, in order for there to be a controlling judgment in this suit, I concur in the judgment vacating and remanding the claims against petitioner Hasty as that disposition is closest to my preferred approach.

\n

II

\n

As for respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims under\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71985(3),I join Part V of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, which holds thatrespondents are entitled to qualified immunity. TheCourt correctly applies our precedents, which no party has asked us to reconsider. I write separately, however, to note my growing concern with our qualified immunity jurisprudence.

\n

The Civil Rights Act of 1871, of which \xc2\xa71985(3) and the more frequently litigated \xc2\xa71983 were originally a part, established causes of action for plaintiffs to seek money damages from Government officers who violated federal law. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71, 2,\n\n17Stat.\n13. Although the Act made no mention of defenses or immunities, \xe2\x80\x9cwe have read it in harmony with general principles of tort immunities and defenses rather than in derogation of them.\xe2\x80\x9d Malley v. Briggs,\n\n475 U.\xc2\xa0S. 335,\n339 (1986)\n (internal quotation marks omitted). We have done so because \xe2\x80\x9c[c]ertain immunities were so well established in 1871 .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that \xe2\x80\x98we presume that Congress would have specifically so provided had it wished to abolish\xe2\x80\x99 them.\xe2\x80\x9d Buckley v. Fitzsimmons,\n\n509 U.\xc2\xa0S. 259,\n268 (1993)\n; accord, Briscoe v. LaHue,\n\n460 U.\xc2\xa0S. 325,\n330 (1983)\n. Immunity is thus available under the statute if it was \xe2\x80\x9chistorically accorded the relevant official\xe2\x80\x9d in an analogous situation \xe2\x80\x9cat common law,\xe2\x80\x9d Imbler v. Pachtman,\n\n424 U.\xc2\xa0S. 409,\n421 (1976)\n, unless the statute provides some reason to think that Congress did not preserve the defense, see Tower v. Glover,\n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 914,\n920 (1984)\n.

\n

In some contexts, we have conducted the common-law inquiry that the statute requires. See Wyatt v. Cole,\n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 158,\n170 (1992)\n (Kennedy, J., concurring). For example, we have concluded that legislators and judges are absolutely immune from liability under \xc2\xa71983 for their official acts because that immunity was well established at common law in 1871. See Tenney v. Brandhove,\n\n341 U.\xc2\xa0S. 367\n\xe2\x80\x93376 (1951) (legislators); Pierson v. Ray,\n\n386 U.\xc2\xa0S. 547\n\xe2\x80\x93555 (1967) (judges). We have similarly looked to the common law in holding that a prosecutor is immune from suits relating to the \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial phase of the criminal process,\xe2\x80\x9d Imbler, supra, at 430; Burns v. Reed,\n\n500 U.\xc2\xa0S. 478\n\xe2\x80\x93492 (1991); but see Kalina v. Fletcher,\n\n522 U.\xc2\xa0S. 118\n\xe2\x80\x93134 (1997) (Scalia, J., joined by Thomas, J., concurring) (arguing that the Court in Imbler misunderstood 1871 common-law rules), although not from suits relating to the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s advice to police officers, Burns, supra, at 493.

\n

In developing immunity doctrine for other executive officers, we also started off by applying common-law rules. In Pierson, we held that police officers are not absolutely immune from a \xc2\xa71983 claim arising from an arrest made pursuant to an unconstitutional statute because the common law never granted arresting officers that sort of immunity. 386 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 555. Rather, we concluded that police officers could assert \xe2\x80\x9cthe defense of good faith and probable cause\xe2\x80\x9d against the claim for an unconstitutional arrest because that defense was available against the analogous torts of \xe2\x80\x9cfalse arrest and imprisonment\xe2\x80\x9d at common law. Id., at 557.

\n

In further elaborating the doctrine of qualified immun-ity for executive officials, however, we have diverged from the historical inquiry mandated by the statute. See Wyatt, supra, at 170 (Kennedy, J., concurring); accord, Crawford-El v. Britton,\n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 574,\n611 (1998)\n (Scalia, J.,joined by Thomas, J., dissenting). In the decisions following Pierson, we have \xe2\x80\x9ccompletely reformulated qualified immunity along principles not at all embodied in the common law.\xe2\x80\x9d Anderson v. Creighton,\n\n483 U.\xc2\xa0S. 635,\n645 (1987)\n (discussing Harlow v. Fitzgerald,\n\n457 U.\xc2\xa0S. 800 (1982)\n). Instead of asking whether the common law in 1871 would have accorded immunity to an officer for a tort analogous to the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s claim under \xc2\xa71983, we instead grant immunity to any officer whose conduct \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not violate clearly established statutory or constitutional rights of which a reasonable person would have known.\xe2\x80\x9d Mullenix v. Luna, 577 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2015) (\xc2\xa0per cu-riam) (slip op., at 4\xe2\x80\x935) (internal quotation marks omitted); Taylor v. Barkes, 575 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2015) (slip op., at 4) (a Government official is liable under the 1871 Act only if \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98existing precedent .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. placed the statutory or constitutional question beyond debate\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Ashcroft v. al-Kidd,\n\n563 U.\xc2\xa0S. 731,\n741 (2011)\n)). We apply this \xe2\x80\x9cclearly established\xe2\x80\x9d standard \xe2\x80\x9cacross the board\xe2\x80\x9d and without regard to \xe2\x80\x9cthe precise nature of the various officials\xe2\x80\x99 duties or the precise character of the particular rights alleged to have been violated.\xe2\x80\x9d Anderson, supra, at 641\xe2\x80\x93643 (internal quotation marks omitted).[1] We have not attempted to locate that standard in the common law as it existed in 1871, however, and some evidence supports the conclusion that common-law immunity as it existed in 1871 looked quite different from our current doctrine. See generally Baude, Is Qualified Immunity Unlawful? 106 Cal. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. (forthcoming 2018) (manuscript, at 7\xe2\x80\x9317), online at https://papers.ssrn.com/abstract=2896508 (as last visited June 15, 2017).

\n

Because our analysis is no longer grounded in thecommon-law backdrop against which Congress enacted the1871 Act, we are no longer engaged in \xe2\x80\x9cinterpret[ing] the intent of Congress in enacting\xe2\x80\x9d the Act. Malley, supra, at 342; see Burns, supra, at 493. Our qualified immunity precedents instead represent precisely the sort of \xe2\x80\x9cfreewheeling policy choice[s]\xe2\x80\x9d that we have previously disclaimed the power to make. Rehberg v. Paulk,\n\n566 U.\xc2\xa0S. 356,\n363 (2012)\n (internal quotation marks omitted); see also Tower, supra, at 922\xe2\x80\x93923 (\xe2\x80\x9cWe do not have a license to establish immunities from\xe2\x80\x9d suits brought under the Act \xe2\x80\x9cin the interests of what we judge to be sound public pol-icy\xe2\x80\x9d). We have acknowledged, in fact, that the \xe2\x80\x9cclearly established\xe2\x80\x9d standard is designed to \xe2\x80\x9cprotec[t] the balance between vindication of constitutional rights and government officials\xe2\x80\x99 effective performance of their duties.\xe2\x80\x9d Reichle v. Howards,\n\n566 U.\xc2\xa0S. 658,\n664 (2012)\n (internal quotation marks omitted); Harlow, supra, at 807 (explaining that \xe2\x80\x9cthe recognition of a qualified immunity defense .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. reflected an attempt to balance competing values\xe2\x80\x9d). The Constitution assigns this kind of balancing to Congress, not the Courts.

\n

In today\xe2\x80\x99s decision, we continue down the path our precedents have marked. We ask \xe2\x80\x9cwhether it would have been clear to a reasonable officer that the alleged conduct was unlawful in the situation he confronted,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 29 (internal quotation marks omitted), rather than whether officers in petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 positions would have been accorded immunity at common law in 1871 from claims analogous to respondents\xe2\x80\x99. Even if we ultimately reach a conclusion consistent with the common-law rules prevailing in 1871, it is mere fortuity. Until we shift the focus of our inquiry to whether immunity existed at common law, we will continue to substitute our own policy preferences for the mandates of Congress. In an appropriate case, we should reconsider our qualified immunity jurisprudence.

In Bivens v. Six Unknown Fed. Narcotics Agents,\n\n403 U.\xc2\xa0S. 388\n (1971), this Court held that the\n \nFourth Amendment provides a damages remedy for those whom federal officials have injured as a result of an unconstitutional search or seizure. In Davis v. Passman,\n\n442 U.\xc2\xa0S. 228 (1979)\n, the Court held that the\n \nFifth Amendment provides a damages remedy to an individual dismissed by her employer (a Member of Congress) on the basis of her sex in violation of the equal protection component of that Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Due Process Clause. And in Carlson v. Green,\n\n446 U.\xc2\xa0S. 14 (1980)\n, the Court held that the\n \nEighth Amendment provides a damages remedy to a prisoner who died as a result of prison official\xe2\x80\x99s deliberate indifference to his medical needs, in violation of the Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition against cruel and unusual punishment.

\n

It is by now well established that federal law provides damages actions at least in similar contexts, where claims of constitutional violation arise. Congress has ratified Bivens actions, plaintiffs frequently bring them, courts accept them, and scholars defend their importance. See J. Pfander, Constitutional Torts and the War on Terror (2017) (canvassing the history of Bivens and cataloguing cases). Moreover, the courts, in order to avoid deterring federal officials from properly performing their work, have developed safeguards for defendants, including the requirement that plaintiffs plead \xe2\x80\x9cplausible\xe2\x80\x9d claims, Ashcroft v. Iqbal,\n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 662,\n679 (2009)\n, as well as the defense of \xe2\x80\x9cqualified immunity,\xe2\x80\x9d which frees federal officials from both threat of liability and involvement in the lawsuit, unless the plaintiffs establish that officials have violated \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98clearly established .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. constitutional rights,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 672 (quoting Harlow v. Fitzgerald,\n\n457 U.\xc2\xa0S. 800,\n818 (1982)\n). \xe2\x80\x9c[This] Court has been reluctant to extend Bivens liability \xe2\x80\x98to any new context or new category of defendants.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Iqbal, supra, at 675 (quoting Correctional Services Corp. v. Malesko,\n\n534 U.\xc2\xa0S. 61,\n68 (2001)\n). But the Court has made clear that it would not narrow Bivens\xe2\x80\x99 existing scope. See FDIC v. Meyer,\n\n510 U.\xc2\xa0S. 471,\n485 (1994)\n (guarding against \xe2\x80\x9cthe evisceration of the Bivens remedy\xe2\x80\x9d so that its \xe2\x80\x9cdeterrent effects .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. would [not] be lost\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

The plaintiffs before us today seek damages for unconstitutional conditions of confinement. They alleged that federal officials slammed them against walls, shackled them, exposed them to nonstop lighting, lack of hygiene, and the like, all based upon invidious discrimination and without penological justification. See ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x935. In my view, these claims are well-pleaded, state violations of clearly established law, and fall within the scope of longstanding Bivens law. For those reasons, I would affirm the judgment of the Court of Appeals. I shall discuss at some length what I believe is the most important point of disagreement. The Court, in my view, is wrong to hold that permitting a constitutional tort action here would \xe2\x80\x9cextend\xe2\x80\x9d Bivens, applying it in a new context. To the contrary, I fear that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding would significantly shrink the existing Bivens contexts, diminishing the compensatory remedy constitutional tort law now offers to harmed individuals.

\n

I shall explain why I believe this suit falls well within the scope of traditional constitutional tort law and why I cannot agree with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s arguments to the contrary. I recognize, and write separately about, the strongest of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s arguments, namely, the fact that plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 claims concern detention that took place soon after a serious attack on the United States and some of them concern actions of high-level Government officials. While these facts may affect the substantive constitutional questions (e.g., were any of the conditions \xe2\x80\x9clegitimate\xe2\x80\x9d?) or the scope of the qualified-immunity defense, they do not extinguish the Bivens action itself. If I may paraphrase Justice Harlan, concurring in Bivens: In wartime as well as in peacetime, \xe2\x80\x9cit is important, in a civilized society, that the judicial branch of the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s government stand ready to afford a remedy\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cfor the most flagrant and pat-ently unjustified,\xe2\x80\x9d unconstitutional \xe2\x80\x9cabuses of official power.\xe2\x80\x9d 403 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 410\xe2\x80\x93411 (opinion concurring in judgment); cf. Boumediene v. Bush,\n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 723,\n798 (2008)\n.

\n

I

\n

The majority opinion well summarizes the particular claims that the plaintiffs make in this suit. All concern the conditions of their confinement, which began soon after the September 11, 2001, attacks and \xe2\x80\x9clasted for days and weeks, then stretching into months.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 1. At some point, the plaintiffs allege, all the defendants knew that they had nothing to do with the September 11 attacks but continued to detain them anyway in harsh conditions. Official Government policy, both before and after the defendants became aware of the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 innocence, led to the plaintiffs being held in \xe2\x80\x9ctiny cells for over 23 hours a day\xe2\x80\x9d with lights continuously left on, \xe2\x80\x9cshackled\xe2\x80\x9d when moved, often \xe2\x80\x9cstrip searched,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cdenied access to most forms of communication with the outside world.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 4 (internal quotation marks omitted). The defendants detained the plaintiffs in these conditions on the basis of their race or religion and without justification.

\n

Moreover, the prison wardens were aware of, but deliberately indifferent to, certain unofficial activities of prison guards involving a pattern of \xe2\x80\x9cphysical and verbal abuse,\xe2\x80\x9d such as \xe2\x80\x9cslam[ming] detainees into walls; twist[ing] their arms, wrists, and fingers; [breaking] their bones;\xe2\x80\x9d and subjecting them to verbal taunts. Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

The plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 complaint alleges that all the defendants\xe2\x80\x94high-level Department of Justice officials and prison wardens alike\xe2\x80\x94were directly responsible for the official confinement policy, which, in some or all of the aspects mentioned, violated the due process and equal protection components of the\n \nFifth Amendment. The complaint adds that, insofar as the prison wardens were deliberately indifferent to the unofficial conduct of the guards, they violated the Fourth and the\n \nFifth Amendments.

\n

I would hold that the complaint properly alleges constitutional torts, i.e., Bivens actions for damages.

\n

A

\n

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s holdings in Bivens, Carlson, and Davis rest upon four basic legal considerations. First, the Bivens Court referred to longstanding Supreme Court precedent stating or suggesting that the Constitution provides fed-eral courts with considerable legal authority to use traditional remedies to right constitutional wrongs. That precedent begins with Marbury v. Madison, 1 Cranch 137 (1803), which effectively placed upon those who would deny the existence of an effective legal remedy the burden of showing why their case was special. Chief Justice John Marshall wrote for the Court that

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[t]he very essence of civil liberty [lies] in the right of every individual to claim the protection of the laws, whenever he receives an injury.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 163.

\n

The Chief Justice referred to Blackstone\xe2\x80\x99s Commentaries stating that there

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98is a general and indisputable rule, that where there is a legal right, there is also a legal remedy .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [and that] it is a settled and invariable principle in the laws of England, that every right, when withheld, must have a remedy, and every injury its proper redress.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 1 Cranch, at 163.

\n

The Chief Justice then wrote:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe government of the United States has been emphatically termed a government of laws, and not of men. It will [not] deserve this high appellation, if the laws furnish no remedy for the violation of a vested legal right.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

He concluded for the Court that there must be something \xe2\x80\x9cpeculiar\xe2\x80\x9d (i.e., special) about a case that warrants \xe2\x80\x9cexclu[ding] the injured party from legal redress .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [and placing it within] that class of cases which come under the description of damnum absque injuria\xe2\x80\x94a loss without an injury.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 163\xe2\x80\x93164; but cf. id., at 164 (placing \xe2\x80\x9cpolitical\xe2\x80\x9d questions in the latter, special category).

\n

Much later, in Bell v. Hood,\n\n327 U.\xc2\xa0S. 678,\n684 (1946)\n, the Court wrote that,

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cwhere federally protected rights have been invaded, it has been the rule from the beginning that courts will be alert to adjust their remedies so as to grant the necessary relief.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

See also Bivens, 403 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 392 (citing opinions of Justices Cardozo and Holmes to similar effect).

\n

The Bivens Court reiterated these principles and confirmed that the appropriate remedial \xe2\x80\x9cadjust[ment]\xe2\x80\x9d in the case before it was an award of money damages, the \xe2\x80\x9cremedial mechanism normally available in the federal courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 392, 397. Justice Harlan agreed, adding that, since Congress\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral\xe2\x80\x9d statutory \xe2\x80\x9cgrant of jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d authorized courts to grant equitable relief in cases arising under federal jurisdiction, courts likewise had the author-ity to award damages\xe2\x80\x94the \xe2\x80\x9ctraditional remedy at law\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94in order to \xe2\x80\x9cvindicate the interests of the individual\xe2\x80\x9d protected by the Bill of Rights. Id., at 405\xe2\x80\x93407 (opinion concur-ring in judgment).

\n

Second, our cases have recognized that Congress\xe2\x80\x99 silence on the subject indicates a willingness to leave this matter to the courts. In Bivens, the Court noted, as an argument favoring its conclusion, the absence of an \xe2\x80\x9cexplicit congressional declaration that persons injured by a federal officer\xe2\x80\x99s violation of the\n \nFourth Amendment may not recover money damages from the agents.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 397. Similarly, in Davis v. Passman, the Court stressed that there was \xe2\x80\x9cno evidence .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that Congress meant .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to foreclose\xe2\x80\x9d a damages remedy. 442 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 247. In Carlson, the Court went further, observing that not only was there no sign \xe2\x80\x9cthat Congress meant to pre-empt a Bivens remedy,\xe2\x80\x9d but there was also \xe2\x80\x9cclear\xe2\x80\x9d evidence that Congress intended to preserve it. 446 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 19\xe2\x80\x9320.

\n

Third, our Bivens cases acknowledge that a constitutional tort may not lie when \xe2\x80\x9cspecial factors counse[l] hesitation\xe2\x80\x9d and when Congress has provided an adequate alternative remedy. 446 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 18\xe2\x80\x9319. The relevant special factors in those cases included whether the court was faced \xe2\x80\x9cwith a question of \xe2\x80\x98federal fiscal policy,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Bivens, supra, at 396, or a risk of \xe2\x80\x9cdeluging federal courts with claims,\xe2\x80\x9d Davis, supra, at 248 (internal quotation marks omitted). Carlson acknowledged an additional factor\xe2\x80\x94that damages suits \xe2\x80\x9cmight inhibit [federal officials\xe2\x80\x99] efforts to perform their official duties\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94but concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthe qualified immunity accorded [federal officials] under [existing law] provides adequate protection.\xe2\x80\x9d 446 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 19.

\n

Fourth, as the Court recognized later in Carlson, a Bivens remedy was needed to cure what would, without it, amount to a constitutional anomaly. Long before this Court incorporated many of the Bill of Rights\xe2\x80\x99 guarantees against the States, see Amar, The Bill of Rights and the\n \nFourteenth Amendment, 101 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J. 1193 (1992), federal civil rights statutes afforded a damages remedy to any person whom a state official deprived of a federal constitutional right, see\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983; Monroe v. Pape,\n\n365 U.\xc2\xa0S. 167\n\xe2\x80\x93187 (1961) (describing this history). But federal statutory law did not provide a damages remedy to a person whom a federal official had deprived of that same right, even though the Bill of Rights was at the time of the founding primarily aimed at constraining the Federal Government. Thus, a person harmed by an unconstitutional search or seizure might sue a city mayor, a state legislator, or even a Governor. But that person could not sue a federal agent, a national legislator, or a Justice Department official for an identical offense. \xe2\x80\x9c[Our] \xe2\x80\x98constitutional design,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the Court wrote, \xe2\x80\x9cwould be stood on its head if federal officials did not face at least the same liability as state officials guilty of the same constitutional transgression.\xe2\x80\x9d Carlson, supra, at 22 (quoting Butz v. Economou,\n\n438 U.\xc2\xa0S. 478,\n504 (1978)\n).

\n

The Bivens Court also recognized that the Court had previously inferred damages remedies caused by violations of certain federal statutes that themselves did not explic-itly authorize damages remedies. 403 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 395\xe2\x80\x93396. At the same time, Bivens, Davis, and Carlson treat the courts\xe2\x80\x99 power to derive a damages remedy from a constitutional provision not as included within a power to find a statute-based damages remedy but as flowing from those statutory cases a fortiori.

\n

As the majority opinion points out, this Court in more recent years has indicated that \xe2\x80\x9cexpanding the Bivens remedy is now a \xe2\x80\x98disfavored\xe2\x80\x99 judicial activity.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11 (quoting Iqbal, 556 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 675; emphasis added). Thus, it has held that the remedy is not available in the context of suits against military officers, see Chappell v. Wallace,\n\n462 U.\xc2\xa0S. 296\n\xe2\x80\x93300 (1983); United States v. Stanley,\n\n483 U.\xc2\xa0S. 669\n\xe2\x80\x93684 (1987); in the context of suits against privately operated prisons and their employees, see Minneci v. Pollard,\n\n565 U.\xc2\xa0S. 118,\n120 (2012)\n; Malesko, 534 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 70\xe2\x80\x9373; in the context of suits seeking to vindicate procedural, rather than substantive, constitutional protections, see Schweiker v. Chilicky,\n\n487 U.\xc2\xa0S. 412,\n423 (1988)\n; and in the context of suits seeking to vindicate two quite different forms of important substantive protection, one involving free speech, see Bush v. Lucas,\n\n462 U.\xc2\xa0S. 367,\n368 (1983)\n, and the other involving protection of land rights, see Wilkie v. Robbins,\n\n551 U.\xc2\xa0S. 537,\n551 (2007)\n. Each of these cases involved a context that differed from that of Bivens, Davis, and Carlson with respect to the kind of defendant, the basic nature of the right, or the kind of harm suffered. That is to say, as we have explicitly stated, these cases were \xe2\x80\x9cfundamentally different from anything recognized in Bivens or subsequent cases.\xe2\x80\x9d Malesko, supra, at 70 (emphasis added). In each of them, the plaintiffs were asking the Court to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98authoriz[e] a new kind of federal litigation.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Wilkie, supra, at 550 (emphasis added).

\n

Thus the Court, as the majority opinion says, repeatedly wrote that it was not \xe2\x80\x9cexpanding\xe2\x80\x9d the scope of the Bivens remedy. Ante, at 11. But the Court nowhere suggested that it would narrow Bivens\xe2\x80\x99 existing scope. In fact, to diminish any ambiguity about its holdings, the Court set out a framework for determining whether a claim of constitutional violation calls for a Bivens remedy. See Wilkie, supra, at 549\xe2\x80\x93550. At Step One, the court must determine whether the case before it arises in a \xe2\x80\x9cnew context,\xe2\x80\x9d that is, whether it involves a \xe2\x80\x9cnew category of defendants,\xe2\x80\x9d Malesko, supra, at 68, or (presumably) a significantly different kind of constitutional harm, such as a purely procedural harm, a harm to speech, or a harm caused to physical property. If the context is new, then the court proceeds to Step Two and asks \xe2\x80\x9cwhether any alternative, existing process for protecting the interest amounts to a convincing reason for the Judicial Branch to refrain from providing a new and freestanding remedy in damages.\xe2\x80\x9d Wilkie, 551 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 550. If there is none, then the court proceeds to Step Three and asks whether there are \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98any special factors counselling hesitation before authorizing a new kind of federal litigation.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

Precedent makes this framework applicable here. I would apply it. And, doing so, I cannot get past Step One. This suit, it seems to me, arises in a context similar to those in which this Court has previously permitted Bivens actions.

\n

B

\n

1

\n

The context here is not \xe2\x80\x9cnew,\xe2\x80\x9d Wilkie, supra, at 550, or \xe2\x80\x9cfundamentally different\xe2\x80\x9d than our previous Bivens cases, Malesko, supra, at 70. First, the plaintiffs are civilians, not members of the military. They are not citizens, but the Constitution protects noncitizens against serious mistreatment, as it protects citizens. See United States v. Verdugo-Urquidez,\n\n494 U.\xc2\xa0S. 259,\n271 (1990)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]liens receive constitutional protections when they have come within the territory of the United States and developed substantial connections with this country\xe2\x80\x9d). Some or all of the plaintiffs here may have been illegally present in the United States. But that fact cannot justify physical mistreatment. Nor does anyone claim that that fact deprives them of a Bivens right available to other persons, citizens and noncitizens alike.

\n

Second, the defendants are Government officials. They are not members of the military or private persons. Two are prison wardens. Three others are high-ranking Department of Justice officials. Prison wardens have been defendants in Bivens actions, as have other high-level Government officials. One of the defendants in Carlson was the Director of the Bureau of Prisons; the defendant in Davis was a Member of Congress. We have also held that the Attorney General of the United States is not entitled to absolute immunity in a damages suit arising out of his actions related to national security. See Mitchell v. Forsyth,\n\n472 U.\xc2\xa0S. 511,\n520 (1985)\n.

\n

Third, from a Bivens perspective, the injuries that the plaintiffs claim they suffered are familiar ones. They focus upon the conditions of confinement. The plaintiffs say that they were unnecessarily shackled, confined in small unhygienic cells, subjected to continuous lighting (presumably preventing sleep), unnecessarily and frequently strip searched, slammed against walls, injured physically, and subject to verbal abuse. They allege that they suffered these harms because of their race or religion, the defendants having either turned a blind eye to what was happening or themselves introduced policies that they knew would lead to these harms even though the defendants knew the plaintiffs had no connections to terrorism.

\n

These claimed harms are similar to, or even worse than, the harms the plaintiffs suffered in Bivens (unreasonable search and seizure in violation of the\n \nFourth Amendment), Davis (unlawful discrimination in violation of the\n \nFifth Amendment), and Carlson (deliberate indifference to medical need in violation of the\n \nEighth Amendment).\xc2\xa0 Indeed, we have said that, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f a federal prisoner in a [Bureau of Prisons] facility alleges a constitutional deprivation, he may bring a Bivens claim against the offending individual officer, subject to the defense of qualified immunity.\xe2\x80\x9d Malesko, 534 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 72; see also Farmer v. Brennan,\n\n511 U.\xc2\xa0S. 825,\n832 (1994)\n (Bivens case about prisoner abuse). The claims in this suit would seem to fill the Bivens\xe2\x80\x99 bill. See Sell v. United States,\n\n539 U.\xc2\xa0S. 166,\n193 (2003)\n (Scalia, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] [Bivens] action .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is available to federal pretrial detainees challenging the conditions of their confinement\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

It is true that the plaintiffs bring their \xe2\x80\x9cdeliberate indifference\xe2\x80\x9d claim against Warden Hasty under the\n \nFifth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Due Process Clause, not the\n \nEighth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Cruel and Unusual Punishment Clause, as in Carlson. But that is because the latter applies to convicted criminals while the former applies to pretrial and immigration detainees. Where the harm is the same, where this Court has held that both the Fifth and\n \nEighth Amendments give rise to Bivens\xe2\x80\x99 remedies, and where the only difference in constitutional scope consists of a circumstance (the absence of a conviction) that makes the violation here worse, it cannot be maintained that the difference between the use of the two Amendments is \xe2\x80\x9cfundamental.\xe2\x80\x9d See City of Revere v. Massachusetts Gen. Hospital,\n\n463 U.\xc2\xa0S. 239,\n244 (1983)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cdue process rights\xe2\x80\x9d of an unconvicted person \xe2\x80\x9care at least as great as the\n \nEighth Amendment protections available to a convicted pris-oner\xe2\x80\x9d); Kingsley v. Hendrickson, 576 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2015) (slip op., at 10\xe2\x80\x9311) (\xe2\x80\x9cpretrial detainees (unlike convicted prisoners) cannot be punished at all\xe2\x80\x9d); Zadvydas v. Davis,\n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 678,\n721 (2001)\n (Kennedy, J., dissenting) (detention \xe2\x80\x9cincident to removal .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. cannot be justified as punishment nor can the confinement or its conditions be designed in order to punish\xe2\x80\x9d). See also Bistrian v. Levi, 696 F.\xc2\xa03d 352, 372 (CA3 2012) (permitting Bivens action brought by detainee in administrative segregation); Thomas v. Ashcroft, 470 F.\xc2\xa03d 491, 493, 496\xe2\x80\x93497 (CA2 2006) (detainee alleging failure to provide adequate medical care); Magluta v. Samples, 375 F.\xc2\xa03d 1269, 1271, 1275\xe2\x80\x931276 (CA11 2004) (detainee in solitary confinement); Papa v. United States, 281 F.\xc2\xa03d 1004, 1010\xe2\x80\x931011 (CA9 2002) (due process claims arising from death of immigration detainee); Loe v. Armistead, 582 F.\xc2\xa02d 1291, 1293\xe2\x80\x931296 (CA4 1978) (detainee\xe2\x80\x99s claim of deliberate indifference to medical need). If an arrestee can bring a claim of excessive force (Bivens itself), and a convicted prisoner can bring a claim for denying medical care (Carlson), someone who has neither been charged nor convicted with a crime should also be able to challenge abuse that causes him to need medical care.

\n

Nor has Congress suggested that it wants to withdraw a damages remedy in circumstances like these. By its express terms, the Prison Litigation Reform Act of 1995 (PLRA) does not apply to immigration detainees. See\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71997e(h) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he term \xe2\x80\x98prisoner\xe2\x80\x99 means any person incarcerated or detained in any facility who is accused of, convicted of, sentenced for, or adjudicated delinquent for, violations of criminal law .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. \xe2\x80\x9d); see also Agyeman v. INS, 296 F.\xc2\xa03d 871, 886 (CA9 2002) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e hold that an alien detained by the INS pending deportation is not a \xe2\x80\x98prisoner\xe2\x80\x99 within the meaning of the PLRA\xe2\x80\x9d); LaFontant v. INS, 135 F.\xc2\xa03d 158, 165 (CADC 1998) (same); Ojo v. INS, 106 F.\xc2\xa03d 680, 683 (CA5 1997) (same). And, in fact, there is strong evidence that Congress assumed that Bivens remedies would be available to prisoners when it enacted the PLRA\xe2\x80\x94e.g., Congress continued to permit prisoners to recover for physical injuries, the typical kinds of Bivens injuries. See\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71346(b)(2); Pfander, Constitutional Torts, at 105\xe2\x80\x93106.

\n

If there were any lingering doubt that the claim against Warden Hasty arises in a familiar Bivens context, the Court has made clear that conditions-of-confinement claims and medical-care claims are subject to the same substantive standard. See Hudson v. McMillian,\n\n503 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1,\n8 (1992)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[Wilson v. Seiter,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 294,\n303 (1991)\n] extended the deliberate indifference standard applied to\n \nEighth Amendment claims involving medical care to claims about conditions of confinement\xe2\x80\x9d). Indeed, the Court made this very point in a Bivens case alleging that prison wardens were deliberately indifferent to an inmate\xe2\x80\x99s safety. See Farmer, supra, at 830, 834.

\n

I recognize that the Court finds a significant difference in the fact that the confinement here arose soon after a national-security emergency, namely, the September 11 attacks. The short answer to this argument, in respect to at least some of the claimed harms, is that some plaintiffs continued to suffer those harms up to eight months after the September 11 attacks took place and after the defendants knew the plaintiffs had no connection to terrorism. See App. to Pet. for Cert. in No. 15\xe2\x80\x931359, p.\xc2\xa0280a. But because I believe the Court\xe2\x80\x99s argument here is its strongest, I will consider it at greater length below. See Part III\xe2\x80\x93C, infra.

\n

Because the context here is not new, I would allow the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 constitutional claims to proceed. The plaintiffs have adequately alleged that the defendants were personally involved in imposing the conditions of confinement and did so with knowledge that the plaintiffs bore no ties to terrorism, thus satisfying Iqbal\xe2\x80\x99s pleading standard. See 556 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 679 (claims must be \xe2\x80\x9cplausible\xe2\x80\x9d); see also id., at 699\xe2\x80\x93700 (Breyer, J., dissenting). And because it is clearly established that it is unconstitutional to subject detainees to punitive conditions of confinement and to target them based solely on their race, religion, or national origin, the defendants are not entitled to qualified immunity on the constitutional claims. See Bell v. Wolfish,\n\n441 U.\xc2\xa0S. 520\n\xe2\x80\x93539, and n.\xc2\xa020 (1979); Davis, 442 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 236 (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is equally clear .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that the\n \nFifth Amendment confers on petitioner a constitutional right to be free from illegal discrimination\xe2\x80\x9d). (Similarly, I would affirm the judgment of the Court of Appeals with respect to the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 statutory claim, namely, that the defendants conspired to deprive the plaintiffs of equal protection of the laws in violation of\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71985(3). See Turkmen v. Hasty, 789 F.\xc2\xa03d 218, 262\xe2\x80\x93264 (CA2 2015). I agree with the Court of Appeals that the defendants are not entitled to qualified immunity on this claim. See ibid.)

\n

2

\n

Even were I wrong and were the context here \xe2\x80\x9cfundamentally different,\xe2\x80\x9d Malesko, 534 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 70, the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 claims would nonetheless survive Step Two and Step Three of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s framework for determining whether Bivens applies, see supra, at 9. Step Two consists of asking whether \xe2\x80\x9cany alternative, existing process for protecting the interest amounts to a convincing reason for the Judicial Branch to refrain from providing a new and freestanding remedy in damages.\xe2\x80\x9d Wilkie, 551 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 550. I can find no such \xe2\x80\x9calternative, existing process\xe2\x80\x9d here.

\n

The Court does not claim that the PLRA provides plaintiffs with a remedy. Ante, at 25\xe2\x80\x9326. Rather, it says that the plaintiffs may have \xe2\x80\x9chad available to them\xe2\x80\x9d relief in the form of a prospective injunction or an application for a writ of habeas corpus. Ante, at 22. Neither a prospective injunction nor a writ of habeas corpus, however, will normally provide plaintiffs with redress for harms they have already suffered. And here plaintiffs make a strong claim that neither was available to them\xe2\x80\x94at least not for a considerable time. Some of the plaintiffs allege that for two or three months they were subject to a \xe2\x80\x9ccommunications blackout\xe2\x80\x9d; that the prison \xe2\x80\x9cstaff did not permit them visitors, legal or social telephone calls, or mail\xe2\x80\x9d; that their families and attorneys did not know where they were being held; that they could not receive visits from their attorneys; that subsequently their lawyers could call them only once a week; and that some or all of the defendants \xe2\x80\x9cinterfered with the detainees\xe2\x80\x99 effective access to legal counsel.\xe2\x80\x9d Office of Inspector General (OIG) Report, App. 223, 293, 251, 391; see App. to Pet. for Cert. in No. 15\xe2\x80\x931359, at\xc2\xa0253a (incorporating the OIG report into the complaint). These claims make it virtually impossible to say that here there is an \xe2\x80\x9celaborate, comprehensive\xe2\x80\x9d alternative remedial scheme similar to schemes that, in the past, we have found block the application of Bivens to new contexts. Bush, 462 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 385. If these allegations are proved, then in this suit, it is \xe2\x80\x9cdamages or noth-ing.\xe2\x80\x9d Bivens, 403 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 410 (Harlan, J., concurring in judgment).

\n

There being no \xe2\x80\x9calternative, existing process\xe2\x80\x9d that provides a \xe2\x80\x9cconvincing reason\xe2\x80\x9d for not applying Bivens, we must proceed to Step Three. Wilkie, supra, at 550. Doing so, I can find no \xe2\x80\x9cspecial factors [that] counse[l] hesitation before authorizing\xe2\x80\x9d this Bivens action. 551 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 550. I turn to this matter next.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

The Court describes two general considerations that it believes argue against an \xe2\x80\x9cextension\xe2\x80\x9d of Bivens. First, the majority opinion points out that the Court is now far less likely than at the time it decided Bivens to imply a cause of action for damages from a statute that does not explicitly provide for a damages claim. See ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939. Second, it finds the \xe2\x80\x9csilence\xe2\x80\x9d of Congress \xe2\x80\x9cnotable\xe2\x80\x9d in that Congress, though likely aware of the \xe2\x80\x9chigh-level policies\xe2\x80\x9d involved in this suit, did not \xe2\x80\x9cchoose to extend to any person the kind of remedies\xe2\x80\x9d that the plaintiffs here \xe2\x80\x9cseek.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 20\xe2\x80\x9321 (internal quotation marks omitted). I doubt the strength of these two general considerations.

\n

The first consideration, in my view, is not relevant. I concede that the majority and concurring opinions in Bivens looked in part for support to the fact that the Court had implied damages remedies from statutes silent on the subject. See 403 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 397; id., at 402\xe2\x80\x93403 (Harlan, J., concurring in judgment). But that was not the main argument favoring the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion. Rather, the Court drew far stronger support from the need for such a remedy when measured against a common-law and constitutional history of allowing traditional legal remedies where necessary. Id., at 392, 396\xe2\x80\x93397. The Court believed such a remedy was necessary to make effective the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s protection of certain basic individual rights. See id., at 392; id., at 407 (opinion of Harlan, J.). Simi-larly, as the Court later explained, a damages remedy against federal officials prevented the serious legal anomaly I previously mentioned. Its existence made basic constitutional protections of the individual against Federal Government abuse (the Bill of Rights\xe2\x80\x99 pre-Civil War objective) as effective as protections against abuse by state officials (the post-Civil War, post selective-incorporation objective). See supra, at 7.

\n

Nor is the second circumstance\xe2\x80\x94congressional silence\xe2\x80\x94relevant in the manner that the majority opinion describes. The Court initially saw that silence as indicating an absence of congressional hostility to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of its traditional remedy-inferring powers. See Bivens, supra, at 397; Davis, 442 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 246\xe2\x80\x93247. Congress\xe2\x80\x99 subsequent silence contains strong signs that it accepted Bivens actions as part of the law. After all, Congress rejected a proposal that would have eliminated Bivens by substituting the U.\xc2\xa0S. Government as a defendant in suits against federal officers that raised constitutional claims. See Pfander, Constitutional Torts, at 102. Later, Congress expressly immunized federal employees acting in the course of their official duties from tort claims except those premised on violations of the Constitution. See Federal Employees Liability Reform and Tort Compensation Act of 1988, commonly known as the Westfall Act,\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72679(b)(2)(A). We stated that it is consequently \xe2\x80\x9ccrystal clear that Congress views [the Federal Tort Claims Act] and Bivens as [providing] parallel, complementary causes of action.\xe2\x80\x9d Carlson, 446 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 20; see Malesko, 534 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 68 (similar). Congress has even assumed the existence of a Bivens remedy in suits brought by noncitizen detainees suspected of terrorism. See\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000dd\xe2\x80\x931 (granting qualified immunity\xe2\x80\x94but not absolute immunity\xe2\x80\x94to military and civilian federal officials who are sued by alien detainees suspected of terrorism).

\n

B

\n

The majority opinion also sets forth a more specific list of factors that it says bear on \xe2\x80\x9cwhether a case presents a new Bivens context.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16. In the Court\xe2\x80\x99s view, a \xe2\x80\x9ccase might differ\xe2\x80\x9d from Bivens \xe2\x80\x9cin a meaningful way because of [1] the rank of the officers involved; [2] the constitutional right at issue; [3] the generality or specifi-city of the individual action; [4] the extent of judicial guidance as to how an officer should respond to the problem or emergency to be confronted; [5] the statutory or other legal mandate under which the officer was operating; [6] the risk of disruptive intrusion by the Judiciary into the functioning of other branches; [7] or the presence of potential special factors that previous Bivens cases did not con-sider.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16. In my view, these factors do not make a \xe2\x80\x9cmeaningful difference\xe2\x80\x9d at Step One of the Bivens framework. Some of them are better cast as \xe2\x80\x9cspecial factors\xe2\x80\x9d relevant to Step Three. But, as I see it, none should normally foreclose a Bivens action and none is determinative here. Consider them one by one:

\n

(1) The rank of the officers. I can understand why an officer\xe2\x80\x99s rank might bear on whether he violated the Constitution, because, for example, a plaintiff might need to show the officer was willfully blind to a harm caused by lower ranking officers or that the officer had actual knowledge of the misconduct. And I can understand that rank might relate to the existence of a legal defense, such as qualified, or even absolute, immunity. But if\xe2\x80\x94and I recognize that this is often a very big if\xe2\x80\x94a plaintiff proves a clear constitutional violation, say, of the\n \nFourth Amendment, and he shows that the defendant does not possess any form of immunity or other defense, then why should he not have a damages remedy for harm suffered? What does rank have to do with that question, namely, the Bivens question? Why should the law treat differently a high-level official and the local constable where each has similarly violated the Constitution and where neither can successfully assert immunity or any other defense?

\n

(2) The constitutional right at issue. I agree that this factor can make a difference, but only when the substance of the right is distinct. See, e.g., Wilkie,\n\n551 U.\xc2\xa0S. 537\n (land rights). But, for reasons I have already pointed out, there is no relevant difference between the rights at issue here and the rights at issue in our previous Bivens cases, namely, the rights to be free of unreasonable searches, invidious discrimination, and physical abuse in federal custody. See supra, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

(3) The generality or specificity of the individual action. I should think that it is not the \xe2\x80\x9cgenerality or specificity\xe2\x80\x9d of an official action but rather the nature of the official action that matters. Bivens should apply to some generally applicable actions, such as actions taken deliberately to jail a large group of known-innocent people. And it should not apply to some highly specific actions, depending upon the nature of those actions.

\n

(4) The extent of judicial guidance. This factor may be relevant to the existence of a constitutional violation or a qualified-immunity defense. Where judicial guidance is lacking, it is more likely that a constitutional violation is not clearly established. See Anderson v. Creighton,\n\n483 U.\xc2\xa0S. 635,\n640 (1987)\n (Officials are protected by qualified immunity unless \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he contours of the right [are] sufficiently clear that a reasonable official would understand that what he is doing violates that right\xe2\x80\x9d). But I do not see how, assuming the violation is clear, the presence or absence of \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial guidance\xe2\x80\x9d is relevant to the existence of a damages remedy.

\n

(5) The statutory (or other) legal mandate under which the officer was operating. This factor too may prove relevant to the question whether a constitutional violation exists or is clearly established. But, again, assuming that it is, I do not understand why this factor is relevant to the existence of a damages remedy. See Stanley, 483 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 684 (the question of immunity is \xe2\x80\x9canalytically distinct\xe2\x80\x9d from the question whether a Bivens action should lie).

\n

(6) Risk of disruptive judicial intrusion. All damages actions risk disrupting to some degree future decisionmaking by members of the Executive or Legislative Branches. Where this Court has authorized Bivens actions, it has found that disruption tolerable, and it has explained why disruption is, from a constitutional perspective, desirable. See Davis, 442 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 242 (Unless constitutional rights \xe2\x80\x9care to become merely precatory, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. litigants who allege that their own constitutional rights have been violated, and who at the same time have no effective means other than the judiciary to enforce these rights, must be able to invoke the existing jurisdiction of the courts for .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. protection\xe2\x80\x9d); Malesko, supra, at 70 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe purpose of Bivens is to deter individual federal officers from committing constitutional violations\xe2\x80\x9d). Insofar as the Court means this consideration to provide a reason why there should be no Bivens action where a Government employee acts in time of security need, I shall discuss the matter next, in Part C.

\n

(7) Other potential special factors. Since I am not certain what these other \xe2\x80\x9cpotential factors\xe2\x80\x9d are and, since the Court does not specify their nature, I would not, and the Court cannot, consider them in differentiating this suit from our previous Bivens cases or as militating against recognizing a Bivens action here.

\n

C

\n

In my view, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s strongest argument is that Bivens should not apply to policy-related actions taken in times of national-security need, for example, during war or national-security emergency. As the Court correctly points out, the Constitution grants primary power to protect the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s security to the Executive and Legislative Branches, not to the Judiciary. But the Constitution also delegates to the Judiciary the duty to protect an individual\xe2\x80\x99s fundamental constitutional rights. Hence when protection of those rights and a determination of security needs conflict, the Court has a role to play. The Court most recently made this clear in cases arising out of the detention of enemy combatants at Guantanamo Bay. Justice O\xe2\x80\x99Connor wrote that \xe2\x80\x9ca state of war is not a blank check.\xe2\x80\x9d Hamdi v. Rumsfeld,\n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 507,\n536 (2004)\n (plurality opinion). In Boumediene, 553 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 732\xe2\x80\x93733, the Court reinforced that point, holding that noncitizens detained as enemy combatants were entitled to challenge their detention through a writ of habeas corpus, notwithstanding the national-security concerns at stake.

\n

We have not, however, answered the specific question the Court places at issue here: Should Bivens actions continue to exist in respect to policy-related actions taken in time of war or national emergency? In my view, they should.

\n

For one thing, a Bivens action comes accompanied by many legal safeguards designed to prevent the courts from interfering with Executive and Legislative Branch activity reasonably believed to be necessary to protect national security. In Justice Jackson\xe2\x80\x99s well-known words, the Constitution is not \xe2\x80\x9ca suicide pact.\xe2\x80\x9d Terminiello v. Chicago,\n\n337 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1,\n37 (1949)\n (dissenting opinion). The Consti-tution itself takes account of public necessity. Thus, for example, the\n \nFourth Amendment does not forbid all Government searches and seizures; it forbids only those that are \xe2\x80\x9cunreasonable.\xe2\x80\x9d Ordinarily, it requires that a police officer obtain a search warrant before entering an apartment, but should the officer observe a woman being dragged against her will into that apartment, he should, and will, act at once. The\n \nFourth Amendment makes allowances for such \xe2\x80\x9cexigent circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Brigham City v. Stuart,\n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 398,\n401 (2006)\n (warrantless entry justified to forestall imminent injury). Similarly, the\n \nFifth Amendment bars only conditions of confinement that are not \xe2\x80\x9creasonably related to a legitimate governmental objective.\xe2\x80\x9d Bell v. Wolfish, 441 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 539. What is unreasonable and illegitimate in time of peace may be reasonable and legitimate in time of war.

\n

Moreover, Bivens comes accompanied with a qualified-immunity defense. Federal officials will face suit only if they have violated a constitutional right that was \xe2\x80\x9cclearly established\xe2\x80\x9d at the time they acted. Harlow, 457 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 818.

\n

Further, in order to prevent the very presence of a Bivens lawsuit from interfering with the work of a Government official, this Court has held that a com-plaint must state a claim for relief that is \xe2\x80\x9cplausible.\xe2\x80\x9d Iqbal, 556 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 679. \xe2\x80\x9c[C]onclusory\xe2\x80\x9d statements and \xe2\x80\x9c[t]hreadbare\xe2\x80\x9d allegations will not suffice. Id., at 678. And the Court has protected high-level officials in particular by requiring that plaintiffs plead that an official was personally involved in the unconstitutional conduct; an official cannot be vicariously liable for another\xe2\x80\x99s misdeeds. Id., at 676.

\n

Finally, where such a claim is filed, courts can, and should, tailor discovery orders so that they do not unnecessarily or improperly interfere with the official\xe2\x80\x99s work. The Second Circuit has emphasized the \xe2\x80\x9cneed to vindicate the purpose of the qualified immunity defense by dismissing non-meritorious claims against public officials at an early stage of litigation.\xe2\x80\x9d Iqbal v. Hasty, 490 F.\xc2\xa03d 143, 158 (2007). Where some of the defendants are \xe2\x80\x9ccurrent or former senior officials of the Government, against whom broad-ranging allegations of knowledge and personal involvement are easily made, a district court\xe2\x80\x9d not only \xe2\x80\x9cmay, but \xe2\x80\x98must exercise its discretion in a way that protects the substance of the qualified immunity defense .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. so that\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d those officials \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98are not subjected to unnecessary and burdensome discovery or trial proceedings.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 158\xe2\x80\x93159. The court can make \xe2\x80\x9call such discovery subject to prior court approval.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 158. It can \xe2\x80\x9cstructure .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. limited discovery by examining written responses to interrogatories and requests to admit before authorizing depositions, and by deferring discovery directed to high-level officials until discovery of front-line officials has been completed and has demonstrated the need for discovery higher up the ranks.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. In a word, a trial court can and should so structure the proceedings with full recognition that qualified immunity amounts to immunity from suit as well as immunity from liability.

\n

Given these safeguards against undue interference by the Judiciary in times of war or national-security emergency, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s abolition, or limitation of, Bivens actions goes too far. If you are cold, put on a sweater, perhaps an overcoat, perhaps also turn up the heat, but do not set fire to the house.

\n

At the same time, there may well be a particular need for Bivens remedies when security-related Government actions are at issue. History tells us of far too many instances where the Executive or Legislative Branch took actions during time of war that, on later examination, turned out unnecessarily and unreasonably to have deprived American citizens of basic constitutional rights. We have read about the Alien and Sedition Acts, the thousands of civilians imprisoned during the Civil War, and the suppression of civil liberties during World War I. See W. Rehnquist, All the Laws but One: Civil Liberties in Wartime 209\xe2\x80\x93210, 49\xe2\x80\x9350, 173\xe2\x80\x93180, 183 (1998); see also Ex\xc2\xa0parte Milligan, 4 Wall. 2 (1866) (decided after the Civil War was over). The pages of the U.\xc2\xa0S. Reports themselves recite this Court\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to set aside the Government\xe2\x80\x99s World War II action removing more than 70,000 American citizens of Japanese origin from their west coast homes and interning them in camps, see Korematsu v. United States,\n\n323 U.\xc2\xa0S. 214 (1944)\n\xe2\x80\x94an action that at least some officials knew at the time was unnecessary, see id., at 233\xe2\x80\x93242 (Murphy, J., dissenting); P. Irons, Justice at War 202\xe2\x80\x93204, 288 (1983). President Franklin Roosevelt\xe2\x80\x99s Attorney General, perhaps exaggerating, once said that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Constitution has not greatly bothered any wartime President.\xe2\x80\x9d Rehnquist, supra, at 191.

\n

Can we, in respect to actions taken during those periods, rely exclusively, as the Court seems to suggest, upon injunctive remedies or writs of habeas corpus, their retail equivalent? Complaints seeking that kind of relief typi-cally come during the emergency itself, when emotions are strong, when courts may have too little or inaccurate information, and when courts may well prove particularly reluctant to interfere with even the least well-founded Executive Branch activity. That reluctance may itself set an unfortunate precedent, which, as Justice Jackson pointed out, can \xe2\x80\x9cli[e] about like a loaded weapon\xe2\x80\x9d awaiting discharge in another case. Korematsu, supra, at 246 (dissenting opinion).

\n

A damages action, however, is typically brought after the emergency is over, after emotions have cooled, and at a time when more factual information is available. In such circumstances, courts have more time to exercise such judicial virtues as calm reflection and dispassionate ap-plication of the law to the facts. We have applied the Constitution to actions taken during periods of war and national-security emergency. See Boumediene, 553 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 732\xe2\x80\x93733; Hamdi v. Rumsfeld,\n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 507\n; cf. Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer,\n\n343 U.\xc2\xa0S. 579 (1952)\n. I should think that the wisdom of permitting courts to consider Bivens actions, later granting monetary compensation to those wronged at the time, would follow a\xc2\xa0fortiori.

\n

\n

As is well known, Lord Atkins, a British judge, wrote in the midst of World War II that \xe2\x80\x9camid the clash of arms, the laws are not silent. They may be changed, but they speak the same language in war as in peace.\xe2\x80\x9d Liversidge v. Anderson, [1942] A. C. 206 (H. L. 1941) 244. The Court, in my view, should say the same of this Bivens action.

\n

\n

With respect, I dissent.
