B&B HARDWARE, INC. v. HARGIS INDUSTRIES, INC., dba SEALTITE BUILDING FASTENERS et\xc2\xa0al., et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eighth circuit

\n

No. 13\xe2\x80\x93352.\xe2\x80\x83Argued December 2, 2014\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 24, 2015

\n

Respondent Hargis Industries, Inc. (Hargis), tried to register its trademark for SEALTITE with the United States Patent and Trademark Office pursuant to the Lanham Act. Petitioner, B&B Hardware, Inc. (B&B), however, opposed registration, claiming that SEALTITE is too similar to B&B\xe2\x80\x99s own SEALTIGHT trademark. The Trademark Trial and Appeal Board (TTAB) concluded that SEALTITE should not be registered because of the likelihood of confusion. Hargis did not seek judicial review of that decision.

\n

\n \n \n \n Later, in an infringement suit before the District Court, B&B argued that Hargis was precluded from contesting the likelihood of confusion because of the TTAB\xe2\x80\x99s decision. The District Court disagreed. The Eighth Circuit affirmed, holding that preclusion was unwarranted because the TTAB and the court used different factors to evaluate likelihood of confusion, the TTAB placed too much emphasis on the appearance and sound of the two marks, and Hargis bore the burden of persuasion before the TTAB while B&B bore it before the District Court.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0So long as the other ordinary elements of issue preclusion are met, when the usages adjudicated by the TTAB are materially the same as those before a district court, issue preclusion should apply. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9322.

\n

\n \n (a)\xc2\xa0An agency decision can ground issue preclusion. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases establish that when Congress authorizes agencies to resolve disputes, \xe2\x80\x9ccourts may take it as given that Congress has legislated with the expectation that [issue preclusion] will apply except when a statutory purpose to the contrary is evident.\xe2\x80\x9d Astoria Fed. Sav. & Loan Assn. v. Solimino,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 104\n. Constitutional avoidance does not compel a different conclusion. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n \n (b)\xc2\xa0Neither the Lanham Act\xe2\x80\x99s text nor its structure rebuts the \xe2\x80\x9cpresumption\xe2\x80\x9d in favor of giving preclusive effect to TTAB decisions where the ordinary elements of issue preclusion are met. Astoria, 501 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 108. This case is unlike Astoria. There, where exhausting the administrative process was a prerequisite to suit in court, giving preclusive effect to the agency\xe2\x80\x99s determination in that very administrative process could have rendered the judicial suit \xe2\x80\x9cstrictly pro\xc2\xa0forma.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 111. By contrast, registration involves a separate proceeding to decide separate rights. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n \n (c)\xc2\xa0There is no categorical reason why registration decisions can never meet the ordinary elements of issue preclusion. That many registrations will not satisfy those ordinary elements does not mean that none will. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9322.

\n

\n \n \n \n (1)\xc2\xa0Contrary to the Eighth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion, the same likelihood-of-confusion standard applies to both registration and infringement. The factors that the TTAB and the Eighth Circuit use to assess likelihood of confusion are not fundamentally different, and, more important, the operative language of each statute is essentially the same.

\n

\n \n Hargis claims that the standards are different, noting that the registration provision asks whether the marks \xe2\x80\x9cresemble\xe2\x80\x9d each other,\n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71052(d), while the infringement provision is directed towards the \xe2\x80\x9cuse in commerce\xe2\x80\x9d of the marks, \xc2\xa71114(1). That the TTAB and a district court do not always consider the same usages, however, does not mean that the TTAB applies a different standard to the usages it does consider. If a mark owner uses its mark in materially the same ways as the usages included in its registration application, then the TTAB is deciding the same likelihood-of-confusion issue as a district court in infringement litigation. For a similar reason, the Eighth Circuit erred in holding that issue preclusion could not apply because the TTAB relied too heavily on \xe2\x80\x9cappearance and sound.\xe2\x80\x9d Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

\n \n \n \n (2)\xc2\xa0The fact that the TTAB and district courts use different procedures suggests only that sometimes issue preclusion might be inappropriate, not that it always is. Here, there is no categorical \xe2\x80\x9creason to doubt the quality, extensiveness, or fairness,\xe2\x80\x9d Montana v. United States, \n\n440 U.\xc2\xa0S. 147\n, 164, n.\xc2\xa011, of the agency\xe2\x80\x99s procedures. In large part they are exactly the same as in federal court. Also contrary to the Eighth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion, B&B, the party opposing registration, not Hargis, bore the burden of persuasion before the TTAB, just as it did in the infringement suit. Pp.\xc2\xa019\xe2\x80\x9321.

\n

\n \n \n \n (3)\xc2\xa0 Hargis is also wrong that the stakes for registration are always too low for issue preclusion in later infringement litigation. When registration is opposed, there is good reason to think that both sides will take the matter seriously. Congress\xe2\x80\x99 creation of an elaborate registration scheme, with many important rights attached and backed up by plenary review, confirms that registration decisions can be weighty enough to ground issue preclusion. Pp.\xc2\xa021\xe2\x80\x9322.

\n

716 F.\xc2\xa03d 1020, reversed and remanded.

B&B HARDWARE, INC., PETITIONER v. HARGIS INDUSTRIES, INC., dba SEALTITE BUILDING FASTENERS, dba EAST TEXAS FASTENERS et\xc2\xa0al.

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eighth circuit

[March 24, 2015]

Justice Alito delivered the opinion of the Court.

Sometimes two different tribunals are asked to decide the same issue. When that happens, the decision of the first tribunal usually must be followed by the second, at least if the issue is really the same. Allowing the same issue to be decided more than once wastes litigants\xe2\x80\x99 resources and adjudicators\xe2\x80\x99 time, and it encourages parties who lose before one tribunal to shop around for another. The doctrine of collateral estoppel or issue preclusion is designed to prevent this from occurring.

This case concerns the application of issue preclusion in the context of trademark law. Petitioner, B&B Hardware, Inc. (B&B), and respondent Hargis Industries, Inc. (Hargis), both use similar trademarks; B&B owns SEALTIGHT while Hargis owns SEALTITE. Under the Lanham Act,60Stat.427, as amended,15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71051 et\xc2\xa0seq., an applicant can seek to register a trademark through an administrative process within the United States Patent and Trademark Office (PTO). But if another party believes that the PTO should not register a mark because it is too similar to its own, that party can oppose registration before the Trademark Trial and Appeal Board (TTAB). Here, Hargis tried to register the mark SEALTITE, but B&B opposed SEALTITE\xe2\x80\x99s registration. After a lengthy proceeding, the TTAB agreed with B&B that SEALTITE should not be registered.

In addition to permitting a party to object to the registration of a mark, the Lanham Act allows a mark owner to sue for trademark infringement. Both a registration proceeding and a suit for trademark infringement, more-over, can occur at the same time. In this case, while the TTAB was deciding whether SEALTITE should be registered, B&B and Hargis were also litigating the SEALTIGHT versus SEALTITE dispute in federal court. In both registration proceedings and infringement litigation, the tribunal asks whether a likelihood of confusion exists between the mark sought to be protected (here, SEALTIGHT) and the other mark (SEALTITE).

The question before this Court is whether the District Court in this case should have applied issue preclusion to the TTAB\xe2\x80\x99s decision that SEALTITE is confusingly similar to SEALTIGHT. Here, the Eighth Circuit rejected issue preclusion for reasons that would make it difficult for the doctrine ever to apply in trademark disputes. We disagree with that narrow understanding of issue preclusion. Instead, consistent with principles of law that apply in innumerable contexts, we hold that a court should give preclusive effect to TTAB decisions if the ordinary elements of issue preclusion are met. We therefore reverse the judgment of the Eighth Circuit and remand for further proceedings.

I

A

Trademark law has a long history, going back at least to Roman times. See Restatement (Third) of Unfair Competition \xc2\xa79, Comment b (1993). The principle underlying trademark protection is that distinctive marks\xe2\x80\x94words, names, symbols, and the like\xe2\x80\x94can help distinguish a particular artisan\xe2\x80\x99s goods from those of others. Ibid. One who first uses a distinct mark in commerce thus acquires rights to that mark. See 2 J. McCarthy, Trademarks and Unfair Competition \xc2\xa716:1 (4th ed. 2014) (hereinafter McCarthy). Those rights include preventing others from using the mark. See 1 A. LaLonde, Gilson on Trademarks \xc2\xa73.02[8] (2014) (hereinafter Gilson).

Though federal law does not create trademarks, see, e.g., Trade-Mark Cases,100 U.\xc2\xa0S. 82,92 (1879), Congress has long played a role in protecting them. In 1946, Congress enacted the Lanham Act, the current federal trademark scheme. As relevant here, the Lanham Act creates at least two adjudicative mechanisms to help protect marks. First, a trademark owner can register its mark with the PTO. Second, a mark owner can bring a suit for infringement in federal court.

Registration is significant. The Lanham Act confers \xe2\x80\x9cimportant legal rights and benefits\xe2\x80\x9d on trademark owners who register their marks. 3 McCarthy \xc2\xa719:3, at 19\xe2\x80\x9321 see also id., \xc2\xa719:9, at 19\xe2\x80\x9334 (listing seven of the \xe2\x80\x9cprocedural and substantive legal advantages\xe2\x80\x9d of registration). Registration, for instance, serves as \xe2\x80\x9cconstructive notice of the registrant\xe2\x80\x99s claim of ownership\xe2\x80\x9d of the mark.15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71072. It also is \xe2\x80\x9cprima facie evidence of the validity of the registered mark and of the registration of the mark, of the owner\xe2\x80\x99s ownership of the mark, and of the owner\xe2\x80\x99s exclusive right to use the registered mark in commerce on or in connection with the goods or services specified in the certificate.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71057(b). And once a mark has been registered for five years, it can become \xe2\x80\x9cincontestable.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71065, 1115(b)

To obtain the benefits of registration, a mark owner files an application with the PTO. \xc2\xa71051. The application must include, among other things, \xe2\x80\x9cthe date of the applicant\xe2\x80\x99s first use of the mark, the date of the applicant\xe2\x80\x99s first use of the mark in commerce, the goods in connection with which the mark is used, and a drawing of the mark.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71051(a)(2). The usages listed in the application\xe2\x80\x94i.e., those goods on which the mark appears along with, if applicable, their channels of distribution\xe2\x80\x94are critical. See, e.g., 3 McCarthy \xc2\xa720:24, at 20\xe2\x80\x9383 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he applicant\xe2\x80\x99s right to register must be made on the basis of the goods described in the application\xe2\x80\x9d); id., \xc2\xa720:15, at 20\xe2\x80\x9385 (explaining that if an \xe2\x80\x9capplication does not delimit any spe-cific trade channels of distribution, no limitation will be\xe2\x80\x9d applied). The PTO generally cannot register a mark which \xe2\x80\x9cso resembles\xe2\x80\x9d another mark \xe2\x80\x9cas to be likely, when used on or in connection with the goods of the applicant, to cause confusion, or to cause mistake, or to deceive.\xe2\x80\x9d15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71052(d).

If a trademark examiner believes that registration is warranted, the mark is published in the Official Gazette of the PTO. \xc2\xa71062. At that point, \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny person who believes that he would be damaged by the registration\xe2\x80\x9d may \xe2\x80\x9cfile an opposition.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71063(a). Opposition proceedings occur before the TTAB (or panels thereof). \xc2\xa71067(a). The TTAB consists of administrative trademark judges and high-ranking PTO officials, including the Director of the PTO and the Commissioner of Trademarks. \xc2\xa71067(b).

Opposition proceedings before the TTAB are in many ways \xe2\x80\x9csimilar to a civil action in a federal district court.\xe2\x80\x9d TTAB Manual of Procedure \xc2\xa7102.03 (2014) (hereinafter TTAB Manual), online at http://www.uspto.gov (as visited Mar. 20, 2015, and available in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file). These proceedings, for instance, are largely governed by the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure and Evidence. See 37 CFR \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72.116(a), 2.122(a) (2014). The TTAB also allows discovery and depositions. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72.120, 2.123(a). The party opposing registration bears the burden of proof, see \xc2\xa72.116(b), and if that burden cannot be met, the opposed mark must be registered, see15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71063(b).

The primary way in which TTAB proceedings differ from ordinary civil litigation is that \xe2\x80\x9cproceedings before the Board are conducted in writing, and the Board\xe2\x80\x99s actions in a particular case are based upon the written record therein.\xe2\x80\x9d TTAB Manual \xc2\xa7102.03. In other words, there is no live testimony. Even so, the TTAB allows parties to submit transcribed testimony, taken under oath and subject to cross-examination, and to request oral argument. See 37 CFR \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72.123, 2.129.

When a party opposes registration because it believes the mark proposed to be registered is too similar to its own, the TTAB evaluates likelihood of confusion by applying some or all of the 13 factors set out in In re E. I. DuPont DeNemours & Co., 476 F. 2d 1357 (CCPA 1973). After the TTAB decides whether to register the mark, a party can seek review in the U.\xc2\xa0S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit, or it can file a new action in district court. See15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71071. In district court, the parties can conduct additional discovery and the judge resolves registration de novo. \xc2\xa71071(b); see also 3 McCarthy \xc2\xa721:20 (explaining differences between the forums); cf. Kappos v. Hyatt, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012) (de\xc2\xa0novo review for analogous scheme in patent law).

The Lanham Act, of course, also creates a federal cause of action for trademark infringement. The owner of a mark, whether registered or not, can bring suit in federal court if another is using a mark that too closely resembles the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s. The court must decide whether the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s use of a mark in commerce \xe2\x80\x9cis likely to cause confusion, or to cause mistake, or to deceive\xe2\x80\x9d with regards to the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s mark. See15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71114(1)(a) (registered marks); \xc2\xa71125(a)(1)(A) (unregistered marks). In infringement litigation, the district court considers the full range of a mark\xe2\x80\x99s usages, not just those in the application.

B

Petitioner B&B and respondent Hargis both manufacture metal fasteners. B&B manufactures fasteners for the aerospace industry, while Hargis manufactures fasteners for use in the construction trade. Although there are obvious differences between space shuttles and A-frame buildings, both aerospace and construction engineers prefer fasteners that seal things tightly. Accordingly, both B&B and Hargis want their wares associated with tight seals. A feud of nearly two decades has sprung from this seemingly commonplace set of facts.

In 1993 B&B registered SEALTIGHT for \xe2\x80\x9cthreaded or unthreaded metal fasteners and other related hardwar[e]; namely, self-sealing nuts, bolts, screws, rivets and washers, all having a captive o-ring, for use in the aerospace industry.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 223a (capitalization omitted). In 1996, Hargis sought to register SEALTITE for \xe2\x80\x9cself-piercing and self-drilling metal screws for use in the manufacture of metal and post-frame buildings.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 70a (capitalization omitted). B&B opposed Hargis\xe2\x80\x99 registration because, although the two companies sell different products, it believes that SEALTITE is confusingly similar to SEALTIGHT.

The twists and turns in the SEALTIGHT versus SEALTITE controversy are labyrinthine. The question whether either of these marks should be registered, and if so, which one, has bounced around within the PTO for about two decades; related infringement litigation has been before the Eighth Circuit three times; and two separate juries have been empaneled and returned verdicts. The full story could fill a long, unhappy book.

For purposes here, we pick up the story in 2002, when the PTO published SEALTITE in the Official Gazette. This prompted opposition proceedings before the TTAB, complete with discovery, including depositions. B&B ar-gued that SEALTITE could not be registered because itis confusingly similar to SEALTIGHT. B&B explained, for instance, that both companies have an online presence, the largest distributor of fasteners sells both companies\xe2\x80\x99 products, and consumers sometimes call the wrong company to place orders. Hargis rejoined that the companies sell different products, for different uses, to different types of consumers, through different channels of trade.

Invoking a number of the DuPont factors, the TTAB sided with B&B. The Board considered, for instance, whether SEALTIGHT is famous (it\xe2\x80\x99s not, said the Board), how the two products are used (differently), how much the marks resemble each other (very much), and whether customers are actually confused (perhaps sometimes). See App. to Pet. for Cert. 55a\xe2\x80\x9371a. Concluding that \xe2\x80\x9cthe most critical factors in [its] likelihood of confusion analysis are the similarities of the marks and the similarity of the goods,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 70a, the TTAB determined that SEALTITE\xe2\x80\x94when \xe2\x80\x9cused in connection with \xe2\x80\x98self-piercing and self-drilling metal screws for use in the manufacture of metal and post-frame buildings\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94could not be registered because it \xe2\x80\x9cso resembles\xe2\x80\x9d SEALTIGHT when \xe2\x80\x9cused in connection with fasteners that provide leakproof protection from liquids and gases, fasteners that have a captive o-ring, and \xe2\x80\x98threaded or unthreaded metal fastners and other related hardware .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. for use in the aerospace industry\xe2\x80\x99 as to be likely to cause confusion,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 71a. Despite a right to do so, Hargis did not seek judicial review in either the Federal Circuit or District Court.

All the while, B&B had sued Hargis for infringement. Before the District Court ruled on likelihood of confusion, however, the TTAB announced its decision. After a series of proceedings not relevant here, B&B argued to the District Court that Hargis could not contest likelihood of confusion because of the preclusive effect of the TTAB decision. The District Court disagreed, reasoning that the TTAB is not an Article III court. The jury returned a verdict for Hargis, finding no likelihood of confusion.

B&B appealed to the Eighth Circuit. Though accepting for the sake of argument that agency decisions can ground issue preclusion, the panel majority affirmed for three reasons: first, because the TTAB uses different factors than the Eighth Circuit to evaluate likelihood of confusion; second, because the TTAB placed too much emphasis on the appearance and sound of the two marks; and third, because Hargis bore the burden of persuasion before the TTAB, while B&B bore it before the District Court. 716 F.\xc2\xa03d 1020 (2013). Judge Colloton dissented, concluding that issue preclusion should apply. After calling for the views of the Solicitor General, we granted certiorari. 573 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014).

II

The first question that we must address is whether an agency decision can ever ground issue preclusion. The District Court rejected issue preclusion because agencies are not Article III courts. The Eighth Circuit did not adopt that view, and, given this Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases, it was right to take that course.

This Court has long recognized that \xe2\x80\x9cthe determination of a question directly involved in one action is conclusive as to that question in a second suit.\xe2\x80\x9d Cromwell v. County of Sac,94 U.\xc2\xa0S. 351,354 (1877). The idea is straightforward: Once a court has decided an issue, it is \xe2\x80\x9cforever settled as between the parties,\xe2\x80\x9d Baldwin v. Iowa State Traveling Men\xe2\x80\x99s Assn.283 U.\xc2\xa0S. 522,525 (1931), thereby \xe2\x80\x9cprotect[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d against \xe2\x80\x9cthe expense and vexation attending multiple lawsuits, conserv[ing] judicial resources, and foster[ing] reliance on judicial action by minimizing the possibility of inconsistent verdicts,\xe2\x80\x9d Montana v. United States,440 U.\xc2\xa0S. 147\xe2\x80\x93154 (1979). In short, \xe2\x80\x9ca losing litigant deserves no rematch after a defeat fairly suffered.\xe2\x80\x9d Astoria Fed. Sav. & Loan Assn. v. Solimino,501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 104,107 (1991).

Although the idea of issue preclusion is straightforward, it can be challenging to implement. The Court, therefore, regularly turns to the Restatement (Second) of Judgments for a statement of the ordinary elements of issue preclusion. See, e.g., Bobby v. Bies,556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 825,834 (2009); New Hampshire v. Maine,532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 742\xe2\x80\x93749 (2001); Baker v. General Motors Corp.,522 U.\xc2\xa0S. 222,233, n.5 (1998). The Restatement explains that subject to certain well-known exceptions, the general rule is that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen an issue of fact or law is actually litigated and determined by a valid and final judgment, and the determination is essential to the judgment, the determination is conclusive in a subsequent action between the parties, whether on the same or a different claim.\xe2\x80\x9d Restatement (Second) of Judgments \xc2\xa727, p. 250 (1980); see also id., \xc2\xa728, at 273 (listing exceptions such as whether appellate review was available or whether there were \xe2\x80\x9cdifferences in the quality or extensiveness of the procedures followed\xe2\x80\x9d).

Both this Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases and the Restatement make clear that issue preclusion is not limited to those situations in which the same issue is before two courts. Rather, where a single issue is before a court and an administrative agency, preclusion also often applies. Indeed, this Court has explained that because the principle of issue preclusion was so \xe2\x80\x9cwell established\xe2\x80\x9d at common law, in those situations in which Congress has authorized agencies to resolve disputes, \xe2\x80\x9ccourts may take it as given that Congress has legislated with the expectation that the principle [of issue preclusion] will apply except when a statutory purpose to the contrary is evident.\xe2\x80\x9d Astoria, supra, at 108. This reflects the Court\xe2\x80\x99s longstanding view that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[w]hen an administrative agency is acting in a judicial capacity and resolves disputed issues of fact properly before it which the parties have had an adequate opportunity to litigate, the courts have not hesitated to apply res judicata to enforce repose.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d University of Tenn. v. Elliott,478 U.\xc2\xa0S. 788\xe2\x80\x93798 (1986) (quoting United States v. UtahConstr. & Mining Co.,384 U.\xc2\xa0S. 394,422 (1966)); see also Hayfield Northern R. Co. v. Chicago & North Western Transp. Co.,467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 622,636, n.15 (1984) (noting Utah Construction); Kremer v. Chemical Constr. Corp.,456 U.\xc2\xa0S. 461\xe2\x80\x93485, n. 26 (1982) (characterizing Utah Construction\xe2\x80\x99s discussion of administrative preclusion as a holding); Restatement (Second) of Judgments \xc2\xa783(1), at 266 (explaining that, with some limits, \xe2\x80\x9ca valid and final adjudicative determination by an administrative tribunal has the same effects under the rules of res judicata, subject to the same exceptions and qualifications, as a judgment of a court\xe2\x80\x9d).

Although apparently accepting Astoria and Utah Construction,[1] Hargis argues that we should not read the Lanham Act (or, presumably, many other federal statutes) as authorizing issue preclusion. Otherwise, Hargis warns, the Court would have to confront \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98grave and doubtful questions\xe2\x80\x99 as to the Lanham Act\xe2\x80\x99s consistency with the Seventh Amendment and Article III of the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 38 (quoting United States ex\xc2\xa0rel. Attorney General v. Delaware & Hudson Co.,213 U.\xc2\xa0S. 366,408 (1909)). We are not persuaded.

At the outset, we note that Hargis does not argue that giving issue preclusive effect to the TTAB\xe2\x80\x99s decision would be unconstitutional. Instead, Hargis contends only that we should read the Lanham Act narrowly because a broad reading might be unconstitutional. See, e.g., Brief for Respondent 37, 39, 40, 41\xe2\x80\x9342. The likely reason that Hargis has not directly advanced a constitutional argument is that, at least as to a jury trial right, Hargis did not even list the Seventh Amendment as an authority in its appellee brief to the Eighth Circuit. Moreover, although Hargis pressed an Article III argument below, in its opposition to certiorari in this Court, Hargis seemingly con-ceded that TTAB decisions can sometimes ground issue preclusion, though it now protests otherwise. SeeSupplemental Brief in Opposition 2. To the extent, if any, that there could be a meritorious constitutional objection, it is not before us. See Plaut v. Spendthrift Farm, Inc.,514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 211\xe2\x80\x93232 (1995).

We reject Hargis\xe2\x80\x99 statutory argument that we should jettison administrative preclusion in whole or in part to avoid potential constitutional concerns. As to the Seventh Amendment, for instance, the Court has already held that the right to a jury trial does not negate the issue-preclusive effect of a judgment, even if that judgment was entered by a juryless tribunal. See Parklane Hosiery Co. v. Shore,439 U.\xc2\xa0S. 322,337 (1979). It would seem to follow naturally that although the Seventh Amendment creates a jury trial right in suits for trademark damages, see Dairy Queen, Inc. v. Wood,369 U.\xc2\xa0S. 469\xe2\x80\x93480 (1962), TTAB decisions still can have preclusive effect in such suits. Hargis disputes this reasoning even though it admits that in 1791 \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a party was not entitled to have a jury determine issues that had been previously adjudi-cated by a chancellor in equity.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 39 (quoting Parklane Hosiery, supra, at 333). Instead, Hargis contends that issue preclusion should not apply to TTAB registration decisions because there were no agencies at common law. But our precedent holds that the Seventh Amendment does not strip competent tribunals of the power to issue judgments with preclusive effect; that logic would not seem to turn on the nature of the competent tribunal. And at the same time, adopting Hargis\xe2\x80\x99 view would dramatically undercut agency preclusion, despite what the Court has already said to the contrary. Nothing in Hargis\xe2\x80\x99 avoidance argument is weighty enough to overcome these weaknesses.

The claim that we should read the Lanham Act narrowly to avoid Article III concerns is equally unavailing\xe2\x80\x94andfor similar reasons. Hargis argues that because it might violate Article III if an agency could make a decision with preclusive effect in a later proceeding before a federal court, we should conclude, as a statutory matter, that issue preclusion is unavailable. Such a holding would not fit with our precedent. For instance, in Elliott, the Court, relying on Utah Construction, explained that absent a contrary indication, Congress presumptively intends that an agency\xe2\x80\x99s determination (there, a state agency) has preclusive effect. 478 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 796\xe2\x80\x93799; see also Astoria, 501 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 110 (recognizing the \xe2\x80\x9cpresumption\xe2\x80\x9d). To be sure, the Court has never addressed whether such preclusion offends Article III. But because this Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases are so clear, there is no ambiguity for this Court to sidestep through constitutional avoidance.[2]

III

The next question is whether there is an \xe2\x80\x9cevident\xe2\x80\x9d reason why Congress would not want TTAB decisions to receive preclusive effect, even in those cases in which the ordinary elements of issue preclusion are met. Astoria, supra, at 108. We conclude that nothing in the Lanham Act bars the application of issue preclusion in such cases.

The Lanham Act\xe2\x80\x99s text certainly does not forbid issue preclusion. Nor does the Act\xe2\x80\x99s structure. Granted, one can seek judicial review of a TTAB registration decision in a de novo district court action, and some courts have concluded from this that Congress does not want unreviewed TTAB decisions to ground issue preclusion. See, e.g., American Heritage Life Ins. Co. v. Heritage Life Ins. Co., 494 F. 2d 3, 9\xe2\x80\x9310 (CA5 1974). But that conclusion does not follow. Ordinary preclusion law teaches that if a party to a court proceeding does not challenge an adverse decision, that decision can have preclusive effect in other cases, even if it would have been reviewed de novo. See Restatement (Second) of Judgments \xc2\xa728, Comment a and Illustration 1 (explaining that the failure to pursue an appeal does not undermine issue preclusion and including an example of an apparently unappealed district court\xe2\x80\x99s dismissal for failure to state a claim); cf. Federated Department Stores, Inc. v. Moitie,452 U.\xc2\xa0S. 394,398 (1981) (noting \xe2\x80\x9cthe res judicata consequences of a final, unappealed judgment on the merits\xe2\x80\x9d).

This case is also unlike Astoria, where a plaintiff claiming discrimination first went to an agency and then sued in court about the same alleged conduct. See 501 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 111. The Court concluded, quite sensibly, that the structure of that scheme indicated that the agency decision could not ground issue preclusion. When exhausting an administrative process is a prerequisite to suit in court, giving preclusive effect to the agency\xe2\x80\x99s determination in that very administrative process could render the judicial suit \xe2\x80\x9cstrictly pro forma.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; see also Elliott, supra, at 795\xe2\x80\x93796 (similar analysis). Here, if a party urged a district court reviewing a TTAB registration decision to give preclusive effect to the very TTAB decision under review, Astoria would apply. But that is not this case.

What matters here is that registration is not a prerequisite to an infringement action. Rather, it is a separate proceeding to decide separate rights. Neither is issue preclusion a one-way street. When a district court, as part of its judgment, decides an issue that overlaps with part of the TTAB\xe2\x80\x99s analysis, the TTAB gives preclusive effect to the court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 54a\xe2\x80\x9355a (giving preclusive effect to the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s ear-lier decision regarding SEALTIGHT\xe2\x80\x99s distinctiveness be-cause the issue \xe2\x80\x9cwas actually litigated and necessarily determined\xe2\x80\x9d).

Hargis also argues that allowing TTAB decisions to have issue-preclusive effect will adversely affect the registration process. Because of the TTAB\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98limited jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the narrowness of the issues\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d before it, Hargis contends, the Court should infer that TTAB proceedings are supposed to be more streamlined than infringement litigation. See Brief for Respondent 30 (quoting TTAB Manual \xc2\xa7402.01). But, the argument goes, if TTAB decisions can have issue-preclusive effect in infringement litigation, parties may spend more time and energy before the TTAB, thus bogging down the registration process. This concern does not change our conclusion. Issue preclusion is available unless it is \xe2\x80\x9cevident,\xe2\x80\x9d Astoria, supra, at 108, that Congress does not want it. Here, if a streamlined process in all registration matters was particularly dear to Congress, it would not have authorized de novo challenges for those \xe2\x80\x9cdissatisfied\xe2\x80\x9d with TTAB decisions.15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71071(b). Plenary review serves many functions, but ensuring a streamlined process is not one of them. Moreover, as explained below, for a great many registration decisions issue preclusion obviously will not apply because the ordinary elements will not be met. For those registrations, nothing we say today is relevant.

IV

At last we turn to whether there is a categorical reason why registration decisions can never meet the ordinary elements of issue preclusion, e.g., those elements set out in \xc2\xa727 of the Restatement (Second) of Judgments. Although many registrations will not satisfy those ordinary elements, that does not mean that none will. We agree with Professor McCarthy that issue preclusion applies where \xe2\x80\x9cthe issues in the two cases are indeed identical and the other rules of collateral estoppel are carefully observed.\xe2\x80\x9d 6 McCarthy \xc2\xa732:99, at 32\xe2\x80\x93244; see also 3 Gilson \xc2\xa711.08[4][i][iii][B], p. 11\xe2\x80\x93319 (\xe2\x80\x9cUltimately, Board decisions on likelihood of confusion .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. should be given preclusive effect on a case-by-case basis\xe2\x80\x9d).

A

The Eighth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s primary objection to issue preclusion was that the TTAB considers different factors than it does. Whereas the TTAB employs some or all of the DuPont factors to assess likelihood of confusion, the Eighth Circuit looks to similar, but not identical, factors identified in SquirtCo v. Seven-Up Co., 628 F. 2d 1086, 1091 (CA8 1980). The court\xe2\x80\x99s instinct was sound: \xe2\x80\x9c[I]ssues are not identical if the second action involves application of a different legal standard, even though the factual setting of both suits may be the same.\xe2\x80\x9d 18 C. Wright, A. Miller, & E. Cooper, Federal Practice & Procedure \xc2\xa74417, p. 449 (2d ed. 2002) (hereinafter Wright & Miller). Here, however, the same likelihood-of-confusion standard applies to both registration and infringement.

To begin with, it does not matter that registration and infringement are governed by different statutory provisions. Often a single standard is placed in different statutes; that does not foreclose issue preclusion. See, e.g., Smith v. Bayer Corp., 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 7). Neither does it matter that the TTAB and the Eighth Circuit use different factors to assess likelihood of confusion. For one thing, the factors are not fundamentally different, and \xe2\x80\x9c[m]inor variations in the application of what is in essence the same legal standard do not defeat preclusion.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___, n.\xc2\xa09 (slip op., at 12, n.\xc2\xa09). More important, if federal law provides a single standard, parties cannot escape preclusion simply by litigating anew in tribunals that apply that one standard differently. A contrary rule would encourage the very evils that issue preclusion helps to prevent.

The real question, therefore, is whether likelihood of confusion for purposes of registration is the same standard as likelihood of confusion for purposes of infringement. We conclude it is, for at least three reasons. First, the operative language is essentially the same; the fact that the registration provision separates \xe2\x80\x9clikely\xe2\x80\x9d from \xe2\x80\x9cto cause confusion, or to cause mistake, or to deceive\xe2\x80\x9d does not change that reality.[3] See 2 Gilson \xc2\xa75.01[2][a], at 5\xe2\x80\x9317 (explaining that \xe2\x80\x9cthe same statutory test\xe2\x80\x9d applies). Second, the likelihood-of-confusion language that Congress used in these Lanham Act provisions has been central to trademark registration since at least 1881. See Act of Mar. 3, 1881, ch. 138, \xc2\xa73,21Stat.503 (using a \xe2\x80\x9clikely to cause confusion\xe2\x80\x9d standard for registration). That could hardly have been by accident. And third, district courts can cancel registrations during infringement litigation, just as they can adjudicate infringement in suits seeking judicial review of registration decisions. See15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71119; 3 McCarthy \xc2\xa721:20. There is no reason to think that the same district judge in the same case should apply two separate standards of likelihood of confusion.

Hargis responds that the text is not actually the same because the registration provision asks whether the marks \xe2\x80\x9cresemble\xe2\x80\x9d each other,15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71052(d), while the infringement provision is directed towards the \xe2\x80\x9cuse in commerce\xe2\x80\x9d of the marks, \xc2\xa71114(1). Indeed, according to Hargis, the distinction between \xe2\x80\x9cresembl[ance]\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cuse\xe2\x80\x9d has been key to trademark law for over a century. There is some force to this argument. It is true that \xe2\x80\x9ca party opposing an application to register a mark before the Board often relies only on its federal registration, not on any common-law rights in usages not encompassed by its registration,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cthe Board typically analyzes the marks, goods, and channels of trade only as set forth in the application and in the opposer\xe2\x80\x99s registration, regardless of whether the actual usage of the marks by either party differs.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 23; see also id., at 5 (explaining that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Board typically reviews only the usages encompassed by the registration\xe2\x80\x9d) (citing 3 Gilson \xc2\xa79.03[2][a][ii]); 3 McCarthy \xc2\xa720:15, at 20\xe2\x80\x9345 (explaining that for registration \xe2\x80\x9cit is the mark as shown in the application and as used on the goods described in the application which must be considered, not the mark as actually used\xe2\x80\x9d). This means that unlike in infringement litigation, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Board\xe2\x80\x99s determination that a likelihood of confusion does or does not exist will not resolve the confusion issue with respect to non-disclosed usages.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 23.

Hargis\xe2\x80\x99 argument falls short, however, because it mistakes a reason not to apply issue preclusion in some or even many cases as a reason never to apply issue preclusion. Just because the TTAB does not always consider the same usages as a district court does, it does not follow that the Board applies a different standard to the usages it does consider.[4] If a mark owner uses its mark in ways that are materially the same as the usages included in its registration application, then the TTAB is deciding the same likelihood-of-confusion issue as a district court in infringement litigation. By contrast, if a mark owner uses its mark in ways that are materially unlike the usages in its application, then the TTAB is not deciding the same issue. Thus, if the TTAB does not consider the marketplace usage of the parties\xe2\x80\x99 marks, the TTAB\xe2\x80\x99s decision should \xe2\x80\x9chave no later preclusive effect in a suit where actual usage in the marketplace is the paramount issue.\xe2\x80\x9d 6 McCarthy \xc2\xa732:101, at 32\xe2\x80\x93246.

Materiality, of course, is essential\xe2\x80\x94trivial variations between the usages set out in an application and the use of a mark in the marketplace do not create different \xe2\x80\x9cissues,\xe2\x80\x9d just as trivial variations do not create different \xe2\x80\x9cmarks.\xe2\x80\x9d See generally 4 id., \xc2\xa723:50, at 23\xe2\x80\x93265 (explaining that \xe2\x80\x9cadding descriptive or non-distinctive\xe2\x80\x9d elements to another\xe2\x80\x99s mark generally will not negate confusion). Otherwise, a party could escape the preclusive effect of an adverse judgment simply by adding an immaterial feature to its mark. That is not the law. See, e.g., Restatement (Second) of Judgments \xc2\xa727, Comment c, at 252\xe2\x80\x93253 (explaining that \xe2\x80\x9cissue\xe2\x80\x9d must be understood broadly enough \xe2\x80\x9cto prevent repetitious litigation of what is essentially the same dispute\xe2\x80\x9d); United States v. Stauffer Chemical Co.,464 U.\xc2\xa0S. 165,172 (1984) (applying issue preclusion where a party sought to \xe2\x80\x9clitigate twice .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. an issue arising .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. from virtually identical facts\xe2\x80\x9d because the \xe2\x80\x9cfactual differences\xe2\x80\x9d were \xe2\x80\x9cof no legal significance\xe2\x80\x9d).

A fortiori, if the TTAB considers a different mark altogether, issue preclusion would not apply. Needless to say, moreover, if the TTAB has not decided the same issue as that before the district court, there is no reason why any deference would be warranted.

For a similar reason, the Eighth Circuit erred in holding that issue preclusion could not apply here because the TTAB relied too heavily on \xe2\x80\x9cappearance and sound.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 10a. Undoubtedly there are cases in which the TTAB places more weight on certain factors than it should. When that happens, an aggrieved party should seek judicial review. The fact that the TTAB may have erred, however, does not prevent preclusion. As Judge Colloton observed in dissent, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98issue preclusion prevent[s] relitigation of wrong decisions just as much as right ones.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 716 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1029 (quoting Clark v. Clark, 984 F.\xc2\xa02d 272, 273 (CA8 1993)); see also Restatement (Second) of Judgments \xc2\xa728, Comment j, at 284 (explaining that \xe2\x80\x9crefusal to give the first judgment preclusive effect should not .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. be based simply on a conclusion that [it] was patently erroneous\xe2\x80\x9d).

B

Hargis also argues that registration is categorically incompatible with issue preclusion because the TTAB uses procedures that differ from those used by district courts. Granted, \xe2\x80\x9c[r]edetermination of issues is warranted if there is reason to doubt the quality, extensiveness, or fairness of procedures followed in prior litigation.\xe2\x80\x9d Montana, 440 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 164, n.\xc2\xa011; see also Parklane Hosiery, 439 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 331, and n.\xc2\xa015 (similar). But again, this only suggests that sometimes issue preclusion might be inappropriate, not that it always is.

No one disputes that the TTAB and district courts use different procedures. Most notably, district courts feature live witnesses. Procedural differences, by themselves, however, do not defeat issue preclusion. Equity courts used different procedures than did law courts, but that did not bar issue preclusion. See id., at 333. Nor is there reason to think that the state agency in Elliott used procedures identical to those in federal court; nonetheless, the Court held that preclusion could apply. See 478 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 796\xe2\x80\x93799. Rather than focusing on whether procedural differences exist\xe2\x80\x94they often will\xe2\x80\x94the correct inquiry is whether the procedures used in the first proceeding were fundamentally poor, cursory, or unfair. See Montana, 440 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 164, n.\xc2\xa011.

Here, there is no categorical \xe2\x80\x9creason to doubt the quality, extensiveness, or fairness,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., of the agency\xe2\x80\x99s procedures. In large part they are exactly the same as in fed-eral court. See 37 CFR \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72.116(a), 2.122(a). For instance, although the \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he scope of discovery in Board proceedings .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is generally narrower than in court proceedings\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94reflecting the fact that there are often fewer usages at issue\xe2\x80\x94the TTAB has adopted almost the whole of Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 26. TTAB Manual \xc2\xa7402.01; see also id., \xc2\xa7401. It is conceivable, of course, that the TTAB\xe2\x80\x99s procedures may prove ill-suited for a particular issue in a particular case, e.g., a party may have tried to introduce material evidence but was prevented by the TTAB from doing so, or the TTAB\xe2\x80\x99s bar on live testimony may materially prejudice a party\xe2\x80\x99s ability to present its case. The ordinary law of issue preclusion, however, already accounts for those \xe2\x80\x9crare\xe2\x80\x9d cases where a \xe2\x80\x9ccompelling showing of unfairness\xe2\x80\x9d can be made. Restatement (Second) of Judgments \xc2\xa728, Comments g and j, at 283\xe2\x80\x93284.

The Eighth Circuit likewise erred by concluding that Hargis bore the burden of persuasion before the TTAB. B&B, the party opposing registration, bore the burden, see 37 CFR \xc2\xa72.116(b); TTAB Manual \xc2\xa7702.04(a), just as it did in the infringement action. Hargis does not defend the decision below on this ground.

C

Hargis also contends that the stakes for registration are so much lower than for infringement that issue preclusion should never apply to TTAB decisions. Issue preclusion may be inapt if \xe2\x80\x9cthe amount in controversy in the first action [was] so small in relation to the amount in controversy in the second that preclusion would be plainly unfair.\xe2\x80\x9d Restatement (Second) of Judgments \xc2\xa728, Comment j, at 283\xe2\x80\x93284. After all, \xe2\x80\x9c[f]ew .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. litigants would spend $50,000 to defend a $5,000 claim.\xe2\x80\x9d Wright & Miller \xc2\xa74423, at 612. Hargis is wrong, however, that this exception to issue preclusion applies to every registration. Tothe contrary: When registration is opposed, there isgood reason to think that both sides will take the matter seriously.

The benefits of registration are substantial. Registration is \xe2\x80\x9cprima facie evidence of the validity of the registered mark,\xe2\x80\x9d15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71057(b), and is a precondition for a mark to become \xe2\x80\x9cincontestable,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71065. Incontestability is a powerful protection. See, e.g., Park \xe2\x80\x99N Fly, Inc. v. Dollar Park & Fly, Inc.,469 U.\xc2\xa0S. 189,194 (1985) (holding that an incontestable mark cannot be challenged as merely descriptive); see also id., at 193 (explaining that \xe2\x80\x9cCon-gress determined that .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. \xe2\x80\x98trademarks should receive nationally the greatest protection that can be given them\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]mong the new protections created by the Lanham Act were the statutory provisions that allow a federally registered mark to become incontestable\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 1333, 79th Cong., 2d Sess., 6 (1946))).

The importance of registration is undoubtedly why Congress provided for de novo review of TTAB decisions in district court. It is incredible to think that a district court\xe2\x80\x99s adjudication of particular usages would not have preclusive effect in another district court. Why would unchallenged TTAB decisions be different? Congress\xe2\x80\x99 creation of this elaborate registration scheme, with so many important rights attached and backed up by plenary review, confirms that registration decisions can be weighty enough to ground issue preclusion.

V

For these reasons, the Eighth Circuit erred in this case. On remand, the court should apply the following rule: So long as the other ordinary elements of issue preclusion are met, when the usages adjudicated by the TTAB are materially the same as those before the district court, issue preclusion should apply.

The judgment of the United States Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

It is so ordered.

Justice Thomas, with whom Justice Scalia joins, dissenting.

The Court today applies a presumption that when Congress enacts statutes authorizing administrative agencies to resolve disputes in an adjudicatory setting, it intends those agency decisions to have preclusive effect in Article III courts. That presumption was first announced in poorly supported dictum in a 1991 decision of this Court, and we have not applied it since. Whatever the validity of that presumption with respect to statutes enacted after its creation, there is no justification for applying it to the Lanham Act, passed in 1946. Seeing no other reason to conclude that Congress implicitly authorized the decisions of the Trademark Trial and Appeal Board (TTAB) to have preclusive effect in a subsequent trademark infringement suit, I would affirm the decision of the Court of Appeals.

I

A

The presumption in favor of administrative preclusion the Court applies today was first announced in Astoria Fed. Sav. & Loan Assn. v. Solimino,501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 104,108 (1991). In that case, the Court confronted the question \xe2\x80\x9cwhether claimants under the Age Discrimination in Employment Act of 1967 [(ADEA)] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. are collaterally estopped to relitigate in federal court the judicially unreviewed findings of a state administrative agency made with respect to an age-discrimination claim.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 106. It answered that question in the negative, concluding that the availability of administrative preclusion was an issue of statutory construction and that the particular statute at issue \xe2\x80\x9ccarrie[d] an implication that the federal courts should recognize no [such] preclusion.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 108, 110.

Despite rejecting the availability of preclusion, the Court nevertheless, in dictum, announced a presumption in favor of giving preclusive effect to administrative determinations \xe2\x80\x9cwhere Congress has failed expressly or impliedly to evince any intention on the issue.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at110. That dictum rested on two premises. First, that \xe2\x80\x9cCon-gress is understood to legislate against a background of common-law adjudicatory principles.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 108. And, second, that the Court had \xe2\x80\x9clong favored application ofthe common-law doctrines of collateral estoppel (as to issues) and res judicata (as to claims) to those determinations of administrative bodies that have attained finality.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 107.

I do not quarrel with the first premise, but I have serious doubts about the second. The Court in Astoria offered only one decision predating the enactment of the ADEA to shore up its assertion that Congress had legislated against a background principle in favor of administrative preclusion\xe2\x80\x94United States v. Utah Constr. & Mining Co.,384 U.\xc2\xa0S. 394,422 (1966). See Astoria, supra, at 107.[1] And that decision cannot be read for the broad proposition asserted by the Court.

Like Astoria itself, Utah Construction discussed administrative preclusion only in dictum. The case arose out of a contract dispute between the United States and a private contractor. 384 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 400. The contract at issue contained a disputes clause providing for an administrative process by which \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98disputes concerning questions of fact arising under th[e] contract\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d would be decided by the contracting officer, subject to written appeal to the head of the department. Id., at 397\xe2\x80\x93398. The Wunderlich Act of 1954 likewise provided that such administrative factfinding would be \xe2\x80\x9cfinal and conclusive\xe2\x80\x9d in a later breach-of-contract action \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98unless the same is fra[u]dulent or capricious or arbitrary or so grossly erroneous as necessarily to imply bad faith, or is not supported by substantial evidence.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 399. Because both \xe2\x80\x9cthe disputes clause [of the contract] and the Wunderlich Act categorically state[d] that administrative findings on factual issues relevant to questions arising under the contract [would] be final and conclusive on the parties,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court required the lower courts to accept those findings. Id., at 419. Only after acknowledging that its decision \xe2\x80\x9crest[ed] upon the agreement of the parties as modified by the Wunderlich Act\xe2\x80\x9d did the Court go on to comment that the decision was \xe2\x80\x9charmonious with general principles of collateral estoppel.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 421.

To create a presumption based solely on dictum would be bad enough, but the principles Utah Construction referred to were far too equivocal to constitute \xe2\x80\x9clong-established and familiar\xe2\x80\x9d background principles of the common law of the sort on which we base our statutory inferences. Isbrandtsen Co. v. Johnson,343 U.\xc2\xa0S. 779,783 (1952). Although Utah Construction asserted that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen an administrative agency is acting in a judicial capacity and resolves disputed issues of fact properly before it which the parties have had an adequate opportunity to litigate, the courts have not hesitated to apply res judicata to enforce repose,\xe2\x80\x9d it admitted that \xe2\x80\x9ccourts have used language to the effect that res judicata principles do not apply to administrative proceedings.\xe2\x80\x9d 384 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 421\xe2\x80\x93422. These contradictory signals are not typically the stuff of which background rules of common law are made. Cf. Kirtsaeng v. John Wiley & Sons, Inc., 568 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2013) (slip op., at 17) (presuming that Congress intended to retain the \xe2\x80\x9cfirst sale\xe2\x80\x9d doctrine in copyright statutes based on that common-law doctrine\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cimpeccable historic pedigree\xe2\x80\x9d).

B

If the occasion had arisen in Astoria for the Court to examine the history of administrative preclusion, it would have discovered that the issue was far from settled.

At common law, principles of res judicata and collateral estoppel applied only to a decision by a \xe2\x80\x9ccourt of competent jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Aurora City v. West, 7 Wall. 82, 102 (1869); accord, Hopkins v. Lee, 6 Wheat. 109, 113 (1821); Restatement of Judgments \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa74, 7, and Comment f, pp.\xc2\xa020, 41, 45 (1942). That rule came with the corollary requirement that the court be \xe2\x80\x9clegally constituted\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94that is, a court \xe2\x80\x9cknown to and recognized by the law.\xe2\x80\x9d 2 H. Black, Law of Judgments \xc2\xa7516, p. 614 (1891). A court not \xe2\x80\x9clegally constituted\xe2\x80\x9d lacked jurisdiction to enter a legally binding judgment, and thus any such judgment could have no preclusive effect. Ibid.

Nineteenth century courts generally understood the term \xe2\x80\x9ccourt of competent jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d to include all courts with authority and jurisdiction conclusively to resolve a dispute. See J. Wells, A Treatise on the Doctrines of Res Judicata and Stare Decisis \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7422\xe2\x80\x93423, pp. 336\xe2\x80\x93338 (1878); 2 Black, supra, \xc2\xa7516, at 613\xe2\x80\x93614. Thus, courts of law, courts of equity, admiralty courts, and foreign courts could all satisfy the requirement of a \xe2\x80\x9c[c]ourt of competent jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Hopkins, 6 Wheat., at 113. This broad definition served the interest in finality that supports preclusion doctrines, without which \xe2\x80\x9can end could never be put to litigation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 114.

But however broadly \xe2\x80\x9c[c]ourt of competent jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d was defined, it would require quite a leap to say that the concept encompasses administrative agencies, which were recognized as categorically different from courts. E.g., Pearson v. Williams,202 U.\xc2\xa0S. 281 (1906); F. Cooper, Administrative Agencies and the Courts 241\xe2\x80\x93242 (1951) (taking the position that agencies \xe2\x80\x9care not courts, and their determinations are not judgments\xe2\x80\x9d). This distinction stems from the Constitution itself, which vests the \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial Power\xe2\x80\x9d not in administrative agencies, but in federal courts, whose independence is safeguarded by certain constitutional requirements. Art.\xc2\xa0III, \xc2\xa71. One of the consequences of this allocation of judicial power is that agencies possess limited ability to act in a judicial capacity in cases resolving traditional disputes between private parties. See infra, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312.

It is therefore unsurprising that federal courts\xe2\x80\x94including this Court\xe2\x80\x94have been far more hesitant than today\xe2\x80\x99s majority to extend common-law preclusion principles to decisions of administrative tribunals. In Pearson, for example, this Court declined to recognize any preclusive effect of a decision of an immigration board. 202 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 284\xe2\x80\x93285. Writing for the Court, Justice Holmes explained that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he board is an instrument of the executive power, not a court\xe2\x80\x9d; that it consisted of officials \xe2\x80\x9cwhose duties are declared to be administrative by\xe2\x80\x9d statute; and that \xe2\x80\x9c[d]ecisions of a similar type long have been recognized as decisions of the executive department, and cannot constitute res judicata in a technical sense.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

Other courts likewise declined to apply general preclusion principles to decisions of administrative agencies. For example, as late as 1947, the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit would rely on the \xe2\x80\x9cwell settled doctrine that res judicata and equitable estoppel do not ordinarily apply to decisions of administrative tribunals.\xe2\x80\x9d Churchill Tabernacle v. FCC, 160 F.\xc2\xa02d 244, 246 (1947).

The Restatement of Judgments also reflected this practice: It contained no provision for administrative preclusion and explained that it would not address \xe2\x80\x9cthe effect of the decisions of administrative tribunals.\xe2\x80\x9d Restatement of Judgments, Scope Note, at 2. It rejected the idea of any consistent practice in favor of administrative preclusion, noting that \xe2\x80\x9cthe question whether the decisions of a particular tribunal are binding in subsequent controversies depends upon the character of the tribunal and the nature of its procedure and the construction of the statute creating the tribunal and conferring powers upon it.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

Consistent with that comment, federal courts approved of administrative preclusion in narrow circumstances arguably involving only claims against the Government, over which Congress exercises a broader measure of control.[2] In the 19th century, for instance, this Court effectively gave preclusive effect to the decisions of the U.\xc2\xa0S. Land Department with respect to land patents when it held such patents unreviewable in federal court \xe2\x80\x9cfor mere errors of judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d Smelting Co. v. Kemp,104 U.\xc2\xa0S. 636,646 (1882) (\xe2\x80\x9cA patent, in a court of law, is conclusive as to all matters properly determined by the Land Department\xe2\x80\x9d). Commentators explained that these cases could not truly be understood to involve an application of res judicata or collateral estoppel\xe2\x80\x94for, after all, administrative agencies are not courts\xe2\x80\x94but rather a \xe2\x80\x9cspecies of equitable estoppel.\xe2\x80\x9d Cooper, supra, at 242; see also 2 A. Freeman, Law of Judgments \xc2\xa7633, p. 1335 (5th ed. rev. 1925) (explaining that \xe2\x80\x9cthe immunity from judicial review\xe2\x80\x9d for certain administrative decisions was \xe2\x80\x9cnot based upon the doctrine of res judicata nor .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. governed by exactly the same rules\xe2\x80\x9d). As one commentator put it, res judicata could \xe2\x80\x9cnot apply, in any strict or technical sense, to the decisions of administrative agencies.\xe2\x80\x9d Cooper, supra, at 241.

This history undercuts any suggestion in Utah Construction that administrative preclusion was widely accepted at common law. Accordingly, at least for statutes passed before Astoria, I would reject the presumption of administrative preclusion.[3]

II

In light of this history, I cannot agree with the majority\xe2\x80\x99s decision to apply administrative preclusion in the context of the Lanham Act.[4] To start, the Lanham Act was en-acted in 1946, 20 years before this Court said\xe2\x80\x94even in dic-tum\xe2\x80\x94that administrative preclusion was an established common-law principle. Thus, even if one thought that the dictum in Utah Construction were sufficient to establish a common-law principle in favor of preclusion, that conclusion would not warrant applying Astoria\xe2\x80\x99s presumption to this enactment from the 1940\xe2\x80\x99s. And, construing the Act on its own terms, I see no reason to conclude that Congress intended administrative preclusion to apply to TTAB findings of fact in a subsequent trademark infringement suit. The Act says nothing to indicate such an intent, and several features of the Act support the contrary inference.

The first feature indicating that Congress did not intend preclusion to apply is the limited authority the Act gives the TTAB. The Act authorizes the TTAB only to \xe2\x80\x9cdetermine and decide the respective rights of [trademark] registration,\xe2\x80\x9d15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71067(a), thereby withholding any authority from the TTAB to \xe2\x80\x9cdetermine the right to use\xe2\x80\x9d a trademark or to \xe2\x80\x9cdecide broader questions of infringement or unfair competition,\xe2\x80\x9d TTAB Manual of Procedure \xc2\xa7102.01 (2014). This limited job description indicates that TTAB\xe2\x80\x99s conclusions regarding registration were never meant to become decisive\xe2\x80\x94through application of administrative preclusion\xe2\x80\x94in subsequent infringement suits. See15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71115(a) (providing that registration of a mark \xe2\x80\x9cshall be prima facie evidence of the validity of the registered mark\xe2\x80\x9d but \xe2\x80\x9cshall not preclude another person from proving any legal or equitable defense or defect\xe2\x80\x9d). Giving preclusive effect to the TTAB\xe2\x80\x99s decision on likelihood of confusion would be an end-run around the statutory limitation on its authority, as all parties agree that likelihood of confusion is the central issue in a subsequent infringement suit.

A second indication that Congress did not intend administrative preclusion to apply is the Lanham Act\xe2\x80\x99s provision for judicial review. After the TTAB issues a registration decision, a party \xe2\x80\x9cwho is dissatisfied with the decision\xe2\x80\x9d may either appeal to the Federal Circuit or file a civil action in district court seeking review. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71071(a)(1), (b)(1).[5] And it is undisputed that a civil action in district court would entail de\xc2\xa0novo review of the TTAB\xe2\x80\x99s decision. Ante, at 5. Although under ordinary preclusion principles \xe2\x80\x9cthe failure to pursue an appeal does not undermine issue preclusion,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 13, the availability of de\xc2\xa0novo judicial review of an administrative decision does. That is true both because the judicial review afforded by the Act marks the first opportunity for consideration of the issue by an Article III court and because Congress has deviated from the usual practice of affording deference to the factfindings of an initial tribunal in affording de\xc2\xa0novo review of the TTAB\xe2\x80\x99s decisions.

The decision to provide this de\xc2\xa0novo review is even more striking in light of the historical background of the choice: Congress passed the Lanham Act the same year it passed the Administrative Procedure Act, following a lengthy period of disagreement in the courts about what deference administrative findings of fact were entitled to receive on direct review. The issue had been the subject of debate for over 50 years, with varying results. See generally 2 J. Dickinson, Administrative Justice and the Supremacy of Law 39\xe2\x80\x9375 (1927). Sometimes this Court refused to review factual determinations of administrative agencies at all, Smelting Co., 104 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 640, 646, and sometimes it allowed lower courts to engage in essentially de\xc2\xa0novo review of factual determinations, see ICC v. Alabama Midland R. Co.,168 U.\xc2\xa0S. 144,174 (1897); Reckendorfer v. Faber,92 U.\xc2\xa0S. 347\xe2\x80\x93355 (1876).

In the early 20th century, the Court began to move toward substantial-evidence review of administrative determinations involving mixed questions of law and fact, ICC v. Union Pacific R. Co.,222 U.\xc2\xa0S. 541\xe2\x80\x93548 (1912), but reserved the authority to review de\xc2\xa0novo any so-called \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional facts.\xe2\x80\x9d Crowell v. Benson,285 U.\xc2\xa0S. 22\xe2\x80\x9363 (1932). Courts then struggled to determine the boundary between jurisdictional and nonjurisdictional facts, and thus to determine the appropriate standard of review for administrative decisions. See, e.g., Estep v. United States,327 U.\xc2\xa0S. 114,142 (1946) (Frankfurter, J., concurring in result) (noting the \xe2\x80\x9ccasuistic difficulties spawned\xe2\x80\x9d in Crowell and the \xe2\x80\x9cattritions of that case through later decisions\xe2\x80\x9d). Although Congress provided for substantial-evidence review in the Administrative Procedure Act,5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7706(2)(E), it required de\xc2\xa0novo review in the Lanham Act.

I need not take a side in this historical debate about the proper level of review for administrative findings of fact to conclude that its existence provides yet another reason to doubt that Congress intended administrative preclusionto apply to the Lanham Act.

III

In addition to being unsupported by our precedents or historical evidence, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s application of administrative preclusion raises serious constitutional concerns.

A

Executive agencies derive their authority from Article II of the Constitution, which vests \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he executive power\xe2\x80\x9d in \xe2\x80\x9ca President of the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa71, cl. 1. Executive agencies are thus part of the political branches of Government and make decisions \xe2\x80\x9cnot by fixed rules of law, but by the application of governmental discretion or pol-icy.\xe2\x80\x9d Dickinson, supra, at 35\xe2\x80\x9336; see, e.g., Motor Vehicle Mfrs. Assn. of United States, Inc. v. State Farm Mut. Automobile Ins. Co.,463 U.\xc2\xa0S. 29,59 (1983) (Rehnquist, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part) (An agency \xe2\x80\x9cis entitled to assess administrative records and evaluate priorities in light of the philosophy of the administration\xe2\x80\x9d). They are not constituted to exercise \xe2\x80\x9cindependent judgment,\xe2\x80\x9d but to be responsive to the pressures of the political branches. Perez v. Mortgage Bankers Assn., ante, at 8 (Thomas,\xc2\xa0J., concurring in judgment).

Because federal administrative agencies are part of the Executive Branch, it is not clear that they have power to adjudicate claims involving core private rights. Under our Constitution, the \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial power\xe2\x80\x9d belongs to Article III courts and cannot be shared with the Legislature or the Executive. Stern v. Marshall, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2011) (slip op., at 16\xe2\x80\x9317); see also Perez, ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x9311 (opinion of Thomas,\xc2\xa0J.). And some historical evidence suggests that the adjudication of core private rights is a function that can be performed only by Article III courts, at least absent the consent of the parties to adjudication in another forum. See Nelson, Adjudication in the Political Branches, 107 Colum. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 559, 561\xe2\x80\x93574 (2007) (hereinafter Nelson); see also Department of Transportation v. Association of American Railroads, ante, at 4 (Thomas,\xc2\xa0J., concurring in judgment) (explaining that \xe2\x80\x9cthere are certain core functions\xe2\x80\x9d that require the exercise of a particular constitutional power and that only one branch can constitutionally perform).

To the extent that administrative agencies could, consistent with the Constitution, function as courts, they might only be able to do so with respect to claims involving public or quasi-private rights. See Northern Pipeline Constr. Co. v. Marathon Pipe Line Co.,458 U.\xc2\xa0S. 50\xe2\x80\x9370 (1982) (plurality opinion); see also Nelson 561\xe2\x80\x93574; Dickinson, supra, at 6. Public rights are those belonging to the public as a whole, see Nelson 566, whereas quasi-private rights, or statutory entitlements, are those \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98privileges\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98franchises\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d that are bestowed by the government on individuals, id., at 567; see, e.g., Ex parte Bakelite Corp.,279 U.\xc2\xa0S. 438,451 (1929) (discussing claims \xe2\x80\x9carising between the government and others, which from their nature do not require judicial determination and yet are susceptible of it\xe2\x80\x9d).

The historical treatment of administrative preclusion is consistent with this understanding. As discussed above, most administrative adjudications that were given preclusive effect in Article III courts involved quasi-private rights like land grants. See Smelting Co., 104 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 646. And in the context of land grants, this Court recognized that once \xe2\x80\x9ctitle had passed from the government,\xe2\x80\x9d a more complete form of judicial review was available because \xe2\x80\x9cthe question became one of private right.\xe2\x80\x9d Johnson v. Towsley, 13 Wall. 72, 87 (1871).

It is true that, in the New Deal era, the Court sometimes gave preclusive effect to administrative findings of fact in tax cases, which could be construed to implicate private rights. See, e.g., Sunshine Anthracite Coal Co. v. Adkins,310 U.\xc2\xa0S. 381\xe2\x80\x93404 (1940); Tait v. Western Maryland R. Co.,289 U.\xc2\xa0S. 620\xe2\x80\x93624 (1933). But administrative tax determinations may simply have enjoyed a special historical status, in which case this practice might be best understood as a limited deviation from a general distinction between public and private rights. See Nelson 588\xe2\x80\x93590.

B

Trademark registration under the Lanham Act has the characteristics of a quasi-private right. Registration is a creature of the Lanham Act, which \xe2\x80\x9cconfers important legal rights and benefits on trademark owners who register their marks.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 3 (internal quotation marks omitted). Because registration is merely a statutory government entitlement, no one disputes that the TTAB may constitutionally adjudicate a registration claim. See Stern, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 19); Nelson 568\xe2\x80\x93569.

By contrast, the right to adopt and exclusively use a trademark appears to be a private property right that \xe2\x80\x9chas been long recognized by the common law and the chancery courts of England and of this country.\xe2\x80\x9d Trade-Mark Cases,100 U.\xc2\xa0S. 82,92 (1879). As this Court explained when addressing Congress\xe2\x80\x99 first trademark statute, enacted in 1870, the exclusive right to use a trademark \xe2\x80\x9cwas not created by the act of Congress, and does not now depend upon it for its enforcement.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. \xe2\x80\x9cThe whole system of trade-mark property and the civil remedies for its protection existed long anterior to that act, and have remained in full force since its passage.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Thus, it appears that the trademark infringement suit at issue in this case might be of a type that must be decided by \xe2\x80\x9cArticle III judges in Article III courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Stern, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 18).

The majority, however, would have Article III courts decide infringement claims where the central issue\xe2\x80\x94whether there is a likelihood of consumer confusion between two trademarks\xe2\x80\x94has already been decided by an executive agency. This raises two potential constitutional concerns. First, it may deprive a trademark holder of the opportunity to have a core private right adjudicated in an Article III court. See id., at ___ (slip op., at 21). Second, it may effect a transfer of a core attribute of the judicial power to an executive agency. Cf. Perez, ante, at 10\xe2\x80\x9312 (opinion of Thomas,\xc2\xa0J.) (explaining that interpretation of regulations having the force and effect of law is likely a core attribute of the judicial power that cannot be transferred to an executive agency). Administrative preclusion thus threatens to \xe2\x80\x9csap the judicial power as it exists under the Federal Constitution, and to establish a government of a bureaucratic character alien to our own system, wher-ever fundamental rights depend .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. upon the facts, and finality as to facts becomes in effect finality in law.\xe2\x80\x9d Crowell, 285 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 57.

At a minimum, this practice raises serious questions that the majority does not adequately confront. The majority does not address the distinction between private rights and public rights or the nature of the power exercised by an administrative agency when adjudicating facts in private-rights disputes. And it fails to consider whether applying administrative preclusion to a core factual determination in a private-rights dispute comports with the separation of powers.

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

I would hold that the TTAB\xe2\x80\x99s trademark-registration decisions are not entitled to preclusive effect in a subsequent infringement suit. The common law does not support a general presumption in favor of administrative preclusion for statutes passed before this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Astoria, and the text, structure, and history of the Lanham Act provide no support for such preclusion. I disagree with the majority\xe2\x80\x99s willingness to endorse Astoria\xe2\x80\x99s unfounded presumption and to apply it to an adjudication in a private-rights dispute, as that analysis raises serious constitutional questions. Because I can resolve this case on statutory grounds, however, I leave these questions for another day. I respectfully dissent.
