TC HEARTLAND LLC v. KRAFT FOODS GROUP BRANDS LLC

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the federal circuit

\n

No. 16\xe2\x80\x93341.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 27, 2017\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 22, 2017

\n

The patent venue statute,\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71400(b), provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny civil action for patent infringement may be brought in the judicial district where the defendant resides, or where the defendant has committed acts of infringement and has a regular and established place of business.\xe2\x80\x9d In Fourco Glass Co. v. Transmirra Products Corp.,\n\n353 U.\xc2\xa0S. 222\n, this Court concluded that for purposes of \xc2\xa71400(b) a domestic corporation \xe2\x80\x9cresides\xe2\x80\x9d only in its State of incorporation, rejecting the argument that \xc2\xa71400(b) incorporates the broader definition of corporate \xe2\x80\x9cresidence\xe2\x80\x9d contained in the general venue statute,\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71391(c). Congress has not amended \xc2\xa71400(b) since Fourco, but it has twice amended \xc2\xa71391, which now provides that, \xe2\x80\x9c[e]xcept as otherwise provided by law\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c[f]or all venue purposes,\xe2\x80\x9d a corporation \xe2\x80\x9cshall be deemed to reside, if a defendant, in any judicial district in which such defendant is subject to the court\xe2\x80\x99s personal jurisdiction with respect to the civil action in question.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71391(a), (c).

\n

Respondent filed a patent infringement suit in the District Court for the District of Delaware against petitioner, a competitor that is organized under Indiana law and headquartered in Indiana but ships the allegedly infringing products into Delaware. Petitioner moved to transfer venue to a District Court in Indiana, claiming that venue was improper in Delaware. Citing Fourco, petitioner argued that it did not \xe2\x80\x9cresid[e]\xe2\x80\x9d in Delaware and had no \xe2\x80\x9cregular and established place of business\xe2\x80\x9d in Delaware under \xc2\xa71400(b). The District Court rejected these arguments. The Federal Circuit denied a petition for a writ of mandamus, concluding that \xc2\xa71391(c) supplies the definition of \xe2\x80\x9cresides\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa71400(b). The Federal Circuit reasoned that because petitioner resided in Delaware under \xc2\xa71391(c), it also resided there under \xc2\xa71400(b).

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0As applied to domestic corporations, \xe2\x80\x9creside[nce]\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa71400(b) refers only to the State of incorporation. The amendments to \xc2\xa71391 did not modify the meaning of \xc2\xa71400(b) as interpreted by Fourco. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0The venue provision of the Judiciary Act of 1789 covered patent cases as well as other civil suits. Stonite Products Co. v. Melvin Lloyd Co.,\n\n315 U.\xc2\xa0S. 561\n. In 1897, Congress enacted a patent specific venue statute. This new statute (\xc2\xa71400(b)\xe2\x80\x99s predecessor) permitted suit in the district of which the defendant was an \xe2\x80\x9cinhabitant\xe2\x80\x9d or in which the defendant both maintained a \xe2\x80\x9cregular and established place of business\xe2\x80\x9d and committed an act of infringement.\n\n29Stat.\n695. A corporation at that time was understood to \xe2\x80\x9cinhabit\xe2\x80\x9d only the State of incorporation. This Court addressed the scope of \xc2\xa71400(b)\xe2\x80\x99s predecessor in Stonite, concluding that it constituted \xe2\x80\x9cthe exclusive provision controlling venue in patent infringement proceedings\xe2\x80\x9d and thus was not supplemented or modified by the general venue provisions. 315 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 563.

\n

In 1948, Congress recodified the patent venue statute as \xc2\xa71400(b). That provision, which remains unaltered today, uses \xe2\x80\x9cresides\xe2\x80\x9d instead of \xe2\x80\x9cinhabit[s].\xe2\x80\x9d At the same time, Congress also enacted the general venue statute, \xc2\xa71391, which defined \xe2\x80\x9cresidence\xe2\x80\x9d for corporate defendants. In Fourco, this Court reaffirmed Stonite\xe2\x80\x99s holding, observing that Congress enacted \xc2\xa71400(b) as a standalone venue statute and that nothing in the 1948 recodification evidenced an intent to alter that status, even the fact that \xc2\xa71391(c) by \xe2\x80\x9cits terms\xe2\x80\x9d embraced \xe2\x80\x9call actions,\xe2\x80\x9d 353 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 228. The Court also concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cresides\xe2\x80\x9d in the recodified version bore the same meaning as \xe2\x80\x9cinhabit[s]\xe2\x80\x9d in the pre-1948 version. See id., at 226.

\n

This landscape remained effectively unchanged until 1988, when Congress amended the general venue statute, \xc2\xa71391(c). The revised provision stated that it applied \xe2\x80\x9c[f]or purposes of venue under this chapter.\xe2\x80\x9d In VE Holding Corp. v. Johnson Gas Appliance Co., 917 F.\xc2\xa02d 1574, 1578, the Federal Circuit held that, in light of this amendment, \xc2\xa71391(c) established the definition for all other venue statutes under the same \xe2\x80\x9cchapter,\xe2\x80\x9d including \xc2\xa71400(b). In 2011, Congress adopted the current version of \xc2\xa71391, which provides that its general definition applies \xe2\x80\x9c[f]or all venue purposes.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federal Circuit reaffirmed VE Holding in the case below. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0In Fourco, this Court definitively and unambiguously held that the word \xe2\x80\x9creside[nce]\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa71400(b), as applied to domestic corporations, refers only to the State of incorporation. Because Congress has not amended \xc2\xa71400(b) since Fourco, and neither party asks the Court to reconsider that decision, the only question here is whether Congress changed \xc2\xa71400(b)\xe2\x80\x99s meaning when it amended \xc2\xa71391. When Congress intends to effect a change of that kind, it ordinarily provides a relatively clear indication of its intent in the amended provision\xe2\x80\x99s text. No such indication appears in the current version of \xc2\xa71391.

\n

Respondent points out that the current \xc2\xa71391(c) provides a default rule that, on its face, applies without exception \xe2\x80\x9c[f]or all venue purposes.\xe2\x80\x9d But the version at issue in Fourco similarly provided a default rule that applied \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98for venue purposes,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 353 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 223, and those phrasings are not materially different in this context. The addition of the word \xe2\x80\x9call\xe2\x80\x9d to the already comprehensive provision does not suggest that Congress intended the Court to reconsider its decision in Fourco. Any argument based on this language is even weaker now than it was when the Court rejected it in Fourco. Fourco held that \xc2\xa71400(b) retained a meaning distinct from the default definition contained in \xc2\xa71391(c), even though the latter, by its terms, included no exceptions. The current version of \xc2\xa71391 includes a saving clause, which expressly states that the provision does not apply when \xe2\x80\x9cotherwise provided by law,\xe2\x80\x9d thus making explicit the qualification that the Fourco Court found implicit in the statute. Finally, there is no indication that Congress in 2011 ratified the Federal Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision in VE Holding. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

821 F.\xc2\xa03d 1338, reversed and remanded.

\n

TC HEARTLAND LLC, PETITIONER v. KRAFTFOODS GROUP BRANDS LLC

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the federal circuit

\n

[May 22, 2017]

\n

\n

Justice Thomas delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

The question presented in this case is where proper venue lies for a patent infringement lawsuit brought against a domestic corporation. The patent venue statute,\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71400(b), provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny civil action for patent infringement may be brought in the judicial district where the defendant resides, or where the defendant has committed acts of infringement and has a regular and established place of business.\xe2\x80\x9d In Fourco Glass Co. v. Transmirra Products Corp.,\n\n353 U.\xc2\xa0S. 222,\n226 (1957)\n, this Court concluded that for purposes of \xc2\xa71400(b) a domestic corporation \xe2\x80\x9cresides\xe2\x80\x9d only in its State of incorporation.

\n

In reaching that conclusion, the Court rejected the argument that \xc2\xa71400(b) incorporates the broader definition of corporate \xe2\x80\x9cresidence\xe2\x80\x9d contained in the general venue statute,\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71391(c). 353 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 228. Congress has not amended \xc2\xa71400(b) since this Court construed it in Fourco, but it has amended \xc2\xa71391 twice. Section 1391 now provides that, \xe2\x80\x9c[e]xcept as otherwise provided by law\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c[f]or all venue purposes,\xe2\x80\x9d a corporation \xe2\x80\x9cshall be deemed to reside, if a defendant, in any judicial district in which such defendant is subject to the court\xe2\x80\x99s personal jurisdiction with respect to the civil action in question.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71391(a), (c). The issue in this case is whether that definition supplants the definition announced in Fourco and allows a plaintiff to bring a patent infringement lawsuit against a corporation in any district in which the corporation is subject to personal jurisdiction. We conclude that the amendments to \xc2\xa71391 did not modify the meaning of \xc2\xa71400(b) as interpreted by Fourco. We therefore hold that a domestic corporation \xe2\x80\x9cresides\xe2\x80\x9d only in its State of incorporation for purposes of the patent venue statute.

\n

I

\n

Petitioner, which is organized under Indiana law and headquartered in Indiana, manufactures flavored drink mixes.[1] Respondent, which is organized under Delaware law and has its principal place of business in Illinois, is a competitor in the same market. As relevant here, respondent sued petitioner in the District Court for the District of Delaware, alleging that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s products infringed one of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s patents. Although petitioner is not registered to conduct business in Delaware and has no meaningful local presence there, it does ship the al-legedly infringing products into the State.

\n

Petitioner moved to dismiss the case or transfer venue to the District Court for the Southern District of Indiana, arguing that venue was improper in Delaware. See\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71406. Citing Fourco\xe2\x80\x99s holding that a corporation resides only in its State of incorporation for patent infringement suits, petitioner argued that it did not \xe2\x80\x9cresid[e]\xe2\x80\x9d in Delaware under the first clause of \xc2\xa71400(b). It further argued that it had no \xe2\x80\x9cregular and established place of business\xe2\x80\x9d in Delaware under the second clause of \xc2\xa71400(b). Relying on Circuit precedent, the District Court rejected these arguments, 2015 WL 5613160 (D Del., Sept. 24, 2015), and the Federal Circuit denied a petition for a writ of mandamus, In\xc2\xa0re TC Heartland LLC, 821 F.\xc2\xa03d 1338 (2016). The Federal Circuit concluded that subsequent statutory amendments had effectively amended \xc2\xa71400(b) as construed in Fourco, with the result that \xc2\xa71391(c) now supplies the definition of \xe2\x80\x9cresides\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa71400(b). 821 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1341\xe2\x80\x931343. Under this logic, because the District of Delaware could exercise personal jurisdiction over petitioner, petitioner resided in Delaware under \xc2\xa71391(c) and, therefore, under \xc2\xa71400(b). We granted certiorari, 580 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2016), and now reverse.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

The history of the relevant statutes provides important context for the issue in this case. The Judiciary Act of 1789 permitted a plaintiff to file suit in a federal district court if the defendant was \xe2\x80\x9can inhabitant\xe2\x80\x9d of that district or could be \xe2\x80\x9cfound\xe2\x80\x9d for service of process in that district. Act of Sept. 24, 1789, \xc2\xa711,\n\n1Stat.\n79. The Act covered patent cases as well as other civil suits. Stonite Products Co. v. Melvin Lloyd Co.,\n\n315 U.\xc2\xa0S. 561,\n563 (1942)\n. In 1887, Congress amended the statute to permit suit only in the district of which the defendant was an inhabitant or, in diversity cases, of which either the plaintiff or defendant was an inhabitant. See Act of Mar. 3, 1887, \xc2\xa71, 24 Stat. 552; see also Stonite, supra, at 563\xe2\x80\x93564.

\n

This Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in In re Hohorst,\n\n150 U.\xc2\xa0S. 653\n\xe2\x80\x93662 (1893), arguably suggested that the 1887 Act did not apply to patent cases. As a result, while some courts continued to apply the Act to patent cases, others refused to do so and instead permitted plaintiffs to bring suit (in line with the pre-1887 regime) anywhere a defendant could be found for service of process. See Stonite, supra, at 564\xe2\x80\x93565. In 1897, Congress resolved the confusion by enacting a patent specific venue statute. See Act of Mar. 3, 1897, ch. 395,\n\n29Stat.\n695. In so doing, it \xe2\x80\x9cplaced pat-ent infringement cases in a class by themselves, outside the scope of general venue legislation.\xe2\x80\x9d Brunette Machine Works, Ltd. v. Kockum Industries, Inc.,\n\n406 U.\xc2\xa0S. 706,\n713 (1972)\n. This new statute (\xc2\xa71400(b)\xe2\x80\x99s predecessor) permitted suit in the district of which the defendant was an \xe2\x80\x9cinhabitant,\xe2\x80\x9d or a district in which the defendant both maintained a \xe2\x80\x9cregular and established place of business\xe2\x80\x9d and committed an act of infringement.\n\n29Stat.\n695. At the time, a corporation was understood to \xe2\x80\x9cinhabit\xe2\x80\x9d only the State in which it was incorporated. Shaw v. Quincy Mining Co.,\n\n145 U.\xc2\xa0S. 444\n\xe2\x80\x93450 (1892).

\n

The Court addressed the scope of \xc2\xa71400(b)\xe2\x80\x99s predecessor in Stonite. In that case, the two defendants inhabited different districts within a single State. The plaintiff sought to sue them both in the same district, invoking a then governing general venue statute that, if applicable, permitted it to do so. 315 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 562\xe2\x80\x93563. This Court rejected the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s venue choice on the ground that the patent venue statute constituted \xe2\x80\x9cthe exclusive provision controlling venue in patent infringement proceedings\xe2\x80\x9d and thus was not supplemented or modified by the general venue provisions. Id., at 563. In the Court\xe2\x80\x99s view, the patent venue statute \xe2\x80\x9cwas adopted to define the exact jurisdiction of the federal courts in actions to enforce patent rights,\xe2\x80\x9d a purpose that would be undermined by interpreting it \xe2\x80\x9cto dovetail with the general provisions relating to the venue of civil suits.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 565\xe2\x80\x93566. The Court thus held that the patent venue statute \xe2\x80\x9calone should control venue in patent infringement proceedings.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 566.

\n

In 1948, Congress recodified the patent venue statute as \xc2\xa71400(b). See Act of June 25, 1948,\n\n62Stat.\n936. The recodified provision, which remains unaltered today, states that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny civil action for patent infringement may be brought in the judicial district where the defendant resides, or where the defendant has committed acts of infringement and has a regular and established place of business.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71400(b) (1952 ed.). This version differs from the previous one in that it uses \xe2\x80\x9cresides\xe2\x80\x9d instead of \xe2\x80\x9cinhabit[s].\xe2\x80\x9d At the same time, Congress also enacted the general venue statute, \xc2\xa71391, which defined \xe2\x80\x9cresidence\xe2\x80\x9d for corporate defendants. That provision stated that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] corporation may be sued in any judicial district in which it is incorporated or licensed to do business or is doing business, and such judicial district shall be regarded as the residence of such corporation for venue purposes.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71391(c) (1952 ed.).

\n

Following the 1948 legislation, courts reached differing conclusions regarding whether \xc2\xa71400(b)\xe2\x80\x99s use of the word \xe2\x80\x9cresides\xe2\x80\x9d incorporated \xc2\xa71391(c)\xe2\x80\x99s definition of \xe2\x80\x9cresidence.\xe2\x80\x9d See Fourco, 353 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 224, n.\xc2\xa03 (listing cases). In Fourco, this Court reviewed a decision of the Second Circuitholding that \xc2\xa71391(c) defined residence for purposes of \xc2\xa71400(b), \xe2\x80\x9cjust as that definition is properly .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. incorporated into other sections of the venue chapter.\xe2\x80\x9d Trans-mirra Prods. Corp. v. Fourco Glass Co., 233 F.\xc2\xa02d 885, 886 (1956). This Court squarely rejected that interpretation, reaffirming Stonite\xe2\x80\x99s holding that \xc2\xa71400(b) \xe2\x80\x9cis the sole and exclusive provision controlling venue in patent infringement actions, and .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is not to be supplemented by .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. \xc2\xa71391(c).\xe2\x80\x9d 353 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 229. The Court observed that Congress enacted \xc2\xa71400(b) as a standalone venue statute and that nothing in the 1948 recodification evidenced an intent to alter that status. The fact that \xc2\xa71391(c) by \xe2\x80\x9cits terms\xe2\x80\x9d embraced \xe2\x80\x9call actions\xe2\x80\x9d was not enough to overcome the fundamental point that Congress designed \xc2\xa71400(b) to be \xe2\x80\x9ccomplete, independent and alone controlling in its sphere.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 228.

\n

The Court also concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cresides\xe2\x80\x9d in the recodified version of \xc2\xa71400(b) bore the same meaning as \xe2\x80\x9cinhabit[s]\xe2\x80\x9d in the pre-1948 version. See id., at 226 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he [w]ords \xe2\x80\x98inhabitant\xe2\x80\x99 and \xe2\x80\x98resident,\xe2\x80\x99 as respects venue, are synonymous\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). The substitution of \xe2\x80\x9cresides\xe2\x80\x9d for \xe2\x80\x9cinhabit[s]\xe2\x80\x9d thus did not suggest any alteration in the venue rules for corporations in patent cases. Accordingly, \xc2\xa71400(b) continued to apply to domestic corporations in the same way it always had: They were subject to venue only in their States of incorporation. See ibid. (The use of \xe2\x80\x9cresides\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cnegat[es] any intention to make corporations suable, in patent infringement cases, where they are merely \xe2\x80\x98doing business,\xe2\x80\x99 because those synonymous words [\xe2\x80\x9cinhabitant\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cresident\xe2\x80\x9d] mean domicile and, in respect of corporations, mean the state of incorporation only\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

B

\n

This landscape remained effectively unchanged until 1988, when Congress amended the general venue statute, \xc2\xa71391(c), to provide that \xe2\x80\x9c[f]or purposes of venue under this chapter, a defendant that is a corporation shall be deemed to reside in any judicial district in which it is subject to personal jurisdiction at the time the action is commenced.\xe2\x80\x9d Judicial Improvements and Access to Justice Act, \xc2\xa71013(a),\n\n102Stat.\n4669. The Federal Circuit in VE Holding Corp. v. Johnson Gas Appliance Co., 917 F.\xc2\xa02d 1574 (1990), announced its view of the effect of this amendment on the meaning of the patent venue statute. The court reasoned that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9c[f]or purposes of venue under this chapter\xe2\x80\x9d was \xe2\x80\x9cexact and classic language of incorporation,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 1579, and that \xc2\xa71391(c) accord-ingly established the definition for all other venue statutes under the same \xe2\x80\x9cchapter.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1580. Because \xc2\xa71400(b) fell within the relevant chapter, the Federal Circuit concluded that \xc2\xa71391(c), \xe2\x80\x9con its face,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cclearly applies to \xc2\xa71400(b), and thus redefines the meaning of the term \xe2\x80\x98resides\xe2\x80\x99 in that section.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1578.

\n

Following VE Holding, no new developments occurred until Congress adopted the current version of \xc2\xa71391 in 2011 (again leaving \xc2\xa71400(b) unaltered). See Federal Courts Jurisdiction and Venue Clarification Act of 2011, \xc2\xa7202,\n\n125Stat.\n763. Section 1391(a) now provides that, \xe2\x80\x9c[e]xcept as otherwise provided by law,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cthis section shall govern the venue of all civil actions brought in district courts of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d And \xc2\xa71391(c)(2), in turn, provides that, \xe2\x80\x9c[f]or all venue purposes,\xe2\x80\x9d certain entities, \xe2\x80\x9cwhether or not incorporated, shall be deemed to reside, if a defendant, in any judicial district in which such defendant is subject to the court\xe2\x80\x99s personal jurisdiction with respect to the civil action in question.\xe2\x80\x9d In its decision below, the Federal Circuit reaffirmed VE Holding, reasoning that the 2011 amendments provided no basis to reconsider its prior decision.

\n

III

\n

We reverse the Federal Circuit. In Fourco, this Court definitively and unambiguously held that the word \xe2\x80\x9creside[nce]\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa71400(b) has a particular meaning as applied to domestic[2] corporations: It refers only to the State of incorporation. Congress has not amended \xc2\xa71400(b) since Fourco, and neither party asks us to reconsider our holding in that case. Accordingly, the only question we must answer is whether Congress changed the meaning of \xc2\xa71400(b) when it amended \xc2\xa71391. When Congress intends to effect a change of that kind, it ordinarily provides a relatively clear indication of its intent in the text of the amended provision. See United States v. Madigan,\n\n300 U.\xc2\xa0S. 500,\n506 (1937)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he modification by implication of the settled construction of an earlier and different section is not favored\xe2\x80\x9d); A. Scalia & B. Garner, Reading Law 331 (2012) (\xe2\x80\x9cA clear, authoritative judicial holding on the meaning of a particular provision should not be cast in doubt and subjected to challenge whenever a related though not utterly inconsistent provision is adopted in the same statute or even in an affiliated statute\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

The current version of \xc2\xa71391 does not contain any indication that Congress intended to alter the meaning of \xc2\xa71400(b) as interpreted in Fourco. Although the current version of \xc2\xa71391(c) provides a default rule that applies \xe2\x80\x9c[f]or all venue purposes,\xe2\x80\x9d the version at issue in Fourco similarly provided a default rule that applied \xe2\x80\x9cfor venue purposes.\xe2\x80\x9d 353 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 223 (internal quotation marks omitted). In this context, we do not see any material difference between the two phrasings. See Pure Oil Co. v. Suarez,\n\n384 U.\xc2\xa0S. 202\n\xe2\x80\x93205 (1966) (construing \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98for venue purposes\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to cover \xe2\x80\x9call venue statutes\xe2\x80\x9d). Respondent argues that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98all venue purposes\xe2\x80\x99 means \xe2\x80\x98all venue purposes\xe2\x80\x99\xe2\x80\x94not \xe2\x80\x98all venue purposes except for patentvenue.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 21. The plaintiffs in Fourco advanced the same argument. See 353 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 228 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe main thrust of respondents\xe2\x80\x99 argument is that \xc2\xa71391(c) is clear and unambiguous and that its terms include all actions\xe2\x80\x94including patent infringement actions\xe2\x80\x9d). This Court was not persuaded then, and the addition of the word \xe2\x80\x9call\xe2\x80\x9d to the already comprehensive provision does not suggest that Congress intended for us to reconsider that conclusion.

\n

This particular argument is even weaker under the current version of \xc2\xa71391 than it was under the provision in place at the time of Fourco, because the current provision includes a saving clause expressly stating that it does not apply when \xe2\x80\x9cotherwise provided by law.\xe2\x80\x9d On its face, the version of \xc2\xa71391(c) at issue in Fourco included no exceptions, yet this Court still held that \xe2\x80\x9cresides\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa71400(b) retained its original meaning contrary to \xc2\xa71391(c)\xe2\x80\x99s default definition. Fourco\xe2\x80\x99s holding rests on even firmer footing now that \xc2\xa71391\xe2\x80\x99s saving clause expressly contemplates that certain venue statutes may retain definitions of \xe2\x80\x9cresides\xe2\x80\x9d that conflict with its default definition. In short, the saving clause makes explicit the qualification that this Court previously found implicit in the statute. See Pure Oil, supra, at 205 (interpreting earlier version of \xc2\xa71391 to apply \xe2\x80\x9cto all venue statutes using residence as a criterion, at least in the absence of contrary restrictive indications in any such statute\xe2\x80\x9d). Respondent suggests that the saving clause in \xc2\xa71391(a) does not apply to the definitional provisions in \xc2\xa71391(c), Brief for Respondent 31\xe2\x80\x9332, but that interpretation is belied by the text of \xc2\xa71391(a), which makes clear that the saving clause applies to the entire \xe2\x80\x9csection.\xe2\x80\x9d See \xc2\xa71391(a)(1) (\xe2\x80\x9cExcept as otherwise provided by law\xe2\x80\x94 .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. this section shall govern the venue of all civil actions\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)).

\n

Finally, there is no indication that Congress in 2011 ratified the Federal Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision in VE Holding. If anything, the 2011 amendments undermine that decision\xe2\x80\x99s rationale. As petitioner points out, VE Holding relied heavily\xe2\x80\x94indeed, almost exclusively\xe2\x80\x94on Congress\xe2\x80\x99 decision in 1988 to replace \xe2\x80\x9cfor venue purposes\xe2\x80\x9d with \xe2\x80\x9c[f]or purposes of venue under this chapter\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added) in \xc2\xa71391(c). Congress deleted \xe2\x80\x9cunder this chapter\xe2\x80\x9d in 2011 and worded the current version of \xc2\xa71391(c) almost identically to the original version of the statute. Compare \xc2\xa71391(c) (2012 ed.) (\xe2\x80\x9c[f]or all venue purposes\xe2\x80\x9d) with \xc2\xa71391(c) (1952 ed.) (\xe2\x80\x9cfor venue purposes\xe2\x80\x9d). In short, nothing in the text suggests congressional approval of VE Holding.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

As applied to domestic corporations, \xe2\x80\x9creside[nce]\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa71400(b) refers only to the State of incorporation. Accordingly, we reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeals and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.
