\n\t\t\t\tCHAIDEZ v. UNITED STATES\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the seventh circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 11\xe2\x80\x93820.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 1, 2012\xe2\x80\x94Decided February 20, 2013\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tImmigration officials initiated removal proceedings against petitioner Chaidez in 2009 upon learning that she had pleaded guilty to mail fraud in 2004. To avoid removal, she sought to overturn that conviction by filing a petition for a writ of coram nobis, contending that her former attorney\xe2\x80\x99s failure to advise her of the guilty plea\xe2\x80\x99s immigration consequences constituted ineffective assistance of counsel under the \n \nSixth Amendment. While her petition was pending, this Court held in Padilla v. Kentucky, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, that the \n \nSixth Amendment requires defense attorneys to inform non-citizen clients of the deportation risks of guilty pleas. The District Court vacated Chaidez\xe2\x80\x99s conviction, determining that Padilla did not announce a \xe2\x80\x9cnew rule\xe2\x80\x9d under Teague v. Lane, \n\n489 U.\xc2\xa0S. 288\n, and thus applied to Chaidez\xe2\x80\x99s case. The Seventh Circuit reversed, holding that Padilla had declared a new rule and should not apply in a challenge to a final conviction.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0Padilla does not apply retroactively to cases already final on direct review. Pp. 3\xe2\x88\x9215.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0Under Teague, a person whose conviction is already final may not benefit from a new rule of criminal procedure on collateral review. A \xe2\x80\x9ccase announces a new rule if the result was not dictated by precedent existing at the time the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conviction became final.\xe2\x80\x9d Teague, 489 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 301. And a holding is not so dictated unless it would have been \xe2\x80\x9capparent to all reasonable jurists.\xe2\x80\x9d Lambrix v. Singletary, \n\n520 U.\xc2\xa0S. 518\n\xe2\x88\x92528. At the same time, a case does not \xe2\x80\x9cannounce a new rule, [when] it [is] merely an application of the principle that governed\xe2\x80\x9d a prior decision to a different set of facts. Teague, 489 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 307. Thus, garden-variety applications of the test in Strickland v. Washington, \n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 668\n, for assessing ineffective assistance claims do not produce new rules, id., at 687\xe2\x88\x92688.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut Padilla did more than just apply Strickland\xe2\x80\x99s general standard to yet another factual situation. Before deciding if failing to inform a client about the risk of deportation \xe2\x80\x9cfell below [Strickland\xe2\x80\x99s] objective standard of reasonableness,\xe2\x80\x9d 466 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 688, Padilla first considered the threshold question whether advice about deportation was \xe2\x80\x9ccategorically removed\xe2\x80\x9d from the scope of the \n \nSixth Amendment right to counsel because it involved only a \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral consequence\xe2\x80\x9d of a conviction, rather than a component of a criminal sentence, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___. That is, prior to asking how the Strickland test applied, Padilla asked whether that test applied at all.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThat preliminary question came to the Court unsettled. Hill v. Lockhart, \n\n474 U.\xc2\xa0S. 52\n, had explicitly left open whether the \n \nSixth Amendment right extends to collateral consequences. That left the issue to the state and lower federal courts, and they almost unanimously concluded that the \n \nSixth Amendment does not require attorneys to inform their clients of a conviction\xe2\x80\x99s collateral consequences, including deportation. Padilla\xe2\x80\x99s contrary ruling thus answered an open question about the \n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s reach, in a way that altered the law of most jurisdictions. In so doing, Padilla broke new ground and imposed a new obligation. Pp. 3\xe2\x88\x9211.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0Chaidez argues that Padilla did no more than apply Strickland to a new set of facts. But she ignores that Padilla had to develop new law to determine that Strickland applied at all. The few lower court decisions she cites held only that a lawyer may not affirmatively misrepresent his expertise or otherwise actively mislead his client as to any important matter. Those rulings do not apply to her case, and they do not show that all reasonable judges thought that lawyers had to advise their clients about deportation risks. Neither does INS v. St. Cyr, \n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 289\n, have any relevance here. In saying that a reasonably competent lawyer would tell a non-citizen client about a guilty plea\xe2\x80\x99s deportation consequences, St. Cyr did not determine that the \n \nSixth Amendment requires a lawyer to provide such information. It took Padilla to decide that question. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9315.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t655 F.\xc2\xa03d 684, affirmed.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tROSELVA CHAIDEZ, PETITIONER v. UNITED STATES\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\ton writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the seventh circuit\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Kagan delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn Padilla v. Kentucky, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2010), this Court held that the \n \nSixth Amendment requires an attorney for a criminal defendant to provide advice about the risk of deportation arising from a guilty plea. We consider here whether that ruling applies retroactively, so that a person whose conviction became final before we decided Padilla can benefit from it. We conclude that, under the prin-ciples set out in Teague v. Lane, \n\n489 U.\xc2\xa0S. 288 (1989)\n, Padilla does not have retroactive effect.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioner Roselva Chaidez hails from Mexico, but became a lawful permanent resident of the United States in 1977. About 20 years later, she helped to defraud an automobile insurance company out of $26,000. After federal agents uncovered the scheme, Chaidez pleaded guilty to two counts of mail fraud, in violation of \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71341. The District Court sentenced her to four years of probation and ordered her to pay restitution. Chaidez\xe2\x80\x99s conviction became final in 2004.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUnder federal immigration law, the offenses to which Chaidez pleaded guilty are \xe2\x80\x9caggravated felonies,\xe2\x80\x9d subjecting her to mandatory removal from this country. See 8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71101(a)(43)(M)(i), 1227(a)(2)(A)(iii). But according to Chaidez, her attorney never advised her of that fact, and at the time of her plea she remained ignorant of it.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tImmigration officials initiated removal proceedings against Chaidez in 2009, after an application she made for citizenship alerted them to her prior conviction. To avoid removal, Chaidez sought to overturn that conviction by filing a petition for a writ of coram nobis in Federal District Court.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t She argued that her former attorney\xe2\x80\x99s failure to advise her of the immigration consequences of pleading guilty constituted ineffective assistance of counsel under the \n \nSixth Amendment.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhile Chaidez\xe2\x80\x99s petition was pending, this Court decided Padilla. Our ruling vindicated Chaidez\xe2\x80\x99s view of the \n \nSixth Amendment: We held that criminal defense attorneys must inform non-citizen clients of the risks of deportation arising from guilty pleas. See 559 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 9). But the Government argued that Chaidez could not benefit from Padilla because it announced a \xe2\x80\x9cnew rule\xe2\x80\x9d and, under Teague, such rules do not apply in collateral challenges to already-final convictions.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe District Court determined that Padilla \xe2\x80\x9cdid not announce a new rule for Teague purposes,\xe2\x80\x9d and therefore should apply to Chaidez\xe2\x80\x99s case. 730 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 896, 904 (ND Ill. 2010). It then found that Chaidez\xe2\x80\x99s counsel had performed deficiently under Padilla and that Chaidez suffered prejudice as a result. Accordingly, the court vacated Chaidez\xe2\x80\x99s conviction. See No. 03 CR 636\xe2\x80\x936, 2010 WL 3979664 (ND Ill., Oct. 6, 2010).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe United States Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit reversed, holding that Padilla had declared a new rule and so should not apply in a challenge to a final conviction. \xe2\x80\x9cBefore Padilla,\xe2\x80\x9d the Seventh Circuit reasoned, \xe2\x80\x9cthe [Supreme] Court had never held that the \n \nSixth Amendment requires a criminal defense attorney to provide advice about matters not directly related to [a] client\xe2\x80\x99s criminal prosecution,\xe2\x80\x9d including the risks of deporta-tion. 655 F.\xc2\xa03d 684, 693 (2011). And state and lower federal courts had uniformly concluded that an attorney need not give \xe2\x80\x9cadvice concerning [such a] collateral (as opposed to direct) consequenc[e] of a guilty plea.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 690. According to the Seventh Circuit, Padilla\xe2\x80\x99s holding was new because it ran counter to that widely accepted \xe2\x80\x9cdistinction between direct and collateral consequences.\xe2\x80\x9d 655 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 691. Judge Williams dissented. Agreeing with the Third Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s view, she argued that Padilla \xe2\x80\x9cbroke no new ground\xe2\x80\x9d because it merely applied established law about a lawyer\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cduty to consult\xe2\x80\x9d with a client. 655 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 695 (quoting United States v. Orocio, 645 F.\xc2\xa03d 630, 638\xe2\x80\x93639 (CA3 2011) (internal quotation marks omitted)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe granted certiorari, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012), to resolve a split among federal and state courts on whether Padilla applies retroactively.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Holding that it does not, we affirm the Seventh Circuit.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTeague makes the retroactivity of our criminal procedure decisions turn on whether they are novel. When we announce a \xe2\x80\x9cnew rule,\xe2\x80\x9d a person whose conviction is already final may not benefit from the decision in a habeas or similar proceeding.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Only when we apply a settled rule may a person avail herself of the decision on collateral review. Here, Chaidez filed her coram nobis petition five years after her guilty plea became final. Her challenge therefore fails if Padilla declared a new rule.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c[A] case announces a new rule,\xe2\x80\x9d Teague explained, \xe2\x80\x9cwhen it breaks new ground or imposes a new obligation\xe2\x80\x9d on the government. 489 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 301. \xe2\x80\x9cTo put it differ-ently,\xe2\x80\x9d we continued, \xe2\x80\x9ca case announces a new rule if the result was not dictated by precedent existing at the time the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conviction became final.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. And a holding is not so dictated, we later stated, unless it would have been \xe2\x80\x9capparent to all reasonable jurists.\xe2\x80\x9d Lambrix v. Singletary, \n\n520 U.\xc2\xa0S. 518\n\xe2\x80\x93528 (1997).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut that account has a flipside. Teague also made clear that a case does not \xe2\x80\x9cannounce a new rule, [when] it \xe2\x80\x98[is] merely an application of the principle that governed\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d a prior decision to a different set of facts. 489 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 307 (quoting Yates v. Aiken, \n\n484 U.\xc2\xa0S. 211, \n217 (1988)\n). As Justice Kennedy has explained, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]here the beginning point\xe2\x80\x9d of our analysis is a rule of \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral application, a rule designed for the specific purpose of evaluating a myriad of factual contexts, it will be the infrequent case that yields a result so novel that it forges a new rule, one not dictated by precedent.\xe2\x80\x9d Wright v. West, \n\n505 U.\xc2\xa0S. 277, \n309 (1992)\n (concurring in judgment); see also Williams v. Taylor, \n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 362, \n391 (2000)\n. Otherwise said, when all we do is apply a general standard to the kind of factual circumstances it was meant to address, we will rarely state a new rule for Teague purposes.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBecause that is so, garden-variety applications of the test in Strickland v. Washington, \n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 668 (1984)\n, for assessing claims of ineffective assistance of counsel do not produce new rules. In Strickland, we held that legal representation violates the \n \nSixth Amendment if it falls \xe2\x80\x9cbelow an objective standard of reasonableness,\xe2\x80\x9d as indicated by \xe2\x80\x9cprevailing professional norms,\xe2\x80\x9d and the defendant suffers prejudice as a result. Id., at 687\xe2\x80\x93688. That standard, we later concluded, \xe2\x80\x9cprovides sufficient guidance for resolving virtually all\xe2\x80\x9d claims of ineffective assistance, even though their particular circumstances will differ. Williams, 529 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 391. And so we have granted relief under Strickland in diverse contexts without ever suggesting that doing so required a new rule. See, e.g., ibid.; Rompilla v. Beard, \n\n545 U.\xc2\xa0S. 374 (2005)\n; Wiggins v. Smith, \n\n539 U.\xc2\xa0S. 510 (2003)\n.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t In like manner, Padilla would not have created a new rule had it only applied Strickland\xe2\x80\x99s general standard to yet another factual situation\xe2\x80\x94that is, had Padilla merely made clear that a lawyer who neglects to inform a client about the risk of deportation is professionally incompetent.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut Padilla did something more. Before deciding if failing to provide such advice \xe2\x80\x9cfell below an objective stan-dard of reasonableness,\xe2\x80\x9d Padilla considered a threshold question: Was advice about deportation \xe2\x80\x9ccategorically removed\xe2\x80\x9d from the scope of the \n \nSixth Amendment right to counsel because it involved only a \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral consequence\xe2\x80\x9d of a conviction, rather than a component of the criminal sentence? 559 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 7\xe2\x80\x939).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t In other words, prior to asking how the Strickland test applied (\xe2\x80\x9cDid this attorney act unreasonably?\xe2\x80\x9d), Padilla asked whether the Strickland test applied (\xe2\x80\x9cShould we even evaluate if this attorney acted unreasonably?\xe2\x80\x9d). And as we will describe, that preliminary question about Strickland\xe2\x80\x99s ambit came to the Padilla Court unsettled\xe2\x80\x94so that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s answer (\xe2\x80\x9cYes, Strickland governs here\xe2\x80\x9d) required a new rule.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe relevant background begins with our decision in Hill v. Lockhart, \n\n474 U.\xc2\xa0S. 52 (1985)\n, which explicitly left open whether advice concerning a collateral consequence must satisfy \n \nSixth Amendment requirements. Hill pleaded guilty to first-degree murder after his attorney misinformed him about his parole eligibility. In addressing his claim of ineffective assistance, we first held that the Strickland standard extends generally to the plea process. See Hill, 474 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 57. We then determined, however, that Hill had failed to allege prejudice from the lawyer\xe2\x80\x99s error and so could not prevail under that standard. See id., at 60. That conclusion allowed us to avoid another, more categorical question: whether advice about parole (however inadequate and prejudicial) could possibly violate the \n \nSixth Amendment. The Court of Appeals, we noted, had held \xe2\x80\x9cthat parole eligibility is a collateral rather than a direct consequence of a guilty plea, of which a defendant need not be informed.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 55. But our ruling on prejudice made \xe2\x80\x9cit unnecessary to determine whether there may be circumstances under which\xe2\x80\x9d ad- vice about a matter deemed collateral violates the \n \nSixth Amendment. Id., at 60.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThat non-decision left the state and lower federal courts to deal with the issue; and they almost unanimously concluded that the \n \nSixth Amendment does not require attorneys to inform their clients of a conviction\xe2\x80\x99s collateral consequences, including deportation. All 10 federal appellate courts to consider the question decided, in the words of one, that \xe2\x80\x9ccounsel\xe2\x80\x99s failure to inform a defendant of the collateral consequences of a guilty plea is never\xe2\x80\x9d a violation of the \n \nSixth Amendment. Santos-Sanchez v. United States, 548 F.\xc2\xa03d 327, 334 (CA5 2008).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t7\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t That constitutional guarantee, another typical decision expounded, \xe2\x80\x9cassures an accused of effective assistance of counsel in \xe2\x80\x98criminal prosecutions\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d; accordingly, advice about matters like de- portation, which are \xe2\x80\x9cnot a part of or enmeshed in the criminal proceeding,\xe2\x80\x9d does not fall within the Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s scope. United States v. George, 869 F.\xc2\xa02d 333, 337 (CA7 1989). Appellate courts in almost 30 States agreed.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t8\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t By contrast, only two state courts held that an attorney could violate the \n \nSixth Amendment by failing to inform a client about deportation risks or other collateral consequences of a guilty plea.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t9\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t That imbalance led the authors of the principal scholarly article on the subject to call the exclusion of advice about collateral consequences from the \n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s scope one of \xe2\x80\x9cthe most widely recognized rules of American law.\xe2\x80\x9d Chin & Holmes, Effective Assistance of Counsel and the Consequences of Guilty Pleas, 87 Cornell L. Rev. 697, 706 (2002).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t10\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSo when we decided Padilla, we answered a question about the \n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s reach that we had left open, in a way that altered the law of most jurisdictions\xe2\x80\x94and our reasoning reflected that we were doing as much. In the normal Strickland case, a court begins by evaluating the reasonableness of an attorney\xe2\x80\x99s conduct in light of professional norms, and then assesses prejudice. But as earlier indicated, see supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x936, Padilla had a different starting point. Before asking whether the performance of Padilla\xe2\x80\x99s attorney was deficient under Strickland, we considered (in a separately numbered part of the opinion) whether Strickland applied at all. See 559 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 7\xe2\x80\x939). Many courts, we acknowledged, had excluded advice about collateral matters from the \n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s ambit; and deportation, because the consequence of a distinct civil proceeding, could well be viewed as such a matter. See id., at ___ (slip op., at 7). But, we continued, no decision of our own committed us to \xe2\x80\x9cappl[y] a distinction between direct and collateral consequences to define the scope\xe2\x80\x9d of the right to counsel. Id., at ___ (slip op., at 8). On Chaidez\xe2\x80\x99s view, Strickland insisted \xe2\x80\x9c[f]rom its inception\xe2\x80\x9d that all aspects of a criminal lawyer\xe2\x80\x99s performance pass a test of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98reasonableness under prevailing professional norms\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d: The decision thus foreclosed any \xe2\x80\x9ccategorical distinction between direct and collateral consequences.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 21\xe2\x80\x9322 (emphasis deleted) (quoting Strickland, 466 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 688). Moreover, we cannot find any case in which a federal court has considered Chaidez\xe2\x80\x99s contention that Teague should not apply to ineffective assistance claims. \xe2\x80\x9c[M]indful that we are a court of review, not of first view,\xe2\x80\x9d we decline to rule on Chaidez\xe2\x80\x99s new arguments. Cutter v. Wilkinson, \n, n.\xc2\xa07 (2005).\n\t\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court holds today that Padilla v. Kentucky, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2010), announced a \xe2\x80\x9cnew\xe2\x80\x9d rule within the meaning of Teague v. Lane, \n\n489 U.\xc2\xa0S. 288, \n301 (1989)\n, and so does not apply to convictions that became final before its announcement. That is wrong, because Padilla did nothing more than apply the existing rule of Strickland v. Washington, \n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 668 (1984)\n, in a new setting, the same way the Court has done repeatedly in the past: by surveying the relevant professional norms and conclud- ing that they unequivocally required attorneys to provide advice about the immigration consequences of a guilty plea. Because Padilla fell squarely within the metes and bounds established by Strickland, I respectfully dissent.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority correctly sets forth the governing legal principles under Teague and Strickland. Ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x935. The Teague inquiry turns centrally on the \xe2\x80\x9cnature of the rule\xe2\x80\x9d in question, and for that reason, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]here the beginning point is a rule of . .\xc2\xa0. general application, . . . it will be the infrequent case that yields a result so novel that it forges a new rule.\xe2\x80\x9d Wright v. West, \n\n505 U.\xc2\xa0S. 277\n\xe2\x80\x93309 (1992) (Kennedy, J., concurring in judgment); see ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x935. The majority makes the important observation that \xe2\x80\x9cwhen all we do is apply a general standard to the kind of factual circumstances it was meant to address, we will rarely state a new rule.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. It makes sense, then, that \xe2\x80\x9cgarden-variety applications of . . . Strickland . . . do not produce new rules.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 5.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn Strickland, we did not provide a comprehensive def- inition of deficient performance, and instead held that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he proper measure of attorney performance remains simply reasonableness under prevailing professional norms.\xe2\x80\x9d 466 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 688. Strickland\xe2\x80\x99s reasonableness prong therefore takes its content from the standards by which lawyers judge their professional obligations, ibid., and those standards are subject to change. That is why, despite the many different settings in which it has been applied, we have never found that an application of Strickland resulted in a new rule.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSignificantly, we have previously found that applications of Strickland to new factual scenarios are not barred under \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) of the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act (AEDPA). Section 2254(d)(1) precludes habeas relief unless a state court decision violates \xe2\x80\x9cclearly established Federal law,\xe2\x80\x9d which, as relevant here, largely overlaps with the inquiry under Teague of whether a decision was \xe2\x80\x9cdictated by precedent.\xe2\x80\x9d 489 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 301 (plurality opinion).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t In Wiggins v. Smith, \n\n539 U.\xc2\xa0S. 510, \n522 (2003)\n, for example, we found that Williams v. Taylor, \n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 362 (2000)\n, \xe2\x80\x9cmade no new law\xe2\x80\x9d when it held that Strickland extended to an attorney\xe2\x80\x99s responsibility to conduct a background investigation in a capital case. Rather, we explained that \xe2\x80\x9cin referring to the ABA Standards for Criminal Justice as guides, [Williams] applied the same \xe2\x80\x98clearly established\xe2\x80\x99 precedent of Strickland we apply today.\xe2\x80\x9d 539 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 522. Similarly, in Lafler v. Cooper, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2012) (slip op., at 6, 14\xe2\x80\x9316), we rejected the argument advanced by the Solicitor General that the \n \nSixth Amendment did not extend to advice about a plea offer because it did not impact the fairness of the trial. Instead, we simply held that Strickland applied to this form of attorney misconduct.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn short, where we merely apply Strickland in a way that corresponds to an evolution in professional norms, we make no new law.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tContrary to the majority\xe2\x80\x99s reconstruction, Padilla is built squarely on the foundation laid out by Strickland. Padilla relied upon controlling precedent. It began by reciting the basic rule that \xe2\x80\x9c[u]nder Strickland, we first determine whether counsel\xe2\x80\x99s representation \xe2\x80\x98fell below an objective standard of reasonableness.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Padilla, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 9) (quoting Strickland, 466 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 688). We recognized that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he first prong\xe2\x80\x94constitutional deficiency\xe2\x80\x94is necessarily linked to the practice and expectations of the legal community: \xe2\x80\x98[t]he proper measure of attorney performance remains reasonableness under pre- vailing professional norms.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Padilla, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 9) (quoting Strickland, 466 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 688).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe therefore examined the substantial changes in fed- eral immigration law that provided the backdrop to the relevant professional standards. Padilla, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 2\xe2\x80\x936). Pursuant to the Immigration Act of 1917, \n\n39Stat.\n889\xe2\x80\x93890, a judge could recommend that a defendant who had committed a deportable offense not be removed from the country. Congress entirely eliminated this procedure in 1990. \n\n104Stat.\n5050. Then the Illegal Immigration Reform and Immigrant Responsibility Act of 1996 (IIRIRA), \n\n110Stat.\n3009\xe2\x80\x93596, abolished the Attor- ney General\xe2\x80\x99s authority to grant discretionary relief from removal for all but a small number of offenses. Padilla, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 6). These changes in immigration law meant that for a noncitizen who committed a removable offense, \xe2\x80\x9cremoval [had become] practically inevitable.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn parallel with these developments, the standards of professional responsibility relating to immigration had become more demanding. \xe2\x80\x9cFor at least the past 15 years,\xe2\x80\x9d we observed in Padilla, \xe2\x80\x9cprofessional norms have gener- ally imposed an obligation on counsel to provide advice on the deportation consequences of a client\xe2\x80\x99s plea.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 15). Citing an array of practice guides and professional responsibility manuals, we noted that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he weight of prevailing professional norms supports the view that counsel must advise her client regarding the risk of deportation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 9). Indeed, \xe2\x80\x9cauthorities of every stripe\xe2\x80\x94including the American Bar Association, criminal defense and public defender organizations, authoritative treatises, and state and city bar publications\xe2\x80\x94universally require defense attorneys to advise as to the risk of deportation consequences for non-citizen clients.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 10) (internal quotation marks omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe drew further support for our conclusion that professional standards required advice about deportation consequences from our decision in INS v. St. Cyr, \n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 289 (2001)\n. See Padilla, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 10\xe2\x80\x9311) (citing St. Cyr, 533 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 323). In St. Cyr, we had explained that the availability of discretionary relief from removal was critical to a noncitizen\xe2\x80\x99s decision to accept a plea offer, and expected counsel to follow the instructions of \xe2\x80\x9cnumerous practice guides,\xe2\x80\x9d such as the ABA\xe2\x80\x99s Standards for Criminal Justice, to inform themselves of the possible immigration consequences of a plea. Padilla, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 11) (citing St. Cyr, 533 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 323, n. 50); see id., at 322, n. 48. And we there found that many States already required that a trial judge advise defendants of the same. Ibid. St. Cyr thus \xe2\x80\x9crecognized that \xe2\x80\x98preserving the client\xe2\x80\x99s right to remain in the United States may be more important to the client than any potential jail sentence.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Padilla, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 10) (quoting St. Cyr, 533 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 322).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur application of Strickland in Padilla followed naturally from these earlier observations about changes in immigration law and the accompanying evolution of professional norms. When we decided St. Cyr and Padilla, nothing about Strickland\xe2\x80\x99s substance or applicability had changed. The only difference from prior law was that the underlying professional norms had changed such that counsel\xe2\x80\x99s failure to give this advice now amounted to constitutionally deficient performance.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Both before Padilla and after, counsel was obligated to follow the relevant professional norms. It was only because those norms reflected changes in immigration law that Padilla reached the result it did, not because the \n \nSixth Amendment right had changed at all.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAccepting that routine applications of Strickland do not result in new rules, the majority nevertheless holds that Padilla went a step further. In its view, Padilla \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98br[oke] new ground\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d by addressing the threshold question of whether advice about deportation is a collateral consequence of a criminal conviction that falls within the scope of the \n \nSixth Amendment. Ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310. But that is wrong, because Padilla declined to embrace the very distinction between collateral and direct consequences of a criminal conviction that the majority says it did. In fact, the Court stated very clearly that it found the distinction irrelevant for the purposes of determining a defense lawyer\xe2\x80\x99s obligation to provide advice about the immigration consequences of a plea. 559 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___, n. 8 (slip op., at 7, n. 8). We asserted that we had \xe2\x80\x9cnever applied a dis- tinction between direct and collateral consequences to define the scope of constitutionally \xe2\x80\x98reasonable professional assistance\xe2\x80\x99 required under Strickland,\xe2\x80\x9d and concluded that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hether that distinction is appropriate is a question we need not consider in this case.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 8) (emphasis added). The distinction was \xe2\x80\x9cill suited\xe2\x80\x9d to the task at hand, we explained, because deportation has a \xe2\x80\x9cclose connection to the criminal process,\xe2\x80\x9d and is \xe2\x80\x9cuniquely difficult to classify as either a direct or a collateral consequence.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 8\xe2\x80\x939). Indeed, \xe2\x80\x9c[o]ur law ha[d] enmeshed criminal convictions and the penalty of de- portation for nearly a century,\xe2\x80\x9d and we had \xe2\x80\x9clong recognized\xe2\x80\x9d that deportation is \xe2\x80\x9cparticularly severe.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 8).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAt bottom, then, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s argument hinges upon a distinction the Court has never embraced and that Padilla found irrelevant to the issue it ultimately decided. Without this revision to our recent decisional history, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s analysis unravels.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority finds that the \xe2\x80\x9clegal landscape,\xe2\x80\x9d Graham v. Collins, \n\n506 U.\xc2\xa0S. 461, \n468 (1993)\n, before Padilla was nearly uniform in its rejection of Strickland\xe2\x80\x99s application to the deportation consequences of a plea. Ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x9310. It concludes that the lower courts were generally in agreement that the \n \nSixth Amendment did not require attorneys to inform clients of the collateral consequences of a plea, and that this weighs heavily in favor of finding that Padilla announced a new rule. Ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x938, nn. 7, 8. But the majority\xe2\x80\x99s discussion of these precedents operates at too high a level of generality and fails to account for the development of professional standards over time. St. Cyr noted the importance of advising clients about immigration consequences was of recent vintage, indeed more re- cent than some of the cases the majority cites. See 533 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 322\xe2\x80\x93323. The Court relies upon decisions issued over a period that spans more than 30 years. See ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x938, nn. 7, 8. Nearly half of them (17) were decided before the enactment of IIRIRA. See ibid. And all but two of the Federal Court of Appeals cases were decided before St. Cyr. See ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x938, nn. 7, 8. These earlier decisions show nothing more than that the underlying professional norms had not yet evolved to require attorneys to provide advice about deportation consequences.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCases from the period following IIRIRA and St. Cyr undermine the majority\xe2\x80\x99s generalizations about the state of the law before Padilla. Deportation had long been un- derstood by lower courts to present \xe2\x80\x9cthe most difficult\xe2\x80\x9d penalty to classify as either a collateral or direct consequence. United States v. Russell, 686 F.\xc2\xa02d 35, 38 (CADC 1982); cf. Janvier v. United States, 793 F.\xc2\xa02d 449, 455 (CA2 1986) (holding that Strickland applied to advice about a judicial recommendation against deportation). Eventually, and in parallel with changes in federal immigration law and the corresponding professional norms, the lower courts had acknowledged an important qualification to the collateral consequences rule. After the passage of IIRIRA and this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in St. Cyr, many courts concluded that a lawyer\xe2\x80\x99s affirmative misstatements about the immigration consequences of a guilty plea can constitute deficient performance under Strickland. Indeed, each Federal Court of Appeals to address the question after St. Cyr so held. See United States v. Couto, 311 F.\xc2\xa03d 179, 188 (CA2 2002); United States v. Kwan, 407 F.\xc2\xa03d 1005, 1015 (CA9 2005); cf. Downs-Morgan v. United States, 765 F.\xc2\xa02d 1534, 1540\xe2\x80\x931541 (CA11 1985).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t State-court decisions from this period were in accord and relied upon similar reasoning.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThese decisions created an important exception to the collateral/direct consequences distinction. They also foreshadowed the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning in Padilla by basing their analysis of the relevant professional norms on the special nature of deportation, the ABA standards governing immigration practice, and the Court\xe2\x80\x99s assessment of those standards in St Cyr. See Kwan, 407 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1016 (\xe2\x80\x9cThat counsel may have misled [the defendant] out of ignorance is no excuse. It is a basic rule of professional conduct that a lawyer must .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [remain] abreast of changes in the law and its practice.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. Counsel\xe2\x80\x99s performance .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. fell be- low the [ABA]\xe2\x80\x99s ethical standard for criminal defense at- torneys with respect to immigration consequences. The Supreme Court noted this standard in [St. Cyr]\xe2\x80\x9d); Couto, 311 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 187\xe2\x80\x93191 (citing St. Cyr and the relevant ABA standards, and concluding that \xe2\x80\x9crecent Supreme Court authority supports [a] broader view of attorney responsibility\xe2\x80\x9d that encompasses affirmative misrepresentations about deportation consequences); see also Downs-Morgan, 765 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 1541 (\xe2\x80\x9c[D]eportation and exclusion [are] harsh consequences\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority believes that these decisions did not meaningfully alter the state of the law in the lower courts before Padilla, because they merely applied the age-old principle that a lawyer may not affirmatively mislead a client. Ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313. But, as explained, the reasoning of these cases renders that characterization at best incomplete. See, e.g., Kwan, 407 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1016. While these lower court precedents are consistent with the general principle that attorneys should not mislead clients by providing incorrect advice, they did not rest primarily on that rule. Rather, they recognized the significant changes in professional norms that predated Padilla and that we had noted in St. Cyr. As a consequence, the \xe2\x80\x9cwall between direct and collateral consequences\xe2\x80\x9d that the lower courts had erected, ante, at 9, had already been dealt a serious blow by the time the Court decided Padilla.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs the majority points out, these misrepresentation cases stopped short of imposing an affirmative obligation on lawyers to consult with clients about the consequences of deportation. Ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313. But the majority places too much emphasis on the absence of lower court authority finding that an attorney\xe2\x80\x99s omissions with respect to deportation resulted in ineffective assistance. The distinction between omissions and affirmative misrepresentations on which these lower court cases depended cannot be reconciled with Strickland. In Padilla itself, we rejected the Solicitor General\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion that Strickland should apply to advice about the immigration consequences of a plea only in cases where defense counsel makes an affirmative misstatement. Padilla, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 12). We did so because we found that Strickland was incompatible with the distinction between an obligation to give advice and a prohibition on affirmative misstatements. 559 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 12\xe2\x80\x9313) (citing Strickland, 466 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 690). Strickland made clear that its standard of attorney performance applied to both \xe2\x80\x9cacts\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9comissions,\xe2\x80\x9d and that a rule limiting the performance inquiry to one or the other was too narrow. 466 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 690. Thus, the distinction between misrepresentations and omissions, on which the majority relies in classifying lower court precedent, implies a categorical rule that is inconsistent with Strickland\xe2\x80\x99s requirement of a case-by-case assessment of an attorney\xe2\x80\x99s performance.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t7\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Id., at 688\xe2\x80\x93689; see, e.g., Roe v. Flores-Ortega, \n\n528 U.\xc2\xa0S. 470, \n479 (2000)\n. In short, that some courts have differentiated between misleading by silence and affirmative misrep- resentation hardly establishes the rationality of the distinction. Notably, the Court offers no reasoned basis for believing that such a distinction can be extracted from Strickland.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo be sure, lower courts did continue to apply the distinction between collateral and direct consequences after St. Cyr. See ante, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314; see, e.g., Broomes v. Ashcroft, 358 F.\xc2\xa03d 1251, 1256\xe2\x80\x931257 (CA10 2004). Even so, and even assuming the misrepresentation cases did not call the distinction into question, the existence of these lower court decisions is not dispositive. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he standard for determining when a case establishes a new rule is \xe2\x80\x98objective,\xe2\x80\x99 and the mere existence of conflicting authority does not necessarily mean a rule is new.\xe2\x80\x9d Wright, 505 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 304 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in judgment) (citing Stringer v. Black, \n\n503 U.\xc2\xa0S. 222, \n237 (1992)\n); see Graham v. Collins, \n\n506 U.\xc2\xa0S. 461, \n506 (1993)\n (Souter, J., dissenting).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhere the application of Strickland was straightforward, rooted in 15 years of professional standards and the Court\xe2\x80\x99s prior St. Cyr decision, there is no reason to put these lower court cases, many from more than a decade earlier, ahead of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s simple and clear reasoning in Padilla. Nevertheless, the majority reaches the paradoxical conclusion that by declining to apply a collateral-consequence doctrine the Court had never adopted, Padilla announced a new rule.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhat truly appears to drive the majority\xe2\x80\x99s analysis is its sense that Padilla occasioned a serious disruption in lower court decisional reasoning. See, e.g., ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310 (\xe2\x80\x9cIf that does not count as \xe2\x80\x98break[ing] new ground\xe2\x80\x99 . . . we are hard pressed to know what would\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Teague, 489 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 301)). The concurring and dissenting opinions in Padilla similarly reflected the impression that it was a significant and destabilizing decision. See 559 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (Alito, J., concurring in judgment) (slip op., at 3); id., at ___ (Scalia, J., dissenting) (slip op., at 5) (describing the majority opinion as a \xe2\x80\x9csledge hammer\xe2\x80\x9d); ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939, n. 10. But the fact that a decision was perceived as momentous or consequential, particularly by those who disagreed with it, does not control in the Teague analysis. Faithfully applying the Teague rule depends instead on an examination of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning and an objective assessment of the precedent at issue. Stringer, 503 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 237. In Padilla, we did nothing more than apply Strickland. By holding to the contrary, today\xe2\x80\x99s decision deprives defendants of the fundamental protection of Strickland, which requires that lawyers comply with professional norms with respect to any advice they provide to clients.\n\t\t\t
