\n\t\t\t\tCARACO PHARMACEUTICAL LABORATORIES, LTD., et\xc2\xa0al. v. NOVO NORDISK A/S et\xc2\xa0al.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the federal circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 10\xe2\x80\x93844.\xe2\x80\x83Argued December 5, 2011\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 17, 2012\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe Food and Drug Administration (FDA) regulates the manufacture, sale, and labeling of prescription drugs. A brand-name drug manufacturer seeking FDA approval for a drug submits a new drug application (NDA) containing, among other things, a statement of the drug\xe2\x80\x99s components and proposed labeling describing the uses for which the drug may be marketed. See 21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7355(b)(1), (d). Once the FDA has approved a brand manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s drug, another company may seek permission to market a generic version by filing an abbreviated new drug application (ANDA). See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7355(j)(2)(A)(ii), (iv). But the FDA cannot authorize a generic drug that would infringe a brand manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s patent. To facilitate the approval of generic drugs as soon as patents allow, the Hatch-Waxman Amendments require a brand manufacturer to submit its patent numbers and expiration dates, \xc2\xa7355(b)(1); and FDA regulations require a description of any method-of-use patent, known as a use code, see 21 CFR \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7314.53(c)(2)(ii)(P)(3), (e). The FDA does not attempt to verify the accuracy of the use codes that brand manufacturers supply. Instead, it simply publishes the codes, patent numbers, and expiration dates in a large volume known as the Orange Book.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAfter consulting the Orange Book, an ANDA applicant enters one of several certifications to assure the FDA that its generic drug will not infringe the brand\xe2\x80\x99s patent. If the patent has not expired, an applicant may fulfill this requirement in one of two ways. First, it may submit a so-called section viii statement asserting that it will market the drug for only those methods of use not covered by the brand\xe2\x80\x99s patent, see \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7355(j)(2)(A)(viii), and proposing a label that \xe2\x80\x9ccarves out\xe2\x80\x9d the still-patented method(s) of use, see 21 CFR \xc2\xa7314.94(a)(8)(iv). The FDA will not approve an ANDA with a section viii statement if the proposed label overlaps at all with the brand\xe2\x80\x99s use code. Second, the ANDA applicant may file a so-called paragraph IV certification stating that the brand\xe2\x80\x99s patent \xe2\x80\x9cis invalid or will not be infringed by the [generic drug\xe2\x80\x99s] manufacture, use, or sale.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7355(j)(2)(A)(vii)(IV). Such filing is treated as an act of infringement, giving the brand an immediate right to sue and resulting in a delay in the generic drug\xe2\x80\x99s approval.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 2002, the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) issued a study detailing evidence that brands were submitting inaccurate patent information to the FDA in order to prevent or delay the marketing of generic drugs. In response, Congress created a statutory counterclaim available to generic manufacturers sued for patent infringement. The provision allows a generic manufacturer to \xe2\x80\x9cassert a counterclaim seeking an order requiring the [brand] to correct or delete the patent information submitted by the [brand] under subsection (b) or (c) [of \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7355] on the ground that the patent does not claim .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. an approved method of using the drug.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7355(j)(5)(C)(ii)(I). This case concerns the scope of the counterclaim provision.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondents (collectively Novo) manufacture the brand-name version of the diabetes drug repaglinide. The FDA has approved three uses of the drug, but Novo\xe2\x80\x99s method-of-use patent claims only one. Petitioners (collectively Caraco) wish to market a generic version of the drug for the other two approved methods of use. Caraco initially filed a paragraph IV certification and, considering this an act of infringement, Novo brought suit. Caraco then submitted a section viii statement and a proposed label carving out Novo\xe2\x80\x99s patented therapy. But before the FDA could approve Caraco\xe2\x80\x99s ANDA, Novo changed its use code to indicate that it held a patent on all three approved methods of using repaglinide. Because Caraco\xe2\x80\x99s proposed label now overlapped with Novo\xe2\x80\x99s use code, the FDA would not permit Caraco to employ section viii to bring its drug to market.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCaraco filed a statutory counterclaim in the ongoing infringement action, seeking an order requiring Novo to \xe2\x80\x9ccorrect\xe2\x80\x9d its use code because the patent did not claim two of the three approved methods of using repaglinide. The District Court granted Caraco summary judgment, but the Federal Circuit reversed. It read the counterclaim\xe2\x80\x99s phrase \xe2\x80\x9cthe patent does not claim .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. an approved method of using the drug\xe2\x80\x9d as requiring Caraco to demonstrate that Novo\xe2\x80\x99s patent does not claim any approved method of use; because the patent covers one approved method, the counterclaim was unavailable. The court also ruled that the counterclaim provision does not reach use codes because they are not \xe2\x80\x9cpatent information submitted by the [brand] under subsection (b) or (c)\xe2\x80\x9d of \xc2\xa7355. That information, the court concluded, consists only of the patent number and expiration date expressly required by the statutory provisions.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0A generic manufacturer may employ the counterclaim provision to force correction of a use code that inaccurately describes the brand\xe2\x80\x99s patent as covering a particular method of using a drug. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9324.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0The parties first dispute the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cnot an\xe2\x80\x9d in the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cthe patent does not claim .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. an approved method of using the drug.\xe2\x80\x9d Novo contends that the counterclaim is available only if the patent claims no approved method of use, but Caraco reads this language to permit a counterclaim whenever a patent does not claim the particular method that the ANDA applicant seeks to market. In isolation, either of these readings is plausible, so the meaning of the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cnot an\xe2\x80\x9d turns on statutory context, see Johnson v. United States, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. This context favors Caraco: Congress understood that a drug may have multiple methods of use, not all of which a patent covers; and a section viii statement allows the FDA to approve a generic drug for unpatented uses so that it can quickly come to market. The statute thus contemplates that one patented use will not foreclose marketing a generic drug for other unpatented ones. Within this scheme, the counterclaim naturally functions to challenge the brand\xe2\x80\x99s assertion of rights over whichever discrete uses the generic company wishes to pursue; the counterclaim\xe2\x80\x99s availability matches the availability of FDA approval under the statute. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9315.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0The parties further dispute whether use codes qualify as \xe2\x80\x9cpatent information submitted by the [brand] under subsection (b) or (c)\xe2\x80\x9d of \xc2\xa7355. A use code, which is a description of the patent, surely qualifies as \xe2\x80\x9cpatent information.\xe2\x80\x9d Novo nonetheless contends that use codes are not \xe2\x80\x9csubmitted under\xe2\x80\x9d subsections (b) and (c) because those provisions expressly require an NDA applicant to provide only \xe2\x80\x9cthe patent number and the expiration date of any patent\xe2\x80\x9d claiming the drug or a method of its use. But \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7355(b) and (c) also govern the regulatory process by which brands provide additional patent information to the FDA. The term \xe2\x80\x9cunder\xe2\x80\x9d is broad enough to include patent information, like use codes, that brands submit as required by this scheme. This reading draws support from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s prior decisions in e.g., Eli Lilly & Co. v. Medtronic, Inc., \n\n496 U.\xc2\xa0S. 661\n\xe2\x80\x93668, and Ardestani v. INS, \n\n502 U.\xc2\xa0S. 129\n; and it is bolstered by Congress\xe2\x80\x99s use of the narrower phrases \xe2\x80\x9cdescribed in\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cprescribed by\xe2\x80\x9d in neighboring provisions. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7355(c)(2), (d)(6). Again, the conclusion that use codes are \xe2\x80\x9csubmitted under\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7355(b) and (c) fits the broader statutory context. Use codes are pivotal to the FDA\xe2\x80\x99s implementation of the Hatch-Waxman Amendments, and so it is unsurprising that the counterclaim provision\xe2\x80\x99s language sweeps widely enough to embrace them. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9318.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0The counterclaim provision\xe2\x80\x99s description of available remedies dispatches whatever remains of Novo\xe2\x80\x99s arguments. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s reading gives content to both remedies: It \xe2\x80\x9cdelete[s]\xe2\x80\x9d a listing from the Orange Book when the brand holds no relevant patent and \xe2\x80\x9ccorrect[s]\xe2\x80\x9d the listing when the brand has misdescribed the patent\xe2\x80\x99s scope. By contrast, Novo\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation would all but read \xe2\x80\x9ccorrect\xe2\x80\x9d out of the statute. If, as Novo contends, the counterclaim is available only where the patent claims no approved method of use, the remedy will always be to delete the patent information. And if the counterclaim reaches only patent numbers and expiration dates, the Orange Book will include few if any mistakes in need of correction. Pp.\xc2\xa018\xe2\x80\x9320.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(d)\xc2\xa0Novo advances two arguments relating to the counterclaim\xe2\x80\x99s drafting history, but neither overcomes the statutory text and context. The company first points out that Congress failed to pass an earlier bill that would have required brands to file descriptions of method-of-use patents and would have allowed generic companies to bring civil actions to \xe2\x80\x9cdelete\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ccorrect\xe2\x80\x9d the information filed. Because that bill would have allowed a generic applicant to challenge overbroad descriptions of a patent, Novo contends that this Court cannot read the statute that was eventually enacted as doing the same. But the earlier bill contained numerous items that may have caused its failure. And the limited criticism of its mechanism for challenging brands\xe2\x80\x99 descriptions of their patents focused on the creation of an independent cause of action\xe2\x80\x94stronger medicine than the counterclaim at issue here. Finally, between that bill\xe2\x80\x99s demise and the counterclaim\xe2\x80\x99s enactment, the FDA issued a rule requiring brands to supply use codes. The counterclaim provision\xe2\x80\x99s drafters thus had no need to require this information.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNovo next contends that Congress established the counterclaim only to address the impossibility of deleting an improperly listed patent from the Orange Book\xe2\x80\x94a problem that had come to light when the Federal Circuit held in Mylan Pharmaceuticals, Inc. v. Thompson, 268 F.\xc2\xa03d 1323, that generics had no cause of action to delist a patent. Novo thus contends that the counterclaim is a mere delisting provision. But this Court thinks Mylan alerted Congress to a broader problem: that generic companies generally had no avenue to challenge the accuracy of brands\xe2\x80\x99 patent listings, and that the FDA therefore could not approve proper applications to bring inexpensive drugs to market. Again, the proof of that lies in the statute itself\xe2\x80\x94its text and context demonstrate that the counterclaim is available not only (as in Mylan) when the patent listing is baseless, but also (as here) when it is overbroad. Moreover, Congress\xe2\x80\x99s equation of the two situations makes perfect sense. In either case, the brand submits misleading patent information to the FDA, delaying or blocking approval of a generic drug that infringes no patent and thus, under the statute, should go to market. Pp.\xc2\xa020\xe2\x80\x9324.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhen the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) evaluates an application to market a generic drug, it considers whether the proposed drug would infringe a patent held by the manufacturer of the brand-name version. To assess that matter, the FDA requires brand manufacturers to submit descriptions of the scope of their patents, known as use codes. The FDA does not attempt to determine if that information is accurate. Rather, the FDA assumes that it is so and decides whether to approve a generic drug on that basis. As a result, the breadth of the use code may make the difference between approval and denial of a generic company\xe2\x80\x99s application.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn this case, we consider whether Congress has authorized a generic company to challenge a use code\xe2\x80\x99s accuracy by bringing a counterclaim against the brand manufac- turer in a patent infringement suit. The relevant statute provides that a generic company \xe2\x80\x9cmay assert a counterclaim seeking an order requiring the [brand manufac- turer] to correct or delete the patent information [it] submitted .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. under [two statutory subsections] on the ground that the patent does not claim .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. an approved method of using the drug.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n117Stat.\n2452, \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7355(j)(5)(C)(ii)(I). We hold that a generic manufacturer may employ this provision to force correction of a use code that inaccurately describes the brand\xe2\x80\x99s patent as covering a particular method of using the drug in question.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe FDA regulates the manufacture, sale, and labeling of prescription drugs under a complex statutory scheme. To begin at the beginning: When a brand manufacturer wishes to market a novel drug, it must submit a new drug application (NDA) to the FDA for approval. The NDA must include, among other things, a statement of the drug\xe2\x80\x99s components, scientific data showing that the drug is safe and effective, and proposed labeling describing the uses for which the drug may be marketed. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7355(b)(1), (d). The FDA may approve a brand-name drug for multiple methods of use\xe2\x80\x94either to treat different conditions or to treat the same condition in different ways.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOnce the FDA has approved a brand manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s drug, another company may seek permission to market a generic version pursuant to legislation known as the Hatch-Waxman Amendments. See Drug Price Competition and Patent Term Restoration Act of 1984, \n\n98Stat.\n1585. Those amendments allow a generic competitor to file an abbreviated new drug application (ANDA) piggy-backing on the brand\xe2\x80\x99s NDA. Rather than providing independent evidence of safety and efficacy, the typical ANDA shows that the generic drug has the same active ingredients as, and is biologically equivalent to, the brand-name drug. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7355(j)(2)(A)(ii), (iv). As we have previously recognized, this process is designed to speed the introduction of low-cost generic drugs to market. See Eli Lilly & Co. v. Medtronic, Inc., \n\n496 U.\xc2\xa0S. 661, \n676 (1990)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBecause the FDA cannot authorize a generic drug that would infringe a patent, the timing of an ANDA\xe2\x80\x99s approval depends on the scope and duration of the patents cover- ing the brand-name drug. Those patents come in different varieties. One type protects the drug compound itself. Another kind\xe2\x80\x94the one at issue here\xe2\x80\x94gives the brand manufacturer exclusive rights over a particular method of using the drug. In some circumstances, a brand manufacturer may hold such a method-of-use patent even after its patent on the drug compound has expired.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo facilitate the approval of generic drugs as soon as patents allow, the Hatch-Waxman Amendments and FDA regulations direct brand manufacturers to file information about their patents. The statute mandates that a brand submit in its NDA \xe2\x80\x9cthe patent number and the expiration date of any patent which claims the drug for which the [brand] submitted the [NDA] or which claims a method of using such drug.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7355(b)(1). And the regulations issued under that statute require that, once an NDA is approved, the brand provide a description of any method-of-use patent it holds. See 21 CFR \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7314.53(c)(2)(ii)(P)(3), (e) (2011). That description is known as a use code, and the brand submits it on FDA Form 3542. As later discussed, the FDA does not attempt to verify the accuracy of the use codes that brand manufacturers supply. It simply pub- lishes the codes, along with the corresponding patent numbers and expiration dates, in a fat, brightly hued volume called the Orange Book (less colorfully but more officially denominated Approved Drug Products with Therapeutic Equivalence Evaluations).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAfter consulting the Orange Book, a company filing an ANDA must assure the FDA that its proposed generic drug will not infringe the brand\xe2\x80\x99s patents. When no patents are listed in the Orange Book or all listed patents have expired (or will expire prior to the ANDA\xe2\x80\x99s approval), the generic manufacturer simply certifies to that effect. See 21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7355(j)(2)(A)(vii)(I)\xe2\x80\x93(III). Otherwise, the applicant has two possible ways to obtain approval.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOne option is to submit a so-called section viii statement, which asserts that the generic manufacturer will market the drug for one or more methods of use not covered by the brand\xe2\x80\x99s patents. See \xc2\xa7355(j)(2)(A)(viii). A section viii statement is typically used when the brand\xe2\x80\x99s patent on the drug compound has expired and the brand holds patents on only some approved methods of using the drug. If the ANDA applicant follows this route, it will propose labeling for the generic drug that \xe2\x80\x9ccarves out\xe2\x80\x9d from the brand\xe2\x80\x99s approved label the still-patented methods of use. See 21 CFR \xc2\xa7314.94(a)(8)(iv). The FDA may approve such a modified label, see \xc2\xa7314.127(a)(7), as an ex- ception to the usual rule that a generic drug must bear the same label as the brand-name product, see 21 U. S. C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7355(j)(2)(A)(v), (j)(4)(G). FDA acceptance of the carve-out label allows the generic company to place its drug on the market (assuming the ANDA meets other requirements), but only for a subset of approved uses\xe2\x80\x94i.e., those not covered by the brand\xe2\x80\x99s patents.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOf particular relevance here, the FDA will not approve such an ANDA if the generic\xe2\x80\x99s proposed carve-out label overlaps at all with the brand\xe2\x80\x99s use code. See 68 Fed. Reg. 36682\xe2\x80\x9336683 (2003). The FDA takes that code as a given: It does not independently assess the patent\xe2\x80\x99s scope or otherwise look behind the description authored by the brand. According to the agency, it lacks \xe2\x80\x9cboth [the] expertise and [the] authority\xe2\x80\x9d to review patent claims; although it will forward questions about the accuracy of a use code to the brand,\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t its own \xe2\x80\x9crole with respect to patent listing is ministerial.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 36683; see ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9cA fundamental assumption of the Hatch-Waxman Amendments is that the courts are the appropriate mechanism for the resolution of disputes about the scope and validity of patents\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Thus, whether section viii is available to a generic manufacturer depends on how the brand describes its patent. Only if the use code provides sufficient space for the generic\xe2\x80\x99s proposed label will the FDA approve an ANDA with a section viii statement.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe generic manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s second option is to file a so-called paragraph IV certification, which states that a listed patent \xe2\x80\x9cis invalid or will not be infringed by the manufacture, use, or sale of the [generic] drug.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7355(j)(2)(A)(vii)(IV). A generic manufacturer will typically take this path in either of two situations: if it wants to market the drug for all uses, rather than carving out those still allegedly under patent; or if it discovers, as described above, that any carve-out label it is willing to adopt cannot avoid the brand\xe2\x80\x99s use code. Filing a paragraph IV certification means provoking litigation. The patent statute treats such a filing as itself an act of infringement, which gives the brand an immediate right to sue. See \n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7271(e)(2)(A). Assuming the brand does so, the FDA generally may not approve the ANDA until 30 months pass or the court finds the patent invalid or not infringed. See \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7355(j)(5)(B)(iii). Accordingly, the paragraph IV process is likely to keep the gen- eric drug off the market for a lengthy period, but may eventually enable the generic company to market its drug for all approved uses.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn the late 1990\xe2\x80\x99s, evidence mounted that some brands were exploiting this statutory scheme to prevent or delay the marketing of generic drugs, and the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) soon issued a study detailing these anticompetitive practices. See FTC, Generic Drug Entry Prior to Patent Expiration: An FTC Study, pp. iii\xe2\x80\x93vi (July 2002) (hereinafter FTC Study). That report focused at- tention on brands\xe2\x80\x99 submission of inaccurate patent information to the FDA. In one case cited by the FTC, Mylan Pharmaceuticals, Inc. v. Thompson, 268 F.\xc2\xa03d 1323 (CA Fed. 2001), a brand whose original patent on a drug was set to expire listed a new patent ostensibly extending its rights over the drug, but in fact covering neither the compound nor any method of using it. The FDA, as was (and is) its wont, accepted the listing at its word and accord- ingly declined to approve a generic product. The generic manufacturer sued to delete the improper listing from the Orange Book, but the Federal Circuit held that the Hatch-Waxman Amendments did not allow such a right of action. See id., at 1330\xe2\x80\x931333. As the FTC noted, that ruling meant that the only option for generic manufacturers in Mylan\xe2\x80\x99s situation was to file a paragraph IV certification (triggering an infringement suit) and then wait out the usual 30-month period before the FDA could approve an ANDA. See FTC Study 40\xe2\x80\x9345.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress responded to these abuses by creating a mechanism, in the form of a legal counterclaim, for generic manufacturers to challenge patent information a brand has submitted to the FDA. See Medicare Prescription Drug, Improvement, and Modernization Act of 2003, \n\n117Stat.\n2452. The provision authorizes an ANDA applicant sued for patent infringement to\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cassert a counterclaim seeking an order requiring the [brand] to correct or delete the patent information submitted by the [brand] under subsection (b) or (c) [of \xc2\xa7355] on the ground that the patent does not claim either\xe2\x80\x94\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c(aa) the drug for which the [brand\xe2\x80\x99s NDA] was\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tapproved; or\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c(bb) an approved method of using the drug.\xe2\x80\x9d 21\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tU.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7355(j)(5)(C)(ii)(I).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe counterclaim thus enables a generic competitor to obtain a judgment directing a brand to \xe2\x80\x9ccorrect or delete\xe2\x80\x9d certain patent information that is blocking the FDA\xe2\x80\x99s approval of a generic product. This case raises the question whether the counterclaim is available to fix a brand\xe2\x80\x99s use code.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe parties to this case sell or seek to sell the diabetes drug repaglinide. Respondents (collectively Novo) manufacture Prandin, the brand-name version of the drug. The FDA has approved three uses of Prandin to treat diabe- tes: repaglinide by itself; repaglinide in combination with metformin; and repaglinide in combination with thiazolidinediones (TZDs). Petitioners (collectively Caraco) wish to market a generic version of the drug for two of those uses.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNovo originally owned a patent for the repaglinide compound, known as the \xe2\x80\x99035 patent, but it expired in 2009. In 2004, Novo also acquired a method-of-use patent for the drug, called the \xe2\x80\x99358 patent, which does not expire until 2018. That patent\xe2\x80\x94the one at issue here\xe2\x80\x94claims a \xe2\x80\x9cmethod for treating [diabetes by] administering .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. repaglinide in combination with metformin.\xe2\x80\x9d 601 F.\xc2\xa03d 1359, 1362 (CA Fed. 2010). Thus, Novo currently holds a patent for one of the three FDA-approved uses of repaglinide\xe2\x80\x94its use with metformin. But Novo holds no patent for the use of repaglinide with TZDs or its use alone.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 2005, Caraco filed an ANDA seeking to market a generic version of repaglinide. At that time, the Orange Book entry for Prandin listed both the \xe2\x80\x99035 patent (the drug compound) and the \xe2\x80\x99358 patent (the use of the drug with metformin). Caraco assured the FDA that it would not market its generic drug until the \xe2\x80\x99035 patent expired, thus making that patent irrelevant to the FDA\xe2\x80\x99s review of the ANDA. Caraco filed a paragraph IV certification for the remaining, \xe2\x80\x99358 patent, stating that it was \xe2\x80\x9cinvalid or [would] not be infringed.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7355(j)(2)(A)(vii)(IV); see supra, at 5. In accord with the patent statute, Novo treated this filing as an act of infringement and brought suit.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhen Caraco filed its ANDA, Novo\xe2\x80\x99s use code for the \xe2\x80\x99358 patent represented that the patent covered \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[u]se of repaglinide in combination with metformin to lower blood glucose.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 601 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1362\xe2\x80\x931363. The FDA therefore advised Caraco that if it did not seek to market repag- linide for use with metformin, it could submit a section viii statement. That would allow Caraco, assuming its ANDA was otherwise in order, to market its generic drug for the other two uses. Caraco took the FDA\xe2\x80\x99s cue and in 2008 submitted a section viii statement, with proposed labeling carving out Novo\xe2\x80\x99s patented metformin therapy. See App. 166\xe2\x80\x93176.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBefore the FDA took further action, however, Novo changed its use code for the \xe2\x80\x99358 patent. The new use code describes \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[a] method for improving glycemic control in adults with type 2 diabetes.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t 601 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1363. Because that code indicates that the \xe2\x80\x99358 patent protects all three approved methods of using repaglinide to treat diabetes, Caraco\xe2\x80\x99s proposed carve-out of metformin ther- apy was no longer sufficient; even with that exclusion, Caraco\xe2\x80\x99s label now overlapped with Novo\xe2\x80\x99s use code on the other two uses. And Caraco could not carve out those uses as well, because at that point nothing would be left for it to market. The FDA has approved repaglinide for only three uses, and Novo\xe2\x80\x99s use code encompassed them all. The FDA accordingly informed Caraco that it could no longer employ section viii to bring its drug to market.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCaraco responded to Novo\xe2\x80\x99s new, preclusive use code by filing a statutory counterclaim in the ongoing infringement suit. The counterclaim sought an order requiring Novo to \xe2\x80\x9ccorrect\xe2\x80\x9d its use code \xe2\x80\x9con the ground that [the \xe2\x80\x99358] patent does not claim\xe2\x80\x9d two approved methods of using repaglinide\xe2\x80\x94alone and in combination with TZDs. \xc2\xa7355(j)(5)(C)(ii)(I); see supra, at 6\xe2\x80\x937. That order would permit the FDA to accept Caraco\xe2\x80\x99s proposed carve-out label and approve the company\xe2\x80\x99s ANDA. The District Court granted summary judgment to Caraco, enjoining Novo to \xe2\x80\x9ccorrect .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. its inaccurate description of the \xe2\x80\x99358 patent\xe2\x80\x9d by submitting a new Form 3542 to the FDA that would \xe2\x80\x9creinstat[e] its former\xe2\x80\x9d use code. App. to Pet. for Cert. 65a\xe2\x80\x9366a.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court of Appeals reversed, holding that Caraco lacked \xe2\x80\x9ca statutory basis to assert a counterclaim.\xe2\x80\x9d 601 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1360. The court first read the statutory phrase \xe2\x80\x9cthe patent does not claim .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. an approved method of using the drug\xe2\x80\x9d to require Caraco to demonstrate that the \xe2\x80\x99358 patent does not claim any approved method of use. See id., at 1365 (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[A]n approved method\xe2\x80\x99 means \xe2\x80\x98any approved method\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). Because the patent covers one approved method of use\xe2\x80\x94repaglinide in combination with metformin\xe2\x80\x94the counterclaim was unavailable. The court further ruled that the counterclaim provision does not reach use codes because they are not \xe2\x80\x9cpatent information submitted by the [brand] under subsection (b) or (c).\xe2\x80\x9d On the Federal Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s view, that information consists only of the patent number and expiration date. See id., at 1366\xe2\x80\x931367. Judge Dyk dissented. He would have read the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cthe patent does not claim .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. an approved method of using the drug\xe2\x80\x9d to include situations where, as here, the use code wrongly indicates that the patent covers one or more particular approved methods of use. See id., at 1376\xe2\x80\x931378. And he would have construed \xe2\x80\x9cpatent in- formation submitted .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. under subsection (b) or (c)\xe2\x80\x9d to include use codes. See id., at 1370\xe2\x80\x931376.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe granted certiorari, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2011), and now reverse.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe begin \xe2\x80\x9cwhere all such inquiries must begin: with the language of the statute itself.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Ron Pair Enterprises, Inc., \n\n489 U.\xc2\xa0S. 235, \n241 (1989)\n. This case requires us to construe two statutory phrases. First, we must decide when a \xe2\x80\x9cpatent does not claim . . . an approved method of using\xe2\x80\x9d a drug. Second, we must determine the content of \xe2\x80\x9cpatent information submitted . . . under subsection (b) or (c)\xe2\x80\x9d of \xc2\xa7355. We consider both of those questions against the backdrop of yet a third statutory phrase, providing that the remedy for a prevailing counterclaimant is an order requiring the brand \xe2\x80\x9cto correct or delete\xe2\x80\x9d that patent information. And we consider each question in the context of the entire statute. See Robinson v. Shell Oil Co., \n\n519 U.\xc2\xa0S. 337, \n341 (1997)\n (Statutory interpretation focuses on \xe2\x80\x9cthe language itself, the specific context in which that language is used, and the broader context of the statute as a whole\xe2\x80\x9d). We cannot say that the counterclaim clause is altogether free of ambiguity. But when we consider statutory text and context together, we conclude that a generic manufacturer in Caraco\xe2\x80\x99s position can use the counterclaim.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAn ANDA applicant sued for patent infringement may bring a counterclaim \xe2\x80\x9con the ground that the patent does not claim .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. an approved method of using the drug.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7355(j)(5)(C)(ii)(I). The parties debate the meaning of this language. Novo (like the Federal Circuit) reads \xe2\x80\x9cnot an\xe2\x80\x9d to mean \xe2\x80\x9cnot any,\xe2\x80\x9d contending that \xe2\x80\x9cthe counterclaim is available only if the listed patent does not claim any (or, equivalently, claims no) approved method of using the drug.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 29 (internal quotation marks omitted). By that measure, Caraco may not bring a counterclaim because Novo\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x99358 patent claims the use of repaglinide with metformin. In contrast, Caraco reads \xe2\x80\x9cnot an\xe2\x80\x9d to mean \xe2\x80\x9cnot a particular one,\xe2\x80\x9d so that the statute permits a counterclaim whenever the patent does not claim a method of use for which the ANDA applicant seeks to market the drug. On that view, the counterclaim is available here\xe2\x80\x94indeed, is available twice over\xe2\x80\x94because the \xe2\x80\x99358 patent does not claim the use of repaglinide with TZDs or its use alone.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTruth be told, the answer to the general question \xe2\x80\x9cWhat does \xe2\x80\x98not an\xe2\x80\x99 mean?\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cIt depends\xe2\x80\x9d: The meaning of the phrase turns on its context. See Johnson v. United States, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 5) (\xe2\x80\x9cUltimately, context determines meaning\xe2\x80\x9d). \xe2\x80\x9cNot an\xe2\x80\x9d sometimes means \xe2\x80\x9cnot any,\xe2\x80\x9d in the way Novo claims. If your spouse tells you he is late because he \xe2\x80\x9cdid not take a cab,\xe2\x80\x9d you will infer that he took no cab at all (but took the bus instead). If your child admits that she \xe2\x80\x9cdid not read a book all summer,\xe2\x80\x9d you will surmise that she did not read any book (but went to the movies a lot). And if a sports-fan friend bemoans that \xe2\x80\x9cthe New York Mets do not have a chance of winning the World Series,\xe2\x80\x9d you will gather that the team has no chance whatsoever (because they have no hitting). But now stop a moment. Suppose your spouse tells you that he got lost because he \xe2\x80\x9cdid not make a turn.\xe2\x80\x9d You would understand that he failed to make a particular turn, not that he drove from the outset in a straight line. Suppose your child explains her mediocre grade on a college exam by saying that she \xe2\x80\x9cdid not read an assigned text.\xe2\x80\x9d You would infer that she failed to read a specific book, not that she read nothing at all on the syllabus. And suppose a lawyer friend laments that in her last trial, she \xe2\x80\x9cdid not prove an element of the offense.\xe2\x80\x9d You would grasp that she is speaking not of all the elements, but of a particular one. The examples could go on and on, but the point is simple enough: When it comes to the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cnot an,\xe2\x80\x9d context matters.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAnd the statutory context here supports Caraco\xe2\x80\x99s position. As described earlier (and as Congress understood), a single drug may have multiple methods of use, only one or some of which a patent covers. See, e.g., \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7355(b)(1) (requiring that an NDA applicant file information about \xe2\x80\x9cany patent which claims the drug .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. or which claims a method of using such drug\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). The Hatch-Waxman Amendments authorize the FDA to approve the marketing of a generic drug for particular unpatented uses; and section viii provides the mechanism for a generic company to identify those uses, so that a product with a label matching them can quickly come to market. The statutory scheme, in other words, contemplates that one patented use will not foreclose marketing a generic drug for other unpatented ones. Within that framework, the counterclaim naturally functions to challenge the brand\xe2\x80\x99s assertion of rights over whichever discrete use (or uses) the generic company wishes to pursue. That assertion, after all, is the thing blocking the generic drug\xe2\x80\x99s entry on the market. The availability of the counterclaim thus matches the availability of FDA approval under the statute: A company may bring a counterclaim to show that a method of use is unpatented because establishing that fact allows the FDA to authorize a generic drug via section viii.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tConsider the point as applied to this case. Caraco wishes to market a generic version of repaglinide for two (and only two) uses. Under the statute, the FDA could approve Caraco\xe2\x80\x99s application so long as no patent covers those uses, regardless whether a patent protects yet a third method of using the drug. Novo agrees that Caraco could bring a counterclaim if Novo\xe2\x80\x99s assertion of patent protection for repaglinide lacked any basis\xe2\x80\x94for example, if Novo held no patent, yet claimed rights to the pair of uses for which Caraco seeks to market its drug. But because Novo has a valid patent on a different use, Novo argues that Caraco\xe2\x80\x99s counterclaim evaporates. And that is so even though, once again, Caraco has no wish to market its product for that patented use and the FDA stands ready, pursuant to the statute, to approve Caraco\xe2\x80\x99s product for the other two. To put the matter simply, Novo thinks the counterclaim disappears because it has a patent for a method of use in which neither Caraco nor the FDA is interested at all. \xe2\x80\x9cIt would take strong evidence to persuade us that this is what Congress wrought.\xe2\x80\x9d Eli Lilly, 496 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 673. That \xe2\x80\x9cnot an\xe2\x80\x9d sometimes (but sometimes not) means \xe2\x80\x9cnot any\xe2\x80\x9d is not enough.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNovo argues that our reading must be wrong because Congress could have expressly \xe2\x80\x9cimpose[d] additional .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. qualifications\xe2\x80\x9d on the term \xe2\x80\x9can approved method of us[e]\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94and indeed did so in another place in the statute. Brief for Respondents 31; \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7355(j)(5)(C)(ii)(I). Novo points here to section viii itself, which applies when the brand\xe2\x80\x99s patent \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not claim a use for which the [ANDA] applicant is seeking approval.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7355(j)(2)(A)(viii) (emphasis added). But the mere possibility of clearer phrasing cannot defeat the most natural reading of a statute; if it could (with all due respect to Congress), we would interpret a great many statutes differently than we do. Nor does Congress\xe2\x80\x99s use of more detailed language in another provision, enacted years earlier, persuade us to put the counterclaim clause at odds with its statutory context. That is especially so because we can turn this form of argument back around on Novo. Congress, after all, could have more clearly expressed Novo\xe2\x80\x99s proposed meaning in the easiest of ways\xe2\x80\x94by adding a single letter to make clear that \xe2\x80\x9cnot an\xe2\x80\x9d really means \xe2\x80\x9cnot any.\xe2\x80\x9d And indeed, Congress used a \xe2\x80\x9cnot any\xe2\x80\x9d construction in the very next subclause, enacted at the very same time. See \xc2\xa7355(j)(5)(C)(ii)(II) (\xe2\x80\x9cSubclause (I) does not authorize the assertion of a claim .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in any [other] civil action\xe2\x80\x9d). So if we needed any proof that Congress knew how to say \xe2\x80\x9cnot any\xe2\x80\x9d when it meant \xe2\x80\x9cnot any,\xe2\x80\x9d here we find it. We think that sees, raises, and bests Novo\xe2\x80\x99s argument.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur more essential point, though, has less gamesmanship about it: We think that the \xe2\x80\x9cnot any\xe2\x80\x9d construction does not appear in the relevant counterclaim provision because Congress did not mean what Novo wishes it had. And we think that is so because Congress meant (as it usually does) for the provision it enacted to fit within the statutory scheme\xe2\x80\x94here, by facilitating the approval of non-infringing generic drugs under section viii.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNovo contends that Caraco\xe2\x80\x99s counterclaim must fail for another, independent reason: On its view (as on the Federal Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s), the counterclaim does not provide a way to correct use codes because they are not \xe2\x80\x9cpatent information submitted by the [brand] under subsection (b) or (c)\xe2\x80\x9d of \xc2\xa7355. Once again, we disagree.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe statute does not define \xe2\x80\x9cpatent information,\xe2\x80\x9d but a use code must qualify. It describes the method of use claimed in a patent. See 21 CFR \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7314.53(c)(2)(ii)(P)(3), (e). That fits under any ordinary understanding of the language.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t7\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe more difficult question arises from the \xe2\x80\x9csubmitted under\xe2\x80\x9d phrase. The subsections mentioned there\xe2\x80\x94(b) and (c) of \xc2\xa7355\xe2\x80\x94require an NDA applicant to submit specified information: \xe2\x80\x9cthe patent number and the expiration date of any patent\xe2\x80\x9d claiming the drug or a method of its use. 21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7355(b)(1), (c)(2). According to Novo, only that information comes within the counterclaim provision. But subsections (b) and (c) as well govern the regulatory process by which brands provide additional patent information to the FDA, both before and after an NDA is approved. In particular, those subsections provide the basis for the regulation requiring brands to submit use codes, see 21 CFR \xc2\xa7314.53; in issuing that regulation, the FDA noted that \xe2\x80\x9c[o]ur principal legal authority . . . is section 505 of the act [codified at \xc2\xa7355], in conjunction with our general rulemaking authority.\xe2\x80\x9d 68 Fed. Reg. 36697\xe2\x80\x9336698 (specifically referring to subsections (b) and (c)). And the form (Form 3542) on which brands submit their use codes states that the information appearing there is \xe2\x80\x9cprovided in accordance with Section [355](b) and (c).\xe2\x80\x9d App. 97. So use codes fall within the counterclaim\xe2\x80\x99s ambit if the phrase \xe2\x80\x9csubmitted under\xe2\x80\x9d reaches filings that not only subsections (b) and (c) themselves, but also their implementing regulations require.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSeveral of our cases support giving \xe2\x80\x9cunder\xe2\x80\x9d this broad meaning. For example, in Eli Lilly, 496 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 665\xe2\x80\x93668, we examined a similar statutory reference to the \xe2\x80\x9csubmission of information under a Federal law which regulates the manufacture, use, or sale of drugs,\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7271(e)(1). We noted there that submitting information \xe2\x80\x9cunder a Federal law\xe2\x80\x9d suggests doing so \xe2\x80\x9cin furtherance of or compliance with a comprehensive scheme of regulation.\xe2\x80\x9d 496 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 667. Likewise, in Ardestani v. INS, \n\n502 U.\xc2\xa0S. 129, \n135 (1991)\n, we held that a regulatory proceeding \xe2\x80\x9cunder section 554,\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7504(b)(1)(C)(i), meant any proceeding \xe2\x80\x9csubject to,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cgoverned by,\xe2\x80\x9d or conducted \xe2\x80\x9cby reason of the authority of\xe2\x80\x9d that statutory provision.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSo too here. \xe2\x80\x9cPatent information submitted .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. under subsection (b) or (c)\xe2\x80\x9d most naturally refers to patent information provided as part of the \xe2\x80\x9ccomprehensive scheme of regulation\xe2\x80\x9d premised on those subsections. Eli Lilly, 496 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 667. It includes everything (about patents) that the FDA requires brands to furnish in the proceedings \xe2\x80\x9csubject to,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cgoverned by,\xe2\x80\x9d or conducted \xe2\x80\x9cby reason of the authority of\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7355(b) and (c). Ardestani, 502 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 135. The breadth of the term \xe2\x80\x9cunder\xe2\x80\x9d becomes particu- larly clear when compared with other phrases\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cdescribed in\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cprescribed by\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94appearing in neighboring provisions. See, e.g., \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7355(c)(2) (\xe2\x80\x9cpatent information described in subsection (b)\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa7355(d)(6) (\xe2\x80\x9cpatent information prescribed by subsection (b)\xe2\x80\x9d). Those phrases denote a patent number and expiration date and nothing more. In contrast, the word \xe2\x80\x9cunder\xe2\x80\x9d naturally reaches beyond that most barebones information to other patent materials the FDA demands in the regulatory process.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOnce again, that congressional choice fits the broader statutory context. Use codes are pivotal to the FDA\xe2\x80\x99s implementation of the Hatch-Waxman Amendments\xe2\x80\x94and no less so because a regulation, rather than the statute itself, requires their submission. Recall that those Amendments instruct the FDA (assuming other requirements are met) to approve an ANDA filed with a section viii statement when it proposes to market a drug for only unpatented methods of use. To fulfill that charge, the FDA must determine whether any patent covers a particular method of use; and to do that, the agency (which views itself as lacking expertise in patent matters, see supra, at 4\xe2\x80\x935, and n.\xc2\xa02) relies on the use codes submitted in the regulatory process. See 68 Fed. Reg. 36682\xe2\x80\x9336683. An overbroad use code therefore throws a wrench into the FDA\xe2\x80\x99s ability to approve generic drugs as the statute con- templates. So it is not surprising that the language Congress used in the counterclaim provision sweeps widely enough to embrace that filing.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tC\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAnother aspect of the counterclaim provision\xe2\x80\x94its description of available remedies\xe2\x80\x94dispatches whatever remains of Novo\xe2\x80\x99s arguments. According to the statute, a successful claimant may obtain an order requiring the brand to \xe2\x80\x9ccorrect or delete\xe2\x80\x9d its patent information. \xc2\xa7355(j)(5)(C)(ii)(I). Our interpretation of the statute gives content to both those remedies: It deletes a listing from the Orange Book when the brand holds no relevant patent and corrects the listing when the brand has misdescribed the patent\xe2\x80\x99s scope. By contrast, Novo\xe2\x80\x99s two arguments would all but read the term \xe2\x80\x9ccorrect\xe2\x80\x9d out of the statute.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tConsider first how Novo\xe2\x80\x99s an-means-any contention would accomplish that result. Recall that on Novo\xe2\x80\x99s view, a counterclaim can succeed only if the patent challenged does not claim either the drug or any approved method of using it. See supra, at 11. But when a generic manufacturer makes that showing, the remedy must be to \xe2\x80\x9cdelete\xe2\x80\x9d the listing; no correction would be enough. Novo agrees with that proposition; \xe2\x80\x9c[a]t bottom,\xe2\x80\x9d Novo avers, \xe2\x80\x9cthe counterclaim is a delisting provision.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 20. But that raises the obvious question: Why did Congress also include the term \xe2\x80\x9ccorrect\xe2\x80\x9d in the statute?\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNovo can come up with just one answer: The counterclaim, it proposes, can correct erroneous patent numbers. Imagine, for example, that Novo mistakenly entered the number \xe2\x80\x99359, instead of \xe2\x80\x99358, when submitting information about its repaglinide patent for publication in the Orange Book. Then, Novo suggests, Caraco could bring a counterclaim to challenge the inaccurate listing (on the ground that \xe2\x80\x99359 does not claim any method of use), and the remedy would be \xe2\x80\x9ccorrect[ion]\xe2\x80\x9d (substituting an 8 for a 9). But we think Novo\xe2\x80\x99s admission that this scenario would be \xe2\x80\x9cunusual,\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 41, considerably understates the matter. As Novo concedes, brands have every incentive to provide the right patent number in the first place, and to immediately rectify any error brought to their attention. See id., at 40\xe2\x80\x9341. By doing so, they place both generic companies and the FDA on notice of their patents and thereby prevent infringement. And conversely, generics have little or no incentive to bring a counterclaim that will merely replace one digit in the Orange Book with another. So we doubt Congress created a legal action to \xe2\x80\x9ccorrect\xe2\x80\x9d patent information just to fix such scrivener\xe2\x80\x99s errors. See, e.g., TRW Inc. v. Andrews, \n\n534 U.\xc2\xa0S. 19, \n31 (2001)\n (refusing to adopt an interpretation of a statute that would render a piece of it \xe2\x80\x9cinsignificant, if not wholly superfluous\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). That would have been, in the most literal sense, to make a federal case out of nothing.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe same problem afflicts Novo\xe2\x80\x99s alternative contention\xe2\x80\x94that \xe2\x80\x9cpatent information submitted .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. under subsection (b) or (c)\xe2\x80\x9d includes only numbers and expiration dates (and not use codes). Once again, we cannot think Congress included the remedy of \xe2\x80\x9ccorrect[ion]\xe2\x80\x9d so that courts could expunge typos in patent numbers. And not even Novo has proffered a way for the counterclaim to \xe2\x80\x9ccorrect\xe2\x80\x9d an erroneous expiration date. Suppose, for example, that a brand incorrectly lists the expiration date of a valid patent as 2018 rather than 2015. The counterclaim would be useless: It authorizes a remedy only \xe2\x80\x9con the ground that\xe2\x80\x9d the listed patent does not claim the drug or an approved method of using it\xe2\x80\x94and notwithstanding the wrong expiration date, this patent does so. Alternatively, suppose the brand lists a patent as having a 2018 expiration date when in fact the patent has already lapsed. Then, a generic manufacturer could bring a counterclaim alleging that the patent no longer claims the drug or a method of using it\xe2\x80\x94but the appropriate remedy would be deletion, not correction, of the brand\xe2\x80\x99s listing. Novo\xe2\x80\x99s reading of \xe2\x80\x9cpatent information,\xe2\x80\x9d like its reading of \xe2\x80\x9cnot an,\xe2\x80\x9d effectively deletes the term \xe2\x80\x9ccorrect\xe2\x80\x9d from the statute.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNovo finally advances two arguments relating to the counterclaim\xe2\x80\x99s drafting history. Neither contention, however, overcomes the statutory text and context. Indeed, consideration of the provision\xe2\x80\x99s background only strengthens our view of its meaning.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNovo first contends that our interpretation of the statute \xe2\x80\x9ceffectively resurrect[s] the scheme rejected by Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 44 (quoting Smith v. United States, \n\n507 U.\xc2\xa0S. 197\n, n.\xc2\xa04 (1993)). In 2002, Novo notes, Congress failed to pass a bill that would have required brands to file specified \xe2\x80\x9cpatent information,\xe2\x80\x9d including, for method-of-use patents, a description of \xe2\x80\x9cthe approved use covered by the [patent] claim.\xe2\x80\x9d S. 812, 107th Cong., 2d Sess., \xc2\xa7103(a)(1), p. 7 (engrossed bill). That bill would have allowed a generic company to bring its own civil action\xe2\x80\x94not merely a counterclaim in ongoing litigation\xe2\x80\x94to \xe2\x80\x9cdelete\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ccorrect\xe2\x80\x9d the information filed. Id., at 8. The Senate approved the bill, but the House of Repre- sentatives took no action on it. Novo argues that because this failed legislation would have allowed a generic company to challenge overbroad descriptions of a patent, we cannot read the statute Congress eventually enacted as doing so.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe disagree. We see no reason to assume, as Novo does, that Congress rejected S. 812 because it required brands to submit patent information beyond a number and expiration date. Indeed, Novo\xe2\x80\x99s argument highlights the perils of relying on the fate of prior bills to divine the meaning of enacted legislation. \xe2\x80\x9cA bill can be proposed for any number of reasons, and it can be rejected for just as many others.\xe2\x80\x9d Solid Waste Agency of Northern Cook Cty. v. Army Corps of Engineers, \n\n531 U.\xc2\xa0S. 159, \n170 (2001)\n). S. 812 contained numerous items, including a title on importing prescription drugs (no controversy there!), that may have caused its failure. See S. 812, Tit. II. Moreover, what criticism there was of the bill\xe2\x80\x99s mechanism for challenging brands\xe2\x80\x99 patent claims focused not on the specification of \xe2\x80\x9cpatent information,\xe2\x80\x9d but instead on the creation of an independent cause of action\xe2\x80\x94stronger medicine than the counterclaim Congress ultimately adopted.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t8\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t And finally, Novo ignores a likely cause for the redrafting of the provision on submitting information. Between S. 812\xe2\x80\x99s demise and the counterclaim\xe2\x80\x99s enactment, the FDA issued a rule requiring brands to supply material concerning method-of-use patents, including use codes. The drafters of the counterclaim provision knew about that rule,\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t9\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t and had no need to duplicate its list of mandated filings. So the drafting history does not support Novo\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion. If anything, the statute\xe2\x80\x99s evolution indicates that Congress determined to enforce the FDA\xe2\x80\x99s new listing provisions, including its use-code requirement, through the new counterclaim.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNovo next argues that Congress established the counterclaim only to solve the problem raised by the Federal Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Mylan, 268 F.\xc2\xa03d 1323\xe2\x80\x94the impossibility of deleting an improperly listed patent from the Orange Book. In Mylan, as earlier described, a generic company alleged that a brand had listed a patent that covered neither the approved drug nor any method of using it, and brought an action seeking delisting. See supra, at 6. The Federal Circuit held that no such action was available, even assuming the allegation was true. Because several legislators saw Mylan as \xe2\x80\x9cexemplif[ying]\xe2\x80\x9d brands\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cperceived abuse\xe2\x80\x9d of the FDA\xe2\x80\x99s patent listing prac- tices, Brief for Respondents 35, Novo contends that we should construe the counterclaim provision to aid only a generic company that \xe2\x80\x9cfinds itself in the same position as Mylan was in Mylan,\xe2\x80\x9d Supp. Brief in Opposition 5\xe2\x80\x936.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOnce again, we think not. Maybe Mylan triggered the legislative effort to enact a counterclaim, or maybe it didn\xe2\x80\x99t: By the time Congress acted, it also had at hand an FTC study broadly criticizing brands\xe2\x80\x99 patent listings and an FDA rule designed to address the very same issue. See supra, at 6, 21. But even assuming Mylan \xe2\x80\x9cprompted the proposal\xe2\x80\x9d of the counterclaim, \xe2\x80\x9cwhether that alone accounted for its enactment is quite a different question.\xe2\x80\x9d Eli Lilly, 496 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 670, n.\xc2\xa03 (emphasis deleted). Here, we think Mylan alerted Congress to a broader problem\xe2\x80\x94that generic companies generally had no avenue to challenge the accuracy of brands\xe2\x80\x99 patent listings, and that the FDA therefore could not approve proper applications to bring inexpensive drugs to market. The proof of that lies in the statute itself (where the best proof of what Congress means to address almost always resides). As we have de- scribed, the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text and context demonstrate that the counterclaim is available not only (as in Mylan) when the patent listing is baseless, but also (as here) when it is overbroad. See supra, at 10\xe2\x80\x9320. In particular, Congress\xe2\x80\x99s decision to allow a counterclaimant to seek \xe2\x80\x9ccorrect[ion]\xe2\x80\x9d of patent information explodes Novo\xe2\x80\x99s theory, because the remedy for a Mylan-type impropriety is complete delisting.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAnd to make matters still easier, Congress\xe2\x80\x99s equation of the two situations\xe2\x80\x94the one in Mylan and the one here\xe2\x80\x94makes perfect sense. Whether a brand lists a patent that covers no use or describes a patent on one use as extending to others, the brand submits misleading patent in- formation to the FDA. In doing so, the brand equally ex- ploits the FDA\xe2\x80\x99s determination that it cannot police patent claims. And the brand\xe2\x80\x99s action may in either case delay or block approval of a generic drug that infringes no patent\xe2\x80\x94and that under the statute should go to market. See su- pra, at 4. That is the danger Caraco faces here, as much as it was the threat in Mylan: Novo seeks to preclude Caraco from selling repaglinide for unpatented uses until 2018, when Novo\xe2\x80\x99s patent on a different use expires.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIndeed, the need for the counterclaim is greater here than in Mylan. When a brand lists a patent that covers no use, a generic company has a pathway aside from the counterclaim to challenge the listing. As described ear- lier, the company may make a paragraph IV certification stating that the listed patent \xe2\x80\x9cis invalid or will not be infringed\xe2\x80\x9d by the generic drug. \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7355(j)(2)(A)(vii)(IV); see supra, at 5. If the brand sues, the generic company can argue that its product would not infringe the patent. Using the counterclaim may enable a generic manufacturer to obtain delisting more quickly, see Tr. of Oral Arg. 54; but even without it, the company can eventually get a judgment of non-infringement enabling the FDA to approve its ANDA. In contrast, where (as here) a brand files an overbroad use code, a generic company cannot use paragraph IV litigation to that end. A paragraph IV certification (unlike a section viii statement) requires the generic company to propose labeling identical to the brand\xe2\x80\x99s; it cannot carve out any uses. See supra, at 4. And that proposed label will necessarily infringe because it will include the use(s) on which the brand does have a patent. So here, a paragraph IV suit cannot lead to a judgment enabling FDA approval; the counterclaim offers the only route to bring the generic drug to market for non-infringing uses. Novo\xe2\x80\x99s view eliminates the counterclaim where it has the greatest value.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIV\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe statutory counterclaim we have considered enables courts to resolve patent disputes so that the FDA can fulfill its statutory duty to approve generic drugs that do not infringe patent rights. The text and context of the provision demonstrate that a generic company can employ the counterclaim to challenge a brand\xe2\x80\x99s overbroad use code. We accordingly hold that Caraco may bring a counterclaim seeking to \xe2\x80\x9ccorrect\xe2\x80\x9d Novo\xe2\x80\x99s use code \xe2\x80\x9con the ground that\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x99358 patent \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not claim .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. an approved method of using the drug\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94indeed, does not claim two.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe judgment of the Court of Appeals is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.\n\t\t\t

The want of an infringing act is a merits problem, not a jurisdictional one. Nothing in the section of the statute defining certain filings as acts of infringement suggests anything to the contrary. And \xe2\x80\x9cwe are not inclined to interpret statutes as creating a jurisdictional bar when they are not framed as such.\xe2\x80\x9d Stern v. Marshall, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 13). In the absence of such a bar, the federal courts have jurisdiction over this suit for a single, simple reason: It \xe2\x80\x9car[ose] under a[n] Act of Congress relating to patents.\xe2\x80\x9d \n.\n\t\n
\n\n6\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0For this reason, we find Novo\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on the occasional dictionary definition of \xe2\x80\x9ca[n]\xe2\x80\x9d unconvincing. Although \xe2\x80\x9can\xe2\x80\x9d sometimes means \xe2\x80\x9cany\xe2\x80\x9d when used in negative structures, see, e.g., Microsoft Encarta College Dictionary 1 (2001) (fifth definition), it sometimes does not. Cf. FCC v. AT&T Inc., 562 U. S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip. op., at 3\xe2\x80\x935) (rejecting a proposed definition of \xe2\x80\x9cpersonal\xe2\x80\x9d because it did not always hold in ordinary usage and the statutory context suggested it did not apply).\n\t\n
\n\n7\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0Novo\xe2\x80\x99s only counter is to redefine a use code. Novo argues that a use code need not be tied to the patent at all\xe2\x80\x94that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he relevant regulation requires [NDA] applicants to provide [only] \xe2\x80\x98a description of each approved method of use or indication.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 48 (quoting 21 CFR \xc2\xa7314.53(c)(2)(ii)(P)(1)). Because an \xe2\x80\x9cindication\xe2\x80\x9d refers generally to what a drug does (here, treat diabetes), see \xc2\xa7201.57(c)(2), Novo claims that a use code may sweep more broadly than the patent. But that is incorrect. First, Novo does not cite the regulations that specify the information required for publication\xe2\x80\x94i.e., use codes. See \xc2\xa7314.53(c)(2)(ii)(P)(3) (requiring a \xe2\x80\x9cdescription of the patented method of use as required for publication\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa7314.53(e) (\xe2\x80\x9c[F]or each use patent,\xe2\x80\x9d FDA will publish \xe2\x80\x9cthe approved indications or other conditions of use covered by a patent\xe2\x80\x9d). Those provisions (whether referring to methods of use, conditions of use, or indications) all demand a description of the patent. And second, even the provision Novo cites\xe2\x80\x94which mandates the submission of additional material, not listed in the Orange Book\xe2\x80\x94ties information about indications to patent coverage; that regulation requires (when quoted in full) that the brand provide \xe2\x80\x9ca description of each approved method of use or indication and related patent claim of the patent being submitted.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7314.53(c)(2)(ii)(P)(1).\n\t\n

\n\t\t\t\ton writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the federal circuit\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t[April 17, 2012] \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Sotomayor, concurring.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court today interprets the counterclaim set forth in \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7355(j)(5)(C)(ii)(I) to permit generic manufacturers to force brand manufacturers to \xe2\x80\x9ccorrect\xe2\x80\x9d inaccurate use codes. While I too find the counterclaim not \xe2\x80\x9cfree of ambiguity,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 10, I join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion because I agree this is the most sensible reading in light of the existing regulatory scheme. I write separately to add the following observations.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI first underscore that the counterclaim can only lessen the difficulties created by an overly broad use code; it cannot fix them. The statutory scheme is designed to speed the introduction of low-cost generic drugs to market. See ante, at 2 (citing Eli Lilly & Co. v. Medtronic, Inc., \n\n496 U.\xc2\xa0S. 661, \n676 (1990)\n). To that end, the statute provides for the rapid approval of a drug that a generic manufacturer seeks to market for unpatented methods of use. The manufacturer need only submit an abbreviated new drug application (ANDA) with a section viii statement and a proposed label that \xe2\x80\x9ccarves out\xe2\x80\x9d from the brand manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s label any patented methods of use. See ante, at 4. So long as the use code is not overly broad (and all else is in order), FDA may approve the application without requiring any further steps relating to the patent, and the generic drug may reach the public without undue delay. See ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAn overly broad use code \xe2\x80\x9cthrows a wrench\xe2\x80\x9d into that scheme. Ante, at 18. The reason why is simple: FDA relies on use codes in determining whether to approve an ANDA, but it refuses to evaluate the accuracy of those use codes. See ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x935. Thus, if the use code overlaps with the generic manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s proposed carve-out label (i.e., if the use code is overly broad), FDA will not approve an ANDA with a section viii statement. See ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAfter today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, the generic manufacturer can re-spond to this situation by taking the following steps: sub-mit an ANDA with a paragraph IV certification (which requires a proposed label materially identical to the brand manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s label, see ante, at 24), wait for the brand manufacturer to institute suit, file a counterclaim, litigate the counterclaim, and, if successful in securing the correction of the use code, return to the start of the process and do what it always wanted to do\xe2\x80\x94file an ANDA with a section viii statement and a carve-out label.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe problem with this process is twofold. First, it results in delay and expense the statutory scheme does not envision. Second, there is no guarantee the process will work. It depends on the brand manufacturer initiating paragraph IV litigation, but it is not obvious the brand will have any incentive to do so. In light of today\xe2\x80\x99s holding, the upshot of such litigation will be the correction of the use code through the assertion of a counterclaim\xe2\x80\x94an outcome that is desirable, to be sure, for the generic manufacturer, but perhaps less so for the brand manufacturer.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMeanwhile, it is not clear what happens if the brand manufacturer does not file suit. FDA may approve the generic manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s application, see \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7355(j)(5)(B)(iii), \xe2\x80\x9cwithout prejudice to infringement claims the patent owner might assert when the ANDA applicant produces or markets the generic drug.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 6 (hereinafter United States Brief). But the generic manufacturer, having been forced to proceed with a paragraph IV certification, will have se-cured approval to market a drug with a label materially identical to the brand manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s. That is not a position I imagine a generic manufacturer wants to be in: As the Solicitor General\xe2\x80\x99s Office informed us at argument, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t would be inducement of infringement to sell a product with labeling that suggests that the product be used for a patented method of use.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 24; see also United States Brief 32 (noting that in this situation, if a generic manufacturer proceeded with a paragraph IV certification, \xe2\x80\x9c[s]o long as the [new drug application (NDA)] holder\xe2\x80\x99s patent covers some approved method of using the approved drug, the proposed labeling will be infringing\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn short, the counterclaim cannot restore the smooth working of a statutory scheme thrown off kilter by an overly broad use code. At best, it permits the generic manufacturer to do what the scheme contemplates it should do\xe2\x80\x94file an ANDA with a section viii statement\xe2\x80\x94but only after expensive and time-consuming litigation. A fix is in order, but it must come from Congress or FDA.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPrecisely because the regulatory scheme depends on the accuracy and precision of use codes, I find FDA\xe2\x80\x99s guidance as to what is required of brand manufacturers in use codes remarkably opaque. The relevant regulation states simply that a brand manufacturer must provide \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he description of the patented method of use as required for publication.\xe2\x80\x9d 21 CFR \xc2\xa7314.53(c)(2)(ii)(P)(3). The form on which brand manufacturers submit that information provides some additional detail, explaining that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ach approved use claimed by the patent should be separately identified .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and contain adequate information to assist .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. applicants in determining whether a listed method of use patent claims a use for which the . . . applicant is not seeking approval.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 214a. But it also provides that brand manufacturers may \xe2\x80\x9cus[e] no more than 240 total characters including spaces,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 213a, and elsewhere FDA acknowledges \xe2\x80\x9cthat in some cases 240 characters may not fully describe the use as claimed in the patent.\xe2\x80\x9d 68 Fed. Reg. 36683 (2003); see also ibid. (indicating for this reason that use codes \xe2\x80\x9care not meant to substitute for the applicant\xe2\x80\x99s review of the patent\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIndeed, in some respects we are here today because of FDA\xe2\x80\x99s opacity in describing what is required of brand manufacturers. In its initial NDA filing, Novo submitted a use code for the \xe2\x80\x99358 patent that was not \xe2\x80\x9coverly broad\xe2\x80\x9d: It described narrowly the single patented method of use. App. 54\xe2\x80\x9355, 99. Some years later FDA required that Novo amend its label to \xe2\x80\x9c[r]eplace all the separate indications\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cwith the following sentence: \xe2\x80\x98Prandin is indicated as an adjunct to diet and exercise to improve glycemic control in adults with type 2 diabetes mellitus.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 163\xe2\x80\x93164, 215. Novo then amended its use code to track the new label, id., at 482\xe2\x80\x93486, explaining that the amendment \xe2\x80\x9ccorrespond[ed] with the change in labeling required by FDA,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 483. Novo understood its amended use code to comply with FDA regulations, likely on the ground it pressed before us: that the regulations permit a brand manufacturer to submit for publication in the Orange Book a description of either the patented method of use or the indication (which refers to \xe2\x80\x9cwhat a drug does,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 15, n. 7). Brief for Respondents 10, 22, 48\xe2\x80\x9350.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor the reasons explained by the Court, see ante, at 15, n.\xc2\xa07, Novo is mistaken. But the company can hardly be faulted for so thinking. The regulations also require submission of \xe2\x80\x9ca description of each approved method of use or indication,\xe2\x80\x9d 21 CFR \xc2\xa7314.53(c)(2)(ii)(P)(1), and the form on which brand manufacturers submit use codes requires \xe2\x80\x9cinformation on the indication or method of use for the Orange Book \xe2\x80\x98Use Code\xe2\x80\x99 description.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 213; see also ibid. (explaining brand manufacturers should \xe2\x80\x9c[s]ubmit the description of the approved indication or method of use that you propose FDA include as the \xe2\x80\x98Use Code\xe2\x80\x99 in the Orange Book\xe2\x80\x9d). Those sources at the least suggest (as Novo thought) that a method of use here is distinct from an indication and that either suffices as a use code.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPrior to enactment of the counterclaim provision, Congress considered a bill that required brand manufacturers to submit a \xe2\x80\x9cdescription of the approved use covered by the [patent] claim,\xe2\x80\x9d and that allowed a generic manufacturer to bring a civil action to correct that information. See ante, at 20. Congress rejected the bill, in part over criticism that it would encourage excess litigation.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Absent greater clarity from FDA concerning what is required of brand manufacturers in use codes, Congress\xe2\x80\x99 fears of un-due litigation may be realized.\n\t\t\t
