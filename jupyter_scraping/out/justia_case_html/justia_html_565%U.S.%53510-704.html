\n\t\t\t\tMESSERSCHMIDT et\xc2\xa0al. v. MILLENDER, executor of ESTATE OF MILLENDER, DECEASED, et\xc2\xa0al.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 10\xe2\x80\x93704.\xe2\x80\x83Argued December 5, 2011\xe2\x80\x94Decided February 22, 2012\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tShelly Kelly was afraid that she would be attacked by her boyfriend, Jerry Ray Bowen, while she moved out of her apartment. She therefore requested police protection. Two officers arrived, but they were called away to an emergency. As soon as the officers left, Bowen showed up at the apartment, yelled \xe2\x80\x9cI told you never to call the cops on me bitch!\xe2\x80\x9d and attacked Kelly, attempting to throw her over a second-story landing. After Kelly escaped to her car, Bowen pointed a sawed-off shotgun at her and threatened to kill her if she tried to leave. Kelly nonetheless sped away as Bowen fired five shots at the car, blowing out one of its tires. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tKelly later met with Detective Curt Messerschmidt to discuss the incident. She described the attack in detail, mentioned that Bowen had previously assaulted her, that he had ties to the Mona Park Crips gang, and that he might be staying at the home of his former foster mother, Augusta Millender. Following this conversation, Messerschmidt conducted a detailed investigation, during which he confirmed Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s connection to the Millenders\xe2\x80\x99 home, verified his membership in two gangs, and learned that Bowen had been arrested and convicted for numerous violent and firearm-related offenses. Based on this investigation, Messerschmidt drafted an application for a warrant authorizing a search of the Millenders\xe2\x80\x99 home for all firearms and ammunition, as well as evidence indicating gang membership. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMesserschmidt included two affidavits in the warrant application. The first detailed his extensive law enforcement experience and his specialized training in gang-related crimes. The second, expressly incorporated into the search warrant, described the incident and explained why Messerschmidt believed there was probable cause for the search. It also requested that the warrant be endorsed for night service because of Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s gang ties. Before submitting the application to a magistrate for approval, Messerschmidt had it reviewed by his supervisor, Sergeant Robert Lawrence, as well as a police lieutenant and a deputy district attorney. Messerschmidt then submitted the application to a magistrate, who issued the warrant. The ensuing search uncovered only Millender\xe2\x80\x99s shotgun, a California Social Services letter addressed to Bowen, and a box of .45-caliber ammunition.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Millenders filed an action under \n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983 against petitioners Messerschmidt and Lawrence, alleging that the officers had subjected them to an unreasonable search in violation of the \n \nFourth Amendment. The District Court granted summary judgment to the Millenders, concluding that the firearm and gang-material aspects of the search warrant were overbroad and that the officers were not entitled to qualified immunity from damages. The Ninth Circuit, sitting en banc, affirmed the denial of qualified immunity. The court held that the warrant\xe2\x80\x99s authorization was unconstitutionally overbroad because the affidavits and warrant failed to establish probable cause that the broad categories of firearms, firearm-related material, and gang-related material were contraband or evidence of a crime, and that a reasonable officer would have been aware of the warrant\xe2\x80\x99s deficiency.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0The officers are entitled to qualified immunity. Pp. 8\xe2\x88\x9219.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0Qualified immunity \xe2\x80\x9cprotects government officials \xe2\x80\x98from liability for civil damages insofar as their conduct does not violate clearly established statutory or constitutional rights of which a reasonable person would have known.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Pearson v. Callahan, \n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 223\n. Where the alleged \n \nFourth Amendment violation involves a search or seizure pursuant to a warrant, the fact that a neutral magistrate has issued a warrant is the clearest indication that the officers acted in an objectively reasonable manner, or in \xe2\x80\x9cobjective good faith.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Leon, \n\n468 U.\xc2\xa0S. 897\n\xe2\x80\x93923. Nonetheless, that fact does not end the inquiry into objective reasonableness. The Court has recognized an exception allowing suit when \xe2\x80\x9cit is obvious that no reasonably competent officer would have concluded that a warrant should issue.\xe2\x80\x9d Malley v. Briggs, \n\n475 U.\xc2\xa0S. 335\n. The \xe2\x80\x9cshield of immunity\xe2\x80\x9d otherwise conferred by the warrant, id., at 345, will be lost, for example, where the warrant was \xe2\x80\x9cbased on an affidavit so lacking in indicia of probable cause as to render official belief in its existence entirely unreasonable.\xe2\x80\x9d Leon, 468 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 923. The threshold for establishing this exception is high. \xe2\x80\x9c[I]n the ordinary case, an officer cannot be expected to question the magistrate\xe2\x80\x99s probable-cause determination\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t is the magistrate\xe2\x80\x99s responsibility to determine whether the officer\xe2\x80\x99s allegations establish probable cause and, if so, to issue a warrant comporting in form with the requirements of the \n \nFourth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d Leon, supra, at 921. Pp. 8\xe2\x88\x9210.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0This case does not fall within that narrow exception. It would not be entirely unreasonable for an officer to believe that there was probable cause to search for all firearms and firearm-related materials. Under the circumstances set forth in the warrant, an officer could reasonably conclude that there was a \xe2\x80\x9cfair probability\xe2\x80\x9d that the sawed-off shotgun was not the only firearm Bowen owned, Illinois v. Gates, \n\n462 U.\xc2\xa0S. 213\n, and that Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s sawed-off shotgun was illegal. Cf. 26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7 5845(a), 5861(d). Given Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s possession of one illegal gun, his gang membership, willingness to use the gun to kill someone, and concern about the police, it would not be unreasonable for an officer to conclude that Bowen owned other illegal guns. An officer also could reasonably believe that seizure of firearms was necessary to prevent further assaults on Kelly. California law allows a magistrate to issue a search warrant for items \xe2\x80\x9cin the possession of any person with the intent to use them as a means of committing a public offense,\xe2\x80\x9d Cal. Penal Code Ann. \xc2\xa71524(a)(3), and the warrant application submitted by the officers specifically referenced this provision as a basis for the search. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9312.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0Regarding the warrant\xe2\x80\x99s authorization to search for gang-related materials, a reasonable officer could view Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s attack as motivated not by the souring of his romantic relationship with Kelly but by a desire to prevent her from disclosing details of his gang activity to the police. It would therefore not be unreasonable\xe2\x80\x94based on the facts set out in the affidavit\xe2\x80\x94for an officer to believe that evidence of Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s gang affiliation would prove helpful in prosecuting him for the attack on Kelly, in supporting additional, related charges against Bowen for the assault, or in impeaching Bowen or rebutting his defenses. Moreover, even if this were merely a domestic dispute, a reasonable officer could still conclude that gang paraphernalia found at the Millenders\xe2\x80\x99 residence could demonstrate Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s control over the premises or his connection to other evidence found there. Pp. 12\xe2\x88\x9216.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(d)\xc2\xa0The fact that the officers sought and obtained approval of the warrant application from a superior and a deputy district attorney before submitting it to the magistrate provides further support for the conclusion that an officer could reasonably have believed that the scope of the warrant was supported by probable cause. A contrary conclusion would mean not only that Messerschmidt and Lawrence were \xe2\x80\x9cplainly incompetent\xe2\x80\x9d in concluding that the warrant was supported by probable cause, Malley, supra, at 341, but that their supervisor, the deputy district attorney, and the magistrate were as well. Pp. 16\xe2\x88\x9218.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(e)\xc2\xa0In holding that the warrant in this case was so obviously defective that no reasonable officer could have believed it to be valid, the court below erred in relying on Groh v. Ramirez, \n\n540 U.\xc2\xa0S. 551\n. There, officers who carried out a warrant-approved search were not entitled to qualified immunity because the warrant failed to describe any of the items to be seized and \xe2\x80\x9ceven a cursory reading of the warrant\xe2\x80\x9d would have revealed this defect. Id., at 557. Here, in contrast, any arguable defect would have become apparent only upon a close parsing of the warrant application, and a comparison of the supporting affidavit to the terms of the warrant to determine whether the affidavit established probable cause to search for all the items listed in the warrant. Unlike in Groh, any error here would not be one that \xe2\x80\x9cjust a simple glance\xe2\x80\x9d would have revealed. Id. at 564. Pp. 18\xe2\x88\x9219.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t620 F.\xc2\xa03d 1016, reversed.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tChief Justice Roberts delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioner police officers conducted a search of respondents\xe2\x80\x99 home pursuant to a warrant issued by a neutral magistrate. The warrant authorized a search for all guns and gang-related material, in connection with the investigation of a known gang member for shooting at his ex-girlfriend with a pistol-gripped sawed-off shotgun, because she had \xe2\x80\x9ccall[ed] the cops\xe2\x80\x9d on him. App. 56. Respondents brought an action seeking to hold the officers personally liable under \n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983, alleging that the search violated their \n \nFourth Amendment rights because there was not sufficient probable cause to believe the items sought were evidence of a crime. In particular, respondents argued that there was no basis to search for all guns simply because the suspect owned and had used a sawed-off shotgun, and no reason to search for gang material because the shooting at the ex-girlfriend for \xe2\x80\x9ccall[ing] the cops\xe2\x80\x9d was solely a domestic dispute. The Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit held that the warrant was invalid, and that the officers were not entitled to immu- nity from personal liability because this invalidity was so obvious that any reasonable officer would have recognized it, despite the magistrate\xe2\x80\x99s approval. We disagree and reverse.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tShelly Kelly decided to break off her romantic relationship with Jerry Ray Bowen and move out of her apartment, to which Bowen had a key. Kelly feared an attack from Bowen, who had previously assaulted her and had been convicted of multiple violent felonies. She therefore asked officers from the Los Angeles County Sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s Department to accompany her while she gathered her things. Deputies from the Sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s Department came to assist Kelly but were called away to respond to an emergency before the move was complete.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs soon as the officers left, an enraged Bowen appeared at the bottom of the stairs to the apartment, yelling \xe2\x80\x9cI told you never to call the cops on me bitch!\xe2\x80\x9d App. 39, 56. Bowen then ran up the stairs to Kelly, grabbed her by her shirt, and tried to throw her over the railing of the second-story landing. When Kelly successfully resisted, Bowen bit her on the shoulder and attempted to drag her inside the apartment by her hair. Kelly again managed to escape Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s grasp, and ran to her car. By that time, Bowen had retrieved a black sawed-off shotgun with a pistol grip. He ran in front of Kelly\xe2\x80\x99s car, pointed the shotgun at her, and told Kelly that if she tried to leave he would kill her. Kelly leaned over, fully depressed the gas pedal, and sped away. Bowen fired at the car a total of five times, blowing out the car\xe2\x80\x99s left front tire in the process, but Kelly managed to escape.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tKelly quickly located police officers and reported the assault. She told the police what had happened\xe2\x80\x94that Bowen had attacked her after becoming \xe2\x80\x9cangry because she had called the Sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s Department\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94and she mentioned that Bowen was \xe2\x80\x9can active member of the \xe2\x80\x98Mona Park Crips,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d a local street gang. Id., at 39. Kelly also provided the officers with photographs of Bowen. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tDetective Curt Messerschmidt was assigned to investigate the incident. Messerschmidt met with Kelly to obtain details of the assault and information about Bowen. Kelly described the attack and informed Messerschmidt that she thought Bowen was staying at his foster mother\xe2\x80\x99s home at 2234 East 120th Street. Kelly also informed Messerschmidt of Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s previous assaults on her and of his gang ties. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMesserschmidt then conducted a background check on Bowen by consulting police records, California Department of Motor Vehicles records, and the \xe2\x80\x9ccal-gang\xe2\x80\x9d database. Based on this research, Messerschmidt confirmed Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s connection to the 2234 East 120th Street address. He also confirmed that Bowen was an \xe2\x80\x9cactive\xe2\x80\x9d member of the Mona Park Crips and a \xe2\x80\x9csecondary\xe2\x80\x9d member of the Dodge City Crips. Id., at 64. Finally, Messerschmidt learned that Bowen had been arrested and convicted for numerous violent and firearm-related offenses. Indeed, at the time of the investigation, Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9crapsheet\xe2\x80\x9d spanned over 17 printed pages, and indicated that he had been arrested at least 31 times. Nine of these arrests were for firearms offenses and six were for violent crimes, including three arrests for assault with a deadly weapon (firearm). Id., at 72\xe2\x80\x9381. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMesserschmidt prepared two warrants: one to authorize Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s arrest and one to authorize the search of 2234 East 120th Street. An attachment to the search warrant described the property that would be the object of the search:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cAll handguns, rifles, or shotguns of any caliber, or any firearms capable of firing ammunition, or firearms or devices modified or designed to allow it [sic] to fire ammunition. All caliber of ammunition, miscellaneous gun parts, gun cleaning kits, holsters which could hold or have held any caliber handgun being sought. Any receipts or paperwork, showing the purchase, ownership, or possession of the handguns being sought. Any firearm for which there is no proof of ownership. Any firearm capable of firing or chambered to fire any caliber ammunition.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cArticles of evidence showing street gang membership or affiliation with any Street Gang to include but not limited to any reference to \xe2\x80\x98Mona Park Crips\xe2\x80\x99, including writings or graffiti depicting gang membership, activity or identity. Articles of personal property tending to establish the identity of person [sic] in control of the premise or premises. Any photographs or photograph albums depicting persons, vehicles, weapons or locations, which may appear relevant to gang membership, or which may depict the item being sought and or believed to be evidence in the case being investigated on this warrant, or which may depict evidence of criminal activity. Additionally to include any gang indicia that would establish the persons being sought in this warrant, affiliation or membership with the \xe2\x80\x98Mona Park Crips\xe2\x80\x99 street gang.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 52.\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTwo affidavits accompanied Messerschmidt\xe2\x80\x99s warrant ap- plications. The first affidavit described Messerschmidt\xe2\x80\x99s extensive law enforcement experience, including that he had served as a peace officer for 14 years, that he was then assigned to a \xe2\x80\x9cspecialized unit\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cinvestigating gang related crimes and arresting gang members for various violations of the law,\xe2\x80\x9d that he had been involved in \xe2\x80\x9chundreds of gang related incidents, contacts, and or arrests\xe2\x80\x9d during his time on the force, and that he had \xe2\x80\x9creceived specialized training in the field of gang related crimes\xe2\x80\x9d and training in \xe2\x80\x9cgang related shootings.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 53\xe2\x80\x9354. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe second affidavit\xe2\x80\x94expressly incorporated into the search warrant\xe2\x80\x94explained why Messerschmidt believed there was sufficient probable cause to support the warrant. That affidavit described the facts of the incident involving Kelly and Bowen in great detail, including the weapon used in the assault. The affidavit recounted that Kelly had identified Bowen as the assailant and that she thought Bowen might be found at 2234 East 120th Street. It also reported that Messerschmidt had \xe2\x80\x9cconducted an extensive background search on the suspect by utilizing departmental records, state computer records, and other police agency records,\xe2\x80\x9d and that from that information he had concluded that Bowen resided at 2234 East 120th Street. Id., at 58. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe affidavit requested that the search warrant be endorsed for night service because \xe2\x80\x9cinformation provided by the victim and the cal-gang data base\xe2\x80\x9d indicated that Bowen had \xe2\x80\x9cgang ties to the Mona Park Crip gang\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9cnight service would provide an added element of safety to the community as well as for the deputy personnel serving the warrant.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 59. The affidavit concluded by noting that Messerschmidt \xe2\x80\x9cbelieve[d] that the items sought\xe2\x80\x9d would be in Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s possession and that \xe2\x80\x9crecovery of the weapon could be invaluable in the successful prosecution of the suspect involved in this case, and the curtailment of further crimes being committed.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMesserschmidt submitted the warrants to his super- visors\xe2\x80\x94Sergeant Lawrence and Lieutenant Ornales\xe2\x80\x94for review. Deputy District Attorney Janet Wilson also reviewed the materials and initialed the search warrant, indicating that she agreed with Messerschmidt\xe2\x80\x99s assessment of probable cause. Id., at 27, 47. Finally, Messerschmidt submitted the warrants to a magistrate. The magistrate approved the warrants and authorized night service.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe search warrant was served two days later by a team of officers that included Messerschmidt and Lawrence. Sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s deputies forced open the front door of 2234 East 120th Street and encountered Augusta Millender\xe2\x80\x94a woman in her seventies\xe2\x80\x94and Millender\xe2\x80\x99s daughter and grandson. As instructed by the police, the Millenders went outside while the residence was secured but remained in the living room while the search was conducted. Bowen was not found in the residence. The search did, however, result in the seizure of Augusta Millender\xe2\x80\x99s shotgun, a California Social Services letter addressed to Bowen, and a box of .45-caliber ammunition.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBowen was arrested two weeks later after Messerschmidt found him hiding under a bed in a motel room.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Millenders filed suit in Federal District Court against the County of Los Angeles, the sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s department, the sheriff, and a number of individual officers, including Messerschmidt and Lawrence. The complaint alleged, as relevant here, that the search warrant was invalid under the \n \nFourth Amendment. It sought damages from Messerschmidt and Lawrence, among others. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe parties filed cross motions for summary judgment on the validity of the search warrant. The District Court found the warrant defective in two respects. The District Court concluded that the warrant\xe2\x80\x99s authorization to search for firearms was unconstitutionally overbroad because the \xe2\x80\x9ccrime specified here was a physical assault with a very specific weapon\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94a black sawed-off shotgun with a pistol grip\xe2\x80\x94negating any need to \xe2\x80\x9csearch for all firearms.\xe2\x80\x9d Millender v. County of Los Angeles, Civ. No. 05\xe2\x80\x932298 (CD Cal., Mar. 15, 2007), App. to Pet. for Cert. 106, 157, 2007 WL 7589200, *21. The court also found the warrant overbroad with respect to the search for gang-related materials, because there \xe2\x80\x9cwas no evidence that the crime at issue was gang-related.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 157. As a result, the District Court granted summary judgment to the Millenders on their constitutional challenges to the firearm and gang material aspects of the search warrant. Id., at 160. The District Court also rejected the officers\xe2\x80\x99 claim that they were entitled to qualified immunity from damages. Id., at 171.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMesserschmidt and Lawrence appealed, and a divided panel of the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit reversed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s denial of qualified immunity. 564 F.\xc2\xa03d 1143 (2009). The court held that the officers were entitled to qualified immunity because \xe2\x80\x9cthey reasonably relied on the approval of the warrant by a deputy district attorney and a judge.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1145. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court of Appeals granted rehearing en banc and affirmed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s denial of qualified immunity. 620 F.\xc2\xa03d 1016 (CA9 2010). The en banc court concluded that the warrant\xe2\x80\x99s authorization was unconstitutionally overbroad because the affidavit and the warrant failed to \xe2\x80\x9cestablish[ ] probable cause that the broad categories of firearms, firearm-related material, and gang-related material described in the warrant were contraband or evidence of a crime.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1033. In the en banc court\xe2\x80\x99s view, \xe2\x80\x9cthe deputies had probable cause to search for a single, identified weapon . . . . They had no probable cause to search for the broad class of firearms and firearm-related materials described in the warrant.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1027. In addition, \xe2\x80\x9c[b]ecause the deputies failed to establish any link between gang-related materials and a crime, the warrant authorizing the search and seizure of all gang-related evidence [was] likewise invalid.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1031. Concluding that \xe2\x80\x9ca reasonable officer in the deputies\xe2\x80\x99 position would have been well aware of this deficiency,\xe2\x80\x9d the en banc court held that the officers were not entitled to qualified immunity. Id., at 1033\xe2\x80\x931035.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThere were two separate dissenting opinions. Judge Callahan determined that \xe2\x80\x9cthe officers had probable cause to search for and seize any firearms in the home in which Bowen, a gang member and felon, was thought to reside.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1036. She also concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthe officers reasonably relied on their superiors, the district attorney, and the magistrate to correct\xe2\x80\x9d any overbreadth in the warrant, and that the officers were entitled to qualified immunity because their actions were not objectively unreasonable. Id., at 1044, 1049. Judge Silverman also dissented, concluding that the \xe2\x80\x9cdeputies\xe2\x80\x99 belief in the validity of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the warrant was entirely reasonable\xe2\x80\x9d and that the \xe2\x80\x9crecord [wa]s totally devoid of any evidence that the deputies acted other than in good faith.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1050. Judge Tallman joined both dissents.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe granted certiorari. 564 U. S. ___ (2011).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Millenders allege that they were subjected to an unreasonable search in violation of the \n \nFourth Amendment because the warrant authorizing the search of their home was not supported by probable cause. They seek damages from Messerschmidt and Lawrence for their roles in obtaining and executing this warrant. The validity of the warrant is not before us. The question instead is whether Messerschmidt and Lawrence are entitled to im- munity from damages, even assuming that the warrant should not have been issued.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cThe doctrine of qualified immunity protects government officials \xe2\x80\x98from liability for civil damages insofar as their conduct does not violate clearly established statutory or constitutional rights of which a reasonable person would have known.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Pearson v. Callahan, \n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 223, \n231 (2009)\n (quoting Harlow v. Fitzgerald, \n\n457 U.\xc2\xa0S. 800, \n818 (1982)\n). Qualified immunity \xe2\x80\x9cgives government officials breathing room to make reasonable but mistaken judgments,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cprotects \xe2\x80\x98all but the plainly incompetent or those who knowingly violate the law.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ashcroft v. al-Kidd, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 12) (quoting Malley v. Briggs, \n\n475 U.\xc2\xa0S. 335, \n341 (1986)\n). \xe2\x80\x9c[W]hether an official protected by qualified immunity may be held personally liable for an allegedly unlawful official action generally turns on the \xe2\x80\x98objective legal reasonableness\xe2\x80\x99 of the action, assessed in light of the legal rules that were \xe2\x80\x98clearly established\xe2\x80\x99 at the time it was taken.\xe2\x80\x9d Anderson v. Creighton, \n\n483 U.\xc2\xa0S. 635, \n639 (1987)\n (citation omitted). \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhere the alleged \n \nFourth Amendment violation involves a search or seizure pursuant to a warrant, the fact that a neutral magistrate has issued a warrant is the clearest indication that the officers acted in an objectively reasonable manner or, as we have sometimes put it, in \xe2\x80\x9cobjective good faith.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Leon, \n\n468 U.\xc2\xa0S. 897\n\xe2\x80\x93923 (1984).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Nonetheless, under our precedents, the fact that a neutral magistrate has issued a warrant authorizing the allegedly unconstitutional search or seizure does not end the inquiry into objective reasonableness. Rather, we have recognized an exception allowing suit when \xe2\x80\x9cit is obvious that no reasonably competent officer would have concluded that a warrant should issue.\xe2\x80\x9d Malley, 475 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 341. The \xe2\x80\x9cshield of immunity\xe2\x80\x9d otherwise conferred by the warrant, id., at 345, will be lost, for example, where the warrant was \xe2\x80\x9cbased on an affidavit so lacking in indicia of probable cause as to render official belief in its existence entirely unreasonable.\xe2\x80\x9d Leon, 468 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 923 (internal quotation marks omitted).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur precedents make clear, however, that the threshold for establishing this exception is a high one, and it should be. As we explained in Leon, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n the ordinary case, an officer cannot be expected to question the magistrate\xe2\x80\x99s probable-cause determination\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t is the magistrate\xe2\x80\x99s responsibility to determine whether the officer\xe2\x80\x99s allegations establish probable cause and, if so, to issue a warrant comporting in form with the requirements of the \n \nFourth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 921; see also Malley, supra, at 346, n.\xc2\xa09 (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is a sound presumption that the magistrate is more qualified than the police officer to make a probable cause determination, and it goes without saying that where a magistrate acts mistakenly in issuing a warrant but within the range of professional competence of a magistrate, the officer who requested the warrant cannot be held liable\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks and citation omitted)). \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Millenders contend, and the Court of Appeals held, that their case falls into this narrow exception. According to the Millenders, the officers \xe2\x80\x9cfailed to provide any facts or circumstances from which a magistrate could properly conclude that there was probable cause to seize the broad classes of items being sought,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o reasonable officer would have presumed that such a warrant was valid.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 27. We disagree. \n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWith respect to the warrant\xe2\x80\x99s authorization to search for and seize all firearms, the Millenders argue that \xe2\x80\x9ca reasonably well-trained officer would have readily perceived that there was no probable cause to search the house for all firearms and firearm-related items.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 32. Noting that \xe2\x80\x9cthe affidavit indicated exactly what item was evidence of a crime\xe2\x80\x94the \xe2\x80\x98black sawed off shotgun with a pistol grip,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d they argue that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o facts established that Bowen possessed any other firearms, let alone that such firearms (if they existed) were \xe2\x80\x98contraband or evidence of a crime.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting App. 56).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEven if the scope of the warrant were overbroad in authorizing a search for all guns when there was information only about a specific one, that specific one was a sawed-off shotgun with a pistol grip, owned by a known gang member, who had just fired the weapon five times in public in an attempt to murder another person, on the asserted ground that she had \xe2\x80\x9ccall[ed] the cops\xe2\x80\x9d on him. Id., at 56. Under these circumstances\xe2\x80\x94set forth in the warrant\xe2\x80\x94it would not have been unreasonable for an officer to conclude that there was a \xe2\x80\x9cfair probability\xe2\x80\x9d that the sawed-off shotgun was not the only firearm Bowen owned. Illinois v. Gates, \n\n462 U.\xc2\xa0S. 213, \n238 (1983)\n. And it certainly would have been reasonable for an officer to assume that Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s sawed-off shotgun was illegal. Cf. 26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa75845(a), 5861(d). Evidence of one crime is not always evidence of several, but given Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s possession of one illegal gun, his gang membership, his willingness to use the gun to kill someone, and his concern about the police, a reasonable officer could conclude that there would be additional illegal guns among others that Bowen owned.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA reasonable officer also could believe that seizure of the firearms was necessary to prevent further assaults on Kelly. California law allows a magistrate to issue a search warrant for items \xe2\x80\x9cin the possession of any person with the intent to use them as a means of committing a public offense,\xe2\x80\x9d Cal. Penal Code Ann. \xc2\xa71524(a)(3) (West 2011), and the warrant application submitted by the officers specifically referenced this provision as a basis for the search. App. 48. Bowen had already attempted to murder Kelly once with a firearm, and had yelled \xe2\x80\x9cI\xe2\x80\x99ll kill you\xe2\x80\x9d as she tried to escape from him. Id., at 56\xe2\x80\x9357. A reasonable officer could conclude that Bowen would make another attempt on Kelly\xe2\x80\x99s life and that he possessed other firearms \xe2\x80\x9cwith the intent to use them\xe2\x80\x9d to that end. Cal. Penal Code Ann. \xc2\xa71524(a)(3).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tGiven the foregoing, it would not have been \xe2\x80\x9centirely unreasonable\xe2\x80\x9d for an officer to believe, in the particular circumstances of this case, that there was probable cause to search for all firearms and firearm-related materials. Leon, supra, at 923 (internal quotation marks omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWith respect to the warrant\xe2\x80\x99s authorization to search for evidence of gang membership, the Millenders contend that \xe2\x80\x9cno reasonable officer could have believed that the affidavit presented to the magistrate contained a sufficient basis to conclude that the gang paraphernalia sought was contraband or evidence of a crime.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 28. They argue that \xe2\x80\x9cthe magistrate [could not] have reasonably concluded, based on the affidavit, that Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s gang membership had anything to do with the crime under investigation\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he affidavit described a \xe2\x80\x98spousal assault\xe2\x80\x99 that ensued after Kelly decided to end her \xe2\x80\x98on going dating relationship\xe2\x80\x99 with Bowen\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c[n]othing in that description suggests that the crime was gang-related.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting App. 55).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis effort to characterize the case solely as a domes- tic dispute, however, is misleading. Cf. post, at 5 (Sotomayor, J., dissenting); post, at 2 (Kagan, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part). Messerschmidt began his affidavit in support of the warrant by explaining that he \xe2\x80\x9chas been investigating an assault with a deadly weapon incident\xe2\x80\x9d and elaborated that the crime was a \xe2\x80\x9cspousal assault and an assault with a deadly weapon.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 55 (emphasis added). The affidavit also stated that Bowen was \xe2\x80\x9ca known Mona Park Crip gang member\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cbased on information provided by the victim and the cal-gang database,\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t and that he had attempted to murder Kelly after becoming enraged that she had \xe2\x80\x9ccall[ed] the cops on [him].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 56, 58\xe2\x80\x9359. A reasonable officer could certainly view Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s attack as motivated not by the souring of his romantic relationship with Kelly but instead by a desire to prevent her from disclosing details of his gang activity to the police. She was, after all, no longer linked with him as a girlfriend; he had assaulted her in the past; and she had indeed called the cops on him. And, as the affidavit supporting the warrant made clear, Kelly had in fact given the police information about Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s gang ties. Id., at 59.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt would therefore not have been unreasonable\xe2\x80\x94based on the facts set out in the affidavit\xe2\x80\x94for an officer to believe that evidence regarding Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s gang affiliation would prove helpful in prosecuting him for the attack on Kelly. See Warden, Md. Penitentiary v. Hayden, \n\n387 U.\xc2\xa0S. 294, \n307 (1967)\n (holding that the \n \nFourth Amendment allows a search for evidence when there is \xe2\x80\x9cprobable cause . . . to believe that the evidence sought will aid in a particular apprehension or conviction\xe2\x80\x9d). Not only would such evidence help to establish motive, either apart from or in addition to any domestic dispute, it would also support the bringing of additional, related charges against Bowen for the assault. See, e.g., Cal. Penal Code Ann. \xc2\xa7136.1(b)(1) (West 1999) (It is a crime to \xe2\x80\x9cattempt[ ] to prevent or dissuade another person who has been the victim of a crime or who is witness to a crime from . . . [m]aking any report of that victimization to any . . . law enforcement officer\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn addition, a reasonable officer could believe that evidence demonstrating Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s membership in a gang might prove helpful in impeaching Bowen or rebutting various defenses he could raise at trial. For example, evidence that Bowen had ties to a gang that uses guns such as the one he used to assault Kelly would certainly be relevant to establish that he had familiarity with or access to this type of weapon. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMoreover, even if this were merely a domestic dispute, a reasonable officer could still conclude that gang paraphernalia found at the Millenders\xe2\x80\x99 residence would aid in the prosecution of Bowen by, for example, demonstrating Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s connection to other evidence found there. The warrant authorized a search for \xe2\x80\x9cany gang indicia that would establish the persons being sought in this warrant,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c[a]rticles of personal property tending to establish the identity of [the] person in control of the premise or premises.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 52. Before the District Court, the Millenders \xe2\x80\x9cacknowledge[d] that evidence of who controlled the premises would be relevant if incriminating evidence were found and it became necessary to tie that evidence to a person,\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and the District Court approved that aspect of the warrant on this basis. App. to Pet. for Cert. 158\xe2\x80\x93159 (internal quotation marks omitted). Given Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s known gang affiliation, a reasonable officer could conclude that gang paraphernalia found at the residence would be an effective means of demonstrating Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s control over the premises or his connection to evidence found there.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t7\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhatever the use to which evidence of Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s gang involvement might ultimately have been put, it would not have been \xe2\x80\x9centirely unreasonable\xe2\x80\x9d for an officer to believe that the facts set out in the affidavit established a fair probability that such evidence would aid the prosecution of Bowen for the criminal acts at issue. Leon, 468 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 923 (internal quotation marks omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhether any of these facts, standing alone or taken together, actually establish probable cause is a question we need not decide. Qualified immunity \xe2\x80\x9cgives government officials breathing room to make reasonable but mistaken judgments.\xe2\x80\x9d al-Kidd, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 12). The officers\xe2\x80\x99 judgment that the scope of the warrant was supported by probable cause may have been mistaken, but it was not \xe2\x80\x9cplainly incompetent.\xe2\x80\x9d Malley, 475 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 341.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOn top of all this, the fact that the officers sought and obtained approval of the warrant application from a superior and a deputy district attorney before submitting it to the magistrate provides further support for the conclusion that an officer could reasonably have believed that the scope of the warrant was supported by probable cause. Ibid. Before seeking to have the warrant issued by a magistrate, Messerschmidt conducted an extensive investigation into Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s background and the facts of the crime. Based on this investigation, Messerschmidt prepared a detailed warrant application that truthfully laid out the pertinent facts. The only facts omitted\xe2\x80\x94the offi- cers\xe2\x80\x99 knowledge of Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s arrest and conviction records, see supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x94would only have strengthened the warrant. Messerschmidt then submitted the warrant application for review by Lawrence, another superior officer, and a deputy district attorney, all of whom approved the application without any apparent misgivings. Only after this did Messerschmidt seek the approval of a neutral magistrate, who issued the requested warrant. The officers thus \xe2\x80\x9ctook every step that could reasonably be expected of them.\xe2\x80\x9d Massachusetts v. Sheppard, \n\n468 U.\xc2\xa0S. 981, \n989 (1984)\n. In light of the foregoing, it cannot be said that \xe2\x80\x9cno officer of reasonable competence would have requested the warrant.\xe2\x80\x9d Malley, 475 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 346, n.\xc2\xa09. Indeed, a contrary conclusion would mean not only that Messerschmidt and Lawrence were \xe2\x80\x9cplainly incompetent,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 341, but that their supervisor, the deputy district attorney, and the magistrate were as well. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court of Appeals, however, gave no weight to the fact that the warrant had been reviewed and approved by the officers\xe2\x80\x99 superiors, a deputy district attorney, and a neutral magistrate. Relying on Malley, the court held that the officers had an \xe2\x80\x9cindependent responsibility to ensure there [was] at least a colorable argument for probable cause.\xe2\x80\x9d 620 F. 3d, at 1034. It explained that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he deputies here had a responsibility to exercise their reasonable professional judgment,\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9cin circumstances such as these a neutral magistrate\xe2\x80\x99s approval (and, a\xc2\xa0fortiori, a non-neutral prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s) cannot absolve an officer of liability.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (citation omitted). \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe rejected in Malley the contention that an officer is automatically entitled to qualified immunity for seeking a warrant unsupported by probable cause, simply because a magistrate had approved the application. 475 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 345. And because the officers\xe2\x80\x99 superior and the deputy district attorney are part of the prosecution team, their review also cannot be regarded as dispositive. But by holding in Malley that a magistrate\xe2\x80\x99s approval does not automatically render an officer\xe2\x80\x99s conduct reasonable, we did not suggest that approval by a magistrate or review by others is irrelevant to the objective reasonableness of the officers\xe2\x80\x99 determination that the warrant was valid. Indeed, we expressly noted that we were not deciding \xe2\x80\x9cwhether [the officer\xe2\x80\x99s] conduct in [that] case was in fact objectively reasonable.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 345, n.\xc2\xa08. The fact that the officers secured these approvals is certainly pertinent in assessing whether they could have held a reasonable belief that the warrant was supported by probable cause.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tC\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn holding that the warrant in this case was so obviously defective that no reasonable officer could have believed it was valid, the court below relied heavily on our decision in Groh v. Ramirez, \n\n540 U.\xc2\xa0S. 551 (2004)\n, but that precedent is far afield. There, we held that officers who carried out a warrant-approved search were not entitled to qualified immunity because the warrant in question failed to describe the items to be seized at all. Id., at 557. We explained that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n the portion of the form that called for a description of the \xe2\x80\x98person or property\xe2\x80\x99 to be seized, [the applicant] typed a description of [the target\xe2\x80\x99s] two-story blue house rather than the alleged stockpile of firearms.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 554. Thus, the warrant stated nonsensically that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98there is now concealed [on the specified premises] a certain person or property, namely [a] single dwelling residence two story in height which is blue in color and has two additions attached to the east.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 554\xe2\x80\x93555, n.\xc2\xa02 (bracketed material in original). Because \xe2\x80\x9ceven a cursory reading of the warrant in [that] case\xe2\x80\x94perhaps just a simple glance\xe2\x80\x94would have revealed a glaring de-ficiency that any reasonable police officer would have known was constitutionally fatal,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 564, we held that the officer was not entitled to qualified immunity.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe instant case is not remotely similar. In contrast to Groh, any defect here would not have been obvious from the face of the warrant. Rather, any arguable defect would have become apparent only upon a close parsing of the warrant application, and a comparison of the affidavit to the terms of the warrant to determine whether the affidavit established probable cause to search for all the items listed in the warrant. This is not an error that \xe2\x80\x9cjust a simple glance\xe2\x80\x9d would have revealed. Ibid. Indeed, unlike in Groh, the officers here did not merely submit their application to a magistrate. They also presented it for review by a superior officer, and a deputy district attorney, before submitting it to the magistrate. The fact that none of the officials who reviewed the application expressed concern about its validity demonstrates that any error was not obvious. Groh plainly does not control the result here. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe question in this case is not whether the magistrate erred in believing there was sufficient probable cause to support the scope of the warrant he issued. It is instead whether the magistrate so obviously erred that any reasonable officer would have recognized the error. The occasions on which this standard will be met may be rare, but so too are the circumstances in which it will be appropriate to impose personal liability on a lay officer in the face of judicial approval of his actions. Even if the warrant in this case were invalid, it was not so obviously lacking in probable cause that the officers can be con- sidered \xe2\x80\x9cplainly incompetent\xe2\x80\x9d for concluding otherwise. Malley, supra, at 341. The judgment of the Court of Appeals denying the officers qualified immunity must therefore be reversed. \n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBoth the Court and the dissent view this case as an all-or-nothing affair: The Court awards immunity across the board to Messerschmidt and his colleagues, while the dissent would grant them none at all. I think the right answer lies in between, although the Court makes the more far-reaching error.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI agree with the Court that a reasonably competent police officer could have thought this warrant valid in authorizing a search for all firearms and related items. See ante, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312. The warrant application recounted that a known gang member had used a sawed-off shotgun\xe2\x80\x94an illegal weapon under California law, see Cal. Penal Code Ann. \xc2\xa733215 (West 2012 Cum. Supp.)\xe2\x80\x94to try to kill another person. See App. 56\xe2\x80\x9357, 59. Perhaps gang ties plus possession of an unlawful gun plus use of that gun to commit a violent assault do not add up to what was needed for this search: probable cause to believe that Bowen had additional illegal firearms (or legal firearms that he intended to use to violate the law) at the place he was staying. But because our and the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decisions leave that conclusion debatable, a reasonable police officer could have found the warrant adequately supported by \xe2\x80\x9cindicia of probable cause.\xe2\x80\x9d Malley v. Briggs, \n\n475 U.\xc2\xa0S. 335, \n345 (1986)\n. So Messerschmidt and his fellow officers should receive qualified immunity for their search for firearms.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court, however, goes astray when it holds that a reasonable officer could have thought the warrant valid in approving a search for evidence of \xe2\x80\x9cstreet gang membership,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 52. Membership in even the worst gang does not violate California law, so the officers could not search for gang paraphernalia just to establish Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s ties to the Crips. Instead, the police needed probable cause to believe that such items would provide evidence of an actual crime\xe2\x80\x94and as the Court acknowledges, see ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9314, the only crime mentioned in the warrant application was the assault on Kelly. The problem for the Court is that nothing in the application supports a link between Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s gang membership and that shooting. Contra the Court\xe2\x80\x99s elaborate theory-spinning, see ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9316, Messerschmidt\xe2\x80\x99s affidavit in fact characterized the violent assault only as a domestic dispute, not as a gang-related one, see App. 55 (describing the crime as a \xe2\x80\x9cspousal assault and an assault with a deadly weapon\xe2\x80\x9d). And that description is consistent with the most natural understanding of the events. The warrant application thus had a hole at its very center: It lacked any explanation of how gang items would (or even might) provide evidence of the domestic assault the police were investigating.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo fill this vacuum, the Court proposes an alternative, but similarly inadequate justification\xe2\x80\x94that gang paraphernalia could have demonstrated Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s connection to the Millender residence and to any evidence of the assault found there. The dissent rightly notes one difficulty with this argument: The discovery of gang items would not have established that Bowen was staying at the house, given that several other gang members regularly did so. See post, at 8\xe2\x80\x939 (opinion of Sotomayor, J.). And even setting that issue aside, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning proves far too much: It would sanction equally well a search for any of Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s possessions on the premises\xe2\x80\x94a result impos- sible to square with the \n \nFourth Amendment. See, e.g., Andresen v. Maryland, \n\n427 U.\xc2\xa0S. 463, \n480 (1976)\n (disapproving \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a general, exploratory rummaging in a person\xe2\x80\x99s belongings\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Coolidge v. New Hampshire, \n\n403 U.\xc2\xa0S. 443, \n467 (1971)\n)). In authorizing a search for all gang-related items, the warrant far outstripped the officers\xe2\x80\x99 probable cause. Because a reasonable officer would have recognized that defect, I would not award qualified immunity to Messerschmidt and his colleagues for this aspect of their search.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tStill more fundamentally, the Court errs in scolding the Court of Appeals for failing to give \xe2\x80\x9cweight to the fact that the warrant had been reviewed and approved by the officers\xe2\x80\x99 superiors, a deputy district attorney, and a neutral magistrate.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 17. As the dissent points out, see post, at 13\xe2\x80\x9315, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding in Malley is to the opposite effect: An officer is not \xe2\x80\x9centitled to rely on the judgment of a judicial officer in finding that probable cause exists and hence issuing the warrant.\xe2\x80\x9d 475 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 345. Malley made clear that qualified immunity turned on the officer\xe2\x80\x99s own \xe2\x80\x9cprofessional judgment,\xe2\x80\x9d considered separately from the mistake of the magistrate. Id., at 346; see ibid., n.\xc2\xa09 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe officer .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. cannot excuse his own default by pointing to the greater incompetence of the magistrate\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 350 (Powell, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part) (objecting to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to \xe2\x80\x9cgive little evidentiary weight to the finding of probable cause by a magistrate\xe2\x80\x9d). And what we said in Malley about a magistrate\xe2\x80\x99s authorization applies still more strongly to the approval of other police officers or state attorneys. All those individuals, as the Court puts it, are \xe2\x80\x9cpart of the prosecution team.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 18. To make their views relevant is to enable those teammates (whether acting in good or bad faith) to confer immunity on each other for unreasonable conduct\xe2\x80\x94like applying for a warrant without anything resembling probable cause.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe fundamental purpose of the \n \nFourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s warrant clause is \xe2\x80\x9cto protect against all general searches.\xe2\x80\x9d Go-Bart Importing Co. v. United States, \n\n282 U.\xc2\xa0S. 344, \n357 (1931)\n. The \n \nFourth Amendment was adopted specifically in response to the Crown\xe2\x80\x99s practice of using general warrants and writs of assistance to search \xe2\x80\x9csuspected places\xe2\x80\x9d for evidence of smuggling, libel, or other crimes. Boyd v. United States, \n\n116 U.\xc2\xa0S. 616\n\xe2\x80\x93626 (1886). Early patriots railed against these practices as \xe2\x80\x9cthe worst instrument of arbitrary power\xe2\x80\x9d and John Adams later claimed that \xe2\x80\x9cthe child Independence was born\xe2\x80\x9d from colonists\xe2\x80\x99 opposition to their use. Id., at 625 (internal quotation marks omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo prevent the issue of general warrants on \xe2\x80\x9cloose, vague or doubtful bases of fact,\xe2\x80\x9d Go-Bart Importing Co., 282 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 357, the Framers established the inviolable principle that should resolve this case: \xe2\x80\x9cno Warrants shall issue, but upon probable cause .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and particularly describing the .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. things to be seized.\xe2\x80\x9d U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Amdt. 4. That is, the police must articulate an adequate reason to search for specific items related to specific crimes.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn this case, police officers investigating a specific, non-gang-related assault committed with a specific firearm (a sawed-off shotgun) obtained a warrant to search for all evidence related to \xe2\x80\x9cany Street Gang,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny photographs .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. which may depict evidence of criminal activity,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cany firearms.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 52. They did so for the asserted reason that the search might lead to evidence related to other gang members and other criminal activity, and that other \xe2\x80\x9c[v]alid warrants commonly allow police to search for \xe2\x80\x98firearms and ammunition.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d See infra, at 8\xe2\x80\x939. That kind of general warrant is antithetical to the \n \nFourth Amendment.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court nonetheless concludes that the officers are entitled to qualified immunity because their conduct was \xe2\x80\x9cobjectively reasonable.\xe2\x80\x9d I could not disagree more. All 13 federal judges who previously considered this case had little difficulty concluding that the police officers\xe2\x80\x99 search for any gang-related material violated the \n \nFourth Amendment. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 28\xe2\x80\x9329, 45, n.\xc2\xa07, 73, 94, 157\xe2\x80\x93158. And a substantial majority agreed that the police\xe2\x80\x99s search for both gang-related material and all firearms not only violated the \n \nFourth Amendment, but was objectively unreasonable. Like them, I believe that any \xe2\x80\x9creasonably well-trained officer in petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s position would have known that his affidavit failed to establish probable cause.\xe2\x80\x9d Malley v. Briggs, \n\n475 U.\xc2\xa0S. 335, \n345 (1986)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court also hints that a police officer\xe2\x80\x99s otherwise unreasonable conduct may be excused by the approval of a magistrate, or more disturbingly, another police officer. Ante, at 16\xe2\x80\x9318. That is inconsistent with our focus on the objective reasonableness of an officer\xe2\x80\x99s decision to submit a warrant application to a magistrate, and we long ago rejected it. See Malley, 475 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 345\xe2\x80\x93346.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis bears little relationship to the record in this case, our precedents, or the purposes underlying qualified immunity analysis. For all these reasons, I respectfully dissent.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t The Court holds that a well-trained officer could have reasonably concluded that there was probable cause to search the Millenders\xe2\x80\x99 residence for any evidence of affiliation with \xe2\x80\x9cany Street Gang,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9call handguns, rifles, or shotguns of any caliber, or any firearms capable of firing ammunition.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 52.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t I cannot agree.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMost troubling is the Court\xe2\x80\x99s determination that petitioners reasonably could have concluded that they had probable cause to search for all evidence of any gang affiliation in the Millenders\xe2\x80\x99 home. The Court reaches this result only by way of an unprecedented, post hoc reconstruction of the crime that wholly ignores the police\xe2\x80\x99s own conclusions, as well as the undisputed facts presented to the District Court.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court primarily theorizes that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] reasonable of- ficer could certainly view Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s attack as motivated not by the souring of his romantic relationship with Kelly but instead by a desire to prevent her from disclosing details of his gang activity to the police.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe police\xe2\x80\x99s conclusions matched the victim\xe2\x80\x99s own account of the attack. Kelly asked police officers to help her move out because Bowen \xe2\x80\x9cha[d] a domestic violence on his record,\xe2\x80\x9d had \xe2\x80\x9chit [her] once or twice\xe2\x80\x9d already, had repeatedly threatened her \xe2\x80\x9cYou\xe2\x80\x99ll never leave me. I\xe2\x80\x99ll kill you if you leave me,\xe2\x80\x9d and she was \xe2\x80\x9cplanning on breaking up\xe2\x80\x9d with him. Record, Doc. 51 (Exh. C), pp. 5\xe2\x80\x936 (hereinafter Kelly Interview). As Kelly described the confrontation, it was only after she fled to her car in order to leave that Bowen reemerged from their shared apartment with the shotgun and told her \xe2\x80\x9cI\xe2\x80\x99m gonna kill your ass right here if you take off,\xe2\x80\x9d consistent with his prior threats. Id., at 7\xe2\x80\x938. Every piece of information, therefore, accorded with Detective Messerschmidt\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion: The crime was domestic violence that was not gang related.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUnlike the Members of this Court, Detective Messerschmidt alone had 14 years of experience as a peace officer, \xe2\x80\x9chundreds of hours of instruction on the dynamics of gangs and gang trends,\xe2\x80\x9d received \xe2\x80\x9cspecialized training in the field of gang related crimes,\xe2\x80\x9d and had been \xe2\x80\x9cinvolved in hundreds of gang related incidents, contacts, and or arrests.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 53\xe2\x80\x9354. The Court provides no justification for sweeping aside the conclusions he reached on the basis of his far greater expertise, let alone the facts found by the District Court. We have repeatedly and recently warned appellate courts, \xe2\x80\x9cfar removed from the scene,\xe2\x80\x9d against second-guessing the judgments made by the police or reweighing the facts as they stood before the district court. Ryburn v. Huff, 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. \xe2\x80\x94, \xe2\x80\x94 (2012) (per\xc2\xa0curiam) (slip op., at 6\xe2\x80\x938). The majority\xe2\x80\x99s decision today is totally inconsistent with those principles.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tQualified immunity analysis does not direct courts to play the role of crime scene investigators, second-guessing police officers\xe2\x80\x99 determinations as to whether a crime was committed with a handgun or a shotgun, or whether violence was gang related or a domestic dispute. Indeed, we have warned courts against asking \xe2\x80\x9cwhether another reasonable, or more reasonable, interpretation of the events can be constructed five years after the fact.\xe2\x80\x9d Hunter v. Bryant, \n\n502 U.\xc2\xa0S. 224, \n228 (1991)\n (per\xc2\xa0curiam). The inquiry our precedents demand is not whether different conclusions might conceivably be drawn from the crime scene. Rather, it is whether \xe2\x80\x9ca reasonably well-trained officer in petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s position would have known that his affidavit failed to establish probable cause.\xe2\x80\x9d Malley, 475 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 345. The operative question in this case, therefore, is whether\xe2\x80\x94given that, as petitioners comprehended, the crime itself was not gang related\xe2\x80\x94a reasonable officer nonetheless could have believed he had probable cause to seek a warrant to search the suspect\xe2\x80\x99s residence for all evidence of affiliation not only with the suspect\xe2\x80\x99s street gang, but \xe2\x80\x9cany Street Gang.\xe2\x80\x9d He could not.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court offers two secondary explanations for why a search for gang-related items might have been justified, but they are equally unpersuasive. First, the majority suggests that such evidence hypothetically \xe2\x80\x9cmight prove helpful in impeaching Bowen or rebutting various de- fenses he could raise at trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 15. That is a non-starter. The \n \nFourth Amendment does not permit the police to search for evidence solely because it could be admissible for impeachment or rebuttal purposes. If it did, the police would be equally entitled to obtain warrants to rifle through the papers of anyone reasonably suspected of a crime for all evidence of his bad character, Fed. Rule Evid. 404(a)(2)(B)(i), or any evidence of any \xe2\x80\x9ccrime, wrong, or other act\xe2\x80\x9d that might prove the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cmotive, opportunity, intent, preparation, plan, knowledge, identity, absence of mistake, or lack of accident,\xe2\x80\x9d Fed. Rule Evid. 404(b)(2). Indeed, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s rationale presumably would authorize the police to search the residence of every member of Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s street gang for similar weapons\xe2\x80\x94which likewise \xe2\x80\x9cmight [have] prove[d] helpful in impeaching Bowen or rebutting various defenses he could raise at trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 15. It has long been the case, however, that such general searches, detached from probable cause, are impermissible. See, e.g., Go-Bart Importing Co., 282 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 357. By their own admission, however, the officers were not searching for gang-related indicia to bolster some hypothetical impeachment theory, but for other reasons: because \xe2\x80\x9cphotos sought re gang membership could be linked with other gang members, evidencing criminal activity as gang affiliation is an enhancement to criminal charges.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 181; see also id., at 145. That kind of fishing expedition for evidence of unidentified criminal activity committed by unspecified persons was the very evil the \n \nFourth Amendment was intended to prevent.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, the Court concludes that \xe2\x80\x9ceven if this were merely a domestic dispute, a reasonable officer could still conclude that gang paraphernalia found at the Millenders\xe2\x80\x99 residence would aid in the prosecution of Bowen by, for example, demonstrating Bowen\xe2\x80\x99s connection to other [unspecified] evidence found there.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 15. That is difficult to understand. The police were well aware before obtaining a warrant that \xe2\x80\x9cother persons associated with the home, the Millender family members, were active Mona Park Crip gang members.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 28. Simply finding gang-related paraphernalia, therefore, would have done little to establish probable cause that particular evidence found in the home was connected to Bowen, rather than any of the several other active gang members who resided full time at the Millender home.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Moreover, it would have done nothing to establish that Bowen had committed the non-gang-related crime specified in the warrant.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t7\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court also errs by concluding that petitioners could have reasonably concluded that they had probable cause to search for all firearms. Notably absent from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s discussion is any acknowledgment of the actual basis for petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 search. The police officers searched for all firearms not for the reasons hypothesized by the majority, but because they determined that \xe2\x80\x9c[v]alid warrants commonly allow police to search for \xe2\x80\x98firearms and ammunition,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9c[h]ere, any caliber of shotgun or receipts would show possession of and/or purchase of guns.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 144, 180\xe2\x80\x93181; see also Brief for Appellant in No. 07\xe2\x80\x9355518 (CA9), p. 41 (hereinafter CA9 Brief). It is small wonder that the District Court found these arguments \xe2\x80\x9cnonsensical and unpersuasive.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 157. It bears repeating that the Founders adopted the \n \nFourth Amendment to protect against searches for evidence of unspecified crimes. And merely possessing other firearms is not a crime at all. See generally District of Columbia v. Heller, \n\n554 U.\xc2\xa0S. 570 (2008)\n.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t8\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBy justifying the officers\xe2\x80\x99 actions on reasons of its own invention, the Court ignores the reasons the officers actually gave, as well as the facts upon which this case was decided below. The majority\xe2\x80\x99s analysis\xe2\x80\x94akin to a rational- basis test\xe2\x80\x94is thus far removed from what qualified immunity analysis demands. Even if the police had searched for the reasons the Court proposes, however, I still would find it inappropriate to afford them qualified immunity.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court correctly recognizes that to satisfy the \n \nFourth Amendment the police were required to demonstrate probable cause that (1) other firearms could be found at the Millenders\xe2\x80\x99 residence; and (2) such weapons were illegal or were \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98possess[ed] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. with the intent to use them as a means of committing a public offense.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12 (quoting Cal. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority has little difficulty concluding that because Bowen fired one firearm, it was reasonable for the police to conclude not only that Bowen must have possessed others, but that he must be storing these other weapons at his 73-year-old former foster mother\xe2\x80\x99s home.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t9\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Again, however, this is not what the police actually concluded, as Detective Messerschmidt\xe2\x80\x99s deposition makes clear.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUndaunted, the majority finds that a well-trained officer could have concluded on this information that he had probable cause to search for \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ll hand guns, . . . [a]ll caliber of ammunition, miscellaneous gun parts, gun cleaning kits, holsters which could hold or have held any caliber handgun being sought,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny receipts or paperwork, showing the purchase, ownership, or possession of the handguns being sought.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 52. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEven assuming that the police reasonably could have concluded that Bowen possessed other guns and was storing them at the Millenders\xe2\x80\x99 home, I cannot agree that the warrant provided probable cause to believe any weapon possessed in a home in which 10 persons regularly lived\xe2\x80\x94none of them the suspect in this case\xe2\x80\x94was either \xe2\x80\x9ccontraband or evidence of a crime.\xe2\x80\x9d Ornelas v. United States, \n\n517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 690, \n696 (1996)\n. The warrant set forth no specific facts or particularized explanation establishing probable cause to believe that other guns found in the home were connected to the crime specified in the warrant or were otherwise illegal.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t10\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t While the Court hypothesizes that the police could have searched for all firearms to uncover evidence of yet unnamed crimes, ante, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312, the warrant specified that the police were investigating one particular crime\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9can assault with a deadly weapon.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 55. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe police told the Ninth Circuit that they searched for all firearms not because, as the majority hypothesizes, \xe2\x80\x9cthere would be additional illegal guns among others that Bowen owned,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312, but on the dubious theory that \xe2\x80\x9cKelly could have been mistaken in her description of the gun.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 20\xe2\x80\x9321. \n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court also finds error in the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 failure to find \xe2\x80\x9cpertinent\xe2\x80\x9d the fact that the officer sought approval of his warrant from a magistrate.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t11\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Ante, at 18. Whether Detective Messerschmidt presented his warrant application to a magistrate surely would be \xe2\x80\x9cpertinent\xe2\x80\x9d to demonstrating his subjective good faith.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t12\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t But qualified immunity does not turn on whether an officer is motivated by good intentions or malice, but rather on the \xe2\x80\x9cobjective reasonableness of an official\xe2\x80\x99s conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d Harlow v. Fitzgerald, \n\n457 U.\xc2\xa0S. 800, \n818 (1982)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority asserts, without citation, that the magistrate\xe2\x80\x99s approval is relevant to objective reasonableness. That view, however, is expressly contradicted by our holding in Malley v. Briggs, \n\n475 U.\xc2\xa0S. 335\n. There, we found that a police officer is not \xe2\x80\x9centitled to rely on the judgment of a judicial officer in finding that probable cause exists and hence issuing the warrant,\xe2\x80\x9d and explained that \xe2\x80\x9c[that] view of objective reasonableness is at odds with our development of that concept in Harlow and [United States v. Leon, \n\n468 U.\xc2\xa0S. 897 (1984)\n].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 345. The appropriate qualified immunity analysis, we held, was not whether an officer reasonably relied on a magistrate\xe2\x80\x99s probable cause determination, but rather \xe2\x80\x9cwhether a reasonably well-trained officer in petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s position would have known that his affidavit failed to establish probable cause and that he should not have applied for the warrant.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t13\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t In such a case, \xe2\x80\x9cthe officer\xe2\x80\x99s application for a warrant [would] not [be] objectively reasonable, because it create[s] the unnecessary danger of an unlawful arrest.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. When \xe2\x80\x9cno officer of reasonable competence would have requested the warrant,\xe2\x80\x9d a \xe2\x80\x9cmagistrate [who] issues the warrant [makes] not just a reasonable mistake, but an unacceptable error indicating gross incompetence or neglect of duty.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 346, n.\xc2\xa09. In such cases, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he officer .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. cannot excuse his own default by pointing to the greater incompetence of the magistrate.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn cases in which it would be not only wrong but un- reasonable for any well-trained officer to seek a warrant, allowing a magistrate\xe2\x80\x99s approval to immunize the police officer\xe2\x80\x99s unreasonable action retrospectively makes little sense. By motivating an officer \xe2\x80\x9cto reflect, before submitting a request for a warrant, upon whether he has a reasonable basis for believing that his affidavit establishes probable cause,\xe2\x80\x9d we recognized that our qualified immu- nity precedents had the \xe2\x80\x9cdesirable\xe2\x80\x9d effect of \xe2\x80\x9creduc[ing] the likelihood that the officer\xe2\x80\x99s request for a warrant will be premature,\xe2\x80\x9d leading to \xe2\x80\x9ca waste of judicial resources\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cpremature arrests.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 343. To the extent it proposes to cut back upon Malley, the majority will promote the opposite result\xe2\x80\x94encouraging sloppy police work and ex-acerbating the risk that searches will not comport with the requirements of the \n \nFourth Amendment.\n\t\t\t
