GLOSSIP et\xc2\xa0al. v. GROSS et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the tenth circuit

\n

No. 14\xe2\x80\x937955.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 29, 2015\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 29, 2015

\n

Because capital punishment is constitutional, there must be a constitutional means of carrying it out. After Oklahoma adopted lethal injection as its method of execution, it settled on a three-drug protocol of (1)\xc2\xa0sodium thiopental (a barbiturate) to induce a state of unconsciousness, (2)\xc2\xa0a paralytic agent to inhibit all muscular-skeletal movements, and (3)\xc2\xa0potassium chloride to induce cardiac arrest. In Baze v. Rees,\n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 35\n, the Court held that this protocol does not violate the\n \nEighth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition against cruel and unusual punishments. Anti-death-penalty advocates then pressured pharmaceutical companies to prevent sodium thiopental (and, later, another barbiturate called pentobarbital) from being used in executions. Unable to obtain either sodium thiopental or pentobarbital, Oklahoma decided to use a 500-milligram dose of midazolam, a sedative, as the first drug in its three-drug protocol.

\n

Oklahoma death-row inmates filed a\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983 action claiming that the use of midazolam violates the\n \nEighth Amendment. Four of those inmates filed a motion for a preliminary injunction and argued that a 500-milligram dose of midazolam will not render them unable to feel pain associated with administration of the second and third drugs. After a three-day evidentiary hearing, the District Court denied the motion. It held that the prisoners failed to identify a known and available alternative method of execution that presented a substantially less severe risk of pain. It also held that the prisoners failed to establish a likelihood of showing that the use of midazolam created a demonstrated risk of severe pain. The Tenth Circuit affirmed.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Petitioners have failed to establish a likelihood of success on the merits of their claim that the use of midazolam violates the Eighth Amendment. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9329.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0To obtain a preliminary injunction, petitioners must establish, among other things, a likelihood of success on the merits of their claim. See Winter v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc.,\n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 7\n. To succeed on an\n \nEighth Amendment method-of-execution claim, a prisoner must establish that the method creates a demonstrated risk of severe pain and that the risk is substantial when compared to the known and available alternatives. Baze, supra, at 61 (plurality opinion). Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0Petitioners failed to establish that any risk of harm was substantial when compared to a known and available alternative method of execution. Petitioners have suggested that Oklahoma could execute them using sodium thiopental or pentobarbital, but the District Court did not commit a clear error when it found that those drugs are unavailable to the State. Petitioners argue that the\n \nEighth Amendment does not require them to identify such an alternative, but their argument is inconsistent with the controlling opinion in Baze, which imposed a requirement that the Court now follows. Petitioners also argue that the requirement to identify an alternative is inconsistent with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s pre-Baze decision in Hill v. McDonough,\n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 573\n, but they misread that decision. Hill concerned a question of civil procedure, not a substantive\n \nEighth Amendment question. That case held that \xc2\xa71983 alone does not require an inmate asserting a method-of-execution claim to plead an acceptable alternative. Baze, on the other hand, made clear that the\n \nEighth Amendment requires a prisoner to plead and prove a known and available alternative. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0The District Court did not commit clear error when it found that midazolam is likely to render a person unable to feel pain associated with administration of the paralytic agent and potassium chloride. Pp.\xc2\xa016\xe2\x80\x9329.

\n

(1)\xc2\xa0Several initial considerations bear emphasis. First, the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s factual findings are reviewed under the deferential \xe2\x80\x9cclear error\xe2\x80\x9d standard. Second, petitioners have the burden of persuasion on the question whether midazolam is effective. Third, the fact that numerous courts have concluded that midazolam is likely to render an inmate insensate to pain during execution heightens the deference owed to the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s findings. Finally, challenges to lethal injection protocols test the boundaries of the authority and competency of federal courts, which should not embroil themselves in ongoing scientific controversies beyond their expertise. Baze, supra, at 51. Pp.\xc2\xa016\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

(2)\xc2\xa0The State\xe2\x80\x99s expert presented persuasive testimony that a 500-milligram dose of midazolam would make it a virtual certainty that an inmate will not feel pain associated with the second and third drugs, and petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 experts acknowledged that they had no contrary scientific proof. Expert testimony presented by both sides lends support to the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion. Evidence suggested that a 500-milligram dose of midazolam will induce a coma, and even one of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 experts agreed that as the dose of midazolam increases, it is expected to produce a lack of response to pain. It is not dispositive that midazolam is not recommended or approved for use as the sole anesthetic during painful surgery. First, the 500-milligram dose at issue here is many times higher than a normal therapeutic dose. Second, the fact that a low dose of midazolam is not the best drug for maintaining unconsciousness says little about whether a 500-milligram dose is constitutionally adequate to conduct an execution. Finally, the District Court did not err in concluding that the safeguards adopted by Oklahoma to ensure proper administration of midazolam serve to minimize any risk that the drug will not operate as intended. Pp.\xc2\xa018\xe2\x80\x9322.

\n

(3)\xc2\xa0Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 speculative evidence regarding midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cceiling effect\xe2\x80\x9d does not establish that the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s findings were clearly erroneous. The mere fact that midazolam has a ceiling above which an increase in dosage produces no effect cannot be dispositive, and petitioners provided little probative evidence on the relevant question, i.e., whether midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s ceiling effect occurs below the level of a 500-milligram dose and at a point at which the drug does not have the effect of rendering a person insensate to pain caused by the second and third drugs. Petitioners attempt to deflect attention from their failure of proof on this point by criticizing the testimony of the State\xe2\x80\x99s expert. They emphasize an apparent conflict between the State\xe2\x80\x99s expert and their own expert regarding the biological process that produces midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s ceiling effect. But even if petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 expert is correct regarding that biological process, it is largely beside the point. What matters for present purposes is the dosage at which the ceiling effect kicks in, not the biological process that produces the effect. Pp. 22\xe2\x80\x9325.

\n

(4) Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 remaining arguments\xe2\x80\x94that an expert report presented in the District Court should have been rejected because it referenced unreliable sources and contained an alleged mathematical error, that only four States have used midazolam in an execution, and that difficulties during two recent executions suggest that midazolam is ineffective\xe2\x80\x94all lack merit. Pp. 26\xe2\x80\x9329.

\n

776 F.\xc2\xa03d 721, affirmed.

RICHARD E. GLOSSIP, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. KEVIN J. GROSS, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the tenth circuit

\n

[June 29, 2015]

\n

\n

Justice Alito delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

Prisoners sentenced to death in the State of Oklahoma filed an action in federal court under Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa71979,\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983, contending that the method of execution now used by the State violates the\n \nEighth Amendment because it creates an unacceptable risk of severe pain. They argue that midazolam, the first drug employed in the State\xe2\x80\x99s current three-drug protocol, fails to render a person insensate to pain. After holding an evidentiary hearing, the District Court denied four prisoners\xe2\x80\x99 application for a preliminary injunction, finding that they had failed to prove that midazolam is ineffective. The Court of Appeals for the Tenth Circuit affirmed and accepted the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s finding of fact regarding midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s efficacy.

\n

For two independent reasons, we also affirm. First, the prisoners failed to identify a known and available alternative method of execution that entails a lesser risk of pain, a requirement of all\n \nEighth Amendment method-of-execution claims. See Baze v. Rees,\n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 35,\n61 (2008)\n (plurality opinion). Second, the District Court did not commit clear error when it found that the prisoners failed to establish that Oklahoma\xe2\x80\x99s use of a massive dose of midazolam in its execution protocol entails a substantial risk of severe pain.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

The death penalty was an accepted punishment at the time of the adoption of the Constitution and the Bill of Rights. In that era, death sentences were usually carried out by hanging. The Death Penalty in America: Current Controversies 4 (H. Bedau ed. 1997). Hanging remained the standard method of execution through much of the 19th century, but that began to change in the century\xe2\x80\x99s later years. See Baze, supra, at 41\xe2\x80\x9342. In the 1880\xe2\x80\x99s, the Legislature of the State of New York appointed a commission to find \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the most humane and practical method known to modern science of carrying into effect the sentence of death in capital cases.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d In re Kemmler,\n\n136 U.\xc2\xa0S. 436,\n444 (1890)\n. The commission recommended electrocution, and in 1888, the Legislature enacted a law providing for this method of execution. Id., at 444\xe2\x80\x93445. In subsequent years, other States followed New York\xe2\x80\x99s lead in the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98belief that electrocution is less painful and more humane than hanging.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Baze, 553 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 42 (quoting Malloy v. South Carolina,\n\n237 U.\xc2\xa0S. 180,\n185 (1915)\n).

\n

In 1921, the Nevada Legislature adopted another new method of execution, lethal gas, after concluding that this was \xe2\x80\x9cthe most humane manner known to modern science.\xe2\x80\x9d State v. Jon, 46 Nev. 418, 437, 211 P. 676, 682 (1923). The Nevada Supreme Court rejected the argument that the use of lethal gas was unconstitutional, id., at 435\xe2\x80\x93437, 211 P., at 681\xe2\x80\x93682, and other States followed Nevada\xe2\x80\x99s lead, see, e.g., Ariz. Const., Art. XXII, \xc2\xa722 (1933); 1937 Cal. Stats. ch. 172, \xc2\xa71; 1933 Colo. Sess. Laws ch. 61, \xc2\xa71; 1955 Md. Laws ch. 625, \xc2\xa71, p. 1017; 1937 Mo. Laws p. 222, \xc2\xa71. Nevertheless, hanging and the firing squad were retained in some States, see, e.g., 1961 Del. Laws ch. 309, \xc2\xa72 (hanging); 1935 Kan. Sess. Laws ch. 155, \xc2\xa71 (hanging); Utah Code Crim. Proc. \xc2\xa7105\xe2\x80\x9337\xe2\x80\x9316 (1933) (hanging or firing squad), and electrocution remained the predominant method of execution until the 9-year hiatus in executions that ended with our judgment in Gregg v. Georgia,\n\n428 U.\xc2\xa0S. 153 (1976)\n. See Baze, supra, at 42.

\n

After Gregg reaffirmed that the death penalty does not violate the Constitution, some States once again sought a more humane way to carry out death sentences. They eventually adopted lethal injection, which today is \xe2\x80\x9cby far the most prevalent method of execution in the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Baze, supra, at 42. Oklahoma adopted lethal injection in 1977, see 1977 Okla. Sess. Laws p. 89, and it eventually settled on a protocol that called for the use of three drugs: (1)\xc2\xa0sodium thiopental, \xe2\x80\x9ca fast-acting barbiturate sedative that induces a deep, comalike unconsciousness when given in the amounts used for lethal injection,\xe2\x80\x9d (2)\xc2\xa0a paralytic agent, which \xe2\x80\x9cinhibits all muscular-skeletal movements and, by paralyzing the diaphragm, stops respiration,\xe2\x80\x9d and (3)\xc2\xa0potassium chloride, which \xe2\x80\x9cinterferes with the electrical signals that stimulate the contractions of the heart, inducing cardiac arrest.\xe2\x80\x9d Baze, supra, at 44; see also Brief for Respondents 9. By 2008, at least 30 of the 36 States that used lethal injection employed that particular three-drug protocol. 553 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 44.

\n

While methods of execution have changed over the years, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]his Court has never invalidated a State\xe2\x80\x99s chosen procedure for carrying out a sentence of death as the infliction of cruel and unusual punishment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 48. In Wilkerson v. Utah,\n\n99 U.\xc2\xa0S. 130\n\xe2\x80\x93135 (1879), the Court upheld a sentence of death by firing squad. In In re Kemmler, supra, at 447\xe2\x80\x93449, the Court rejected a challenge to the use of the electric chair. And the Court did not retreat from that holding even when presented with a case in which a State\xe2\x80\x99s initial attempt to execute a pris-oner by electrocution was unsuccessful. Louisiana ex rel. Francis v. Resweber,\n\n329 U.\xc2\xa0S. 459\n\xe2\x80\x93464 (1947) (plurality opinion). Most recently, in Baze, supra, seven Justices agreed that the three-drug protocol just discussed does not violate the\n \nEighth Amendment.

\n

Our decisions in this area have been animated in part by the recognition that because it is settled that capital punishment is constitutional, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t necessarily follows that there must be a [constitutional] means of carrying it out.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 47. And because some risk of pain is inherent in any method of execution, we have held that the Constitution does not require the avoidance of all risk of pain. Ibid. After all, while most humans wish to die a painless death, many do not have that good fortune. Holding that the\n \nEighth Amendment demands the elimination of essentially all risk of pain would effectively outlaw the death penalty altogether.

\n

B

\n

Baze cleared any legal obstacle to use of the most common three-drug protocol that had enabled States to carry out the death penalty in a quick and painless fashion. But a practical obstacle soon emerged, as anti-death-penalty advocates pressured pharmaceutical companies to refuse to supply the drugs used to carry out death sentences. The sole American manufacturer of sodium thiopental, the first drug used in the standard three-drug protocol, was persuaded to cease production of the drug. After suspending domestic production in 2009, the company planned to resume production in Italy. Koppel, Execution Drug Halt Raises Ire of Doctors, Wall Street Journal, Jan. 25, 2011, p. A6. Activists then pressured both the company and the Italian Government to stop the sale of sodium thiopental for use in lethal injections in this country. Bonner, Letter from Europe: Drug Company in Cross Hairs of Death Penalty Opponents, N.\xc2\xa0Y. Times, Mar. 30, 2011; Koppel, Drug Halt Hinders Executions in the U.\xc2\xa0S., Wall Street Journal, Jan. 22, 2011, p. A1. That effort proved successful, and in January 2011, the company announced that it would exit the sodium thiopental market entirely. See Hospira, Press Release, Hospira Statement Regarding PentothalTM (sodium thiopental) Market Exit (Jan. 21, 2011).

\n

After other efforts to procure sodium thiopental proved unsuccessful, States sought an alternative, and they eventually replaced sodium thiopental with pentobarbital, another barbiturate. In December 2010, Oklahoma became the first State to execute an inmate using pentobarbital. See Reuters, Chicago Tribune, New Drug Mix Used in Oklahoma Execution, Dec. 17 2010, p. 41. That execution occurred without incident, and States gradually shifted to pentobarbital as their supplies of sodium thiopentalran out. It is reported that pentobarbital was used in all of the 43 executions carried out in 2012. The Death Penalty Institute, Execution List 2012, online at www.deathpenaltyinfo.org/execution-list-2012 (all Internet materials as visited June 26, 2015, and available in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file). Petitioners concede that pentobarbital, like sodium thiopental, can \xe2\x80\x9creliably induce and maintain a comalike state that renders a person insensate to pain\xe2\x80\x9d caused by administration of the second and third drugs in the protocol. Brief for Petitioners 2. And courts across the country have held that the use of pentobarbital in executions does not violate the\n \nEighth Amendment. See, e.g., Jackson v. Danberg, 656 F.\xc2\xa03d 157 (CA3 2011); Beaty v. Brewer, 649 F.\xc2\xa03d 1071 (CA9 2011); DeYoung v. Owens, 646 F.\xc2\xa03d 1319 (CA11 2011); Pavatt v. Jones, 627 F.\xc2\xa03d 1336 (CA10 2010).

\n

Before long, however, pentobarbital also became unavailable. Anti-death-penalty advocates lobbied the Danish manufacturer of the drug to stop selling it for use in executions. See Bonner, supra. That manufacturer opposed the death penalty and took steps to block the shipment of pentobarbital for use in executions in the United States. Stein, New Obstacle to Death Penalty in U.\xc2\xa0S., Washington Post, July 3, 2011, p. A4. Oklahoma eventually became unable to acquire the drug through any means. The District Court below found that both sodium thiopental and pentobarbital are now unavailable to Oklahoma. App. 67\xe2\x80\x9368.

\n

C

\n

Unable to acquire either sodium thiopental or pentobarbital, some States have turned to midazolam, a sedative in the benzodiazepine family of drugs. In October 2013, Florida became the first State to substitute midazolam for pentobarbital as part of a three-drug lethal injection protocol. Fernandez, Executions Stall As States Seek Different Drugs, N.\xc2\xa0Y. Times, Nov. 9, 2013, p. A1. To date, Florida has conducted 11 executions using that protocol, which calls for midazolam followed by a paralytic agent and potassium chloride. See Brief for State of Florida as Amicus Curiae 2\xe2\x80\x933; Chavez v. Florida SP Warden, 742 F.\xc2\xa03d 1267, 1269 (CA11 2014). In 2014, Oklahoma also substituted midazolam for pentobarbital as part of its three-drug protocol. Oklahoma has already used this three-drug protocol twice: to execute Clayton Lockett in April 2014 and Charles Warner in January 2015. (Warner was one of the four inmates who moved for a preliminary injunction in this case.)

\n

The Lockett execution caused Oklahoma to implement new safety precautions as part of its lethal injection protocol. When Oklahoma executed Lockett, its protocol called for the administration of 100 milligrams of midazolam, as compared to the 500 milligrams that are currently required. On the morning of his execution, Lockett cut himself twice at \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the bend of the elbow.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d App. 50. That evening, the execution team spent nearly an hour making at least one dozen attempts to establish intravenous (IV) access to Lockett\xe2\x80\x99s cardiovascular system, including at his arms and elsewhere on his body. The team eventually believed that it had established intravenous access through Lockett\xe2\x80\x99s right femoral vein, and it covered the injection access point with a sheet, in part to preserve Lockett\xe2\x80\x99s dignity during the execution. After the team administered the midazolam and a physician determined that Lockett was unconscious, the team next administered the paralytic agent (vecuronium bromide) and most of the potassium chloride. Lockett began to move and speak, at which point the physician lifted the sheet and determined that the IV had \xe2\x80\x9cinfiltrated,\xe2\x80\x9d which means that \xe2\x80\x9cthe IV fluid, rather than entering Lockett\xe2\x80\x99s blood stream, had leaked into the tissue surrounding the IV access point.\xe2\x80\x9d Warner v. Gross, 776 F.\xc2\xa03d 721, 725 (CA10 2015) (case below). The execution team stopped administering the remaining potassium chloride and terminated the execution about 33 minutes after the midazolam was first injected. About 10 minutes later, Lockett was pronounced dead.

\n

An investigation into the Lockett execution concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthe viability of the IV access point was the single greatest factor that contributed to the difficulty in administering the execution drugs.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 398. The investigation, which took five months to complete, recommended several changes to Oklahoma\xe2\x80\x99s execution protocol, and Oklahoma adopted a new protocol with an effective date of September 30, 2014. That protocol allows the Oklahoma Department of Corrections to choose among four different drug combinations. The option that Oklahoma plans to use to execute petitioners calls for the administration of 500 milligrams of midazolam followed by a paralytic agent and potassium chloride.[1] The paralytic agent may be pancuronium bromide, vecuronium bromide, or rocuronium bromide, three drugs that, all agree, are functionally equivalent for purposes of this case. The protocol also includes procedural safeguards to help ensure that an inmate remains insensate to any pain caused by the administration of the paralytic agent and potassium chloride. Those safeguards include: (1)\xc2\xa0the insertion of both a primary and backup IV catheter, (2)\xc2\xa0procedures to confirm the viability of the IV site, (3)\xc2\xa0the option to postpone an execution if viable IV sites cannot be established within an hour, (4)\xc2\xa0a mandatory pause between administration of the first and second drugs, (5)\xc2\xa0numerous procedures for monitoring the offender\xe2\x80\x99s consciousness, including the use of an electrocardiograph and direct observation, and (6)\xc2\xa0detailed provisions with respect to the training and preparation of the execution team. In January of this year, Oklahoma executed Warner using these revised procedures and the combination of midazolam, a paralytic agent, and potassium chloride.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

In June 2014, after Oklahoma switched from pentobarbital to midazolam and executed Lockett, 21 Oklahoma death row inmates filed an action under\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983 challenging the State\xe2\x80\x99s new lethal injection protocol. The complaint alleged that Oklahoma\xe2\x80\x99s use of midazolam violates the\n \nEighth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition of cruel and unusual punishment.

\n

In November 2014, four of those plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x94Richard

\n

Glossip, Benjamin Cole, John Grant, and Warner\xe2\x80\x94filed a motion for a preliminary injunction. All four men had been convicted of murder and sentenced to death by Oklahoma juries. Glossip hired Justin Sneed to kill his employer, Barry Van Treese. Sneed entered a room where Van Treese was sleeping and beat him to death with a baseball bat. See Glossip v. State, 2007 OK CR 12, 157 P.\xc2\xa03d 143, 147\xe2\x80\x93149. Cole murdered his 9-month-old daughter after she would not stop crying. Cole bent her body backwards until he snapped her spine in half. After the child died, Cole played video games. See Cole v. State, 2007 OK CR 27, 164 P.\xc2\xa03d 1089, 1092\xe2\x80\x931093. Grant, while serving terms of imprisonment totaling 130 years, killed Gay Carter, a prison food service supervisor, by pulling her into a mop closet and stabbing her numerous times with a shank. See Grant v. State, 2002 OK CR 36, 58 P.\xc2\xa03d 783, 789. Warner anally raped and murdered an 11-month-old girl. The child\xe2\x80\x99s injuries included two skull fractures, internal brain injuries, two fractures to her jaw, a lacerated liver, and a bruised spleen and lungs. See Warner v. State, 2006 OK CR 40, 144 P.\xc2\xa03d 838, 856\xe2\x80\x93857.

\n

The Oklahoma Court of Criminal Appeals affirmed the murder conviction and death sentence of each offender. Each of the men then unsuccessfully sought both state postconviction and federal habeas corpus relief. Having exhausted the avenues for challenging their convictions and sentences, they moved for a preliminary injunction against Oklahoma\xe2\x80\x99s lethal injection protocol.

\n

B

\n

In December 2014, after discovery, the District Court held a 3-day evidentiary hearing on the preliminary injunction motion. The District Court heard testimony from 17 witnesses and reviewed numerous exhibits. Dr. David Lubarsky, an anesthesiologist, and Dr. Larry Sasich, a doctor of pharmacy, provided expert testimony about midazolam for petitioners, and Dr. Roswell Evans, adoctor of pharmacy, provided expert testimony forrespondents.

\n

After reviewing the evidence, the District Court issued an oral ruling denying the motion for a preliminary injunction. The District Court first rejected petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 challenge under Daubert v. Merrell Dow Pharmaceuticals, Inc.,\n\n509 U.\xc2\xa0S. 579 (1993)\n, to the testimony of Dr. Evans. It concluded that Dr. Evans, the Dean of Auburn University\xe2\x80\x99s School of Pharmacy, was well qualified to testify about midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s properties and that he offered reliable testimony. The District Court then held that petitioners failed to establish a likelihood of success on the merits of their claim that the use of midazolam violates the\n \nEighth Amendment. The court provided two independent reasons for this conclusion. First, the court held that petitioners failed to identify a known and available method of execution that presented a substantially less severe risk of pain than the method that the State proposed to use. Second, the court found that petitioners failed to prove that Oklahoma\xe2\x80\x99s protocol \xe2\x80\x9cpresents a risk that is \xe2\x80\x98sure or very likely to cause serious illness and needless suffering,\xe2\x80\x99 amounting to \xe2\x80\x98an objectively intolerable risk of harm.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d App. 96 (quoting Baze, 553 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 50). The court emphasized that the Oklahoma protocol featured numerous safeguards, including the establishment of two IV access sites, confirmation of the viability of those sites, and monitoring of the offender\xe2\x80\x99s level of consciousness throughout the procedure.

\n

The District Court supported its decision with findings of fact about midazolam. It found that a 500-milligram dose of midazolam \xe2\x80\x9cwould make it a virtual certainty that any individual will be at a sufficient level of unconsciousness to resist the noxious stimuli which could occur from the application of the second and third drugs.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 77. Indeed, it found that a 500-milligram dose alone would likely cause death by respiratory arrest within 30 minutes or an hour.

\n

The Court of Appeals for the Tenth Circuit affirmed. 776 F.\xc2\xa03d 721. The Court of Appeals explained that our decision in Baze requires a plaintiff challenging a lethal injection protocol to demonstrate that the risk of severe pain presented by an execution protocol is substantial \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98when compared to the known and available alternatives.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 732 (quoting Baze, supra, at 61). And it agreed with the District Court that petitioners had not identified any such alternative. The Court of Appeals added, however, that this holding was \xe2\x80\x9cnot outcome-determinative in this case\xe2\x80\x9d because petitioners additionally failed to establish that the use of midazolam creates a demonstrated risk of severe pain. 776 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 732. The Court of Appeals found that the District Court did not abuse its discretion by relying on Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 testimony, and it concluded that the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s factual findings about midazolam were not clearly erroneous. It also held that alleged errors in Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 testimony did not render his testimony unreliable or the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s findings clearly erroneous.

\n

Oklahoma executed Warner on January 15, 2015, but we subsequently voted to grant review and then stayed the executions of Glossip, Cole, and Grant pending the resolution of this case. 574 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015).

\n

III

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cA plaintiff seeking a preliminary injunction must establish that he is likely to succeed on the merits, that he is likely to suffer irreparable harm in the absence of preliminary relief, that the balance of equities tips in his favor, and that an injunction is in the public interest.\xe2\x80\x9d Winter v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc.,\n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 7,\n20 (2008)\n. The parties agree that this case turns on whether petitioners are able to establish a likelihood of success on the merits.

\n

The\n \nEighth Amendment, made applicable to the States through the\n \nFourteenth Amendment, prohibits the infliction of \xe2\x80\x9ccruel and unusual punishments.\xe2\x80\x9d The controlling opinion in Baze outlined what a prisoner must establish to succeed on an\n \nEighth Amendment method-of-execution claim. Baze involved a challenge by Kentucky death row inmates to that State\xe2\x80\x99s three-drug lethal injection protocol of sodium thiopental, pancuronium bromide, and potassium chloride. The inmates conceded that the protocol, if properly administered, would result in a humane and constitutional execution because sodium thiopental would render an inmate oblivious to any pain caused by the second and third drugs. 553 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 49. But they argued that there was an unacceptable risk that sodium thiopental would not be properly administered. Ibid. The inmates also maintained that a significant risk of harm could be eliminated if Kentucky adopted a one-drug protocol and additional monitoring by trained personnel. Id., at 51.

\n

The controlling opinion in Baze first concluded that prisoners cannot successfully challenge a method of execution unless they establish that the method presents a risk that is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98sure or very likely to cause serious illness and needless suffering,\xe2\x80\x99 and give rise to \xe2\x80\x98sufficiently imminent dangers.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 50 (quoting Helling v. McKinney,\n\n509 U.\xc2\xa0S. 25\n\xe2\x80\x9335 (1993)). To prevail on such a claim, \xe2\x80\x9cthere must be a \xe2\x80\x98substantial risk of serious harm,\xe2\x80\x99 an \xe2\x80\x98objectively intolerable risk of harm\xe2\x80\x99 that prevents prison officials from pleading that they were \xe2\x80\x98subjectively blameless for purposes of the\n \nEighth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 553 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 50 (quoting Farmer v. Brennan,\n\n511 U.\xc2\xa0S. 825\n, and n.\xc2\xa09 (1994)). The controlling opinion also stated that prisoners \xe2\x80\x9ccannot successfully challenge a State\xe2\x80\x99s method of execution merely by showing a slightly or marginally safer alternative.\xe2\x80\x9d 553 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 51. Instead, prisoners must identify an alternative that is \xe2\x80\x9cfeasible, readily implemented, and in fact significantly reduce[s] a substantial risk of severe pain.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 52.

\n

The controlling opinion summarized the requirements of an\n \nEighth Amendment method-of-execution claim as follows: \xe2\x80\x9cA stay of execution may not be granted on grounds such as those asserted here unless the condemned prisoner establishes that the State\xe2\x80\x99s lethal injection protocol creates a demonstrated risk of severe pain. [And] [h]e must show that the risk is substantial when compared to the known and available alternatives.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 61. The preliminary injunction posture of the present case thus requires petitioners to establish a likelihood that they can establish both that Oklahoma\xe2\x80\x99s lethal injection protocol creates a demonstrated risk of severe pain and that the risk is substantial when compared to the known and available alternatives.

\n

The challenge in Baze failed both because the Kentucky inmates did not show that the risks they identified were substantial and imminent, id., at 56, and because they did not establish the existence of a known and available alternative method of execution that would entail a significantly less severe risk, id., at 57\xe2\x80\x9360. Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 argumentshere fail for similar reasons. First, petitioners have not proved that any risk posed by midazolam is substantial when compared to known and available alternative methods of execution. Second, they have failed to establish that the District Court committed clear error when it found that the use of midazolam will not result in severe pain and suffering. We address each reason in turn.

\n

IV

\n

Our first ground for affirmance is based on petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 failure to satisfy their burden of establishing that any risk of harm was substantial when compared to a known and available alternative method of execution. In their amended complaint, petitioners proffered that the State could use sodium thiopental as part of a single-drug protocol. They have since suggested that it might also be constitutional for Oklahoma to use pentobarbital. But the District Court found that both sodium thiopental and pentobarbital are now unavailable to Oklahoma\xe2\x80\x99s Department of Corrections. The Court of Appeals affirmed that finding, and it is not clearly erroneous. On the contrary, the record shows that Oklahoma has been unable to procure those drugs despite a good-faith effort to do so.

\n

Petitioners do not seriously contest this factual finding, and they have not identified any available drug or drugs that could be used in place of those that Oklahoma is now unable to obtain. Nor have they shown a risk of pain so great that other acceptable, available methods must be used. Instead, they argue that they need not identify a known and available method of execution that presents less risk. But this argument is inconsistent with the controlling opinion in Baze, 553 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 61, which imposed a requirement that the Court now follows.[2]

\n

Petitioners contend that the requirement to identify an alternative method of execution contravenes our pre-Baze decision in Hill v. McDonough,\n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 573 (2006)\n, but they misread that decision. The portion of the opinion in Hill on which they rely concerned a question of civil procedure, not a substantive\n \nEighth Amendment question. In

\n

\n

Hill, the issue was whether a challenge to a method of execution must be brought by means of an application for a writ of habeas corpus or a civil action under \xc2\xa71983. Id., at 576. We held that a method-of-execution claim must be brought under \xc2\xa71983 because such a claim does not attack the validity of the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s conviction or death sentence. Id., at 579\xe2\x80\x93580. The United States as amicus curiae argued that we should adopt a special pleading requirement to stop inmates from using \xc2\xa71983 actions to attack, not just a particular means of execution, but the death penalty itself. To achieve this end, the United States proposed that an inmate asserting a method-of-execution claim should be required to plead an acceptable alternative method of execution. Id., at 582. We rejected that argument because \xe2\x80\x9c[s]pecific pleading requirements are mandated by the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, and not, as a general rule, through case-by-case determinations of the federal courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Hill thus held that \xc2\xa71983 alone does not impose a heightened pleading requirement. Baze, on the other hand, addressed the substantive elements of an\n \nEighth Amendment method-of-execution claim, and it made clear that the\n \nEighth Amendment requires a prisoner to plead and prove a known and available alternative. Because petitioners failed to do this, the District Court properly held that they did not establish a likelihood of success on their\n \nEighth Amendment claim.

\n

Readers can judge for themselves how much distance there is between the principal dissent\xe2\x80\x99s argument against requiring prisoners to identify an alternative and the view, now announced by Justices Breyer and Ginsburg, that the death penalty is categorically unconstitutional. Post, p. ___ (Breyer, J., dissenting). The principal dissent goes out of its way to suggest that a State would violate the\n \nEighth Amendment if it used one of the methods of execution employed before the advent of lethal injection. Post, at 30\xe2\x80\x9331. And the principal dissent makes this suggestion even though the Court held in Wilkerson that this method (the firing squad) is constitutional and even though, in the words of the principal dissent, \xe2\x80\x9cthere is some reason to think that it is relatively quick and painless.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 30. Tellingly silent about the methods of execution most commonly used before States switched to lethal injection (the electric chair and gas chamber), the principal dissent implies that it would be unconstitutional to use a method that \xe2\x80\x9ccould be seen as a devolution to a more primitive era.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. If States cannot return to any of the \xe2\x80\x9cmore primitive\xe2\x80\x9d methods used in the past and if no drug that meets with the principal dissent\xe2\x80\x99s approval is available for use in carrying out a death sentence, the logical conclusion is clear. But we have time and again reaffirmed that capital punishment is not per\xc2\xa0se unconstitutional. See, e.g., Baze, 553 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 47; id., at 87\xe2\x80\x9388 (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment); Gregg, 428 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 187 (joint opinion of Stewart, Powell, and Stevens, JJ.); id., at 226 (White, J., concurring in judgment); Resweber, 329 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 464; In re Kemmler, 136 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 447; Wilkerson, 99 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 134\xe2\x80\x93135. We decline to effectively overrule these decisions.

\n

V

\n

We also affirm for a second reason: The District Court did not commit clear error when it found that midazolam is highly likely to render a person unable to feel pain during an execution. We emphasize four points at the outset of our analysis.

\n

First, we review the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s factual findings under the deferential \xe2\x80\x9cclear error\xe2\x80\x9d standard. This standard does not entitle us to overturn a finding \xe2\x80\x9csimply because [we are] convinced that [we] would have decided the case differently.\xe2\x80\x9d Anderson v. Bessemer City,\n\n470 U.\xc2\xa0S. 564,\n573 (1985)\n.

\n

Second, petitioners bear the burden of persuasion on this issue. Baze, supra, at 41. Although petitionersexpend great effort attacking peripheral aspects of Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 testimony, they make little attempt to prove what is critical, i.e., that the evidence they presented to the District Court establishes that the use of midazolam is sure or very likely to result in needless suffering.

\n

Third, numerous courts have concluded that the use of midazolam as the first drug in a three-drug protocol is likely to render an inmate insensate to pain that might result from administration of the paralytic agent and potassium chloride. See, e.g., 776 F.\xc2\xa03d 721 (case below affirming the District Court); Chavez v. Florida SP Warden, 742 F.\xc2\xa03d 1267 (affirming the District Court); Banks v. State, 150 So. 3d 797 (Fla. 2014) (affirming the lower court); Howell v. State, 133 So. 3d 511 (Fla. 2014) (same); Muhammad v. State, 132 So. 3d 176 (Fla. 2013) (same). (It is noteworthy that one or both of the two key witnesses in this case\xe2\x80\x94Dr. Lubarsky for petitioners and Dr. Evans for respondents\xe2\x80\x94were witnesses in the Chavez, Howell, and Muhammad cases.) \xe2\x80\x9cWhere an intermediate court reviews, and affirms, a trial court\xe2\x80\x99s factual findings, this Court will not \xe2\x80\x98lightly overturn\xe2\x80\x99 the concurrent findings of the two lower courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Easley v. Cromartie,\n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 234,\n242 (2001)\n. Our review is even more deferential where, as here, multiple trial courts have reached the same finding, and multiple appellate courts have affirmed those findings. Cf. Exxon Co., U. S. A. v. Sofec, Inc.,\n\n517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 830,\n841 (1996)\n (explaining that this Court \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98cannot undertake to review concurrent findings of fact by two courts below in the absence of a very obvious and exceptional showing of error\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Graver Tank & Mfg. Co. v. Linde Air Products Co.,\n\n336 U.\xc2\xa0S. 271,\n275 (1949)\n)).

\n

Fourth, challenges to lethal injection protocols test the boundaries of the authority and competency of federal courts. Although we must invalidate a lethal injection protocol if it violates the\n \nEighth Amendment, federal courts should not \xe2\x80\x9cembroil [themselves] in ongoing scientific controversies beyond their expertise.\xe2\x80\x9d Baze, supra, at 51. Accordingly, an inmate challenging a protocol bears the burden to show, based on evidence presented to the court, that there is a substantial risk of severe pain.

\n

A

\n

Petitioners attack the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s findings of fact on two main grounds.[3] First, they argue that even if midazolam is powerful enough to induce unconsciousness, it is too weak to maintain unconsciousness and insensitivity to pain once the second and third drugs are administered. Second, while conceding that the 500-milligram dose of midazolam is much higher than the normal therapeutic dose, they contend that this fact is irrelevant because midazolam has a \xe2\x80\x9cceiling effect\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94that is, at a certain point, an increase in the dose administered will not have any greater effect on the inmate. Neither argumentsucceeds.

\n

The District Court found that midazolam is capable of placing a person \xe2\x80\x9cat a sufficient level of unconsciousness to resist the noxious stimuli which could occur from the

\n

application of the second and third drugs.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 77. This conclusion was not clearly erroneous. Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 expert, Dr. Evans, testified that the proper administration of a 500-milligram dose of midazolam would make it \xe2\x80\x9ca virtual certainty\xe2\x80\x9d that any individual would be \xe2\x80\x9cat a sufficient level of unconsciousness to resist the noxious stimuli which could occur from application of the 2nd and 3rd drugs\xe2\x80\x9d used in the Oklahoma protocol. Id., at 302; see also id., at 322. And petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 experts acknowledged that they had no contrary scientific proof. See id., at 243\xe2\x80\x93244 (Dr. Sasich stating that the ability of midazolam to render a person insensate to the second and third drugs \xe2\x80\x9chas not been subjected to scientific testing\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 176 (Dr. Lubarksy stating that \xe2\x80\x9cthere is no scientific literature addressing the use of midazolam as a manner to administer lethal injections in humans\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

In an effort to explain this dearth of evidence, Dr. Sasich testified that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t\xe2\x80\x99s not my responsibility or the [Food and Drug Administration\xe2\x80\x99s] responsibility to prove that the drug doesn\xe2\x80\x99t work or is not safe.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Preliminary Injunction Hearing 357 (Tr.). Instead, he stated, \xe2\x80\x9cit\xe2\x80\x99s the responsibility of the proponent to show that the drug is safe and effective.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Dr. Sasich confused the standard imposed on a drug manufacturer seeking approval of a therapeutic drug with the standard that must be borne by a party challenging a State\xe2\x80\x99s lethal injection protocol. When a method of execution is authorized under state law, a party contending that this method violates the\n \nEighth Amendment bears the burden of showing that the method creates an unacceptable risk of pain. Here, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 own experts effectively conceded that they lacked evidence to prove their case beyond dispute.

\n

Petitioners attempt to avoid this deficiency by criticizing respondents\xe2\x80\x99 expert. They argue that the District Court should not have credited Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 testimony because he admitted that his findings were based on \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98extrapolat[ions]\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d from studies done about much lower therapeutic doses of midazolam. See Brief for Petitioners 34 (citing Tr. 667\xe2\x80\x93668; emphasis deleted). But because a 500-milligram dose is never administered for a therapeutic purpose, extrapolation was reasonable. And the conclusions of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 experts were also based on extrapolations and assumptions. For example, Dr. Lubarsky relied on \xe2\x80\x9cextrapolation of the ceiling effect data.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 177.

\n

Based on the evidence that the parties presented to the District Court, we must affirm. Testimony from both sides supports the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that midazolam can render a person insensate to pain. Dr. Evans testified that although midazolam is not an analgesic, it can nonetheless \xe2\x80\x9crender the person unconscious and \xe2\x80\x98insensate\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0during the remainder of the procedure.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 294. In his discussion about the ceiling effect, Dr. Sasich agreed that as the dose of midazolam increases, it is \xe2\x80\x9cexpected to produce sedation, amnesia, and finally lack of response to stimuli such as pain (unconsciousness).\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 243. Petitioners argue that midazolam is not powerful enough to keep a person insensate to pain after the administration of the second and third drugs, but Dr. Evans presented creditable testimony to the contrary. See, e.g., Tr. 661 (testifying that a 500-milligram dose of midazolam will induce a coma).[4] Indeed, low doses of midazolam are sufficient to induce unconsciousness and are even some-

\n

times used as the sole relevant drug in certain medical procedures. Dr. Sasich conceded, for example, that midazolam might be used for medical procedures like colonoscopies and gastroscopies. App. 267\xe2\x80\x93268; see also Brief for Respondents 6\xe2\x80\x938.[5]

\n

Petitioners emphasize that midazolam is not recommended or approved for use as the sole anesthetic during painful surgery, but there are two reasons why this is not dispositive. First, as the District Court found, the 500-milligram dose at issue here \xe2\x80\x9cis many times higher than a normal therapeutic dose of midazolam.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 76. The effect of a small dose of midazolam has minimal probative value about the effect of a 500-milligram dose. Second, the fact that a low dose of midazolam is not the best drug for maintaining unconsciousness during surgery says little about whether a 500-milligram dose of midazolam is constitutionally adequate for purposes of conducting an execution. We recognized this point in Baze, where we concluded that although the medical standard of care might require the use of a blood pressure cuff and an electrocardiogram during surgeries, this does not mean those procedures are required for an execution to pass\n \nEighth Amendment scrutiny. 553 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 60.

\n

Oklahoma has also adopted important safeguards to ensure that midazolam is properly administered. The

\n

District Court emphasized three requirements in particular: The execution team must secure both a primary and backup IV access site, it must confirm the viability of the IV sites, and it must continuously monitor the offender\xe2\x80\x99s level of consciousness. The District Court did not commit clear error in concluding that these safeguards help to minimize any risk that might occur in the event that midazolam does not operate as intended. Indeed, we concluded in Baze that many of the safeguards that Oklahoma employs\xe2\x80\x94including the establishment of a primary and backup IV and the presence of personnel to monitor an inmate\xe2\x80\x94help in significantly reducing the risk that an execution protocol will violate the\n \nEighth Amendment. Id., at 55\xe2\x80\x9356. And many other safeguards that Oklahoma has adopted mirror those that the dissent in Baze complained were absent from Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s protocol in that case. For example, the dissent argued that because a consciousness check before injection of the second drug \xe2\x80\x9ccan reduce a risk of dreadful pain,\xe2\x80\x9d Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s failure to include that step in its procedure was unconstitutional. Id., at 119 (opinion of Ginsburg, J.). The dissent also complained that Kentucky did not monitor the effectiveness of the first drug or pause between injection of the first and second drugs. Id., at 120\xe2\x80\x93121. Oklahoma has accommodated each of those concerns.

\n

B

\n

Petitioners assert that midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cceiling effect\xe2\x80\x9d undermines the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s finding about the effectiveness of the huge dose administered in the Oklahoma protocol. Petitioners argue that midazolam has a \xe2\x80\x9cceiling\xe2\x80\x9d above which any increase in dosage produces no effect. As a result, they maintain, it is wrong to assume that a 500-milligram dose has a much greater effect than a therapeutic dose of about 5 milligrams. But the mere fact that midazolam has such a ceiling cannot be dispositive. Dr. Sasich testified that \xe2\x80\x9call drugs essentially have a ceiling effect.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. 343. The relevant question here is whether midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s ceiling effect occurs below the level of a 500-milligram dose and at a point at which the drug does not have the effect of rendering a person insensate to pain caused by the second and third drugs.

\n

Petitioners provided little probative evidence on this point, and the speculative evidence that they did present to the District Court does not come close to establishing that its factual findings were clearly erroneous. Dr.\xc2\xa0Sasich stated in his expert report that the literature \xe2\x80\x9cindicates\xe2\x80\x9d that midazolam has a ceiling effect, but he conceded that he \xe2\x80\x9cwas unable to determine the midazolam dose for a ceiling effect on unconsciousness because there is no literature in which such testing has been done.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 243\xe2\x80\x93244. Dr. Lubarsky\xe2\x80\x99s report was similar, id., at 171\xe2\x80\x93172, and the testimony of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 experts at the hearing was no more compelling. Dr. Sasich frankly admitted that he did a \xe2\x80\x9csearch to try and determine at what dose of midazolam you would get a ceiling effect,\xe2\x80\x9d but concluded: \xe2\x80\x9cI could not find one.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. 344. The closest petitioners came was Dr. Lubarsky\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion that the ceiling effect occurs \xe2\x80\x9c[p]robably after about .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. 40 to 50 milligrams,\xe2\x80\x9d but he added that he had not actually done the relevant calculations, and he admitted: \xe2\x80\x9cI can\xe2\x80\x99t tell you right now\xe2\x80\x9d at what dose the ceiling effect occurs. App. 225. We cannot conclude that the District Court committed clear error in declining to find, based on such speculative evidence, that the ceiling effect negates midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s ability to render an inmate insensate to pain caused by the second and third drugs in the protocol.

\n

The principal dissent discusses the ceiling effect at length, but it studiously avoids suggesting that petitioners presented probative evidence about the dose at which the ceiling effect occurs or about whether the effect occurs before a person becomes insensate to pain. The principal dissent avoids these critical issues by suggesting that such evidence is \xe2\x80\x9cirrelevant if there is no dose at which the drug can .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. render a person \xe2\x80\x98insensate to pain.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 17. But the District Court heard evidence that the drug can render a person insensate to pain, and not just from Dr. Evans: Dr. Sasich (one of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 own experts) testified that higher doses of midazolam are \xe2\x80\x9cexpected to produce .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. lack of response to stimuli such as pain.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 243.[6]

\n

In their brief, petitioners attempt to deflect attention from their failure of proof regarding midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s ceiling effect by criticizing Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 testimony. But it was petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 burden to establish that midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s ceiling occurred at a dosage below the massive 500-milligram dose employed in the Oklahoma protocol and at a point at which the drug failed to render the recipient insensate to pain. They did not meet that burden, and their criticisms do not undermine Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 central point, which the District Court credited, that a properly administered 500-milligram dose of midazolam will render the recipient unable to feel pain.

\n

One of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 criticisms of Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 testimony is little more than a quibble about the wording chosen by Dr. Evans at one point in his oral testimony. Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 expert, Dr. Lubarsky, stated in his report that midazolam \xe2\x80\x9cincreases effective binding of [gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA)] to its receptor to induce unconsciousness.\xe2\x80\x9d[7] App. 172. Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 report provided a similar explanation of the way in which midazolam works, see id., at 293\xe2\x80\x93294, and Dr. Lubarsky did not dispute the accuracy of that explanation when he testified at the hearing. Petitioners contend, however, that Dr. Evans erred when he said at the hearing that \xe2\x80\x9c[m]idazolam attaches to GABA receptors, inhibiting GABA.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 312 (emphasis added). Petitioners contend that this statement was incorrect because \xe2\x80\x9cfar from inhibiting GABA, midazolam facilitates its binding to GABA receptors.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 38.

\n

In making this argument, petitioners are simply quarrelling with the words that Dr. Evans used during oral testimony in an effort to explain how midazolam works in terms understandable to a layman. Petitioners do not suggest that the discussion of midazolam in Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 expert report was inaccurate, and as for Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 passing use of the term \xe2\x80\x9cinhibiting,\xe2\x80\x9d Dr. Lubarksy\xe2\x80\x99s own expert report states that GABA\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cinhibition of brain activity is accentuated by midazolam.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 232 (emphasis added). Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 oral use of the word \xe2\x80\x9cinhibiting\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94particularly in light of his written testimony\xe2\x80\x94does not invalidate the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to rely on his testimony.

\n

Petitioners also point to an apparent conflict between Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 testimony and a declaration by Dr. Lubarsky (submitted after the District Court ruled) regarding the biological process that produces midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s ceiling effect. But even if Dr. Lubarsky\xe2\x80\x99s declaration is correct, it is largely beside the point. What matters for present purposes is the dosage at which the ceiling effect kicks in, not the biological process that produces the effect. And Dr. Lubarsky\xe2\x80\x99s declaration does not render the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s findings clearly erroneous with respect to that critical issue.

\n

C

\n

Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 remaining arguments about midazolam all lack merit. First, we are not persuaded by petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 argument that Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 testimony should have been rejected because of some of the sources listed in his report. Petitioners criticize two of the \xe2\x80\x9cselected references\xe2\x80\x9d that Dr. Evans cited in his expert report: the Web site drugs.com and a material safety data sheet (MSDS) about midazolam. Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 argument is more of a Daubert challenge to Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 testimony than an argument that the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s findings were clearly erroneous. The District Court concluded that Dr. Evans was \xe2\x80\x9cwell-qualified to give the expert testimony that he gave\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9chis testimony was the product of reliable principles and methods reliably applied to the facts of this case.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 75\xe2\x80\x9376. To the extent that the reliability of Dr.Evans\xe2\x80\x99 testimony is even before us, the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s con-clusion that his testimony was based on reliable sources is reviewed under the deferential \xe2\x80\x9cabuse-of-discretion\xe2\x80\x9d standard. General Elec. Co. v. Joiner,\n\n522 U.\xc2\xa0S. 136\n\xe2\x80\x93143 (1997). Dr. Evans relied on multiple sources and his own expertise, and his testimony may not be disqualified simply because one source (drugs.com) warns that it \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98is not intended for medical advice\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and another (the MSDS) states that its information is provided \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98without any warranty, express or implied, regarding its correctness.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 36. Medical journals that both parties rely upon typically contain similar disclaimers. See, e.g., Anesthesiology, Terms and Conditions of Use, online at http://anesthesiology.pubs.asahq.org/ss/terms.aspx (\xe2\x80\x9cNone of the information on this Site shall be used to diagnose or treat any health problem or disease\xe2\x80\x9d). Dr. Lubarsky\xe2\x80\x94petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 own expert\xe2\x80\x94relied on an MSDS to argue that midazolam has a ceiling effect. And petitioners do not identify any incorrect statements from drugs.com on which Dr. Evans relied. In fact, although Dr. Sasich submitted a declaration to the Court of Appeals criticizing Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 reference to drugs.com, that declaration does not identify a single fact from that site\xe2\x80\x99s discussion of midazolam that was materially inaccurate.

\n

Second, petitioners argue that Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 expert report contained a mathematical error, but we find this argument insignificant. Dr. Evans stated in his expert report that the lowest dose of midazolam resulting in human deaths, according to an MSDS, is 0.071 mg/kg delivered intravenously. App. 294. Dr. Lubarsky agreed with this statement. Specifically, he testified that fatalities have occurred in doses ranging from 0.04 to 0.07 mg/kg, and he stated that Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 testimony to that effect was \xe2\x80\x9ca true statement\xe2\x80\x9d (though he added those fatalities occurred among the elderly). Id., at 217. We do not understand petitioners to dispute the testimony of Dr. Evans and their own expert that 0.071 mg/kg is a potentially fatal dose of midazolam. Instead, they make much of the fact that the MSDS attached to Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 report apparently contained a typographical error and reported the lowest toxic dose as 71 mg/kg. That Dr. Evans did not repeat that incorrect figure but instead reported the correct dose supports rather than undermines his testimony. In any event, the alleged error in the MSDS is irrelevant because the District Court expressly stated that it did not rely on the figure in the MSDS. See id., at 75.

\n

Third, petitioners argue that there is no consensus among the States regarding midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s efficacy because only four States (Oklahoma, Arizona, Florida, and Ohio) have used midazolam as part of an execution. Petitioners rely on the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s statement in Baze that \xe2\x80\x9cit is difficult to regard a practice as \xe2\x80\x98objectively intolerable\xe2\x80\x99 when it is in fact widely tolerated,\xe2\x80\x9d and the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s emphasis on the fact that 36 States had adopted lethal injection and 30 States used the particular three-drug protocol at issue in that case. 553 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 53. But while the near-universal use of the particular protocol at issue in Baze supported our conclusion that this protocol did not violate the\n \nEighth Amendment, we did not say that the converse was true, i.e., that other protocols or methods of execution are of doubtful constitutionality. That argument, if accepted, would hamper the adoption of new and potentially more humane methods of execution and would prevent States from adapting to changes in the availability of suitable drugs.

\n

Fourth, petitioners argue that difficulties with Oklahoma\xe2\x80\x99s execution of Lockett and Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s July 2014 execution of Joseph Wood establish that midazolam is sure or very likely to cause serious pain. We are not persuaded. Aside from the Lockett execution, 12 other executions have been conducted using the three-drug protocol at issue here, and those appear to have been conducted without any significant problems. See Brief for Respondents 32; Brief for State of Florida as Amicus Curiae 1. Moreover, Lockett was administered only 100 milligrams of midazolam, and Oklahoma\xe2\x80\x99s investigation into that execution concluded that the difficulties were due primarily to the execution team\xe2\x80\x99s inability to obtain an IV access site. And the Wood execution did not involve the protocol at issue here. Wood did not receive a single dose of 500 milligrams of midazolam; instead, he received fifteen 50-milligram doses over the span of two hours.[8] Brief for Respondents 12, n.\xc2\xa09. And Arizona used a different two-drug protocol that paired midazolam with hydromorphone, a drug that is not at issue in this case. Ibid. When all of the circumstances are considered, the Lockett and Wood executions have little probative value for present purposes.

\n

Finally, we find it appropriate to respond to the principal dissent\xe2\x80\x99s groundless suggestion that our decision is tantamount to allowing prisoners to be \xe2\x80\x9cdrawn and quartered, slowly tortured to death, or actually burned at the stake.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 28. That is simply not true, and the principal dissent\xe2\x80\x99s resort to this outlandish rhetoric reveals the weakness of its legal arguments.

\n

VI

\n

For these reasons, the judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Tenth Circuit is affirmed.

I agree with the Court that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99\n \nEighth Amendment claim fails. That claim has no foundation in the\n \nEighth Amendment, which prohibits only those \xe2\x80\x9cmethod[s] of execution\xe2\x80\x9d that are \xe2\x80\x9cdeliberately designed to inflict pain.\xe2\x80\x9d Baze v. Rees,\n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 35,\n94 (2008)\n (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment). Because petitioners make no allegation that Oklahoma adopted its lethal injection protocol \xe2\x80\x9cto add elements of terror, pain, or disgrace to the death penalty,\xe2\x80\x9d they have no valid claim. Id., at 107. That should have been the end of this case, but our precedents have predictably transformed the federal courts \xe2\x80\x9cinto boards of inquiry charged with determining the \xe2\x80\x98best practices\xe2\x80\x99 for executions,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 101 (internal quotation marks omitted), necessitating the painstaking factual inquiry the Court undertakes today. Although I continue to believe that the broader interpretation of the\n \nEighth Amendment advanced in the plurality opinion in Baze is erroneous, I join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in full because it correctly explains why petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claim fails even under that controlling opinion.

\n

I write separately to respond to Justice Breyer\xe2\x80\x99s dissent questioning the constitutionality of the death penalty generally. No more need be said about the constitutional arguments on which Justice Breyer relies, as my colleagues and I have elsewhere refuted them.[1] But Justice Breyer\xe2\x80\x99s assertion, post, at 10, that the death penalty in this country has fallen short of the aspiration that capital punishment be reserved for the \xe2\x80\x9cworst of the worst\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x94a notion itself based on an implicit proportionality principle that has long been discredited, see Harmelin v. Michigan, \n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 957,\n966 (1991)\n (opinion of Scalia, J.)\xe2\x80\x94merits further comment. His conclusion is based on an analysis that itself provides a powerful case against enforcing an imaginary constitutional rule against \xe2\x80\x9carbitrariness.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

The thrust of Justice Breyer\xe2\x80\x99s argument is that empirical studies performed by death penalty abolitionists reveal that the assignment of death sentences does not necessarily correspond to the \xe2\x80\x9cegregiousness\xe2\x80\x9d of the crimes, but instead appears to be correlated to \xe2\x80\x9carbitrary\xe2\x80\x9d factors, such as the locality in which the crime was committed. Relying on these studies to determine the constitutionality of the death penalty fails to respect the values implicit in the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s allocation of decisionmaking in this context. The Donohue study, on which Justice Breyer relies most heavily, measured the \xe2\x80\x9cegregiousness\xe2\x80\x9d (or \xe2\x80\x9cdeathworthiness\xe2\x80\x9d) of murders by asking lawyers to identify the legal grounds for aggravation in each case, and by asking law students to evaluate written summaries of the murders and assign \xe2\x80\x9cegregiousness\xe2\x80\x9d scores based on a rubric designed to capture and standardize their moral judgments. Donohue, An Empirical Evaluation of the Connecticut Death Penalty System Since 1973, Are There Unlawful Racial, Gender, and Geographic Disparities? 11 J. of Empirical Legal Studies 637, 644\xe2\x80\x93645 (2014). This exercise in some ways approximates the function performed by jurors, but there is at least one critical difference: The law students make their moral judgments based on written summaries\xe2\x80\x94they do not sit through hours, days, or weeks of evidence detailing the crime; they do not have an opportunity to assess the credibility of witnesses, to see the remorse of the defendant, to feel the impact of the crime on the victim\xe2\x80\x99s family; they do not bear the burden of deciding the fate of another human being; and they are not drawn from the community whose sense of security and justice may have been torn asunder by an act of callous disregard for human life. They are like appellate judges and justices, reviewing only a paper record of each side\xe2\x80\x99s case for life or death.

\n

There is a reason the choice between life and death, within legal limits, is left to the jurors and judges who sit through the trial, and not to legal elites (or law students).[2] That reason is memorialized not once, but twice, in our Constitution: Article III guarantees that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Trial of all Crimes, except in cases of Impeachment, shall be by Jury\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9csuch Trial shall be held in the State where the said Crimes shall have been committed.\xe2\x80\x9d Art. III, \xc2\xa72, cl.\xc2\xa03. And the\n \nSixth Amendment promises that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n all criminal prosecutions, the accused shall enjoy the right to a .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. trial, by an impartial jury of the State and district wherein the crime shall have been committed.\xe2\x80\x9d Those provisions ensure that capital defendants are given the option to be sentenced by a jury of their peers who, collectively, are better situated to make the moral judgment between life and death than are the products of contemporary American law schools.

\n

It should come as no surprise, then, that the primary explanation a regression analysis revealed for the gap between the egregiousness scores and the actual sentences was not the race or sex of the offender or victim, but the locality in which the crime was committed. Donohue, supra, at 640; see also post, at 12 (Breyer, J., dissenting). What is more surprising is that Justice Breyer considers this factor to be evidence of arbitrariness. See ibid. The constitutional provisions just quoted, which place such decisions in the hands of jurors and trial courts located where \xe2\x80\x9cthe crime shall have been committed,\xe2\x80\x9d seem deliberately designed to introduce that factor.

\n

In any event, the results of these studies are inherently unreliable because they purport to control for egregiousness by quantifying moral depravity in a process that is itself arbitrary, not to mention dehumanizing. One such study\xe2\x80\x99s explanation of how the author assigned \xe2\x80\x9cdepravity points\xe2\x80\x9d to identify the \xe2\x80\x9cworst of the worst\xe2\x80\x9d murderers proves the point well. McCord, Lightning Still Strikes, 71 Brooklyn L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 797, 833\xe2\x80\x93834 (2005). Each aggravating factor received a point value based on the \xe2\x80\x9cblameworth[iness]\xe2\x80\x9d of the action associated with it. Id., at 830. Killing a prison guard, for instance, earned a defendant three \xe2\x80\x9cdepravity points\xe2\x80\x9d because it improved the case for complete incapacitation, while killing a police officer merited only two, because, \xe2\x80\x9cconsidered dispassionately,\xe2\x80\x9d such acts do \xe2\x80\x9cnot seem be a sine qua non of the worst criminals.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 834\xe2\x80\x93836. (Do not worry, the author reassures us, \xe2\x80\x9cmany killers of police officers accrue depravity points in other ways that clearly put them among the worst criminals.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 836.) Killing a child under the age of 12 was worth two depravity points, because such an act \xe2\x80\x9cseems particularly heartless,\xe2\x80\x9d but killing someone over the age of 70 earned the murderer only one, for although \xe2\x80\x9celderly victims tug at our hearts,\xe2\x80\x9d they do so \xe2\x80\x9cless\xe2\x80\x9d than children \xe2\x80\x9cbecause the promise of a long life is less.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 836, 838. Killing to make a political statement was worth three depravity points; killing out of racial hatred, only two. Id., at 835, 837. It goes on, but this small sample of the moral judgments on which this study rested shows just how unsuitable this evidence is to serve as a basis for a judicial decision declaring unconstitutional a punishment duly enacted in more than 30 States, and by the Federal Government.

\n

We owe victims more than this sort of pseudoscientific assessment of their lives. It is bad enough to tell a mother that her child\xe2\x80\x99s murder is not \xe2\x80\x9cworthy\xe2\x80\x9d of society\xe2\x80\x99s ultimate expression of moral condemnation. But to do so based on cardboard stereotypes or cold mathematical calculations is beyond my comprehension. In my decades on the Court, I have not seen a capital crime that could not be considered sufficiently \xe2\x80\x9cblameworthy\xe2\x80\x9d to merit a death sentence (even when genuine constitutional errors justified a vacatur of that sentence).[3]

\n

A small sample of the applications for a stay of execution that have come before the Court this Term alone proves my point. Mark Christeson was due to be executed in October 2014 for his role in the murder of Susan Brouk and her young children, Adrian and Kyle. After raping Ms. Brouk at gunpoint, he and his accomplice drove the family to a remote pond, where Christeson cut Ms. Brouk\xe2\x80\x99s throat with a bone knife. State v. Christeson, 50 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d 251, 257\xe2\x80\x93258 (Mo. 2001). Although bleeding profusely, she stayed alive long enough to tell her children she loved them and to watch as Christeson murdered them\xe2\x80\x94her son, by cutting his throat twice and drowning him; her daughter, by pressing down on her throat until she suffocated. Ibid. Christeson and his accomplice then threw Ms. Brouk\xe2\x80\x94alive but barely breathing\xe2\x80\x94into the pond to drown on top of her dead children. Ibid. This Court granted him a stay of execution. Christeson v. Roper, 574 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014). Lisa Ann Coleman was not so lucky. She was executed on September 17, 2014, for murdering her girlfriend\xe2\x80\x99s son, 9-year-old Davontae Williams, by slowly starving him to death. Coleman v. State, 2009 WL 4696064, *1 (Tex. Crim. App., Dec. 9, 2009). When he died, Davontae had over 250 distinct injuries\xe2\x80\x94including cigarette burns and ligature marks\xe2\x80\x94on his 36-pound frame. Id., at *2. Infections from untreated wounds contributed to his other cause of death: pneumonia. Id., at *1\xe2\x80\x93*2. And Johnny Shane Kormondy, who met his end on January 15, 2015, did so after he and his two accomplices invaded the home of a married couple, took turns raping the wife and forcing her to perform oral sex at gunpoint\xe2\x80\x94at one point, doing both simultaneously\xe2\x80\x94and then put a bullet in her husband\xe2\x80\x99s head during the final rape. Kormondy v. Secretary, Fla. Dept. of Corrections, 688 F.\xc2\xa03d 1244, 1247\xe2\x80\x931248 (CA11 2012).

\n

Some of our most \xe2\x80\x9cegregious\xe2\x80\x9d cases have been those in which we have granted relief based on an unfounded\n \nEighth Amendment claim. For example, we have granted relief in a number of egregious cases based on this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Atkins v. Virginia,\n\n536 U.\xc2\xa0S. 304 (2002)\n, exempting certain \xe2\x80\x9cmentally retarded\xe2\x80\x9d offenders from the death penalty. Last Term, the Court granted relief to a man who kidnaped, beat, raped, and murdered a 21-year-old pregnant newlywed, Karol Hurst, also murdering her unborn child, and then, on the same day, murdered a sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s deputy acting in the line of duty. Hall v. Florida, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 1). And in Atkins itself, the Court granted relief to a man who carjacked Eric Michael Nesbitt, forced him to withdraw money from a bank, drove him to a secluded area, and then shot him multiple times before leaving him to bleed to death. Atkins v. Commonwealth, 257 Va. 160, 166\xe2\x80\x93167, 510 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 445, 449\xe2\x80\x93450 (1999).

\n

The Court has also misinterpreted the\n \nEighth Amendment to grant relief in egregious cases involving rape. In Kennedy v. Louisiana,\n\n554 U.\xc2\xa0S. 407 (2008)\n, the Court granted relief to a man who had been sentenced to death for raping his 8-year-old stepdaughter. The rape was so violent that it \xe2\x80\x9cseparated her cervix from the back of her vagina, causing her rectum to protrude into the vaginal structure,\xe2\x80\x9d and tore her \xe2\x80\x9centire perineum .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. from the posterior fourchette to the anus.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 414. The evidence indicated that the petitioner spent at least an hour and half attempting to destroy the evidence of his crime before seeking emergency assistance, even as his stepdaughter bled profusely from her injuries. Id., at 415. And in Coker v. Georgia,\n\n433 U.\xc2\xa0S. 584 (1977)\n (plurality opinion), the Court granted relief to a petitioner who had escaped from prison, broken into the home of a young married couple and their newborn, forced the wife to bind her husband, gagged her husband with her underwear, raped her (even after being told that she was recovering from a recent childbirth), and then kidnaped her after threatening her husband, Coker v. State, 234 Ga. 555, 556\xe2\x80\x93557, 216 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 782, 786\xe2\x80\x93787 (1975). In each case, the Court crafted an\n \nEighth Amendment right to be free from execution for the crime of rape\xe2\x80\x94whether it be of an adult, Coker, 433 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 592, or a child, Kennedy, supra, at 413.

\n

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s recent decision finding that the\n \nEighth Amendment prohibits the execution of those who committed their crimes as juveniles is no different. See Roper v. Simmons,\n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 551 (2005)\n. Although the Court had rejected the claim less than two decades earlier, Stanford v. His randomly chosen victim was Shirley Crook, whom he and his friends kidnaped in the middle of the night, bound with duct tape and electrical wire, and threw off a bridge to drown in the river below. Id., at 556\xe2\x80\x93557. The State of Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s brief in that case warned the Court that its decision would free from death row a number of killers who had been sentenced for crimes committed as juveniles. Brief for State of Alabama et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae in Roper v. Simmons, O. T. 2014, No. 03\xe2\x80\x93633. Mark Duke, for example, murdered his father for refusing to loan him a truck, and his father\xe2\x80\x99s girlfriend and her two young daughters because he wanted no witnesses to the crime. Id., at 4. He shot his father and his father\xe2\x80\x99s girlfriend pointblank in the face as they pleaded for their lives. Id., at 5\xe2\x80\x936. \n

Whatever one\xe2\x80\x99s views on the permissibility or wisdom of the death penalty, I doubt anyone would disagree that each of these crimes was egregious enough to merit the severest condemnation that society has to offer. The only constitutional problem with the fact that these criminals were spared that condemnation, while others were not, is that their amnesty came in the form of unfounded claims. Arbitrariness has nothing to do with it.[4] To the extent that we are ill at ease with these disparate outcomes, it seems to me that the best solution is for the Court to stop making up\n \nEighth Amendment claims in its ceaseless quest to end the death penalty through undemocratic means.

Justice Scalia, with whom Justice Thomas joins, concurring.

\n

I join the opinion of the Court, and write to respond to Justice Breyer\xe2\x80\x99s plea for judicial abolition of the death penalty.

\n

Welcome to Groundhog Day. The scene is familiar: Petitioners, sentenced to die for the crimes they committed (including, in the case of one petitioner since put to death, raping and murdering an 11\xe2\x80\x93month-old baby), come before this Court asking us to nullify their sentences as \xe2\x80\x9ccruel and unusual\xe2\x80\x9d under the\n \nEighth Amendment. They rely on this provision because it is the only provision they can rely on. They were charged by a sovereign State with murder. They were afforded counsel and tried before a jury of their peers\xe2\x80\x94tried twice, once to determine whether they were guilty and once to determine whether death was the appropriate sentence. They were duly convicted and sentenced. They were granted the right to appeal and to seek postconviction relief, first in state and then in federal court. And now, acknowledging that their convictions are unassailable, they ask us for clemency, as though clemency were ours to give.

\n

The response is also familiar: A vocal minority of the Court, waving over their heads a ream of the most recent abolitionist studies (a superabundant genre) as though they have discovered the lost folios of Shakespeare, insist that now, at long last, the death penalty must be abolished for good. Mind you, not once in the history of the American Republic has this Court ever suggested the death penalty is categorically impermissible. The reason is obvious: It is impossible to hold unconstitutional that which the Constitution explicitly contemplates. The\n \nFifth Amendment provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o person shall be held to answer for a capital .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. crime, unless on a presentment or indictment of a Grand Jury,\xe2\x80\x9d and that no person shall be \xe2\x80\x9cdeprived of life .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. without due process of law.\xe2\x80\x9d Nevertheless, today Justice Breyer takes on the role of the abolitionists in this long-running drama, arguing that the text of the Constitution and two centuries of history must yield to his \xe2\x80\x9c20 years of experience on this Court,\xe2\x80\x9d and inviting full briefing on the continued permissibility of capital punishment, post, at 2 (dissenting opinion).

\n

Historically, the\n \nEighth Amendment was understood to bar only those punishments that added \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98terror, pain, or disgrace\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to an otherwise permissible capital sentence. Baze v. Rees,\n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 35,\n96 (2008)\n (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment). Rather than bother with this troubling detail, Justice Breyer elects to contort the constitutional text. Redefining \xe2\x80\x9ccruel\xe2\x80\x9d to mean \xe2\x80\x9cunreliable,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9carbitrary,\xe2\x80\x9d or causing \xe2\x80\x9cexcessive delays,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cunusual\xe2\x80\x9d to include a \xe2\x80\x9cdecline in use,\xe2\x80\x9d he proceeds to offer up a white paper devoid of any meaningful legal argument.

\n

Even accepting Justice Breyer\xe2\x80\x99s rewriting of the\n \nEighth Amendment, his argument is full of internal contradictions and (it must be said) gobbledy-gook. He says that the death penalty is cruel because it is unreliable; but it is convictions, not punishments, that are unreliable. Moreover, the \xe2\x80\x9cpressure on police, prosecutors, and jurors to secure a conviction,\xe2\x80\x9d which he claims increases the risk of wrongful convictions in capital cases, flows from the nature of the crime, not the punishment that follows its commission. Post, at 6. Justice Breyer acknowledges as much: \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he crimes at issue in capital cases are typically horrendous murders, and thus accompanied by intense community pressure.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. That same pressure would exist, and the same risk of wrongful convictions, if horrendous death-penalty cases were converted into equally horrendous life-without-parole cases. The reality is that any innocent defendant is infinitely better off appealing a death sentence than a sentence of life imprisonment. (Which, again, Justice Breyer acknowledges: \xe2\x80\x9c[C]ourts (or State Governors) are 130 times more likely to exonerate a defendant where a death sentence is at issue,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 5.) The capital convict will obtain endless legal assistance from the abolition lobby (and legal favoritism from abolitionist judges), while the lifer languishes unnoticed behind bars.

\n

Justice Breyer next says that the death penalty is cruel because it is arbitrary. To prove this point, he points to a study of 205 cases that \xe2\x80\x9cmeasured the \xe2\x80\x98egregiousness\xe2\x80\x99 of the murderer\xe2\x80\x99s conduct\xe2\x80\x9d with \xe2\x80\x9ca system of metrics,\xe2\x80\x9d and then \xe2\x80\x9ccompared the egregiousness of the conduct of the 9 defendants sentenced to death with the egregiousness of the conduct of defendants in the remaining 196 cases [who were not sentenced to death],\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311. If only Aristotle, Aquinas, and Hume knew that moral philosophy could be so neatly distilled into a pocket-sized, vade mecum \xe2\x80\x9csystem of metrics.\xe2\x80\x9d Of course it cannot: Egregiousness is a moral judgment susceptible of few hard-and-fast rules. More importantly, egregiousness of the crime is only one of several factors that render a punishment condign\xe2\x80\x94culpability, rehabilitative potential, and the need for deterrence also are relevant. That is why this Court has required an individualized consideration of all mitigating circumstances, rather than formulaic application of some egregiousness test.

\n

It is because these questions are contextual and admit of no easy answers that we rely on juries to make judgments about the people and crimes before them. The fact that these judgments may vary across cases is an inevitable consequence of the jury trial, that cornerstone of Anglo-American judicial procedure. But when a punishment is authorized by law\xe2\x80\x94if you kill you are subject to death\xe2\x80\x94the fact that some defendants receive mercy from their jury no more renders the underlying punishment \xe2\x80\x9ccruel\xe2\x80\x9d than does the fact that some guilty individuals are never apprehended, are never tried, are acquitted, or arepardoned.

\n

Justice Breyer\xe2\x80\x99s third reason that the death penalty is cruel is that it entails delay, thereby (1) subjecting inmates to long periods on death row and (2) undermining the penological justifications of the death penalty. The first point is nonsense. Life without parole is an even lengthier period than the wait on death row; and if the objection is that death row is a more confining environment, the solution should be modifying the environment rather than abolishing the death penalty. As for the argument that delay undermines the penological rationales for the death penalty: In insisting that \xe2\x80\x9cthe major alternative to capital punishment\xe2\x80\x94namely, life in prison without possibility of parole\xe2\x80\x94also incapacitates,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 24, Justice Breyer apparently forgets that one of the plaintiffs in this very case was already in prison when he committed the murder that landed him on death row. Justice Breyer further asserts that \xe2\x80\x9cwhatever interest in retribution might be served by the death penalty as currently administered, that interest can be served almost as well by a sentence of life in prison without parole,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 27. My goodness. If he thinks the death penalty not much more harsh (and hence not much more retributive), why is he so keen to get rid of it? With all due respect, whether the death penalty and life imprisonment constitute more-or-less equivalent retribution is a question far above the judiciary\xe2\x80\x99s pay grade. Perhaps Justice Breyer is more forgiving\xe2\x80\x94or more enlightened\xe2\x80\x94than those who, like Kant, believe that death is the only just punishment for taking a life. I would not presume to tell parents whose life has been forever altered by the brutal murder of a child that life imprisonment is punishment enough.

\n

And finally, Justice Breyer speculates that it does not \xe2\x80\x9cseem likely\xe2\x80\x9d that the death penalty has a \xe2\x80\x9csignificant\xe2\x80\x9d deterrent effect. Post, at 25. It seems very likely to me, and there are statistical studies that say so. See, e.g., Zimmerman, State Executions, Deterrence, and the Incidence of Murder, 7 J. Applied Econ. 163, 166 (2004) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is estimated that each state execution deters approximately fourteen murders per year on average\xe2\x80\x9d); Dezhbakhsh, Rubin, & Shepherd, Does Capital Punishment Have a Deterrent Effect? New Evidence from Postmoratorium Panel Data, 5 Am. L. & Econ. Rev. 344 (2003) (\xe2\x80\x9c[E]ach execution results, on average, in eighteen fewer murders\xe2\x80\x9d per year); Sunstein & Vermeule, Is Capital Punishment Morally Required? Acts, Omissions, and Life-Life Tradeoffs, 58 Stan. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 703, 713 (2005) (\xe2\x80\x9cAll in all, the recent evidence of a deterrent effect from capital punishment seems impressive, especially in light of its \xe2\x80\x98apparent power and unanimity\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). But we federal judges live in a world apart from the vast majority of Americans. After work, we retire to homes in placid suburbia or to high-rise co-ops with guards at the door. We are not confronted with the threat of violence that is ever present in many Americans\xe2\x80\x99 everyday lives. The suggestion that the incremental deterrent effect of capital punishment does not seem \xe2\x80\x9csignificant\xe2\x80\x9d reflects, it seems to me, a let-them-eat-cake obliviousness to the needs of others. Let the People decide how much incremental deterrence is appropriate.

\n

Of course, this delay is a problem of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s own making. As Justice Breyer concedes, for more than 160 years, capital sentences were carried out in an average of two years or less. Post, at 18. But by 2014, he tells us, it took an average of 18 years to carry out a death sentence. Id., at 19. What happened in the intervening years? Nothing other than the proliferation of labyrinthine restrictions on capital punishment, promulgated by this Court under an interpretation of the\n \nEighth Amendment that empowered it to divine \xe2\x80\x9cthe evolving standards of decency that mark the progress of a maturing society,\xe2\x80\x9d Trop v. Dulles,\n\n356 U.\xc2\xa0S. 86,\n101 (1958)\n (plurality opinion)\xe2\x80\x94a task for which we are eminently ill suited. Indeed, for the past two decades, Justice Breyer has been the Drum Major in this parade. His invocation of the resultant delay as grounds for abolishing the death penalty calls to mind the man sentenced to death for killing his parents, who pleads for mercy on the ground that he is an orphan. Amplifying the surrealism of his argument, Justice Breyer uses the fact that many States have abandoned capital punishment\xe2\x80\x94have abandoned it precisely because of the costs those suspect decisions have imposed\xe2\x80\x94to conclude that it is now \xe2\x80\x9cunusual.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 33\xe2\x80\x9339. (A caution to the reader: Do not use the creative arithmetic that Justice Breyer employs in counting the number of States that use the death penalty when you prepare your next tax return; outside the world of our\n \nEighth Amendment abolitionist-inspired jurisprudence, it will be regarded as more misrepresentation than math.)

\n

If we were to travel down the path that Justice Breyer sets out for us and once again consider the constitutionality of the death penalty, I would ask that counsel also brief whether our cases that have abandoned the historical understanding of the\n \nEighth Amendment, beginning with Trop, should be overruled. That case has caused more mischief to our jurisprudence, to our federal system, and to our society than any other that comes to mind. Justice Breyer\xe2\x80\x99s dissent is the living refutation of Trop\xe2\x80\x99s assumption that this Court has the capacity to recognize \xe2\x80\x9cevolving standards of decency.\xe2\x80\x9d Time and again, the People have voted to exact the death penalty as punishment for the most serious of crimes. Time and again, this Court has upheld that decision. And time and again, a vocal minor-ity of this Court has insisted that things have \xe2\x80\x9cchanged radically,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 2, and has sought to replace the judgments of the People with their own standards of decency.

\n

Capital punishment presents moral questions that philosophers, theologians, and statesmen have grappled with for millennia. The Framers of our Constitution disagreed bitterly on the matter. For that reason, they handled it the same way they handled many other controversial issues: they left it to the People to decide. By arrogating to himself the power to overturn that decision, Justice Breyer does not just reject the death penalty, he rejects the Enlightenment.

Petitioners, three inmates on Oklahoma\xe2\x80\x99s death row, challenge the constitutionality of the State\xe2\x80\x99s lethal injection protocol. The State plans to execute petitioners using three drugs: midazolam, rocuronium bromide, and potassium chloride. The latter two drugs are intended to paralyze the inmate and stop his heart. But they do so in a torturous manner, causing burning, searing pain. It is thus critical that the first drug, midazolam, do what it is supposed to do, which is to render and keep the inmate unconscious. Petitioners claim that midazolam cannot be expected to perform that function, and they have presented ample evidence showing that the State\xe2\x80\x99s planned use of this drug poses substantial, constitutionally intolerable risks.

\n

Nevertheless, the Court today turns aside petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 plea that they at least be allowed a stay of execution while they seek to prove midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s inadequacy. The Court achieves this result in two ways: first, by deferring to the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to credit the scientifically unsupported and implausible testimony of a single expert witness; and second, by faulting petitioners for failing to satisfy the wholly novel requirement of proving the availability of an alternative means for their own executions. On both counts the Court errs. As a result, it leaves petitioners exposed to what may well be the chemical equivalent of being burned at the stake.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

The\n \nEighth Amendment succinctly prohibits the infliction of \xe2\x80\x9ccruel and unusual punishments.\xe2\x80\x9d Seven years ago, in Baze v. Rees,\n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 35 (2008)\n, the Court addressed the application of this mandate to Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s lethal injection protocol. At that time, Kentucky, like at least 29 of the 35 other States with the death penalty, utilized a series of three drugs to perform executions: (1) sodium thiopental, a \xe2\x80\x9cfast-acting barbiturate sedative that induces a deep, comalike unconsciousness when given in the amounts used for lethal injection\xe2\x80\x9d; (2) pancuronium bromide, \xe2\x80\x9ca paralytic agent that inhibits all muscular-skeletal movements and .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. stops respiration\xe2\x80\x9d; and (3) potassium chloride, which \xe2\x80\x9cinterferes with the electrical signals that stimulate the contractions of the heart, inducing cardiac arrest.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 44 (plurality opinion of Roberts, C.\xc2\xa0J.).

\n

In Baze, it was undisputed that absent a \xe2\x80\x9cproper dose of sodium thiopental,\xe2\x80\x9d there would be a \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial, constitutionally unacceptable risk of suffocation from the administration of pancuronium bromide and pain from the injection of potassium chloride.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 53. That is because, if given to a conscious inmate, pancuronium bromide would leave him or her asphyxiated and unable to demonstrate \xe2\x80\x9cany outward sign of distress,\xe2\x80\x9d while potassium chloride would cause \xe2\x80\x9cexcruciating pain.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 71 (Stevens, J., concurring in judgment). But the Baze petitioners conceded that if administered as intended, Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s method of execution would nevertheless \xe2\x80\x9cresult in a humane death,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 41 (plurality opinion), as the \xe2\x80\x9cproper administration\xe2\x80\x9d of sodium thiopental \xe2\x80\x9celiminates any meaningful risk that a prisoner would experience pain from the subsequent injections of pancuronium and potassium chloride,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 49. Based on that premise, the Court ultimately rejected the challenge to Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s protocol, with the plurality opinion concluding that the State\xe2\x80\x99s procedures for administering these three drugs ensured there was no \xe2\x80\x9cobjectively intolerable risk\xe2\x80\x9d of severe pain. Id., at 61\xe2\x80\x9362 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

B

\n

For many years, Oklahoma performed executions using the same three drugs at issue in Baze. After Baze was decided, however, the primary producer of sodium thiopental refused to continue permitting the drug to be used in executions. Ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x935. Like a number of other States, Oklahoma opted to substitute pentobarbital, another barbiturate, in its place. But in March 2014, shortly before two scheduled executions, Oklahoma found itself unable to secure this drug. App. 144.

\n

The State rescheduled the executions for the following month to give it time to locate an alternative anesthetic. In less than a week, a group of officials from the Okla-homa Department of Corrections and the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s office selected midazolam to serve as a replacement for pentobarbital. Id., at 145, 148\xe2\x80\x93149.

\n

Soon thereafter, Oklahoma used midazolam for the first time in its execution of Clayton Lockett. That execution did not go smoothly. Ten minutes after an intravenous (IV) line was set in Lockett\xe2\x80\x99s groin area and 100 milligrams of midazolam were administered, an attending physician declared Lockett unconscious. Id., at 392\xe2\x80\x93393. When the paralytic and potassium chloride were administered, however, Lockett awoke. Ibid. Various witnesses reported that Lockett began to writhe against his restraints, saying, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]his s*** is f***ing with my mind,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9csomething is wrong,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he drugs aren\xe2\x80\x99t working.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 53 (internal quotation marks omitted). State officials ordered the blinds lowered, then halted the execution. Id., at 393, 395. But 10 minutes later\xe2\x80\x94approximately 40 minutes after the execution began\xe2\x80\x94Lockett was pronounced dead. Id., at 395.

\n

The State stayed all future executions while it sought to determine what had gone wrong in Lockett\xe2\x80\x99s. Five months later, the State released an investigative report identifying a flaw in the IV line as the principal difficulty: The IV had failed to fully deliver the lethal drugs into Lockett\xe2\x80\x99s veins. Id., at 398. An autopsy determined, however, that the concentration of midazolam in Lockett\xe2\x80\x99s blood was more than sufficient to render an average person unconscious. Id., at 397, 405.

\n

In response to this report, the State modified its lethal injection protocol. The new protocol contains a number of procedures designed to guarantee that members of the execution team are able to insert the IV properly, and charges them with ensuring that the inmate is unconscious. Id., at 57\xe2\x80\x9366, 361\xe2\x80\x93369. But the protocol continues to authorize the use of the same three-drug formula used to kill Lockett\xe2\x80\x94though it does increase the intended dose of midazolam from 100 milligrams to 500 milligrams. Id., at 61. The State has indicated that it plans to use this drug combination in all upcoming executions, subject to only an immaterial substitution of paralytic agents. Ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

C

\n

In June 2014, inmates on Oklahoma\xe2\x80\x99s death row filed a\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983 suit against respondent prison officials challenging the constitutionality of Oklahoma\xe2\x80\x99s method of execution. After the State released its revised execution protocol, the four inmates whose executions were most imminent\xe2\x80\x94Charles Warner, along with petitioners Richard Glossip, John Grant, and Benjamin Cole\xe2\x80\x94moved for a preliminary injunction. They contended, among other things, that the State\xe2\x80\x99s intended use of midazolam would violate the\n \nEighth Amendment because, unlike sodium thiopental or pentobarbital, the drug \xe2\x80\x9cis incapable of producing a state of unawareness that will be reliably maintained after either of the other two pain-producing drugs .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is injected.\xe2\x80\x9d Amended Complaint \xc2\xb6101.

\n

The District Court held a 3-day evidentiary hearing, at which petitioners relied principally on the testimony of two experts: Dr. David Lubarsky, an anesthesiologist, and Dr. Larry Sasich, a doctor of pharmacy. The State, in turn, based its case on the testimony of Dr. Roswell Evans, also a doctor of pharmacy.

\n

To a great extent, the experts\xe2\x80\x99 testimony overlapped. All three experts agreed that midazolam is from a class of sedative drugs known as benzodiazepines (a class that includes Valium and Xanax), and that it has no analgesic\xe2\x80\x94or pain-relieving\xe2\x80\x94effects. App. 205 (Lubarsky), 260\xe2\x80\x93261 (Sasich), 311 (Evans). They further agreed that while midazolam can be used to render someone unconscious, it is not approved by the Federal Drug Administration (FDA) for use as, and is not in fact used as, a \xe2\x80\x9csole drug to produce and maintain anesthesia in surgical proceedings.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 307, 327 (Evans); see id., at 171 (Lubarsky); id., at 262 (Sasich). Finally, all three experts recognized that midazolam is subject to a ceiling effect, which means that there is a point at which increasing the dose of the drug does not result in any greater effect. Id., at 172 (Lubarsky), 243 (Sasich), 331 (Evans).

\n

The experts\xe2\x80\x99 opinions diverged, however, on the crucial questions of how this ceiling effect operates, and whether it will prevent midazolam from keeping a condemned inmate unconscious when the second and third lethal injection drugs are administered. Dr. Lubarsky testified that while benzodiazepines such as midazolam may, like barbiturate drugs such as sodium thiopental and pentobarbital, induce unconsciousness by inhibiting neuron function, they do so in a materially different way. Id., at 207. More specifically, Dr. Lubarsky explained that both barbiturates and benzodiazepines initially cause sedation by facilitating the binding of a naturally occurring chemical called gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA) with GABA receptors, which then impedes the flow of electrical impulses through the neurons in the central nervous system. Id., at 206. But at higher doses, barbiturates also act as a GABA substitute and mimic its neuron-suppressing effects. Ibid. By contrast, benzodiazepines lack this mimicking function, which means their effect is capped at a lower level of sedation. Ibid. Critically, according to Dr. Lubarsky, this ceiling on midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s sedative effect is reached before full anesthesia can be achieved. Ibid. Thus, in his view, while \xe2\x80\x9cmidazolam unconsciousness is .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. sufficient\xe2\x80\x9d for \xe2\x80\x9cminor procedure[s],\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Preliminary Injunction Hearing 132\xe2\x80\x93133 (Tr.), it is incapable of keeping someone \xe2\x80\x9cinsensate and immobile in the face of [more] noxious stimuli,\xe2\x80\x9d including the extreme pain and discomfort associated with administration of the second and third drugs in Oklahoma\xe2\x80\x99s lethal injection protocol, App. 218. Dr. Sasich endorsed Dr. Lubarsky\xe2\x80\x99s description of the ceiling effect, and offered similar reasons for reaching the same conclusion. See id., at 243, 248, 262.

\n

In support of these assertions, both experts cited a variety of evidence. Dr. Lubarsky emphasized, in particular, Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s 2014 execution of Joseph Wood, which had been conducted using midazolam and the drug hydromorphone rather than the three-drug cocktail Oklahoma intends to employ.[1] Id., at 176. Despite being administered 750 milligrams of midazolam, Wood had continued breathing and moving for nearly two hours\xe2\x80\x94which, according to Dr. Lubarsky, would not have occurred \xe2\x80\x9cduring extremely deep levels of anesthesia.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 177. Both experts also cited various scientific articles and textbooks to support their conclusions. For instance, Dr. Lubarsky relied on a study measuring the brain activity of rats that were administered midazolam, which showed that the drug\xe2\x80\x99s impact significantly tailed off at higher doses. See Hovinga et al., Pharmacokinetic-EEG Effect Relationship of Midazolam in Aging BN/BiRij Rats, 107 British J. Pharmacology 171, 173, Fig. 2 (1992). He also pointed to a pharmacology textbook that confirmed his description of how benzodiazepines and barbiturates produce their effects, see Stoelting & Hillier 127\xe2\x80\x93128, 140\xe2\x80\x93144, and a survey article concluding that \xe2\x80\x9c[m]idazolam cannot be used alone .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to maintain adequate anesthesia,\xe2\x80\x9d Reves, Fragen, Vinik, & Greenblatt, Midazolam: Pharmacology and Uses, 62 Anesthesiology 310, 318 (1985) (Reves). For his part, Dr. Sasich referred to a separate survey article, which similarly recognized and described the ceiling effect to which benzodiazepines are subject. See Saari, Uusi- Oukari, Ahonen, & Olkkola, Enhancement of GABAergic Activity: Neuropharmacological Effects of Benzodiazepines and Therapeutic Use in Anesthesiology, 63 Pharamacological Rev. 243, 244, 250 (2011) (Saari).

\n

By contrast, Dr. Evans, the State\xe2\x80\x99s expert, asserted that a 500-milligram dose of midazolam would \xe2\x80\x9crender the person unconscious and \xe2\x80\x98insensate\xe2\x80\x99 during the remainder of the [execution] procedure.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 294. He rested this conclusion on two interrelated propositions.

\n

First, observing that a therapeutic dose of midazolam to treat anxiety is less than 5 milligrams for a 70-kilogram adult, Dr. Evans emphasized that Oklahoma\xe2\x80\x99s planned administration of 500 milligrams of the drug was \xe2\x80\x9cat least 100 times the normal therapeutic dose.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. While he acknowledged that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here are no studies that have been done .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. administering that much .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. midazolam .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to anybody,\xe2\x80\x9d he noted that deaths had occurred in doses as low as 0.04 to 0.07 milligrams per kilogram (2.8 to 4.9 milligrams for a 70-kilogram adult), and contended that a 500-milligram dose would itself cause death within less than an hour\xe2\x80\x94a conclusion he characterized as \xe2\x80\x9cessentially an extrapolation from a toxic effect.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 327; see id.,at 308.

\n

Second, in explaining how he reconciled his opinion with the evidence of midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s ceiling effect, Dr. Evans testified that while \xe2\x80\x9cGABA receptors are found across the entire body,\xe2\x80\x9d midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s ceiling effect is limited to the \xe2\x80\x9cspinal cord\xe2\x80\x9d and there is \xe2\x80\x9cno ceiling effect\xe2\x80\x9d at the \xe2\x80\x9chigher level of [the] brain.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 311\xe2\x80\x93312. Consequently, in his view, \xe2\x80\x9cas you increase the dose of midazolam, it\xe2\x80\x99s a linear effect, so you\xe2\x80\x99re going to continue to get an impact from higher doses of the drug,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 332, until eventually \xe2\x80\x9cyou\xe2\x80\x99re paralyzing the brain,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 314. Dr. Evans also understood the chemical source of midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s ceiling effect somewhat differently from petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 experts. Although he agreed that midazolam produces its effect by \xe2\x80\x9cbinding to [GABA] receptors,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 293, he appeared to believe that midazolam produced sedation by \xe2\x80\x9cinhibiting GABA\xe2\x80\x9d from attaching to GABA receptors, not by promoting GABA\xe2\x80\x99s sedative effects, id., at 312. Thus, when asked about Dr. Lubarsky\xe2\x80\x99s description of the ceiling effect, Dr. Evans characterized the phenomenon as stemming from \xe2\x80\x9cthe competitive nature of substances trying to attach to GABA receptors.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 313.

\n

Dr. Evans cited no scholarly research in support of his opinions. Instead, he appeared to rely primarily on two sources: the Web site www.drugs.com, and a \xe2\x80\x9cMaterial Safety Data Sheet\xe2\x80\x9d produced by a midazolam manufacturer. See id., at 303. Both simply contained general information that covered the experts\xe2\x80\x99 areas of agreement.

\n

D

\n

The District Court denied petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 motion for a preliminary injunction. It began by making a series of factual findings regarding the characteristics of midazolam and its use in Oklahoma\xe2\x80\x99s execution protocol. Most relevant here, the District Court found that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he proper administration of 500 milligrams of midazolam .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. would make it a virtual certainty that an individual will be at a sufficient level of unconsciousness to resist the noxious stimuli which could occur from the application of the second and third drugs.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 77. Respecting petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 contention that there is a \xe2\x80\x9cceiling effect which prevents an increase in dosage from having a corresponding incremental effect on anesthetic depth,\xe2\x80\x9d the District Court concluded:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cDr. Evans testified persuasively .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that whatever the ceiling effect of midazolam may be with respect to anesthesia, which takes effect at the spinal cord level, there is no ceiling effect with respect to the ability of a 500 milligram dose of midazolam to effectively paralyze the brain, a phenomenon which is not anesthesia but does have the effect of shutting down respiration and eliminating the individual\xe2\x80\x99s awareness of pain.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 78.

\n

Having made these findings, the District Court held that petitioners had shown no likelihood of success on the merits of their\n \nEighth Amendment claim for two independent reasons. First, it determined that petitioners had \xe2\x80\x9cfailed to establish that proceeding with [their] execution[s] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. on the basis of the revised protocol presents .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. \xe2\x80\x98an objectively intolerable risk of harm.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 96. Second, the District Court held that petitioners were unlikely to prevail because they had not identified any \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98known and available alternative\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d means by which they could be executed\xe2\x80\x94a requirement it understood Baze to impose. Id., at 97. The District Court concluded that the State \xe2\x80\x9cha[d] affirmatively shown that sodium thiopental and pentobarbital, the only alternatives to which the [petitioners] have even alluded, are not available to the [State].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 98.

\n

The Court of Appeals for the Tenth Circuit affirmed. Warner v. Gross, 776 F.\xc2\xa03d 721 (2015). It, like the District Court, held that petitioners were unlikely to prevail on the merits because they had failed to prove the existence of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98known and available alternatives.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 732. \xe2\x80\x9cIn any event,\xe2\x80\x9d the court continued, it was unable to conclude that the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s factual findings had been clearly erroneous, and thus petitioners had also \xe2\x80\x9cfailed to establish that the use of midazolam in their executions .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. creates a demonstrated risk of severe pain.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

Petitioners and Charles Warner filed a petition for certiorari and an application to stay their executions. The Court denied the stay application, and Charles Warner was executed on January 15, 2015. See Warner v. Gross, 574 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015) (Sotomayor, J., dissenting from denial of certiorari). The Court subsequently granted certiorari and, at the request of the State, stayed petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 pending executions.

\n

II

\n

I begin with the second of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s two holdings: that the District Court properly found that petitioners did not demonstrate a likelihood of showing that Oklahoma\xe2\x80\x99s execution protocol poses an unconstitutional risk of pain. In reaching this conclusion, the Court sweeps aside substantial evidence showing that, while midazolam may be able to induce unconsciousness, it cannot be utilized to maintain unconsciousness in the face of agonizing stimuli. Instead, like the District Court, the Court finds comfort in Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 wholly unsupported claims that 500 milligrams of midazolam will \xe2\x80\x9cparalyz[e] the brain.\xe2\x80\x9d In so holding, the Court disregards an objectively intolerable risk of severe pain.

\n

A

\n

Like the Court, I would review for clear error the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s finding that 500 milligrams of midazolam will render someone sufficiently unconscious \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98to resist the noxious stimuli which could occur from the application of the second and third drugs.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 18\xe2\x80\x9319 (quoting App. 77). Unlike the Court, however, I would do so without abdicating our duty to examine critically the factual predicates for the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s finding\xe2\x80\x94namely, Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 testimony that midazolam has a \xe2\x80\x9cceiling effect\xe2\x80\x9d only \xe2\x80\x9cat the spinal cord level,\xe2\x80\x9d and that a \xe2\x80\x9c500 milligram dose of midazolam\xe2\x80\x9d can therefore \xe2\x80\x9ceffectively paralyze the brain.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 78. To be sure, as the Court observes, such scientific testimony may at times lie at the boundaries of fed-eral courts\xe2\x80\x99 expertise. See ante, at 17\xe2\x80\x9318. But just because a purported expert says something does not make it so. Especially when important constitutional rights are at stake, federal district courts must carefully evaluate the premises and evidence on which scientific conclusions are based, and appellate courts must ensure that the courts below have in fact carefully considered all the evidence presented. Clear error exists \xe2\x80\x9cwhen although there is evidence to support\xe2\x80\x9d a finding, \xe2\x80\x9cthe reviewing court on the entire evidence is left with the definite and firm conviction that a mistake has been committed.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. United States Gypsum Co.,\n\n333 U.\xc2\xa0S. 364,\n395 (1948)\n. Here, given the numerous flaws in Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 testimony, there can be little doubt that the District Court clearly erred in relying on it.

\n

To begin, Dr. Evans identified no scientific literature to support his opinion regarding midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s properties at higher-than-normal doses. Apart from a Material Safety Data Sheet that was relevant only insofar as it suggests that a low dose of midazolam may occasionally be toxic, see ante, at 27\xe2\x80\x94an issue I discuss further below\xe2\x80\x94Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 testimony seems to have been based on the Web site www.drugs.com. The Court may be right that \xe2\x80\x9cpetitioners do not identify any incorrect statements from drugs.com on which Dr. Evans relied.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 27. But that is because there were no statements from drugs.com that supported the critically disputed aspects of Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 opinion. If anything, the Web site supported petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 contentions, as it expressly cautioned that midazolam \xe2\x80\x9c[s]hould not be used alone for maintenance of anesthesia,\xe2\x80\x9d App. H to Pet. for Cert. 6159, and contained no warning that an excessive dose of midazolam could \xe2\x80\x9cparalyze the brain,\xe2\x80\x9d see id., at 6528\xe2\x80\x936529.

\n

Most importantly, nothing from drugs.com\xe2\x80\x94or, for that matter, any other source in the record\xe2\x80\x94corroborated Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 key testimony that midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s ceiling effect is limited to the spinal cord and does not pertain to the brain. Indeed, the State appears to have disavowed Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 spinal-cord theory, refraining from even mentioning it in its brief despite the fact that the District Court expressly relied on this testimony as the basis for finding that larger doses of midazolam will have greater anesthetic effects. App. 78. The Court likewise assiduously avoids defending this theory.

\n

That is likely because this aspect of Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 testi-mony was not just unsupported, but was directly refuted by the studies and articles cited by Drs. Lubarsky and Sasich. Both of these experts relied on academic texts describing benzodiazepines\xe2\x80\x99 ceiling effect and explaining why it prevents these drugs from rendering a person completely insensate. See Stoelting & Hillier 141, 144 (describing midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s ceiling effect and contrasting the drug with barbiturates); Saari 244 (observing that \xe2\x80\x9cabolishment of perception of environmental stimuli cannot usually be generated\xe2\x80\x9d). One study further made clear that the ceiling effect is apparent in the brain. See id., at 250.

\n

These scientific sources also appear to demonstrate that Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 spinal-cord theory\xe2\x80\x94i.e., that midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s ceiling effect is limited to the spinal cord\xe2\x80\x94was premised on a basic misunderstanding of midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s mechanism of action. I say \xe2\x80\x9cappear\xe2\x80\x9d not because the sources themselves are unclear about how midazolam operates: They plainly state that midazolam functions by promoting GABA\xe2\x80\x99s inhibitory effects on the central nervous system. See, e.g., Stoelting & Hillier 140. Instead, I use \xe2\x80\x9cappear\xe2\x80\x9d because discerning the rationale underlying Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 testimony is difficult. His spinal-cord theory might, however, be explained at least in part by his apparent belief that rather than promoting GABA\xe2\x80\x99s inhibitory effects, midazolam produces sedation by \xe2\x80\x9ccompet[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d with GABA and thus \xe2\x80\x9cinhibit[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d GABA\xe2\x80\x99s effect. App. 312\xe2\x80\x93313.[2] Regardless, I need not delve too deeply into Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 alternative scientific reality. It suffices to say that to the extent that Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 testimony was based on his understanding of the source of midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s pharmacological properties, that understanding was wrong.

\n

These inconsistencies and inaccuracies go to the very heart of Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 expert opinion, as they were the key components of his professed belief that one can extrapolate from what is known about midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s effect at low doses to conclude that the drug would \xe2\x80\x9cparalyz[e] the brain\xe2\x80\x9d at Oklahoma\xe2\x80\x99s planned dose. Id., at 314. All three experts recognized that there had been no scientific testing on the use of this amount of midazolam in conjunction with these particular lethal injection drugs. See ante, at 19; App. 176 (Lubarsky), 243\xe2\x80\x93244 (Sasich), 327 (Evans). For this reason, as the Court correctly observes, \xe2\x80\x9cextrapolation was reasonable.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 20. But simply because extrapolation may be reasonable or even required does not mean that every conceivable method of extrapolation can be credited, or that all estimates stemming from purported extrapolation are worthy of belief. Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 view was that because 40 milligrams of midazolam could be used to induce unconsciousness, App. 294, and because more drug will generally produce more effect, a significantly larger dose of 500 milligrams would not just induce unconsciousness but allow for its maintenance in the face of extremely painful stimuli, and ultimately even cause death itself. In his words: \xe2\x80\x9c[A]s you increase the dose of midazolam, it\xe2\x80\x99s a linear effect, so you\xe2\x80\x99re going to continue to get an impact from higher doses of the drug.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 332. If, however, there is a ceiling with respect to midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s effect on the brain\xe2\x80\x94as petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 experts established there is\xe2\x80\x94then such simplistic logic is not viable. In this context, more is not necessarily better, and Dr. Evans was plainly wrong to presume it would be.

\n

If Dr. Evans had any other basis for the \xe2\x80\x9cextrapolation\xe2\x80\x9d that led him to conclude 500 milligrams of midazolam would \xe2\x80\x9cparalyz[e] the brain,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 314, it was even further divorced from scientific evidence and logic. Having emphasized that midazolam had been known to cause approximately 80 deaths, Dr. Evans asserted that his opinion regarding the efficacy of Oklahoma\xe2\x80\x99s planned use of the drug represented \xe2\x80\x9cessentially an extrapolation from a toxic effect.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 327 (emphasis added); see id., at 308. Thus, Dr. Evans appeared to believe\xe2\x80\x94and again, I say \xe2\x80\x9cappeared\xe2\x80\x9d because his rationale is not clear\xe2\x80\x94that because midazolam caused some deaths, it would necessarily cause complete unconsciousness and then death at especially high doses. But Dr. Evans also thought, and Dr. Lubarsky confirmed, that these midazolam fatalities had occurred at very low doses\xe2\x80\x94well below what any expert said would produce unconsciousness. See id., at 207, 308. These deaths thus seem to represent the rare, unfortunate side effects that one would expect to see with any drug at normal therapeutic doses; they provide no indication of the effect one would expect midazolam to have on the brain at substantially higher doses. Deaths occur with almost any product. One might as well say that because some people occasionally die from eating one peanut, one hundred peanuts would necessarily induce a coma and death in anyone.[3]

\n

In sum, then, Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 conclusions were entirely unsupported by any study or third-party source, contradicted by the extrinsic evidence proffered by petitioners, inconsistent with the scientific understanding of midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s properties, and apparently premised on basic logical errors. Given these glaring flaws, the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s acceptance of Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 claim that 500 milligrams of midazolam would \xe2\x80\x9cparalyz[e] the brain\xe2\x80\x9d cannot be credited. This is not a case \xe2\x80\x9c[w]here there are two permissibleviews of the evidence,\xe2\x80\x9d and the District Court chose one; rather, it is one where the trial judge credited \xe2\x80\x9cone of two or more witnesses\xe2\x80\x9d even though that witness failed to tell \xe2\x80\x9ca coherent and facially plausible story that is not contradicted by extrinsic evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Anderson v. Bessemer City,\n\n470 U.\xc2\xa0S. 564\n\xe2\x80\x93575 (1985). In other words, this is a case in which the District Court clearly erred. See ibid.

\n

B

\n

Setting aside the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s erroneous factual finding that 500 milligrams of midazolam will necessarily \xe2\x80\x9cparalyze the brain,\xe2\x80\x9d the question is whether the Court is nevertheless correct to hold that petitioners failed to demonstrate that the use of midazolam poses an \xe2\x80\x9cobjectively intolerable risk\xe2\x80\x9d of severe pain. See Baze, 553 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 50 (plurality opinion) (internal quotation marks omitted). I would hold that they made this showing. That is because, in stark contrast to Dr. Evans, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 experts were able to point to objective evidence indicating that midazolam cannot serve as an effective anesthetic that \xe2\x80\x9crender[s] a person insensate to pain caused by the second and third [lethal injection] drugs.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 23.

\n

As observed above, these experts cited multiple sources supporting the existence of midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s ceiling effect. That evidence alone provides ample reason to doubt midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s efficacy. Again, to prevail on their claim, petitioners need only establish an intolerable risk of pain, not a certainty. See Baze, 553 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 50. Here, the State is attempting to use midazolam to produce an effect the drug has never previously been demonstrated to produce, and despite studies indicating that at some point increasing the dose will not actually increase the drug\xe2\x80\x99s effect. The State is thus proceeding in the face of a very real risk that the drug will not work in the manner it claims.

\n

Moreover, and perhaps more importantly, the record provides good reason to think this risk is substantial. The Court insists that petitioners failed to provide \xe2\x80\x9cprobative evidence\xe2\x80\x9d as to whether \xe2\x80\x9cmidazolam\xe2\x80\x99s ceiling effect occurs below the level of a 500-milligram dose and at a point at which the drug does not have the effect of rendering a person insensate to pain.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 23. It emphasizes that Dr. Lubarsky was unable to say \xe2\x80\x9cat what dose the ceiling effect occurs,\xe2\x80\x9d and could only estimate that it was \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[p]robably after about .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. 40 to 50 milligrams.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 23 (quoting App. 225).

\n

But the precise dose at which midazolam reaches its ceiling effect is irrelevant if there is no dose at which the drug can, in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s words, render a person \xe2\x80\x9cinsensate to pain.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 23. On this critical point, Dr. Lubarsky was quite clear.[4] He explained that the drug \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not work to produce\xe2\x80\x9d a \xe2\x80\x9clack of consciousness as noxious stimuli are applied,\xe2\x80\x9d and is \xe2\x80\x9cnot sufficient to produce a surgical plane of anesthesia in human beings.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 204. He also noted that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he drug would never be used and has never been used as a sole anesthetic to give anesthesia during a surgery,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 223, and asserted that \xe2\x80\x9cthe drug was not approved by the FDA as a sole anesthetic because after the use of fairly large doses that were sufficient to reach the ceiling effect and produce induction of unconsciousness, the patients responded to the surgery,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 219. Thus, Dr. Lubarsky may not have been able to identify whether this effect would be reached at 40, 50, or 60 milligrams or some higher threshold, but he could specifythat at no level would midazolam reliably keep an in-mate unconscious once the second and third drugs were delivered.[5]

\n

These assertions were amply supported by the evidence of the manner in which midazolam is and can be used. All three experts agreed that midazolam is utilized as the sole sedative only in minor procedures. Dr. Evans, for example, acknowledged that while midazolam may be used as the sole drug in some procedures that are not \xe2\x80\x9cterribly invasive,\xe2\x80\x9d even then \xe2\x80\x9cyou would [generally] see it used in combination with a narcotic.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 307. And though, as the Court observes, Dr. Sasich believed midazolam could be \xe2\x80\x9cused for medical procedures like colonoscopies and gastroscopies,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 21, he insisted that these procedures were not necessarily painful, and that it would be a \xe2\x80\x9cbig jump\xe2\x80\x9d to conclude that midazolam would be effective to maintain unconsciousness throughout an execution. Tr. 369\xe2\x80\x93370. Indeed, the record provides no reason to think that these procedures cause excruciating pain remotely comparable to that produced by the second and third lethal injection drugs Oklahoma intends to use.

\n

As for more painful procedures, the consensus was also clear: Midazolam is not FDA-approved for, and is not used as, a sole drug to maintain unconsciousness. See App. 171 (Lubarsky), 262 (Sasich), 327 (Evans). One might infer from the fact that midazolam is not used as the sole anesthetic for more serious procedures that it cannot be used for them. But drawing such an inference is unnecessary, as petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 experts invoked sources expressly stating as much. In particular, Dr. Lubarsky pointed to a survey article that cited four separate authorities and declared that \xe2\x80\x9c[m]idazolam cannot be used alone .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to maintain adequate anesthesia.\xe2\x80\x9d Reves 318; see also Stoelting & Hillier 145 (explaining that midzolam is used for \xe2\x80\x9cinduction of anesthesia,\xe2\x80\x9d and that, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n combination with other drugs, [it] may be used for maintenance of anesthesia\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)).

\n

This evidence was alone sufficient, but if one wanted further support for these conclusions it was provided by the Lockett and Wood executions. The procedural flaws that marred the Lockett execution created the conditions for an unintended (and grotesque) experiment on midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s efficacy. Due to problems with the IV line, Lockett was not fully paralyzed after the second and third drugs were administered. He had, however, been administered more than enough midazolam to \xe2\x80\x9crender an average person unconscious,\xe2\x80\x9d as the District Court found. App. 57. When Lockett awoke and began to writhe and speak, he demonstrated the critical difference between midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s ability to render an inmate unconscious and its ability to maintain the inmate in that state. The Court insists that Lockett\xe2\x80\x99s execution involved \xe2\x80\x9conly 100 milligrams of midazolam,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 28, but as explained previously, more is not necessarily better given midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s ceiling effect.

\n

The Wood execution is perhaps even more probative. Despite being given over 750 milligrams of midazolam, Wood gasped and snorted for nearly two hours. These reactions were, according to Dr. Lubarsky, inconsistent with Wood being fully anesthetized, App. 177\xe2\x80\x93178, and belie the claim that a lesser dose of 500 milligrams would somehow suffice. The Court attempts to distinguish the Wood execution on the ground that the timing of Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s administration of midazolam was different. Ante, at 28. But as Dr. Lubarsky testified, it did not \xe2\x80\x9cmatter\xe2\x80\x9d whether in Wood\xe2\x80\x99s execution the \xe2\x80\x9cmidazolam was introduced all at once or over .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. multiple doses,\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he drug has a sufficient half life that the effect is cumulative.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 220; see also Saari 253 (midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9celimination half-life ranges from 1.7 to 3.5 h[ours]\xe2\x80\x9d).[6] Nor does the fact that Wood\xe2\x80\x99s dose of midazolam was paired with hydromorphone rather than a paralytic and potassium chromide, see ante, at 29, appear to have any relevance\xe2\x80\x94other than that the use of this analgesic drug may have meant that Wood did not experience the same degree of searing pain that an inmate executed under Oklahoma\xe2\x80\x99s protocol may face.

\n

By contrast, Florida\xe2\x80\x99s use of this same three-drug protocol in 11 executions, see ante, at 28 (citing Brief for State of Florida as Amicus Curiae 1), tells us virtually nothing. Although these executions have featured no obvious mishaps, the key word is \xe2\x80\x9cobvious.\xe2\x80\x9d Because the protocol involves the administration of a powerful paralytic, it is, as Drs. Sasich and Lubarsky explained, impossible to tell whether the condemned inmate in fact remained unconscious. App. 218, 273; see also Baze, 553 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 71 (Stevens, J., concurring in judgment). Even in these executions, moreover, there have been indications of the inmates\xe2\x80\x99 possible awareness. See Brief for State of Alabama et al. as Amici Curiae 9\xe2\x80\x9313 (describing the 11 Flor-ida executions, and noting that some allegedly involved blinking and other movement after administration of the three drugs).[7]

\n

Finally, none of the State\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csafeguards\xe2\x80\x9d for administering these drugs would seem to mitigate the substantial risk that midazolam will not work, as the Court contends. See ante, at 21\xe2\x80\x9322. Protections ensuring that officials have properly secured a viable IV site will not enable midazolam to have an effect that it is chemically incapable of having. Nor is there any indication that the State\xe2\x80\x99s monitoring of the inmate\xe2\x80\x99s consciousness will be able to anticipate whether the inmate will remain unconscious while the second and third drugs are administered. No one questions whether midazolam can induce unconsciousness. The problem, as Lockett\xe2\x80\x99s execution vividly illustrates, is that an unconscious inmate may be awakened by the pain and respiratory distress caused by administration of the second and third drugs. At that point, even if it were possible to determine whether the inmate is conscious\xe2\x80\x94dubious, given the use of a paralytic\xe2\x80\x94it is already too late. Presumably for these reasons, the Tenth Circuit characterized the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on these procedural mechanisms as \xe2\x80\x9cnot relevant to its rejection of [petitioners\xe2\x80\x99] claims regarding the inherent characteristics of midazolam.\xe2\x80\x9d Warner, 776 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 733.

\n

C

\n

The Court not only disregards this record evidence of midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s inadequacy, but also fails to fully appreciate the procedural posture in which this case arises. Petitioners have not been accorded a full hearing on the merits of their claim. They were granted only an abbreviated evidentiary proceeding that began less than three months after the State issued its amended execution protocol; they did not even have the opportunity to present rebuttal evidence after Dr. Evans testified. They sought a preliminary injunction, and thus were not required to prove their claim, but only to show that they were likely to succeed on the merits. See Winter v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc.,\n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 7,\n20 (2008)\n; Hill v. McDonough,\n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 573,\n584 (2006)\n.

\n

Perhaps the State could prevail after a full hearing, though this would require more than Dr. Evans\xe2\x80\x99 unsupported testimony. At the preliminary injunction stage, however, petitioners presented compelling evidence suggesting that midazolam will not work as the State intends. The State, by contrast, offered absolutely no contrary evidence worth crediting. Petitioners are thus at the very least likely to prove that, due to midazolam\xe2\x80\x99s inherent deficiencies, there is a constitutionally intolerable risk that they will be awake, yet unable to move, while chemicals known to cause \xe2\x80\x9cexcruciating pain\xe2\x80\x9d course through their veins. Baze, 553 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 71 (Stevens, J., concurring in judgment).

\n

III

\n

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s determination that the use of midazolam poses no objectively intolerable risk of severe pain is factually wrong. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 challenge also fails because they identified no available alternative means by which the State may kill them is legally indefensible.

\n

A

\n

This Court has long recognized that certain methods of execution are categorically off-limits. The Court first confronted an\n \nEighth Amendment challenge to a method of execution in Wilkerson v. Utah,\n\n99 U.\xc2\xa0S. 130 (1879)\n. Although Wilkerson approved the particular method at issue\xe2\x80\x94the firing squad\xe2\x80\x94it made clear that \xe2\x80\x9cpublic dissection,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cburning alive,\xe2\x80\x9d and other \xe2\x80\x9cpunishments of torture .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in the same line of unnecessary cruelty, are forbidden by [the Eighth A]mendment to the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 135\xe2\x80\x93136. Eleven years later, in rejecting a challenge to the first proposed use of the electric chair, the Court again reiterated that \xe2\x80\x9cif the punishment prescribed for an offense against the laws of the State were manifestly cruel and unusual, as burning at the stake, crucifixion, breaking on the wheel, or the like, it would be the duty of the courts to adjudge such penalties to be within the constitutional prohibition.\xe2\x80\x9d In re Kemmler,\n\n136 U.\xc2\xa0S. 436,\n446 (1890)\n.

\n

In the more than a century since, the Members of this Court have often had cause to debate the full scope of the\n \nEighth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition of cruel and unusual punishment. See, e.g., Furman v. Georgia,\n\n408 U.\xc2\xa0S. 238 (1972)\n. But there has been little dispute that it at the very least precludes the imposition of \xe2\x80\x9cbarbarous physical punishments.\xe2\x80\x9d Rhodes v. Chapman,\n\n452 U.\xc2\xa0S. 337,\n345 (1981)\n; see, e.g., Solem v. Helm,\n\n463 U.\xc2\xa0S. 277,\n284 (1983)\n; id., at 312\xe2\x80\x93313 (Burger, C.\xc2\xa0J., dissenting); Baze, 553 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 97\xe2\x80\x9399 (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment); Harmelin v. Michigan,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 957,\n976 (1991)\n (opinion of Scalia, J.). Nor has there been any question that the Amendment prohibits such \xe2\x80\x9cinherently barbaric punishments under all circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Graham v. Florida,\n\n560 U.\xc2\xa0S. 48\n (2010) (emphasis added). Simply stated, the \xe2\x80\x9c\n \nEighth Amendment categorically prohibits the infliction of cruel and unusual punishments.\xe2\x80\x9d Penry v. Lynaugh,\n\n492 U.\xc2\xa0S. 302,\n330 (1989)\n (emphasis added).

\n

B

\n

The Court today, however, would convert this categorical prohibition into a conditional one. A method of execution that is intolerably painful\xe2\x80\x94even to the point of being the chemical equivalent of burning alive\xe2\x80\x94will, the Court holds, be unconstitutional if, and only if, there is a \xe2\x80\x9cknown and available alternative\xe2\x80\x9d method of execution. Ante, at 15. It deems Baze to foreclose any argument to the contrary. Ante, at 14.

\n

Baze held no such thing. In the first place, the Court cites only the plurality opinion in Baze as support for its known-and-available-alternative requirement. See ibid. Even assuming that the Baze plurality set forth such a requirement\xe2\x80\x94which it did not\xe2\x80\x94none of the Members of the Court whose concurrences were necessary to sustain the Baze Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment articulated a similar view. See 553 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 71\xe2\x80\x9377, 87 (Stevens, J., concurring in judgment); id., at 94, 99\xe2\x80\x93107 (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment); id., at 107\xe2\x80\x93108, 113 (Breyer, J., concurring in judgment). In general, \xe2\x80\x9cthe holding of the Court may be viewed as that position taken by those Members who concurred in the judgments on the narrowest grounds.\xe2\x80\x9d Marks v. United States,\n\n430 U.\xc2\xa0S. 188,\n193 (1977)\n (internal quotation marks omitted). And as the Court observes, ante, at 14, n.\xc2\xa02, the opinion of Justice Thomas, joined by Justice Scalia, took the broadest position with respect to the degree of intent that state officials must have inorder to have violated the\n \nEighth Amendment, concluding that only a method of execution deliberately designedto inflict pain, and not one simply designed with deliberate indifference to the risk of severe pain, would be un-constitutional. 553 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 94 (Thomas, J., concurringin judgment). But this understanding of the\n \nEighth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s intent requirement is unrelated to, and thus not any broader or narrower than, the requirement the Court now divines from Baze. Because the position that a plaintiff challenging a method of execution under the\n \nEighth Amendment must prove the availability of an alternative means of execution did not \xe2\x80\x9crepresent the views of a majority of the Court,\xe2\x80\x9d it was not the holding of the Baze Court. CTS Corp. v. Dynamics Corp. of America,\n\n481 U.\xc2\xa0S. 69,\n81 (1987)\n.

\n

In any event, even the Baze plurality opinion provides no support for the Court\xe2\x80\x99s proposition. To be sure, that opinion contains the following sentence: \xe2\x80\x9c[The condemned] must show that the risk is substantial when compared to the known and available alternatives.\xe2\x80\x9d 553 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 61. But the meaning of that key sentence and the limits of the requirement it imposed are made clear by the sentence directly preceding it: \xe2\x80\x9cA stay of execution may not be granted on grounds such as those asserted here unless the condemned prisoner establishes that the State\xe2\x80\x99s lethal injection protocol creates a demonstrated risk of severe pain.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added). In Baze, the very premise of the petitioners\xe2\x80\x99\n \nEighth Amendment claim was that they had \xe2\x80\x9cidentified a significant risk of harm [in Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s protocol] that [could] be eliminated by adopting alternative procedures.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 51. Their basic theory was that even if the risk of pain was only, say, 25%, that risk would be objectively intolerable if there was an obvious alternative that would reduce the risk to 5%. See Brief for Petitioners in Baze v. Rees, O.\xc2\xa0T. 2007, No. 07\xe2\x80\x935439, p.\xc2\xa029 (\xe2\x80\x9cIn view of the severity of the pain risked and the ease with which it could be avoided, Petitioners should not have been required to show a high likelihood that they would suffer such pain .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). Thus, the \xe2\x80\x9cgrounds .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. asserted\xe2\x80\x9d for relief in Baze were that the State\xe2\x80\x99s protocol was intolerably risky given the alternative procedures the State could have employed.

\n

Addressing this claim, the Baze plurality clarified that \xe2\x80\x9ca condemned prisoner cannot successfully challenge a State\xe2\x80\x99s method of execution merely by showing a slightly or marginally safer alternative,\xe2\x80\x9d 553 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 51; instead, to succeed in a challenge of this type, the comparative risk must be \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 61. Nowhere did the plurality suggest that all challenges to a State\xe2\x80\x99s method of execution would require this sort of comparative-risk analysis. Recognizing the relevance of available alternatives is not at all the same as concluding that their absence precludes a claimant from showing that a chosen method carries objectively intolerable risks. If, for example, prison officials chose a method of execution that has a 99% chance of causing lingering and excruciating pain, certainly that risk would be objectively intolerable whether or not the officials ignored other methods in making this choice. Irrespective of the existence of alternatives, there are some risks \xe2\x80\x9cso grave that it violates contemporary standards of decency to expose anyone unwillingly to\xe2\x80\x9d them. Helling v. McKinney,\n\n509 U.\xc2\xa0S. 25,\n36 (1993)\n (emphasis in original).

\n

That the Baze plurality\xe2\x80\x99s statement regarding a condemned inmate\xe2\x80\x99s ability to point to an available alternative means of execution pertained only to challenges premised on the existence of such alternatives is further evidenced by the opinion\xe2\x80\x99s failure to distinguish or even mention the Court\xe2\x80\x99s unanimous decision in Hill v. McDonough,\n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 573\n. Hill held that a \xc2\xa71983 plaintiff challenging a State\xe2\x80\x99s method of execution need not \xe2\x80\x9cidentif[y] an alternative, authorized method of execution.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 582. True, as the Court notes, ante, at 14\xe2\x80\x9315, Hill did so in the context of addressing \xc2\xa71983\xe2\x80\x99s pleading standard, rejecting the proposed alternative-means requirement because the Court saw no basis for the \xe2\x80\x9c[i]mposition of heightened pleading requirements.\xe2\x80\x9d 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 582. But that only confirms that the Court in Hill did not view the availability of an alternative means of execution as an element of an\n \nEighth Amendment claim: If it had, then requiring the plaintiff to plead this element would not have meant imposing a heightened standard at all, but rather would have been entirely consistent with \xe2\x80\x9ctraditional pleading requirements.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; see Ashcroft v. Iqbal,\n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 662,\n678 (2009)\n. The Baze plurality opinion should not be understood to have so carelessly tossed aside Hill\xe2\x80\x99s underlying premise less than two years later.

\n

C

\n

In reengineering Baze to support its newfound rule, the Court appears to rely on a flawed syllogism. If the death penalty is constitutional, the Court reasons, then there must be a means of accomplishing it, and thus some available method of execution must be constitutional. See ante, at 4, 15\xe2\x80\x9316. But even accepting that the death penalty is, in the abstract, consistent with evolving standards of decency, but see ante, p. ___ (Breyer, J., dissenting), the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion does not follow. The constitutionality of the death penalty may inform our conception of the degree of pain that would render a particular method of imposing it unconstitutional. See Baze, 553 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 47 (plurality opinion) (because \xe2\x80\x9c[s]ome risk of pain is inherent in any method of execution,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t is clear .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the Constitution does not demand the avoidance of all risk of pain\xe2\x80\x9d). But a method of execution that is \xe2\x80\x9cbarbarous,\xe2\x80\x9d Rhodes, 452 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 345, or \xe2\x80\x9cinvolve[s] torture or a lingering death,\xe2\x80\x9d Kemmler, 136 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 447, does not become less so just because it is the only method currently available to a State. If all available means of conducting an execution constitute cruel and unusual punishment, then conducting the execution will constitute cruel and usual punishment. Nothing compels a State to perform an execution. It does not get a constitutional free pass simply because it desires to deliver the ultimate penalty; its ends do not justify any and all means. If a State wishes to carry out an execution, it must do so subject to the constraints that our Constitution imposes on it, including the obligation to ensure that its chosen method is not cruel and unusual. Certainly the condemned has no duty to devise or pick a constitutional instrument of his or her own death.

\n

For these reasons, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s available-alternative requirement leads to patently absurd consequences. Petitioners contend that Oklahoma\xe2\x80\x99s current protocol is a barbarous method of punishment\xe2\x80\x94the chemical equivalent of being burned alive. But under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s new rule, it would not matter whether the State intended to use midazolam, or instead to have petitioners drawn and quartered, slowly tortured to death, or actually burned at the stake: because petitioners failed to prove the availability of sodium thiopental or pentobarbital, the State could execute them using whatever means it designated. But see Baze, 553 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 101\xe2\x80\x93102 (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9cIt strains credulity to suggest that the defining characteristic of burning at the stake, disemboweling, drawing and quartering, beheading, and the like was that they involved risks of pain that could be eliminated by using alternative methods of execution\xe2\x80\x9d).[8] The\n \nEighth Amendment cannot possibly countenance such a result.

\n

D

\n

In concocting this additional requirement, the Court is motivated by a desire to preserve States\xe2\x80\x99 ability to conduct executions in the face of changing circumstances. See ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x936, 27\xe2\x80\x9328. It is true, as the Court details, that States have faced \xe2\x80\x9cpractical obstacle[s]\xe2\x80\x9d to obtaining lethal injection drugs since Baze was decided. Ante, at 4. One study concluded that recent years have seen States change their protocols \xe2\x80\x9cwith a frequency that is unprecedented among execution methods in this country\xe2\x80\x99s history.\xe2\x80\x9d Denno, Lethal Injection Chaos Post-Baze, 102 Geo. L.\xc2\xa0J. 1331, 1335 (2014).

\n

But why such developments compel the Court\xe2\x80\x99s imposition of further burdens on those facing execution is a mystery. Petitioners here had no part in creating the shortage of execution drugs; it is odd to punish them for the actions of pharmaceutical companies and others who seek to disassociate themselves from the death penalty\xe2\x80\x94actions which are, of course, wholly lawful. Nor, certainly, should these rapidly changing circumstances give us any greater confidence that the execution methods ultimately selected will be sufficiently humane to satisfy the\n \nEighth Amendment. Quite the contrary. The execution protocols States hurriedly devise as they scramble to locate new and untested drugs, see supra, at 3, are all the more likely to be cruel and unusual\xe2\x80\x94presumably, these drugs would have been the States\xe2\x80\x99 first choice were they in fact more effective. But see Denno, The Lethal Injection Quandry: How Medicine Has Dismantled the Death Penalty, 76 Ford. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 49, 65\xe2\x80\x9379 (2007) (describing the hurried and unreasoned process by which States first adopted the original three-drug protocol). Courts\xe2\x80\x99 review of execution methods should be more, not less, searching when States are engaged in what is in effect human experimentation.

\n

It is also worth noting that some condemned inmates may read the Court\xe2\x80\x99s surreal requirement that they iden-tify the means of their death as an invitation to propose methods of executions less consistent with modern sensibilities. Petitioners here failed to meet the Court\xe2\x80\x99s new test because of their assumption that the alternative drugs to which they pointed, pentobarbital and sodium thiopental, were available to the State. See ante, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314. This was perhaps a reasonable assumption, especially given that neighboring Texas and Missouri still to this day continue to use pentobarbital in executions. See The Death Penalty Institute, Execution List 2015, online at www.deathpenaltyinfo.org/execution-list-2015 (as visited June 26, 2015, and available in the Clerk of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file).

\n

In the future, however, condemned inmates might well decline to accept States\xe2\x80\x99 current reliance on lethal injection. In particular, some inmates may suggest the firing squad as an alternative. Since the 1920\xe2\x80\x99s, only Utah has utilized this method of execution. See S. Banner, The Death Penalty 203 (2002); Johnson, Double Murderer Executed by Firing Squad in Utah, N.\xc2\xa0Y. Times, June 19, 2010, p. A12. But there is evidence to suggest that the firing squad is significantly more reliable than other methods, including lethal injection using the various combinations of drugs thus far developed. See A. Sarat, Gruesome Spectacles: Botched Executions and America\xe2\x80\x99s Death Penalty, App. A, p.\xc2\xa0177 (2014) (calculating that while 7.12% of the 1,054 executions by lethal injection between 1900 and 2010 were \xe2\x80\x9cbotched,\xe2\x80\x9d none of the 34 executions by firing squad had been). Just as important, there is some reason to think that it is relatively quick and painless. See Banner, supra, at 203.

\n

Certainly, use of the firing squad could be seen as a devolution to a more primitive era. See Wood v. Ryan, 759 F.\xc2\xa03d 1076, 1103 (CA9 2014) (Kozinski, C.\xc2\xa0J., dissenting from denial of rehearing en banc). That is not to say, of course, that it would therefore be unconstitutional. But lethal injection represents just the latest iteration of the States\xe2\x80\x99 centuries-long search for \xe2\x80\x9cneat and non-disfiguring homicidal methods.\xe2\x80\x9d C. Brandon, The Electric Chair: An Unnatural American History 39 (1999) (quoting Editorial, New York Herald, Aug. 10, 1884); see generally Banner, supra, at 169\xe2\x80\x93207. A return to the firing squad\xe2\x80\x94and the blood and physical violence that comes with it\xe2\x80\x94is a step in the opposite direction. And some might argue that the visible brutality of such a death could conceivably give rise to its own\n \nEighth Amendment concerns. See Campbell v. Wood,\n\n511 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1119\n\xe2\x80\x931123 (1994) (Blackmun, J., dissenting from denial of stay of execution and certiorari); Glass v. Louisiana,\n\n471 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1080,\n1085 (1985)\n (Brennan, J., dissenting from denial of certiorari). At least from a condemned inmate\xe2\x80\x99s perspective, however, such visible yet relatively painless violence may be vastly preferable to an excruciatingly painful death hidden behind a veneer of medication. The States may well be reluctant to pull back the curtain for fear of how the rest of us might react to what we see. But we deserve to know the price of our collective comfort before we blindly allow a State to make condemned inmates pay it in our names.

\xe2\x80\x9cBy protecting even those convicted of heinous crimes, the\n \nEighth Amendment reaffirms the duty of the government to respect the dignity of all persons.\xe2\x80\x9d Roper v. Simmons,\n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 551,\n560 (2005)\n. Today, however, the Court absolves the State of Oklahoma of this duty. It does so by misconstruing and ignoring the record evidence regarding the constitutional insufficiency of midazolam as a sedative in a three-drug lethal injection cocktail, and by imposing a wholly unprecedented obligation on the condemned inmate to identify an available means for his or her own execution. The contortions necessary to save this particular lethal injection protocol are not worth the price. I dissent.

Justice Breyer, with whom Justice Ginsburg joins, dissenting.

\n

For the reasons stated in Justice Sotomayor\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, I dissent from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding. But rather than try to patch up the death penalty\xe2\x80\x99s legal wounds one at a time, I would ask for full briefing on a more basic question: whether the death penalty violates the Constitution.

\n

The relevant legal standard is the standard set forth in the\n \nEighth Amendment. The Constitution there forbids the \xe2\x80\x9cinflict[ion]\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9ccruel and unusual punishments.\xe2\x80\x9d Amdt. 8. The Court has recognized that a \xe2\x80\x9cclaim that punishment is excessive is judged not by the standards that prevailed in 1685 when Lord Jeffreys presided over the \xe2\x80\x98Bloody Assizes\xe2\x80\x99 or when the Bill of Rights was adopted, but rather by those that currently prevail.\xe2\x80\x9d Atkins v. Virginia,\n\n536 U.\xc2\xa0S. 304,\n311 (2002)\n. Indeed, the Constitution prohibits various gruesome punishments that were common in Blackstone\xe2\x80\x99s day. See 4 W. Blackstone, Commentaries on the Laws of England 369\xe2\x80\x93370 (1769) (listing mutilation and dismembering, among other punishments).

\n

Nearly 40 years ago, this Court upheld the death pen-alty under statutes that, in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s view, contained safeguards sufficient to ensure that the penalty would be applied reliably and not arbitrarily. See Gregg v. Georgia,\n\n428 U.\xc2\xa0S. 153,\n187 (1976)\n (joint opinion of Stewart, Powell, and Stevens, JJ.); Proffitt v. Florida,\n\n428 U.\xc2\xa0S. 242,\n247 (1976)\n (joint opinion of Stewart, Powell, and Stevens, JJ.); Jurek v. Texas,\n\n428 U.\xc2\xa0S. 262,\n268 (1976)\n (joint opinion of Stewart, Powell, and Stevens, JJ.); but cf. Woodson v. North Carolina,\n\n428 U.\xc2\xa0S. 280,\n303 (1976)\n (plurality opinion) (striking down mandatory death penalty); Roberts v. Louisiana,\n\n428 U.\xc2\xa0S. 325,\n331 (1976)\n (plurality opinion) (similar). The circumstances and the evidence of the death penalty\xe2\x80\x99s application have changed radically since then. Given those changes, I believe that it is now time to reopen the question.

\n

In 1976, the Court thought that the constitutional infirmities in the death penalty could be healed; the Court in effect delegated significant responsibility to the States to develop procedures that would protect against those constitutional problems. Almost 40 years of studies, surveys, and experience strongly indicate, however, that this effort has failed. Today\xe2\x80\x99s administration of the death penalty involves three fundamental constitutional defects: (1) serious unreliability, (2) arbitrariness in application, and (3) unconscionably long delays that undermine the death penalty\xe2\x80\x99s penological purpose. Perhaps as a result, (4) most places within the United States have abandoned its use.

\n

I shall describe each of these considerations, emphasizing changes that have occurred during the past four decades. For it is those changes, taken together with my own 20 years of experience on this Court, that lead me to believe that the death penalty, in and of itself, now likely constitutes a legally prohibited \xe2\x80\x9ccruel and unusual punishmen[t].\xe2\x80\x9d U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Amdt. 8.

\n

I

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cCruel\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94Lack of Reliability

\n

This Court has specified that the finality of death creates a \xe2\x80\x9cqualitative difference\xe2\x80\x9d between the death penalty and other punishments (including life in prison). Woodson, 428 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 305 (plurality opinion). That \xe2\x80\x9cqualitative difference\xe2\x80\x9d creates \xe2\x80\x9ca corresponding difference in the need for reliability in the determination that death is the appropriate punishment in a specific case.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. There is increasing evidence, however, that the death penalty as now applied lacks that requisite reliability. Cf. Kansas v. Marsh,\n\n548 U.\xc2\xa0S. 163\n\xe2\x80\x93211 (2006) (Souter, J., dis-senting) (DNA exonerations constitute \xe2\x80\x9ca new body offact\xe2\x80\x9d when considering the constitutionality of capital punishment).

\n

For one thing, despite the difficulty of investigating the circumstances surrounding an execution for a crime that took place long ago, researchers have found convincing evidence that, in the past three decades, innocent people have been executed. See, e.g., Liebman, Fatal Injustice; Carlos DeLuna\xe2\x80\x99s Execution Shows That a Faster, Cheaper Death Penalty is a Dangerous Idea, L.\xc2\xa0A. Times, June 1, 2012, p. A19 (describing results of a 4-year investigation, later published as The Wrong Carlos: Anatomy of a Wrongful Execution (2014), that led its authors to conclude that Carlos DeLuna, sentenced to death and executed in 1989, six years after his arrest in Texas for stabbinga single mother to death in a convenience store, was innocent); Grann, Trial By Fire: Did Texas Execute An Innocent Man? The New Yorker, Sept. 7, 2009, p.\xc2\xa042 (describing evidence that Cameron Todd Willingham was convicted, and ultimately executed in 2004, for the apparently motiveless murder of his three children as the result of invalid scientific analysis of the scene of the house fire that killed his children). See also, e.g., Press Release: Gov. Ritter Grants Posthumous Pardon in Case Dating Back to 1930s, Jan. 7, 2011, p. 1 (Colorado Governor granted full and unconditional posthumous pardon to Joe Arridy, a man with an IQ of 46 who was executed in 1936, because, according to the Governor, \xe2\x80\x9can overwhelming body of evidence indicates the 23-year-old Arridy was innocent, including false and coerced confessions, the likelihood that Arridy was not in Pueblo at the time of the killing, and an admission of guilt by someone else\xe2\x80\x9d); R. Warden, Wilkie Collins\xe2\x80\x99s The Dead Alive: The Novel, the Case, and Wrongful Convictions 157\xe2\x80\x93158 (2005) (in 1987, Nebraska Governor Bob Kerrey pardoned William Jackson Marion, who had been executed a century earlier for the murder of John Cameron, a man who later turned up alive; the alleged victim, Cameron, had gone to Mexico to avoid a shotgun wedding).

\n

For another, the evidence that the death penalty has been wrongly imposed (whether or not it was carried out), is striking. As of 2002, this Court used the word \xe2\x80\x9cdisturbing\xe2\x80\x9d to describe the number of instances in which individuals had been sentenced to death but later exonerated. Atthat time, there was evidence of approximately 60exonerations in capital cases. Atkins, 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at320, n. 25; National Registry of Exonerations, online at http://www.law.umich.edu/special/exoneration/Pages/about.aspx (all Internet materials as visited June 25, 2015, and available in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file). (I use \xe2\x80\x9cexoneration\xe2\x80\x9d to refer to relief from all legal consequences of a capital conviction through a decision by a prosecutor, a Governor or a court, after new evidence of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s innocence was discovered.) Since 2002, the number of exonerations in capital cases has risen to 115. Ibid.; National Registry of Exonerations, Exonerations in the United States, 1989\xe2\x80\x932012, pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x937 (2012) (Exonerations 2012 Report) (defining exoneration); accord, Death Penalty Information Center (DPIC), Innocence: List of Those Freed from Death Row, online at http://www.deathpenaltyinfo.org/innocence-and-death-penalty (DPIC Innocence List) (calculating, under a slightly different definition of exoneration, the number of exonerations since 1973 as 154). Last year, in 2014, six death row inmates were exonerated based on actual innocence. All had been imprisoned for more than 30 years (and one for almost 40 years) at the time of their exonerations. National Registry of Exonerations, Exonerations in 2014, p. 2 (2015).

\n

The stories of three of the men exonerated within the last year are illustrative. DNA evidence showed that Henry Lee McCollum did not commit the rape and murder for which he had been sentenced to death. Katz & Eckholm, DNA Evidence Clears Two Men in 1983 Murder, N.\xc2\xa0Y. Times, Sept. 3, 2014, p. A1. Last Term, this Court ordered that Anthony Ray Hinton, who had been convicted of murder, receive further hearings in state court; he was exonerated earlier this year because the forensic evidence used against him was flawed. Hinton v. Alabama, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014) (per curiam); Blinder, Alabama Man on Death Row for Three Decades Is Freed as State\xe2\x80\x99s Case Erodes, N.\xc2\xa0Y. Times, Apr. 4, 2014, p. A11. And when Glenn Ford, also convicted of murder, was exonerated, the prosecutor admitted that even \xe2\x80\x9c[a]t the time this case was tried there was evidence that would have cleared Glenn Ford.\xe2\x80\x9d Stroud, Lead Prosecutor Apologizes for Role in Sending Man to Death Row, Shreveport Times, Mar. 27, 2015. All three of these men spent 30 years on death row before being exonerated. I return to these examples infra.

\n

Furthermore, exonerations occur far more frequently where capital convictions, rather than ordinary criminal convictions, are at issue. Researchers have calculated that courts (or State Governors) are 130 times more likely to exonerate a defendant where a death sentence is at issue. They are nine times more likely to exonerate where a capital murder, rather than a noncapital murder, is at issue. Exonerations 2012 Report 15\xe2\x80\x9316, and nn. 24\xe2\x80\x9326.

\n

Why is that so? To some degree, it must be because the law that governs capital cases is more complex. To some degree, it must reflect the fact that courts scrutinize capital cases more closely. But, to some degree, it likely also reflects a greater likelihood of an initial wrongful conviction. How could that be so? In the view of researchers who have conducted these studies, it could be so because the crimes at issue in capital cases are typically horrendous murders, and thus accompanied by intense community pressure on police, prosecutors, and jurors to secure a conviction. This pressure creates a greater likelihood of convicting the wrong person. See Gross, Jacoby, Matheson, Montgomery, & Patil, Exonerations in the United States 1989 Through 2003, 95 J. Crim. L. & C. 523, 531\xe2\x80\x93533 (2005); Gross & O\xe2\x80\x99Brien, Frequency and Predictors of False Conviction: Why We Know So Little, and New Data on Capital Cases, 5 J. Empirical L. Studies 927, 956\xe2\x80\x93957 (2008) (noting that, in comparing those who were exonerated from death row to other capital defendants who were not so exonerated, the initial police investigations tended to be shorter for those exonerated); see also B. Garrett, Convicting the Innocent: Where Criminal Prosecutions Go Wrong (2011) (discussing other common causes of wrongful convictions generally including false confessions, mistaken eyewitness testimony, untruthful jailhouse informants, and ineffective defense counsel).

\n

In the case of Cameron Todd Willingham, for example, who (as noted earlier) was executed despite likely innocence, the State Bar of Texas recently filed formal misconduct charges against the lead prosecutor for his actions\xe2\x80\x94actions that may have contributed to Willingham\xe2\x80\x99s conviction. Possley, Prosecutor Accused of Misconduct in Death Penalty Case, Washington Post, Mar. 19, 2015, p.\xc2\xa0A3. And in Glenn Ford\xe2\x80\x99s case, the prosecutor admitted that he was partly responsible for Ford\xe2\x80\x99s wrongful conviction, issuing a public apology to Ford and explaining that, at the time of Ford\xe2\x80\x99s conviction, he was \xe2\x80\x9cnot as interested in justice as [he] was in winning.\xe2\x80\x9d Stroud, supra.

\n

Other factors may also play a role. One is the practice of death-qualification; no one can serve on a capital jury who is not willing to impose the death penalty. See Rozelle, The Principled Executioner: Capital Juries\xe2\x80\x99 Bias and the Benefits of True Bifurcation, 38 Ariz. S. L.\xc2\xa0J. 769, 772\xe2\x80\x93793, 807 (2006) (summarizing research and concluding that \xe2\x80\x9c[f]or over fifty years, empirical investigation has demonstrated that death qualification skews juries toward guilt and death\xe2\x80\x9d); Note, Mandatory Voir Dire Questions in Capital Cases: A Potential Solution to the Biases of Death Qualification, 10 Roger Williams Univ. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 211, 214\xe2\x80\x93223 (2004) (similar).

\n

Another is the more general problem of flawed forensic testimony. See Garrett, supra, at 7. The Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI), for example, recently found that flawed microscopic hair analysis was used in 33 of 35 capital cases under review; 9 of the 33 had already been executed. FBI, National Press Releases, FBI Testimony on Microscopic Hair Analysis Contained Errors in at Least 90 Percent of Cases in Ongoing Review, Apr. 20, 2015. See also Hsu, FBI Admits Errors at Trials: False Matches on Crime-Scene Hair, Washington Post, Apr. 19, 2015, p.\xc2\xa0A1 (in the District of Columbia, which does not have the death penalty, five of seven defendants in cases with flawed hair analysis testimony were eventually exonerated).

\n

In light of these and other factors, researchers estimate that about 4% of those sentenced to death are actually innocent. See Gross, O\xe2\x80\x99Brien, Hu, & Kennedy, Rate of False Conviction of Criminal Defendants Who Are Sentenced to Death, 111 Proceeding of the National Academy of Sciences 7230 (2014) (full-scale study of all death sentences from 1973 through 2004 estimating that 4.1% of those sentenced to death are actually innocent); Risinger, Innocents Convicted: An Empirically Justified Factual Wrongful Conviction Rate, 97 J. Crim. L. & C. 761 (2007) (examination of DNA exonerations in death penalty cases for murder-rapes between 1982 and 1989 suggesting an analogous rate of between 3.3% and 5%).

\n

Finally, if we expand our definition of \xe2\x80\x9cexoneration\xe2\x80\x9d (which we limited to errors suggesting the defendant was actually innocent) and thereby also categorize as \xe2\x80\x9cerroneous\xe2\x80\x9d instances in which courts failed to follow legally required procedures, the numbers soar. Between 1973 and 1995, courts identified prejudicial errors in 68% of the capital cases before them. Gelman, Liebman, West, & Kiss, A Broken System: The Persistent Patterns of Reversals of Death Sentences in the United States, 1 J. Empirical L. Studies 209, 217 (2004). State courts on direct and postconviction review overturned 47% of the sentences they reviewed. Id., at 232. Federal courts, reviewing capital cases in habeas corpus proceedings, found error in 40% of those cases. Ibid.

\n

This research and these figures are likely controversial. Full briefing would allow us to scrutinize them with more care. But, at a minimum, they suggest a serious problem of reliability. They suggest that there are too many instances in which courts sentence defendants to death without complying with the necessary procedures; and they suggest that, in a significant number of cases, the death sentence is imposed on a person who did not commit the crime. See Earley, A Pink Cadillac, An IQ of 63, and A Fourteen-Year-Old from South Carolina: Why I Can No Longer Support the Death Penalty, 49 U. Rich. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 811, 813 (2015) (\xe2\x80\x9cI have come to the conclusion that the death penalty is based on a false utopian premise. That false premise is that we have had, do have, will have 100% accuracy in death penalty convictions and executions\xe2\x80\x9d); Earley, I Oversaw 36 Executions. Even Death Penalty Supporters Can Push for Change, Guardian, May 12, 2014 (Earley presided over 36 executions as Virginia Attorney General from 1998\xe2\x80\x932001); but see ante, at 2\xe2\x80\x933 (Scalia, J., concurring) (apparently finding no special constitutional problem arising from the fact that the execution of an innocent person is irreversible). Unlike 40 years ago, we now have plausible evidence of unreliability that (perhaps due to DNA evidence) is stronger than the evidence we had before. In sum, there is significantly more research-based evidence today indicating that courts sentence to death individuals who may well be actually innocent or whose convictions (in the law\xe2\x80\x99s view) do not warrant the death penalty\xe2\x80\x99s application.

\n

II

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cCruel\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94Arbitrariness

\n

The arbitrary imposition of punishment is the antithesis of the rule of law. For that reason, Justice Potter Stewart (who supplied critical votes for the holdings in Furman v. Georgia,\n\n408 U.\xc2\xa0S. 238 (1972)\n (per curiam), and Gregg) found the death penalty unconstitutional as administered in 1972:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThese death sentences are cruel and unusual in the same way that being struck by lightning is cruel and unusual. For, of all the people convicted of [death-eligible crimes], many just as reprehensible as these, the[se] petitioners are among a capriciously selected random handful upon which the sentence of death has in fact been imposed.\xe2\x80\x9d Furman, 408 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 309\xe2\x80\x93310 (concurring opinion).

\n

See also id., at 310 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he Eighth and\n \nFourteenth Amendments cannot tolerate the infliction of a sentence of death under legal systems that permit this unique penalty to be so wantonly and so freakishly imposed\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 313 (White, J., concurring) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he death penalty is exacted with great infrequency even for the most atrocious crimes and .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. there is no meaningful basis for distinguishing the few cases in which it is imposed from the many cases in which it is not\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

When the death penalty was reinstated in 1976, this Court acknowledged that the death penalty is (and would be) unconstitutional if \xe2\x80\x9cinflicted in an arbitrary and capricious manner.\xe2\x80\x9d Gregg, 428 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 188 (joint opinion of Stewart, Powell, and Stevens, JJ.); see also id., at 189 (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]here discretion is afforded a sentencing body on a matter so grave as the determination of whether a human life should be taken or spared, that discretion must be suitably directed and limited so as to minimize the risk of wholly arbitrary and capricious action\xe2\x80\x9d); Godfrey v. Georgia,\n\n446 U.\xc2\xa0S. 420,\n428 (1980)\n (plurality opinion) (similar).

\n

The Court has consequently sought to make the application of the death penalty less arbitrary by restricting its use to those whom Justice Souter called \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the worst of the worst.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Kansas v. Marsh, 548 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 206 (dissenting opinion); see also Roper v. Simmons,\n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 551,\n568 (2005)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cCapital punishment must be limited to those offenders who commit a narrow category of the most serious crimes and whose extreme culpability makes them the most deserving of execution\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)); Kennedy v. Louisiana,\n\n554 U.\xc2\xa0S. 407,\n420 (2008)\n (citing Roper, supra, at 568).

\n

Despite the Gregg Court\xe2\x80\x99s hope for fair administration of the death penalty, 40 years of further experience make it increasingly clear that the death penalty is imposed arbitrarily, i.e., without the \xe2\x80\x9creasonable consistency\xe2\x80\x9d legally necessary to reconcile its use with the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s commands. Eddings v. Oklahoma,\n\n455 U.\xc2\xa0S. 104,\n112 (1982)\n.

\n

Thorough studies of death penalty sentences support this conclusion. A recent study, for example, examined all death penalty sentences imposed between 1973 and 2007 in Connecticut, a State that abolished the death penalty in 2012. Donohue, An Empirical Evaluation of the Connecticut Death Penalty System Since 1973: Are There Unlawful Racial, Gender, and Geographic Disparities? 11 J. Empirical Legal Studies 637 (2014). The study reviewed treatment of all homicide defendants. It found 205 instances in which Connecticut law made the defendant eligible for a death sentence. Id., at 641\xe2\x80\x93643. Courts imposed a death sentence in 12 of these 205 cases, of which 9 were sustained on appeal. Id., at 641. The study then measured the \xe2\x80\x9cegregiousness\xe2\x80\x9d of the murderer\xe2\x80\x99s conduct in those 9 cases, developing a system of metrics designed to do so. Id., at 643\xe2\x80\x93645. It then compared the egregiousness of the conduct of the 9 defendants sentenced to death with the egregiousness of the conduct of defendants in the remaining 196 cases (those in which the defendant, though found guilty of a death-eligible offense, was ultimately not sentenced to death). Application of the studies\xe2\x80\x99 metrics made clear that only 1 of those 9 defendants was indeed the \xe2\x80\x9cworst of the worst\xe2\x80\x9d (or was, at least, within the 15% considered most \xe2\x80\x9cegregious\xe2\x80\x9d). The remaining eight were not. Their behavior was no worse than the behavior of at least 33 and as many as 170 other defendants (out of a total pool of 205) who had not been sentenced to death. Id., at 678\xe2\x80\x93679.

\n

Such studies indicate that the factors that most clearly ought to affect application of the death penalty\xe2\x80\x94namely, comparative egregiousness of the crime\xe2\x80\x94often do not. Other studies show that circumstances that ought not to affect application of the death penalty, such as race, gender, or geography, often do.

\n

Numerous studies, for example, have concluded that individuals accused of murdering white victims, as opposed to black or other minority victims, are more likely to receive the death penalty. See GAO, Report to the Senate and House Committees on the Judiciary: Death Penalty Sentencing 5 (GAO/GGD\xe2\x80\x9390\xe2\x80\x9357, 1990) (82% of the 28 studies conducted between 1972 and 1990 found that race of victim influences capital murder charge or death sentence, a \xe2\x80\x9cfinding .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. remarkably consistent across data sets, states, data collection methods, and analytic techniques\xe2\x80\x9d); Shatz & Dalton, Challenging the Death Penalty with Statistics: Furman, McCleskey, and a Single County Case Study, 34 Cardozo L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1227, 1245\xe2\x80\x931251 (2013) (same conclusion drawn from 20 plus studies conducted between 1990 and 2013).

\n

Fewer, but still many, studies have found that the gender of the defendant or the gender of the victim makes a not-otherwise-warranted difference. Id., at 1251\xe2\x80\x931253 (citing many studies).

\n

Geography also plays an important role in determining who is sentenced to death. See id., at 1253\xe2\x80\x931256. And that is not simply because some States permit the death penalty while others do not. Rather within a death pen-alty State, the imposition of the death penalty heavily de-pends on the county in which a defendant is tried. Smith, The Geography of the Death Penalty and its Ramifications, 92 B. U. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 227, 231\xe2\x80\x93232 (2012) (hereinafter Smith); see also Donohue, supra, at 673 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he single most important influence from 1973\xe2\x80\x932007 explaining whether a death-eligible defendant [in Connecticut] would be sentenced to death was whether the crime occurred in Waterbury [County]\xe2\x80\x9d). Between 2004 and 2009, for example, just 29 counties (fewer than 1% of counties in the country) accounted for approximately half of all death sentences imposed nationwide. Smith 233. And in 2012, just 59 counties (fewer than 2% of counties in the country) accounted for all death sentences imposed nationwide. DPIC, The 2% Death Penalty: How A Minority of Counties Produce Most Death Cases At Enormous Costs to All 9 (Oct. 2013).

\n

What accounts for this county-by-county disparity? Some studies indicate that the disparity reflects the decisionmaking authority, the legal discretion, and ultimately the power of the local prosecutor. See, e.g., Goelzhauser, Prosecutorial Discretion Under Resource Constraints: Budget Allocations and Local Death-Charging Decisions, 96 Judicature 161, 162\xe2\x80\x93163 (2013); Barnes, Sloss, & Thaman, Place Matters (Most): An Empirical Study of Prosecutorial Decision-Making in Death-Eligible Cases, 51 Ariz. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 305 (2009) (analyzing Missouri); Donohue, An Empirical Evaluation of the Connecticut Death Pen-alty System, at 681 (Connecticut); Marceau, Kamin, & Foglia, Death Eligibility in Colorado: Many Are Called, Few Are Chosen, 84 U. Colo. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1069 (2013) (Colo-rado); Shatz & Dalton, supra, at 1260\xe2\x80\x931261 (Alameda County).

\n

Others suggest that the availability of resources for defense counsel (or the lack thereof) helps explain geographical differences. See, e.g., Smith 258\xe2\x80\x93265 (counties with higher death-sentencing rates tend to have weaker public defense programs); Liebman & Clarke, Minority Practice, Majority\xe2\x80\x99s Burden: The Death Penalty Today, 9 Ohio S. J. Crim. L. 255, 274 (2011) (hereinafter Liebman & Clarke) (similar); see generally Bright, Counsel for the Poor: The Death Sentence Not for the Worst Crime but for the Worst Lawyer, 103 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J. 1835 (1994).

\n

Still others indicate that the racial composition of and distribution within a county plays an important role. See, e.g., Levinson, Smith, & Young, Devaluing Death: An Empirical Study of Implicit Racial Bias on Jury-Eligible Citizens in Six Death Penalty States, 89 N.\xc2\xa0Y.\xc2\xa0U. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 513, 533\xe2\x80\x93536 (2014) (summarizing research on this point); see also Shatz & Dalton, supra, at 1275 (describing research finding that death-sentencing rates were lowest in counties with the highest nonwhite population); cf. Cohen & Smith, The Racial Geography of the Federal Death Penalty, 85 Wash. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 425 (2010) (arguing that the federal death penalty is sought disproportionately where the federal district, from which the jury will be drawn, has a dramatic racial difference from the county in which the federal crime occurred).

\n

Finally, some studies suggest that political pressures, including pressures on judges who must stand for election, can make a difference. See Woodward v. Alabama, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2013) (Sotomayor, J., dissenting from denial of certiorari) (slip op., at 7) (noting that empirical evidence suggests that, when Alabama judges reverse jury recommendations, these \xe2\x80\x9cjudges, who are elected in partisan proceedings, appear to have succumbed to electoral pressures\xe2\x80\x9d); Harris v. Alabama,\n\n513 U.\xc2\xa0S. 504,\n519 (1995)\n (Stevens, J., dissenting) (similar); Gelman, 1 J. Empirical L. Studies, at 247 (elected state judges are less likely to reverse flawed verdicts in capital cases in small towns than in larger communities).

\n

Thus, whether one looks at research indicating that irrelevant or improper factors\xe2\x80\x94such as race, gender, local geography, and resources\xe2\x80\x94do significantly determine who receives the death penalty, or whether one looks at research indicating that proper factors\xe2\x80\x94such as \xe2\x80\x9cegregiousness\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94do not determine who receives the death penalty, the legal conclusion must be the same: The research strongly suggests that the death penalty is imposedarbitrarily.

\n

Justice Thomas catalogues the tragic details of various capital cases, ante, at 6\xe2\x80\x9310 (concurring opinion), but this misses my point. Every murder is tragic, but unless we return to the mandatory death penalty struck down in Woodson, 428 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 304\xe2\x80\x93305, the constitutionality of capital punishment rests on its limited application to the worst of the worst, supra, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310. And this extensive body of evidence suggests that it is not so limited.

\n

Four decades ago, the Court believed it possible to interpret the\n \nEighth Amendment in ways that would significantly limit the arbitrary application of the death sentence. See Gregg, 428 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 195 (joint opinion of Stewart, Powell, and Stevens, JJ.) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he concerns expressed in Furman that the penalty of death not be imposed in an arbitrary or capricious manner can be met\xe2\x80\x9d). But that no longer seems likely.

\n

The Constitution does not prohibit the use of prosecutorial discretion. Id., at 199, and n. 50 (joint opinion of Stewart, Powell, and Stevens, JJ.); McCleskey v. Kemp,\n\n481 U.\xc2\xa0S. 279\n\xe2\x80\x93308, and n. 28, 311\xe2\x80\x93312 (1987). It has not proved possible to increase capital defense funding significantly. Smith, The Supreme Court and the Politics of Death, 94 Va. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 283, 355 (2008) (\xe2\x80\x9cCapital defenders are notoriously underfunded, particularly in states .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that lead the nation in executions\xe2\x80\x9d); American Bar Assn. (ABA) Guidelines for the Appointment and Performance of Defense Counsel in Death Penalty Cases, Guideline 9.1, Commentary (rev. ed. Feb. 2003), in 31 Hofstra L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 913, 985 (2003) (\xe2\x80\x9c[C]ompensation of attorneys for death penalty representation remains notoriously inadequate\xe2\x80\x9d). And courts cannot easily inquire into judicial motivation. See, e.g., Harris, supra.

\n

Moreover, racial and gender biases may, unfortunately, reflect deeply rooted community biases (conscious or unconscious), which, despite their legal irrelevance, may affect a jury\xe2\x80\x99s evaluation of mitigating evidence, see Callins v. Collins,\n\n510 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1141,\n1153 (1994)\n (Blackmun, J., dissenting from denial of certiorari) (\xe2\x80\x9cPerhaps it should not be surprising that the biases and prejudices that infect society generally would influence the determination of who is sentenced to death\xe2\x80\x9d). Nevertheless, it remains the jury\xe2\x80\x99s task to make the individualized assessment of whether the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s mitigation evidence entitles him to mercy. See, e.g., Penry v. Lynaugh,\n\n492 U.\xc2\xa0S. 302,\n319 (1989)\n; Lockett v. Ohio,\n\n438 U.\xc2\xa0S. 586\n\xe2\x80\x93605 (1978) (opinion of Burger, C.\xc2\xa0J.); Woodson, 428 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 304\xe2\x80\x93305 (plurality opinion).

\n

Finally, since this Court held that comparative proportionality review is not constitutionally required, Pulley v. Harris,\n\n465 U.\xc2\xa0S. 37 (1984)\n, it seems unlikely that appeals can prevent the arbitrariness I have described. See Kaufman-Osborn, Capital Punishment, Proportionality Review, and Claims of Fairness (with Lessons from Washington State), 79 Wash. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 775, 791\xe2\x80\x93792 (2004) (after Pulley, many States repealed their statutes requiring comparative proportionality review, and most state high courts \xe2\x80\x9creduced proportionality review to a perfunctory exercise\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).

\n

The studies bear out my own view, reached after considering thousands of death penalty cases and last-minute petitions over the course of more than 20 years. I see discrepancies for which I can find no rational explanations. Cf. Godfrey, 446 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 433 (plurality opinion) (\xe2\x80\x9cThere is no principled way to distinguish this case, in which the death penalty was imposed, from the many cases in which it was not\xe2\x80\x9d). Why does one defendant who committed a single-victim murder receive the death pen-alty (due to aggravators of a prior felony conviction and an after-the-fact robbery), while another defendant does not, despite having kidnapped, raped, and murdered a young mother while leaving her infant baby to die at the scene of the crime. Compare State v. Badgett, 361 N.\xc2\xa0C. 234, 644 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 206 (2007), and Pet. for Cert. in Badgett v. North Carolina, O.\xc2\xa0T. 2006, No. 07\xe2\x80\x936156, with Charbonneau, Andre Edwards Sentenced to Life in Prison for 2001 Murder, WRAL, Mar. 26, 2004, online at http://www.wral.com/news/local/story/109648. Why does one defendant who committed a single-victim murder receive the death pen-alty (due to aggravators of a prior felony conviction and acting recklessly with a gun), while another defendant does not, despite having committed a \xe2\x80\x9ctriple murder\xe2\x80\x9d by killing a young man and his pregnant wife? Compare Commonwealth v. Boxley, 596 Pa. 620, 948 A. 2d 742 (2008), and Pet. for Cert., O.\xc2\xa0T. 2008, No. 08\xe2\x80\x936172, with Shea, Judge Gives Consecutive Life Sentences for Triple Murder, Philadelphia Inquirer, June 29, 2004, p. B5. For that matter, why does one defendant who participated in a single-victim murder-for-hire scheme (plus an after-the-fact robbery) receive the death penalty, while another defendant does not, despite having stabbed his wife 60 times and killed his 6-year-old daughter and 3-year-old son while they slept? See Donohue, Capital Punishment in Connecticut, 1973\xe2\x80\x932007: A Comprehensive Evaluation from 4686 Murders to One Execution, pp. 128\xe2\x80\x93134 (2013), online at http://works.bepress.com/john_donohue/87. In each instance, the sentences compared were imposed in the same State at about the same time.

\n

The question raised by these examples (and the many more I could give but do not), as well as by the research to which I have referred, is the same question Justice Stewart, Justice Powell, and others raised over the course of several decades: The imposition and implementation of the death penalty seems capricious, random, indeed, arbitrary. From a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s perspective, to receive that sentence, and certainly to find it implemented, is the equivalent of being struck by lightning. How then can we reconcile the death penalty with the demands of a Constitution that first and foremost insists upon a rule of law?

\n

III

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cCruel\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94Excessive Delays

\n

The problems of reliability and unfairness almost inevitably lead to a third independent constitutional problem: excessively long periods of time that individuals typically spend on death row, alive but under sentence of death. That is to say, delay is in part a problem that the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s own demands create. Given the special need for reliability and fairness in death penalty cases, the\n \nEighth Amendment does, and must, apply to the death penalty \xe2\x80\x9cwith special force.\xe2\x80\x9d Roper, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 568. Those who face \xe2\x80\x9cthat most severe sanction must have a fair opportunity to show that the Constitution prohibits their execution.\xe2\x80\x9d Hall v. Florida, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 22). At the same time, the Constitution insists that \xe2\x80\x9cevery safeguard\xe2\x80\x9d be \xe2\x80\x9cobserved\xe2\x80\x9d when \xe2\x80\x9ca defendant\xe2\x80\x99s life is at stake.\xe2\x80\x9d Gregg, 428 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 187 (joint opinion of Stewart, Powell, and Stevens, JJ.); Furman, 408 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 306 (Stewart, J., concurring) (death \xe2\x80\x9cdiffers from all other forms of criminal punishment, not in degree but in kind\xe2\x80\x9d); Woodson, supra, at 305 (plurality opinion) (\xe2\x80\x9cDeath, in its finality, differs more from life imprisonment than a 100-year prison term differs from one of only a year or two\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

These procedural necessities take time to implement. And, unless we abandon the procedural requirements that assure fairness and reliability, we are forced to confront the problem of increasingly lengthy delays in capital cases. Ultimately, though these legal causes may help to explain, they do not mitigate the harms caused by delay itself.

\n

A

\n

Consider first the statistics. In 2014, 35 individualswere executed. Those executions occurred, on average,nearly 18 years after a court initially pronounced itssentence of death. DPIC, Execution List 2014, onlineat http:\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0www.deathpenaltyinfo.org\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0execution\xc2\xa0-\xc2\xa0list-2014 (showing an average delay of 17 years, 7 months). In some death penalty States, the average delay is longer. Inan oral argument last year, for example, the State admitted that the last 10 prisoners executed in Florida had spent an average of nearly 25 years on death row before execution. Tr. of Oral Arg. in Hall v. Florida, O. T. 2013, No. 12\xe2\x80\x9310882, p. 46.

\n

The length of the average delay has increased dramatically over the years. In 1960, the average delay between sentencing and execution was two years. See Aarons, Can Inordinate Delay Between a Death Sentence and Execution Constitute Cruel and Unusual Punishment? 29 Seton Hall L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 147, 181 (1998). Ten years ago (in 2004) the average delay was about 11 years. See Dept. of Justice, Bureau of Justice Statistics (BJS), T. Snell, Capital Punishment, 2013\xe2\x80\x94Statistical Tables 14 (Table 10) (rev. Dec. 2014) (hereinafter BJS 2013 Stats). By last year the average had risen to about 18 years. DPIC, Execution List 2014, supra. Nearly half of the 3,000 inmates now on death row have been there for more than 15 years. And, at present execution rates, it would take more than 75 years to carry out those 3,000 death sentences; thus, the average person on death row would spend an additional 37.5 years there before being executed. BJS 2013 Stats, at 14, 18 (Tables 11 and 15).

\n

I cannot find any reasons to believe the trend will soon be reversed.

\n

B

\n

These lengthy delays create two special constitutional difficulties. See Johnson v. Bredesen,\n\n558 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1067,\n1069 (2009)\n (Stevens, J., statement respecting denial of certiorari). First, a lengthy delay in and of itself is especially cruel because it \xe2\x80\x9csubjects death row inmates to decades of especially severe, dehumanizing conditions of confinement.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; Gomez v. Fierro,\n\n519 U.\xc2\xa0S. 918 (1996)\n (Stevens, J., dissenting) (excessive delays from sentencing to execution can themselves \xe2\x80\x9cconstitute cruel and unusual punishment prohibited by the\n \nEighth Amendment\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Lackey v. Texas,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1045 (1995)\n (memorandum of Stevens, J., respecting denial of certiorari); Knight v. Florida,\n\n528 U. S. 990,\n993 (1999)\n (Breyer, J., dissenting from denial of certiorari). Second, lengthy delay undermines the death penalty\xe2\x80\x99s penological rationale. Johnson, supra, at 1069; Thompson v. McNeil,\n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1114\n,1115 (2009) (statement of Stevens, J., respecting denial of certiorari).

\n

1

\n

Turning to the first constitutional difficulty, nearly all death penalty States keep death row inmates in isolation for 22 or more hours per day. American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU), A Death Before Dying: Solitary Confinement on Death Row 5 (July 2013) (ACLU Report). This occurs even though the ABA has suggested that death row inmates be housed in conditions similar to the general population, and the United Nations Special Rapporteur on Torture has called for a global ban on solitary confinement longer than 15 days. See id., at 2, 4; ABA Standards for Criminal Justice: Treatment of Prisoners 6 (3d ed. 2011). And it is well documented that such prolonged solitary confinement produces numerous deleterious harms. See, e.g., Haney, Mental Health Issues in Long-Term Solitary and \xe2\x80\x9cSupermax\xe2\x80\x9d Confinement, 49 Crime & Delinquency 124, 130 (2003) (cataloguing studies finding that solitary confinement can cause prisoners to experience \xe2\x80\x9canxiety, panic, rage, loss of control, paranoia, hallucinations, and self-mutilations,\xe2\x80\x9d among many other symptoms); Grassian, Psychiatric Effects of Solitary Confinement, 22 WashU. J.\xc2\xa0L. & Policy 325, 331 (2006) (\xe2\x80\x9c[E]ven a few days of solitary confinement will predictably shift the [brain\xe2\x80\x99s] electroencephalogram (EEG) pattern toward an abnormal pattern characteristic of stupor and delirium\xe2\x80\x9d); accord, In re Medley,\n\n134 U.\xc2\xa0S. 160\n\xe2\x80\x93168 (1890); see also Davis v. Ayala, ante, at 1\xe2\x80\x934 (Kennedy, J., concurring).

\n

The dehumanizing effect of solitary confinement is aggravated by uncertainty as to whether a death sentence will in fact be carried out. In 1890, this Court recognized that, \xe2\x80\x9cwhen a prisoner sentenced by a court to death is confined in the penitentiary awaiting the execution of the sentence, one of the most horrible feelings to which he can be subjected during that time is the uncertainty during the whole of it.\xe2\x80\x9d Medley, supra, at 172. The Court was there describing a delay of a mere four weeks. In the past century and a quarter, little has changed in this respect\xe2\x80\x94except for duration. Today we must describe delays measured, not in weeks, but in decades. Supra, at 18\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

Moreover, we must consider death warrants that have been issued and revoked, not once, but repeatedly. See, e.g., Pet. for Cert. in Su\xc3\xa1rez Medina v. Texas, O. Several inmates have come within hours or days of execution before later being exonerated. 2

\n

The second constitutional difficulty resulting from lengthy delays is that those delays undermine the death penalty\xe2\x80\x99s penological rationale, perhaps irreparably so. The rationale for capital punishment, as for any punishment, classically rests upon society\xe2\x80\x99s need to secure deterrence, incapacitation, retribution, or rehabilitation. Capital punishment by definition does not rehabilitate. It does, of course, incapacitate the offender. But the major alternative to capital punishment\xe2\x80\x94namely, life in prison without possibility of parole\xe2\x80\x94also incapacitates. See Ring v. Arizona,\n\n536 U.\xc2\xa0S. 584,\n615 (2002)\n (Breyer, J., concurring in judgment).

\n

Thus, as the Court has recognized, the death penalty\xe2\x80\x99s penological rationale in fact rests almost exclusively upon a belief in its tendency to deter and upon its ability to satisfy a community\xe2\x80\x99s interest in retribution. See, e.g., Gregg, 428 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 183 (joint opinion of Stewart, Powell, and Stevens, JJ.). Many studies have examined the death penalty\xe2\x80\x99s deterrent effect; some have found such an effect, whereas others have found a lack of evidence that it deters crime. Compare ante, at 5 (Scalia, J., concurring) (collecting studies finding deterrent effect), with e.g., Sorensen, Wrinkle, Brewer, & Marquart, Capital Punishment and Deterrence: Examining the Effect of Executions on Murder in Texas, 45 Crime & Delinquency 481 (1999) (no evidence of a deterrent effect); Bonner & Fessenden, Absence of Executions: A Special Report, States With No Death Penalty Share Lower Homicide Rates, N. Y. Times, Sept. 22, 2000, p. A1 (from 1980\xe2\x80\x932000, homicide rate in death-penalty States was 48% to 101% higher than in non-death-penalty States); Radelet & Akers, Deterrence and the Death Penalty: The Views of the Experts, 87 J. Crim. L. & C. 1, 8 (1996) (over 80% of criminologists believe existing research fails to support deterrence justification); Donohue & Wolfers, Uses and Abuses of Empirical Evidence in the Death Penalty Debate, 58 Stan. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 791, 794 (2005) (evaluating existing statistical evidence and concluding that there is \xe2\x80\x9cprofound uncertainty\xe2\x80\x9d about the existence of a deterrent effect).

\n

Recently, the National Research Council (whose members are drawn from the councils of the National Academy of Sciences, the National Academy of Engineering, and the Institute of Medicine) reviewed 30 years of empirical evidence and concluded that it was insufficient to establish a deterrent effect and thus should \xe2\x80\x9cnot be used to inform\xe2\x80\x9d discussion about the deterrent value of the death penalty. National Research Council, Deterrence and the Death Penalty 2 (D. Nagin & J. Pepper eds. 2012); accord, Baze v. Rees,\n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 35,\n79 (2008)\n (Stevens, J., concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9cDespite 30 years of empirical re-search in the area, there remains no reliable statistical evi-dence that capital punishment in fact deters potential offenders\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

I recognize that a \xe2\x80\x9clack of evidence\xe2\x80\x9d for a proposition does not prove the contrary. See Ring, supra, at 615 (one might believe the studies \xe2\x80\x9cinconclusive\xe2\x80\x9d). But suppose that we add to these studies the fact that, today, very few of those sentenced to death are actually executed, and that even those executions occur, on average, after nearly two decades on death row. DPIC, Execution List 2014, supra. Then, does it still seem likely that the death penalty has a significant deterrent effect?

\n

Consider, for example, what actually happened to the 183 inmates sentenced to death in 1978. As of 2013 (35 years later), 38 (or 21% of them) had been executed; 132 (or 72%) had had their convictions or sentences overturned or commuted; and 7 (or 4%) had died of other (likely natural) causes. Six (or 3%) remained on death row. BJS 2013 Stats, at 19 (Table 16).

\n

The example illustrates a general trend. Of the 8,466 inmates under a death sentence at some point between 1973 and 2013, 16% were executed, 42% had their convictions or sentences overturned or commuted, and 6% died by other causes; the remainder (35%) are still on death row. Id., at 20 (Table 17); see also Baumgartner & Dietrich, Most Death Penalty Sentences Are Overturned: Here\xe2\x80\x99s Why That Matters, Washington Post Blog, Monkey Cage, Mar. 17, 2015 (similar).

\n

Thus an offender who is sentenced to death is two or three times more likely to find his sentence overturned or commuted than to be executed; and he has a good chance of dying from natural causes before any execution (or exoneration) can take place. In a word, executions are rare. And an individual contemplating a crime but evaluating the potential punishment would know that, in any event, he faces a potential sentence of life without parole.

\n

These facts, when recurring, must have some offsetting effect on a potential perpetrator\xe2\x80\x99s fear of a death penalty. And, even if that effect is no more than slight, it makes it difficult to believe (given the studies of deterrence cited earlier) that such a rare event significantly deters horrendous crimes. See Furman, 408 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 311\xe2\x80\x93312 (White, J., concurring) (It cannot \xe2\x80\x9cbe said with confidence that society\xe2\x80\x99s need for specific deterrence justifies death for so few when for so many in like circumstances life imprisonment or shorter prison terms are judged sufficient\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

But what about retribution? Retribution is a valid penological goal. I recognize that surviving relatives of victims of a horrendous crime, or perhaps the community itself, may find vindication in an execution. And a community that favors the death penalty has an understand-able interest in representing their voices. But see A. Sarat, Mercy on Trial: What It Means To Stop an Execution 130 (2005) (Illinois Governor George Ryan explained his decision to commute all death sentences on the ground that it was \xe2\x80\x9ccruel and unusual\xe2\x80\x9d for \xe2\x80\x9cfamily members to go through this .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. legal limbo for [20] years\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

The relevant question here, however, is whether a \xe2\x80\x9ccommunity\xe2\x80\x99s sense of retribution\xe2\x80\x9d can often find vindication in \xe2\x80\x9ca death that comes,\xe2\x80\x9d if at all, \xe2\x80\x9conly several decades after the crime was committed.\xe2\x80\x9d Valle v. Florida, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (Breyer, J., dissenting from denial of stay) (slip op., at 3). By then the community is a different group of people. The offenders and the victims\xe2\x80\x99 families have grown far older. Feelings of outrage may have subsided. The offender may have found himself a changed human being. And sometimes repentance and even forgiveness can restore meaning to lives once ruined. At the same time, the community and victims\xe2\x80\x99 families will know that, even without a further death, the offender will serve decades in prison under a sentence of life without parole.

\n

I recognize, of course, that this may not always be the case, and that sometimes the community believes that an execution could provide closure. Nevertheless, the delays and low probability of execution must play some role in any calculation that leads a community to insist on death as retribution. As I have already suggested, they may well attenuate the community\xe2\x80\x99s interest in retribution to the point where it cannot by itself amount to a significant justification for the death penalty. Id., at ___ (slip op., at 3). In any event, I believe that whatever interest in retribution might be served by the death penalty as currently administered, that interest can be served almost as well by a sentence of life in prison without parole (a sentence that every State now permits, see ACLU, A Living Death: Life Without Parole for Nonviolent Offenses 11, and n.\xc2\xa010 (2013)).

\n

Finally, the fact of lengthy delays undermines any effort to justify the death penalty in terms of its prevalence when the Founders wrote the\n \nEighth Amendment. When the Founders wrote the Constitution, there were no 20- or 30-year delays. Execution took place soon after sentencing. See P. Mackey, Hanging in the Balance: The Anti-Capital Punishment Movement in New York State, 1776\xe2\x80\x931861, p. 17 (1982); T. Jefferson, A Bill for Proportioning Crimes and Punishments (1779), reprinted in The Complete Jefferson 90, 95 (S. Padover ed. 1943); 2 Papers of John Marshall 207\xe2\x80\x93209 (C. Cullen & H. Johnson eds. 1977) (describing petition for commutation based in part on 5-month delay); Pratt v. Attorney Gen. of Jamaica, [1994] 2 A.\xc2\xa0C., at 17 (same in United Kingdom) (collecting cases). And, for reasons I shall describe, infra, at 29\xe2\x80\x9333, we cannot return to the quick executions in the founding era.

\n

3

\n

The upshot is that lengthy delays both aggravate the cruelty of the death penalty and undermine its jurisprudential rationale. And this Court has said that, if the death penalty does not fulfill the goals of deterrence or retribution, \xe2\x80\x9cit is nothing more than the purposeless and needless imposition of pain and suffering and hence an unconstitutional punishment.\xe2\x80\x9d Atkins, 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 319 (quoting Enmund v. Florida,\n\n458 U.\xc2\xa0S. 782,\n798 (1982)\n; internal quotation marks omitted); see also Gregg, 428 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 183 (joint opinion of Stewart, Powell, and Stevens, JJ.) (\xe2\x80\x9csanction imposed cannot be so totally without penological justification that it results in the gratuitous infliction of suffering\xe2\x80\x9d); Furman, supra, at 312 (White, J., concurring) (a \xe2\x80\x9cpenalty with such negligible returns to the State would be patently excessive and cruel and unusual punishment violative of the\n \nEighth Amendment\xe2\x80\x9d); Thompson, 556 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 1115 (statement of Stevens, J., respecting denial of certiorari) (similar).

\n

Indeed, Justice Lewis Powell (who provided a crucial vote in Gregg) came to much the same conclusion, albeit after his retirement from this Court. Justice Powell had come to the Court convinced that the Federal Constitution did not outlaw the death penalty but rather left the matter up to individual States to determine. Furman, supra, at 431\xe2\x80\x93432 (Powell, J., dissenting); see also J. Jeffries, Justice Lewis F. Powell, Jr., p. 409 (2001) (describing Powell, during his time on the Court, as a \xe2\x80\x9cfervent partisan\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9cthe constitutionality of capital punishment\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Soon after Justice Powell\xe2\x80\x99s retirement, Chief Justice Rehnquist appointed him to chair a committee addressing concerns about delays in capital cases, the Ad\xc2\xa0Hoc Committee on Federal Habeas Corpus in Capital Cases (Committee). The Committee presented a report to Congress, and Justice Powell testified that \xe2\x80\x9c[d]elay robs the penalty of much of its deterrent value.\xe2\x80\x9d Habeas Corpus Reform, Hearings before the Senate Committee on the Judiciary, 100th Cong., 1st and 2d Sess., 35 (1989 and 1990). Justice Powell, according to his official biographer, ultimately concluded that capital punishment:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98serves no useful purpose.\xe2\x80\x99 The United States was \xe2\x80\x98unique among the industrialized nations of the West in maintaining the death penalty,\xe2\x80\x99 and it was enforced so rarely that it could not deter. More important, the haggling and delay and seemingly endless litigation in every capital case brought the law itself into disrepute.\xe2\x80\x9d Jeffries, supra, at 452.

\n

In short, the problem of excessive delays led Justice Powell, at least in part, to conclude that the death penalty was unconstitutional.

\n

As I have said, today delays are much worse. When Chief Justice Rehnquist appointed Justice Powell to the Committee, the average delay between sentencing and execution was 7 years and 11 months, compared with 17 years and 7 months today. Compare BJS, L. Greenfeld, Capital Punishment, 1990, p. 11 (Table 12) (Sept. 1991) with supra, at 18\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

C

\n

One might ask, why can Congress or the States not deal directly with the delay problem? Why can they not take steps to shorten the time between sentence and execution, and thereby mitigate the problems just raised? The answer is that shortening delay is much more difficult than one might think. And that is in part because efforts to do so risk causing procedural harms that also undermine the death penalty\xe2\x80\x99s constitutionality.

\n

For one thing, delays have helped to make application of the death penalty more reliable. Recall the case of Henry Lee McCollum, whom DNA evidence exonerated 30 years after his conviction. Katz & Eckholm, N.\xc2\xa0Y. Times, at A1. If McCollum had been executed earlier, he would not have lived to see the day when DNA evidence exonerated him and implicated another man; that man is already serving a life sentence for a rape and murder that he committed just a few weeks after the murder McCollum was convicted of. Ibid. In fact, this Court had earlier denied reviewof McCollum\xe2\x80\x99s claim over the public dissent of only one Justice. McCollum v. North Carolina,\n\n512 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1254 (1994)\n. And yet a full 20 years after the Court denied review, McCollum was exonerated by DNA evidence. There are a significant number of similar cases, some of which I have discussed earlier. See also DPIC Innocence List, supra (Nathson Fields, 23 years; Paul House, 23 years; Nicholas Yarris, 21 years; Anthony Graves, 16 years; Damon Thibodeaux, 15 years; Ricky Jackson, Wiley Bridgeman, and Kwame Ajamu, all exonerated for the same crime 39 years after their convictions).

\n

In addition to those who are exonerated on the ground that they are innocent, there are other individuals whose sentences or convictions have been overturned for other reasons (as discussed above, state and federal courts found error in 68% of the capital cases they reviewed between 1973 and 1995). See Part I, supra. In many of these cases, a court will have found that the individual did not merit the death penalty in a special sense\xe2\x80\x94namely, he failed to receive all the procedural protections that the law requires for the death penalty\xe2\x80\x99s application. By eliminating some of these protections, one likely could reduce delay. But which protections should we eliminate? Should we eliminate the trial-related protections we have established for capital defendants: that they be able to present to the sentencing judge or jury all mitigating circumstances, Lockett v. Ohio,\n\n438 U.\xc2\xa0S. 586\n; that the State provide guidance adequate to reserve the application of the death penalty to particularly serious murders, Gregg,\n\n428 U.\xc2\xa0S. 153\n; that the State provide adequate counsel and, where warranted, adequate expert assistance, Powell v. Alabama,\n\n287 U.\xc2\xa0S. 45 (1932)\n; Wiggins v. Smith,\n\n539 U.\xc2\xa0S. 510 (2003)\n; Ake v. Oklahoma,\n\n470 U.\xc2\xa0S. 68 (1985)\n; or that a jury must find the aggravating factors necessary to impose the death penalty, Ring,\n\n536 U.\xc2\xa0S. 584\n; see also id., at 614 (Breyer, J., concurring in judgment)? Should we no longer ensure that the State does not execute those who are seriously intellectually disabled, Atkins,\n\n536 U.\xc2\xa0S. 304\n? Should we eliminate the requirement that the manner of execution be constitutional, Baze,\n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 35\n, or the requirement that the inmate be mentally competent at the time of his execution, Ford v. Wainwright,\n\n477 U.\xc2\xa0S. 399 (1986)\n? Or should we get rid of the criminal protections that all criminal defendants receive\xe2\x80\x94for instance, that defendants claiming violation of constitutional guarantees (say \xe2\x80\x9cdue process of law\xe2\x80\x9d) may seek a writ of habeas corpus in federal courts? See, e.g., O\xe2\x80\x99Neal v. McAninch,\n\n513 U.\xc2\xa0S. 432 (1995)\n. My answer to these questions is \xe2\x80\x9csurely not.\xe2\x80\x9d But see ante, at 5\xe2\x80\x937 (Scalia, J., concurring).

\n

One might, of course, argue that courts, particularly federal courts providing additional layers of review, apply these and other requirements too strictly, and that causes delay. But, it is difficult for judges, as it would be difficult for anyone, not to apply legal requirements punctiliously when the consequence of failing to do so may well be death, particularly the death of an innocent person. See, e.g., Zant v. Stephens,\n\n462 U.\xc2\xa0S. 862,\n885 (1983)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]lthough not every imperfection in the deliberative process is sufficient, even in a capital case, to set aside a state-court judgment, the severity of the sentence mandates careful scrutiny in the review of any colorable claim of error\xe2\x80\x9d); Kyles v. Whitley,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 419,\n422 (1995)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[O]ur duty to search for constitutional error with painstaking care is never more exacting than it is in a capital case\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)); Thompson, 556 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 1116 (statement of Stevens, J.) (\xe2\x80\x9cJudicial process takes time, but the error rate in capital cases illustrates its necessity\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Moreover, review by courts at every level helps to ensure reliability; if this Court had not ordered that Anthony Ray Hinton receive further hearings in state court, see Hinton v. Alabama, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, he may well have been executed rather than exonerated. In my own view, our legal system\xe2\x80\x99s complexity, our federal system with its separate state and federal courts, our constitutional guarantees, our commitment to fair procedure, and, above all, a special need for reliability and fairness in capital cases, combine to make significant procedural \xe2\x80\x9creform\xe2\x80\x9d unlikely in practice to reduce delays to an acceptable level.

\n

And that fact creates a dilemma: A death penalty system that seeks procedural fairness and reliability brings with it delays that severely aggravate the cruelty of capital punishment and significantly undermine the rationale for imposing a sentence of death in the first place. See Knight, 528 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 998 (Breyer, J., dissenting from denial of certiorari) (one of the primary causes of the delay is the States\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cfailure to apply constitutionally sufficient procedures at the time of initial [conviction or] sentencing\xe2\x80\x9d). But a death penalty system that minimizes delays would undermine the legal system\xe2\x80\x99s efforts to secure reliability and procedural fairness.

\n

In this world, or at least in this Nation, we can have a death penalty that at least arguably serves legitimate penological purposes or we can have a procedural system that at least arguably seeks reliability and fairness in the death penalty\xe2\x80\x99s application. We cannot have both. And that simple fact, demonstrated convincingly over the past 40 years, strongly supports the claim that the death penalty violates the\n \nEighth Amendment. A death penalty system that is unreliable or procedurally unfair would violate the\n \nEighth Amendment. Woodson, 428 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 305 (plurality opinion); Hall, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 22); Roper, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 568. And so would a system that, if reliable and fair in its application of the death penalty, would serve no legitimate penological purpose. Furman, 408 U. S., at 312 (White, J., concurring); Gregg, supra, at 183 (joint opinion of Stewart, Powell, and Stevens, JJ.); Atkins, supra, at 319.

\n

IV

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cUnusual\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94Decline in Use of the Death Penalty

\n

The\n \nEighth Amendment forbids punishments that are cruel and unusual. Last year, in 2014, only seven States carried out an execution. Perhaps more importantly, in the last two decades, the imposition and implementation of the death penalty have increasingly become unusual. I can illustrate the significant decline in the use of the death penalty in several ways.

\n

An appropriate starting point concerns the trajectory of the number of annual death sentences nationwide, from the 1970\xe2\x80\x99s to present day. In 1977\xe2\x80\x94just after the Supreme Court made clear that, by modifying their legislation, States could reinstate the death penalty\xe2\x80\x94137 people were sentenced to death. BJS 2013 Stats, at 19 (Table 16). Many States having revised their death penalty laws to meet Furman\xe2\x80\x99s requirements, the number of death sentences then increased. Between 1986 and 1999, 286 persons on average were sentenced to death each year. BJS 2013 Stats, at 14, 19 (Tables 11 and 16). But, approximately 15 years ago, the numbers began to decline, and they have declined rapidly ever since. See Appendix A, infra (showing sentences from 1977\xe2\x80\x932014). In 1999, 279 persons were sentenced to death. BJS 2013 Stats, at 19 (Table 16). Last year, just 73 persons were sentenced to death. DPIC, The Death Penalty in 2014: Year End Report 1 (2015).

\n

That trend, a significant decline in the last 15 years, also holds true with respect to the number of annual executions. See Appendix B, infra (showing executions from 1977\xe2\x80\x932014). In 1999, 98 people were executed. BJS, Data Collection: National Prisoner Statistics Program (BJS Prisoner Statistics) (available in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file). Last year, that number was only 35. DPIC, The Death Penalty in 2014, supra, at 1.

\n

Next, one can consider state-level data. Often when deciding whether a punishment practice is, constitutionally speaking, \xe2\x80\x9cunusual,\xe2\x80\x9d this Court has looked to the num-ber of States engaging in that practice. Atkins, 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 313\xe2\x80\x93316; Roper, supra, at 564\xe2\x80\x93566. In this respect, the number of active death penalty States has fallen dramatically. In 1972, when the Court decided Furman, the death penalty was lawful in 41 States. Nine States had abolished it. E. Mandery, A Wild Justice: The Death and Resurrection of Capital Punishment in America 145 (2013). As of today, 19 States have abolished the death penalty (along with the District of Columbia), although some did so prospectively only. See DPIC, States With and Without the Death Penalty, online at http://www.deathpenaltyinfo.org/states-and-without-death-penalty. In 11 other States that maintain the death penalty on the books, no execution has taken place for more than eight years: Arkansas (last execution 2005); California (2006); Colorado (1997); Kansas (no executions since the death penalty was reinstated in 1976); Montana (2006); Nevada (2006); New Hampshire (no executions since the death penalty was reinstated in 1976); North Carolina (2006); Oregon (1997); Pennsylvania (1999); and Wyoming (1992). DPIC, Executions by State and Year, online at http://www.deathpenaltyinfo.org/node/5741.

\n

Accordingly, 30 States have either formally abolished the death penalty or have not conducted an execution in more than eight years. Of the 20 States that have conducted at least one execution in the past eight years, 9 have conducted fewer than five in that time, making an execution in those States a fairly rare event. BJS Prisoner Statistics (Delaware, Idaho, Indiana, Kentucky, Louisiana, South Dakota, Tennessee, Utah, Washington). That leaves 11 States in which it is fair to say that capital punishment is not \xe2\x80\x9cunusual.\xe2\x80\x9d And just three of those States (Texas, Missouri, and Florida) accounted for 80% of the executions nationwide (28 of the 35) in 2014. See DPIC, Number of Executions by State and Region Since 1976, online at http://www.deathpenaltyinfo.org/number-executions-state-and-region-1976. Indeed, last year, only seven States conducted an execution. DPIC, Executions by State and Year, supra; DPIC, Death Sentences in the United States From 1977 by State and by Year, online at http\xc2\xa0:\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0www\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0deathpenaltyinfo\xc2\xa0.org\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0death\xc2\xa0-\xc2\xa0sentences\xc2\xa0-\xc2\xa0united\xc2\xa0-states-1977-2008. In other words, in 43 States, no one was executed.

\n

In terms of population, if we ask how many Americans live in a State that at least occasionally carries out an execution (at least one within the prior three years), the answer two decades ago was 60% or 70%. Today, that number is 33%. See Appendix C, infra.

\n

At the same time, use of the death penalty has become increasingly concentrated geographically. County-by-county figures are relevant, for decisions to impose the death penalty typically take place at a county level. See supra, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313. County-level sentencing figures show that, between 1973 and 1997, 66 of America\xe2\x80\x99s 3,143 counties accounted for approximately 50% of all death sentences imposed. Liebman & Clarke 264\xe2\x80\x93265; cf. id., at 266. (counties with 10% of the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s population imposed 43% of its death sentences). By the early 2000\xe2\x80\x99s, the death penalty was only actively practiced in a very small number of counties: between 2004 and 2009, only 35 counties imposed 5 or more death sentences, i.e., approximately one per year. See Appendix D, infra (such counties colored in red) (citing Ford, The Death Penalty\xe2\x80\x99s Last Stand, The Atlantic, Apr. 21, 2015). And more recent data show that the practice has diminished yet further: between 2010 and 2015 (as of June 22), only 15 counties imposed five or more death sentences. See Appendix E, infra. In short, the number of active death penalty counties is small and getting smaller. And the overall statistics on county-level executions bear this out. Between 1976 and 2007, there were no executions in 86% of America\xe2\x80\x99s counties. Liebman & Clarke 265\xe2\x80\x93266, and n.\xc2\xa047; cf. ibid. (counties with less than 5% of the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s population carried out over half of its executions from 1976\xe2\x80\x932007).

\n

In sum, if we look to States, in more than 60% there is effectively no death penalty, in an additional 18% an execution is rare and unusual, and 6%, i.e., three States, account for 80% of all executions. If we look to population, about 66% of the Nation lives in a State that has not carried out an execution in the last three years. And if we look to counties, in 86% there is effectively no death pen-alty. It seems fair to say that it is now unusual to find capital punishment in the United States, at least when we consider the Nation as a whole. See Furman, 408 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 311 (1972) (White, J., concurring) (executions could be so infrequently carried out that they \xe2\x80\x9cwould cease to be a credible deterrent or measurably to contribute to any other end of punishment in the criminal justice system .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. when imposition of the penalty reaches a certain degreeof infrequency, it would be very doubtful that any exist-ing general need for retribution would be measurably satisfied\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Moreover, we have said that it \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98is not so much the number of these States that is significant, but the consistency of the direction of change.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Roper, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 566 (quoting Atkins, supra, at 315) (finding significant that five States had abandoned the death penalty for juveniles, four legislatively and one judicially, since the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Stanford v. Kentucky,\n\n492 U.\xc2\xa0S. 361 (1989)\n). Judged in that way, capital punishment has indeed become unusual. Seven States have abolished the death penalty in the last decade, including (quite recently) Nebraska. DPIC, States With and Without the Death Penalty, supra. And several States have come within a single vote of eliminating the death penalty. Seelye, Measure to Repeal Death Penalty Fails by a Single Vote in New Hampshire Senate, N.\xc2\xa0Y. Times, Apr. 17, 2014, p. A12; Dennison, House Deadlocks on Bill To Abolish Death Penalty in Montana, Billings Gazette, Feb. 23, 2015; see also Offredo, Delaware Senate Passes Death Penalty Repeal Bill, Delaware News Journal, Apr. 3, 2015. Eleven States, as noted earlier, have not executed anyone in eight years. Supra, at 34\xe2\x80\x9335. And several States have formally stopped executing inmates. See Yardley, Oregon\xe2\x80\x99s Governor Says He Will Not Allow Executions, N.\xc2\xa0Y. Times, Nov. 23, 2011, p. A14 (Oregon); Governor of Colorado, Exec. Order No. D2013\xe2\x80\x93006, May 22, 2013 (Colorado); Lovett, Executions Are Suspended by Governor in Washington, N.\xc2\xa0Y. Times, Feb. 12, 2014, p. A12 (Washington); Begley, Pennsylvania Stops Using the Death Penalty, Time, Feb. 13, 2015 (Pennsylvania); see also Welsh-Huggins, Associated Press, Ohio Executions Rescheduled, Jan. 30, 2015 (Ohio).

\n

Moreover, the direction of change is consistent. In the past two decades, no State without a death penalty has passed legislation to reinstate the penalty. See Atkins, supra, at 315\xe2\x80\x93316; DPIC, States With and Without the Death Penalty, supra. Indeed, even in many States most associated with the death penalty, remarkable shifts have occurred. In Texas, the State that carries out the most executions, the number of executions fell from 40 in 2000 to 10 in 2014, and the number of death sentences fell from 48 in 1999 to 9 in 2013 (and 0 thus far in 2015). DPIC, Executions by State and Year, supra; BJS, T. \n

I recognize a strong counterargument that favors constitutionality. We are a court. Why should we not leave the matter up to the people acting democratically through legislatures? The Constitution foresees a country that will make most important decisions democratically. Most nations that have abandoned the death penalty have done so through legislation, not judicial decision. And legislators, unlike judges, are free to take account of matters such as monetary costs, which I do not claim are relevant here. See, e.g., Berman, Nebraska Lawmakers Abolish the Death Penalty, Narrowly Overriding Governor\xe2\x80\x99s Veto, Washington Post Blog, Post Nation, May 27, 2015) (listing cost as one of the reasons why Nebraska legislators re-cently repealed the death penalty in that State); cf. California Commission on the Fair Administration of Justice, Report and Recommendations on the Administration of the Death Penalty in California 117 (June 30, 2008) (death penalty costs California $137 million per year; a comparable system of life imprisonment without parole would cost $11.5 million per year), online at http://www.ccfaj.org/rr-dp-official.html; D\xc3\xa1te, The High Price of Killing Killers, Palm Beach Post, Jan. 4, 2000, p. 1A (cost of each execution is $23 million above cost of life imprisonment without parole in Florida).

\n

The answer is that the matters I have discussed, such as lack of reliability, the arbitrary application of a serious and irreversible punishment, individual suffering caused by long delays, and lack of penological purpose are quintessentially judicial matters. They concern the infliction\xe2\x80\x94indeed the unfair, cruel, and unusual infliction\xe2\x80\x94of a serious punishment upon an individual. I recognize that in 1972 this Court, in a sense, turned to Congress and the state legislatures in its search for standards that would increase the fairness and reliability of imposing a death penalty. The legislatures responded. But, in the last four decades, considerable evidence has accumulated that those responses have not worked.

\n

Thus we are left with a judicial responsibility. The\n \nEighth Amendment sets forth the relevant law, and we must interpret that law. See Marbury v. Madison, 1 Cranch 137, 177 (1803); Hall, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 19) (\xe2\x80\x9cThat exercise of independent judgment is the Court\xe2\x80\x99s judicial duty\xe2\x80\x9d). We have made clear that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the Constitution contemplates that in the end our own judgment will be brought to bear on the question of the acceptability of the death penalty under the\n \nEighth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 19) (quoting Coker v. Georgia,\n\n433 U.\xc2\xa0S. 584,\n597 (1977)\n (plurality opinion)); see also Thompson v. Oklahoma,\n\n487 U.\xc2\xa0S. 815,\n833, n.\n40 (1988)\n (plurality opinion).

\n

For the reasons I have set forth in this opinion, I believe it highly likely that the death penalty violates the\n \nEighth Amendment. At the very least, the Court should call for full briefing on the basic question.

\n

With respect, I dissent.
