\nCITY OF SHERRILL V. ONEIDA INDIAN NATION OF N.&NBSP;Y.
\n
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\n
\n

CITY OF SHERRILL, NEW YORK v. ONEIDA INDIAN NATION OF NEW YORK et al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the second circuit

\n

No. 03\xe2\x80\x93855.Argued January 11, 2005\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 29, 2005

\n

Respondent Oneida Indian Nation of New York (OIN or Tribe) is a direct descendant of the Oneida Indian Nation (Oneida Nation), whose aboriginal homeland, at the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s birth, comprised some six million acres in what is now central New York State (State). See, e.g., Oneida Indian Nation of N.\xc2\xa0Y. v. County of Oneida, 414 U. S. 661, 664 (Oneida I). In 1788, the State and the Oneida Nation entered into a treaty whereby the Oneidas ceded all their lands to the State, but retained a reservation of about 300,000 acres for their own use. See County of Oneida v. Oneida Indian Nation of N. Y., 470 U. S. 226, 231 (Oneida II). The Federal Government initially pursued a policy protective of the New York Indians. In 1790, Congress passed the first Indian Trade and Intercourse Act (Nonintercourse Act), barring sales of tribal land without the Government\xe2\x80\x99s acquiescence. And in the 1794 Treaty of Canandaigua, the United States \xe2\x80\x9cacknowledge[d]\xe2\x80\x9d the Oneidas\xe2\x80\x99 300,000-acre reservation and guaranteed their \xe2\x80\x9cfree use and enjoyment\xe2\x80\x9d of the reserved territory. Act of Nov. 11, 1794, 7 Stat. 44, 45, Art.\xc2\xa0III. Nevertheless, New York continued to purchase reservation land from the Oneidas. Although the Washington administration objected, later administrations made not even a pretense of interfering with New York\xe2\x80\x99s purchases, and ultimately pursued a policy designed to open reservation lands to white settlers and to remove tribes westward. Pressured by the removal policy, many Oneidas left the State. Those who stayed continued to diminish in number and, during the 1840\xe2\x80\x99s, sold most of their remaining lands to New York. By 1920, the New York Oneidas retained only 32 acres in the State.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although early litigation over Oneida land claims trained on monetary recompense from the United States for past deprivations, the Oneidas ultimately shifted to suits against local governments. In 1970, they filed a federal \xe2\x80\x9ctest case\xe2\x80\x9d against two New York counties, alleging that the cession of 100,000 acres to the State in 1795 violated the Nonintercourse Act and thus did not terminate the Oneidas\xe2\x80\x99 right to possession. They sought damages measured by the fair rental value, for the years 1968 and 1969, of 872 acres of their ancestral land owned and occupied by the two counties. The District Court, affirmed by the Court of Appeals, dismissed the complaint for failure to state a federal claim. This Court reversed in Oneida I, 414 U. S., at 675, 682, holding that federal jurisdiction was properly invoked. After the Oneidas prevailed in the lower courts, this Court held, inter alia, that the Oneidas could maintain their claim to be compensated \xe2\x80\x9cfor violation of their possessory rights based on federal common law,\xe2\x80\x9d Oneida II, 470 U. S., at 236, but reserved \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he question whether equitable considerations should limit the relief available to present day Oneida Indians,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 253, n.\xc2\xa027.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1997 and 1998, OIN purchased separate parcels of land in petitioner city of Sherrill, New York. These properties, once contained within the historic Oneida Reservation, were last possessed by the Oneidas as a tribal entity in 1805. In that year, the Oneida Nation transferred the parcels to one of its members, who sold the land to a non-Indian in 1807. The properties thereafter remained in non-Indian hands until OIN reacquired them in open-market transactions. For two centuries, governance of the area in which the properties are located has been provided by the State and its county and municipal units. According to the 2000 census, over 99% of the area\xe2\x80\x99s present-day population is non-Indian. Nevertheless, because the parcels lie within the boundaries of the reservation originally occupied by the Oneidas, OIN maintained that the properties are tax exempt and accordingly refused to pay property taxes assessed by Sherrill. Sherrill initiated state-court eviction proceedings, and OIN brought this federal-court suit. In contrast to Oneida I and II, which involved demands for monetary compensation, OIN sought equitable relief prohibiting, currently and in the future, the imposition of property taxes. The District Court concluded that the parcels are not taxable, and the Second Circuit affirmed. In this Court, OIN resists the payment of the property taxes on the ground that OIN\xe2\x80\x99s acquisition of fee title to discrete parcels of historic reservation land revived the Oneidas\xe2\x80\x99 ancient sovereignty piecemeal over each parcel, so that regulatory authority over the newly purchased properties no longer resides in Sherrill.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Given the longstanding, distinctly non-Indian character of central New York and its inhabitants, the regulatory authority over the area constantly exercised by the State and its counties and towns for 200 years, and the Oneidas\xe2\x80\x99 long delay in seeking judicial relief against parties other than the United States, standards of federal Indian law and federal equity practice preclude the Tribe from unilaterally reviving its ancient sovereignty, in whole or in part, over the parcels at issue. The Oneidas long ago relinquished governmental reins and cannot regain them through open-market purchases from current titleholders. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9321.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The Court rejects the theory of OIN and the United States that, because Oneida II recognized the Oneidas\xe2\x80\x99 aboriginal title to their ancient reservation land and because the Tribe has now acquired the specific parcels at issue in the open market, it has unified fee and aboriginal title and may now assert sovereign dominion over the parcels. The Oneidas sought only money damages in Oneida II, see 470 U. S., at 229, and the Court reserved the question whether \xe2\x80\x9cequitable considerations\xe2\x80\x9d should limit the relief available to the present-day Oneidas, id., at 253, n.\xc2\xa027. Substantive questions of rights and duties are very different from remedial questions. Here, OIN seeks declaratory and injunctive relief recognizing its present and future sovereign immunity from local taxation on parcels the Tribe purchased in the open market, properties that had been subject to state and local taxation for generations. The appropriateness of such relief must be evaluated in light of the long history of state sovereign control over the territory. From the early 1800\xe2\x80\x99s into the 1970\xe2\x80\x99s, the United States largely accepted, or was indifferent to, New York\xe2\x80\x99s governance of the land in question and the validity vel non of the Oneidas\xe2\x80\x99 sales to the State. Moreover, the properties here involved have greatly increased in value since the Oneidas sold them 200 years ago. The longstanding assumption of jurisdiction by the State over an area that is predominantly non-Indian in population and land use creates \xe2\x80\x9cjustifiable expectations.\xe2\x80\x9d E.g., Rosebud Sioux Tribe v. Kneip, 430 U. S. 584, 604\xe2\x80\x93605. Similar justifiable expectations, grounded in two centuries of New York\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of regulatory jurisdiction, until recently uncontested by OIN, merit heavy weight here. The wrongs of which OIN complains occurred during the early years of the Republic, whereas, for the past two centuries, New York and its local units have continuously governed the territory. The Oneidas did not seek to regain possession of their aboriginal lands by court decree until the 1970\xe2\x80\x99s. And not until the 1990\xe2\x80\x99s did OIN acquire the properties in question and assert its unification theory to ground its demand for exemption of the parcels from local taxation. This long lapse of time, during which the Oneidas did not seek to revive their sovereign control through equitable relief in court, and the attendant dramatic changes in the character of the properties, preclude OIN from gaining the disruptive remedy it now seeks. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The distance from 1805 to the present day, the Oneidas\xe2\x80\x99 long delay in seeking equitable relief against New York or its local units, and developments in Sherrill spanning several generations, evoke the doctrines of laches, acquiescence, and impossibility, and render inequitable the piecemeal shift in governance this suit seeks unilaterally to initiate. This Court has long recognized that the passage of time can preclude relief. For example, the doctrine of laches focuses on one side\xe2\x80\x99s inaction and the other\xe2\x80\x99s legitimate reliance to bar long-dormant claims for equitable relief. See, e.g., Badger v. Badger, 2 Wall. 87, 94. Moreover, long acquiescence may have controlling effect on the exercise of States\xe2\x80\x99 dominion and sovereignty over territory. E.g., Ohio v. Kentucky, 410 U. S. 641, 651. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s original-jurisdiction state-sovereignty cases do not dictate a result here, but they provide a helpful point of reference: When a party belatedly asserts a right to present and future sovereign control over territory, longstanding observances and settled expectations are prime considerations. It has been two centuries since the Oneidas last exercised regulatory control over the properties here or held them free from local taxation. Parcel-by-parcel revival of their sovereign status, given the extraordinary passage of time, would dishonor \xe2\x80\x9cthe historic wisdom in the value of repose.\xe2\x80\x9d Oneida II, 470 U. S., at 262. Finally, this Court has recognized the impracticability of returning to Indian control land that generations earlier passed into numerous private hands. See, e.g., Yankton Sioux Tribe v. United States, 272 U. S. 351, 357. The unilateral reestablishment of present and future Indian sovereign control, even over land purchased at the market price, would have disruptive practical consequences similar to those that led the Yankton Sioux Court to initiate the impossibility doctrine: Sherrill and the surrounding area are today overwhelmingly populated by non-Indians, and a checkerboard of state and tribal jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x94created unilaterally at OIN\xe2\x80\x99s behest\xe2\x80\x94would \xe2\x80\x9cseriously burde[n] the administration of state and local governments\xe2\x80\x9d and would adversely affect landowners neighboring the tribal patches. Hagen v. Utah, 510 U. S. 399, 421. If OIN may unilaterally reassert sovereign control and remove these parcels from the local tax rolls, little would prevent it from initiating a new generation of litigation to free the parcels from local zoning or other regulatory controls that protect all landowners in the area. See Felix, 145 U. S., at 335. Recognizing these practical concerns, Congress has provided, in 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7465, a mechanism for the acquisition of lands for tribal communities that takes account of the interests of others with stakes in the area\xe2\x80\x99s governance and well being. Section 465 provides the proper avenue for OIN to reestablish sovereign authority over territory last held by the Oneidas 200 years ago. Pp.\xc2\xa016\xe2\x80\x9321.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0The question of damages for the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s ancient dispossession, resolved in Oneida II, is not at issue here, and the Court leaves undisturbed its Oneida II holding. P.\xc2\xa021.

\n

337 F.\xc2\xa03d 139, reversed and remanded.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case concerns properties in the city of Sherrill, New York, purchased by the Oneida Indian Nation of New York (OIN or Tribe) in 1997 and 1998. The separate parcels of land in question, once contained within the Oneidas\xe2\x80\x99 300,000-acre reservation, were last possessed by the Oneidas as a tribal entity in 1805. For two centuries, governance of the area in which the properties are located has been provided by the State of New York and its county and municipal units. In County of Oneida v. Oneida Indian Nation of N.\xc2\xa0Y., 470 U. S. 226 (1985) (Oneida II), this Court held that the Oneidas stated a triable claim for damages against the County of Oneida for wrongful possession of lands they conveyed to New York State in 1795 in violation of federal law. In the instant action, OIN resists the payment of property taxes to Sherrill on the ground that OIN\xe2\x80\x99s acquisition of fee title to discrete parcels of historic reservation land revived the Oneidas\xe2\x80\x99 ancient sovereignty piecemeal over each parcel. Consequently, the Tribe maintains, regulatory authority over OIN\xe2\x80\x99s newly purchased properties no longer resides in Sherrill.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our 1985 decision recognized that the Oneidas could maintain a federal common-law claim for damages for ancient wrongdoing in which both national and state governments were complicit. Today, we decline to project redress for the Tribe into the present and future, thereby disrupting the governance of central New York\xe2\x80\x99s counties and towns. Generations have passed during which non-Indians have owned and developed the area that once composed the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s historic reservation. And at least since the middle years of the 19th century, most of the Oneidas have resided elsewhere. Given the longstanding, distinctly non-Indian character of the area and its inhabitants, the regulatory authority constantly exercised by New York State and its counties and towns, and the Oneidas\xe2\x80\x99 long delay in seeking judicial relief against parties other than the United States, we hold that the Tribe cannot unilaterally revive its ancient sovereignty, in whole or in part, over the parcels at issue. The Oneidas long ago relinquished the reins of government and cannot regain them through open-market purchases from current titleholders.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0OIN is a federally recognized Indian Tribe and a direct descendant of the Oneida Indian Nation (Oneida Nation), \xe2\x80\x9cone of the six nations of the Iroquois, the most powerful Indian Tribe in the Northeast at the time of the American Revolution.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 230. At the birth of the United States, the Oneida Nation\xe2\x80\x99s aboriginal homeland comprised some six million acres in what is now central New York. Ibid.; Oneida Indian Nation of N.\xc2\xa0Y. v. County of Oneida, 414 U. S. 661, 664 (1974) (Oneida I).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the years after the Revolutionary War, \xe2\x80\x9cthe State of New York came under increasingly heavy pressure to open the Oneidas\xe2\x80\x99 land for settlement.\xe2\x80\x9d Oneida II, 470 U. S., at 231. Reflective of that pressure, in 1788, New York State and the Oneida Nation entered into the Treaty of Fort Schuyler. For payments in money and kind, the Oneidas ceded to New York \xe2\x80\x9call their lands.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. A136. Of the vast area conveyed, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Oneidas retained a reservation of about 300,000 acres,\xe2\x80\x9d Oneida II, 470 U. S., at 231, \xe2\x80\x9cfor their own use and cultivation,\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. A137 (internal quotation marks omitted).[Footnote 1] OIN does not here contest the legitimacy of the Fort Schuyler conveyance or the boundaries of the reserved area.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Federal Government initially pursued a policy protective of the New York Indians, undertaking to secure the Tribes\xe2\x80\x99 rights to reserved lands. See Oneida II, 470 U. S., at 231\xe2\x80\x93232; Oneida I, 414 U. S., at 667; F. Cohen, Handbook of Federal Indian Law 418\xe2\x80\x93419 (1942 ed.); F. Cohen, Handbook of Federal Indian Law 73\xe2\x80\x9374 (1982 ed.) (hereinafter Handbook). In 1790, Congress passed the first Indian Trade and Intercourse Act, commonly known as the Nonintercourse Act. Act of July 22, 1790, ch.\xc2\xa033, 1 Stat. 137. Periodically renewed, see Oneida I, 414 U. S., at 667\xe2\x80\x93668, and n.\xc2\xa04, and remaining substantially in force today, see Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa72116, 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7177, the Act bars sales of tribal land without the acquiescence of the Federal Government.[Footnote 2] In 1794, in further pursuit of its protective policy, the United States entered into the Treaty of Canandaigua with the Six (Iroquois) Nations. Act of Nov. 11, 1794, 7 Stat. 44. That treaty both \xe2\x80\x9cacknowledge[d]\xe2\x80\x9d the Oneida Reservation as established by the Treaty of Fort Schuyler and guaranteed the Oneidas\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cfree use and enjoyment\xe2\x80\x9d of the reserved territory. Id., at 45, Art. II. The Oneidas in turn agreed they would \xe2\x80\x9cnever claim any other lands within the boundaries of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 45, Art. IV.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0New York State nonetheless continued to purchase reservation land from the Oneidas. The Washington administration objected to New York\xe2\x80\x99s 1795 negotiations to buy 100,000 acres of the Oneidas\xe2\x80\x99 Reservation without federal supervision. Oneida II, 470 U. S., at 229, 232. Later administrations, however, \xe2\x80\x9c[made not] even a pretense of interfer[ing] with [the] State\xe2\x80\x99s attempts to negotiate treaties [with the Oneidas] for land cessions.\xe2\x80\x9d Oneida Nation of N.\xc2\xa0Y. v. United States, 43 Ind. Cl. Comm\xe2\x80\x99n 373, 385 (1978); see also id., at 390; Campisi, The Oneida Treaty Period, 1783\xe2\x80\x931838, in The Oneida Indian Experience: Two Perspectives 48, 59 (J. Campisi & L. Hauptman eds. 1988) (hereinafter Campisi). See generally Gunther 6 (\xe2\x80\x9cNew York acquired much land from Indians through treaties\xe2\x80\x94perhaps as many as 200\xe2\x80\x94not participated in, though apparently known and not objected to, by the national government.\xe2\x80\x9d (footnote omitted)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Federal Government\xe2\x80\x99s policy soon veered away from protection of New York and other east coast reservations. In lieu of the commitment made in the Treaty of Canandaigua, the United States pursued a policy designed to open reservation lands to white settlers and to remove tribes westward. D. Getches, C. Wilkinson, & R. Williams, Cases and Materials on Federal Indian Law 94 (4th ed. 1998) (After the Louisiana Purchase in 1803, federal policymakers \xe2\x80\x9cbegan to debate the tactics of inducing [eastern Indians] to exchange their remaining ancestral lands for a permanent territory in the West.\xe2\x80\x9d). As recounted by the Indian Claims Commission in 1978, early 19th-century federal Indian agents in New York State did not simply fail to check New York\xe2\x80\x99s land purchases, they \xe2\x80\x9ctook an active role \xe2\x80\xa6 in encouraging the removal of the Oneidas \xe2\x80\xa6 to the west.\xe2\x80\x9d Oneida Nation of N.\xc2\xa0Y., 43 Ind. Cl. Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, at 390; see id., at 391 (noting that some federal agents were \xe2\x80\x9cdeeply involved\xe2\x80\x9d in \xe2\x80\x9cplans \xe2\x80\xa6 to bring about removal of the [Oneidas]\xe2\x80\x9d and in the State\xe2\x80\x99s acquisition of Oneida land). Beginning in 1817, the Federal Government accelerated its efforts to remove Indian tribes from their east coast homelands. Handbook 78\xe2\x80\x9379, and n.\xc2\xa0142.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Pressured by the removal policy to leave their ancestral lands in New York, some 150 Oneidas, by 1825, had moved to Wisconsin. Horsman, The Wisconsin Oneidas in the Preallotment Years, in The Oneida Indian Experience, supra, at 65, 67. In 1838, the Oneidas and the United States entered into the Treaty of Buffalo Creek, which envisioned removal of all remaining New York Indians, including the Oneidas, to Kansas. Act of Jan. 15, 1838, 7 Stat. 550. By this time, the Oneidas had sold all but 5,000 acres of their original reservation. 337 F.\xc2\xa03d 139, 149 (CA2 2003). Six hundred of their members resided in Wisconsin, while 620 remained in New York State. 7 Stat. 556 (Sched. A).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Article 13 of the Buffalo Creek Treaty, the Oneidas agreed to remove to the Kansas lands the United States had set aside for them \xe2\x80\x9cas soon as they c[ould] make satisfactory arrangements\xe2\x80\x9d for New York State\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cpurchase of their lands at Oneida.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 554. As a condition of the treaty\xe2\x80\x99s ratification, the Senate directed that a federal commissioner \xe2\x80\x9cfully and fairly explai[n]\xe2\x80\x9d the terms to each signatory tribe and band. New York Indians v. United States, 170 U. S. 1, 21\xe2\x80\x9322 (1898). Commissioner Ransom H. Gillet, who had originally negotiated the treaty terms with the Oneidas, met with them again and assured them they would not be forced to move but could remain on \xe2\x80\x9ctheir lands where they reside,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., they could \xe2\x80\x9cif they ch[ose] to do so remain where they are forever.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 146 (emphases added).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Oneidas who stayed on in New York after the proclamation of the Buffalo Creek Treaty continued to diminish in number and, during the 1840\xe2\x80\x99s, sold most of their remaining lands to the State. New York Indians v. United States, 40 Ct. Cl. 448, 458, 469\xe2\x80\x93471 (1905). A few hundred Oneidas moved to Canada in 1842, id., at 458, and \xe2\x80\x9cby the mid-1840s, only about 200 Oneidas remained in New York State,\xe2\x80\x9d Introduction to Part I, The Oneida Indian Journey: From New York to Wisconsin, 1784\xe2\x80\x931860, pp.\xc2\xa09, 13 (L. Hauptman & L. McLester eds. 1999). By 1843, the New York Oneidas retained less than 1,000 acres in the State. Campisi 61. That acreage dwindled to 350 in 1890; ultimately, by 1920, only 32 acres continued to be held by the Oneidas. Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The United States eventually abandoned its efforts to remove the New York Indians to Kansas. In 1860, the Federal Government restored the Kansas lands to the public domain, and sold them thereafter. New York Indians, 170 U. S., at 24, 28\xe2\x80\x9329, 31.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Early litigation concerning the Oneidas\xe2\x80\x99 land claims trained on monetary recompense from the United States for past deprivations. In 1893, the United States agreed to be sued for disposing of the Kansas lands to settlers, and the Oneidas in New York shared in the resulting award of damages. See New York Indians, 170 U. S. 1; New York Indians, 40 Ct. Cl. 448 (identifying the Tribes qualified to share in the distribution of the sum recovered).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Seeking further compensation from the United States a half century later, the New York and Wisconsin Oneidas initiated proceedings before the Indian Claims Commission in 1951. Oneida Indian Nation of N.\xc2\xa0Y. v. County of Oneida, 622 F.\xc2\xa02d 624, 626 (CA2 1980). They sought redress for lands New York had acquired through 25 treaties of cession concluded between 1795 and 1846. The Oneidas alleged, and the Claims Commission agreed, that under the Nonintercourse Act of 1790 and successor statutes, the Federal Government had a fiduciary duty to assure that the Oneidas received from New York \xe2\x80\x9cconscionable consideration\xe2\x80\x9d for the lands in question. Oneida Nation of N.\xc2\xa0Y. v. United States, 26 Ind. Cl. Comm\xe2\x80\x99n 138, 145 (1971). The Court of Claims affirmed the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s core determination, but held that the United States\xe2\x80\x99 duty extended only to land transactions of which the Government had knowledge. United States v. Oneida Nation of N.\xc2\xa0Y., 201 Ct. Cl. 546, 554, 477 F.\xc2\xa02d 939, 944 (1973). Accordingly, the Court of Claims directed the Commission to determine whether the Government actually or constructively knew of the land transactions at issue. Id., at 555, 477 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 945.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On remand, the Commission found that the Federal Government had actual or constructive knowledge of all of the treaties and would be liable if the Oneidas had not received conscionable consideration. Oneida Nation of N.\xc2\xa0Y., 43 Ind. Cl. Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, at 375, 406\xe2\x80\x93407. The Commission anticipated further proceedings to determine the Federal Government\xe2\x80\x99s ultimate liability, but the Oneidas had by then decided to pursue a different course. On the Oneidas\xe2\x80\x99 request, the Court of Claims dismissed the proceedings. See Oneida Nation of N.\xc2\xa0Y. v. United States, 231 Ct. Cl. 990, 991 (1982) (per curiam).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In lieu of concentrating on recovery from the United States, the Oneidas pursued suits against local governments. In 1970, the Oneidas of New York and Wisconsin, asserting federal-question jurisdiction under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71331 or \xc2\xa71362, instituted a \xe2\x80\x9ctest case\xe2\x80\x9d against the New York Counties of Oneida and Madison. They alleged that the cession of 100,000 acres to New York State in 1795, see supra, at 4, violated the Nonintercourse Act and thus did not terminate the Oneidas\xe2\x80\x99 right to possession under the applicable federal treaties and statutes. In this initial endeavor to gain compensation from governmental units other than the United States, the Oneidas confined their demand for relief. They sought only damages measured by the fair rental value, for the years 1968 and 1969, of 872 acres of their ancestral land owned and occupied by the two counties. The District Court, affirmed by the Court of Appeals, dismissed the Oneidas\xe2\x80\x99 complaint for failure to state a claim arising under federal law. We reversed that determination, holding that federal jurisdiction was properly invoked. Oneida I, 414 U. S., at 675, 682.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the next round, the Oneidas prevailed in the lower courts. On review in Oneida II, we rejected various defenses the counties presented that might have barred the action for damages, 470 U. S., at 240\xe2\x80\x93250, and held that the Oneidas could maintain their claim to be compensated \xe2\x80\x9cfor violation of their possessory rights based on federal common law,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 236. While upholding the judgment of the Court of Appeals regarding the counties\xe2\x80\x99 liability under federal common law, we noted that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he question whether equitable considerations should limit the relief available to the present day Oneida Indians was not addressed by the Court of Appeals or presented to this Court.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 253, n.\xc2\xa027. Accordingly, \xe2\x80\x9cwe express[ed] no opinion as to whether other considerations m[ight] be relevant to the final disposition of this case.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. On remand, the District Court entered a final judgment which fixed the amount of damages payable by the counties. Allowing setoffs for the counties\xe2\x80\x99 good-faith improvements to the land, the court ordered recoveries of $15,994 from Oneida County and $18,970 from Madison County, plus prejudgment interest. Oneida Indian Nation of N.\xc2\xa0Y. v. County of Oneida, 217 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 292, 310 (NDNY 2002).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 2000, litigation resumed in an action held in abeyance during the pendency of the test case. In that revitalized action, the Oneidas sought damages from Oneida and Madison Counties for a period spanning over 200 years. The amended complaint alleged that, through a series of agreements concluded during the years 1795 to 1846, approximately 250,000 acres of the Oneidas\xe2\x80\x99 ancestral land had been unlawfully conveyed to New York. Oneida Indian Nation of N.\xc2\xa0Y. v. County of Oneida, 199 F.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0D. 61, 66\xe2\x80\x9368 (NDNY 2000).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Oneidas further sought to enlarge the action by demanding recovery of land they had not occupied since the 1795\xe2\x80\x931846 conveyances.[Footnote 3] They attempted to join as defendants, inter alia, approximately 20,000 private landowners, and to obtain declaratory relief that would allow the Oneidas to eject these landowners. Id., at 67\xe2\x80\x9368.[Footnote 4] The District Court refused permission to join the landowners so late in the day, resting in part on the Oneidas\xe2\x80\x99 bad faith and undue delay. Id., at 79\xe2\x80\x9385. Further, the court found the proposed amendment \xe2\x80\x9cfutile.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 94. In this regard, the court emphasized the \xe2\x80\x9csharp distinction between the existence of a federal common law right to Indian homelands,\xe2\x80\x9d a right this Court recognized in Oneida II, \xe2\x80\x9cand how to vindicate that right.\xe2\x80\x9d 199 F.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0D., at 90. That distinction \xe2\x80\x9cmust be drawn,\xe2\x80\x9d the court stated, ibid., for in the two centuries since the alleged wrong, \xe2\x80\x9cdevelopment of every type imaginable has been ongoing,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 92. Referring to the \xe2\x80\x9cpractical concerns\xe2\x80\x9d that blocked restoration of Indians to their former lands, the court found it high time \xe2\x80\x9cto transcend the theoretical.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Cases of this genre, the court observed, \xe2\x80\x9ccr[ied] out for a pragmatic approach.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The District Court therefore excluded the imposition of any liability against private landowners. Id., at 93\xe2\x80\x9395.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This brings us to the present case, which concerns parcels of land in the city of Sherrill, located in Oneida County, New York. According to the 2000 census, over 99% of the population in the area is non-Indian: American Indians represent less than 1% of the city of Sherrill\xe2\x80\x99s population and less than 0.5% of Oneida County\xe2\x80\x99s population. U. S. Dept. of Commerce, Census Bureau, 2000 Census of Population and Housing, Summary Population and Housing Characteristics: New York, 2000 PHC\xe2\x80\x931\xe2\x80\x9334, Table 3, p. 124 (July 2002), available at http://\nwww.census.gov/prod/cen2000/phc-1-34.pdf (as visited Mar. 24, 2005, and available in the Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file). OIN owns approximately 17,000 acres of land scattered throughout the Counties of Oneida and Madison, representing less than 1.5% of the counties\xe2\x80\x99 total area. OIN\xe2\x80\x99s predecessor, the Oneida Nation, had transferred the parcels at issue to one of its members in 1805, who sold the land to a non-Indian in 1807. The properties thereafter remained in non-Indian hands until OIN\xe2\x80\x99s acquisitions in 1997 and 1998 in open-market transactions. See 337 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 144, n.\xc2\xa03. OIN now operates commercial enterprises on these parcels: a gasoline station, a convenience store, and a textile facility. Id., at 144.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because the parcels lie within the boundaries of the reservation originally occupied by the Oneidas, OIN maintained that the properties are exempt from taxation, and accordingly refused to pay the assessed property taxes. The city of Sherrill initiated eviction proceedings in state court, and OIN sued Sherrill in federal court. In contrast to Oneida I and II, which involved demands for monetary compensation, OIN sought equitable relief prohibiting, currently and in the future, the imposition of property taxes. OIN also sued Madison County, seeking a declaration that the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s properties in Madison are tax exempt. The litigation involved a welter of claims and counterclaims. Relevant here, the District Court concluded that parcels of land owned by the Tribe in Sherrill and Madison are not taxable. See 145 F. Supp. 2d 226, 254\xe2\x80\x93259 (NDNY 2001).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A divided panel of the Second Circuit affirmed. 337 F.\xc2\xa03d 139. Writing for the majority, Judge Parker ruled that the parcels qualify as \xe2\x80\x9cIndian country,\xe2\x80\x9d as that term is defined in 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71151,[Footnote 5] because they fall within the boundaries of a reservation set aside by the 1794 Canandaigua Treaty for Indian use under federal supervision. 337 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 155\xe2\x80\x93156; see supra, at 4. The court further held that the Buffalo Creek Treaty did not demonstrate a clear congressional purpose to disestablish or diminish the Oneida Reservation. 337 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 161, 165; see supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x936. Finally, the court found no legal requirement \xe2\x80\x9cthat a federally recognized tribe demonstrate its continuous existence in order to assert a claim to its reservation land.\xe2\x80\x9d 337 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 165. In any case, the court held, the record demonstrated OIN\xe2\x80\x99s continuous tribal existence. Id., at 166\xe2\x80\x93167. Judge Van Graafeiland dissented as to the majority\xe2\x80\x99s primary holding. In his view, the record raised a substantial question whether OIN had \xe2\x80\x9cforfeited\xe2\x80\x9d its aboriginal rights to the land because it abandoned \xe2\x80\x9cits tribal existence \xe2\x80\xa6 for a discernable period of time.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 171.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted the city of Sherrill\xe2\x80\x99s petition for a writ of certiorari, 542 U. S. ___ (2004), and now reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeals.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0OIN and the United States argue that because the Court in Oneida II recognized the Oneidas\xe2\x80\x99 aboriginal title to their ancient reservation land and because the Tribe has now acquired the specific parcels involved in this suit in the open market, it has unified fee and aboriginal title and may now assert sovereign dominion over the parcels. Brief for Respondents 1, 12\xe2\x80\x9319; Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 9\xe2\x80\x9310. When the Oneidas came before this Court 20 years ago in Oneida II, they sought money damages only. 470 U. S., at 229; see also id., at 244, n.\xc2\xa016 (recognizing that the suit was an \xe2\x80\x9caction at law\xe2\x80\x9d). The Court reserved for another day the question whether \xe2\x80\x9cequitable considerations\xe2\x80\x9d should limit the relief available to the present-day Oneidas. Id., at 253, n.\xc2\xa027; supra, at 9.[Footnote 6]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cThe substantive questions whether the plaintiff has any right or the defendant any duty, and if so what it is, are very different questions from the remedial questions whether this remedy or that is preferred, and what the measure of the remedy is.\xe2\x80\x9d D. Dobbs, Law of Remedies \xc2\xa71.2, p.\xc2\xa03 (1973); see also Navajo Tribe of Indians v. New Mexico, 809 F.\xc2\xa02d 1455, 1467 (CA10 1987) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe distinction between a claim or substantive right and a remedy is fundamental.\xe2\x80\x9d). \xe2\x80\x9c[S]tandards of federal Indian law and federal equity practice\xe2\x80\x9d led the District Court, in the litigation revived after Oneida II, see supra, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310, to reject OIN\xe2\x80\x99s plea for ejectment of 20,000 private landowners. Oneida Indian Nation of N.\xc2\xa0Y., 199 F.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0D., at 90 (internal quotation marks omitted); ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]here is a sharp distinction between the existence of a federal common\nlaw right to Indian homelands and how to vindicate that right \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d). In this action, OIN seeks declaratory and injunctive relief recognizing its present and future sovereign immunity from local taxation on parcels of land the Tribe purchased in the open market, properties that had been subject to state and local taxation for generations.[Footnote 7] We now reject the unification theory of OIN and the United States and hold that \xe2\x80\x9cstandards of federal Indian law and federal equity practice\xe2\x80\x9d preclude the Tribe from rekindling embers of sovereignty that long ago grew cold.[Footnote 8]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The appropriateness of the relief OIN here seeks must be evaluated in light of the long history of state sovereign control over the territory. From the early 1800\xe2\x80\x99s into the 1970\xe2\x80\x99s, the United States largely accepted, or was indifferent to, New York\xe2\x80\x99s governance of the land in question and the validity vel non of the Oneidas\xe2\x80\x99 sales to the State. See generally Gunther 23\xe2\x80\x9325 (attributing much of the confusion and conflict in the history of New York Indian affairs to \xe2\x80\x9cFederal inattention and ambivalence\xe2\x80\x9d). In fact, the United States\xe2\x80\x99 policy and practice through much of the early 19th century was designed to dislodge east coast lands from Indian possession. See supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x936. Moreover, the properties here involved have greatly increased in value since the Oneidas sold them 200 years ago. Notably, it was not until lately that the Oneidas sought to regain ancient sovereignty over land converted from wilderness to become part of cities like Sherrill. See supra, at 9\xe2\x80\x9312; Oneida II, 470 U. S., at 264\xe2\x80\x93265 (Stevens, J., dissenting in part).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court has observed in the different, but related, context of the diminishment of an Indian reservation that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he longstanding assumption of jurisdiction by the State over an area that is over 90% non-Indian, both in population and in land use,\xe2\x80\x9d may create \xe2\x80\x9cjustifiable expectations.\xe2\x80\x9d Rosebud Sioux Tribe v. Kneip, 430 U. S. 584, 604\xe2\x80\x93605 (1977); accord Hagen v. Utah, 510 U. S. 399, 421 (1994) (\xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional history\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cthe current population situation \xe2\x80\xa6 demonstrat[e] a practical acknowledgment\xe2\x80\x9d of reservation diminishment; \xe2\x80\x9ca contrary conclusion would seriously disrupt the justifiable expectations of the people living in the area\xe2\x80\x9d (some internal quotation marks omitted)).[Footnote 9] Similar justifiable expectations, grounded in two centuries of New York\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of regulatory jurisdiction, until recently uncontested by OIN, merit heavy weight here.[Footnote 10]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The wrongs of which OIN complains in this action occurred during the early years of the Republic. For the past two centuries, New York and its county and municipal units have continuously governed the territory. The Oneidas did not seek to regain possession of their aboriginal lands by court decree until the 1970\xe2\x80\x99s. See, supra, at 10, n.\xc2\xa04. And not until the 1990\xe2\x80\x99s did OIN acquire the properties in question and assert its unification theory to ground its demand for exemption of the parcels from local taxation. 337 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 144.[Footnote 11] This long lapse of time, during which the Oneidas did not seek to revive their sovereign control through equitable relief in court, and the attendant dramatic changes in the character of the properties, preclude OIN from gaining the disruptive remedy it now seeks.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The principle that the passage of time can preclude relief has deep roots in our law, and this Court has recognized this prescription in various guises. It is well established that laches, a doctrine focused on one side\xe2\x80\x99s inaction and the other\xe2\x80\x99s legitimate reliance, may bar long-dormant claims for equitable relief. See, e.g., Badger v. Badger, 2 Wall. 87, 94 (1865) (\xe2\x80\x9c[C]ourts of equity act upon their own inherent doctrine of discouraging, for the peace of society, antiquated demands, refuse to interfere where there has been gross laches in prosecuting the claim, or long acquiescence in the assertion of adverse rights.\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)); Wagner v. Baird, 7 How. 234, 258 (1849) (same); Bowman v. Wathen, 1 How. 189, 194 (1843) (\xe2\x80\x9c[The] doctrine of an equitable bar by lapse of time, so distinctly announced by the chancellors of England and Ireland, \xe2\x80\xa6 should now be regarded as settled law in this court.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court applied the doctrine of laches in Felix v. Patrick, 145 U. S. 317 (1892), to bar the heirs of an Indian from establishing a constructive trust over land their Indian ancestor had conveyed in violation of a statutory restriction. In the nearly three decades between the conveyance and the lawsuit, \xe2\x80\x9c[a] large part of the tract ha[d] been platted and recorded as an addition to the city of Omaha, and \xe2\x80\xa6 sold to purchasers.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 326. \xe2\x80\x9c[A]s the case stands at present,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court observed, \xe2\x80\x9cjustice requires only what the law \xe2\x80\xa6 would demand\xe2\x80\x94the repayment of the value of the [illegally conveyed] scrip.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 334. The Court also recognized the disproportion between the value of the scrip issued to the Indian ($150) and the value of the property the heirs sought to acquire (over $1 million). Id., at 333. The sort of changes to the value and character of the land noted by the Felix Court are present in even greater magnitude in this suit. Cf. Galliher v. Cadwell, 145 U. S. 368, 373 (1892) (\xe2\x80\x9c[L]aches is not \xe2\x80\xa6 a mere matter of time; but principally a question of the inequity of permitting the claim to be enforced\xe2\x80\x94an inequity founded upon some change in the condition or relations of the property or the parties.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As between States, long acquiescence may have controlling effect on the exercise of dominion and sovereignty over territory. Ohio v. Kentucky, 410 U. S. 641, 651 (1973) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe rule, long-settled and never doubted by this court, is that long acquiescence by one state in the possession of territory by another and in the exercise of sovereignty and dominion over it is conclusive of the latter\xe2\x80\x99s title and rightful authority.\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Michigan v. Wisconsin, 270 U. S. 295, 308 (1926))); Massachusetts v. New York, 271 U. S. 65, 95 (1926) (\xe2\x80\x9cLong acquiescence in the possession of territory and the exercise of dominion and sovereignty over it may have a controlling effect in the determination of a disputed boundary.\xe2\x80\x9d). The acquiescence doctrine does not depend on the original validity of a boundary line; rather, it attaches legal consequences to acquiescence in the observance of the boundary. California v. Nevada, 447 U. S. 125, 131 (1980) (No relationship need exist \xe2\x80\x9cbetween the origins of a boundary and the legal consequences of acquiescence in that boundary\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. Longstanding acquiescence by California and Nevada can give [the boundary lines] the force of law whether or not federal authorities had the power to draw them.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court\xe2\x80\x99s original-jurisdiction state-sovereignty cases do not dictate a result here, but they provide a helpful point of reference: When a party belatedly asserts a right to present and future sovereign control over territory,[Footnote 12] longstanding observances and settled expectations are prime considerations. There is no dispute that it has been two centuries since the Oneidas last exercised regulatory control over the properties here or held them free from local taxation. Parcel-by-parcel revival of their sovereign status, given the extraordinary passage of time, would dishonor \xe2\x80\x9cthe historic wisdom in the value of repose.\xe2\x80\x9d Oneida II, 470 U. S., at 262 (Stevens, J., dissenting in part).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, this Court has recognized the impracticability of returning to Indian control land that generations earlier passed into numerous private hands. See Yankton Sioux Tribe v. United States, 272 U. S. 351, 357 (1926) (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is impossible \xe2\x80\xa6 to rescind the cession and restore the Indians to their former rights because the lands have been opened to settlement and large portions of them are now in the possession of innumerable innocent purchasers \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d); Felix, 145 U. S., at 334 (observing, in declining to award equitable relief, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]hat which was wild land thirty years ago is now intersected by streets, subdivided into blocks and lots, and largely occupied by persons who have bought upon the strength of Patrick\xe2\x80\x99s title, and have erected buildings of a permanent character\xe2\x80\x9d). The District Court, in the litigation dormant during the pendency of Oneida II, see supra, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310, rightly found these pragmatic concerns about restoring Indian sovereign control over land \xe2\x80\x9cmagnified exponentially here, where development of every type imaginable has been ongoing for more than two centuries.\xe2\x80\x9d Oneida Indian Nation of N.\xc2\xa0Y., 199 F.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0D., at 92.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this case, the Court of Appeals concluded that the \xe2\x80\x9cimpossibility\xe2\x80\x9d doctrine had no application because OIN acquired the land in the open market and does not seek to uproot current property owners. 337 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 157. But the unilateral reestablishment of present and future Indian sovereign control, even over land purchased at the market price, would have disruptive practical consequences similar to those that led this Court in Yankton Sioux to initiate the impossibility doctrine. The city of Sherrill and Oneida County are today overwhelmingly populated by non-Indians. See supra, at 11. A checkerboard of alternating state and tribal jurisdiction in New York State\xe2\x80\x94created unilaterally at OIN\xe2\x80\x99s behest\xe2\x80\x94would \xe2\x80\x9cseriously burde[n] the administration of state and local governments\xe2\x80\x9d and would adversely affect landowners neighboring the tribal patches. Hagen, 510 U. S., at 421 (quoting Solem v. Bartlett, 465 U. S. 463, 471\xe2\x80\x93472, n.\xc2\xa012 (1984)). If OIN may unilaterally reassert sovereign control and remove these parcels from the local tax rolls, little would prevent the Tribe from initiating a new generation of litigation to free the parcels from local zoning or other regulatory controls that protect all landowners in the area. See Felix, 145 U. S., at 335 (\xe2\x80\x9cdecree prayed for in this case, if granted, would offer a distinct encouragement to \xe2\x80\xa6 similar claims\xe2\x80\x9d); cf. Brendale v. Confederated Tribes and Bands of Yakima Nation, 492 U. S. 408, 433\xe2\x80\x93437 (1989) (opinion of Stevens, J.) (discussing tribal land-use controls); post, at 5, n.\xc2\xa06 (Stevens, J., dissenting) (noting that \xe2\x80\x9cthe balance of interests\xe2\x80\x9d supports continued state zoning jurisdiction).[Footnote 13]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Recognizing these practical concerns, Congress has provided a mechanism for the acquisition of lands for tribal communities that takes account of the interests of others with stakes in the area\xe2\x80\x99s governance and well being. Title 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7465 authorizes the Secretary of the Interior to acquire land in trust for Indians and provides that the land \xe2\x80\x9cshall be exempt from State and local taxation.\xe2\x80\x9d See Cass County v. Leech Lake Band of Chippewa Indians, 524 U. S. 103, 114\xe2\x80\x93115 (1998). The regulations implementing \xc2\xa7465 are sensitive to the complex interjurisdictional concerns that arise when a tribe seeks to regain sovereign control over territory. Before approving an acquisition, the Secretary must consider, among other things, the tribe\xe2\x80\x99s need for additional land; \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he purposes for which the land will be used\xe2\x80\x9d; \xe2\x80\x9cthe impact on the State and its political subdivisions resulting from the removal of the land from the tax rolls\xe2\x80\x9d; and \xe2\x80\x9c[j]urisdictional problems and potential conflicts of land use which may arise.\xe2\x80\x9d 25 CFR \xc2\xa7151.10 (2004). Section 465 provides the proper avenue for OIN to reestablish sovereign authority over territory last held by the Oneidas 200 years ago.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, the question of damages for the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s ancient dispossession is not at issue in this case, and we therefore do not disturb our holding in Oneida II. However, the distance from 1805 to the present day, the Oneidas\xe2\x80\x99 long delay in seeking equitable relief against New York or its local units, and developments in the city of Sherrill spanning several generations, evoke the doctrines of laches, acquiescence, and impossibility, and render inequitable the piecemeal shift in governance this suit seeks unilaterally to initiate.[Footnote 14]

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For the reasons stated, the judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case involves an Indian tribe\xe2\x80\x99s claim to tax immunity on its own property located within its reservation. It does not implicate the tribe\xe2\x80\x99s immunity from other forms of state jurisdiction, nor does it concern the tribe\xe2\x80\x99s regulatory authority over property owned by non-Indians within the reservation.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For the purposes of its decision the Court assumes that the District Court and the Court of Appeals correctly resolved the major issues of fact and law that the parties debated in those courts and that the City of Sherrill (City) presented to us in its petition for certiorari. Thus, we accept those courts\xe2\x80\x99 conclusions that the Oneida Indian Nation of New York (Tribe) is a federally recognized Indian Tribe; that it is the successor-in-interest to the original Oneida Nation; that in 1788 the Treaty of Fort Schuyler created a 300,000 acre reservation for the Oneida; that in 1794 the Treaty of Canandaigua established that tract as a federally protected reservation; and that the reservation was not disestablished or diminished by the Treaty of Buffalo Creek in 1838. It is undisputed that the City seeks to collect property taxes on parcels of land that are owned by the Tribe and located within the historic boundaries of its reservation.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Since the outset of this litigation it has been common ground that if the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s properties are \xe2\x80\x9cIndian Country,\xe2\x80\x9d the City has no jurisdiction to tax them without express congressional consent.[Footnote 1] For the reasons set forth at length in the opinions of the District Court and the Court of Appeals, it is abundantly clear that all of the land owned by the Tribe within the boundaries of its reservation qualifies as Indian country. Without questioning the accuracy of that conclusion, the Court today nevertheless decides that the fact that most of the reservation has been occupied and governed by non-Indians for a long period of time precludes the Tribe \xe2\x80\x9cfrom rekindling embers of sovereignty that long ago grew cold.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14. This is a novel holding, and in my judgment even more unwise than the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding in County of Oneida v. Oneida Indian Nation of N. Y., 470 U. S. 226 (1985), that the Tribe may recover damages for the alleged illegal conveyance of its lands that occurred in 1795. In that case, I argued that the \xe2\x80\x9cremedy for the ancient wrong established at trial should be provided by Congress, not by judges seeking to rewrite history at this late date,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 270 (opinion dissenting in part). In the present case, the Tribe is not attempting to collect damages or eject landowners as a remedy for a wrong that occurred centuries ago; rather, it is invoking an ancient immunity against a city\xe2\x80\x99s present-day attempts to tax its reservation lands.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Without the benefit of relevant briefing from the parties, the Court has ventured into legal territory that belongs to Congress. Its decision today is at war with at least two bedrock principles of Indian law. First, only Congress has the power to diminish or disestablish a tribe\xe2\x80\x99s reservation.[Footnote 2] Second, as a core incident of tribal sovereignty, a tribe enjoys immunity from state and local taxation of its reservation lands, until that immunity is explicitly revoked by Congress.[Footnote 3] Far from revoking this immunity, Congress has specifically reconfirmed it with respect to the reservation lands of the New York Indians.[Footnote 4] Ignoring these principles, the Court has done what only Congress may do\xe2\x80\x94it has effectively proclaimed a diminishment of the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s reservation and an abrogation of its elemental right to tax immunity. Under our precedents, whether it is wise policy to honor the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s tax immunity is a question for Congress, not this Court, to decide.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As a justification for its lawmaking decision, the Court relies heavily on the fact that the Tribe is seeking equitable relief in the form of an injunction. The distinction between law and equity is unpersuasive because the outcome of the case turns on a narrow legal issue that could just as easily, if not most naturally, be raised by a tribe as a defense against a state collection proceeding. In fact, that scenario actually occurred in this case: The City brought an eviction proceeding against the Tribe based on its refusal to pay property taxes; that proceeding was removed to federal court and consolidated with the present action; the District Court granted summary judgment for the Tribe; and the Court of Appeals affirmed on the basis of tribal tax immunity.[Footnote 5] Either this defensive use of tax immunity should still be available to the Tribe on remand, but see ante, at 14, n.\xc2\xa07, or the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on the distinctions between law and equity and between substantive rights and remedies, see ante, at 13, is indefensible.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In any event, as a matter of equity I believe that the \xe2\x80\x9cprinciple that the passage of time can preclude relief,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 16, should be applied sensibly and with an even hand. It seems perverse to hold that the reliance interests of non-Indian New Yorkers that are predicated on almost two centuries of inaction by the Tribe do not foreclose the Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s enforcement of judicially created damages remedies for ancient wrongs, but do somehow mandate a forfeiture of a tribal immunity that has been consistently and uniformly protected throughout our history. In this case, the Tribe reacquired reservation land in a peaceful and lawful manner that fully respected the interests of innocent landowners\xe2\x80\x94it purchased the land on the open market. To now deny the Tribe its right to tax immunity\xe2\x80\x94at once the most fundamental of tribal rights and the least disruptive to other sovereigns\xe2\x80\x94is not only inequitable, but also irreconcilable with the principle that only Congress may abrogate or extinguish tribal sovereignty. I would not decide this case on the basis of speculation about what may happen in future litigation over other regulatory issues.[Footnote 6] For the answer to the question whether the City may require the Tribe to pay taxes on its own property within its own reservation is pellucidly clear. Under settled law, it may not.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, I respectfully dissent.
