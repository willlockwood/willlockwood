SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

Syllabus

BRAVO-FERNANDEZ et\xc2\xa0al. v. UNITED STATES

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the first circuit

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x93537.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 4, 2016\xe2\x80\x94Decided November 29, 2016

\n

The issue-preclusion component of the Double Jeopardy Clause bars a second contest of an issue of fact or law raised and necessarily resolved by a prior judgment. Ashe v. Swenson,\n\n397 U.\xc2\xa0S. 436\n. The burden is on the defendant to demonstrate that the issue he seeks to shield from reconsideration was actually decided by a prior jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict of acquittal. Schiro v. Farley,\n\n510 U. S. 222\n. When the same jury returns irreconcilably inconsistent verdicts on the issue in question, a defendant cannot meet that burden. The acquittal, therefore, gains no preclusive effect regarding the count of conviction. United States v. Powell,\n\n469 U.\xc2\xa0S. 57\n\xe2\x80\x9369. Issue preclusion does, however, attend a jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict of acquittal if the same jury in the same proceeding fails to reach a verdict on a different count turning on the same issue of ultimate fact. Yeager v. United States,\n\n557 U.\xc2\xa0S. 110\n\xe2\x80\x93122.

\n

In this case, a jury convicted petitioners Juan Bravo-Fernandez (Bravo) and Hector Mart\xc3\xadnez-Maldonado (Mart\xc3\xadnez) of bribery in violation of\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7666. Simultaneously, the jury acquitted them of conspiring to violate \xc2\xa7666 and traveling in interstate commerce to violate \xc2\xa7666. Because the only contested issue at trial was whether Bravo and Mart\xc3\xadnez had violated \xc2\xa7666 (the other elements of the acquitted charges\xe2\x80\x94agreement and travel\xe2\x80\x94were undisputed), the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdicts were irreconcilably inconsistent. Unlike the guilty verdicts in Powell, however, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 convictions were later vacated on appeal because of error in the judge\xe2\x80\x99s instructions unrelated to the verdicts\xe2\x80\x99 inconsistency. In the First Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s view, \xc2\xa7666 proscribes only quid pro quo bribery, yet the charge had permitted the jury to find petitioners guilty on a gratuity theory. On remand, Bravo and Mart\xc3\xadnez moved for judgments of acquittal on the standalone \xc2\xa7666 charges. They argued that the issue-preclusion component of the Double Jeopardy Clause barred the Government from retrying them on those charges because the jury necessarily determined that they were not guilty of violating \xc2\xa7666 when it acquitted them of the related conspiracy and Travel Act offenses. The District Court denied the motions, and the First Circuit affirmed, holding that the eventual invalidation of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 \xc2\xa7666 convictions did not undermine Powell\xe2\x80\x99s instruction that issue preclusion does not apply when the same jury returns logically inconsistent verdicts.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The issue-preclusion component of the Double Jeopardy Clause does not bar the Government from retrying defendants, like petitioners, after a jury has returned irreconcilably inconsistent verdicts of conviction and acquittal and the convictions are later vacated for legal error unrelated to the inconsistency. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0Because petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 trial yielded incompatible jury verdicts, petitioners cannot establish that the jury necessarily resolved in their favor the question whether they violated \xc2\xa7666. In view of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s inability to obtain review of the acquittals, Powell, 469 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 68, the inconsistent jury findings weigh heavily against according those acquittals issue-preclusive effect. The subsequent vacatur of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 bribery convictions does not alter this analysis. The critical inquiry is whether the jury actually decided that petitioners did not violate \xc2\xa7666. Ashe instructs courts to approach that task with \xe2\x80\x9crealism and rationality,\xe2\x80\x9d 397 U. S., at 444, in particular, to examine the trial record \xe2\x80\x9cwith an eye to all the circumstances of the proceedings,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. The jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdicts convicting petitioners of violating \xc2\xa7666 remain relevant to this practical inquiry, even if the convictions are later vacated on appeal for unrelated trial error.

\n

Petitioners could not be retried if the Court of Appeals had vacated their \xc2\xa7666 bribery convictions because of insufficient evidence, see Burks v. United States,\n\n437 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n\xe2\x80\x9311, or if the trial error could resolve the apparent inconsistency in the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdicts. But the evidence here was sufficient to convict petitioners on the quid pro quo bribery theory the First Circuit approved. And the instructional error cannot account for the jury\xe2\x80\x99s inconsistent determinations, for the error applied equally to every \xc2\xa7666-related count. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0Petitioners argue that vacated judgments should be excluded from the Ashe inquiry because vacated convictions, like the hung counts in Yeager, are legal nullities that \xe2\x80\x9chave never been accorded respect as a matter of law or history.\xe2\x80\x9d Yeager, 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 124. That argument misapprehends the Ashe inquiry. Bravo and Mart\xc3\xadnez bear the burden of showing that the issue whether they violated \xc2\xa7666 has been \xe2\x80\x9cdetermined by a valid and final judgment of acquittal.\xe2\x80\x9d 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 119 (internal quotation marks omitted). To judge whether they carried that burden, a court must realistically examine the record to identify the ground for the \xc2\xa7666-based acquittals. Ashe, 397 U. S., at 444. A conviction that contradicts those acquittals is plainly relevant to that determination, no less so simply because it is later overturned on appeal for unrelated legal error. See Powell, 469 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 65.

\n

Petitioners further contend that, under Yeager, the \xc2\xa7666 convictions are meaningless because the jury was allowed to convict on the basis of conduct not criminal in the First Circuit\xe2\x80\x94payment of a gratuity. But Yeager did not rest on a court\xe2\x80\x99s inability to detect the basis for a decision the jury in fact rendered. Rather, when a jury hangs, there is no decision, hence no inconsistency. 557 U. S., at 124\xe2\x80\x93125. By contrast, a verdict of guilt is a jury decision, even if subsequently vacated, and therefore can evince jury inconsistency. That is the case here. Petitioners gained a second trial on the standalone bribery charges, but they are not entitled to more. Issue preclusion is not a doctrine they can commandeer when inconsistent verdicts shroud in mystery what the jury necessarily decided. Pp.\xc2\xa016\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

790 F.\xc2\xa03d 41, affirmed.

JUAN BRAVO-FERNANDEZ AND HECTOR MARTINEZ-MALDONADO, PETITIONERS v.UNITED STATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the first circuit

\n

[November 29, 2016]

\n

\n

Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

This case concerns the issue-preclusion component of the Double Jeopardy Clause.[1] In criminal prosecutions, as in civil litigation, the issue-preclusion principle means that \xe2\x80\x9cwhen an issue of ultimate fact has once been determined by a valid and final judgment, that issue cannot again be litigated between the same parties in any future lawsuit.\xe2\x80\x9d Ashe v. Swenson,\n\n397 U.\xc2\xa0S. 436,\n443 (1970)\n.

\n

Does issue preclusion apply when a jury returns inconsistent verdicts, convicting on one count and acquitting on another count, where both counts turn on the very same issue of ultimate fact? In such a case, this Court has held, both verdicts stand. The Government is barred by the Double Jeopardy Clause from challenging the acquittal, see Green v. United States,\n\n355 U.\xc2\xa0S. 184,\n188 (1957)\n, but because the verdicts are rationally irreconcilable, the acquittal gains no preclusive effect, United States v. Powell,\n\n469 U.\xc2\xa0S. 57,\n68 (1984)\n.

\n

Does issue preclusion attend a jury\xe2\x80\x99s acquittal verdict if the same jury in the same proceeding fails to reach a verdict on a different count turning on the same critical issue? This Court has answered yes, in those circumstances, the acquittal has preclusive force. Yeager v. United States,\n\n557 U.\xc2\xa0S. 110\n\xe2\x80\x93122 (2009). As \xe2\x80\x9cthere is no way to decipher what a hung count represents,\xe2\x80\x9d we reasoned, a jury\xe2\x80\x99s failure to decide \xe2\x80\x9chas no place in the issue-preclusion analysis.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; see id., at 125 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he fact that a jury hangs is evidence of nothing\xe2\x80\x94other than, of course, that it has failed to decide anything.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

In the case before us, the jury returned irreconcilably inconsistent verdicts of conviction and acquittal. Without more, Powell would control. There could be no retrial of charges that yielded acquittals but, in view of the inconsistent verdicts, the acquittals would have no issue-preclusive effect on charges that yielded convictions. In this case, however, unlike Powell, the guilty verdicts were vacated on appeal because of error in the judge\xe2\x80\x99s instructions unrelated to the verdicts\xe2\x80\x99 inconsistency. Petitioners urge that, just as a jury\xe2\x80\x99s failure to decide has no place in issue-preclusion analysis, so vacated guilty verdicts should not figure in that analysis.

\n

We hold otherwise. One cannot know from the jury\xe2\x80\x99s report why it returned no verdict. \xe2\x80\x9cA host of reasons\xe2\x80\x9d could account for a jury\xe2\x80\x99s failure to decide\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9csharp dis-agreement, confusion about the issues, exhaustion after a long trial, to name but a few.\xe2\x80\x9d Yeager, 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 121. But actual inconsistency in a jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdicts is a reality; vacatur of a conviction for unrelated legal error does not reconcile the jury\xe2\x80\x99s inconsistent returns. We therefore bracket this case with Powell, not Yeager, and affirm the judgment of the Court of Appeals, which held that issue preclusion does not apply when verdict inconsistency renders unanswerable \xe2\x80\x9cwhat the jury necessarily decided.\xe2\x80\x9d 790 F.\xc2\xa03d 41, 47 (CA1 2015).

\n

I

\n

A

\n

The doctrine of claim preclusion instructs that a final judgment on the merits \xe2\x80\x9cforeclos[es] successive litigation of the very same claim.\xe2\x80\x9d New Hampshire v. Maine,\n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 742,\n748 (2001)\n; see Restatement (Second) of Judgments \xc2\xa719, p. 161 (1980) (hereinafter Restatement). So instructing, the doctrine serves to \xe2\x80\x9cavoid multiple suits on identical entitlements or obligations between the same parties.\xe2\x80\x9d 18 C. Wright, A. Miller, & E. Cooper, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa74402, p. 9 (2d ed. 2002) (herein-after Wright & Miller). Long operative in civil litigation, Restatement, at\xc2\xa02, claim preclusion is also essential to the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition against successive criminal prosecutions. No person, the Double Jeopardy Clause states, shall be \xe2\x80\x9csubject for the same offense to be twice put in jeopardy of life or limb.\xe2\x80\x9d Amdt. 5. The Clause \xe2\x80\x9cprotects against a second prosecution for the same offense after conviction\xe2\x80\x9d; as well, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t protects against a second prosecution for the same offense after acquittal.\xe2\x80\x9d North Carolina v. Pearce,\n\n395 U.\xc2\xa0S. 711,\n717 (1969)\n. \xe2\x80\x9c[A] verdict of acquittal [in our justice system] is final,\xe2\x80\x9d the last word on a criminal charge, and therefore operates as \xe2\x80\x9ca bar to a subsequent prosecution for the same offense.\xe2\x80\x9d Green v. United States,\n\n355 U.\xc2\xa0S. 184,\n188 (1957)\n.

\n

The allied doctrine of issue preclusion ordinarily bars relitigation of an issue of fact or law raised and necessarily resolved by a prior judgment. See Restatement \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa717, 27, at 148, 250; Wright & Miller \xc2\xa74416, at 386. It applies in both civil and criminal proceedings, with an important distinction. In civil litigation, where issue preclusion and its ramifications first developed, the availability of appellate review is a key factor. Restatement \xc2\xa728, Comment a, at\xc2\xa0274; see id., \xc2\xa728, Reporter\xe2\x80\x99s Note, at\xc2\xa0284 (noting \xe2\x80\x9cthe pervasive importance of reviewability in the application of preclusion doctrine\xe2\x80\x9d). In significant part, preclusion doctrine is premised on \xe2\x80\x9can underlying confidence that the result achieved in the initial litigation was substantially correct.\xe2\x80\x9d Standefer v. United States,\n\n447 U.\xc2\xa0S. 10\n, n.\xc2\xa018 (1980); see Restatement \xc2\xa729, Comment f, at 295. \xe2\x80\x9cIn the absence of appellate review,\xe2\x80\x9d we have observed, \xe2\x80\x9csuch confidence is often unwarranted.\xe2\x80\x9d Standefer, 447 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 23, n.\xc2\xa018.

\n

In civil suits, inability to obtain review is exceptional; it occurs typically when the controversy has become moot. In criminal cases, however, only one side (the defendant) has recourse to an appeal from an adverse judgment on the merits. The Government \xe2\x80\x9ccannot secure appellate review\xe2\x80\x9d of an acquittal, id., at 22, even one \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon an egregiously erroneous foundation,\xe2\x80\x9d Arizona v. Washington,\n\n434 U.\xc2\xa0S. 497,\n503 (1978)\n. Juries enjoy an \xe2\x80\x9cunreviewable power .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to return a verdict of not guilty for impermissible reasons,\xe2\x80\x9d for \xe2\x80\x9cthe Government is precluded from appealing or otherwise upsetting such an acquittal by the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s Double Jeopardy Clause.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Powell,\n\n469 U.\xc2\xa0S. 57,\n63,\n65 (1984)\n. The absence of appellate review of acquittals, we have cautioned, calls for guarded application of preclusion doctrine in criminal cases. See Standefer, 447 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 22\xe2\x80\x9323, and n.\xc2\xa018. Particularly where it appears that a jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict is the result of compromise, compassion, lenity, or misunderstanding of the governing law, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s inability to gain review \xe2\x80\x9cstrongly militates against giving an acquittal [issue] preclusive effect.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 23. See also Restatement \xc2\xa729, Comment g, at 295 (Where circumstances suggest that an issue was resolved on erroneous considerations, \xe2\x80\x9ctaking the prior determination at face value for purposes of the second action would [impermissibly] extend the .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. imperfections in the adjudicative process.\xe2\x80\x9d); id., \xc2\xa728, Comment j, at 283 (Issue preclusion may be denied where it is \xe2\x80\x9cevident from the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict that the verdict was the result of compromise.\xe2\x80\x9d); Wright & Miller \xc2\xa74423, at 617 (same).

\n

B

\n

This case requires us to determine whether an appellate court\xe2\x80\x99s vacatur of a conviction alters issue-preclusion analysis under the Double Jeopardy Clause. Three prior decisions guide our disposition.

\n

This Court first interpreted the Double Jeopardy Clause to incorporate the principle of issue preclusion in Ashe v. Swenson,\n\n397 U.\xc2\xa0S. 436 (1970)\n.[2] Ashe involved a robbery of six poker players by a group of masked men. Ashe was charged with robbing one of the players, but a jury acquitted him \xe2\x80\x9cdue to insufficient evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 439. The State then tried Ashe again, this time for robbing another of the poker players. Aided by \xe2\x80\x9csubstantially stronger\xe2\x80\x9d testimony from \xe2\x80\x9cwitnesses [who] were for the most part the same,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 439\xe2\x80\x93440, the State secured a conviction. We held that the second prosecution violated the Double Jeopardy Clause. Because the sole issue in dispute in the first trial was whether Ashe had been one of the robbers, the jury\xe2\x80\x99s acquittal verdict precluded the State from trying to convince a different jury of that very same fact in a second trial. Id., at 445.

\n

Our decision in Ashe explained that issue preclusion in criminal cases must be applied with \xe2\x80\x9crealism and rationality.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 444. To identify what a jury in a previous trial necessarily decided, we instructed, a court must \xe2\x80\x9cexamine the record of a prior proceeding, taking into account the pleadings, evidence, charge, and other relevant matter.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Mayers & Yarbrough, Bis Vexari: New Trials and Successive Prosecutions, 74 Harv. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1, 38 (1960)). This inquiry, we explained, \xe2\x80\x9cmust be set in a practical frame and viewed with an eye to all the circumstances of the proceedings.\xe2\x80\x9d 397 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 444 (quoting Sealfon v. United States,\n\n332 U.\xc2\xa0S. 575,\n579 (1948)\n). We have also made clear that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he burden is on the defendant to demonstrate that the issue whose relitigation he seeks to foreclose was actually decided\xe2\x80\x9d by a prior jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict of acquittal. Schiro v. Farley,\n\n510 U.\xc2\xa0S. 222,\n233 (1994)\n (internal quotation marks omitted); accord Dowling v. United States,\n\n493 U.\xc2\xa0S. 342,\n350 (1990)\n.

\n

In United States v. Powell,\n\n469 U.\xc2\xa0S. 57\n, we held that a defendant cannot meet this burden when the same jury returns irreconcilably inconsistent verdicts on the question she seeks to shield from reconsideration. Powell\xe2\x80\x99s starting point was our holding in Dunn v. United States,\n\n284 U.\xc2\xa0S. 390 (1932)\n, that a criminal defendant may not attack a jury\xe2\x80\x99s finding of guilt on one count as inconsistent with the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict of acquittal on another count. Powell, 469 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 58\xe2\x80\x9359. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in Dunn stated no exceptions to this rule, and after Dunn the Court had several times \xe2\x80\x9calluded to [the] rule as an established principle,\xe2\x80\x9d 469 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 63. Nevertheless, several Courts of Appeals had \xe2\x80\x9crecogniz[ed] exceptions to the rule,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 62, and Powell sought an exception for the verdicts of guilt she faced.

\n

At trial, a jury had acquitted Powell of various substantive drug charges but convicted her of using a telephone in \xe2\x80\x9ccausing and facilitating\xe2\x80\x9d those same offenses. Id., at 59\xe2\x80\x9360. She appealed, arguing that \xe2\x80\x9cthe verdicts were inconsistent, and that she therefore was entitled to reversal of the telephone facilitation convictions.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 60. Issue preclusion, she maintained, barred \xe2\x80\x9cacceptance of [the] guilty verdict[s]\xe2\x80\x9d on the auxiliary offenses because the same jury had acquitted her of the predicate felonies. Id., at 64.

\n

Rejecting Powell\xe2\x80\x99s argument, we noted that issue preclusion is \xe2\x80\x9cpredicated on the assumption that the jury acted rationally.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 68. When a jury returns irreconcilably inconsistent verdicts, we said, one can glean no more than that \xe2\x80\x9ceither in the acquittal or the conviction the jury did not speak their real conclusions.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 64 (quoting Dunn, 284 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 393). Although it is impos-sible to discern which verdict the jurors arrived at rationally, we observed, \xe2\x80\x9cthat does not show that they were not convinced of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s guilt.\xe2\x80\x9d Powell, 469 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 64\xe2\x80\x9365 (quoting Dunn, 284 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 393). In the event of inconsistent verdicts, we pointed out, it is just as likely that \xe2\x80\x9cthe jury, convinced of guilt, properly reached its conclusion on [one count], and then through mistake, compromise, or lenity, arrived at an inconsistent conclusion on the [related] offense.\xe2\x80\x9d Powell, 469 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 65. Because a court would be at a loss to know which verdict the jury \xe2\x80\x9creally meant,\xe2\x80\x9d we reasoned, principles of issue preclusion are not useful, for they are \xe2\x80\x9cpredicated on the assumption that the jury acted rationally and found certain facts in reaching its verdict.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 68. Holding that the acquittals had no preclusive effect on the counts of conviction, we reaffirmed Dunn\xe2\x80\x99s rule, under which both Powell\xe2\x80\x99s convictions and her acquittals, albeit inconsistent, remained undisturbed. 469 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 69.

\n

Finally, in Yeager v. United States,\n\n557 U.\xc2\xa0S. 110 (2009)\n, we clarified that Powell\xe2\x80\x99s holding on inconsistent verdicts does not extend to an apparent inconsistency between a jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict of acquittal on one count and its inability to reach a verdict on another count. See 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 124 (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]nconsistent verdicts\xe2\x80\x9d present an \xe2\x80\x9centirely different context\xe2\x80\x9d than one involving \xe2\x80\x9cboth verdicts and seemingly inconsistent hung counts.\xe2\x80\x9d). Yeager was tried on charges of fraud and insider trading. Id., at 114. The jury acquitted him of the fraud offenses, which the Court of Appeals concluded must have reflected a finding that he \xe2\x80\x9cdid not have any insider information that contradicted what was presented to the public.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 116. Yet the jury failed to reach a verdict on the insider-trading charges, as to which \xe2\x80\x9cthe possession of insider information was [likewise] a critical issue of ultimate fact.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 123. Arguing that the jury had therefore acted inconsistently, the Government sought to retry Yeager on the hung counts. We ruled that retrial was barred by the Double Jeopardy Clause.

\n

A jury \xe2\x80\x9cspeaks only through its verdict,\xe2\x80\x9d we noted. Id., at 121. Any number of reasons\xe2\x80\x94including confusion about the issues and sheer exhaustion, we observed\xe2\x80\x94could cause a jury to hang. Ibid. Accordingly, we said, only \xe2\x80\x9ca jury\xe2\x80\x99s decisions, not its failures to decide,\xe2\x80\x9d identify \xe2\x80\x9cwhat a jury necessarily determined at trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 122. Because a hung count reveals nothing more than a jury\xe2\x80\x99s failure to reach a decision, we further reasoned, it supplies no evidence of the jury\xe2\x80\x99s irrationality. Id., at 124\xe2\x80\x93125. Hung counts, we therefore held, \xe2\x80\x9cha[ve] no place in the issue-preclusion analysis,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 122: When a jury acquits on one count while failing to reach a verdict on another count concerning the same issue of ultimate fact, the acquittal, and only the acquittal, counts for preclusion purposes. Given the preclusive effect of the acquittal, the Court concluded, Yeager could not be retried on the hung count. Id., at 122\xe2\x80\x93125.

\n

C

\n

With our controlling precedent in view, we turn to the inconsistent verdicts rendered in this case. The prosecution stemmed from an alleged bribe paid by petitioner Juan Bravo-Fernandez (Bravo), an entrepreneur, to petitioner Hector Mart\xc3\xadnez-Maldonado (Mart\xc3\xadnez), then a senator serving the Commonwealth of Puerto Rico. The alleged bribe took the form of an all-expenses-paid trip to Las Vegas, including a $1,000 seat at a professional boxing match featuring a popular Puerto Rican contender. United States v. Fernandez, 722 F.\xc2\xa03d 1, 6 (CA1 2013). According to the Government, Bravo intended the bribe to secure Mart\xc3\xadnez\xe2\x80\x99 help in shepherding legislation through the Puerto Rico Senate that, if enacted, would \xe2\x80\x9cprovid[e] substantial financial benefits\xe2\x80\x9d to Bravo\xe2\x80\x99s enterprise. Ibid. In the leadup to the Las Vegas trip, Mart\xc3\xadnez submitted the legislation for the Senate\xe2\x80\x99s consideration and issued a committee report supporting it; within a week of returning from Las Vegas, Mart\xc3\xadnez issued another favorable report and voted to enact the legislation. Id., at 6\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

Based on these events, a federal grand jury in Puerto Rico indicted petitioners for, inter alia, federal-program bribery, in violation of\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7666; conspiracy to violate \xc2\xa7666, in violation of \xc2\xa7371; and traveling in interstate commerce to further violations of \xc2\xa7666, in violation of the Travel Act, \xc2\xa71952(a)(3)(A).[3] Following a three-week trial, a jury convicted Bravo and Mart\xc3\xadnez of the standalone \xc2\xa7666 bribery offense, but acquitted them of the related conspiracy and Travel Act charges. Fernandez, 722 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 7. Each received a sentence of 48 months in prison. Id., at 8.

\n

The Court of Appeals for the First Circuit vacated the \xc2\xa7666 convictions for instructional error. Id., at 27. In the First Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s view, the jury had been erroneously charged on what constitutes criminal conduct under that statute. Id., at 22\xe2\x80\x9327. The charge permitted the jury to find Bravo and Mart\xc3\xadnez \xe2\x80\x9cguilty of offering and receiving a gratuity,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 16, but, the appeals court held, \xc2\xa7666 proscribes only quid pro quo bribes, and not gratuities, id., at 6, 22.[4] True, the court acknowledged, the jury was instructed on both theories of bribery, and the evidence at trial sufficed to support a guilty verdict on either theory. Id., at 19\xe2\x80\x9320. But the Court of Appeals could not say with confidence that the erroneous charge was harmless, so it vacated the \xc2\xa7666 convictions and remanded for further proceedings. Id., at 27, 39.

\n

On remand, relying on the issue-preclusion component of the Double Jeopardy Clause, Bravo and Mart\xc3\xadnez moved for judgments of acquittal on the standalone \xc2\xa7666 charges. 988 F.\xc2\xa0Supp.\xc2\xa02d 191 (PR 2013). They could not be retried on the bribery offense, they insisted, because the jury necessarily determined that they were not guilty of violating \xc2\xa7666 when it acquitted them of conspiring to violate \xc2\xa7666 and traveling in interstate commerce to further violations of \xc2\xa7666. Id., at 193. That was so, petitioners maintained, because the only contested issue at trial was whether Bravo had offered, and Mart\xc3\xadnez had accepted, a bribe within the meaning of \xc2\xa7666. Id., at 196; see Tr.of Oral Arg. 4 (\xe2\x80\x9cThere was no dispute that they agreed to go to a boxing match together\xe2\x80\x9d; nor was there any dispute \xe2\x80\x9cthat to get to Las Vegas from Puerto Rico, you have to travel\xe2\x80\x9d across state lines.). The District Court denied the motions for acquittal. 988 F.\xc2\xa0Supp.\xc2\xa02d, at 196\xe2\x80\x93198. If the sole issue disputed at trial was whether Bravo and Mart\xc3\xadnez had violated \xc2\xa7666, the court explained, then \xe2\x80\x9cthe jury [had] acted irrationally.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 196. Because the same jury had simultaneously convicted Bravo and Mart\xc3\xadnez on the standalone \xc2\xa7666 charges, \xe2\x80\x9cthe verdict simply was inconsistent.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

The First Circuit affirmed the denial of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 motions for acquittal, agreeing that the jury\xe2\x80\x99s inconsistent returns were fatal to petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 issue-preclusion plea. 790 F.\xc2\xa03d 41. The jury received the same bribery instructions for each count involving \xc2\xa7666, the court noted, so the \xc2\xa7666-based verdicts\xe2\x80\x94convicting on the standalone bribery charges but acquitting on the related Travel Act and conspiracy counts\xe2\x80\x94could not be reconciled. Id., at 54\xe2\x80\x9355.[5]

\n

The Court of Appeals rejected petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 argument that the eventual invalidation of the bribery convictions rendered Powell\xe2\x80\x99s inconsistent-verdicts rule inapplicable. Ashe, the court reminded, calls for a practical appraisal based on the complete record of the prior proceeding; the \xc2\xa7666 bribery convictions, like the \xc2\xa7666-based acquittals, were part of that record. See 790 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 50. Nor are vacated convictions like hung counts for issue-preclusion purposes, the court continued. Informed by our decision in Yeager, the First Circuit recognized that a hung count reveals only a jury\xe2\x80\x99s failure to decide, and therefore cannot evidence actual inconsistency with a jury\xe2\x80\x99s decision. 790 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 50\xe2\x80\x9351. In contrast, the court said, vacated convictions \xe2\x80\x9care jury decisions, through which the jury has spoken.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 51. The later upset of a conviction on an unrelated ground, the court reasoned, does not undermine Powell\xe2\x80\x99s recognition that \xe2\x80\x9cinconsistent verdicts make it impossible to determine what a jury necessarily decided.\xe2\x80\x9d 790 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 51. The First Circuit therefore concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cvacated convictions, unlike hung counts, are relevant to the Ashe [issue-preclusion] inquiry.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

We granted certiorari to resolve a conflict among courts on this question: Does the issue-preclusion component of the Double Jeopardy Clause bar the Government from retrying defendants, like Bravo and Mart\xc3\xadnez, after a jury has returned irreconcilably inconsistent verdicts of conviction and acquittal, and the convictions are later vacated for legal error unrelated to the inconsistency?[6] 557 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2016). Holding that the Double Jeopardy Clause does not bar retrial in these circumstances, we affirm the First Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment.

\n

II

\n

When a conviction is overturned on appeal, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he general rule is that the [Double Jeopardy] Clause does not bar reprosecution.\xe2\x80\x9d Justices of Boston Municipal Court v. Lydon,\n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 294,\n308 (1984)\n. The ordinary consequence of vacatur, if the Government so elects, is a new trial shorn of the error that infected the first trial. This \xe2\x80\x9ccontinuing jeopardy\xe2\x80\x9d rule neither gives effect to the vacated judgment nor offends double jeopardy principles. Rather, it reflects the reality that the \xe2\x80\x9ccriminal proceedings against an accused have not run their full course.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. And by permitting a new trial post vacatur, the continuing-jeopardy rule serves both society\xe2\x80\x99s and criminal defendants\xe2\x80\x99 interests in the fair administration of justice. \xe2\x80\x9cIt would be a high price indeed for society to pay,\xe2\x80\x9d we have recognized, \xe2\x80\x9cwere every accused granted immunity from punishment because of any defect sufficient to constitute reversible error in the proceedings leading to conviction.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Tateo,\n\n377 U.\xc2\xa0S. 463,\n466 (1964)\n. And the rights of criminal defendants would suffer too, for \xe2\x80\x9cit is at least doubtful that appellate courts would be as zealous as they now are in protecting against the effects of improprieties at the trial or pretrial stage if they knew that reversal of a conviction would put the accused irrevocably beyond the reach of further prosecution.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

Bravo and Mart\xc3\xadnez ask us to deviate from the general rule that, post vacatur of a conviction, a new trial is in order. When a conviction is vacated on appeal, they maintain, an acquittal verdict simultaneously returned should preclude the Government from retrying the defendant on the vacated count. Our precedent, harmonious with issue-preclusion doctrine, opposes the foreclosure petitioners seek.

\n

A

\n

Bravo and Mart\xc3\xadnez bear the burden of demonstrating that the jury necessarily resolved in their favor the question whether they violated \xc2\xa7666. Schiro, 510 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 233. But, as we have explained, see supra, at 7, a defendant cannot meet that burden where the trial yielded incompatible jury verdicts on the issue the defendant seeks to insulate from relitigation. Here, the jury convicted Bravo and Mart\xc3\xadnez of violating \xc2\xa7666 but acquitted them of conspiring, and traveling with the intent, to violate \xc2\xa7666. The convictions and acquittals are irreconcilable because other elements of the Travel Act and conspiracy counts were not disputed. See supra, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311, and n.\xc2\xa05. It is unknowable \xe2\x80\x9cwhich of the inconsistent verdicts\xe2\x80\x94the acquittal[s] or the conviction[s]\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x98the jury really meant.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 790 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 47 (quoting Powell, 469 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 68); see Restatement \xc2\xa729, Comment f, at 295 (\xe2\x80\x9cWhere a determination relied on as preclusive is itself inconsistent with some other adjudication of the same issue, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. confidence [in that determination] is generally unwarranted.\xe2\x80\x9d). In view of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s inability to obtain review of the acquittals, Powell, 469 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 68, the inconsistent jury findings weigh heavily against according those acquittals issue-preclusive effect. See Standefer, 447 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 23, n.\xc2\xa017.

\n

That petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 bribery convictions were later vacated for trial error does not alter our analysis. The critical inquiry is whether the jury actually decided that Bravo and Mart\xc3\xadnez did not violate \xc2\xa7666. Ashe counsels us to approach that task with \xe2\x80\x9crealism and rationality,\xe2\x80\x9d 397 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 444, in particular, to examine the trial record \xe2\x80\x9cwith an eye to all the circumstances of the proceedings,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. As the Court of Appeals explained, \xe2\x80\x9cthe fact [that] the jury .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. convicted [Bravo and Mart\xc3\xadnez] of violating \xc2\xa7666 would seem to be of quite obvious relevance\xe2\x80\x9d to this practical inquiry, \xe2\x80\x9ceven though the convictions were later vacated.\xe2\x80\x9d 790 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 50. Because issue preclusion \xe2\x80\x9cdepends on the jury\xe2\x80\x99s assessment of the facts in light of the charges as presented at trial,\xe2\x80\x9d a conviction overturned on appeal is \xe2\x80\x9cappropriately considered in our assessment of [an acquittal] verdict\xe2\x80\x99s preclusive effect.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Citron, 853 F.\xc2\xa02d 1055, 1061 (CA2 1988). Indeed, the jurors in this case might not have acquitted on the Travel Act and conspiracy counts absent their belief that the \xc2\xa7666 bribery convictions would stand. See ibid.

\n

Bravo and Mart\xc3\xadnez could not be retried on the bribery counts, of course, if the Court of Appeals had vacated their \xc2\xa7666 convictions because there was insufficient evidence to support those convictions. For double jeopardy purposes, a court\xe2\x80\x99s evaluation of the evidence as insufficient to convict is equivalent to an acquittal and therefore bars a second prosecution for the same offense. See Burks v. United States,\n\n437 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n\xe2\x80\x9311 (1978); cf. Powell, 469 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 67 (noting that defendants are \xe2\x80\x9cafforded protection against jury irrationality or error by [courts\xe2\x80\x99] independent review of the sufficiency of the evidence\xe2\x80\x9d). But this is scarcely a case in which the prosecution \xe2\x80\x9cfailed to muster\xe2\x80\x9d sufficient evidence in the first proceeding. Burks, 437 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 11. Quite the opposite. The evidence presented at petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 trial, the Court of Appeals determined, supported a guilty verdict on the gratuity theory (which the First Circuit ruled impermissible) as well as the quid pro quo theory (which the First Circuit approved). 790 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 44. Vacatur was compelled for the sole reason that the First Circuit found the jury charge erroneous to the extent that it encompassed gratuities. See supra, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310, and n.\xc2\xa04. Therefore, the general rule of \xe2\x80\x9callowing a new trial to rectify trial error\xe2\x80\x9d applied. Burks, 437 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 14 (emphasis deleted).

\n

Nor, as the Government acknowledges, would retrial be tolerable if the trial error could resolve the apparent inconsistency in the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdicts. See Brief for United States 30 (If, for example, \xe2\x80\x9ca jury receives an erroneous instruction on the count of conviction but the correct instruction on the charge on which it acquits, the instructional error may reconcile the verdicts.\xe2\x80\x9d). But the instructional error here cannot account for the jury\xe2\x80\x99s contradictory determinations because the error applied equally to every \xc2\xa7666-related count. See supra, at 11.

\n

As in Powell, so in this case, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he problem is that the same jury reached inconsistent results.\xe2\x80\x9d 469 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 68. The convictions\xe2\x80\x99 later invalidation on an unrelated ground does not erase or reconcile that inconsistency: It does not bear on \xe2\x80\x9cthe factual determinations actually and necessarily made by the jury,\xe2\x80\x9d nor does it \xe2\x80\x9cserv[e] to turn the jury\xe2\x80\x99s otherwise inconsistent and irrational verdict into a consistent and rational verdict.\xe2\x80\x9d People v. Wilson, 496 Mich. 91, 125, 852 N.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa02d 134, 151 (2014) (Markman, J., dissenting). Bravo and Mart\xc3\xadnez, therefore, cannot establish the factual predicate necessary to preclude the Government from retrying them on the standalone \xc2\xa7666 charges\xe2\x80\x94namely, that the jury in the first proceeding actually decided that they did not violate the federal bribery statute.

\n

B

\n

To support their argument for issue preclusion, Bravo and Mart\xc3\xadnez highlight our decision in Yeager. In Yeager, they point out, we recognized that hung counts \xe2\x80\x9chave never been accorded respect as a matter of law or history.\xe2\x80\x9d 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 124. That is also true of vacated convictions, they urge, so vacated convictions, like hung counts, should be excluded from the Ashe inquiry into what the jury necessarily determined. Brief for Petitioners 20\xe2\x80\x9324. Asserting that we have \xe2\x80\x9cnever held an invalid conviction .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. relevant to or evidence of anything,\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 5, Bravo and Mart\xc3\xadnez argue that taking account of a vacated conviction in our issue-preclusion analysis would im-permissibly give effect to \xe2\x80\x9ca legal nullity,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 39; see Wilson, 496 Mich., at 107, 852 N. W. 2d, at 142 (majority opinion) (considering a vacated count would impermissibly \xe2\x80\x9cbring that legally vacated conviction back to life\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

This argument misapprehends the Ashe inquiry. It is undisputed that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 convictions are invalid judgments that may not be used to establish their guilt. The question is whether issue preclusion stops the Government from prosecuting them anew. On that question, Bravo and Mart\xc3\xadnez bear the burden of showing that the issue whether they violated \xc2\xa7666 has been \xe2\x80\x9cdetermined by a valid and final judgment of acquittal.\xe2\x80\x9d Yeager, 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 119 (internal quotation marks omitted). To judge whether they carried that burden, a court must realisti-cally examine the record to identify the ground for the \xc2\xa7666-based acquittals. Ashe, 397 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 444. A conviction that contradicts those acquittals is plainly relevant to that determination, no less so simply because it is later overturned on appeal for unrelated legal error: The split verdict\xe2\x80\x94finding \xc2\xa7666 violated on the standalone counts, but not violated on the related Travel Act and conspiracy counts\xe2\x80\x94tells us that, on one count or the other, \xe2\x80\x9cthe jury [did] not follo[w] the court\xe2\x80\x99s instructions,\xe2\x80\x9d whether because of \xe2\x80\x9cmistake, compromise, or lenity.\xe2\x80\x9d Powell, 469 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 65; see supra, at 7. Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 acquittals therefore do not support the application of issue preclusion here.[7]

\n

Further relying on Yeager, Bravo and Mart\xc3\xadnez contend that their vacated convictions should be ignored because, as with hung counts, \xe2\x80\x9cthere is no way to decipher\xe2\x80\x9d what they represent. Brief for Petitioners 28 (quoting Yeager, 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 121). The \xc2\xa7666 convictions are meaningless, they maintain, because the jury was allowed to convict on the basis of conduct not criminal in the First Circuit\xe2\x80\x94payment of a gratuity. Brief for Petitioners 24.

\n

This argument trips on Yeager\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning. Yeager did not rest on a court\xe2\x80\x99s inability to detect the basis for a jury\xe2\x80\x99s decision. Rather, this Court reasoned that, when a jury hangs, there is no decision, hence no evidence of irrationality. 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 124\xe2\x80\x93125. A verdict of guilt, by contrast, is a jury decision, even if subsequently vacated on appeal. It therefore can evince irrationality.

\n

That is the case here. Petitioners do not dispute that the Government\xe2\x80\x99s evidence at trial supported a guilty verdict on the quid pro quo theory, or that the gratuity instruction held erroneous by the Court of Appeals applied to every \xc2\xa7666-based offense. Because no rational jury could have reached conflicting verdicts on those counts, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 \xc2\xa7666 convictions \xe2\x80\x9creveal the jury\xe2\x80\x99s inconsis-tency\xe2\x80\x94which is the relevant issue here\xe2\x80\x94even if they do not reveal which theory of liability jurors relied upon in reaching those inconsistent verdicts.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 31. In other words, because we do not know what the jury would have concluded had there been no instructional error, Brief for Petitioners 28\xe2\x80\x9329, a new trial on the counts of conviction is in order. Bravo and Mart\xc3\xadnez have succeeded on appeal to that extent, but they are entitled to no more. The split verdict does not impede the Government from renewing the prosecution.[8]

\n

The Double Jeopardy Clause, as the First Circuit explained, forever bars the Government from again prosecuting Bravo and Mart\xc3\xadnez on the \xc2\xa7666-based conspiracy and Travel Act offenses; \xe2\x80\x9cthe acquittals themselves remain inviolate.\xe2\x80\x9d 790 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 51, n.\xc2\xa06. Bravo and Mart\xc3\xadnez have also gained \xe2\x80\x9cthe benefit of their appellate victory,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid.: a second trial on the standalone bribery charges, in which the Government may not invoke a gratuity theory. But issue preclusion is not a doctrine they can commandeer when inconsistent verdicts shroud in mystery what the jury necessarily decided.

\n

\n

For the reasons stated, the judgment of the Court of Appeals for the First Circuit is

\n

Affirmed.

JUAN BRAVO-FERNANDEZ AND HECTOR MARTINEZ-MALDONADO, PETITIONERS v.UNITED STATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the first circuit

\n

[November 29, 2016]

\n

\n

Justice Thomas, concurring.

\n

The question presented in this case is whether, under Ashe v. Swenson,\n\n397 U.\xc2\xa0S. 436 (1970)\n, and Yeager v. United States,\n\n557 U.\xc2\xa0S. 110 (2009)\n, a vacated conviction can nullify the preclusive effect of an acquittal under the issue-preclusion prong of the Double Jeopardy Clause.

\n

As originally understood, the Double Jeopardy Clause does not have an issue-preclusion prong. \xe2\x80\x9cThe English common-law pleas of auterfoits acquit and auterfoits convict, on which the Clause was based, barred only repeated \xe2\x80\x98prosecution for the same identical act and crime.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 128 (Scalia, J., dissenting) (quoting 4 W. Blackstone, Commentaries on the Laws of England 330 (1769); emphasis added by dissent); see also Grady v. Corbin, \n\n495 U.\xc2\xa0S. 508\n\xe2\x80\x93535 (1990) (Scalia, J., dissenting). But \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n Ashe the Court departed from the original meaning of the Double Jeopardy Clause, holding that it precludes suc-cessive prosecutions on distinct crimes when facts essential to conviction of the second crime have necessarilybeen resolved in the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s favor by a verdict of ac-quittal of the first crime.\xe2\x80\x9d Yeager, supra, at 128 (Scalia, J., dissenting).

\n

In Yeager, this Court erroneously and illogically extended Ashe. See 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 128\xe2\x80\x93131. \xe2\x80\x9cAshe held only thatthe Clause sometimes bars successive prosecution of facts found during \xe2\x80\x98a prior proceeding.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 129 (quoting Ashe, supra, at 444). Yeager, however, \xe2\x80\x9cbar[red] retrial on hung counts after what was not .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a prior proceeding but simply an earlier stage of the same proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 129 (Scalia, J., dissenting).

\n

In an appropriate case, we should reconsider the holdings of Ashe and Yeager. Because the Court today prop-erly declines to extend those cases, and indeed reaches the correct result under the Clause\xe2\x80\x99s original meaning, I join its opinion.
