\n\t\t\t\tPPL CORP. et\xc2\xa0al. v. COMMISSIONER OF INTERNAL REVENUE\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the third circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 12\xe2\x80\x9343.\xe2\x80\x83Argued February 20, 2013\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 20, 2013\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIn 1997, the United Kingdom (U.\xc2\xa0K.), newly under Labour Party rule, imposed a one-time \xe2\x80\x9cwindfall tax\xe2\x80\x9d on 32 U.\xc2\xa0K. companies privatized between 1984 and 1996 by the Conservative government. The companies had been sold to private parties through an initial sale of shares, known as a \xe2\x80\x9cflotation.\xe2\x80\x9d Some of the companies were required to continue providing services for a fixed period at the same rates they had offered under government control. Many of those companies became dramatically more efficient and earned substantial profits in the process.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioner PPL Corporation (PPL), part owner of a privatized U.\xc2\xa0K. company subject to the windfall tax, claimed a credit for its share of the bill in its 1997 federal income-tax return, relying on Internal Revenue Code \xc2\xa7901(b)(1), which states that any \xe2\x80\x9cincome, war profits, and excess profits taxes\xe2\x80\x9d paid overseas are creditable against U.\xc2\xa0S. income taxes. Treasury Regulation \xc2\xa71.901\xe2\x80\x932(a)(1) interprets this section to mean that a foreign tax is creditable if its \xe2\x80\x9cpredominant character\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cis that of an income tax in the U.\xc2\xa0S. sense.\xe2\x80\x9d The Commissioner of Internal Revenue (Commissioner) rejected PPL\xe2\x80\x99s claim, but the Tax Court held that the U.\xc2\xa0K. windfall tax was creditable for U.\xc2\xa0S. tax purposes under \xc2\xa7901. The Third Circuit reversed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0The U. K. tax is creditable under \xc2\xa7901. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9314.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0Treasury Regulation \xc2\xa71.901\xe2\x80\x932, which codifies longstanding doctrine dating back to Biddle v. Commissioner, \n\n302 U.\xc2\xa0S. 573\n\xe2\x80\x93579 (1938), provides the relevant legal standard. First, a tax\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cpredominant character,\xe2\x80\x9d or the normal manner in which a tax applies, is controlling. See id., at 579. Thus, a foreign tax that operates as an income, war profits, or excess profits tax for most taxpayers is generally creditable. Second, foreign tax creditability depends not on the way a foreign government characterizes its tax but on whether the tax, if enacted in the U.\xc2\xa0S., would be an income, war profits, or excess profits tax. See \xc2\xa71.901\xe2\x80\x932(a)(1)(ii). Giving further form to these principles, \xc2\xa71.901\xe2\x80\x932(a)(3)(i) explains that a foreign tax\xe2\x80\x99s predominant character is that of a U.\xc2\xa0S. income tax \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the foreign tax is likely to reach net gain in the normal circumstances in which it applies.\xe2\x80\x9d Three tests set forth in the regulations provide guidance in making this assessment, see \xc2\xa71.901\xe2\x80\x932(b)(1). The tests indicate that net gain consists of realized gross receipts reduced by significant costs and expenses attributable to such gross receipts, in combination known as net income. A foreign tax that reaches net income, or profits, is creditable. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x937.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0The U.\xc2\xa0K. windfall tax\xe2\x80\x99s predominant character is that of an excess profits tax, a category of income tax in the U.\xc2\xa0S. sense. The Labour government\xe2\x80\x99s conception of \xe2\x80\x9cprofit-making value\xe2\x80\x9d as a backward-looking analysis of historic profits is not a typical valuation method. Rather, it is a tax on realized net income disguised as a tax on the difference between two values, one of which is a fictitious value calculated using an imputed price-to-earnings ratio. The substance of the windfall tax confirms this conclusion. When rearranged, the U.\xc2\xa0K\xe2\x80\x99s formula demonstrates that the windfall tax is economically equivalent to the difference between the profits each company actually earned and the amount the Labour government believed it should have earned given its flotation value. For most of the relevant companies, the U.\xc2\xa0K. formula\xe2\x80\x99s substantive effect was to impose a 51.71 percent tax on all profits above a threshold, a classic excess profits tax. The Commissioner claims that any algebraic rearrangement is improper because U.\xc2\xa0S. courts must take the foreign tax rate as written and accept whatever tax base the foreign tax purports to adopt. But such a rigid construction cannot be squared with the black-letter principle that \xe2\x80\x9ctax law deals in economic realities, not legal abstractions.\xe2\x80\x9d Commissioner v. Southwest Exploration Co., \n\n350 U.\xc2\xa0S. 308\n. Given the artificiality of the U.\xc2\xa0K.\xe2\x80\x99s calculation method, this Court follows substance over form and recognizes that the windfall tax is nothing more than a tax on actual profits above a threshold. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9311.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0The Commissioner\xe2\x80\x99s additional arguments in support of his position are similarly unpersuasive. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9314.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t665 F.\xc2\xa03d 60, reversed.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 1997, the United Kingdom (U.\xc2\xa0K.) imposed a one-time \xe2\x80\x9cwindfall tax\xe2\x80\x9d on 32 U.\xc2\xa0K. companies privatized between 1984 and 1996. This case addresses whether that tax is creditable for U.\xc2\xa0S. tax purposes. Internal Revenue Code \xc2\xa7901(b)(1) states that any \xe2\x80\x9cincome, war profits, and excess profits taxes\xe2\x80\x9d paid overseas are creditable against U.\xc2\xa0S. income taxes. \n\n26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7901(b)(1). Treasury Regulations interpret this section to mean that a foreign tax is creditable if its \xe2\x80\x9cpredominant character\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cis that of an income tax in the U.\xc2\xa0S. sense.\xe2\x80\x9d Treas. Reg. \xc2\xa71.901\xe2\x80\x932(a)(1)(ii), 26 CFR \xc2\xa71.901\xe2\x80\x932(a)(1) (1992). Consistent with precedent and the Tax Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis below, we apply the predominant character test using a commonsense approach that considers the substantive effect of the tax. Under this approach, we hold that the U. K. tax is credit-able under \xc2\xa7901 and reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tDuring the 1980\xe2\x80\x99s and 1990\xe2\x80\x99s, the U.\xc2\xa0K.\xe2\x80\x99s Conservative Party controlled Parliament and privatized a number of government-owned companies. These companies were sold to private parties through an initial sale of shares, known as a \xe2\x80\x9cflotation.\xe2\x80\x9d As part of privatization, many com-panies were required to continue providing services at the same rates they had offered under government control for a fixed period, typically their first four years of private operation. As a result, the companies could only increase profits during this period by operating more efficiently. Responding to market incentives, many of the companies became dramatically more efficient and earned substantial profits in the process.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe U.\xc2\xa0K.\xe2\x80\x99s Labour Party, which had unsuccessfully opposed privatization, used the companies\xe2\x80\x99 profitability as a campaign issue against the Conservative Party. In part because of campaign promises to tax what it characterized as undue profits, the Labour Party defeated the Conservative Party at the polls in 1997. Prior to coming to power, Labour Party leaders hired accounting firm Arthur Andersen to structure a tax that would capture excess, or \xe2\x80\x9cwindfall,\xe2\x80\x9d profits earned during the initial years in which the companies were prohibited from increasing rates. Par-liament eventually adopted the tax, which applied only to the regulated companies that were prohibited from raising their rates. See Finance (No. 2) Act, 1997, ch. 58, pt. I, cls.\xc2\xa01 and 2(5) (Eng.) (U.\xc2\xa0K. Windfall Tax Act). It imposed a 23 percent tax on any \xe2\x80\x9cwindfall\xe2\x80\x9d earned by such companies. Id., cl.\xc2\xa01(2). A separate schedule \xe2\x80\x9cse[t] out how to quantify the windfall from which a company was benefitting.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., cl.\xc2\xa01(3). See id., sched. 1.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn the proceedings below, the parties stipulated that the following formula summarizes the tax imposed by the Labour Party:\n\t\t\t

For 27 of the 32 companies subject to the tax, the number of days in the initial period was 1,461 days (or four years). Of the remaining five companies, one had no tax liability because it did not earn any windfall profits. Three had initial periods close to four years (1,463, 1,456, and 1,380 days). The last was privatized shortly before the Labour Party took power and had an initial period of only 316 days.\n\t\t\t

Thus, it claimed (and the Commissioner here reiterates) that the tax was simply a 23 percent tax on the difference between what the companies\xe2\x80\x99 flotation values should have been and what they actually were.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioner PPL Corporation (PPL) was an owner, through a number of subsidiaries, of 25 percent of South Western Electricity plc, 1 of 12 government-owned elec-tric companies that were privatized in 1990 and that were subject to the tax. See 135 T.\xc2\xa0C. 304, 307, App. (2010) (diagram of PPL corporate structure in 1997). South Western Electricity\xe2\x80\x99s total U.\xc2\xa0K. windfall tax burden was \xc2\xa390,419,265. In its 1997 federal income-tax return, PPL claimed a credit under \xc2\xa7901 for its share of the bill. The Commissioner of Internal Revenue (Commissioner) rejected the claim, but the Tax Court held that the U.\xc2\xa0K. wind- fall tax was creditable for U.\xc2\xa0S. tax purposes under \xc2\xa7901. See id., at 342. The Third Circuit reversed. 665 F.\xc2\xa03d 60, 68 (2011). We granted certiorari, 568 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012), to resolve a Circuit split concerning the windfall tax\xe2\x80\x99s creditability under \xc2\xa7901. Compare 665 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 68, with Entergy Corp. & Affiliated Subsidiaries v. Commissioner, 683 F.\xc2\xa03d 233, 239 (CA5 2012).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tInternal Revenue Code \xc2\xa7901(b)(1) provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n the case of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a domestic corporation, the amount of any income, war profits, and excess profits taxes paid or accrued during the taxable year to any foreign country or to any possession of the United States\xe2\x80\x9d shall be creditable.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Under relevant Treasury Regulations, \xe2\x80\x9c[a] foreign levy is an income tax if and only if .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [t]he predominant character of that tax is that of an income tax in the U.\xc2\xa0S. sense.\xe2\x80\x9d 26 CFR \xc2\xa71.901\xe2\x80\x932(a)(1). The parties agree that Treasury Regulation \xc2\xa71.901\xe2\x80\x932 applies to this case. That regulation codifies longstanding doctrine dating back to Biddle v. Commissioner, \n\n302 U.\xc2\xa0S. 573\n\xe2\x80\x93579 (1938), and provides the relevant legal standard.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe regulation establishes several principles relevant to our inquiry. First, the \xe2\x80\x9cpredominant character\xe2\x80\x9d of a tax, or the normal manner in which a tax applies, is controlling. See id., at 579 (\xe2\x80\x9cWe are here concerned only with the \xe2\x80\x98standard\xe2\x80\x99 or normal tax\xe2\x80\x9d). Under this principle, a for-eign tax that operates as an income, war profits, or excess profits tax in most instances is creditable, even if it may affect a handful of taxpayers differently. Creditability is an all or nothing proposition. As the Treasury Regulations confirm, \xe2\x80\x9ca tax either is or is not an income tax, in its entirety, for all persons subject to the tax.\xe2\x80\x9d 26 CFR \xc2\xa71.901\xe2\x80\x932(a)(1).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSecond, the way a foreign government characterizes its tax is not dispositive with respect to the U.\xc2\xa0S. creditability analysis. See \xc2\xa71.901\xe2\x80\x932(a)(1)(ii) (foreign tax creditable if predominantly \xe2\x80\x9can income tax in the U.\xc2\xa0S. sense\xe2\x80\x9d). In Biddle, the Court considered the creditability of certain U.\xc2\xa0K. taxes on stock dividends under the substantively identical predecessor to \xc2\xa7901. The Court recognized that \xe2\x80\x9cthere is nothing in [the statute\xe2\x80\x99s] language to suggest that in allowing the credit for foreign tax payments, a shifting standard was adopted by reference to foreign characterizations and classifications of tax legislation.\xe2\x80\x9d 302 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 578\xe2\x80\x93579. See also United States v. Goodyear Tire & Rubber Co., \n\n493 U.\xc2\xa0S. 132, \n145 (1989)\n (noting in interpreting \n\n26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7902 that Biddle is particularly applicable \xe2\x80\x9cwhere a contrary interpretation would leave\xe2\x80\x9d tax interpretation \xe2\x80\x9cto the varying tax policies of foreign tax authorities\xe2\x80\x9d); Heiner v. Mellon, \n\n304 U.\xc2\xa0S. 271\n, and n.\xc2\xa07 (1938) (state-law definitions generally not controlling in federal tax context). Instead of the foreign government\xe2\x80\x99s characterization of the tax, the crucial inquiry is the tax\xe2\x80\x99s economic effect. See Biddle, supra, at 579 (inquiry is \xe2\x80\x9cwhether [a tax] is the substantial equivalent of payment of the tax as those terms are used in our own statute\xe2\x80\x9d). In other words, foreign tax creditability depends on whether the tax, if enacted in the U.\xc2\xa0S., would be an income, war profits, or excess profits tax.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tGiving further form to these principles, Treasury Regulation \xc2\xa71.901\xe2\x80\x932(a)(3)(i) explains that a foreign tax\xe2\x80\x99s predominant character is that of a U.\xc2\xa0S. income tax \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the foreign tax is likely to reach net gain in the normal circumstances in which it applies.\xe2\x80\x9d The regulation then sets forth three tests for assessing whether a foreign tax reaches net gain. A tax does so if, \xe2\x80\x9cjudged on the basis of its predominant character, [it] satisfies each of the realization, gross receipts, and net income requirements set forth in paragraphs (b)(2), (b)(3) and (b)(4), respectively, of this section.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71.901\xe2\x80\x932(b)(1).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t The tests indicate that net gain (also referred to as net income) consists of realized gross receipts reduced by significant costs and expenses attrib-utable to such gross receipts. A foreign tax that reaches net income, or profits, is creditable.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is undisputed that net income is a component of the U.\xc2\xa0K.\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cwindfall tax\xe2\x80\x9d formula. See Brief for Respondent 23 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe windfall tax takes into account a company\xe2\x80\x99s prof-its during its four-year initial period\xe2\x80\x9d). Indeed, annual profit is a variable in the tax formula. U.\xc2\xa0K. Windfall Tax Act, sched. 1, \xc2\xa71, cls. 2(2) and 5. It is also undisputed that there is no meaningful difference for our purposes in the accounting principles by which the U. K. and the U.\xc2\xa0S. calculate profits. See Brief for Petitioners 47. The dis-agreement instead centers on how to characterize the tax formula the Labour Party adopted.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Third Circuit, following the Commissioner\xe2\x80\x99s lead, believed it could look no further than the tax formula that the Parliament enacted and the way in which the Labour government characterized it. Under that view, the windfall tax must be considered a tax on the difference between a company\xe2\x80\x99s flotation value (the total amount investors paid for the company when the government sold it) and an imputed \xe2\x80\x9cprofit-making value,\xe2\x80\x9d defined as a company\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9caverage annual profit during its \xe2\x80\x98initial period\xe2\x80\x99 .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. times 9, the assumed price-to-earnings ratio.\xe2\x80\x9d 665 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 65. So characterized, the tax captures a portion of the difference between the price at which each company was sold and the price at which the Labour government believed each company should have been sold given the actual profits earned during the initial period. Relying on this characterization, the Third Circuit believed the windfall tax failed at least the Treasury Regulation\xe2\x80\x99s realization and gross receipts tests because it reached some artificial form of valuation instead of profits. See id., at 67, and n.\xc2\xa03.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn contrast, PPL\xe2\x80\x99s position is that the substance of the windfall tax is that of an income tax in the U.\xc2\xa0S. sense. While recognizing that the tax ostensibly is based on the difference between two values, it argues that every \xe2\x80\x9cvari-able\xe2\x80\x9d in the windfall tax formula except for profits and flotation value is fixed (at least with regard to 27 of the 32 companies). PPL emphasizes that the only way the Labour government was able to calculate the imputed \xe2\x80\x9cprofit-making value\xe2\x80\x9d at which it claimed companies should have been privatized was by looking after the fact at the actual profits earned by each company. In PPL\xe2\x80\x99s view, it matters not how the U.\xc2\xa0K. chose to arrange the formula or what it claimed to be taxing, because a tax based on profits above some threshold is an excess profits tax, regardless of how it is mathematically arranged or what labels foreign law places on it. PPL, thus, contends that the windfall taxes it paid meet the Treasury Regulation\xe2\x80\x99s tests and are credit-able under \xc2\xa7901.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe agree with PPL and conclude that the predominant character of the windfall tax is that of an excess profits tax, a category of income tax in the U.\xc2\xa0S. sense. It is important to note that the Labour government\xe2\x80\x99s conception of \xe2\x80\x9cprofit-making value\xe2\x80\x9d as a backward-looking analysis of historic profits is not a recognized valuation method; instead, it is a fictitious value calculated using an imputed price-to-earnings ratio. At trial, one of PPL\xe2\x80\x99s expert witnesses explained that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x989 is not an accurate P/E multiple, and it is not applied to current or expected future earnings.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 135 T.\xc2\xa0C., at 326, n.\xc2\xa017 (quoting testimony). Instead, the windfall tax is a tax on realized net income disguised as a tax on the difference between two values, one of which is completely fictitious. See App. 251, Report \xc2\xb61.7 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he value in profit making terms described in the wording of the act . . . is not a real value: it is rather a construct based on realised profits that would not have been known at the date of privatisation\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

For the 27 companies that had 1,461-day initial periods, the U.\xc2\xa0K. tax formula\xe2\x80\x99s substantive effect was to impose a 51.71 percent tax on all profits earned above a threshold. That is a classic excess profits tax. See, e.g., Act of Mar. 3, 1917, ch. 159, Tit. II, \xc2\xa7201, \n\n39Stat.\n1000 (8 percent tax imposed on excess profits exceeding the sum of $5,000 plus 8 percent of invested capital).\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOf course, other algebraic reformulations of the windfall tax equation are possible. See 665 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 66; Brief for Anne Alstott et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 21\xe2\x80\x9323 (Alstott Brief). The point of the reformulation is not that it yields a particular percentage (51.75 percent for most of the companies). Rather, the algebraic reformulations illustrate the economic substance of the tax and its interrelationship with net income.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Commissioner argues that any algebraic rearrangement is improper, asserting that U.\xc2\xa0S. courts must take the foreign tax rate as written and accept whatever tax base the foreign tax purports to adopt. Brief for Respondent 28. As a result, the Commissioner claims that the analysis begins and ends with the Labour government\xe2\x80\x99s choice to characterize its tax base as the difference between \xe2\x80\x9cprofit-making value\xe2\x80\x9d and flotation value. Such a rigid construction is unwarranted. It cannot be squared with the black-letter principle that \xe2\x80\x9ctax law deals in economic realities, not legal abstractions.\xe2\x80\x9d Commissioner v. Southwest Exploration Co., \n\n350 U.\xc2\xa0S. 308, \n315 (1956)\n. Given the artificiality of the U. K.\xe2\x80\x99s method of calculating purported \xe2\x80\x9cvalue,\xe2\x80\x9d we follow substance over form and recognize that the windfall tax is nothing more than a tax on actual profits above a threshold.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe find the Commissioner\xe2\x80\x99s other arguments unpersuasive as well. First, the Commissioner attempts to buttress the argument that the windfall tax is a tax on value by noting that some U.\xc2\xa0S. gift and estate taxes use actual, past profits to estimate value. Brief for Respondent 17\xe2\x80\x9318 (citing 26 CFR \xc2\xa720.2031\xe2\x80\x933 (2012) and \n\n26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72032A). This argument misses the point. In the case of valuation for gift and estate taxes, past income may be used to estimate future income streams. But, it is future revenue-earning potential, reduced to market value, that is subject to taxation. The windfall profits tax, by contrast, undisputedly taxed past, realized net income alone.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Commissioner contends that the U.\xc2\xa0K. was not trying to establish valuation as of the 1997 date on which the windfall tax was enacted but instead was attempting to derive a proper flotation valuation as of each company\xe2\x80\x99s flotation date. Brief for Respondent 21. The Commissioner asserts that there was no need to estimate future in- come (as in the case of the gift or estate recipient) because actual revenue numbers for the privatized companies were available. Ibid. That argument also misses the mark. It is true, of course, that the companies might have been privatized at higher flotation values had the government recognized how efficient\xe2\x80\x94and thus how profitable\xe2\x80\x94the companies would become. But, the windfall tax requires an underlying concept of value (based on actual ex post earnings) that would be alien to any valuer. Taxing ac-tual, realized net income in hindsight is not the same as considering past income for purposes of estimating future earning potential.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Commissioner\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on Example 3 to the Treasury Regulation\xe2\x80\x99s gross receipts test is also misplaced. Id., at 37\xe2\x80\x9338; 26 CFR \xc2\xa71.901\xe2\x80\x932(b)(3)(ii), Ex. 3. That example posits a petroleum tax in which \xe2\x80\x9cgross receipts from extraction income are deemed to equal 105 percent of the fair market value of petroleum extracted. This computation is designed to produce an amount that is greater than the fair market value of actual gross receipts.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Under the example, a tax based on inflated gross receipts is not creditable.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Third Circuit believed that the same type of algebraic rearrangement used above could also be used to rearrange a tax imposed on Example 3. It hypothesized:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cSay that the tax rate on the hypothetical extraction tax is 20%. It is true that a 20% tax on 105% of receipts is mathematically equivalent to a 21% tax on 100% of receipts, the latter of which would satisfy the gross receipts requirement. PPL proposes that we make the same move here, increasing the tax rate from 23% to 51.75% so that there is no multiple of receipts in the tax base. But if the regulation allowed us to do that, the example would be a nullity. Any tax on a multiple of receipts or profits could satisfy the gross receipts requirement, because we could reduce the starting point of its tax base to 100% of gross receipts by imagining a higher tax rate.\xe2\x80\x9d 665 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 67.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe Commissioner reiterates the Third Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s argument. Brief for Respondent 37\xe2\x80\x9338.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThere are three basic problems with this approach. As the Fifth Circuit correctly recognized, there is a difference between imputed and actual receipts. \xe2\x80\x9cExample 3 hypothesizes a tax on the extraction of petroleum where the income value of the petroleum is deemed to be .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. deliberately greater than actual gross receipts.\xe2\x80\x9d Entergy Corp., 683 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 238. In contrast, the windfall tax depends on actual figures. Ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9cThere was no need to calculate imputed gross receipts; gross receipts were actually known\xe2\x80\x9d). Example 3 simply addresses a different foreign taxation issue.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe argument also incorrectly equates imputed gross receipts under Example 3 with net income. See 665 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 67 (\xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny tax on a multiple of receipts or profits\xe2\x80\x9d). As noted, a tax is creditable only if it applies to realized gross receipts reduced by significant costs and expenses attributable to such gross receipts. 26 CFR \xc2\xa71.901\xe2\x80\x932(b)(4)(i). A tax based solely on gross receipts (like the Third Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s analysis) would be noncreditable because it would fail the Treasury Regulation\xe2\x80\x99s net income requirement.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, even if expenses were subtracted from imputed gross receipts before a tax was imposed, the effect of inflating only gross receipts would be to inflate revenue while holding expenses (the other component of net income) constant. A tax imposed on inflated income minus actual expenses is not the same as a tax on net income.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor these reasons, a tax based on imputed gross receipts is not creditable. But, as the Fifth Circuit explained in rejecting the Third Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s analysis, Example 3 is \xe2\x80\x9cfaci-ally irrelevant\xe2\x80\x9d to the analysis of the U.\xc2\xa0K. windfall tax, which is based on true net income. Entergy Corp., supra, at 238.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe economic substance of the U.\xc2\xa0K. windfall tax is that of a U.\xc2\xa0S. income tax. The tax is based on net income, and the fact that the Labour government chose to characterize it as a tax on the difference between two values is not dispositive under Treasury Regulation \xc2\xa71.901\xe2\x80\x932. Therefore, the tax is creditable under \xc2\xa7901.\n\t\t\t

An excess profits tax does the same, but incorporates a threshold, which may or may not vary across taxpayers, to exempt a portion of the base from taxation. In contrast, here both of the rate and threshold components vary from company to company according to the D variable.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSeen through this lens, the windfall tax is really a tax on average profits. See Alstott Brief 28\xe2\x80\x9330. Under the parties\xe2\x80\x99 stipulated form of the windfall tax, each company pays a fixed tax rate of 23% on a base that is calculated by first multiplying a company\xe2\x80\x99s daily average profits during its initial period (i.e., P/D , or total profits over the initial period divided by the length of the initial period) by a fixed price-to-earnings ratio; and then subtracting that com- pany\xe2\x80\x99s flotation value FV. See ante, at 2. In practice, this means that, for example, a company that earns $100 million over 1,461 days would pay approximately the same amount of taxes as a company that has earned $25 million over 365 days. These two companies would have almost the same average profits. See Alstott Brief 28. This is not how an income tax works.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe difference between a tax on profits and tax on average profits is especially significant for properly characterizing a tax such as the windfall tax. Average daily profits multiplied by a price-to-earnings ratio, rather than being a way of approximating income, is a way of approximating value.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t See Thompson, A Lawyer\xe2\x80\x99s Guide to Modern Valuation Techniques in Mergers and Acquisitions, 21 J. Corp. L. 457, 532\xe2\x80\x93533 (1996) (describing similar valuation techniques using price-to-earnings ratios). Accordingly, incorporating an outlier like Railtrack Group into the predominant character analysis suggests that the windfall tax is a tax on a company\xe2\x80\x99s value. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis argument, however, rests on the premise that because the relevant regulations state that \xe2\x80\x9ca tax either is or is not an income tax, in its entirety, for all persons subject to the tax,\xe2\x80\x9d 26 CFR \xc2\xa71.901\xe2\x80\x932(a)(1)(ii), a tax\xe2\x80\x99s predominant character must be as an income tax for all taxpayers. Under this reading, the regulations tell courts to treat outliers like Railtrack Group as flukes.\n\t\t\t

This situation often arises when a tax relies on imperfect estimates and assumptions in attempting to calculate net gain. Such a tax strives to treat similarly situated taxpayers the same but fails to do so only because the estimated component inadvertently affects some taxpayers differently. A situation of this kind occurred in Texasgulf, Inc. v. Commissioner, 172 F.\xc2\xa03d 209 (CA2 1999). In that case, a Canadian mining tax did not permit taxpayers to deduct their specific expenses, but did permit them to deduct a fixed \xe2\x80\x9cprocessing allowance.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 211\xe2\x80\x93213. The taxpayer argued that the tax was creditable because the processing allowance was an attempt to reach net income, gross income minus expenses, by using \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a method that is likely to produce an amount that approximates, or is greater than, recovery of such significant costs and expenditures.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 215 (quoting 26 CFR \xc2\xa71.901\xe2\x80\x932(b)(4)(i)(B) (1999)). To support its argument, the taxpayer introduced empirical evidence that roughly 85% of companies facing mining tax liability had nonrecoverable expenses less than the processing allowance. Texasgulf, Inc., 172 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 215\xe2\x80\x93216. The Court of Appeals agreed with the taxpayer that the tax was a creditable income tax because it was clear that the mining tax was attempting to reach net income, albeit by using an estimate to calculate deductions. Id.,at 216\xe2\x80\x93217. This result is sensible: A company that happens to have deductible expenses greater than the fixed amount set by the processing allowance is not an instructive outlier regarding the mining taxes predominant character. The mining tax is attempting to reach that company\xe2\x80\x99s net income, but fails to do only because it relies on an approximate value for deductions.\n\t\n
