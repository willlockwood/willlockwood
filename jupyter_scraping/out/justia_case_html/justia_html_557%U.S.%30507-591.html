MELENDEZ-DIAZ v. MASSACHUSETTS

\n

certiorari to the appeals court of massachusetts

\n

No. 07\xe2\x80\x93591.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 10, 2008\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 25, 2009

\n

At petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s state-court drug trial, the prosecution introduced certificates of state laboratory analysts stating that material seized by police and connected to petitioner was cocaine of a certain quantity. As required by Massachusetts law, the certificates were sworn to before a notary public and were submitted as prima facie evidence of what they asserted. Petitioner objected, asserting that Crawford v. Washington, 541 U. S. 36, required the analysts to testify in person. The trial court disagreed, the certificates were admitted, and petitioner was convicted. The Massachusetts Appeals Court affirmed, rejecting petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claim that the certificates\xe2\x80\x99 admission violated the Sixth Amendment.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The admission of the certificates violated petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s Sixth Amendment right to confront the witnesses against him. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9323.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0 Under Crawford, a witness\xe2\x80\x99s testimony against a defendant is inadmissible unless the witness appears at trial or, if the witness is unavailable, the defendant had a prior opportunity for cross-examination. 541 U. S., at 54. The certificates here are affidavits, which fall within the \xe2\x80\x9ccore class of testimonial statements\xe2\x80\x9d covered by the Confrontation Clause, id., at 51. They asserted that the substance found in petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s possession was, as the prosecution claimed, cocaine of a certain weight\xe2\x80\x94the precise testimony the analysts would be expected to provide if called at trial. Not only were the certificates made, as Crawford required for testimonial statements, \xe2\x80\x9cunder circumstances which would lead an objective witness reasonably to believe that the statement would be available for use at a later trial,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 52, but under the relevant Massachusetts law their sole purpose was to provide prima facie evidence of the substance\xe2\x80\x99s composition, quality, and net weight. Petitioner was entitled to \xe2\x80\x9cbe confronted with\xe2\x80\x9d the persons giving this testimony at trial. Id., at 54. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The arguments advanced to avoid this rather straightforward application of Crawford are rejected. Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s claim that the analysts are not subject to confrontation because they are not \xe2\x80\x9caccusatory\xe2\x80\x9d witnesses finds no support in the Sixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s text or in this Court\xe2\x80\x99s case law. The affiants\xe2\x80\x99 testimonial statements were not \xe2\x80\x9cnearly contemporaneous\xe2\x80\x9d with their observations, nor, if they had been, would that fact alter the statements\xe2\x80\x99 testimonial character. There is no support for the proposition that witnesses who testify regarding facts other than those observed at the crime scene are exempt from confrontation. The absence of interrogation is irrelevant; a witness who volunteers his testimony is no less a witness for Sixth Amendment purposes. The affidavits do not qualify as traditional official or business records. The argument that the analysts should not be subject to confrontation because their statements result from neutral scientific testing is little more than an invitation to return to the since-overruled decision in Ohio v. Roberts, 448 U. S. 56, 66, which held that evidence with \xe2\x80\x9cparticularized guarantees of trustworthiness\xe2\x80\x9d was admissible without confrontation. Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s power to subpoena the analysts is no substitute for the right of confrontation. Finally, the requirements of the Confrontation Clause may not be relaxed because they make the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s task burdensome. In any event, the practice in many States already accords with today\xe2\x80\x99s decision, and the serious disruption predicted by respondent and the dissent has not materialized. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9323.

\n

69 Mass. App. 1114, 870 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 676, reversed and remanded.

LUIS E. MELENDEZ-DIAZ, PETITIONER v.\nMASSACHUSETTS

\n

on writ of certiorari to the appeals court of\nmassachusetts

\n

[June 25, 2009]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Massachusetts courts in this case admitted into evidence affidavits reporting the results of forensic analysis which showed that material seized by the police and connected to the defendant was cocaine. The question presented is whether those affidavits are \xe2\x80\x9ctestimonial,\xe2\x80\x9d rendering the affiants \xe2\x80\x9cwitnesses\xe2\x80\x9d subject to the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s right of confrontation under the Sixth Amendment.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 2001, Boston police officers received a tip that a Kmart employee, Thomas Wright, was engaging in suspicious activity. The informant reported that Wright repeatedly received phone calls at work, after each of which he would be picked up in front of the store by a blue sedan, and would return to the store a short time later. The police set up surveillance in the Kmart parking lot and witnessed this precise sequence of events. When Wright got out of the car upon his return, one of the officers detained and searched him, finding four clear white plastic bags containing a substance resembling cocaine. The officer then signaled other officers on the scene to arrest the two men in the car\xe2\x80\x94one of whom was petitioner Luis Melendez-Diaz. The officers placed all three men in a police cruiser.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0During the short drive to the police station, the officers observed their passengers fidgeting and making furtive movements in the back of the car. After depositing the men at the station, they searched the police cruiser and found a plastic bag containing 19 smaller plastic bags hidden in the partition between the front and back seats. They submitted the seized evidence to a state laboratory required by law to conduct chemical analysis upon police request. Mass. Gen. Laws, ch. 111, \xc2\xa712 (West 2006).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Melendez-Diaz was charged with distributing cocaine and with trafficking in cocaine in an amount between 14 and 28 grams. Ch. 94C, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa732A, 32E(b)(1). At trial, the prosecution placed into evidence the bags seized from Wright and from the police cruiser. It also submitted three \xe2\x80\x9ccertificates of analysis\xe2\x80\x9d showing the results of the forensic analysis performed on the seized substances. The certificates reported the weight of the seized bags and stated that the bags \xe2\x80\x9c[h]a[ve] been examined with the following results: The substance was found to contain: Cocaine.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 24a, 26a, 28a. The certificates were sworn to before a notary public by analysts at the State Laboratory Institute of the Massachusetts Department of Public Health, as required under Massachusetts law. Mass. Gen. Laws, ch. 111, \xc2\xa713.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner objected to the admission of the certificates, asserting that our Confrontation Clause decision in Crawford v. Washington, 541 U. S. 36 (2004), required the analysts to testify in person. The objection was overruled, and the certificates were admitted pursuant to state law as \xe2\x80\x9cprima facie evidence of the composition, quality, and the net weight of the narcotic \xe2\x80\xa6 analyzed.\xe2\x80\x9d Mass. Gen. Laws, ch. 111, \xc2\xa713.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The jury found Melendez-Diaz guilty. He appealed, contending, among other things, that admission of the certificates violated his Sixth Amendment right to be confronted with the witnesses against him. The Appeals Court of Massachusetts rejected the claim, affirmance order, 69 Mass. App. 1114, 870 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 676, 2007 WL 2189152, *4, n.\xc2\xa03 (July 31, 2007), relying on the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Commonwealth v. Verde, 444 Mass. 279, 283\xe2\x80\x93285, 827 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 701, 705\xe2\x80\x93706 (2005), which held that the authors of certificates of forensic analysis are not subject to confrontation under the Sixth Amendment. The Supreme Judicial Court denied review. 449 Mass. 1113, 874 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 407 (2007). We granted certiorari. 552 U. S. ___ (2008).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Sixth Amendment to the United States Constitution, made applicable to the States via the Fourteenth Amendment, Pointer v. Texas, 380 U. S. 400, 403 (1965), provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n all criminal prosecutions, the accused shall enjoy the right \xe2\x80\xa6 to be confronted with the witnesses against him.\xe2\x80\x9d In Crawford, after reviewing the Clause\xe2\x80\x99s historical underpinnings, we held that it guarantees a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s right to confront those \xe2\x80\x9cwho \xe2\x80\x98bear testimony\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d against him. 541 U. S., at 51. A witness\xe2\x80\x99s testimony against a defendant is thus inadmissible unless the witness appears at trial or, if the witness is unavailable, the defendant had a prior opportunity for cross-examination. Id., at 54.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our opinion described the class of testimonial statements covered by the Confrontation Clause as follows:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cVarious formulations of this core class of testimonial statements exist: ex parte in-court testimony or its functional equivalent\xe2\x80\x94that is, material such as affidavits, custodial examinations, prior testimony that the defendant was unable to cross-examine, or similar pretrial statements that declarants would reasonably expect to be used prosecutorially; extrajudicial statements \xe2\x80\xa6 contained in formalized testimonial materials, such as affidavits, depositions, prior testimony, or confessions; statements that were made under circumstances which would lead an objective witness reasonably to believe that the statement would be available for use at a later trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 51\xe2\x80\x9352 (internal quotation marks and citations omitted).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is little doubt that the documents at issue in this case fall within the \xe2\x80\x9ccore class of testimonial statements\xe2\x80\x9d thus described. Our description of that category mentions affidavits twice. See also White v. Illinois, 502 U. S. 346, 365 (1992) (Thomas, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he Confrontation Clause is implicated by extrajudicial statements only insofar as they are contained in formalized testimonial materials, such as affidavits, depositions, prior testimony, or confessions\xe2\x80\x9d). The documents at issue here, while denominated by Massachusetts law \xe2\x80\x9ccertificates,\xe2\x80\x9d are quite plainly affidavits: \xe2\x80\x9cdeclaration[s] of facts written down and sworn to by the declarant before an officer authorized to administer oaths.\xe2\x80\x9d Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 62 (8th ed. 2004). They are incontrovertibly a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98solemn declaration or affirmation made for the purpose of establishing or proving some fact.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Crawford, supra, at 51 (quoting 2 N. Webster, An American Dictionary of the English Language (1828)). The fact in question is that the substance found in the possession of Melendez-Diaz and his codefendants was, as the prosecution claimed, cocaine\xe2\x80\x94the precise testimony the analysts would be expected to provide if called at trial. The \xe2\x80\x9ccertificates\xe2\x80\x9d are functionally identical to live, in-court testimony, doing \xe2\x80\x9cprecisely what a witness does on direct examination.\xe2\x80\x9d Davis v. Washington, 547 U. S. 813, 830 (2006) (emphasis deleted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Here, moreover, not only were the affidavits \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98made under circumstances which would lead an objective witness reasonably to believe that the statement would be available for use at a later trial,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Crawford, supra, at 52, but under Massachusetts law the sole purpose of the affidavits was to provide \xe2\x80\x9cprima facie evidence of the composition, quality, and the net weight\xe2\x80\x9d of the analyzed substance, Mass. Gen. Laws, ch. 111, \xc2\xa713. We can safely assume that the analysts were aware of the affidavits\xe2\x80\x99 evidentiary purpose, since that purpose\xe2\x80\x94as stated in the relevant state-law provision\xe2\x80\x94was reprinted on the affidavits themselves. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 25a, 27a, 29a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In short, under our decision in Crawford the analysts\xe2\x80\x99 affidavits were testimonial statements, and the analysts were \xe2\x80\x9cwitnesses\xe2\x80\x9d for purposes of the Sixth Amendment. Absent a showing that the analysts were unavailable to testify at trial and that petitioner had a prior opportunity to cross-examine them, petitioner was entitled to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98be confronted with\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the analysts at trial. Crawford, supra, at 54.[Footnote 1]

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent and the dissent advance a potpourri of analytic arguments in an effort to avoid this rather straightforward application of our holding in Crawford. Before addressing them, however, we must assure the reader of the falsity of the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s opening alarum that we are \xe2\x80\x9csweep[ing] away an accepted rule governing the admission of scientific evidence\xe2\x80\x9d that has been \xe2\x80\x9cestablished for at least 90 years\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cextends across at least 35 States and six Federal Courts of Appeals.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 1 (opinion of Kennedy, J.).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The vast majority of the state-court cases the dissent cites in support of this claim come not from the last 90 years, but from the last 30, and not surprisingly nearly all of them rely on our decision in Ohio v. Roberts, 448 U. S. 56 (1980), or its since-rejected theory that unconfronted testimony was admissible as long as it bore indicia of reliability, id., at 66. See post, at 30.[Footnote 2] As for the six Federal Courts of Appeals cases cited by the dissent, five of them postdated and expressly relied on Roberts. See post, at 21\xe2\x80\x9322. The sixth predated Roberts but relied entirely on the same erroneous theory. See Kay v. United States, 255 F.\xc2\xa02d 476, 480\xe2\x80\x93481 (CA4 1958) (rejecting confrontation clause challenge \xe2\x80\x9cwhere there is reasonable necessity for [the evidence] and where . . . the evidence has those qualities of reliability and trustworthiness\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A review of cases that predate the Roberts era yields a mixed picture. As the dissent notes, three state supreme court decisions from the early 20th century denied confrontation with respect to certificates of analysis regarding a substance\xe2\x80\x99s alcohol content. See post, at 21 (citing cases from Massachusetts, Connecticut, and Virginia). But other state courts in the same era reached the opposite conclusion. See Torres v. State, 18 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa02d 179, 180 (Tex. Crim. App. 1929); Volrich v. State, No. 278, 1925 WL 2473 (Ohio App., Nov. 2, 1925). At least this much is entirely clear: In faithfully applying Crawford to the facts of this case, we are not overruling 90 years of settled jurisprudence. It is the dissent that seeks to overturn precedent by resurrecting Roberts a mere five years after it was rejected in Crawford.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We turn now to the various legal arguments raised by respondent and the dissent.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent first argues that the analysts are not subject to confrontation because they are not \xe2\x80\x9caccusatory\xe2\x80\x9d witnesses, in that they do not directly accuse petitioner of wrongdoing; rather, their testimony is inculpatory only when taken together with other evidence linking petitioner to the contraband. See Brief for Respondent 10. This finds no support in the text of the Sixth Amendment or in our case law.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Sixth Amendment guarantees a defendant the right \xe2\x80\x9cto be confronted with the witnesses against him.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.) To the extent the analysts were witnesses (a question resolved above), they certainly provided testimony against petitioner, proving one fact necessary for his conviction\xe2\x80\x94that the substance he possessed was cocaine. The contrast between the text of the Confrontation Clause and the text of the adjacent Compulsory Process Clause confirms this analysis. While the Confrontation Clause guarantees a defendant the right to be confronted with the witnesses \xe2\x80\x9cagainst him,\xe2\x80\x9d the Compulsory Process Clause guarantees a defendant the right to call witnesses \xe2\x80\x9cin his favor.\xe2\x80\x9d U. S. Const., Amdt. 6. The text of the Amendment contemplates two classes of witnesses\xe2\x80\x94those against the defendant and those in his favor. The prosecution must produce the former;[Footnote 3] the defendant may call the latter. Contrary to respondent\xe2\x80\x99s assertion, there is not a third category of witnesses, helpful to the prosecution, but somehow immune from confrontation.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is often, indeed perhaps usually, the case that an adverse witness\xe2\x80\x99s testimony, taken alone, will not suffice to convict. Yet respondent fails to cite a single case in which such testimony was admitted absent a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s opportunity to cross-examine.[Footnote 4] Unsurprisingly, since such a holding would be contrary to longstanding case law. In Kirby v. United States, 174 U. S. 47 (1899), the Court considered Kirby\xe2\x80\x99s conviction for receiving stolen property, the evidence for which consisted, in part, of the records of conviction of three individuals who were found guilty of stealing the relevant property. Id., at 53. Though this evidence proved only that the property was stolen, and not that Kirby received it, the Court nevertheless ruled that admission of the records violated Kirby\xe2\x80\x99s rights under the Confrontation Clause. Id., at 55. See also King v. Turner, 1 Mood. 347, 168 Eng. Rep. 1298 (1832) (confession by one defendant to having stolen certain goods could not be used as evidence against another defendant accused of receiving the stolen property).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent and the dissent argue that the analysts should not be subject to confrontation because they are not \xe2\x80\x9cconventional\xe2\x80\x9d (or \xe2\x80\x9ctypical\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cordinary\xe2\x80\x9d) witnesses of the sort whose ex parte testimony was most notoriously used at the trial of Sir Walter Raleigh. Post, at 15\xe2\x80\x9316; Brief for Respondent 28. It is true, as the Court recognized in Crawford, that ex parte examinations of the sort used at Raleigh\xe2\x80\x99s trial have \xe2\x80\x9clong been thought a paradigmatic confrontation violation.\xe2\x80\x9d 541 U. S., at 52. But the paradigmatic case identifies the core of the right to confrontation, not its limits. The right to confrontation was not invented in response to the use of the ex parte examinations in Raleigh\xe2\x80\x99s Case, 2 How. St. Tr. 1 (1603). That use provoked such an outcry precisely because it flouted the deeply rooted common-law tradition \xe2\x80\x9cof live testimony in court subject to adversarial testing.\xe2\x80\x9d Crawford, supra, at 43 (citing 3 W. Blackstone, Commentaries on the Laws of England 373\xe2\x80\x93374 (1768)). See also Crawford, supra, at 43\xe2\x80\x9347.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In any case, the purported distinctions respondent and the dissent identify between this case and Sir Walter Raleigh\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cconventional\xe2\x80\x9d accusers do not survive scrutiny. The dissent first contends that a \xe2\x80\x9cconventional witness recalls events observed in the past, while an analyst\xe2\x80\x99s report contains near-contemporaneous observations of the test.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 16\xe2\x80\x9317. It is doubtful that the analyst\xe2\x80\x99s reports in this case could be characterized as reporting \xe2\x80\x9cnear-contemporaneous observations\xe2\x80\x9d; the affidavits were completed almost a week after the tests were performed. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 24a\xe2\x80\x9329a (the tests were performed on November 28, 2001, and the affidavits sworn on December 4, 2001). But regardless, the dissent misunderstands the role that \xe2\x80\x9cnear-contemporaneity\xe2\x80\x9d has played in our case law. The dissent notes that that factor was given \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial weight\xe2\x80\x9d in Davis, post, at 17, but in fact that decision disproves the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s position. There the Court considered the admissibility of statements made to police officers responding to a report of a domestic disturbance. By the time officers arrived the assault had ended, but the victim\xe2\x80\x99s statements\xe2\x80\x94written and oral\xe2\x80\x94were sufficiently close in time to the alleged assault that the trial court admitted her affidavit as a \xe2\x80\x9cpresent sense impression.\xe2\x80\x9d Davis, 547 U. S., at 820 (internal quotation marks omitted). Though the witness\xe2\x80\x99s statements in Davis were \xe2\x80\x9cnear-contemporaneous\xe2\x80\x9d to the events she reported, we nevertheless held that they could not be admitted absent an opportunity to confront the witness. Id., at 830.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A second reason the dissent contends that the analysts are not \xe2\x80\x9cconventional witnesses\xe2\x80\x9d (and thus not subject to confrontation) is that they \xe2\x80\x9cobserve[d] neither the crime nor any human action related to it.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 17. The dissent provides no authority for this particular limitation of the type of witnesses subject to confrontation. Nor is it conceivable that all witnesses who fit this description would be outside the scope of the Confrontation Clause. For example, is a police officer\xe2\x80\x99s investigative report describing the crime scene admissible absent an opportunity to examine the officer? The dissent\xe2\x80\x99s novel exception from coverage of the Confrontation Clause would exempt all expert witnesses\xe2\x80\x94a hardly \xe2\x80\x9cunconventional\xe2\x80\x9d class of witnesses.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A third respect in which the dissent asserts that the analysts are not \xe2\x80\x9cconventional\xe2\x80\x9d witnesses and thus not subject to confrontation is that their statements were not provided in response to interrogation. Ibid. See also Brief for Respondent 29. As we have explained, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Framers were no more willing to exempt from cross-examination volunteered testimony or answers to open-ended questions than they were to exempt answers to detailed interrogation.\xe2\x80\x9d Davis, supra, at 822\xe2\x80\x93823, n. 1. Respondent and the dissent cite no authority, and we are aware of none, holding that a person who volunteers his testimony is any less a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98witness against\xe2\x80\x99 the defendant,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 26, than one who is responding to interrogation. In any event, the analysts\xe2\x80\x99 affidavits in this case were presented in response to a police request. See Mass. Gen. Laws, ch. 111, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa712\xe2\x80\x9313. If an affidavit submitted in response to a police officer\xe2\x80\x99s request to \xe2\x80\x9cwrite down what happened\xe2\x80\x9d suffices to trigger the Sixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s protection (as it apparently does, see Davis, 547 U. S., at 819\xe2\x80\x93820; id., at 840, n.\xc2\xa05 (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment in part and dissenting in part)), then the analysts\xe2\x80\x99 testimony should be subject to confrontation as well.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent claims that there is a difference, for Confrontation Clause purposes, between testimony recounting historical events, which is \xe2\x80\x9cprone to distortion or manipulation,\xe2\x80\x9d and the testimony at issue here, which is the \xe2\x80\x9cresul[t] of neutral, scientific testing.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 29. Relatedly, respondent and the dissent argue that confrontation of forensic analysts would be of little value because \xe2\x80\x9cone would not reasonably expect a laboratory professional \xe2\x80\xa6 to feel quite differently about the results of his scientific test by having to look at the defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 31 (internal quotation marks omitted); see post, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This argument is little more than an invitation to return to our overruled decision in Roberts, 448 U. S. 56, which held that evidence with \xe2\x80\x9cparticularized guarantees of trustworthiness\xe2\x80\x9d was admissible notwithstanding the Confrontation Clause. Id., at 66. What we said in Crawford in response to that argument remains true:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cTo be sure, the Clause\xe2\x80\x99s ultimate goal is to ensure reliability of evidence, but it is a procedural rather than a substantive guarantee. It commands, not that evidence be reliable, but that reliability be assessed in a particular manner: by testing in the crucible of cross-examination. \xe2\x80\xa6 Dispensing with confrontation because testimony is obviously reliable is akin to dispensing with jury trial because a defendant is obviously guilty. This is not what the Sixth Amendment prescribes.\xe2\x80\x9d 541 U. S., at 61\xe2\x80\x9362.

\n

\nRespondent and the dissent may be right that there are other ways\xe2\x80\x94and in some cases better ways\xe2\x80\x94to challenge or verify the results of a forensic test.[Footnote 5] But the Constitution guarantees one way: confrontation. We do not have license to suspend the Confrontation Clause when a preferable trial strategy is available.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor is it evident that what respondent calls \xe2\x80\x9cneutral scientific testing\xe2\x80\x9d is as neutral or as reliable as respondent suggests. Forensic evidence is not uniquely immune from the risk of manipulation. According to a recent study conducted under the auspices of the National Academy of Sciences, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he majority of [laboratories producing forensic evidence] are administered by law enforcement agencies, such as police departments, where the laboratory administrator reports to the head of the agency.\xe2\x80\x9d National Research Council of the National Academies, Strengthening Forensic Science in the United States: A Path Forward 6\xe2\x80\x931 (Prepublication Copy Feb. 2009) (hereinafter National Academy Report). And \xe2\x80\x9c[b]ecause forensic scientists often are driven in their work by a need to answer a particular question related to the issues of a particular case, they sometimes face pressure to sacrifice appropriate methodology for the sake of expediency.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at S\xe2\x80\x9317. A forensic analyst responding to a request from a law enforcement official may feel pressure\xe2\x80\x94or have an incentive\xe2\x80\x94to alter the evidence in a manner favorable to the prosecution.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Confrontation is one means of assuring accurate forensic analysis. While it is true, as the dissent notes, that an honest analyst will not alter his testimony when forced to confront the defendant, post, at 10, the same cannot be said of the fraudulent analyst. See Brief for National Innocence Network as Amicus Curiae 15\xe2\x80\x9317 (discussing cases of documented \xe2\x80\x9cdrylabbing\xe2\x80\x9d where forensic analysts report results of tests that were never performed); National Academy Report 1\xe2\x80\x938 to 1\xe2\x80\x9310 (discussing documented cases of fraud and error involving the use of forensic evidence). Like the eyewitness who has fabricated his account to the police, the analyst who provides false results may, under oath in open court, reconsider his false testimony. See Coy v. Iowa, 487 U. S. 1012, 1019 (1988). And, of course, the prospect of confrontation will deter fraudulent analysis in the first place.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Confrontation is designed to weed out not only the fraudulent analyst, but the incompetent one as well. Serious deficiencies have been found in the forensic evidence used in criminal trials. One commentator asserts that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he legal community now concedes, with varying degrees of urgency, that our system produces erroneous convictions based on discredited forensics.\xe2\x80\x9d Metzger, Cheating the Constitution, 59 Vand. L. Rev. 475, 491 (2006). One study of cases in which exonerating evidence resulted in the overturning of criminal convictions concluded that invalid forensic testimony contributed to the convictions in 60% of the cases. Garrett & Neufeld, Invalid Forensic Science Testimony and Wrongful Convictions, 95 Va. L. Rev. 1, 14 (2009). And the National Academy Report concluded:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe forensic science system, encompassing both research and practice, has serious problems that can only be addressed by a national commitment to overhaul the current structure that supports the forensic science community in this country.\xe2\x80\x9d National Academy Report P\xe2\x80\x931 (emphasis in original).[Footnote 6]

\n

\nLike expert witnesses generally, an analyst\xe2\x80\x99s lack of proper training or deficiency in judgment may be disclosed in cross-examination.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case is illustrative. The affidavits submitted by the analysts contained only the bare-bones statement that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he substance was found to contain: Cocaine.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 24a, 26a, 28a. At the time of trial, petitioner did not know what tests the analysts performed, whether those tests were routine, and whether interpreting their results required the exercise of judgment or the use of skills that the analysts may not have possessed. While we still do not know the precise tests used by the analysts, we are told that the laboratories use \xe2\x80\x9cmethodology recommended by the Scientific Working Group for the Analysis of Seized Drugs,\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Brief for Petitioner 1a\xe2\x80\x932a. At least some of that methodology requires the exercise of judgment and presents a risk of error that might be explored on cross-examination. See 2 P. Giannelli & E. Imwinkelried, Scientific Evidence \xc2\xa723.03[c], pp. 532\xe2\x80\x93533, ch. 23A, p. 607 (4th ed. 2007) (identifying four \xe2\x80\x9ccritical errors\xe2\x80\x9d that analysts may commit in interpreting the results of the commonly used gas chromatography/mass spectrometry analysis); Shellow, The Application of Daubert to the Identification of Drugs, 2 Shepard\xe2\x80\x99s Expert & Scientific Evidence Quarterly 593, 600 (1995) (noting that while spectrometers may be equipped with computerized matching systems, \xe2\x80\x9cforensic analysts in crime laboratories typically do not utilize this feature of the instrument, but rely exclusively on their subjective judgment\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The same is true of many of the other types of forensic evidence commonly used in criminal prosecutions. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]here is wide variability across forensic science disciplines with regard to techniques, methodologies, reliability, types and numbers of potential errors, research, general acceptability, and published material.\xe2\x80\x9d National Academy Report S\xe2\x80\x935. See also id., at 5\xe2\x80\x939, 5\xe2\x80\x9312, 5\xe2\x80\x9317, 5\xe2\x80\x9321 (discussing problems of subjectivity, bias, and unreliability of common forensic tests such as latent fingerprint analysis, pattern/impression analysis, and toolmark and firearms analysis). Contrary to respondent\xe2\x80\x99s and the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion, there is little reason to believe that confrontation will be useless in testing analysts\xe2\x80\x99 honesty, proficiency, and methodology\xe2\x80\x94the features that are commonly the focus in the cross-examination of experts.

\n

D

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent argues that the analysts\xe2\x80\x99 affidavits are admissible without confrontation because they are \xe2\x80\x9cakin to the types of official and business records admissible at common law.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 35. But the affidavits do not qualify as traditional official or business records, and even if they did, their authors would be subject to confrontation nonetheless.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Documents kept in the regular course of business may ordinarily be admitted at trial despite their hearsay status. See Fed. Rule Evid. 803(6). But that is not the case if the regularly conducted business activity is the production of evidence for use at trial. Our decision in Palmer v. Hoffman, 318 U. S. 109 (1943), made that distinction clear. There we held that an accident report provided by an employee of a railroad company did not qualify as a business record because, although kept in the regular course of the railroad\xe2\x80\x99s operations, it was \xe2\x80\x9ccalculated for use essentially in the court, not in the business.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 114.[Footnote 7] The analysts\xe2\x80\x99 certificates\xe2\x80\x94like police reports generated by law enforcement officials\xe2\x80\x94do not qualify as business or public records for precisely the same reason. See Rule 803(8) (defining public records as \xe2\x80\x9cexcluding, however, in criminal cases matters observed by police officers and other law enforcement personnel\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent seeks to rebut this limitation by noting that at common law the results of a coroner\xe2\x80\x99s inquest were admissible without an opportunity for confrontation. But as we have previously noted, whatever the status of coroner\xe2\x80\x99s reports at common law in England, they were not accorded any special status in American practice. See Crawford, 541 U. S., at 47, n.\xc2\xa02; Giles v. California, 554 U. S. ___, ___ (2008) (slip op., at 20) (Breyer, J., dissenting); Evidence\xe2\x80\x94Official Records\xe2\x80\x94Coroner\xe2\x80\x99s Inquest, 65 U. Pa. L. Rev. 290 (1917).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dissent identifies a single class of evidence which, though prepared for use at trial, was traditionally admissible: a clerk\xe2\x80\x99s certificate authenticating an official record\xe2\x80\x94or a copy thereof\xe2\x80\x94for use as evidence. See post, at 19. But a clerk\xe2\x80\x99s authority in that regard was narrowly circumscribed. He was permitted \xe2\x80\x9cto certify to the correctness of a copy of a record kept in his office,\xe2\x80\x9d but had \xe2\x80\x9cno authority to furnish, as evidence for the trial of a lawsuit, his interpretation of what the record contains or shows, or to certify to its substance or effect.\xe2\x80\x9d State v. Wilson, 141 La. 404, 409, 75 So. 95, 97 (1917). See also State v. Champion, 116 N.\xc2\xa0C. 987, 21 S.\xc2\xa0E. 700, 700\xe2\x80\x93701 (1895); 5 J. Wigmore, Evidence \xc2\xa71678 (3d ed. 1940). The dissent suggests that the fact that this exception was \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98narrowly circumscribed\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d makes no difference. See post, at 20. To the contrary, it makes all the difference in the world. It shows that even the line of cases establishing the one narrow exception the dissent has been able to identify simultaneously vindicates the general rule applicable to the present case. A clerk could by affidavit authenticate or provide a copy of an otherwise admissible record, but could not do what the analysts did here: create a record for the sole purpose of providing evidence against a defendant.[Footnote 8]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Far more probative here are those cases in which the prosecution sought to admit into evidence a clerk\xe2\x80\x99s certificate attesting to the fact that the clerk had searched for a particular relevant record and failed to find it. Like the testimony of the analysts in this case, the clerk\xe2\x80\x99s statement would serve as substantive evidence against the defendant whose guilt depended on the nonexistence of the record for which the clerk searched. Although the clerk\xe2\x80\x99s certificate would qualify as an official record under respondent\xe2\x80\x99s definition\xe2\x80\x94it was prepared by a public officer in the regular course of his official duties\xe2\x80\x94and although the clerk was certainly not a \xe2\x80\x9cconventional witness\xe2\x80\x9d under the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s approach, the clerk was nonetheless subject to confrontation. See People v. Bromwich, 200 N.\xc2\xa0Y. 385, 388\xe2\x80\x93389, 93 N.\xc2\xa0E. 933, 934 (1911); People v. Goodrode, 132 Mich. 542, 547, 94 N.\xc2\xa0W. 14, 16 (1903); Wigmore, supra, \xc2\xa71678.[Footnote 9]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent also misunderstands the relationship between the business-and-official-records hearsay exceptions and the Confrontation Clause. As we stated in Crawford: \xe2\x80\x9cMost of the hearsay exceptions covered statements that by their nature were not testimonial\xe2\x80\x94for example, business records or statements in furtherance of a conspiracy.\xe2\x80\x9d 541 U. S., at 56. Business and public records are generally admissible absent confrontation not because they qualify under an exception to the hearsay rules, but because\xe2\x80\x94having been created for the administration of an entity\xe2\x80\x99s affairs and not for the purpose of establishing or proving some fact at trial\xe2\x80\x94they are not testimonial. Whether or not they qualify as business or official records, the analysts\xe2\x80\x99 statements here\xe2\x80\x94prepared specifically for use at petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s trial\xe2\x80\x94were testimony against petitioner, and the analysts were subject to confrontation under the Sixth Amendment.

\n

E

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent asserts that we should find no Confrontation Clause violation in this case because petitioner had the ability to subpoena the analysts. But that power\xe2\x80\x94whether pursuant to state law or the Compulsory Process Clause\xe2\x80\x94is no substitute for the right of confrontation. Unlike the Confrontation Clause, those provisions are of no use to the defendant when the witness is unavailable or simply refuses to appear. See, e.g., Davis, 547 U. S., at 820 (\xe2\x80\x9c[The witness] was subpoenaed, but she did not appear at \xe2\x80\xa6 trial\xe2\x80\x9d). Converting the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s duty under the Confrontation Clause into the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s privilege under state law or the Compulsory Process Clause shifts the consequences of adverse-witness no-shows from the State to the accused. More fundamentally, the Confrontation Clause imposes a burden on the prosecution to present its witnesses, not on the defendant to bring those adverse witnesses into court. Its value to the defendant is not replaced by a system in which the prosecution presents its evidence via ex parte affidavits and waits for the defendant to subpoena the affiants if he chooses.

\n

F

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, respondent asks us to relax the requirements of the Confrontation Clause to accommodate the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98necessities of trial and the adversary process.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 59. It is not clear whence we would derive the authority to do so. The Confrontation Clause may make the prosecution of criminals more burdensome, but that is equally true of the right to trial by jury and the privilege against self-incrimination. The Confrontation Clause\xe2\x80\x94like those other constitutional provisions\xe2\x80\x94is binding, and we may not disregard it at our convenience.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We also doubt the accuracy of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s and the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s dire predictions. The dissent, respondent, and its amici highlight the substantial total number of controlled-substance analyses performed by state and federal laboratories in recent years. But only some of those tests are implicated in prosecutions, and only a small fraction of those cases actually proceed to trial. See Brief for Law Professors as Amici Curiae 7\xe2\x80\x938 (nearly 95% of convictions in state and federal courts are obtained via guilty plea).[Footnote 10]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Perhaps the best indication that the sky will not fall after today\xe2\x80\x99s decision is that it has not done so already. Many States have already adopted the constitutional rule we announce today,[Footnote 11] while many others permit the defendant to assert (or forfeit by silence) his Confrontation Clause right after receiving notice of the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s intent to use a forensic analyst\xe2\x80\x99s report, id., at 13\xe2\x80\x9315 (cataloging such state laws). Despite these widespread practices, there is no evidence that the criminal justice system has ground to a halt in the States that, one way or another, empower a defendant to insist upon the analyst\xe2\x80\x99s appearance at trial. Indeed, in Massachusetts itself, a defendant may subpoena the analyst to appear at trial, see Brief for Respondent 57, and yet there is no indication that obstructionist defendants are abusing the privilege.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dissent finds this evidence \xe2\x80\x9cfar less reassuring than promised.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 28. But its doubts rest on two flawed premises. First, the dissent believes that those state statutes \xe2\x80\x9crequiring the defendant to give early notice of his intent to confront the analyst,\xe2\x80\x9d are \xe2\x80\x9cburden-shifting statutes [that] may be invalidated by the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 22, 28\xe2\x80\x9329. That is not so. In their simplest form, notice-and-demand statutes require the prosecution to provide notice to the defendant of its intent to use an analyst\xe2\x80\x99s report as evidence at trial, after which the defendant is given a period of time in which he may object to the admission of the evidence absent the analyst\xe2\x80\x99s appearance live at trial. See, e.g, Ga. Code Ann. \xc2\xa735\xe2\x80\x933\xe2\x80\x93154.1 (2006); Tex. Code Crim. Proc. Ann., Art. 38.41, \xc2\xa74 (Vernon 2005); Ohio Rev. Code Ann. \xc2\xa72925.51(C) (West 2006). Contrary to the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s perception, these statutes shift no burden whatever. The defendant always has the burden of raising his Confrontation Clause objection; notice-and-demand statutes simply govern the time within which he must do so. States are free to adopt procedural rules governing objections. See Wainwright v. Sykes, 433 U. S. 72, 86\xe2\x80\x9387 (1977). It is common to require a defendant to exercise his rights under the Compulsory Process Clause in advance of trial, announcing his intent to present certain witnesses. See Fed. Rules Crim. Proc. 12.1(a), (e), 16(b)(1)(C); Comment: Alibi Notice Rules: The Preclusion Sanction as Procedural Default, 51 U. Chi. L. Rev. 254, 254\xe2\x80\x93255, 281\xe2\x80\x93285 (1984) (discussing and cataloguing State notice-of-alibi rules); Taylor v. Illinois, 484 U. S. 400, 411 (1988); Williams v. Florida, 399 U. S. 78, 81\xe2\x80\x9382 (1970). There is no conceivable reason why he cannot similarly be compelled to exercise his Confrontation Clause rights before trial. See Hinojos-Mendoza v. People, 169 P. 3d 662, 670 (Colo. 2007) (discussing and approving Colorado\xe2\x80\x99s notice-and-demand provision). Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision will not disrupt criminal prosecutions in the many large States whose practice is already in accord with the Confrontation Clause.[Footnote 12]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, the dissent notes that several of the state-court cases that have already adopted this rule did so pursuant to our decision in Crawford, and not \xe2\x80\x9cindependently \xe2\x80\xa6 as a matter of state law.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 28. That may be so. But in assessing the likely practical effects of today\xe2\x80\x99s ruling, it is irrelevant why those courts adopted this rule; it matters only that they did so. It is true that many of these decisions are recent, but if the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s dire predictions were accurate, and given the large number of drug prosecutions at the state level, one would have expected immediate and dramatic results. The absence of such evidence is telling.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But it is not surprising. Defense attorneys and their clients will often stipulate to the nature of the substance in the ordinary drug case. It is unlikely that defense counsel will insist on live testimony whose effect will be merely to highlight rather than cast doubt upon the forensic analysis. Nor will defense attorneys want to antagonize the judge or jury by wasting their time with the appearance of a witness whose testimony defense counsel does not intend to rebut in any fashion.[Footnote 13] The amicus brief filed by District Attorneys in Support of the Commonwealth in the Massachusetts Supreme Court case upon which the Appeals Court here relied said that \xe2\x80\x9cit is almost always the case that [analysts\xe2\x80\x99 certificates] are admitted without objection. Generally, defendants do not object to the admission of drug certificates most likely because there is no benefit to a defendant from such testimony.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for District Attorneys in Support of the Commonwealth in No. SJC\xe2\x80\x9309320 (Mass.), p.\xc2\xa07 (footnote omitted). Given these strategic considerations, and in light of the experience in those States that already provide the same or similar protections to defendants, there is little reason to believe that our decision today will commence the parade of horribles respondent and the dissent predict.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case involves little more than the application of our holding in Crawford v. Washington, 541 U. S. 36. The Sixth Amendment does not permit the prosecution to prove its case via ex parte out-of-court affidavits, and the admission of such evidence against Melendez-Diaz was error.[Footnote 14] We therefore reverse the judgment of the Appeals Court of Massachusetts and remand the case for further proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

\xc2\xa0As the dissent notes, post, at 27, some state statutes, \xe2\x80\x9crequir[e] defense counsel to subpoena the analyst, to show good cause for demanding the analyst\xe2\x80\x99s presence, or even to affirm under oath an intent to cross-examine the analyst.\xe2\x80\x9d We have no occasion today to pass on the constitutionality of every variety of statute commonly given the notice-and-demand label. It suffices to say that what we have referred to as the \xe2\x80\x9csimplest form [of] notice-and-demand statutes,\xe2\x80\x9d supra, at 21, is constitutional; that such provisions are in place in a number of States; and that in those States, and in other States that require confrontation without notice-and-demand, there is no indication that the dire consequences predicted by the dissent have materialized.

\nFootnote 13

\xc2\xa0Contrary to the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion, post, at 24\xe2\x80\x9325, we do not cast aspersions on trial judges, who we trust will not be antagonized by good-faith requests for analysts\xe2\x80\x99 appearance at trial. Nor do we expect defense attorneys to refrain from zealous representation of their clients. We simply do not expect defense attorneys to believe that their clients\xe2\x80\x99 interests (or their own) are furthered by objections to analysts\xe2\x80\x99 reports whose conclusions counsel have no intention of challenging.

\nFootnote 14

\xc2\xa0We of course express no view as to whether the error was harmless. The Massachusetts Court of Appeals did not reach that question and we decline to address it in the first instance. Cf. Coy v. Iowa, 487 U. S. 1012, 1021\xe2\x80\x931022 (1988). In connection with that determination, however, we disagree with the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s contention, post, at 25, that \xe2\x80\x9conly an analyst\xe2\x80\x99s testimony suffices to prove [the] fact\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cthe substance is cocaine.\xe2\x80\x9d Today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, while insisting upon retention of the confrontation requirement, in no way alters the type of evidence (including circumstantial evidence) sufficient to sustain a conviction.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Kennedy, with whom The Chief Justice, Justice Breyer, and Justice Alito join, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court sweeps away an accepted rule governing the admission of scientific evidence. Until today, scientific analysis could be introduced into evidence without testimony from the \xe2\x80\x9canalyst\xe2\x80\x9d who produced it. This rule has been established for at least 90 years. It extends across at least 35 States and six Federal Courts of Appeals. Yet the Court undoes it based on two recent opinions that say nothing about forensic analysts: Crawford v. Washington, 541 U. S. 36 (2004), and Davis v. Washington, 547 U. S. 813 (2006).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is remarkable that the Court so confidently disregards a century of jurisprudence. We learn now that we have misinterpreted the Confrontation Clause\xe2\x80\x94hardly an arcane or seldom-used provision of the Constitution\xe2\x80\x94for the first 218 years of its existence. The immediate systemic concern is that the Court makes no attempt to acknowledge the real differences between laboratory analysts who perform scientific tests and other, more conventional witnesses\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cwitnesses\xe2\x80\x9d being the word the\n

\n

\n\nFramers used in the Confrontation Clause.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Crawford and Davis dealt with ordinary witnesses\xe2\x80\x94women who had seen, and in two cases been the victim of, the crime in question. Those cases stand for the proposition that formal statements made by a conventional witness\xe2\x80\x94one who has personal knowledge of some aspect of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s guilt\xe2\x80\x94may not be admitted without the witness appearing at trial to meet the accused face to face. But Crawford and Davis do not say\xe2\x80\x94indeed, could not have said, because the facts were not before the Court\xe2\x80\x94that anyone who makes a testimonial statement is a witness for purposes of the Confrontation Clause, even when that person has, in fact, witnessed nothing to give them personal knowledge of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s guilt.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because Crawford and Davis concerned typical witnesses, the Court should have done the sensible thing and limited its holding to witnesses as so defined. Indeed, as Justice Thomas warned in his opinion in Davis, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s approach has become \xe2\x80\x9cdisconnected from history and unnecessary to prevent abuse.\xe2\x80\x9d 547 U. S., at 838. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on the word \xe2\x80\x9ctestimonial\xe2\x80\x9d is of little help, of course, for that word does not appear in the text of the Clause.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court dictates to the States, as a matter of constitutional law, an as-yet-undefined set of rules governing what kinds of evidence may be admitted without in-court testimony. Indeed, under today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion the States bear an even more onerous burden than they did before Crawford. Then, the States at least had the guidance of the hearsay rule and could rest assured that \xe2\x80\x9cwhere the evidence f[ell] within a firmly rooted hearsay exception,\xe2\x80\x9d the Confrontation Clause did not bar its admission. Ohio v. Roberts, 448 U. S. 56, 66 (1980) (overruled by Crawford). Now, without guidance from any established body of law, the States can only guess what future rules this Court will distill from the sparse constitutional text. See, e.g., M\xc3\xa9ndez, Crawford v. Washington: A Critique, 57 Stan. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 569, 586\xe2\x80\x93593 (2004) (discussing unanswered questions regarding testimonial statements).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion suggests this will be a body of formalistic and wooden rules, divorced from precedent, common sense, and the underlying purpose of the Clause. Its ruling has vast potential to disrupt criminal procedures that already give ample protections against the misuse of scientific evidence. For these reasons, as more fully explained below, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion elicits my respectful dissent.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\n1

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court says that, before the results of a scientific test may be introduced into evidence, the defendant has the right to confront the \xe2\x80\x9canalyst.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x935. One must assume that this term, though it appears nowhere in the Confrontation Clause, nevertheless has some constitutional substance that now must be elaborated in future cases. There is no accepted definition of analyst, and there is no established precedent to define that term.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Consider how many people play a role in a routine test for the presence of illegal drugs. One person prepares a sample of the drug, places it in a testing machine, and retrieves the machine\xe2\x80\x99s printout\xe2\x80\x94often, a graph showing the frequencies of radiation absorbed by the sample or the masses of the sample\xe2\x80\x99s molecular fragments. See 2 P. Giannelli & E. Imwinkelried, Scientific Evidence \xc2\xa723.03 (4th ed. 2007) (describing common methods of identifying drugs, including infrared spectrophotometry, nuclear magnetic resonance, gas chromatography, and mass spectrometry). A second person interprets the graph the machine prints out\xe2\x80\x94perhaps by comparing that printout with published, standardized graphs of known drugs. Ibid. Meanwhile, a third person\xe2\x80\x94perhaps an independent contractor\xe2\x80\x94has calibrated the machine and, having done so, has certified that the machine is in good working order. Finally, a fourth person\xe2\x80\x94perhaps the laboratory\xe2\x80\x99s director\xe2\x80\x94certifies that his subordinates followed established procedures.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is not at all evident which of these four persons is the analyst to be confronted under the rule the Court announces today. If all are witnesses who must appear for in-court confrontation, then the Court has, for all practical purposes, forbidden the use of scientific tests in criminal trials. As discussed further below, requiring even one of these individuals to testify threatens to disrupt if not end many prosecutions where guilt is clear but a newly found formalism now holds sway. See Part I\xe2\x80\x93C, infra.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is possible to read the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, however, to say that all four must testify. Each one has contributed to the test\xe2\x80\x99s result and has, at least in some respects, made a representation about the test. Person One represents that a pure sample, properly drawn, entered the machine and produced a particular printout. Person Two represents that the printout corresponds to a known drug. Person Three represents that the machine was properly calibrated at the time. Person Four represents that all the others performed their jobs in accord with established procedures.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0And each of the four has power to introduce error. A laboratory technician might adulterate the sample. The independent contractor might botch the machine\xe2\x80\x99s calibration. And so forth. The reasons for these errors may range from animus against the particular suspect or all criminal suspects to unintentional oversight; from gross negligence to good-faith mistake. It is no surprise that a plausible case can be made for deeming each person in the testing process an analyst under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Consider the independent contractor who has calibrated the testing machine. At least in a routine case, where the machine\xe2\x80\x99s result appears unmistakable, that result\xe2\x80\x99s accuracy depends entirely on the machine\xe2\x80\x99s calibration. The calibration, in turn, can be proved only by the contractor\xe2\x80\x99s certification that he or she did the job properly. That certification appears to be a testimonial statement under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s definition: It is a formal, out-of-court statement, offered for the truth of the matter asserted, and made for the purpose of later prosecution. See ante, at 3\xe2\x80\x935. It is not clear, under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling, why the independent contractor is not also an analyst.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Consider the person who interprets the machine\xe2\x80\x99s printout. His or her interpretation may call for the exercise of professional judgment in close cases. See Giannelli & Imwinkelried, supra. If we assume no person deliberately introduces error, this interpretive step is the one most likely to permit human error to affect the test\xe2\x80\x99s result. This exercise of judgment might make this participant an analyst. The Court implies as much. See ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0And we must yet consider the laboratory director who certifies the ultimate results. The director is arguably the most effective person to confront for revealing any ambiguity in findings, variations in procedures, or problems in the office, as he or she is most familiar with the standard procedures, the office\xe2\x80\x99s variations, and problems in prior cases or with particular analysts. The prosecution may seek to introduce his or her certification into evidence. The Court implies that only those statements that are actually entered into evidence require confrontation. See ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x935. This could mean that the director is also an analyst, even if his or her certification relies upon or restates work performed by subordinates.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court offers no principles or historical precedent to determine which of these persons is the analyst. All contribute to the test result. And each is equally remote from the scene, has no personal stake in the outcome, does not even know the accused, and is concerned only with the performance of his or her role in conducting the test.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It could be argued that the only analyst who must testify is the person who signed the certificate. Under this view, a laboratory could have one employee sign certificates and appear in court, which would spare all the other analysts this burden. But the Court has already rejected this arrangement. The Court made clear in Davis that it will not permit the testimonial statement of one witness to enter into evidence through the in-court testimony of a second:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e do not think it conceivable that the protections of the Confrontation Clause can readily be evaded by having a note-taking policeman [here, the laboratory employee who signs the certificate] recite the unsworn hearsay testimony of the declarant [here, the analyst who performs the actual test], instead of having the declarant sign a deposition. Indeed, if there is one point for which no case\xe2\x80\x94English or early American, state or federal\xe2\x80\x94can be cited, that is it.\xe2\x80\x9d 547 U. S., at 826.

\n

\nUnder this logic, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding cannot be cabined to the person who signs the certificates. If the signatory is restating the testimonial statements of the true analysts\xe2\x80\x94whoever they might be\xe2\x80\x94then those analysts, too, must testify in person.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision demonstrates that even in the narrow category of scientific tests that identify a drug, the Court cannot define with any clarity who the analyst is. Outside this narrow category, the range of other scientific tests that may be affected by the Court\xe2\x80\x99s new confrontation right is staggering. See, e.g., Comment, Toward a Definition of \xe2\x80\x9cTestimonial\xe2\x80\x9d: How Autopsy Reports Do Not Embody the Qualities of a Testimonial Statement, 96 Cal. L. Rev. 1093, 1094, 1115 (2008) (noting that every court post-Crawford has held that autopsy reports are not testimonial, and warning that a contrary rule would \xe2\x80\x9ceffectively functio[n] as a statute of limitations for murder\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is difficult to confine at this point the damage the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding will do in other contexts. Consider just two\xe2\x80\x94establishing the chain of custody and authenticating a copy of a document.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is the obligation of the prosecution to establish the chain of custody for evidence sent to testing laboratories\xe2\x80\x94that is, to establish \xe2\x80\x9cthe identity and integrity of physical evidence by tracing its continuous whereabouts.\xe2\x80\x9d 23 C.\xc2\xa0J.\xc2\xa0S., Criminal Law \xc2\xa71142, p. 66 (2008). Meeting this obligation requires representations\xe2\x80\x94that one officer retrieved the evidence from the crime scene, that a second officer checked it into an evidence locker, that a third officer verified the locker\xe2\x80\x99s seal was intact, and so forth. The iron logic of which the Court is so enamored would seem to require in-court testimony from each human link in the chain of custody. That, of course, has never been the law. See, e.g., United States v. Lott, 854 F.\xc2\xa02d 244, 250 (CA7 1988) (\xe2\x80\x9c[G]aps in the chain [of custody] normally go to the weight of the evidence rather than its admissibility\xe2\x80\x9d); 29A Am. Jur. 2d, Evidence \xc2\xa7962, p. 269 (2009) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe fact that one of the persons in control of a fungible substance does not testify at trial does not, without more, make the substance or testimony relating to it inadmissible\xe2\x80\x9d); C.\xc2\xa0J.\xc2\xa0S., supra, \xc2\xa71142, at 67 (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is generally not necessary that every witness who handled the evidence testify\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is no answer for the Court to say that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t is up to the prosecution to decide what steps in the chain of custody are so crucial as to require evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 5, n.\xc2\xa01. The case itself determines which links in the chain are crucial\xe2\x80\x94not the prosecution. In any number of cases, the crucial link in the chain will not be available to testify\nand so the evidence will be excluded for lack of a proper foundation.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Consider another context in which the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding may cause disruption: The long-accepted practice of authenticating copies of documents by means of a certificate from the document\xe2\x80\x99s custodian stating that the copy is accurate. See, e.g., Fed. Rule Evid. 902(4) (in order to be self-authenticating, a copy of a public record must be \xe2\x80\x9ccertified as correct by the custodian\xe2\x80\x9d); Rule 902(11) (business record must be \xe2\x80\x9caccompanied by a written declaration of its custodian\xe2\x80\x9d). Under one possible reading of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, recordkeepers will be required to testify. So far, courts have not read Crawford and Davis to impose this largely meaningless requirement. See, e.g., United States v. Adefehinti, 510 F.\xc2\xa03d 319, 327\xe2\x80\x93328 (CADC 2008) (certificates authenticating bank records may be admitted without confrontation); United States v. Ellis, 460 F.\xc2\xa03d 920, 927 (CA7 2006) (certificate authenticating hospital records). But the breadth of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling today, and its undefined scope, may well be such that these courts now must be deemed to have erred. The risk of that consequence ought to tell us that something is very wrong with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because the Court is driven by nothing more than a wooden application of the Crawford and Davis definition of \xe2\x80\x9ctestimonial,\xe2\x80\x9d divorced from any guidance from history, precedent, or common sense, there is no way to predict the future applications of today\xe2\x80\x99s holding. Surely part of the justification for the Court\xe2\x80\x99s formalism must lie in its predictability. There is nothing predictable here, however, other than the uncertainty and disruption that now must ensue.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0With no precedent to guide us, let us assume that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s analyst is the person who interprets the machine\xe2\x80\x99s printout. This result makes no sense. The Confrontation Clause is not designed, and does not serve, to detect errors in scientific tests. That should instead be done by conducting a new test. Or, if a new test is impossible, the defendant may call his own expert to explain to the jury the test\xe2\x80\x99s flaws and the dangers of relying on it. And if, in an extraordinary case, the particular analyst\xe2\x80\x99s testimony is necessary to the defense, then, of course, the defendant may subpoena the analyst. The Court frets that the defendant may be unable to do so \xe2\x80\x9cwhen the [analyst] is unavailable or simply refuses to appear.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 19. But laboratory analysts are not difficult to locate or to compel. As discussed below, analysts already devote considerable time to appearing in court when subpoenaed to do so. See Part I\xe2\x80\x93C, infra; see also Brief for State of Alabama et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 26\xe2\x80\x9328. Neither the Court, petitioner, nor amici offer any reason to believe that defendants have trouble subpoenaing analysts in cases where the analysts\xe2\x80\x99 in-court testimony is necessary.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The facts of this case illustrate the formalistic and pointless nature of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reading of the Clause. Petitioner knew, well in advance of trial, that the Commonwealth would introduce the tests against him. The bags of cocaine were in court, available for him to test, and entered into evidence. Yet petitioner made no effort, before or during trial, to mount a defense against the analysts\xe2\x80\x99 results. Petitioner could have challenged the tests\xe2\x80\x99 reliability by seeking discovery concerning the testing methods used or the qualifications of the laboratory analysts. See Mass. Rule Crim. Proc.\xc2\xa014(a)(2) (2009). He did not do so. Petitioner could have sought to conduct his own test. See Rule 41. Again, he did not seek a test; indeed, he did not argue that the drug was not cocaine. Rather than dispute the authenticity of the samples tested or the accuracy of the tests performed, petitioner argued to the jury that the prosecution had not shown that he had possessed or dealt in the drugs.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Despite not having prepared a defense to the analysts\xe2\x80\x99 results, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s counsel made what can only be described as a pro forma objection to admitting the results without in-court testimony, presumably from one particular analyst. Today the Court, by deciding that this objection should have been sustained, transforms the Confrontation Clause from a sensible procedural protection into a distortion of the criminal justice system.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is difficult to perceive how the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding will advance the purposes of the Confrontation Clause. One purpose of confrontation is to impress upon witnesses the gravity of their conduct. See Coy v. Iowa, 487 U. S. 1012, 1019\xe2\x80\x931020 (1988). A witness, when brought to face the person his or her words condemn, might refine, reformulate, reconsider, or even recant earlier statements. See ibid. A further purpose is to alleviate the danger of one-sided interrogations by adversarial government officials who might distort a witness\xe2\x80\x99s testimony. The Clause guards against this danger by bringing the interrogation into the more neutral and public forum of the courtroom. See Maryland v. Craig, 497 U. S. 836, 869\xe2\x80\x93870 (1990) (Scalia, J., dissenting) (discussing the \xe2\x80\x9cvalue of the confrontation right in guarding against a child\xe2\x80\x99s distorted or coerced recollections\xe2\x80\x9d); see also 96 Cal. L.\xc2\xa0Rev., supra, at 1120\xe2\x80\x931122 (\xe2\x80\x9cDuring private law-enforcement questioning, police officers or prosecutors can exert pressure on the witness without a high risk of being discovered. Courtroom questioning, in contrast, is public and performed in front of the jury, judge and defendant. Pressure is therefore harder to exert in court\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But neither purpose is served by the rule the Court announces today. It is not plausible that a laboratory analyst will retract his or her prior conclusion upon catching sight of the defendant the result condemns. After all, the analyst is far removed from the particular defendant and, indeed, claims no personal knowledge of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s guilt. And an analyst performs hundreds if not thousands of tests each year and will not remember a particular test or the link it had to the defendant.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This is not to say that analysts are infallible. They are not. It may well be that if the State does not introduce the machine printout or the raw results of a laboratory analysis; if it does not call an expert to interpret a test, particularly if that test is complex or little known; if it does not establish the chain of custody and the reliability of the laboratory; then the State will have failed to meet its burden of proof. That result follows because the State must prove its case beyond a reasonable doubt, without relying on presumptions, unreliable hearsay, and the like. See United States v. United States Gypsum Co., 438 U. S. 422, 446 (1978) (refusing to permit a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98conclusive presumption [of intent],\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d which \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98would effectively eliminate intent as an ingredient of the offense\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Morissette v. United States, 342 U. S. 246, 274\xe2\x80\x93275 (1952)). The State must permit the defendant to challenge the analyst\xe2\x80\x99s result. See Holmes v. South Carolina, 547 U. S. 319, 331 (2006) (affirming the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s right to \xe2\x80\x9chave a meaningful opportunity to present a complete defense\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). The rules of evidence, including those governing reliability under hearsay principles and the latitude to be given expert witnesses; the rules against irrebutable presumptions; and the overriding principle that the prosecution must make its case beyond a reasonable doubt\xe2\x80\x94all these are part of the protections for the accused. The States, however, have some latitude in determining how these rules should be defined.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Confrontation Clause addresses who must testify. It simply does not follow, however, that this clause, in lieu of the other rules set forth above, controls who the prosecution must call on every issue. Suppose, for instance, that the defense challenges the procedures for a secure chain of custody for evidence sent to a lab and then returned to the police. The defense has the right to call its own witnesses to show that the chain of custody is not secure. But that does not mean it can demand that, in the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s case in chief, each person who is in the chain of custody\xe2\x80\x94and who had an undoubted opportunity to taint or tamper with the evidence\xe2\x80\x94must be called by the prosecution under the Confrontation Clause. And the same is true with lab technicians.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Confrontation Clause is simply not needed for these matters. Where, as here, the defendant does not even dispute the accuracy of the analyst\xe2\x80\x99s work, confrontation adds nothing.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For the sake of these negligible benefits, the Court threatens to disrupt forensic investigations across the country and to put prosecutions nationwide at risk of dismissal based on erratic, all-too-frequent instances when a particular laboratory technician, now invested by the Court\xe2\x80\x99s new constitutional designation as the analyst, simply does not or cannot appear.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Consider first the costs today\xe2\x80\x99s decision imposes on criminal trials. Our own Court enjoys weeks, often months, of notice before cases are argued. We receive briefs well in advance. The argument itself is ordered. A busy trial court, by contrast, must consider not only attorneys\xe2\x80\x99 schedules but also those of witnesses and juries. Trial courts have huge caseloads to be processed within strict time limits. Some cases may unexpectedly plead out at the last minute; others, just as unexpectedly, may not. Some juries stay out longer than predicted; others must be reconstituted. An analyst cannot hope to be the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s top priority in scheduling. The analyst must instead face the prospect of waiting for days in a hallway outside the courtroom before being called to offer testimony that will consist of little more than a rote recital of the written report. See Part I\xe2\x80\x93B, supra.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As matters stood before today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, analysts already spent considerable time appearing as witnesses in those few cases where the defendant, unlike petitioner in this case, contested the analyst\xe2\x80\x99s result and subpoenaed the analyst. See Brief for Alabama et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 26\xe2\x80\x9328 (testifying takes time); ante, at 23 (before today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, it was \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98almost always the case that analysts\xe2\x80\x99 certificates [we]re admitted without objection\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in Massachusetts courts). By requiring analysts also to appear in the far greater number of cases where defendants do not dispute the analyst\xe2\x80\x99s result, the Court imposes enormous costs on the administration of justice.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Setting aside, for a moment, all the other crimes for which scientific evidence is required, consider the costs the Court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling will impose on state drug prosecutions alone. In 2004, the most recent year for which data are available, drug possession and trafficking resulted in 362,850 felony convictions in state courts across the country. See Dept. of Justice, Bureau of Justice Statistics, M. Durose & P. Langan, Felony Sentences in State Courts 2004, p. 2 (July 2007). Roughly 95% of those convictions were products of plea bargains, see id., at 1, which means that state courts saw more than 18,000 drug trials in a single year.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The analysts responsible for testing the drugs at issue in those cases now bear a crushing burden. For example, the district attorney in Philadelphia prosecuted 25,000 drug crimes in 2007. Brief for National Dist. Attorneys Association et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 12\xe2\x80\x9313. Assuming that number remains the same, and assuming that 95% of the cases end in a plea bargain, each of the city\xe2\x80\x99s 18 drug analysts, ibid., will be required to testify in more than 69 trials next year. Cleveland\xe2\x80\x99s district attorney prosecuted 14,000 drug crimes in 2007. Ibid. Assuming that number holds, and that 95% of the cases end in a plea bargain, each of the city\xe2\x80\x99s 6 drug analysts (two of whom work only part time) must testify in 117 drug cases next year. Id., at 13.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Federal Government may face even graver difficulties than the States because its operations are so widespread. For example, the FBI laboratory at Quantico, Virginia, supports federal, state, and local investigations across the country. Its 500 employees conduct over one million scientific tests each year. Dept. of Justice, FBI Laboratory 2007, Message from the FBI Laboratory Director, http://www.fbi.gov/hq/lab/lab2007/labannual07.pdf (as visited June 22, 2009, and available in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file). The Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision means that before any of those million tests reaches a jury, at least one of the laboratory\xe2\x80\x99s analysts must board a plane, find his or her way to an unfamiliar courthouse, and sit there waiting to read aloud notes made months ago.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court purchases its meddling with the Confrontation Clause at a dear price, a price not measured in taxpayer dollars alone. Guilty defendants will go free, on the most technical grounds, as a direct result of today\xe2\x80\x99s decision, adding nothing to the truth-finding process. The analyst will not always make it to the courthouse in time. He or she may be ill; may be out of the country; may be unable to travel because of inclement weather; or may at that very moment be waiting outside some other courtroom for another defendant to exercise the right the Court invents today. If for any reason the analyst cannot make it to the courthouse in time, then, the Court holds, the jury cannot learn of the analyst\xe2\x80\x99s findings (unless, by some unlikely turn of events, the defendant previously cross-examined the analyst). Ante, at 3. The result, in many cases, will be that the prosecution cannot meet its burden of proof, and the guilty defendant goes free on a technicality that, because it results in an acquittal, cannot be reviewed on appeal.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding is a windfall to defendants, one that is unjustified by any demonstrated deficiency in trials, any well-understood historical requirement, or any established constitutional precedent.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0All of the problems with today\xe2\x80\x99s decision\xe2\x80\x94the imprecise definition of \xe2\x80\x9canalyst,\xe2\x80\x9d the lack of any perceptible benefit, the heavy societal costs\xe2\x80\x94would be of no moment if the Constitution did, in fact, require the Court to rule as it does today. But the Constitution does not.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s fundamental mistake is to read the Confrontation Clause as referring to a kind of out-of-court statement\xe2\x80\x94namely, a testimonial statement\xe2\x80\x94that must be excluded from evidence. The Clause does not refer to kinds of statements. Nor does the Clause contain the word \xe2\x80\x9ctestimonial.\xe2\x80\x9d The text, instead, refers to kinds of persons, namely, to \xe2\x80\x9cwitnesses against\xe2\x80\x9d the defendant. Laboratory analysts are not \xe2\x80\x9cwitnesses against\xe2\x80\x9d the defendant as those words would have been understood at the framing. There is simply no authority for this proposition.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Instead, the Clause refers to a conventional \xe2\x80\x9cwitness\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94meaning one who witnesses (that is, perceives) an event that gives him or her personal knowledge of some aspect of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s guilt. Both Crawford and Davis concerned just this kind of ordinary witness\xe2\x80\x94and nothing in the Confrontation Clause\xe2\x80\x99s text, history, or precedent justifies the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to expand those cases.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Clause states: \xe2\x80\x9cIn all criminal prosecutions, the accused shall enjoy the right . . . to be confronted with the witnesses against him.\xe2\x80\x9d U. S. Const., Amdt. 6. Though there is \xe2\x80\x9cvirtually no evidence of what the drafters of the Confrontation Clause intended it to mean,\xe2\x80\x9d White v. Illinois, 502 U. S. 346, 359 (1992) (Thomas, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment), it is certain the Framers did not contemplate that an analyst who conducts a scientific test far removed from the crime would be considered a \xe2\x80\x9cwitnes[s] against\xe2\x80\x9d the defendant.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Framers were concerned with a typical witness\xe2\x80\x94one who perceived an event that gave rise to a personal belief in some aspect of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s guilt. There is no evidence that the Framers understood the Clause to extend to unconventional witnesses. As discussed below, there is significant evidence to the contrary. See Part II\xe2\x80\x93B, infra. In these circumstances, the historical evidence in support of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s position is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98too meager\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0to form a solid basis in history, preceding and contemporaneous with the framing of the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Boumediene v. Bush, 553 U. S. ___, ___ (2008) (slip op., at 22) (quoting Reid v. Covert, 354 U. S. 1, 64 (1957) (Frankfurter, J., concurring in result)). The Court goes dangerously wrong when it bases its constitutional interpretation upon historical guesswork.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The infamous treason trial of Sir Walter Raleigh provides excellent examples of the kinds of witnesses to whom the Confrontation Clause refers. Raleigh\xe2\x80\x99s Case, 2 How. St. Tr. 1 (1603); see Crawford, 541 U. S., at 44\xe2\x80\x9345 (Raleigh\xe2\x80\x99s trial informs our understanding of the Clause because it was, at the time of the framing, one of the \xe2\x80\x9cmost notorious instances\xe2\x80\x9d of the abuse of witnesses\xe2\x80\x99 out-of-court statements); ante, at 9 (same). Raleigh\xe2\x80\x99s accusers claimed to have heard Raleigh speak treason, so they were witnesses in the conventional sense. We should limit the Confrontation Clause to witnesses like those in Raleigh\xe2\x80\x99s trial.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court today expands the Clause to include laboratory analysts, but analysts differ from ordinary witnesses in at least three significant ways. First, a conventional witness recalls events observed in the past, while an analyst\xe2\x80\x99s report contains near-contemporaneous observations of the test. An observation recorded at the time it is made is unlike the usual act of testifying. A typical witness must recall a previous event that he or she perceived just once, and thus may have misperceived or misremembered. But an analyst making a contemporaneous observation need not rely on memory; he or she instead reports the observations at the time they are made. We gave this consideration substantial weight in Davis. There, the \xe2\x80\x9cprimary purpose\xe2\x80\x9d of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s 911 call was \xe2\x80\x9cto enable police assistance to meet an ongoing emergency,\xe2\x80\x9d rather than \xe2\x80\x9cto establish or prove past events potentially relevant to later criminal prosecution.\xe2\x80\x9d 547 U. S., at 822, 827. See also People v. Geier, 41 Cal. 4th 555, 605\xe2\x80\x93609, 161 P.\xc2\xa03d 104, 139\xe2\x80\x93141 (2007). The Court cites no authority for its holding that an observation recorded at the time it is made is an act of \xe2\x80\x9cwitness[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d for purposes of the Confrontation Clause.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, an analyst observes neither the crime nor any human action related to it. Often, the analyst does not know the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s identity, much less have personal knowledge of an aspect of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s guilt. The analyst\xe2\x80\x99s distance from the crime and the defendant, in both space and time, suggests the analyst is not a witness against the defendant in the conventional sense.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Third, a conventional witness responds to questions under interrogation. See, e.g., Raleigh\xe2\x80\x99s Case, supra, at 15\xe2\x80\x9320. But laboratory tests are conducted according to scientific protocols; they are not dependent upon or controlled by interrogation of any sort. Put differently, out-of-court statements should only \xe2\x80\x9crequire confrontation if they are produced by, or with the involvement of, adversarial government officials responsible for investigating and prosecuting crime.\xe2\x80\x9d 96 Cal. L.\xc2\xa0Rev., at 1118. There is no indication that the analysts here\xe2\x80\x94who work for the State Laboratory Institute, a division of the Massachusetts Department of Public Health\xe2\x80\x94were adversarial to petitioner. Nor is there any evidence that adversarial officials played a role in formulating the analysts\xe2\x80\x99 certificates.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Rather than acknowledge that it expands the Confrontation Clause beyond conventional witnesses, the Court relies on our recent opinions in Crawford and Davis. Ante, at 3\xe2\x80\x935. The Court assumes, with little analysis, that Crawford and Davis extended the Clause to any person who makes a \xe2\x80\x9ctestimonial\xe2\x80\x9d statement. But the Court\xe2\x80\x99s confident tone cannot disguise the thinness of these two reeds. Neither Crawford nor Davis considered whether the Clause extends to persons far removed from the crime who have no connection to the defendant. Instead, those cases concerned conventional witnesses. Davis, supra, at 826\xe2\x80\x93830 (witnesses were victims of defendants\xe2\x80\x99 assaults); Crawford, supra, at 38 (witness saw defendant stab\nvictim).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is true that Crawford and Davis employed the term \xe2\x80\x9ctestimonial,\xe2\x80\x9d and thereby suggested that any testimonial statement, by any person, no matter how distant from the defendant and the crime, is subject to the Confrontation Clause. But that suggestion was not part of the holding of Crawford or Davis. Those opinions used the adjective \xe2\x80\x9ctestimonial\xe2\x80\x9d to avoid the awkward phrasing required by reusing the noun \xe2\x80\x9cwitness.\xe2\x80\x9d The Court today transforms that turn of phrase into a new and sweeping legal rule, by holding that anyone who makes a formal statement for the purpose of later prosecution\xe2\x80\x94no matter how removed from the crime\xe2\x80\x94must be considered a \xe2\x80\x9cwitness against\xe2\x80\x9d the defendant. Ante, at 3\xe2\x80\x935. The Court cites no authority to justify this expansive new interpretation.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0No historical evidence supports the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that the Confrontation Clause was understood to extend beyond conventional witnesses to include analysts who conduct scientific tests far removed from the crime and the defendant. Indeed, what little evidence there is contradicts this interpretation.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Though the Framers had no forensic scientists, they did use another kind of unconventional witness\xe2\x80\x94the copyist. A copyist\xe2\x80\x99s work may be as essential to a criminal prosecution as the forensic analyst\xe2\x80\x99s. To convict a man of bigamy, for example, the State often requires his marriage records. See, e.g., Williams v. State, 54 Ala. 131, 134, 135 (1875); State v. Potter, 52 Vt. 33, 38 (1879). But if the original records cannot be taken from the archive, the prosecution must rely on copies of those records, made for the purpose of introducing the copies into evidence at trial. See ibid. In that case, the copyist\xe2\x80\x99s honesty and diligence are just as important as the analyst\xe2\x80\x99s here. If the copyist falsifies a copy, or even misspells a name or transposes a date, those flaws could lead the jury to convict. Because so much depends on his or her honesty and diligence, the copyist often prepares an affidavit certifying that the copy is true and accurate.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Such a certificate is beyond question a testimonial statement under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s definition: It is a formal out-of-court statement offered for the truth of two matters (the copyist\xe2\x80\x99s honesty and the copy\xe2\x80\x99s accuracy), and it is prepared for a criminal prosecution. \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0During the Framers\xe2\x80\x99 era copyists\xe2\x80\x99 affidavits were accepted without hesitation by American courts. See, e.g., United States v. Percheman, 7 Pet. 51, 85 (1833) (opinion for the Court by Marshall, C.\xc2\xa0J.); see also Advisory Committee\xe2\x80\x99s Note on Fed. Rule Evid. 902(4), 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. App., p. 390 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe common law\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0recognized the procedure of authenticating copies of public records by certificate\xe2\x80\x9d); 5 J. Wigmore, Evidence \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71677, 1678 (J. Chadbourn rev. 1974). And courts admitted copyists\xe2\x80\x99 affidavits in criminal as well as civil trials. See Williams, supra; Potter, supra. This demonstrates that the framing generation, in contrast to the Court today, did not consider the Confrontation Clause to require in-court confrontation of unconventional authors of testimonial statements.

\n

\n\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court attempts to explain away this historical exception to its rule by noting that a copyist\xe2\x80\x99s authority is \xe2\x80\x9cnarrowly circumscribed.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16. But the Court does not explain why that matters, nor, if it does matter, why laboratory analysts\xe2\x80\x99 authority should not also be deemed \xe2\x80\x9cnarrowly circumscribed\xe2\x80\x9d so that they, too, may be excused from testifying. And drawing these fine distinctions cannot be squared with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s avowed allegiance to formalism. Determining whether a witness\xe2\x80\x99 authority is \xe2\x80\x9cnarrowly circumscribed\xe2\x80\x9d has nothing to do with Crawford\xe2\x80\x99s testimonial framework. It instead appears much closer to the pre-Crawford rule of Ohio v. Roberts, under which a statement could be admitted without testimony if it \xe2\x80\x9cbears adequate indicia of reliability.\xe2\x80\x9d 448 U. S., at 66 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In keeping with the traditional understanding of the Confrontation Clause, this Court in Dowdell v. United States, 221 U. S. 325 (1911), rejected a challenge to the use of certificates, sworn out by a clerk of court, a trial judge, and a court reporter, stating that defendants had been present at trial. Those certificates, like a copyist\xe2\x80\x99s certificate, met every requirement of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s current definition of \xe2\x80\x9ctestimonial.\xe2\x80\x9d In rejecting the defendants\xe2\x80\x99 claim that use of the certificates violated the Confrontation Clause, the Court in Dowdell explained that the officials who executed the certificates \xe2\x80\x9cwere not witnesses against the accused\xe2\x80\x9d because they \xe2\x80\x9cwere not asked to testify to facts concerning [the defendants\xe2\x80\x99] guilt or innocence.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 330. Indeed, as recently as Davis, the Court reaffirmed Dowdell. 547 U. S., at 825.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0By insisting that every author of a testimonial statement appear for confrontation, on pain of excluding the statement from evidence, the Court does violence to the Framers\xe2\x80\x99 sensible, and limited, conception of the right to confront \xe2\x80\x9cwitnesses against\xe2\x80\x9d the defendant.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In addition to lacking support in historical practice or in this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision is also contrary to authority extending over at least 90 years, 35 States, and six Federal Courts of Appeals.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Almost 100 years ago three state supreme courts held that their state constitutions did not require analysts to testify in court. In a case much like this one, the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court upheld the admission of a certificate stating that the liquid seized from the defendant contained alcohol, even though the author of the certificate did not testify. Commonwealth v. Slavski, 245 Mass. 405, 413, 140 N.\xc2\xa0E. 465, 467 (1923). The highest courts in Connecticut and Virginia reached similar conclusions under their own constitutions. State v. Torello, 103 Conn. 511, 131 A. 429 (1925); Bracey v. Commonwealth, 119 Va. 867, 89 S.\xc2\xa0E. 144 (1916). Just two state courts appear to have read a state constitution to require a contrary result. State v. Clark, 290 Mont. 479, 484\xe2\x80\x93489, 964 P.\xc2\xa02d 766, 770\xe2\x80\x93772 (1998) (laboratory drug report requires confrontation under Montana\xe2\x80\x99s Constitution, which is \xe2\x80\x9c[u]nlike its federal counterpart\xe2\x80\x9d); State v. Birchfield, 342 Ore. 624, 157 P.\xc2\xa03d 216 (2007), but see id., at 631\xe2\x80\x93632, 157 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 220 (suggesting that a \xe2\x80\x9ctypical notice requirement\xe2\x80\x9d would be lawful).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As for the Federal Constitution, before Crawford the authority was stronger still: The Sixth Amendment does not require analysts to testify in court. All Federal Courts of Appeals to consider the issue agreed. Sherman v. Scott, 62 F.\xc2\xa03d 136, 139\xe2\x80\x93142 (CA5 1995); Minner v. Kerby, 30 F.\xc2\xa03d 1311, 1313\xe2\x80\x931315 (CA10 1994); United States v. Baker, 855 F.\xc2\xa02d 1353, 1359\xe2\x80\x931360 (CA8 1988); Reardon v. Manson, 806 F.\xc2\xa02d 39 (CA2 1986); Kay v. United States, 255 F.\xc2\xa02d 476, 480\xe2\x80\x93481 (CA4 1958); see also Manocchio v. Moran, 919 F.\xc2\xa02d 770, 777\xe2\x80\x93782 (CA1 1990) (autopsy report stating cause of victim\xe2\x80\x99s death). Some 24 state courts, and the Court of Appeals for the Armed Forces, were in accord. See Appendix A, infra. (Some cases cited in the appendixes concern doctors, coroners, and calibrators rather than laboratory analysts, but their reasoning is much the same.) Eleven more state courts upheld burden-shifting statutes that reduce, if not eliminate, the right to confrontation by requiring the defendant to take affirmative steps prior to trial to summon the analyst. See ibid. Because these burden-shifting statutes may be invalidated by the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning, these 11 decisions, too, appear contrary to today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion. See Part III\xe2\x80\x93B, infra. Most of the remaining States, far from endorsing the Court\xe2\x80\x99s view, appear not to have addressed the question prior to Crawford. Against this weight of authority, the Court proffers just two cases from intermediate state courts of appeals. Ante, at 6\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On a practical level, today\xe2\x80\x99s ruling would cause less disruption if the States\xe2\x80\x99 hearsay rules had already required analysts to testify. But few States require this. At least sixteen state courts have held that their evidentiary rules permit scientific test results, calibration certificates, and the observations of medical personnel to enter evidence without in-court testimony. See Appendix B, infra. The Federal Courts of Appeals have reached the same conclusion in applying the federal hearsay rule. United States v. Garnett, 122 F.\xc2\xa03d 1016, 1018\xe2\x80\x931019 (CA11 1997) (per curiam); United States v. Gilbert, 774 F.\xc2\xa02d 962, 965 (CA9 1985) (per curiam); United States v. Ware, 247 F.\xc2\xa02d 698, 699\xe2\x80\x93700 (CA7 1957); but see United States v. Oates, 560 F.\xc2\xa02d 45, 82 (CA2 1977) (report prepared by law enforcement not admissible under public-records or business-records exceptions to federal hearsay rule).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The modern trend in the state courts has been away from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s rule and toward the admission of scientific test results without testimony\xe2\x80\x94perhaps because the States have recognized the increasing reliability of scientific testing. See Appendix B, infra (citing cases from three States overruling or limiting previous precedents that had adopted the Court\xe2\x80\x99s rule as a matter of state law). It appears that a mere six courts continue to interpret their States\xe2\x80\x99 hearsay laws to require analysts to testify. See ibid. And, of course, where courts have grounded their decisions in state law, rather than the Constitution, the legislatures in those States have had, until now, the power to abrogate the courts\xe2\x80\x99 interpretation if the costs were shown to outweigh the benefits. Today the Court strips that authority from the States by carving the minority view into the constitutional text.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0State legislatures, and not the Members of this Court, have the authority to shape the rules of evidence. The Court therefore errs when it relies in such great measure on the recent report of the National Academy of Sciences. Ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9314 (discussing National Research Council of the National Academies, Strengthening Forensic Science in the United States: A Path Forward (Prepublication Copy Feb. 2009)). That report is not directed to this Court, but rather to the elected representatives in Congress and the state legislatures, who, unlike Members of this Court, have the power and competence to determine whether scientific tests are unreliable and, if so, whether testimony is the proper solution to the problem.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court rejects the well-established understanding\xe2\x80\x94extending across at least 90 years, 35 States and six Federal Courts of Appeals\xe2\x80\x94that the Constitution does not require analysts to testify in court before their analysis may be introduced into evidence. The only authority on which the Court can rely is its own speculation on the meaning of the word \xe2\x80\x9ctestimonial,\xe2\x80\x9d made in two recent opinions that said nothing about scientific analysis or scientific analysts.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In an attempt to show that the \xe2\x80\x9csky will not fall after today\xe2\x80\x99s decision,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 20, the Court makes three arguments, none of which withstands scrutiny.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In an unconvincing effort to play down the threat that today\xe2\x80\x99s new rule will disrupt or even end criminal prosecutions, the Court professes a hope that defense counsel will decline to raise what will soon be known as the Melendez-Diaz objection. Ante, at 22. The Court bases this expectation on its understanding that defense attorneys surrender constitutional rights because the attorneys do not \xe2\x80\x9cwant to antagonize the judge or jury by wasting their time.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning is troubling on at least two levels. First, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s speculation rests on the apparent belief that our Nation\xe2\x80\x99s trial judges and jurors are unwilling to accept zealous advocacy and that, once \xe2\x80\x9cantagonize[d]\xe2\x80\x9d by it, will punish such advocates with adverse rulings. Ibid. The Court offers no support for this stunning slur on the integrity of the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s courts. It is commonplace for the defense to request, at the conclusion of the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s opening case, a directed verdict of acquittal. If the prosecution has failed to prove an element of the crime\xe2\x80\x94even an element that is technical and rather obvious, such as movement of a car in interstate commerce\xe2\x80\x94then the case must be dismissed. Until today one would not have thought that judges should be angered at the defense for making such motions, nor that counsel has some sort of obligation to avoid being troublesome when the prosecution has not done all the law requires to prove its case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, even if the Court were right to expect trial judges to feel \xe2\x80\x9cantagonize[d]\xe2\x80\x9d by Melendez-Diaz objections and to then vent their anger by punishing the lawyer in some way, there is no authority to support the Court\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion that a lawyer may shirk his or her professional duties just to avoid judicial displeasure. There is good reason why the Court cites no authority for this suggestion\xe2\x80\x94it is contrary to what some of us, at least, have long understood to be defense counsel\xe2\x80\x99s duty to be a zealous advocate for every client. This Court has recognized the bedrock principle that a competent criminal defense lawyer must put the prosecution to its proof:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he adversarial process protected by the Sixth Amendment requires that the accused have \xe2\x80\x98counsel acting in the role of an advocate.\xe2\x80\x99 Anders v. California, 386 U. S. 738, 743 (1967). The right to the effective assistance of counsel is thus the right of the accused to require the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s case to survive the crucible of meaningful adversarial testing. When a true adversarial criminal trial has been conducted \xe2\x80\xa6 the kind of testing envisioned by the Sixth Amendment has occurred. But if the process loses its character as a confrontation between adversaries, the constitutional guarantee is violated.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Cronic, 466 U. S. 648, 656\xe2\x80\x93657 (1984) (footnotes\nomitted).

\n

\nSee also ABA Model Code of Professional Responsibility, Canon 7\xe2\x80\x931, in ABA Compendium of Professional Responsibility Rules and Standards (2008) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe duty of a lawyer, both to his client and to the legal system, is to represent his client zealously within the bounds of the law\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xe2\x80\x9d (footnotes omitted)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The instant case demonstrates how zealous defense counsel will defend their clients. To convict, the prosecution must prove the substance is cocaine. Under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s new rule, apparently only an analyst\xe2\x80\x99s testimony suffices to prove that fact. (Of course there will also be a large universe of other crimes, ranging from homicide to robbery, where scientific evidence is necessary to prove an element.) In cases where scientific evidence is necessary to prove an element of the crime, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s rule requires the prosecution to call the person identified as the analyst; this requirement has become a new prosecutorial duty linked with proving the State\xe2\x80\x99s case beyond a reasonable doubt. Unless the Court is ashamed of its new rule, it is inexplicable that the Court seeks to limit its damage by hoping that defense counsel will be derelict in their duty to insist that the prosecution prove its case. That is simply not the way the adversarial system works.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In any event, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s hope is sure to prove unfounded. The Court surmises that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t is unlikely that defense counsel will insist on live testimony whose effect will be merely to highlight rather than cast doubt upon the forensic analysis.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 22. This optimistic prediction misunderstands how criminal trials work. If the defense does not plan to challenge the test result, \xe2\x80\x9chighlight[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d that result through testimony does not harm the defense as the Court supposes. If the analyst cannot reach the courtroom in time to testify, however, a Melendez-Diaz objection grants the defense a great windfall: The analyst\xe2\x80\x99s work cannot come into evidence. Given the prospect of such a windfall (which may, in and of itself, secure an acquittal) few zealous advocates will pledge, prior to trial, not to raise a Melendez-Diaz objection. Defense counsel will accept the risk that the jury may hear the analyst\xe2\x80\x99s live testimony, in exchange for the chance that the analyst fails to appear and the government\xe2\x80\x99s case collapses. And if, as here, the defense is not that the substance was harmless, but instead that the accused did not possess it, the testimony of the technician is a formalism that does not detract from the defense case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In further support of its unlikely hope, the Court relies on the Brief for Law Professors as Amici Curiae 7\xe2\x80\x938, which reports that nearly 95% of convictions are obtained via guilty plea and thus do not require in-court testimony from laboratory analysts. Ante, at 20. What the Court does not consider is how its holding will alter these statistics. The defense bar today gains the formidable power to require the government to transport the analyst to the courtroom at the time of trial. Zealous counsel will insist upon concessions: a plea bargain, or a more lenient sentence in exchange for relinquishing this remarkable power.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As further reassurance that the \xe2\x80\x9csky will not fall after today\xe2\x80\x99s decision,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 20, the Court notes that many States have enacted burden-shifting statutes that require the defendant to assert his Confrontation Clause right prior to trial or else \xe2\x80\x9cforfeit\xe2\x80\x9d it \xe2\x80\x9cby silence.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The Court implies that by shifting the burden to the defendant to take affirmative steps to produce the analyst, these statutes reduce the burden on the prosecution.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court holds that these burden-shifting statutes are valid because, in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s view, they \xe2\x80\x9cshift no burden whatever.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 21. While this conclusion is welcome, the premise appears flawed. Even what the Court calls the \xe2\x80\x9csimplest form\xe2\x80\x9d of burden-shifting statutes do impose requirements on the defendant, who must make a formal demand, with proper service, well before trial. Some statutes impose more requirements, for instance by requiring defense counsel to subpoena the analyst, to show good cause for demanding the analyst\xe2\x80\x99s presence, or even to affirm under oath an intent to cross-examine the analyst. See generally Metzger, Cheating the Constitution, 59 Vand. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 475, 481\xe2\x80\x93485 (2006). In a future case, the Court may find that some of these more onerous burden-shifting statutes violate the Confrontation Clause because they \xe2\x80\x9cimpos[e] a burden\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0on the defendant to bring \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0adverse witnesses into court.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 19.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The burden-shifting statutes thus provide little reassurance that this case will not impose a meaningless formalism across the board.

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In a further effort to support its assessment that today\xe2\x80\x99s decision will not cause disruption, the Court cites 10 decisions from States that, the Court asserts, \xe2\x80\x9chave already adopted the constitutional rule we announce today.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 20, and n.\xc2\xa011. The Court assures us that \xe2\x80\x9cthere is no evidence that the criminal justice system has ground to a halt in the[se] States.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 20.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On inspection, the citations prove far less reassuring than promised. Seven were decided by courts that considered themselves bound by Crawford. These cases thus offer no support for the Court\xe2\x80\x99s assertion that the state jurists independently \xe2\x80\x9cadopted\xe2\x80\x9d the Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation as a matter of state law. Quite the contrary, the debate in those seven courts was over just how far this Court intended Crawford to sweep. See, e.g., State v. Belvin, 986 So.\xc2\xa02d 516, 526 (Fla. 2008) (Wells, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part) (\xe2\x80\x9cI believe that the majority has extended the Crawford and Davis decisions beyond their intended reach\xe2\x80\x9d (citations omitted)). The Court should correct these courts\xe2\x80\x99 overbroad reading of Crawford, not endorse it. Were the Court to do so, these seven jurisdictions might well change their position.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, because these seven courts only \xe2\x80\x9cadopted\xe2\x80\x9d the Court\xe2\x80\x99s position in the wake of Crawford, their decisions are all quite recent. These States have not yet been subject to the widespread, adverse results of the formalism the Court mandates today.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0 The citations also fail to reassure for a different reason. Five of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s 10 citations\xe2\x80\x94including all 3 pre-Crawford cases\xe2\x80\x94come from States that have reduced the confrontation right. Four States have enacted a burden-shifting statute requiring the defendant to give early notice of his intent to confront the analyst. See Part III\xe2\x80\x93B, supra; Colorado: Hinojos-Mendoza v. People, 169 P. 3d 662, 668\xe2\x80\x93671 (Colo. 2007), Colo. Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa716\xe2\x80\x933\xe2\x80\x93309 (2008) (defendant must give notice 10 days before trial); Georgia: Compare Miller v. State, 266 Ga. 850, 854\xe2\x80\x93855, 472 S. E. 2d 74, 78\xe2\x80\x9379 (1996) (striking down earlier notice statute requiring defendant to show good cause, prior to trial, to call the analyst), with Ga. Code Ann. \xc2\xa735\xe2\x80\x933\xe2\x80\x93154.1 (2006) (defendant must give notice 10 days before trial); Illinois: People v. McClanahan, 191 Ill. 2d 127, 133\xe2\x80\x93134, 729 N. E. 2d 470, 474\xe2\x80\x93475 (2000), Ill. Comp. Stat., ch. 725, \xc2\xa75/115\xe2\x80\x9315 (2006) (defendant must give notice \xe2\x80\x9cwithin 7 days\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9creceipt of the report\xe2\x80\x9d); Oregon: State v. Birchfield, 342 Ore., at 631\xe2\x80\x93632, 157 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 220 (suggesting that a \xe2\x80\x9ctypical notice requirement\xe2\x80\x9d would be lawful), see Ore. Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa7475.235 (2007) (defendant must give notice 15 days before trial). A fifth State, Mississippi, excuses the prosecution from producing the analyst who conducted the test, so long as it produces someone. Compare Barnette v. State, 481 So. 2d 788, 792 (Miss. 1985) (cited by the Court), with McGowen v. State, 859 So. 2d 320, 339\xe2\x80\x93340 (Miss. 2003) (the Sixth Amendment does not require confrontation with the particular analyst who conducted the test). It is possible that neither Mississippi\xe2\x80\x99s practice nor the burden-shifting statutes can be reconciled with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding. See Part III\xe2\x80\x93B, supra. The disruption caused by today\xe2\x80\x99s decision has yet to take place in these States.
