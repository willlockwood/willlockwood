BROWN, WARDEN v. PAYTON

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 03\xe2\x80\x931039.Argued November 10, 2004\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 22, 2005

\n

In the penalty phase of respondent Payton\xe2\x80\x99s trial following his conviction on capital murder and related charges, his counsel presented witnesses who testified that, during the one year and nine months Payton had been incarcerated since his arrest, he had made a sincere commitment to God, participated in prison Bible study and a prison ministry, and had a calming effect on other prisoners. The trial judge gave jury instructions that followed verbatim the text of a California statute, setting forth 11 different factors, labeled (a) through (k), to guide the jury in determining whether to impose a death sentence or life imprisonment. The last such instruction, the so-called factor (k) instruction, directed jurors to consider \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny other circumstance which extenuates the gravity of the crime even though it is not a legal excuse for the crime.\xe2\x80\x9d In his closing, the prosecutor offered jurors his incorrect opinion that factor (k) did not allow them to consider anything that happened after the crime. Although he also told them several times that, in his view, they had not heard any evidence of mitigation, he discussed Payton\xe2\x80\x99s evidence in considerable detail and argued that the circumstances and facts of the case, coupled with Payton\xe2\x80\x99s prior violent acts, outweighed the mitigating effect of Payton\xe2\x80\x99s religious conversion. When the defense objected to the argument, the court admonished the jury that the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s comments were merely argument, but it did not explicitly instruct that the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation was incorrect. Finding the special circumstance of murder in the course of rape, the jury recommended that Payton be sentenced to death, and the judge complied. The California Supreme Court affirmed. Applying Boyde v. California, 494 U. S. 370, which had considered the constitutionality of the identical factor (k) instruction, the state court held that, considering the context of the proceedings, there was no reasonable likelihood that the jury believed it was required to disregard Payton\xe2\x80\x99s mitigating evidence. The Federal District Court disagreed and granted Payton habeas relief, ruling also that the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA) did not apply. The en banc Ninth Circuit affirmed and, like the District Court, held that AEDPA did not apply. On remand from this Court in light of Woodford v. Garceau, 538 U. S. 202, the Ninth Circuit purported to decide the case under the deferential standard AEDPA mandates. It again affirmed, concluding that the California Supreme Court had unreasonably applied Boyde in holding the factor (k) instruction was not unconstitutionally ambiguous in Payton\xe2\x80\x99s case. The error, the court determined, was that the factor (k) instruction did not make it clear to the jury that it could consider the evidence concerning Payton\xe2\x80\x99s postcrime religious conversion and the prosecutor was allowed to urge this erroneous interpretation.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision was contrary to the limits on federal habeas review imposed by AEDPA. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0AEDPA provides that, when a habeas petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claim has been adjudicated on the merits in state court, a federal court may not grant relief unless the state court\xe2\x80\x99s adjudication \xe2\x80\x9cresulted in a decision that was contrary to, or involved an unreasonable application of, clearly established Federal law, as determined by the Supreme Court of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1). A state-court decision is contrary to this Court\xe2\x80\x99s clearly established precedents if it applies a rule that contradicts the governing law set forth in this Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases, or if it confronts a set of facts that is materially indistinguishable from a decision of this Court but reaches a different result. E.g., Williams v. Taylor, 529 U. S. 362, 405. A state-court decision involves an unreasonable application of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s clearly established precedents if the state court applies such precedents to the facts in an objectively unreasonable manner. E.g., ibid. These conditions have not been established. P.\xc2\xa07.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0In light of Boyde, the California Supreme Court cannot be said to have acted unreasonably in declining to distinguish between precrime and postcrime mitigating evidence. The California Supreme Court read Boyde as establishing that factor (k)\xe2\x80\x99s text was broad enough to accommodate Payton\xe2\x80\x99s postcrime mitigating evidence, but the Ninth Circuit held that Boyde\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning did not control in this case because Boyde concerned precrime, not postcrime, mitigation evidence. However, Boyde held that factor (k) directed consideration of any circumstance that might excuse the crime, see 494 U. S., at 382, and it is not unreasonable to believe that a postcrime character transformation could do so. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Even were the Court to assume that the California Supreme Court was incorrect in concluding that the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s argument and remarks did not mislead the jury into believing it could not consider Payton\xe2\x80\x99s mitigation evidence, the state court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion was not unreasonable, and is therefore just the type of decision that AEDPA shields on habeas review. The state court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion was an application of Boyde to similar but not identical facts. Considering the whole context of the proceedings, it was not unreasonable for the state court to determine that the jury most likely believed that the mitigation evidence, while within the factor (k) instruction\xe2\x80\x99s reach, was simply too insubstantial to overcome the arguments for imposing the death penalty; nor was it unreasonable for the state court to rely upon Boyde to support its analysis. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

346 F.\xc2\xa03d 1204, reversed.

JILL L. BROWN, WARDEN, PETITIONER v.\nWILLIAM CHARLES PAYTON

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[March 22, 2005]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Kennedy delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit, convening en banc, granted habeas relief to respondent William Payton. It held that the jury instructions in the penalty phase of his trial for capital murder did not permit consideration of all the mitigation evidence Payton presented. The error, the court determined, was that the general mitigation instruction did not make it clear to the jury that it could consider evidence concerning Payton\xe2\x80\x99s postcrime religious conversion and the prosecutor was allowed to urge this erroneous interpretation. We granted the petition for certiorari, 541 U. S. 1062 (2004), to decide whether the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision was contrary to the limits on federal habeas review imposed by 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d). We now reverse.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1980, while spending the night at a boarding house, Payton raped another boarder, Pamela Montgomery, and then used a butcher knife to stab her to death. Payton proceeded to enter the bedroom of the house\xe2\x80\x99s patron, Patricia Pensinger and to stab her as she slept aside her 10-year-old son, Blaine. When Blaine resisted, Payton started to stab him as well. Payton\xe2\x80\x99s knife blade bent, and he went to the kitchen to retrieve another. Upon the intervention of other boarders, Payton dropped the second knife and fled.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Payton was arrested and tried for the first-degree murder and rape of Pamela Montgomery and for the attempted murders of Patricia and Blaine Pensinger. Payton presented no evidence in the guilt phase of the trial and was convicted on all counts. The trial proceeded to the penalty phase, where the prosecutor introduced evidence of a prior incident when Payton stabbed a girlfriend; a prior conviction for rape; a prior drug-related felony conviction; and evidence of jailhouse conversations in which Payton admitted he had an \xe2\x80\x9curge to kill\xe2\x80\x9d and a \xe2\x80\x9csevere problem with sex and women\xe2\x80\x9d that caused him to view all women as potential victims to \xe2\x80\x9cstab \xe2\x80\xa6 and rape.\xe2\x80\x9d People v. Payton, 3 Cal. 4th 1050, 1058, 839 P.\xc2\xa02d 1035, 1040 (1992) (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Defense counsel concentrated on Payton\xe2\x80\x99s postcrime behavior and presented evidence from eight witnesses. They testified that in the year and nine months Payton spent in prison since his arrest, he had made a sincere commitment to God, participated in prison Bible study classes and a prison ministry, and had a calming effect on other prisoners.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Before the penalty phase closing arguments, the judge held an in-chambers conference with counsel to discuss jury instructions. He proposed to give\xe2\x80\x94and later did give\xe2\x80\x94an instruction which followed verbatim the text of a California statute. Cal. Penal Code Ann. \xc2\xa7190.3 (West 1988). The instruction set forth 11 different factors, labeled (a) through (k), for the jury to \xe2\x80\x9cconsider, take into account and be guided by\xe2\x80\x9d in determining whether to impose a sentence of life imprisonment or death. 1 Cal. Jury Instr., Crim., No. 8.84.1 (4th rev. ed. 1979).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The in-chambers conference considered in particular the last instruction in the series, the so-called factor (k) instruction. Factor (k) was a catchall instruction, in contrast to the greater specificity of the instructions that preceded it. As set forth in the statute, and as explained to the jury, it directed jurors to consider \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny other circumstance which extenuates the gravity of the crime even though it is not a legal excuse for the crime.\xe2\x80\x9d Cal. Penal Code Ann. \xc2\xa7190.3 (West 1988). (The statute has since been amended).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Defense counsel objected to the instruction and asked that it be modified to direct the jury, in more specific terms, to consider evidence of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s character and background. The prosecution, on the other hand, indicated that in its view factor (k) was not intended to encompass evidence concerning a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s background or character. The court agreed with defense counsel that factor (k) was a general instruction covering all mitigating evidence. It declined, however, to modify the wording, in part because the instruction repeated the text of the statute. In addition, the court stated \xe2\x80\x9cI assume you gentlemen, as I said, in your argument can certainly relate\xe2\x80\x94relate back to those factors and certainly can argue the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s character, background, history, mental condition, physical condition; certainly fall into category \xe2\x80\x98k\xe2\x80\x99 and certainly make a clear argument to the jury.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 59.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judge prefaced closing arguments by instructing the jury that what it would hear from counsel was \xe2\x80\x9cnot evidence but argument\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c[you] should rely on your own recollection of the evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 62. In his closing, the prosecutor offered jurors his opinion that factor (k) did not allow them to consider anything that happened \xe2\x80\x9cafter the [crime] or later.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 68. The parties do not now dispute that this was a misstatement of law. The defense objected to the comment and moved for a mistrial, which the trial court denied. The court admonished the jury that the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s comments were merely argument, but it did not explicitly instruct the jury that the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation was incorrect. Id., at 69\xe2\x80\x9370.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although the prosecutor again told the jury several times that, in his view, the jury had not heard any evidence of mitigation, he proceeded to argue that the circumstances and facts of the case, coupled with Payton\xe2\x80\x99s prior violent acts, outweighed the mitigating effect of Payton\xe2\x80\x99s newfound Christianity. Id., at 70. He discussed the mitigation evidence in considerable detail and concluded by urging that the circumstances of the case and Payton\xe2\x80\x99s prior violent acts outweighed his religious conversion. Id., at 75\xe2\x80\x9376. In his closing, defense counsel argued to the jury that, although it might be awkwardly worded, factor (k) was a catchall instruction designed to cover precisely the kind of evidence Payton had presented.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The trial court\xe2\x80\x99s final instructions to the jury included the factor (k) instruction, as well as an instruction directing the jury to consider all evidence presented during the trial. Id., at 94. The jury found the special circumstance of murder in the course of committing rape and returned a verdict recommending a death sentence. The judge sentenced Payton to death for murder and to 21 years and 8 months for rape and attempted murder.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On direct appeal to the California Supreme Court, Payton argued that his penalty phase jury incorrectly was led to believe it could not consider the mitigating evidence of his postconviction conduct in determining whether he should receive a sentence of life imprisonment or death, in violation of the Eighth Amendment of the U. S. Constitution. Lockett v. Ohio, 438 U. S. 586, 602\xe2\x80\x93609 (1978) (plurality opinion). The text of the factor (k) instruction, he maintained, was misleading, and rendered more so in light of the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s argument.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In a 5-to-2 decision, the California Supreme Court rejected Payton\xe2\x80\x99s claims and affirmed his convictions and sentence. People v. Payton, 3 Cal. 4th 1050, 839 P.\xc2\xa02d 1035 (1992). Applying Boyde v. California, 494 U. S. 370 (1990), which had considered the constitutionality of the same factor (k) instruction, the state court held that in the context of the proceedings there was no reasonable likelihood that Payton\xe2\x80\x99s jury believed it was required to disregard his mitigating evidence. 3 Cal. 4th, at 1070\xe2\x80\x931071, 839 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 1048. Payton sought review of the California Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision here. We declined to grant certiorari. Payton v. California, 510 U. S. 1040 (1994).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Payton filed a petition for a writ of habeas corpus in the United States District Court for the Central District of California, reiterating that the jury was prevented from considering his mitigation evidence. The District Court held that the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA), 110 Stat. 1214, did not apply to Payton\xe2\x80\x99s petition because he had filed a motion for appointment of counsel before AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s effective date, even though he did not file the petition until after that date. The District Court considered his claims de novo and granted the petition.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On appeal to the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit, a divided panel reversed. Payton v. Woodford, 258 F.\xc2\xa03d 905 (2001). The Court of Appeals granted Payton\xe2\x80\x99s petition for rehearing en banc and, by a 6-to-5 vote, affirmed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order granting habeas relief. Payton v. Woodford, 299 F.\xc2\xa03d 815 (2002). The en banc panel, like the District Court, held that AEDPA did not govern Payton\xe2\x80\x99s petition. It, too, conducted a de novo review of his claims, and concluded that postcrime mitigation evidence was not encompassed by the factor (k) instruction, a view it found to have been reinforced by the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s arguments.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The State petitioned for certiorari. Pursuant to Woodford v. Garceau, 538 U. S. 202 (2003), which held that a request for appointment of counsel did not suffice to make \xe2\x80\x9cpending\xe2\x80\x9d a habeas petition filed after AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s effective date, we granted the State\xe2\x80\x99s petition, Woodford v. Payton, 538 U. S. 975 (2003), and remanded to the Court of Appeals for reconsideration of its decision under AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s deferential standards. See Williams v. Taylor, 529 U. S. 362 (2000).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On remand, the en banc panel affirmed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s previous grant of habeas relief by the same 6-to-5 vote. Payton v. Woodford, 346 F. 3d 1204 (CA9 2003). In light of Garceau, the Court of Appeals purported to decide the case under the deferential standard AEDPA mandates. It concluded, however, that the California Supreme Court had unreasonably applied this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents in holding the factor (k) instruction was not unconstitutionally ambiguous in Payton\xe2\x80\x99s case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals relied, as it had in its initial decision, on the proposition that Boyde concerned precrime, not postcrime, mitigation evidence. Boyde, in its view, reasoned that a jury would be unlikely to disregard mitigating evidence as to character because of the long-held social belief that defendants who commit criminal acts attributable to a disadvantaged background may be less culpable than defendants who have no such excuse. As to postcrime mitigating evidence, however, the Court of Appeals concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthere is reason to doubt that a jury would similarly consider post-crime evidence of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s religious conversion and good behavior in prison.\xe2\x80\x9d 346 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1212. It cited no precedent of this Court to support that supposition.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In addition, it reasoned that unlike in Boyde the prosecutor in Payton\xe2\x80\x99s case misstated the law and the trial court did not give a specific instruction rejecting that misstatement, relying instead on a general admonition that counsel\xe2\x80\x99s arguments were not evidence. These two differences, the Court of Appeals concluded, made Payton\xe2\x80\x99s case unlike Boyde. 346 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1216. In its view, the factor (k) instruction was likely to have misled the jury and it was an unreasonable application of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases for the California Supreme Court to have concluded otherwise.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0AEDPA provides that, when a habeas petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claim has been adjudicated on the merits in state-court proceedings, a federal court may not grant relief unless the state court\xe2\x80\x99s adjudication of the claim \xe2\x80\x9cresulted in a decision that was contrary to, or involved an unreasonable application of, clearly established Federal law, as determined by the Supreme Court of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1). A state-court decision is contrary to this Court\xe2\x80\x99s clearly established precedents if it applies a rule that contradicts the governing law set forth in our cases, or if it confronts a set of facts that is materially indistinguishable from a decision of this Court but reaches a different result. Williams v. Taylor, supra, at 405; Early v. Packer, 537 U. S. 3, 8 (2002) (per curiam). A state-court decision involves an unreasonable application of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s clearly established precedents if the state court applies this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents to the facts in an objectively unreasonable manner. Williams v. Taylor, supra, at 405; Woodford v. Visciotti, 537 U. S. 19, 24\xe2\x80\x9325 (2002) (per curiam). These conditions for the grant of federal habeas relief have not been established.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The California Supreme Court was correct to identify Boyde as the starting point for its analysis. Boyde involved a challenge to the same instruction at issue here, factor (k). As to the text of factor (k), Boyde established that it does not limit the jury\xe2\x80\x99s consideration of extenuating circumstances solely to circumstances of the crime. See 494 U. S., at 382. In so holding, we expressly rejected the suggestion that factor (k) precluded the jury from considering evidence pertaining to a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s background and character because those circumstances did not concern the crime itself. Boyde instead found that factor (k), by its terms, directed the jury to consider any other circumstance that might excuse the crime, including factors related to a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s background and character. We held:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe [factor (k)] instruction did not, as petitioner seems to suggest, limit the jury\xe2\x80\x99s consideration to \xe2\x80\x98any other circumstance of the crime which extenuates the gravity of the crime.\xe2\x80\x99 The jury was directed to consider any other circumstance that might excuse the crime, which certainly includes a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s background and character.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis in original).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The California Supreme Court read Boyde as establishing that the text of factor (k) was broad enough to accommodate the postcrime mitigating evidence Payton presented. People v. Payton, 3 Cal. 4th, at 1070, 839 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 1048. The Court of Appeals held Boyde\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning did not control Payton\xe2\x80\x99s case because Boyde concerned precrime, not postcrime, mitigation evidence. 346 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1211\xe2\x80\x931212.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We do not think that, in light of Boyde, the California Supreme Court acted unreasonably in declining to distinguish between precrime and postcrime mitigating evidence. After all, Boyde held that factor (k) directed consideration of any circumstance that might excuse the crime, and it is not unreasonable to believe that a postcrime character transformation could do so. Indeed, to accept the view that such evidence could not because it occurred after the crime, one would have to reach the surprising conclusion that remorse could never serve to lessen or excuse a crime. But remorse, which by definition can only be experienced after a crime\xe2\x80\x99s commission, is something commonly thought to lessen or excuse a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s culpability.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That leaves respondent to defend the decision of the Court of Appeals on grounds that, even if it was at least reasonable for the California Supreme Court to conclude that the text of factor (k) allowed the jury to consider the postcrime evidence, it was unreasonable to conclude that the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s argument and remarks did not mislead the jury into believing it could not consider Payton\xe2\x80\x99s mitigation evidence. As we shall explain, however, the California Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that the jury was not reasonably likely to have accepted the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s narrow view of factor (k) was an application of Boyde to similar but not identical facts. Even on the assumption that its conclusion was incorrect, it was not unreasonable, and is therefore just the type of decision that AEDPA shields on habeas review.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The following language from Boyde should be noted at the outset:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cWe think the proper inquiry in such a case is whether there is a reasonable likelihood that the jury has applied the challenged instruction in a way that prevents the consideration of constitutionally relevant evidence. \xe2\x80\xa6 [J]urors do not sit in solitary isolation booths parsing instructions for subtle shades of meaning in the same way that lawyers might. Differences among them in interpretation of instructions may be thrashed out in the deliberative process, with commonsense understanding of the instructions in the light of all that has taken place at the trial likely to prevail over technical hairsplitting.\xe2\x80\x9d 494 U. S., at 380\xe2\x80\x93381 (footnote omitted).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Unlike in Boyde\xc2\xb8 the prosecutor here argued to jurors during his closing that they should not consider Payton\xe2\x80\x99s mitigation evidence, evidence which concerned postcrime as opposed to precrime conduct. Because Boyde sets forth a general framework for determining whether a challenged instruction precluded jurors from considering a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s mitigation evidence, however, the California Supreme Court was correct to structure its own analysis on the premises that controlled Boyde. The Boyde analysis applies here, and, even if it did not dictate a particular outcome in Payton\xe2\x80\x99s case, it refutes the conclusion of the Court of Appeals that the California Supreme Court was unreasonable.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s mistaken approach appears most prominently at three different points in the penalty phase. First, in chambers and outside the presence of the jury he argued to the judge that background and character (whether of precrime or postcrime) was simply beyond the ambit of the instruction. Second, he told the jurors in his closing statement that factor (k) did not allow them to consider what happened \xe2\x80\x9cafter the [crime] or later.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 68. Third, after defense counsel objected to his narrow view, he argued to the jury that it had not heard any evidence of mitigation. Id., at 70. Boyde, however, mandates that the whole context of the trial be considered. And considering the whole context of the trial, it was not unreasonable for the state court to have concluded that this line of prosecutorial argument did not put Payton\xe2\x80\x99s mitigating evidence beyond the jury\xe2\x80\x99s reach.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s argument came after the defense presented eight witnesses, spanning two days of testimony without a single objection from the prosecution as to its relevance. As the California Supreme Court recognized, like in Boyde, for the jury to have believed it could not consider Payton\xe2\x80\x99s mitigating evidence, it would have had to believe that the penalty phase served virtually no purpose at all. Payton\xe2\x80\x99s counsel recognized as much, arguing to the jury that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he whole purpose for the second phase [of the] trial is to decide the proper punishment to be imposed. Everything that was presented by the defense relates directly to that.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 88. He told the jury that if the evidence Payton presented was not entitled to consideration, and therefore \xe2\x80\x9call the evidence we presented [would not be] applicable, why didn\xe2\x80\x99t we hear any objections to its relevance?\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The prosecutor was not given an opportunity to rebut defense counsel\xe2\x80\x99s argument that factor (k) required the jury to consider Payton\xe2\x80\x99s mitigating evidence.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For his part, the prosecutor devoted specific attention to disputing the sincerity of Payton\xe2\x80\x99s evidence, stating that \xe2\x80\x9ceverybody seems to get religion in jail when facing the death penalty\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9c[s]tate prison is full of people who get religion when they are in jail.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 74. Later, he intimated the timing of Payton\xe2\x80\x99s religious conversion was suspect, stating \xe2\x80\x9che becomes a newborn Christian, after he\xe2\x80\x99s in custody\xe2\x80\x9d after \xe2\x80\x9che gets caught.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. As the California Supreme Court reasonably surmised, this exercise would have been pointless if the jury believed it could not consider the evidence.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Along similar lines, although the prosecutor characterized Payton\xe2\x80\x99s evidence as not being evidence of mitigation, he devoted substantial attention to discounting its importance as compared to the aggravating factors. He said:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe law in its simplicity is that the aggravating\xe2\x80\x94if the aggravating factors outweigh the mitigating, the sentence the jury should vote for should be the death penalty. How do the factors line up? The circumstances and facts of the case, the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s other acts showing violence . . . , the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s two prior convictions line up against really nothing except [the] defendant\xe2\x80\x99s newborn Christianity and the fact that he\xe2\x80\x99s 28 years old. This is not close. You haven\xe2\x80\x99t heard anything to mitigate what he\xe2\x80\x99s done. If you wanted to distribute a thousand points over the factors, 900 would have to go to what he did to [the victim], and I really doubt if [defense counsel] would dispute that breakdown of the facts.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 76.

\n

\nIndeed, the prosecutor characterized testimony concerning Payton\xe2\x80\x99s religious conversion as \xe2\x80\x9cevidence\xe2\x80\x9d on at least four separate occasions. Id., at 68, 70, 73. In context, it was not unreasonable for the state court to conclude that the jury believed Payton\xe2\x80\x99s evidence was neither credible nor sufficient to outweigh the aggravating factors, not that it was not evidence at all.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To be sure, the prosecutor advocated a narrow interpretation of factor (k), an interpretation that neither party accepts as correct. There is, however, no indication that the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s argument was made in bad faith, nor does Payton suggest otherwise. In addition, the first time the jury was exposed to the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s narrow and incorrect view of factor (k), it had already heard the entirety of Payton\xe2\x80\x99s mitigating evidence. Defense counsel immediately objected to the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s narrow characterization, and the trial court, noting at a side bar that one could \xe2\x80\x9cargue it either way,\xe2\x80\x9d admonished the jury that \xe2\x80\x9cthe comments by both the prosecution and the defense are not evidence. You\xe2\x80\x99ve heard the evidence and, as I said, this is argument. And it\xe2\x80\x99s to be placed in its proper perspective.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 69\xe2\x80\x9370.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The trial judge, of course, should have advised the jury that it could consider Payton\xe2\x80\x99s evidence under factor (k), and allowed counsel simply to argue the evidence\xe2\x80\x99s persuasive force instead of the meaning of the instruction itself. The judge is, after all, the one responsible for instructing the jury on the law, a responsibility that may not be abdicated to counsel. Even in the face of the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s failure to give an instant curative instruction, however, it was not unreasonable to find that the jurors did not likely believe Payton\xe2\x80\x99s mitigation evidence beyond their reach. The jury was not left without any judicial direction. Before it began deliberations as to what penalty was appropriate, the court instructed it to consider all evidence received \xe2\x80\x9cduring any part of the trial in this case, except as you may be hereafter instructed,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 94, and it was not thereafter instructed to disregard anything. It was also instructed as to factor (k) which, as we held in Boyde, by its terms directs jurors to consider any other circumstance that might lessen a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s culpability.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Testimony about a religious conversion spanning one year and nine months may well have been considered altogether insignificant in light of the brutality of the crimes, the prior offenses, and a proclivity for committing violent acts against women. It was not unreasonable for the state court to determine that the jury most likely believed that the evidence in mitigation, while within the reach of the factor (k) instruction, was simply too insubstantial to overcome the arguments for imposing the death penalty; nor was it unreasonable for the state court to rely upon Boyde to support its analysis. Even were we to assume the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98relevant state-court decision applied clearly established federal law erroneously or incorrectly,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Lockyer v. Andrade, 538 U. S. 63, 76 (2003) (quoting Williams v. Taylor, 529 U. S., at 411), there is no basis for further concluding that the application of our precedents was \xe2\x80\x9cobjectively unreasonable.\xe2\x80\x9d Lockyer, supra, at 76. The Court of Appeals made this last mentioned assumption, and it was in error to do so. The judgment of the Ninth Circuit is reversed.

\n

It is so ordered.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Souter, with whom Justice Stevens and Justice Ginsburg join, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0From a time long before William Payton\xe2\x80\x99s trial, it has been clear law under the Eighth and Fourteenth Amendments that a sentencing jury in a capital case must be able to consider and give effect to all relevant mitigating evidence a defendant offers for a sentence less than death. The prosecutor in Payton\xe2\x80\x99s case effectively negated this principle in arguing repeatedly to the jury that the law required it to disregard Payton\xe2\x80\x99s mitigating evidence of postcrime religious conversion and rehabilitation. The trial judge utterly failed to correct these repeated misstatements or in any other way to honor his duty to give the jury an accurate definition of legitimate mitigation. It was reasonably likely in these circumstances that the jury failed to consider Payton\xe2\x80\x99s mitigating evidence, and in concluding otherwise, the Supreme Court of California unreasonably applied settled law, with substantially injurious effect. The Court of Appeals was correct, and I respectfully dissent.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the time the Supreme Court of California took up Payton\xe2\x80\x99s direct appeal of his death sentence for homicide, it was settled law that a capital defendant has a plenary right to present evidence going to any aspect of his character, background, or record, as well as to any circumstance particular to the offense, that might justify a sentence less than death, Penry v. Lynaugh, 492 U. S. 302 (1989); Eddings v. Oklahoma, 455 U. S. 104 (1982); Lockett v. Ohio, 438 U. S. 586 (1978), including evidence of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s behavior after the offense, Skipper v. South Carolina, 476 U. S. 1, 4\xe2\x80\x935 (1986). The law was equally explicit that the sentencer may not refuse to consider any evidence in mitigation, or be precluded from giving it whatever effect it may merit. Penry v. Lynaugh, supra, at 318\xe2\x80\x93320; Eddings v. Oklahoma, supra, at 113\xe2\x80\x93114.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When Payton was tried, California\xe2\x80\x99s sentencing law was not well designed to satisfy the State\xe2\x80\x99s obligation to provide the sentencer with a way to give effect to all mitigating evidence including developments after commission of the crime. Trial courts were generally bound to charge a sentencing jury to take into account and be guided by a set of legislatively adopted pattern instructions that described relevant subjects of aggravation and mitigation in terms of eleven \xe2\x80\x9cfactors.\xe2\x80\x9d These factors ran the gamut from a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s age and state of mind at the time of the crime to a qualified catchall at the end: \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98(k) [a]ny other circumstance which extenuates the gravity of the crime even though it is not a legal excuse for the crime.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Boyde v. California, 494 U. S. 370, 373\xe2\x80\x93374, and n. 1 (1990); 1 Cal. Jury Instr., Crim., No. 8.84.1 (4th rev. ed. 1979).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This catchall provision, known as factor (k), was the subject of Boyde, in which the capital defendant had presented extensive testimony of favorable character in struggling against great childhood disadvantages. 494 U. S., at 381\xe2\x80\x93383. It was understood that the evidence was not open to the jury\xe2\x80\x99s consideration under any factor except possibly (k), and the question was whether the instruction to consider \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny other circumstance which extenuates the gravity of the crime\xe2\x80\x9d adequately conveyed the idea that character was such a circumstance, even though it was not a fact limited to the setting of the crime itself.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court first laid down the general standard: \xe2\x80\x9cwhether there is a reasonable likelihood that the jury has applied the challenged instruction in a way that prevents the consideration of constitutionally relevant evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 380. A \xe2\x80\x9creasonable likelihood\xe2\x80\x9d is more than a mere possibility that the jury mistook the law, but a defendant \xe2\x80\x9cneed not establish that the jury was more likely than not to have been impermissibly inhibited by the instruction.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. A majority of the Court then concluded on the facts of Boyde\xe2\x80\x99s trial that there had been no showing that any ambiguity in the instruction had kept the jury from considering the character evidence. Id., at 383\xe2\x80\x93385.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In support of its application of the general standard in Boyde\xe2\x80\x99s case, the Court noted that not all of the other factors in the instruction were tied to the specifics of the crime; the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s youth at the time of commission could be considered, for example, along with prior criminal activity and prior felony record. Id., at 383. It was, moreover, only natural for the jury to consider evidence of character in the face of hardships, since society generally holds people less culpable for bad acts related to disadvantages in life. Id., at 382, and n. 5. The Court found it highly implausible that the jury would have thought it had to ignore testimony of such evidence, spanning four days and generating over 400 pages of transcript. Id., at 383\xe2\x80\x93384. The pattern instructions as read by the judge included the admonition to make the penalty decision after considering \xe2\x80\x9call of the evidence which has been received during any part of the trial,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 383 (emphasis deleted), and the prosecutor never claimed that the testimony was not relevant, id., at 385. Rather, \xe2\x80\x9cthe prosecutor explicitly assumed that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s character evidence was a proper factor in the weighing process, but argued that it was minimal in relation to the aggravating circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Payton, too, was sentenced to death by a jury that had been given a version of the same pattern instructions, including factor (k). Both the nature of Payton\xe2\x80\x99s evidence, however, and the behavior of Payton\xe2\x80\x99s prosecutor contrasted sharply with their counterparts in Boyde, and in a significant respect the version of the pattern instructions read to Payton\xe2\x80\x99s jury differed from the version the Boyde jury heard.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although the penalty phase of Payton\xe2\x80\x99s trial stretched over three days, mitigation evidence offered through testimony on Payton\xe2\x80\x99s behalf came in during parts of two half days. App. 15\xe2\x80\x9354. In the first such session, two witnesses, one a minister and the other her congregation\xe2\x80\x99s missions director, said that since the commission of his crimes Payton had made a \xe2\x80\x9ccommitment to the Lord\xe2\x80\x9d that they believed to be sincere, id., at 18, 23; that he had demonstrated remorse, id., at 18; and that he manifested his new faith in Bible study, writing, and spiritual help to fellow inmates, id., at 22\xe2\x80\x9329. Because Payton\xe2\x80\x99s remaining witnesses were not available, the trial judge excused the jury after just \xe2\x80\x9ca short day.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 31.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Following a weekend break, six witnesses appeared for Payton, including four former fellow inmates who testified that he frequently led religious discussions among prisoners, that he exerted \xe2\x80\x9ca very good influence\xe2\x80\x9d on others, id., at 34, and that he \xe2\x80\x9calways tr[ied] to help people out,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 39. See generally id., at 32\xe2\x80\x9344. A fifth witness, a deputy sheriff at Payton\xe2\x80\x99s jail, corroborated this testimony, id., at 45\xe2\x80\x9348, and said that he was glad to have Payton at the jail because he had a calming influence on other inmates, and because he occasionally informed the authorities of developing problems, id., at 49. Finally, Payton\xe2\x80\x99s mother testified that she had seen a change in him during incarceration and believed his religious conversion was sincere. Id., at 52\xe2\x80\x9354. Thus, Payton\xe2\x80\x99s evidence went entirely to his postcrime conversion and his potential for rehabilitation and usefulness; the presentation of this evidence produced a transcript of only 50 pages.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The trial court sent the case to the jury the next day, after meeting with the prosecutor and defense counsel to discuss the charge, including the factor (k) instruction to consider any other circumstance extenuating the gravity of the crime. Boyde had not been decided at that point, and defense counsel expressed concern that factor (k) could be understood to exclude consideration of Payton\xe2\x80\x99s mitigating evidence because the facts shown \xe2\x80\x9chave something to do with his potential for rehabilitation or his character or his background, but they don\xe2\x80\x99t have anything to do with the crime itself\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 55. The prosecutor readily agreed with that reading. He responded that the language of factor (k) was intended to reach only circumstances extenuating the gravity of the crime, to the exclusion of character and background. Ibid. Indeed, the prosecutor maintained that he did not see \xe2\x80\x9cany ambiguity\xe2\x80\x9d in factor (k), id., at 57, and that if the legislature had meant background or character to be considered under factor (k), it would have said so explicitly, id., at 58.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The trial court agreed with defense counsel that background and character (including the claimed conversion) should be subject to consideration under factor (k), but declined to alter the instruction because it was hesitant to depart from the statutory text. Id., at 58, 61. Instead, the judge advised the lawyers that they were free to \xe2\x80\x9cargue [that] the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s background, history, mental condition, physical condition \xe2\x80\xa6 certainly fall into category \xe2\x80\x98k\xe2\x80\x99 and certainly make a clear argument to the jury.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 59. After the judge said explicitly that he thought \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98k\xe2\x80\x99 is the all encompassing one that includes \xe2\x80\xa6 what you want added,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 60, defense counsel lobbied one last time for a more accurate instruction, but was rebuffed:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[Defense counsel]: My only problem is I think we all agree that that\xe2\x80\x99s the law, but the jury\xe2\x80\x99s not going to know.

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9cThe Court: I agree with you\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. But I\xe2\x80\x99m going to deny [your request], and for the reasons stated.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 61.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The trial court then brought in the jury for argument and charge. When the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s closing argument got to the subject of factor (k), this is what he said to the jury:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98K\xe2\x80\x99 says any other circumstance which extenuates or lessens the gravity of the crime. What does that mean? That to me means some fact\xe2\x80\x94okay?\xe2\x80\x94some factors at the time of the offense that somehow operates to reduce the gravity for what the defendant did. It doesn\xe2\x80\x99t refer to anything after the fact or later. That\xe2\x80\x99s particularly important here because the only defense evidence you have heard has been about this new born Christianity.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 68.

\n

\nPayton\xe2\x80\x99s lawyer interrupted, both counsel approached the bench, and, out of the jury\xe2\x80\x99s hearing, defense counsel moved for mistrial on the ground that the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s statement was \xe2\x80\x9ccompletely contrary\xe2\x80\x9d to the previously agreed interpretation of factor (k). Ibid. When the prosecutor replied that defense counsel was wrong and that Payton\xe2\x80\x99s mitigating evidence did not fall within factor (k), id., at 69, the trial court failed to resolve the matter, saying that \xe2\x80\x9cyou can argue it either way,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. Upon return to open court, the judge instructed the jury that \xe2\x80\x9cthe comments by both the prosecution and the defense are not evidence. You\xe2\x80\x99ve heard the evidence and, as I said, this is argument. And it\xe2\x80\x99s to be placed in its proper perspective.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 69\xe2\x80\x9370.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The prosecutor then took up exactly where he had left off, arguing that Payton\xe2\x80\x99s proffered mitigating evidence could not be considered in the jury\xe2\x80\x99s deliberations:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cReferring back to \xe2\x80\x98k\xe2\x80\x99 which I was talking about, any other circumstance which extenuates or lessens the gravity of the crime, the only defense evidence you\xe2\x80\x99ve heard had to do with defendant\xe2\x80\x99s new Christianity and that he helped the module deputies in the jail while he was in custody.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cThe problem with that is that evidence is well after the fact of the crime and cannot seem to me in any way to logically lessen the gravity of the offense that the defendant has committed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c[Defense counsel] will tell you that somehow that becoming a newborn Christian, if in fact he really believed that took place, makes it a less severe crime, but there is no way that can happen when\xe2\x80\x94under any other circumstance which extenuates or lessens the gravity of the crime, refers\xe2\x80\x94seems to refer to a fact in operation at the time of the offense.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cWhat I am getting at, you have not heard during the past few days any legal evidence mitigation. What you\xe2\x80\x99ve heard is just some jailhouse evidence to win your sympathy, and that\xe2\x80\x99s all. You have not heard any evidence of mitigation in this trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 70.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After the prosecutor recounted the aggravating circumstances and argued for the death penalty, he turned to the evidence of Payton\xe2\x80\x99s religious conversion, questioned its sincerity, and argued that it did not warrant a sentence less than death when weighed against the aggravating factors. Throughout this discussion, he returned to his point that factor (k) authorizes consideration only of facts as of the time of the crime. He reminded the jurors again that they had \xe2\x80\x9cheard no evidence of any mitigating factors.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 73. And again: \xe2\x80\x9cI don\xe2\x80\x99t really want to spend too much time on [religion] because I don\xe2\x80\x99t think it\xe2\x80\x99s really applicable and I don\xe2\x80\x99t think it comes under any of the eleven factors.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. And again: \xe2\x80\x9cYou haven\xe2\x80\x99t heard anything to mitigate what he\xe2\x80\x99s done.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 76.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0With the prosecutor arguing that Payton\xe2\x80\x99s mitigation evidence was not open to consideration under (k) or any other factor, and with the trial judge sitting on the fence, defense counsel was left to argue the law himself, stating that \xe2\x80\x9csection (k) may be awkwardly worded, but it does not preclude or exclude the kind of evidence that was presented. It\xe2\x80\x99s a catch-all ph[r]ase. It was designed to include, not exclude, that kind of evidence,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 88. Defense counsel discussed the mitigating evidence at some length before concluding that \xe2\x80\x9cI think there are a lot of good reasons to keep Bill Payton alive, an awful lot of good reasons. And that\xe2\x80\x99s exactly what I think \xe2\x80\x98k\xe2\x80\x99 is talking about.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 92.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The trial court then gave the jury its final instructions:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cIn determining the penalty to be imposed on the defendant, you shall consider all of the evidence which has been received during any part of the trial in this case, except as you may be hereafter instructed. You shall consider, take into account and be guided by the following factors, [including] \xe2\x80\xa6 (k), [which says] [a]ny other circumstance which extenuates the gravity of the crime even though not a legal excuse for the crime\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cAfter having heard all of the evidence and after having heard and considered the argument of counsel, you shall consider, take into account and be guided by the applicable factors of aggravating and mitigating circumstances upon which you have been instructed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cIf you conclude that the aggravating circumstances outweigh the mitigating circumstances, you shall impose a sentence of death.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cHowever, if you determine that the mitigating circumstances outweigh the aggravating circumstances, you shall impose a sentence of confinement in the state prison for life without the possibility of parole.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 94\xe2\x80\x9396.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The jury returned a death verdict.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The failure of the State to provide Payton with a process for sentencing that respected his clearly established right to consideration of all mitigating evidence is plain at every step of the jury\xe2\x80\x99s instruction, starting with the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on the pattern jury charge adopted by the legislature.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is undisputed that factor (k) was the instruction that comes closest to addressing the jury\xe2\x80\x99s obligation to consider Payton\xe2\x80\x99s evidence of postoffense conversion, and the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s remarks in the chambers colloquy both demonstrate the inadequacy of factor (k) to explain that responsibility and point to the seriousness of the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s failure to give a group of lay persons an intelligible statement of the controlling law. Factor (k) calls on the jury to consider evidence going to the \xe2\x80\x9cgravity of the crime,\xe2\x80\x9d a notion commonly understood as the joint product of intent, act, and consequence: intentionally shooting a police officer through the heart is worse than knocking down a pedestrian by careless skateboarding. It is coherent with this understanding to say, as the Court did in Boyde, that evaluating a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s state of mind at the time of the offense can include consideration of his general character and the experiences that affected its development, 494 U. S., at 381\xe2\x80\x93382; as the Court explained, when society sits in judgment, it does not ignore the early hardships of those who turn out bad, id., at 382. But it would be more than a stretch to say that the seriousness of the crime itself is affected by a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s subsequent experience. A criminal\xe2\x80\x99s subsequent religious conversion is not a fact commonly accepted as affecting the gravity of the crime, and even jurors who could overcome their skepticism about the sincerity of the conversion claim would see it as addressed not to the nature of the crime but to other issues bearing on sentence: the moral argument for executing a defendant who claims to have realized the awfulness of what he had done, and the practical argument for protecting others in the future by taking a life of one who claims to have been transformed. See, e.g., Skipper v. South Carolina, 476 U. S., at 4\xe2\x80\x935. I will assume that a jury instructed by a judge to consider evidence of postoffense experience that extenuates the gravity of the crime could have given effect to the instruction, but without such an explanation it would have been unnatural to think of evidence of later events as affecting the seriousness of an earlier crime.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Indications of the way factor (k) was understood in California at the time of Payton\xe2\x80\x99s trial, in fact, point this way. The prosecutor who spoke for the State at the trial repeatedly argued to judge and jury that a \xe2\x80\x9ccircumstance which extenuates or lessens the gravity of the crime, refers\xe2\x80\x94seems to refer to a fact in operation at the time of the offense.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 70. The prosecutor held this view in good faith, ante, at 12 (majority opinion), and, indeed, his view was shared by the state judiciary; even before Boyde, the Supreme Court of California had found factor (k) inadequate to require consideration of all types of mitigating evidence. In 1983, following our discussion in Eddings, that court directed that factor (k) be adorned in future cases so as to inform the jury that it may consider \xe2\x80\x9cany other \xe2\x80\x98aspect of [the] defendant\xe2\x80\x99s character or record \xe2\x80\xa6 that the defendant proffers as a basis for a sentence less than death.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d People v. Easley, 34 Cal. 3d 858, 878, n.\xc2\xa010, 671 P.\xc2\xa02d 813, 826, n.\xc2\xa010 (alterations in original). And, again before Boyde came down, the Legislature of California amended factor (k) to instruct the jury to consider \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny other circumstance which extenuates the gravity of the crime even though it is not a legal excuse for the crime [and any sympathetic or other aspect of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s character or record [that the defendant offers] as a basis for a sentence less than death, whether or not related to the offense for which he is on trial \xe2\x80\xa6 ].\xe2\x80\x9d 494 U. S., at 374, n.\xc2\xa02 (quoting 1 Cal. Jury Instr., Crim., No. 8.85(k) (5th ed. 1988) (alterations in original)). Without that amendment, any claim that factor (k) called for consideration of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s personal development in the wake of his crime was simply at odds with common attitudes and the English language.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The next step in the process that failed to give the jury an intelligible instruction to consider all mitigating evidence consisted of the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s repeated statements telling the jury to ignore Payton\xe2\x80\x99s conversion evidence because it was not legally relevant:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c[Defense counsel] will tell you that somehow that becoming a newborn Christian, if in fact he really believed that took place, makes it a less severe crime, but there is no way that can happen when\xe2\x80\x94under any other circumstance which extenuates or lessens the gravity of the crime, refers\xe2\x80\x94seems to refer to a fact in operation at the time of the offense.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cWhat I am getting at, you have not heard during the past few days any legal evidence mitigation. What you\xe2\x80\x99ve heard is just some jailhouse evidence to win your sympathy, and that\xe2\x80\x99s all. You have not heard any evidence of mitigation in this trial.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 70.

\n

\nAlthough the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s argument rested on a perfectly fair reading of the text of the pattern instruction, its effect, in the absence of any further instruction, was to tell the jury that it could not consider the conversion evidence as mitigating. Payton\xe2\x80\x99s lawyer immediately objected. He expressed his understanding that the trial judge had agreed that consideration of the mitigating evidence was constitutionally required and meant to let respective counsel argue only about its probative value, even though the judge himself had refused to address this essential constitutional issue specifically in any particular instruction. One would reasonably suppose that the trial judge would have realized that the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s argument put him on the spot, forcing him to correct the misleading statement of law with an explicit instruction that the jury was free to treat the conversion evidence as mitigating, evaluating its weight as the jury saw fit. It is, after all, elementary law, federal and state, that the judge bears ultimate responsibility for instructing a lay jury in the law. Carter v. Kentucky, 450 U. S. 288, 302\xe2\x80\x93303 (1981); Bollenbach v. United States, 326 U. S. 607, 612\xe2\x80\x93614 (1946); Quercia v. United States, 289 U. S. 466, 469 (1933); Sparf v. United States, 156 U. S. 51, 102 (1895); People v. Roberge, 29 Cal. 4th 979, 988, 62 P.\xc2\xa03d 97, 102 (2003); People v. Beardslee, 53 Cal. 3d 68, 97, 806 P.\xc2\xa02d 1311, 1326 (1991). But the trial judge did no such thing. Instead, he merely told the jury that the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s argument was not evidence. This instruction cured nothing. The prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s objectionable comment was not a statement about evidence but a statement of law. Telling the jury that a statement of law was not evidence did nothing to correct its functional error in misstating the law.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is true that the prosecutor argued that Payton\xe2\x80\x99s postcrime evidence was not only beyond the jury\xe2\x80\x99s consideration legally, but also insufficient to outweigh the aggravating circumstances. The prosecutor, however, minimized the significance even of these brief observations by saying, \xe2\x80\x9cI don\xe2\x80\x99t really want to spend too much time on it because I don\xe2\x80\x99t think it\xe2\x80\x99s really applicable and I don\xe2\x80\x99t think it comes under any of the eleven factors.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 73. Far from \xe2\x80\x9cexplicitly assum[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d that the jury\xe2\x80\x99s consideration of the evidence was proper, Boyde, supra, at 385, the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s comments, interwoven with his clear statements on the scope of factor (k), could not have left the listener with any doubt about the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s view of the legal relevance of the evidence.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nothing could be further from the circumstances in Boyde. There the prosecutor agreed that the character evidence was properly subject to the jury\xe2\x80\x99s consideration as mitigating, even under the ambiguous terms of factor (k). Ibid. The Boyde jury heard argument about the weight of the evidence, but not a word denying its relevance. Ibid. Indeed, the Boyde majority specifically distinguished the facts before it from the facts confronting us here, in disclaiming any suggestion \xe2\x80\x9cthat prosecutorial misrepresentations may never have a decisive effect on the jury,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 384; \xe2\x80\x9carguments of counsel, like the instructions of the court, must be judged in the context in which they are made,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 385. If the Boyde majority thus anticipated a case like this one, with a possibility of substantial prejudice arising from misrepresentation of the law, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s prescience is attributable to the State\xe2\x80\x99s position in the Boyde argument: the Supervising Deputy Attorney General of California appearing for the State in Boyde urged the Court to see that case in a light favorable to the State, in contrast to Payton\xe2\x80\x99s case, to which counsel referred by name, as a case in which the prosecutor had \xe2\x80\x9cmisled the jurors.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. in O.\xc2\xa0T. 1989, No. 88\xe2\x80\x936613, p.\xc2\xa029. Boyde is thus no authority for giving the State a pass here. The Court is faced with the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s conceded misstatement, ante, at 3 (majority opinion), misleading to the jury, which obliged the trial court, however \xe2\x80\x9creluctant to strike out on its own\xe2\x80\x9d beyond the pattern instructions, to \xe2\x80\x9cdo more than figuratively throw up its hands.\xe2\x80\x9d People v. Beardslee, supra, at 97, 806 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 1326.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The final misstep that distinguishes this case from the authority of Boyde is the judge\xe2\x80\x99s charge, which must be understood against the background of the mitigating testimonial evidence that the jury did, after all, hear. At each stage of Payton\xe2\x80\x99s appeal and collateral challenge, the State has argued that it makes no sense to suggest the jury would have disregarded substantive evidence with no other purpose than mitigation, when ignoring it would have meant that Payton\xe2\x80\x99s mitigation witnesses were just putting on a pointless charade. An argument like this was one of the reasons for affirming the conviction in Boyde, 494 U. S., at 383, and both the Supreme Court of California and the majority today rely on a reprise of it to affirm here, People v. Payton, 3 Cal. 4th 1050, 1072, 839 P.\xc2\xa02d 1035, 1049 (1992); ante, at 10 (majority opinion). This is, however, an argument to be entertained only with great caution in the best of circumstances, and while Boyde\xe2\x80\x99s circumstances were good, this is a very different case from Boyde.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The need for caution is plain: the constitutional concern with mitigating evidence is not satisfied by the mere ability of a defendant to present it. The sentencing body must have a genuine opportunity to consider it and give effect to it. Penry v. Lynaugh, 492 U. S., at 320. As the Court said in Boyde, \xe2\x80\x9c[p]resentation of mitigating evidence alone \xe2\x80\xa6 does not guarantee that a jury will feel entitled to consider that evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d 494 U. S., at 384. For this reason, the Court has found Eighth Amendment violations in circumstances precluding the sentencing body from considering the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s mitigating evidence, even where the evidence was extensive and where it accordingly might have been thought unnatural for the sentencer to disregard it. See, e.g., Penry v. Johnson, 532 U. S. 782, 788, 803\xe2\x80\x93804 (2001); Eddings v. Oklahoma, 455 U. S., at 107, 113\xe2\x80\x93114.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0What is equally plain is that Boyde is no authority for thinking the combination of evidence, argument, and charge passes muster here. Boyde\xe2\x80\x99s mitigation evidence was extensive enough to take four days and produce over 400 pages of transcript. It addressed character and hardship, subjects recognized by the Court as commonly thought relevant to sentencing, and ignoring it would thus have ignored a large chunk of intuitively acceptable evidence. Payton\xe2\x80\x99s evidence, in contrast, required parts of two half days and generated only 50 pages, addressing a claim of dramatic self-reformation that most people would treat with considerable caution. While it would have been unnatural for the jury in Boyde to feel barred from considering the character evidence when no lawyer or judge had ever called it irrelevant, Payton\xe2\x80\x99s jury had plenty of reason to feel itself precluded: the prosecutor emphatically and repeatedly said that the evidence did not count as the kind of evidence that could extenuate the crime, and the trial judge allowed the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s statements to go uncorrected.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0More significant even than those contrasts between Boyde and the facts here is the difference between the two sets of instructions from the trial judges. In Boyde, this Court found it significant that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he jury was instructed that it \xe2\x80\x98shall consider all the evidence which has been received during any part of the trial of this case.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 494 U. S., at 383 (emphasis added by Boyde majority). Reasonable jurors could therefore hardly \xe2\x80\x9chave felt constrained by the factor (k) instruction to ignore all of the evidence presented by the petitioner during the sentencing phase.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 383\xe2\x80\x93384 (emphasis again supplied by Boyde majority).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Here, however, the instruction was different, a variant permitted by the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s pattern charge. Here the instruction was not simply to consider all the evidence, but rather, \xe2\x80\x9cyou shall consider all of the evidence which has been received during any part of the trial in this case, except as you may be hereafter instructed.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 94. \xe2\x80\x9cHereafter,\xe2\x80\x9d of course, came the instruction to determine the penalty by applying the eleven enumerated factors, including factor (k). As to the factor (k) focus on the \xe2\x80\x9cgravity of the crime,\xe2\x80\x9d the prosecutor repeatedly had said that evidence of postcrime conversion was irrelevant, and his mistaken and misleading statements of law had never been corrected by the trial judge.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The upshot was this. The jury was told by the judge that some evidence could be excluded from its consideration. The judge presumably had some reason to say this. The only evidence that could reasonably have fallen within the exception was the evidence the prosecutor had just said was legally irrelevant, in a statement that was eminently plausible owing to the language of factor (k) and the subject matter of the evidence. The jurors could naturally have made sense of all they had heard by concluding they were required not to scrutinize and discount the conversion evidence if they found it unpersuasive, but to skip the scrutiny altogether and ignore the evidence as legally beside the point. This case is nothing like Boyde.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But even if the case were closer to Boyde than it is, and even if the course of Payton\xe2\x80\x99s penalty trial were best viewed the way the majority suggests, that would not satisfy Boyde\xe2\x80\x99s test. Boyde asks only whether there is a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable likelihood\xe2\x80\x9d that the jury understood an instruction as foreclosing consideration of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s mitigating evidence. 494 U. S., at 380. A defendant has no need to show it is \xe2\x80\x9cmore likely than not\xe2\x80\x9d that the jury misunderstood. Ibid. Accordingly, even if the best explanation for the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict were the one the majority offers, that would not resolve Payton\xe2\x80\x99s claim. Identifying the \xe2\x80\x9cmost likely\xe2\x80\x9d interpretation of events at Payton\xe2\x80\x99s trial, ante, at 13 (majority opinion), falls short of negating the reasonably likely alternative that the jury believed it could not consider the story of Payton\xe2\x80\x99s postcrime conversion.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s oft-repeated conclusion that the state court did not unreasonably apply Boyde seems to rest on two assumptions. The first is a loose understanding of Boyde as holding that factor (k) \xe2\x80\x9cdirects jurors to consider any other circumstance that might lessen a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s culpability,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 13 (majority opinion). The second is that factor (k) as so understood directs jurors to consider circumstances that do not excuse a crime or lessen a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s culpability but nevertheless supply some different (even postcrime) reason to forgo a sentence of death. But Boyde held only that the factor (k) instruction tells jurors \xe2\x80\x9cto consider any other circumstance that might excuse the crime, which certainly includes a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s background and character,\xe2\x80\x9d 494 U. S., at 382 (emphasis deleted). Boyde did not purport to hold that factor (k) naturally called for consideration of postcrime changes of fundamental views. It is thus only by broadening Boyde to sanction a misreading of factor (k), a misreading that the prosecutor himself rejected in good faith, that the Court can find a reasonable application of law in the state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision. The mistake will unfortunately reverberate even beyond this case, for the majority further obscures the necessarily inexact distinction between cases that are merely wrong and cases with objectively unreasonable error. Compare Penry v. Johnson, 532 U. S. 782 (finding that a confusing jury instruction created a reasonable likelihood the jury would not feel free to consider mitigating evidence, and that the state court\xe2\x80\x99s contrary conclusion was \xe2\x80\x9cobjectively unreasonable,\xe2\x80\x9d even though the jury heard extensive mitigating evidence submitted without objection as to relevance, even though the judge took care to instruct the jury to consider \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98any aspect of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s character and record or circumstances of the crime which you believe could make a death sentence inappropriate,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d even though the prosecutor never questioned the relevance of the evidence when addressing the jury, and even though both counsel argued at length to the jury about the weight of the evidence).

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0By the State\xe2\x80\x99s admission in this case, the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s argument was a \xe2\x80\x9cmisstatement\xe2\x80\x9d of constitutional law. By the State\xe2\x80\x99s admission in Boyde, the prosecutor here \xe2\x80\x9cmisled\xe2\x80\x9d the jury. Despite objection by defense counsel, the trial judge refused to correct the misstatement, which the prosecutor proceeded to repeat. The judge\xe2\x80\x99s subsequent charge to consider all evidence was subject to a qualification that the jury could reasonably have understood only as referring to the mitigation evidence the prosecutor had branded as irrelevant under a straightforward reading of the pattern instructions.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If a prosecutor had stood before a jury and denied that a defendant was entitled to a presumption of innocence; if the judge refused to correct him and failed to give any instruction on the presumption of innocence; if the judge\xe2\x80\x99s instructions affirmatively suggested there might not be a presumption of innocence; would anyone doubt that there was a reasonable possibility that the jury had been misled? There is no more room here to doubt the reasonable possibility that Payton\xe2\x80\x99s jurors failed to consider the postoffense mitigation evidence that the Constitution required them to consider. In a case that contrasts with Boyde at every significant step, the State Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s affirmance of Payton\xe2\x80\x99s conviction can only be seen as an unreasonable misapplication of the governing federal standard, not mere error. And since Payton\xe2\x80\x99s death sentence is subject to this reasonable possibility of constitutional error, since he may die as a consequence, the effect of the instruction failure is surely substantial and injurious, Brecht v. Abrahamson, 507 U. S. 619, 638 (1993), beyond any possible excuse as harmless error.
