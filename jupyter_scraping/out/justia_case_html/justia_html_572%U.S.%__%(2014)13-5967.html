\n

\n\t\t\t\tESTEBAN MARTINEZ, PETITIONER v. ILLINOIS\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\ton petition for writ of certiorari to the supreme court of illinois\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 13\xe2\x80\x935967.\xe2\x80\x83Decided May 27, 2014\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPer Curiam.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe trial of Esteban Martinez was set to begin on May 17, 2010. His counsel was ready; the State was not. When the court swore in the jury and invited the State to present its first witness, the State declined to present any evidence. So Martinez moved for a directed not-guilty verdict, and the court granted it. The State appealed, arguing that the trial court should have granted its motion for a continuance. The question is whether the Double Jeopardy Clause bars the State\xe2\x80\x99s attempt to appeal in the hope of subjecting Martinez to a new trial.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Illinois Supreme Court manifestly erred in allowing the State\xe2\x80\x99s appeal, on the theory that jeopardy never attached because Martinez \xe2\x80\x9cwas never at risk of conviction.\xe2\x80\x9d 2013 IL 113475, \xc2\xb639, 990 N.\xc2\xa0E.\xc2\xa02d 215, 224. Our cases have repeatedly stated the bright-line rule that \xe2\x80\x9cjeopardy attaches when the jury is empaneled and sworn.\xe2\x80\x9d Crist v. Bretz, \n\n437 U.\xc2\xa0S. 28, \n35 (1978)\n; see infra, at 6. There is simply no doubt that Martinez was subjected to jeopardy. And because the trial court found the State\xe2\x80\x99s evidence insufficient to sustain a conviction, there is equally no doubt that Martinez may not be retried.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe therefore grant Martinez\xe2\x80\x99s petition for certiorari and reverse the judgment of the Illinois Supreme Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe State of Illinois indicted Martinez in August 2006 on charges of aggravated battery and mob action against Avery Binion and Demarco Scott. But Martinez\xe2\x80\x99s trial date did not arrive for nearly four years.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe story picks up for present purposes on July 20, 2009, when the State moved to continue an August 3 trial date because it had not located the complaining witnesses, Binion and Scott. The State subpoenaed both men four days later, and the court rescheduled Martinez\xe2\x80\x99s trial to September 28. But the State sought another continuance, shortly before that date, because it still had not found Binion and Scott. The court rescheduled the trial to November 9, and the State reissued subpoenas. But November 9 came and went (the court continued the case when Martinez showed up late) and the trial was eventually delayed to the following March 29. In early February, the State yet again subpoenaed Binion and Scott. When March 29 arrived, the trial court granted the State an-other continuance. It reset the trial date for May 17 and ordered Binion and Scott to appear in court on May 10. And the State once more issued subpoenas.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOn the morning of May 17, however, Binion and Scott were again nowhere to be found. At 8:30, when the trial was set to begin, the State asked for a brief continuance. The court offered to delay swearing the jurors until a complete jury had been empaneled and told the State that it could at that point either have the jury sworn or move to dismiss its case. When Binion and Scott still had not shown up after the jury was chosen, the court offered to call the other cases on its docket so as to delay swearing the jury a bit longer. But when all these delays had run out, Binion and Scott were still nowhere in sight. The State filed a written motion for a continuance, arguing that it was \xe2\x80\x9cunable to proceed\xe2\x80\x9d without Binion and Scott. Tr. 7. The court denied that motion:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cThe case before the Court began on July 7, 2006. In two months we will then be embarking upon half a decade of pending a Class 3 felony. Avery Binion, Jr., and Demarco [Scott] are well known in Elgin, both are convicted felons. One would believe that the Elgin Police Department would know their whereabouts. They were ordered to be in court today. The Court will issue body writs for both of these gentlemen.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cIn addition, the State\xe2\x80\x99s list of witnesses indi- cates twelve witnesses. Excluding Mr. Scott and Mr. Binion, that\xe2\x80\x99s ten witnesses. The Court would anticipate it would take every bit of today and most of tomorrow to get through ten witnesses. By then the People may have had a chance to execute the arrest warrant body writs for these two gentlemen.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cThe Court will deny the motion for continuance. I will swear the jury in in 15, 20 minutes. Perhaps you might want to send the police out to find these two gentlemen.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 8\xe2\x80\x939.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAfter a brief recess, the court offered to delay the start of the trial for several more hours if the continuance would \xe2\x80\x9cbe of any help\xe2\x80\x9d to the State. Id., at 9. But when the State made clear that Binion and Scott\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cwhereabouts\xe2\x80\x9d remained \xe2\x80\x9cunknown,\xe2\x80\x9d the court concluded that the delay \xe2\x80\x9cwould be a further waste of time.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 10. The following colloquy ensued:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cTHE COURT: .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. It\xe2\x80\x99s a quarter to eleven and [Binion and Scott] have not appeared on their own will, so I\xe2\x80\x99m going to bring the jury in now then to swear them.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c[The Prosecutor]: Okay. Your Honor, may I approach briefly?\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cTHE COURT: Yes.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c[The Prosecutor]: Your Honor, just so your Honor is aware, I know that it\xe2\x80\x99s the process to bring them in and swear them in; however, the State will not be participating in the trial. I wanted to let you know that.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cTHE COURT: Very well. We\xe2\x80\x99ll see how that works.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 10\xe2\x80\x9311.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe jury was then sworn. After instructing the jury, the court directed the State to proceed with its opening statement. The prosecutor demurred: \xe2\x80\x9cYour Honor, respect- fully, the State is not participating in this case.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 20. After the defense waived its opening statement, the court directed the State to call its first witness. Again, the prosecutor demurred: \xe2\x80\x9cRespectfully, your Honor, the State is not participating in this matter.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The defense then moved for a judgment of acquittal:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c[Defense Counsel]: Judge, the jury has been sworn. The State has not presented any evidence. I believe they\xe2\x80\x99ve indicated their intention not to present any evidence or witnesses.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cBased on that, Judge, I would ask the Court to enter directed findings of not guilty to both counts, ag- gravated battery and mob action.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cTHE COURT: Do the People wish to reply?\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c[The Prosecutor]: No, your Honor. Respectfully, the State is not participating.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cTHE COURT: The Court will grant the motion for a directed finding and dismiss the charges.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 21.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe State appealed, arguing that the trial court should have granted a continuance. Martinez responded that the State\xe2\x80\x99s appeal was improper because he had been acquitted. The Illinois Appellate Court sided with the State, holding that jeopardy had never attached and that the trial court had erred in failing to grant a continuance. 2011 IL App (2d) 100498, \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb646, 53\xe2\x80\x9356, 969 N. E. 2d 840, 854, 856\xe2\x80\x93858.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Illinois Supreme Court granted review on the jeopardy issue and affirmed. 2013 IL 113475, 990 N. E. 2d 215. It began by recognizing that \xe2\x80\x9c[g]enerally, in cases of a jury trial, jeopardy attaches when a jury is empaneled and sworn, as that is the point when the defendant is \xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cput to trial before the trier of the facts.\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., \xc2\xb623, 990 N. E. 2d, at 222 (quoting Serfass v. United States, \n\n420 U.\xc2\xa0S. 377, \n394 (1975)\n). But it reasoned that under this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9crigid, mechanical\xe2\x80\x9d rules\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d should not govern the inquiry into whether jeopardy has attached. 2013 IL 113475, \xc2\xb624, 990 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 222 (quoting Serfass, supra, at 390). Rather, it opined, the relevant question is whether a defendant \xe2\x80\x9cwas \xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9csubjected to the hazards of trial and possible conviction.\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 2013 IL 113475, \xc2\xb624, 990 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 222 (quoting Serfass, supra, at 391).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tHere, the court concluded, Martinez \xe2\x80\x9cwas never at risk of conviction\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94and jeopardy therefore did not attach\xe2\x80\x94because \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he State indicated it would not participate prior to the jury being sworn.\xe2\x80\x9d 2013 IL 113475, \xc2\xb639, 990 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 224. And because Martinez \xe2\x80\x9cwas not placed in jeopardy,\xe2\x80\x9d the court held, the trial \xe2\x80\x9ccourt\xe2\x80\x99s entry of directed verdicts of not guilty did not constitute true acquittals.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., \xc2\xb640, 990 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 225. Indeed, the court remarked, the trial court \xe2\x80\x9crepeatedly referred to its action as a \xe2\x80\x98dismissal\xe2\x80\x99 rather than an acquittal.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Burke dissented, writing that the majority\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion \xe2\x80\x9cthat impaneling and swearing the jury had no legal significance\xe2\x80\x9d ran \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary to well-established principles regarding double jeopardy.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., \xc2\xb657, 990 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 227. Moreover, she argued, its assertion that Martinez was not in danger of conviction was \xe2\x80\x9cbelied by the actions of the court and the prosecutor.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., \xc2\xb663, 990 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 229. She explained that under the majority\xe2\x80\x99s holding, the State could \xe2\x80\x9cunilaterally render a trial a \xe2\x80\x98sham\xe2\x80\x99 simply by refusing to call witnesses after a jury has been selected.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., \xc2\xb664, 990 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 229.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis case presents two issues. First, did jeopardy attach to Martinez? Second, if so, did the proceeding end in such a manner that the Double Jeopardy Clause bars his retrial? Our precedents clearly dictate an affirmative answer to each question.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThere are few if any rules of criminal procedure clearer than the rule that \xe2\x80\x9cjeopardy attaches when the jury is empaneled and sworn.\xe2\x80\x9d Crist, 437 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 35; see also United States v. Martin Linen Supply Co., \n\n430 U.\xc2\xa0S. 564, \n569 (1977)\n; Serfass, supra, at 388; 6 W. LaFave, J. Israel, N. King, & O. Kerr, Criminal Procedure \xc2\xa725.1(d) (3d ed. 2007).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur clearest exposition of this rule came in Crist, which addressed the constitutionality of a Montana statute providing that jeopardy did not attach until the swearing of the first witness. As Crist explains, \xe2\x80\x9cthe precise point at which jeopardy [attaches] in a jury trial might have been open to argument before this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Downum v. United States, \n\n372 U.\xc2\xa0S. 734\n [(1963)],\xe2\x80\x9d in which \xe2\x80\x9cthe Court held that the Double Jeopardy Clause prevented a second prosecution of a defendant whose first trial had ended just after the jury had been sworn and before any testimony had been taken.\xe2\x80\x9d 437 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 35. But Downum put any such argument to rest: Its holding \xe2\x80\x9cnecessarily pinpointed the stage in a jury trial when jeopardy attaches, and [it] has since been understood as explicit authority for the proposition that jeopardy attaches when the jury is empaneled and sworn.\xe2\x80\x9d Crist, supra, at 35.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Illinois Supreme Court misread our precedents in suggesting that the swearing of the jury is anything other than a bright line at which jeopardy attaches. It relied on Serfass, understanding that case to mean \xe2\x80\x9cthat in assessing whether and when jeopardy attaches, \xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9crigid, mechanical\xe2\x80\x9d rules\xe2\x80\x99 should not be applied.\xe2\x80\x9d 2013 IL 113475, \xc2\xb624, 990 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 222. Under Serfass, the court reasoned, the relevant question is whether a defendant was as a functional matter \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9csubjected to the hazards of trial and possible conviction.\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 2013 IL 113475, \xc2\xb624, 990 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 222.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut Serfass does not apply a functional approach to the determination of when jeopardy has attached. As to that question, it states the same bright-line rule as every other case: Jeopardy attaches when \xe2\x80\x9ca defendant is \xe2\x80\x98put to trial,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and in a jury trial, that is \xe2\x80\x9cwhen a jury is empaneled and sworn.\xe2\x80\x9d 420 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 388. Indeed, Serfass explicitly rejects a functional approach to the question whether jeopardy has attached. See id., at 390 (refuting the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s argument that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98constructiv[e] jeopardy had attached\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d upon the pretrial grant of a motion to dismiss the indictment, which the defendant characterized as \xe2\x80\x9cthe \xe2\x80\x98functional equivalent of an acquittal on the merits\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). The Serfass Court acknowledged \xe2\x80\x9cthat we have disparaged \xe2\x80\x98rigid, mechanical\xe2\x80\x99 rules in the interpretation of the Double Jeopardy Clause.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. But it was referring to the case of Illinois v. Somerville, \n\n410 U.\xc2\xa0S. 458 (1973)\n, in which we declined to apply \xe2\x80\x9crigid, mechanical\xe2\x80\x9d reasoning in answering a very different question: not whether jeopardy had attached, but whether the manner in which it terminated (by mistrial) barred the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s retrial. Id., at 467. By contrast, Serfass explains, the rule that jeopardy attaches at the start of a trial is \xe2\x80\x9cby no means a mere technicality, nor is it a \xe2\x80\x98rigid, mechanical\xe2\x80\x99 rule.\xe2\x80\x9d 420 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 391. And contrary to the Illinois Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation, Serfass creates not the slightest doubt about when a \xe2\x80\x9ctrial\xe2\x80\x9d begins.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Illinois Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s error was consequential, for it introduced confusion into what we have consistently treated as a bright-line rule: A jury trial begins, and jeopardy attaches, when the jury is sworn. We have never suggested the exception perceived by the Illinois Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x94that jeopardy may not have attached where, under the circumstances of a particular case, the defendant was not genuinely at risk of conviction.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Martinez was subjected to jeopardy because the jury in his case was sworn.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[T]he conclusion that jeopardy has attached,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d how- ever, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98begins, rather than ends, the inquiry as to whether the Double Jeopardy Clause bars retrial.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 390. The remaining question is whether the jeopardy ended in such a manner that the defendant may not be retried. See 6 LaFave \xc2\xa725.1(g) (surveying circumstances in which retrial is and is not allowed). Here, there is no doubt that Martinez\xe2\x80\x99s jeopardy ended in a manner that bars his retrial: The trial court acquitted him of the charged offenses. \xe2\x80\x9cPerhaps the most fundamental rule in the history of double jeopardy jurisprudence has been that \xe2\x80\x98[a] verdict of acquittal .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. could not be reviewed .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. without putting [a defendant] twice in jeopardy, and thereby violating the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Martin Linen, supra, at 571.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c[O]ur cases have defined an acquittal to encompass any ruling that the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s proof is insufficient to establish criminal liability for an offense.\xe2\x80\x9d Evans v. Michigan, 568 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2013) (slip op., at 4\xe2\x80\x935). And the trial court clearly made such a ruling here. After the State declined to present evidence against Martinez, his counsel moved for \xe2\x80\x9cdirected findings of not guilty to both counts,\xe2\x80\x9d and the court \xe2\x80\x9cgrant[ed] the motion for a directed finding.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. 21. That is a textbook acquittal: a finding that the State\xe2\x80\x99s evidence cannot support a conviction.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Illinois Supreme Court thought otherwise. It first opined that \xe2\x80\x9c[b]ecause [Martinez] was not placed in jeopardy, the [trial] court\xe2\x80\x99s entry of directed verdicts of not guilty did not constitute true acquittals.\xe2\x80\x9d 2013 IL 113475, \xc2\xb640, 990 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 225. But the premise of that argument is incorrect: Martinez was in jeopardy, for the reasons given above. The court went on to \xe2\x80\x9cnote that, in directing findings of not guilty,\xe2\x80\x9d the trial court \xe2\x80\x9creferred to its action as a \xe2\x80\x98dismissal\xe2\x80\x99 rather than an acquittal.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Under our precedents, however, that is immaterial: \xe2\x80\x9c[W]e have emphasized that what constitutes an \xe2\x80\x98acquittal\xe2\x80\x99 is not to be controlled by the form of the judge\xe2\x80\x99s action\xe2\x80\x9d; it turns on \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the ruling of the judge, whatever its label, actually represents a resolution .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of some or all of the factual elements of the offense charged.\xe2\x80\x9d Martin Linen, 430 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 571; see also Evans, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 11) (\xe2\x80\x9cOur decision turns not on the form of the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s action, but rather whether it \xe2\x80\x98serve[s]\xe2\x80\x99 substantive \xe2\x80\x98purposes\xe2\x80\x99 or procedural ones\xe2\x80\x9d); United States v. Scott, \n\n437 U.\xc2\xa0S. 82, \n96 (1978)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cWe have previously noted that \xe2\x80\x98the trial judge\xe2\x80\x99s characterization of his own action cannot control the classification of the action\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tHere, as in Evans and Martin Linen, the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s action was an acquittal because the court \xe2\x80\x9cacted on its view that the prosecution had failed to prove its case.\xe2\x80\x9d Evans, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 11); see Martin Linen, supra, at 572 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he District Court in this case evaluated the Government\xe2\x80\x99s evidence and determined that it was legally insufficient to sustain a conviction\xe2\x80\x9d). And because Martinez was acquitted, the State cannot retry him.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe functional rule adopted by the Illinois Supreme Court is not necessary to avoid unfairness to prosecutors or to the public. On the day of trial, the court was acutely aware of the significance of swearing a jury. It repeatedly delayed that act to give the State additional time to find its witnesses. It had previously granted the State a number of continuances for the same purpose. See supra, at 2. And, critically, the court told the State on the day of trial that it could \xe2\x80\x9cmove to dismiss [its] case\xe2\x80\x9d before the jury was sworn. Tr. 3. Had the State accepted that invitation, the Double Jeopardy Clause would not have barred it from recharging Martinez. Instead, the State participated in the selection of jurors and did not ask for dismissal before the jury was sworn. When the State declined to dismiss its case, it \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98took a chance[,] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. enter[ing] upon the trial of the case without sufficient evidence to convict.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Downum v. United States, \n\n372 U.\xc2\xa0S. 734, \n737 (1963)\n. Here, the State knew, or should have known, that an acquittal forever bars the retrial of the defendant when it occurs after jeopardy has attached. The Illinois Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding is understandable, given the significant consequence of the State\xe2\x80\x99s mistake, but it runs directly counter to our precedents and to the protection conferred by the Double Jeopardy Clause.\n\t\t\t
