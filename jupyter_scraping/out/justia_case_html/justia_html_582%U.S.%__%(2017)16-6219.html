DAVILA v. DAVIS, DIRECTOR, TEXAS DEPARTMENT OF CRIMINAL JUSTICE, CORRECTIONAL INSTITUTIONS DIVISION

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit

\n

No. 16\xe2\x80\x936219.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 24, 2017\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 26, 2017

\n

In petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s state capital murder trial, the trial court overruled counsel\xe2\x80\x99s objection to a proposed jury instruction and submitted the instruction to the jury, which convicted petitioner. Appellate counsel did not challenge the jury instruction, and petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s conviction and sentence were affirmed. Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s state habeas counsel did not raise the instructional issue or challenge appellate counsel\xe2\x80\x99s failure to raise it on appeal, and the state habeas court denied relief. Petitioner then sought federal habeas relief. Invoking Martinez v. Ryan,\n\n566 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n, and Trevino v. Thaler,\n\n569 U.\xc2\xa0S. 413\n, petitioner argued that his state habeas counsel\xe2\x80\x99s ineffective assistance in failing to raise an ineffective-assistance-of-appellate-counsel claim provided cause to excuse the procedural default of that claim. The District Court denied relief, concluding that Martinez and Trevino apply exclusively to ineffective-assistance-of-trial-counsel claims. The Fifth Circuit denied a certificate of appealability.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The ineffective assistance of postconviction counsel does not provide cause to excuse the procedural default of ineffective-assistance-of-appellate-counsel claims. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0In Coleman v. Thompson,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 722\n, this Court held that attorney error committed in the course of state postconviction proceedings\xe2\x80\x94for which the Constitution does not guarantee the right to counsel\xe2\x80\x94cannot supply cause to excuse a procedural default that occurs in those proceedings. Id., at 755. In Martinez, the Court announced an \xe2\x80\x9cequitable .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. qualification\xe2\x80\x9d of Coleman\xe2\x80\x99s rule that applies where state law requires a claim of ineffective assistance of trial counsel to be raised in an \xe2\x80\x9cinitial-review collateral proceeding,\xe2\x80\x9d rather than on direct appeal. 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 16, 17. In those situations, \xe2\x80\x9ca procedural default will not bar a federal habeas court from hearing a substantial claim of ineffective assistance at trial if\xe2\x80\x9d the default results from the ineffective assistance of the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s counsel in the collateral proceeding. Id., at 17. The Court clarified in Trevino that Martinez\xe2\x80\x99s exception also applies where the State\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cprocedural framework, by reason of its design and operation, makes it unlikely in a typical case that a defendant will have a meaningful opportunity to raise\xe2\x80\x9d the claim on direct appeal. 569 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0This Court declines to extend the Martinez exception to allow a federal court to hear a substantial, but procedurally defaulted, claim of appellate ineffectiveness when a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s state postconviction counsel provides ineffective assistance by failing to raise it. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

(1)\xc2\xa0Martinez itself does not support extending this exception to new categories of procedurally defaulted claims. The Martinez Court did not purport to displace Coleman as the general rule governing procedural default. Rather, it \xe2\x80\x9cqualifie[d] Coleman by recognizing a narrow exception,\xe2\x80\x9d 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 9, and made clear that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he rule of Coleman governs in all but th[ose] limited circumstances,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 16. Applying Martinez\xe2\x80\x99s highly circumscribed, equitable exception to new categories of procedurally defaulted claims would do precisely what this Court disclaimed in that case. P.\xc2\xa07.

\n

(2)\xc2\xa0Martinez\xe2\x80\x99s underlying rationale does not support extending its exception to appellate-ineffectiveness claims. Petitioner argues that his situation is analogous to Martinez, where the Court expressed concern that trial-ineffectiveness claims might completely evade review. The Court in Martinez made clear, however, that it exercised its equitable discretion in view of the unique importance of protecting a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s trial rights, particularly the right to effective assistance of trial counsel. Declining to expand Martinez to the appellate-ineffectiveness context does no more than respect that judgment. Nor is petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s rule required to ensure that meritorious claims of trial error receive review by at least one state or federal court\xe2\x80\x94Martinez\xe2\x80\x99s chief concern. See 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 10, 12. A claim of trial error, preserved by trial counsel but not raised by counsel on appeal, will have been addressed by the trial court. If an unpreserved trial error was so obvious that appellate counsel was constitutionally required to raise it on appeal, then trial counsel likely provided ineffective assistance by failing to raise it at trial. In that circumstance, the prisoner likely could invoke Martinez or Coleman to obtain review of trial counsel\xe2\x80\x99s failure to object. Similarly, if the underlying, defaulted claim of trial error was ineffective assistance of trial counsel premised on something other than the failure to object, then Martinez and Coleman again already provide a vehicle for obtaining review of that error in most circumstances. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

(3)\xc2\xa0The equitable concerns addressed in Martinez do not apply to appellate-ineffectiveness claims. In Martinez and Trevino, the States deliberately chose to make postconviction process the only means for raising trial-ineffectiveness claims. The Court determined that it would be inequitable to refuse to hear a defaulted claim when the State had channeled that claim to a forum where the prisoner might lack the assistance of counsel in raising it. The States have not made a similar choice with respect to appellate-ineffectiveness claims\xe2\x80\x94nor could they, since such claims generally cannot be presented until after the termination of direct appeal. The fact that appellate-ineffectiveness claims are considered in proceedings in which counsel is not constitutionally guaranteed is a function of the nature of the claim, not of the States\xe2\x80\x99 deliberate choices. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

(4)\xc2\xa0The Martinez decision was also grounded in part on the belief that its narrow exception was unlikely to impose significant systemic costs. See 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 15\xe2\x80\x9316. But adopting petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s proposed extension could flood the federal courts with defaulted appellate-ineffectiveness claims, and potentially serve as a gateway to federal review of a host of defaulted claims of trial error. It would also aggravate the harm to federalism that federal habeas review of state convictions necessarily causes. Not only would these burdens on the federal courts and federal system be severe, but the systemic benefit would be small, as claims heard in federal court solely by virtue of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s proposed rule would likely be largely meritless. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

650 Fed. Appx. 860, affirmed.

\n

ERICK DANIEL DAVILA, PETITIONER v. LORIE DAVIS, DIRECTOR, TEXAS DEPARTMENT OF CRIMINAL JUSTICE, CORRECTIONAL INSTITUTIONSDIVISION

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit

\n

[June 26, 2017]

\n

Justice Thomas delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

Federal habeas courts reviewing convictions from state courts will not consider claims that a state court refused to hear based on an adequate and independent state procedural ground. A state prisoner may be able to overcome this bar, however, if he can establish \xe2\x80\x9ccause\xe2\x80\x9d to excuse the procedural default and demonstrate that he suffered actual prejudice from the alleged error. An attorney error does not qualify as \xe2\x80\x9ccause\xe2\x80\x9d to excuse a procedural default unless the error amounted to constitutionally ineffective assistance of counsel. Because a prisoner does not have a constitutional right to counsel in state postconviction proceedings, ineffective assistance in those proceedings does not qualify as cause to excuse a procedural default. See Coleman v. Thompson,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 722 (1991)\n.

\n

In Martinez v. Ryan,\n\n566 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1 (2012)\n, and Trevino v. Thaler,\n\n569 U.\xc2\xa0S. 413 (2013)\n, this Court announced a narrow exception to Coleman\xe2\x80\x99s general rule. That exception treats ineffective assistance by a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s state postconviction counsel as cause to overcome the default of a single claim\xe2\x80\x94ineffective assistance of trial counsel\xe2\x80\x94in a single context\xe2\x80\x94where the State effectively requires a defendant to bring that claim in state postconviction proceedings rather than on direct appeal. The question in this case is whether we should extend that exception to allow federal courts to consider a different kind of defaulted claim\xe2\x80\x94ineffective assistance of appellate counsel. We decline to do so.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

On April 6, 2008, a group of family and friends gathered at Annette Stevenson\xe2\x80\x99s home to celebrate her granddaughter\xe2\x80\x99s birthday. Petitioner Erick Daniel Davila, believing he had seen a member of a rival street gang at the celebration, fired a rifle at the group while they were eating cake and ice cream. He shot and killed Annette and her 5-year-old granddaughter Queshawn, and he wounded three other children and one woman.

\n

After the police arrested petitioner, he confessed to the killings. He stated that he \xe2\x80\x9cwasn\xe2\x80\x99t aiming at the kids or the woman,\xe2\x80\x9d but that he was trying to kill Annette\xe2\x80\x99s son (and Queshawn\xe2\x80\x99s father) Jerry Stevenson and the other \xe2\x80\x9cguys on the porch.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 38. The other \xe2\x80\x9cguys on the porch\xe2\x80\x9d were, apparently, women.

\n

The State indicted petitioner for capital murder under Tex. Penal Code Ann. \xc2\xa719.03(a)(7)(A) (West 2016), which makes it a capital crime to \xe2\x80\x9cmurde[r] more than one person .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. during the same criminal transaction.\xe2\x80\x9d In response to the jury\xe2\x80\x99s request for clarification during deliberations, the trial court proposed instructing the jury on transferred intent. Under that doctrine, the jury could find petitioner guilty of murder if it determined that he intended to kill one person but instead killed a different person. Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s counsel objected to the additional instruction, arguing that the trial judge should \xe2\x80\x9cwait\xe2\x80\x9d to submit it \xe2\x80\x9cuntil the jury indicates that they can\xe2\x80\x99t reach .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a resolution.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 51. The trial court overruled the objection and submitted the instruction to the jury. The jury convicted petitioner of capital murder, and the trial court sentenced petitioner to death.

\n

B

\n

Petitioner appealed his conviction and sentence. Al-though his appellate counsel argued that the State presented insufficient evidence to show that he acted with the requisite intent, counsel did not challenge the instruction about transferred intent. The Texas Court of Criminal Appeals affirmed petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s conviction and sentence. Davila v. State, 2011 WL 303265 (Jan. 26, 2011), cert. denied,\n\n565 U.\xc2\xa0S. 885 (2011)\n.

\n

Petitioner next sought habeas relief in Texas state court. His counsel did not challenge the instruction about transferred intent, nor did he challenge the failure of his appellate counsel to raise the alleged instructional error on direct appeal. The Texas Court of Criminal Appeals denied relief. Ex\xc2\xa0parte Davila, 2013 WL 1655549 (Apr. 17, 2013), cert. denied, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2013).

\n

C

\n

Petitioner then sought habeas relief in Federal District Court under\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254. As relevant here, he argued that his appellate counsel provided ineffective assistance by failing to challenge the jury instruction about transferred intent. Petitioner conceded that he had failed to raise his claim of ineffective assistance of appellate counsel in his state habeas petition, but argued that the failure was the result of his state habeas counsel\xe2\x80\x99s ineffective assistance. Petitioner invoked this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions in Martinez and Trevino to argue that his state habeas attorney\xe2\x80\x99s ineffective assistance provided cause to excuse the procedural default of his claim of ineffective assistance of appellate counsel.

\n

The District Court denied petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s \xc2\xa72254 petition. It concluded that Martinez and Trevino did not supply cause to excuse the procedural default of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claim of ineffective assistance of appellate counsel because those decisions applied exclusively to claims of ineffective assistance of trial counsel. See Davila v. Stephens, 2015 WL 1808689, *20 (ND Tex., Apr. 21, 2015). The Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit denied a certificate of appealability on the same ground. 650 Fed.\xc2\xa0Appx. 860, 867\xe2\x80\x93868 (2016). Petitioner then sought a writ of certiorari, asking us to reverse the Fifth Circuit on the ground that Martinez and Trevino should be extended to claims of ineffective assistance of appellate counsel. We granted certiorari, 580 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2017), and now affirm.

\n

II

\n

Our decision in this case is guided by two fundamental tenets of federal review of state convictions. First, a state prisoner must exhaust available state remedies before presenting his claim to a federal habeas court. \xc2\xa72254(b)(1)(A). The exhaustion requirement is designed to avoid the \xe2\x80\x9cunseemly\xe2\x80\x9d result of a federal court \xe2\x80\x9cupset[ting] a state court conviction without\xe2\x80\x9d first according the state courts an \xe2\x80\x9copportunity to .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. correct a constitutional violation,\xe2\x80\x9d Rose v. Lundy,\n\n455 U.\xc2\xa0S. 509,\n518 (1982)\n (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

Second, a federal court may not review federal claims that were procedurally defaulted in state court\xe2\x80\x94that is, claims that the state court denied based on an adequate and independent state procedural rule. E.g., Beard v. Kindler,\n\n558 U.\xc2\xa0S. 53,\n55 (2009)\n. This is an important \xe2\x80\x9ccorollary\xe2\x80\x9d to the exhaustion requirement. Dretke v. Haley,\n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 386,\n392 (2004)\n. \xe2\x80\x9cJust as in those cases in which a state prisoner fails to exhaust state remedies, a habeas petitioner who has failed to meet the State\xe2\x80\x99s procedural requirements for presenting his federal claims has deprived the state courts of an opportunity to address\xe2\x80\x9d the merits of \xe2\x80\x9cthose claims in the first instance.\xe2\x80\x9d Coleman, 501 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 731\xe2\x80\x93732.[1] The procedural default doctrine thus advances the same comity, finality, and federalism interests advanced by the exhaustion doctrine. See McCleskey v. Zant,\n\n499 U.\xc2\xa0S. 467,\n493 (1991)\n.

\n

A state prisoner may overcome the prohibition on reviewing procedurally defaulted claims if he can show \xe2\x80\x9ccause\xe2\x80\x9d to excuse his failure to comply with the state procedural rule and \xe2\x80\x9cactual prejudice resulting from the alleged constitutional violation.\xe2\x80\x9d Wainwright v. Sykes,\n\n433 U.\xc2\xa0S. 72,\n84 (1977)\n; Coleman, supra, at 750. To establish \xe2\x80\x9ccause\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94the element of the doctrine relevant in this case\xe2\x80\x94the prisoner must \xe2\x80\x9cshow that some objective factor external to the defense impeded counsel\xe2\x80\x99s efforts to comply with the State\xe2\x80\x99s procedural rule.\xe2\x80\x9d Murray v. Carrier,\n\n477 U.\xc2\xa0S. 478,\n488 (1986)\n. A factor is external to the defense if it \xe2\x80\x9ccannot fairly be attributed to\xe2\x80\x9d the prisoner. Coleman, supra, at 753.

\n

It has long been the rule that attorney error is an objective external factor providing cause for excusing a procedural default only if that error amounted to a deprivation of the constitutional right to counsel. See Edwards v. Carpenter,\n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 446,\n451 (2000)\n. An error amounting to constitutionally ineffective assistance is \xe2\x80\x9cimputed to the State\xe2\x80\x9d and is therefore external to the prisoner. Murray, supra, at 488. Attorney error that does not violate the Constitution, however, is attributed to the prisoner \xe2\x80\x9cunder well-settled principles of agency law.\xe2\x80\x9d Coleman, supra, at 754. It follows, then, that in proceedings for which the Constitution does not guarantee the assistance of counsel at all, attorney error cannot provide cause to excuse a default. Thus, in Coleman, this Court held that attorney error committed in the course of state postconviction proceedings\xe2\x80\x94for which the Constitution does not guarantee the right to counsel, see Murray v. Giarratano,\n\n492 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1 (1989)\n (plurality opinion)\xe2\x80\x94cannot supply cause to excuse a procedural default that occurs in those proceedings. 501 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 755.

\n

In Martinez, this Court announced a narrow, \xe2\x80\x9cequitable .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. qualification\xe2\x80\x9d of the rule in Coleman that applies where state law requires prisoners to raise claims of ineffective assistance of trial counsel \xe2\x80\x9cin an initial-review collateral proceeding,\xe2\x80\x9d rather than on direct appeal. Martinez, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 16, 17. It held that, in those situations, \xe2\x80\x9ca procedural default will not bar a federal habeas court from hearing a substantial claim of ineffective assistance at trial if\xe2\x80\x9d the default results from the ineffective assistance of the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s counsel in the collateral proceeding. Id., at 17. In Trevino, the Court clarified that this exception applies both where state law explicitly prohibits prisoners from bringing claims of ineffective assistance of trial counsel on direct appeal and where the State\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cprocedural framework, by reason of its design and operation, makes it unlikely in a typical case that a defendant will have a meaningful opportunity to raise\xe2\x80\x9d that claim on direct appeal. 569 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 14).

\n

III

\n

Petitioner asks us to extend Martinez to allow a federal court to hear a substantial, but procedurally defaulted, claim of ineffective assistance of appellate counsel when a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s state postconviction counsel provides ineffective assistance by failing to raise that claim. We decline to do so.

\n

A

\n

On its face, Martinez provides no support for extending its narrow exception to new categories of procedurally defaulted claims. Martinez did not purport to displace Coleman as the general rule governing procedural default. Rather, it \xe2\x80\x9cqualifie[d] Coleman by recognizing a narrow exception\xe2\x80\x9d that applies only to claims of \xe2\x80\x9cineffective assistance of counsel at trial\xe2\x80\x9d and only when, \xe2\x80\x9cunder state law,\xe2\x80\x9d those claims \xe2\x80\x9cmust be raised in an initial-review collateral proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d Martinez, supra, at 9, 17. And Trevino merely clarified that the exception applies whether state law explicitly or effectively forecloses review of the claim on direct appeal. 569 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op. at 2, 13). In all but those \xe2\x80\x9climited circumstances,\xe2\x80\x9d Martinez made clear that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he rule of Coleman governs.\xe2\x80\x9d 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 16. Applying Martinez\xe2\x80\x99s highly circumscribed, equitable exception to new categories of procedurally defaulted claims would thus do precisely what this Court disclaimed in Martinez: Replace the rule of Coleman with the exception of Martinez.

\n

B

\n

Petitioner also finds no support in the underlying rationale of Martinez. Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s primary argument is that his claim of ineffective assistance of appellate counsel might never be reviewed by any court, state or federal, without expanding the exception to the rule in Coleman. He argues that this situation is analogous to Martinez, where the Court expressed that same concern about claims of ineffective assistance of trial counsel. But the Court in Martinez was principally concerned about trial errors\xe2\x80\x94in particular, claims of ineffective assistance of trial counsel. Ineffective assistance of appellate counsel is not a trial error. Nor is petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s rule necessary to ensure that a meritorious trial error (of any kind) receives review.

\n

1

\n

Petitioner argues that allowing a claim of ineffective assistance of appellate counsel to evade review is just as concerning as allowing a claim of ineffective assistance of trial counsel to evade review. Brief for Petitioner 12; see also id., at 18\xe2\x80\x9326. We do not agree.

\n

The criminal trial enjoys pride of place in our criminal justice system in a way that an appeal from that trial does not. The Constitution twice guarantees the right to a criminal trial, see Art. III, \xc2\xa72; Amdt. 6, but does not guarantee the right to an appeal at all, Halbert v. Michigan,\n\n545 U.\xc2\xa0S. 605,\n610 (2005)\n. The trial \xe2\x80\x9cis the main event at which a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s rights are to be determined,\xe2\x80\x9d McFarland v. Scott,\n\n512 U.\xc2\xa0S. 849,\n859 (1994)\n (internal quotation marks omitted), \xe2\x80\x9cand not simply a tryout on the road to appellate review,\xe2\x80\x9d Freytag v. Commissioner,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 868,\n895 (1991)\n (Scalia, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment) (internal quotation marks omitted). And it is where the stakes for the defendant are highest, not least because it is where a presumptively innocent defendant is adjudged guilty, see Ross v. Moffitt,\n\n417 U.\xc2\xa0S. 600,\n610 (1974)\n; Wainwright, 433 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 90, and where the trial judge or jury makes factual findings that nearly always receive deference on appeal and collateral review, see Jackson v. Virginia,\n\n443 U.\xc2\xa0S. 307\n\xe2\x80\x93319 (1979); see also Cavazos v. Smith,\n\n565 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1,\n2 (2011)\n (per curiam) (under deferential standard of review, \xe2\x80\x9cjudges will sometimes encounter convictions that they believe to be mistaken, but that they must nevertheless uphold\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

The Court in Martinez made clear that it exercised its equitable discretion in view of the unique importance of protecting a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s trial rights, particularly the right to effective assistance of trial counsel. As the Court explained, \xe2\x80\x9cthe limited nature\xe2\x80\x9d of its holding \xe2\x80\x9creflect[ed] the importance of the right to the effective assistance of trial counsel,\xe2\x80\x9d which is \xe2\x80\x9ca bedrock principle in our justice system.\xe2\x80\x9d 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 12, 16 (emphasis added). In declining to expand the Martinez exception to the distinct context of ineffective assistance of appellate counsel, we do no more than respect that judgment.

\n

2

\n

Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s rule also is not required to ensure that meritorious claims of trial error receive review by at least one state or federal court\xe2\x80\x94the chief concern identified by this Court in Martinez. See id., at 10, 12. Martinez was concerned that a claim of trial error\xe2\x80\x94specifically, ineffective assistance of trial counsel\xe2\x80\x94might escape review in a State that required prisoners to bring the claim for the first time in state postconviction proceedings rather than on direct appeal. Because it is difficult to assess a trial attorney\xe2\x80\x99s performance until the trial has ended, a trial court ordinarily will not have the opportunity to rule on such a claim. And when the State requires a prisoner to wait until postconviction proceedings to raise the claim, the appellate court on direct appeal also will not have the opportunity to review it. If postconviction counsel then fails to raise the claim, no state court will ever review it. Finally, because attorney error in a state postconviction proceeding does not qualify as cause to excuse procedural default under Coleman, no federal court could consider the claim either.

\n

Claims of ineffective assistance of appellate counsel, however, do not pose the same risk that a trial error\xe2\x80\x94of any kind\xe2\x80\x94will escape review altogether, at least in a way that could be remedied by petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s proposed rule. This is true regardless of whether trial counsel preserved the alleged error at trial. If trial counsel preserved the error by properly objecting, then that claim of trial error \xe2\x80\x9cwill have been addressed by .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the trial court.\xe2\x80\x9d Martinez, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 11. A claim of appellate ineffectiveness premised on a preserved trial error thus does not present the same concern that animated the Martinez exception because at least \xe2\x80\x9cone court\xe2\x80\x9d will have considered the claim on the merits. Ibid.; see also Coleman, 501 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 755\xe2\x80\x93756.

\n

If trial counsel failed to preserve the error at trial, then petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s proposed rule ordinarily would not give the prisoner access to federal review of the error, anyway. Effective appellate counsel should not raise every nonfrivolous argument on appeal, but rather only those arguments most likely to succeed. Smith v. Murray,\n\n477 U.\xc2\xa0S. 527,\n536 (1986)\n; Jones v. Barnes,\n\n463 U.\xc2\xa0S. 745\n\xe2\x80\x93753 (1983). Declining to raise a claim on appeal, therefore, is not deficient performance unless that claim was plainly stronger than those actually presented to the appellate court. See Smith v. Robbins,\n\n528 U.\xc2\xa0S. 259,\n288 (2000)\n. In most cases, an unpreserved trial error will not be a plainly stronger ground for appeal than preserved errors. See 2 B. Means, Postconviction Remedies \xc2\xa735:19, p. 627, and n.\xc2\xa016 (2016). Thus, in most instances in which the trial court did not rule on the alleged trial error (because it was not preserved), the prisoner could not make out a substantial claim of ineffective assistance of appellate counsel and therefore could not avail himself of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s expanded Martinez exception.

\n

Adopting petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s proposed rule would be unnecessary to ensure review of a claim of trial error even when a prisoner has a legitimate claim of ineffective assistance of appellate counsel based on something other than a preserved trial error. If an unpreserved trial error was so obvious that appellate counsel was constitutionally required to raise it on appeal, then trial counsel likely provided ineffective assistance by failing to object to it in the first instance. In that circumstance, the prisoner likely could invoke Martinez or Coleman to obtain review of trial counsel\xe2\x80\x99s failure to object. Similarly, if the underlying, defaulted claim of trial error was ineffective assistance of trial counsel premised on something other than the failure to object, then Martinez and Coleman again already provide a vehicle for obtaining review of that error in most circumstances. Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s proposed rule is thus unnecessary for ensuring that trial errors are reviewed by at least one court.

\n

C

\n

The Court in Martinez also was responding to an equitable consideration that is unique to claims of ineffective assistance of trial counsel and accordingly inapplicable to claims of ineffective assistance of appellate counsel. In Martinez, the State \xe2\x80\x9cdeliberately cho[se] to move trial-ineffectiveness claims outside of the direct-appeal process, where counsel is constitutionally guaranteed,\xe2\x80\x9d into the postconviction review process, where we have never held that the Constitution guarantees a right to counsel. 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 13; id., at 9. By doing so, \xe2\x80\x9cthe State significantly diminishe[d] prisoners\xe2\x80\x99 ability to file such claims.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 13. Similarly, in Trevino, the State had chosen a procedural framework pursuant to which collateral review was, \xe2\x80\x9cas a practical matter, the onl[y] method for raising an ineffective-assistance-of-trial-counsel claim.\xe2\x80\x9d 569 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 13).

\n

Although this Court acknowledged in Martinez that there was nothing inappropriate about the State\xe2\x80\x99s choice, it explained that the choice was \xe2\x80\x9cnot without consequences for the State\xe2\x80\x99s ability to assert a procedural default\xe2\x80\x9d in subsequent federal habeas proceedings. 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 13. Specifically, the Court concluded that it would be inequitable to refuse to hear a defaulted claim of ineffective assistance of trial counsel when the State had channeled that claim to a forum where the prisoner might lack the assistance of counsel in raising it.

\n

The States have not made a similar choice with respect to claims of ineffective assistance of appellate counsel\xe2\x80\x94nor could they. By their very nature, such claims gener-ally cannot be presented until after the termination of direct appeal. Put another way, they necessarily must be heard in collateral proceedings, where counsel is not constitutionally guaranteed. The fact that claims of appellate ineffectiveness are considered in proceedings in which counsel is not constitutionally guaranteed is a function of the nature of the claim, not of the State\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cdeliberat[e] cho[ice] to move .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. claims outside of the direct-appeal process.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The equitable concerns raised in Martinez therefore do not apply.

\n

D

\n

Finally, the Court in Martinez grounded its decision in part on the belief that its narrow exception was unlikely to impose significant systemic costs. See id., at 15\xe2\x80\x9316. The same cannot be said of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s proposed extension.

\n

1

\n

Adopting petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s argument could flood the federal courts with defaulted claims of appellate ineffectiveness. For one thing, every prisoner in the country could bring these claims. Martinez currently applies only to States that deliberately choose to channel claims of ineffective assistance of trial counsel into collateral proceedings. See, e.g., Lee v. Corsini, 777 F.\xc2\xa03d 46, 60\xe2\x80\x9361 (CA1 2015) (Martinez and Trevino do not apply to Massachusetts); Henness v. Bagley, 766 F.\xc2\xa03d 550, 557 (CA6 2014) (Martinez does not apply to Ohio). If we applied Martinez to claims of appellate ineffectiveness, however, we would bring every State within Martinez\xe2\x80\x99s ambit, because claims of appellate ineffectiveness necessarily must be heard in collateral proceedings. See supra, at 12.

\n

Extending Martinez to defaulted claims of ineffective assistance of appellate counsel would be especially troublesome because those claims could serve as the gatewayto federal review of a host of trial errors, while Martinez covers only one trial error (ineffective assistance of trial counsel). If a prisoner can establish ineffective assistance of trial counsel under Martinez, he ordinarily is entitled to a new trial. See United States v. Morrison,\n\n449 U.\xc2\xa0S. 361\n\xe2\x80\x93365 (1981); see also Hagens v. State, 979 S.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 788, 792 (Tex. App. 1998). But if he cannot, Martinez provides no avenue for litigating other defaulted trial errors.[2]

\n

An expanded Martinez exception, however, would mean that any defaulted trial error could result in a new trial. In Carpenter, this Court held that, when a prisoner can show cause to excuse a defaulted claim of ineffective assistance of appellate counsel, he can in turn rely on that claim as cause to litigate an underlying claim of trial error that was defaulted due to appellate counsel\xe2\x80\x99s ineffectiveness. 529 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 453. Expanding Martinez as petitioner suggests would thus produce a domino effect: Prisoners could assert their postconviction counsel\xe2\x80\x99s inadequacy as cause to excuse the default of their appellate ineffectiveness claims, and use those newly reviewable appellate ineffectiveness claims as cause to excuse the default of their underlying claims of trial error. Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s rule thus could ultimately knock down the procedural barriers to federal habeas review of nearly any defaulted claim of trial error. The scope of that review would exceed anything the Martinez Court envisioned when it established its narrow exception to Coleman.

\n

Petitioner insists that these concerns are overstated because many of the newly raised claims will be meritless. See Brief for Petitioner 28. But even if that were true, courts would still have to undertake the task of separating the wheat from the chaff. And we are not reassured by petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion that extending Martinez would increase only the number of claims in each petition rather than the number of federal habeas petitions themselves. Reply Brief 14. Each additional claim would require the district court to review the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s trial record, appellate briefing, and state postconviction record to determine the claim\xe2\x80\x99s viability. This effort could be repeated at each level of federal review. We cannot \xe2\x80\x9cassume that these costs would be negligible,\xe2\x80\x9d Murray, 477 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 487, and we are loath to further \xe2\x80\x9cburden .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. scarce federal judicial resources\xe2\x80\x9d in this way, McCleskey, 499 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 491.

\n

2

\n

Expanding Martinez would not only impose significant costs on the federal courts, but would also aggravate the harm to federalism that federal habeas review necessarily causes. Federal habeas review of state convictions \xe2\x80\x9centails significant costs,\xe2\x80\x9d Engle v. Isaac,\n\n456 U.\xc2\xa0S. 107,\n126 (1982)\n, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98and intrudes on state sovereignty to a degree matched by few exercises of federal judicial authority,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Harrington v. Richter,\n\n562 U.\xc2\xa0S. 86,\n103 (2011)\n (quoting Harris v. Reed,\n\n489 U.\xc2\xa0S. 255,\n282 (1989)\n (Kennedy, J., dissenting)). It \xe2\x80\x9cfrustrates both the States\xe2\x80\x99 sovereign power to punish offenders and their good-faith attempts to honor constitutional rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Calderon v. Thompson,\n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 538\n\xe2\x80\x93556 (1998) (internal quotation marks omitted). It \xe2\x80\x9cdegrades the prominence of the [State] trial,\xe2\x80\x9d Engle, supra, at 127, and it \xe2\x80\x9cdisturbs the State\xe2\x80\x99s significant interest in repose for concluded litigation [and] denies society the right to punish some admitted offenders,\xe2\x80\x9d Harrington, supra, at 103 (internal quotation marksomitted).

\n

Apart from increasing the sheer frequency of federal intrusion into state criminal affairs, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s proposed rule would also undermine the doctrine of procedural default and the values it serves. That doctrine, like the federal habeas statute generally, is designed to ameliorate the injuries to state sovereignty that federal habeas review necessarily inflicts by giving state courts the first opportunity to address challenges to convictions in state court, thereby \xe2\x80\x9cpromoting comity, finality, and federalism.\xe2\x80\x9d Cullen v. Pinholster,\n\n563 U.\xc2\xa0S. 170,\n185 (2011)\n; McCleskey, supra, at 493. Expanding the narrow exception announced in Martinez would unduly aggravate the \xe2\x80\x9cspecial costs on our federal system\xe2\x80\x9d that federal habeas review already imposes. Engle, supra, at 128.

\n

3

\n

Not only would these burdens on the federal courts and our federal system be severe, but the benefit would\xe2\x80\x94as a systemic matter\xe2\x80\x94be small. To be sure, permitting a state prisoner to bring a meritorious constitutional claim that could not otherwise be heard is beneficial to that prisoner. Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s counsel concedes, however, that relief is granted in, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f any, a very minute number\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9cpost-conviction ineffective assistance of appellate counselcases.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 14. Indeed, he concedes that the number of meritorious cases is \xe2\x80\x9cinfinitesimally small.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. We think it is likely that the claims heard in federal court because of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s proposed rule would also be largely meritless, given that the proposed rule would generally affect only those cases in which the trial court already adjudicated, and rejected, the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s argument regarding the alleged underlying trial error. See supra, at 11. Given that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s proposed rule would likely generate high systemic costs and low systemic benefits, and that the unique concerns of Martinez are not implicated in cases like his, we do not think equity requires anexpansion of Martinez.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

For the foregoing reasons, we affirm the judgment of the Court of Appeals.

\n

It is so ordered.

ERICK DANIEL DAVILA, PETITIONER v. LORIE DAVIS, DIRECTOR, TEXAS DEPARTMENT OF CRIMINAL JUSTICE, CORRECTIONAL INSTITUTIONSDIVISION

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit

\n

[June 26, 2017]

\n

\n

Justice Breyer, with whom Justice Ginsburg, Justice Sotomayor, and Justice Kagan join, dissenting.

\n

As the Court explains, normally a federal habeas court cannot hear a state prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s claim that his trial lawyer was, constitutionally speaking, \xe2\x80\x9cineffective\xe2\x80\x9d if the prisoner failed to assert that claim in state court at the appropriate time, that is, if he procedurally defaulted the claim. See ante, at 1 (the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s failure to raise his federal claim at the initial-review state collateral proceeding amounts to an \xe2\x80\x9cadequate and independent state procedural ground\xe2\x80\x9d for denying habeas relief).

\n

But there are equitable exceptions. In Martinez v. Ryan,\n\n566 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1 (2012)\n, and later in Trevino v. Thaler,\n\n569 U.\xc2\xa0S. 413 (2013)\n, we held that, despite the presence of a procedural default, a federal court can nonetheless hear a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s claim that his trial counsel was ineffective, where (1) the framework of state procedural law \xe2\x80\x9cmakes it highly unlikely in a typical case that a defendant will have a meaningful opportunity to raise a claim of ineffective assistance of trial counsel on direct appeal,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 429; (2) in the state \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98initial-review collateral proceeding, there was no counsel or counsel in that proceeding was ineffective,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. (quoting Martinez, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 17); and (3) \xe2\x80\x9cthe underlying ineffective-assistance-of-trial-counsel claim is a substantial one, which is to say that the prisoner must demonstrate that the claim has some merit,\xe2\x80\x9d id.,at 14.

\n

In my view, this same exception (with the same qualifications) should apply when a prisoner raises a constitutional claim of ineffective assistance of appellate counsel. See, e.g., Evitts v. Lucey,\n\n469 U.\xc2\xa0S. 387,\n396 (1985)\n (Constitution guarantees a defendant an effective appellate counsel, just as it guarantees a defendant an effective trial counsel).

\n

I

\n

Two simple examples help make clear why I believe Martinez and Trevino should govern the outcome of this case.

\n

Example One: Ineffective assistance of trial counsel. The prisoner claims that his trial lawyer was ineffective, say, because counsel failed to object to an obviously unfair jury selection, failed to point out that the prosecution had promised numerous benefits to its main witness in return for the witness\xe2\x80\x99 testimony, or failed to object to an erroneous jury instruction that made conviction and imposition of the death penalty far more likely. Next suppose the prisoner appeals but, per state law, may not bring his ineffective-assistance claim until collateral review in state court (i.e., state habeas corpus), where the prisoner will have a better opportunity to develop his claim and the attorney will be better able to explain his (perhaps strategic) reasons for his actions at trial. Suppose that, on collateral review, the prisoner fails to bring up his ineffective-assistance claim, perhaps because he is no longer represented by counsel or because his counsel there is ineffective. Under these circumstances, if his ineffective-assistance claim is a \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial\xe2\x80\x9d one, i.e., it has \xe2\x80\x9csome merit,\xe2\x80\x9d then Martinez and Trevino hold that a federal court can hear the claim even though the state habeas court did not consider it. See Trevino, supra, at 429; Martinez, supra, at 14. The fact that the prisoner had no lawyer in the initial state habeas proceeding (or his lawyer in that proceeding was ineffective) constitutes grounds for excusing the procedural default.

\n

Example Two: Ineffective assistance of appellate counsel. Now suppose that a prisoner claims that the trial court made an important error of law, say, improperly instructing the jury, or that the prosecution engaged in misconduct. He believes his lawyer on direct appeal should have raised those errors because they led to his conviction or (as here) a death sentence. The appellate lawyer\xe2\x80\x99s failure to do so, the prisoner might claim, amounts to ineffective assistance of appellate counsel. The prisoner cannot make this argument on direct appeal, for the direct appeal is the very proceeding in which he is represented by the lawyer he says was ineffective. Next suppose the prisoner fails to raise his appellate lawyer\xe2\x80\x99s ineffectiveness at the initial state habeas proceeding, either because he was not represented by counsel in that proceeding or because his counsel there also was ineffective. When he brings his case to the federal habeas court, the State contends that the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s failure to present his claim during the initial state habeas proceeding constitutes a procedural default that precludes federal review of his claim.

\n

Given Martinez and Trevino, the prisoner in the first example who complains about his trial counsel can overcome the procedural default but, in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s view today, the prisoner in the second example who complains about his appellate counsel cannot. Why should the law treat the second prisoner differently? Why should the Court not apply the rules of Martinez and Trevino to claims of ineffective assistance of both trial and appellate counsel?

\n

II

\n

As I have said, the Constitution applies similarly to both prisoners: It guarantees them effective assistance of counsel at both trial and during an initial appeal. See Strickland v. Washington,\n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 668,\n686 (1984)\n (trial); Evitts, supra, at 396 (appeal). Moreover, the reasoning of Martinez and Trevino applies similarly to both situations.

\n

Four features of the claim of ineffective assistance of trial counsel led the Martinez Court to its conclusion. Each equally applies here. First, the Court stressed the importance of the underlying constitutional right to effective assistance of trial counsel, describing it as \xe2\x80\x9ca bedrock principle in our justice system.\xe2\x80\x9d 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 12. Our cases make clear that the constitutional right to effective assistance of appellate counsel is also critically important. The Court wrote in Douglas v. California,\n\n372 U.\xc2\xa0S. 353,\n357 (1963)\n, that \xe2\x80\x9cwhere the merits of the one and only appeal .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. as of right are decided without benefit of counsel, we think an unconstitutional line has been drawn between rich and poor.\xe2\x80\x9d The Court held in Evitts that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] first appeal as of right .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is not adjudicated in accord with due process of law if the appellant does not have the effective assistance of an attorney.\xe2\x80\x9d 469 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 396. The Court added that \xe2\x80\x9cthe promise of Gideon [v. Wainwright,\n\n372 U.\xc2\xa0S. 335 (1963)\n,] that a criminal defendant has a right to counsel at trial .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. would be a futile gesture unless it comprehended the right to the effective assistance of counsel\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9con appeal.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 397. And we stated in Martinez itself that \xe2\x80\x9cif the attorney appointed by the State to pursue the direct appeal is ineffective, the prisoner has been denied fair process.\xe2\x80\x9d 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 11 (citing Coleman v. Thompson,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 722,\n754 (1991)\n; Evitts, supra, at 396; Douglas, supra, at 357\xe2\x80\x93358). The fact that, according to Department of Justice statistics, nearly a third of convictions or sentences in capital cases are overturned at some stage of review suggests the practical importance of the appeal right, particularly in a capital case such as this one. See Dept. of Justice, Bureau of Justice Statistics, Capital Punishment, 2013\xe2\x80\x93Statistical Tables, p. 19, (rev. Dec. 2014) (Table 16); see also Brief for National Association of Criminal Defense Lawyers et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 10.

\n

Second, we pointed out in Martinez that the \xe2\x80\x9cinitial\xe2\x80\x9d state collateral review proceeding \xe2\x80\x9cis the first designated proceeding for a prisoner to raise a claim of ineffective assistance at trial.\xe2\x80\x9d 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 11. We added that it \xe2\x80\x9cis in many ways the equivalent of a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s direct appeal as to the ineffective-assistance claim.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. In Trevino, we applied Martinez despite the theoretical possibility that a prisoner might raise an ineffective-assistance-of-trial-counsel claim on direct appeal. We wrote that the State\xe2\x80\x99s procedural system denied prisoners a \xe2\x80\x9cmeaningful opportunity\xe2\x80\x9d to bring ineffective-assistance claims on appeal; in effect, it required them to raise the claim for the first time in state collateral review proceedings. 569 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 429.

\n

This consideration applies a fortiori where the constitutional claim at issue is ineffective assistance of appellate counsel. The prisoner cannot raise that kind of claim in the very appeal in which he claims his counsel was ineffective. See Ha Van Nguyen v. Curry, 736 F.\xc2\xa03d 1287, 1294\xe2\x80\x931295 (CA9 2013). It makes no difference that the nature of the claim, rather than the State\xe2\x80\x99s express rule, makes that so. See Trevino, supra, at 429 (extending Martinez where the \xe2\x80\x9cstate procedural framework, by reason of its design and operation, makes it highly unlikely in a typical case that a defendant will have a meaningful opportunity to raise\xe2\x80\x9d the claim on direct appeal); Trevino, supra, at 424 (referring to \xe2\x80\x9cthe\xc2\xa0inherent nature of most ineffective assistance of trial counsel claims\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added; internal quotation marks omitted)); see also Martinez, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 19\xe2\x80\x9320, n.\xc2\xa01 (Scalia, J., dissenting) (There is no \xe2\x80\x9crelevant difference between cases in which the State says that certain claims can only be brought on collateral review and cases in which those claims by their nature can only be brought on collateral review\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Third, Martinez pointed out that, unless \xe2\x80\x9ccounsel\xe2\x80\x99s errors in an initial-review collateral proceeding .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. establish cause to excuse the procedural default in a federal habeas proceeding, no court will review the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s claims.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 10\xe2\x80\x9311 (majority opinion). The same is true when the prisoner claims ineffective assistance of appellate counsel.

\n

The Court argues to the contrary. It says that at least one court\xe2\x80\x94namely, the trial court\xe2\x80\x94will have considered the underlying legal error. Ante, at 11. (If not, perhaps trial counsel was ineffective.) But I believe the Court here misses the point. The prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s complaint is about the ineffectiveness of his appellate counsel. That ineffectiveness could consist, for example, in counsel\xe2\x80\x99s failure to appeal 10 different erroneous decisions of the trial court. The fact that the trial court made those decisions (assuming they are erroneous) does not help the prisoner. To the contrary, it forms the basis of his ineffectiveness claim. In the absence of a Martinez-like rule, the prisoner here (and prisoners in similar cases) would receive no review of their ineffective-assistance claims. Moreover, there will be cases in which no court will consider the underlying trial error, either. Suppose that, during the pendency of the appeal, appellate counsel learns of a Brady violation, juror misconduct, judicial bias, or some similar violation whose basis was not known during the trial. See Brady v. Maryland,\n\n373 U.\xc2\xa0S. 83 (1963)\n. And suppose appellate counsel fails to pursue the claim in the manner prescribed by state law. Without the exception petitioner here seeks, no court will hear either the appellate-ineffective-assistance claim or the underlying Brady, misconduct, or bias claim.

\n

Fourth, the Martinez Court believed that its decision would \xe2\x80\x9cnot .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. put a significant strain on state resources.\xe2\x80\x9d 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 15. That is because Martinez imposed limiting conditions: It excuses only those defaults that (1) occur at the initial-review collateral proceeding; (2) where prisoner had no counsel, or ineffective counsel, in that proceeding; and (3) where the underlying claim of ineffective assistance is \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., has \xe2\x80\x9csome merit.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 14\xe2\x80\x9316. Moreover, as the Court pointed out, because many States provide prisoners with counsel in initial-review collateral proceedings (or at least when the prisoner seems to have a meritorious claim), it is unlikely that prisoners will default substantial ineffective-assistance claims. See id., at 14\xe2\x80\x9315 (providing examples). Finally, there is no evidence before us that Martinez has produced a greater-than-expected increase in courts\xe2\x80\x99 workload, even though Martinez applies, as Texas concedes, \xe2\x80\x9cin most States.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 38.

\n

It therefore seems unlikely that applying Martinez to ineffective-assistance-of-appellate-counsel claims will \xe2\x80\x9cput a significant strain on\xe2\x80\x9d state or federal resources. As I have said, the same limitations as the Court placed upon the assertion of a Martinez claim would apply here. And the Court\xe2\x80\x99s fear of triggering federal second-guessing of many, if not all, trial errors is of no greater concern here than it was in Martinez, for both trial- and appellate-level ineffectiveness claims \xe2\x80\x9ccould serve as the gateway to federal review of a host of trial errors.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13. Given a natural judicial hesitation to second-guess counsels\xe2\x80\x99 decisions, it is not surprising that we have no significant evidence of defaulted claims of ineffective assistance with \xe2\x80\x9csome merit\xe2\x80\x9d flooding the federal courts, either in respect to trial counsel (as in Martinez) or in respect to appellate counsel (as here). See Strickland, 466 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 690\xe2\x80\x93691 (To prevail on an ineffective-assistance claim, the defendant must show that his attorney\xe2\x80\x99s actions \xe2\x80\x9cwere outside the wide range of professionally competent assistance,\xe2\x80\x9d rather than strategic decisions to which the courtmust defer, and that those actions had an \xe2\x80\x9ceffect on the judgment\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

In fact, Texas has supplied some empirical evidence, but that evidence suggests that courts can manage a Martinez exception expanded to include claims of ineffective assistance of appellate counsel. Texas says that in the Ninth Circuit, which has applied Martinez to ineffective-assistance-of-appellate-counsel claims since late 2013, petitioners have used the expanded version of Martinez \xe2\x80\x9cin dozens\xe2\x80\x9d of federal habeas cases. Brief for Respondent 37. (Texas specifically refers to 10 cases, in only 1 of which the petitioner prevailed. Ibid., n.\xc2\xa013.) During that period, state prisoners filed at least 7,500 federal habeas petitions in the Ninth Circuit. See Ninth Circuit Ann. Rep. 71 (2015) (2,468 cases referred to magistrate judges in 2014; 2,693 in 2015). Hence, Texas\xe2\x80\x99 estimate of added workload comes down to an increase of \xe2\x80\x9cdozens\xe2\x80\x9d of cases out of 7,500 cases in total. That figure represents an increase, but not an increase significant enough to warrant depriving a prisoner of any forum to adjudicate a substantial claim that he was deprived of his constitutional right to effective assistance of appellate counsel.

\n

III

\n

In my view, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s effort to distinguish Martinez comes down to the following points: (1) Martinez concerned only claims of ineffective trial counsel; (2) Martinez involved trial errors that, at least sometimes, would have escaped review, while here at least one court (the trial court) may have reviewed the underlying legal error; (3) Martinez involved cases in which the State itself pre-vented its appellate courts from reviewing the claim of trial counsel\xe2\x80\x99s ineffectiveness, whereas here it is the nature of the ineffectiveness claim that prevents the appellate courts from reviewing it; and (4) extending Martinez could flood the federal system with normally meritless claims.

\n

I have explained why I believe the last mentioned empirical prediction does not distinguish Martinez and why, in any event, it is unlikely to prove correct. See supra, at 7\xe2\x80\x938. And I have explained why the second and third points do not successfully distinguish Martinez. The second fails to focus on the relevant claim: ineffective assistance of counsel. See supra, at 6. And it fails to acknowledge that there may be cases in which the trial court will not have considered the legal error underlying the ineffective-assistance claim. Ibid. The third has little to do with the matter. It overlooks the fact that there is no \xe2\x80\x9crelevant difference\xe2\x80\x9d between cases in which the State requires that certain claims be brought only on collateral review and \xe2\x80\x9ccases in which those claims by their nature can only be brought on collateral review,\xe2\x80\x9d such as claims of ineffective assistance of appellate counsel. See supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x936 (quoting Martinez, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 19\xe2\x80\x9320, n.\xc2\xa01 (Scalia, J., dissenting)). In both cases, the State\xe2\x80\x99s scheme deprives a prisoner from having his substantial constitutional claim heard, through no fault of his own.

\n

As to the first point, the Court is of course right. Martinez had to do only with the ineffectiveness of trial counsel. But our cases make clear that due process requires a criminal defendant to have effective assistance of appellate counsel as well. See supra, at 4. Indeed, effective trial counsel and appellate counsel are inextricably connected elements of a fair trial.

\n

The basic legal principle that should determine the outcome of this case is the principle that requires courts to treat like cases alike. To put the matter more familiarly, what is sauce for the goose is sauce for the gander. The dissent in Martinez wrote that there \xe2\x80\x9cis not a dime\xe2\x80\x99s worth of difference in principle between [ineffective-assistance-of-trial-counsel] cases and many other cases in which initial state habeas will be the first opportunity for a particular claim to be raised,\xe2\x80\x9d including \xe2\x80\x9cclaims asserting ineffective assistance of appellate counsel.\xe2\x80\x9d 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 19 (opinion of Scalia, J.). I agree.

\n

With respect, I dissent.
